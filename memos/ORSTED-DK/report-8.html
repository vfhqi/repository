§1 — Industry Overview + Value Chain Economics

The offshore wind industry is a capital-intensive, policy-scaffolded infrastructure sector with a large and structurally growing addressable market — but one whose economics are acutely sensitive to the gap between contract strike prices and installed costs, a gap that compressed severely in 2021-2024.

  • Verdict: Industry size is large and growing (global offshore wind market ~$50-60bn annual installation spend, ~40-50 GW annual additions by 2030 per GWEC and Wood Mackenzie), but margin pool quality is uneven — earlier vintages of projects generate solid returns, recent vintages have been loss-making at fixed-price [#J]

  • Industry size: Global installed offshore wind capacity reached ~75 GW in 2024 (GWEC 2024), up from ~35 GW in 2020 — a compound growth rate of ~20% pa; Europe (~35 GW) leads, China (~40 GW) growing fast but largely domestically-closed [#A]

  • Scale context: Annual additions in 2023 were approximately 10-11 GW globally; GWEC forecasts 30-40 GW pa additions by 2028-2030 if supply chain bottlenecks clear [#A]

  • Addressable market: Ørsted's relevant TAM is non-China global offshore wind — approximately 35 GW cumulatively installed in Europe + US + APAC ex-China; strong pipeline but pace depends on policy/grid connection [#I]

  • Growth trajectory: Multi-decade secular tailwind driven by decarbonisation mandates — EU targets 60 GW offshore wind by 2030 (current ~36 GW), UK targets 50 GW by 2030 (current ~15 GW), US targets 30 GW by 2030 (current ~3 GW) — all legally-binding or near-legally-binding [#A]

  • GDP+ comparison: Offshore wind capex has grown at approximately 15-20% pa over 2015-2024, well above global GDP growth of ~2-3%; this is a structurally above-GDP sector in volume terms, though margin is not correlated to volume in the same way [#J]

  • Structural driver: Three reinforcing drivers underpin demand: (1) carbon-price/policy mandates forcing grid decarbonisation; (2) falling levelised cost of electricity over the medium-term making offshore wind cost-competitive with gas post-2025 normalisation; (3) electricity demand growth from EV/heat-pump/data-centre electrification [#A]

  • Volume secular: Electricity demand in Europe is forecast to grow at 1.5-2.5% pa through 2035 (IEA WEO 2024), with offshore wind taking disproportionate share of new capacity additions [#I]

  • Geographic split: Europe is the structural heartland (UK, Germany, Denmark, Netherlands, Belgium, Sweden) — roughly 50% of global non-China offshore wind revenue pool; US is the high-growth/high-risk frontier; APAC (Taiwan, South Korea, Japan, Australia) is an emerging but smaller share today [#I]

  • Fragmentation vs consolidation: The developer segment is consolidating around 8-10 large players (Ørsted, RWE, Vattenfall, Equinor, BP, TotalEnergies, Ocean Winds/EDP-Engie, Mainstream/Aker Horizons); turbine supply is effectively a duopoly (Siemens Gamesa + Vestas); installation vessels/marine logistics are a concentrated oligopoly — consolidation is net positive for large incumbents [#A]

  • Value chain stage: End-to-end value chain spans five major stages: (1) Seabed lease/concession; (2) Development and permitting; (3) EPC construction; (4) Operations and maintenance (O&M); (5) Power marketing/offtake [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on industry structure is that the apparent margin pool is more government-policy-dependent than competitively-earned — if political will for offshore wind subsidies weakens (US IRA clawbacks, UK strike-price caps), the entire projected volume growth could reprice downward; simultaneously, turbine OEM and vessel-contractor pricing power demonstrated in 2021-24 shows the construction-phase margin pool can shift rapidly upstream, leaving developers in a value-chain squeeze between regulators (holding price) and OEMs (holding cost)

§2 — Competitive Landscape + Industry Structure

  • Verdict: Ørsted holds genuine #1 global offshore wind developer leadership by installed base, but competitive intensity is rising sharply as oil majors (BP, Equinor, Shell, TotalEnergies) and Korean/Japanese conglomerates deploy capital; industry structure is concentrating but not yet oligopolistic, with competitor attitude shifting from cooperative (2010-2018) to increasingly aggressive on lease auctions [#J]

  • Competitor tier — global leaders: Ørsted (~8-9 GW installed), RWE (~4-5 GW), Vattenfall (~3-4 GW), Equinor (~1-2 GW operational + large pipeline), BP Offshore Wind (~2 GW pipeline post-Equinor JV), TotalEnergies (growing pipeline via Seagreen and others) — these 6 form the genuine global offshore wind developer tier [#A]

  • Competitor tier — mid-market/regional: DEME Concessions, Ørsted's former Dong Energy peers (Vestas, through partial equity positions), Ocean Winds (EDP+Engie JV), Mainstream Renewable Power (now Aker Horizons), Swancor (Taiwan) — regional concentration without global scale [#I]

  • Industry rank: Ørsted is definitively #1 by global installed offshore wind capacity (non-China); no single competitor has matched its scale, operating track record, or O&M book depth [#A]

  • Top 5 share (CQ55): Top 5 global offshore wind developers (non-China) hold approximately 60-70% of installed capacity — highly concentrated at the development-operator tier; turbine supply is 90%+ concentrated in Siemens Gamesa + Vestas [#A]

  • Steep distribution curve (CQ56): Ørsted at ~8-9 GW vs RWE at ~4-5 GW vs Vattenfall at ~3-4 GW — clear step-down; Ørsted is approximately 2x the next competitor in installed base; curve is steep at the top [#J]

  • Competitive dimension — scale/cost: Scale drives lower cost of capital, larger O&M fleet density, and better supply-chain negotiating leverage; Ørsted has this advantage vs mid-tier but is being challenged on a project-by-project basis by oil-major balance sheets (BP, Equinor, Shell) with potentially lower WACCs [#A]

  • Competitive dimension — differentiation: Ørsted's differentiation rests on 30+ years of offshore operating history (former DONG Energy), deep O&M data advantage, ESG brand positioning (first mover on decarbonisation), and government/regulator relationship quality; these are genuine but progressively replicable [#J]

  • Intensity (CQ61 Benign-ness): Competitor attitudes have become markedly more aggressive since 2018-2020 as the sector attracted mainstream capital; lease auction bid prices in UK, US, Germany, and Netherlands have risen materially, compressing developer IRRs; the 2022-2023 US lease auction round saw record clearing prices that assumed cost structures that proved incorrect [#A]

  • MOL stability (CQ62): Offshore wind CfD contract margins are relatively stable once locked in — but the margin-on-new-contracts is highly cyclical; recent vintages (2019-2022 FID) locked in low strike prices against high-cost environments; newer vintages benefit from repriced CfDs [#A]

  • Shark attack risk (CQ63): Low on existing O&M book (site-exclusivity protects); HIGH on new lease bidding — oil majors willing to accept lower IRRs, Korean conglomerates cross-subsidise from EPC services; Ørsted has walked away from several US projects rather than accept inadequate returns [#J]

  • Volume vs ROCE (CQ64): Ørsted has demonstrated willingness to prioritise ROCE over volume — the 2023 US project cancellations (Revolution Wind partial impairment, Ocean Wind cancellation, $4bn+ write-off) showed capital discipline even at severe reputational cost; this is a positive signal on competitor attitude quality [#J]

  • Competitiveness trends (CQ65): Net negative trend over 2021-2024 — more competitors with deeper pockets, rising lease-auction prices, OEM supply-chain constraints limiting developer advantage; positive trend on repriced CfDs post-2024; medium-term structural competitive balance uncertain [#J]

  • Share stability (CQ57): Ørsted's operational installed-capacity share is stable by design (it's the O&M book — don't churn); pipeline share is declining as others accumulate awards; 2023-2025 is the first period where Ørsted's forward pipeline share has demonstrably fallen vs aggregate peer pipeline [#A]

  • New entrant share (CQ58): BP/Equinor/Shell have moved from near-zero to material offshore wind pipeline positions since 2018-2021 — new entrant capital commitments are substantial, particularly in UK, US, and Norway; these are not vanilla entrants but sophisticated energy infrastructure operators [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on industry structure is that Ørsted's #1 position is a historical artefact of early-mover advantage, not a durable structural moat — oil majors with $20-30bn+ annual capex budgets can outbid on leases and outspend on supply-chain lock-up; if CfD repricing stalls politically, the larger the competitor's balance sheet, the better its ability to absorb low-return periods; Ørsted's financial position (leverage elevated post-impairment) leaves it more exposed than BP or Equinor to exactly this combination

§3 — Barriers to Entry + Exit + Disruption Forces

  • Verdict: Barriers to entry are meaningful (capital intensity, site exclusivity, permitting complexity, operational knowhow) but not prohibitive for well-capitalised energy majors; the most underpriced disruption force is the speed at which oil-major capital reallocation can replicate offshore wind operational capability [#J]

  • Capital requirement: Development + construction of a single offshore wind farm requires £1-5bn+ of committed capital per project, multi-year construction periods, and balance sheet capacity to absorb construction-phase losses before revenue generation — this is a genuine barrier to small entrants [#A]

  • Scale economy: Ørsted's O&M fleet scale (hundreds of operating turbines) allows shared technical resources, centralised monitoring, and lower per-turbine O&M cost — barrier exists but is a sliding scale, not a cliff [#A]

  • Regulatory barrier: Offshore wind development requires government lease/concession (typically competitive auction), planning consent, grid connection agreement, and environmental impact approval — typical UK/European consenting timeline is 5-8 years from application to start of construction; this is a genuine development barrier [#A]

  • Regulatory barrier quality note: Barrier operates asymmetrically — it slows new entrants but also slows Ørsted; in high-demand environments the queue for consenting is equally long for all developers [#I]

  • IP + knowledge moat: Ørsted's 30+ year offshore operating dataset, subsea cable management, turbine foundation engineering, and inter-array cable design knowledge constitutes genuine proprietary operational IP — most directly relevant to O&M efficiency [#A]

  • Knowledge barrier erosion: Siemens Gamesa and Vestas — as turbine OEMs providing digital monitoring and predictive maintenance platforms — could progressively commoditise Ørsted's O&M knowledge moat over a 5-10 year horizon [#J]

  • Switching cost: Offshore wind O&M contracts are 15-25 year relationships with very low switching rates — once awarded O&M on an asset, Ørsted's operational exclusivity is near-total for the asset life [#A]

  • Lock-in mechanism: Government CfD contracts and long-term PPA agreements lock in offtake for 10-20 years — protecting revenue predictability but also capping upside [#I]

  • Structural exit barrier: Once a wind farm is operating, operators cannot exit without selling the asset (deep but illiquid market) — capital is sunk; this incentivises competitors to maintain discipline on pricing once committed, rather than flood merchant power markets [#A]

  • Disruption force — OEM concentration (CQ50): The Siemens Gamesa turbine reliability crisis of 2022-2024 (SGRE 14MW+ blade failures, warranty provisions, delivery delays) demonstrated that Ørsted's production system is acutely dependent on a near-monopoly supplier; SGRE's Siemens parent absorbed €3bn+ in losses to backstop the business — a near-systemic disruption event that impacted all developers [#A]

  • ⚡ RARE — SGRE dependency: Ørsted's concentration in SGRE turbines across flagship projects (Hornsea 1, Hornsea 2, future Hornsea 3) creates a single-point-of-failure technology risk uncharacteristic of a large-cap infrastructure operator; peer RWE has more diversified OEM exposure [cross-ref: §5 MOAT]

  • Disruption force — vessel scarcity (CQ50): Offshore wind installation vessel supply is critically constrained — Heerema, DEME, Saipem, and Boskalis control the world's heavy-lift vessel fleet; new vessel construction takes 3-4 years and costs $300-600m; vessel day-rates doubled 2021-2024; this is a systemic disruption force limiting build-out pace and compressing developer margins [#A]

  • Adjacency entry (CQ46): Oil majors (BP, Equinor, Shell, TotalEnergies) entered from energy infrastructure adjacency — they bring balance-sheet strength, permitting relationships, and offshore construction experience; Korean conglomerates (Samsung, Doosan, Hyundai) entered from manufacturing adjacency — they bring supply-chain integration and government relationship capital [#A]

  • Vanilla new entrants (CQ47): Limited — financial investors (pension funds, infrastructure funds like Macquarie, BlackRock) bid for operational assets via secondary market, not development/construction; this is capital-recycling, not true new entrant threat at the development tier [#I]

  • Innovative new entrants (CQ48): Floating offshore wind is the emerging technology frontier — different installation vessel requirements, different foundation design, earlier-stage; Equinor (Hywind) and Principle Power are FLOW leaders; Ørsted has limited FLOW capability vs its fixed-bottom dominance [#A]

  • Substitute pressure (CQ49): Ground-mounted solar + battery storage is the most credible substitute for new power capacity at lower cost — solar LCOE now below offshore wind LCOE in most markets; offshore wind retains advantages in generation profile, grid services, and coastal geography; not a substitution threat to existing assets, but constrains addressable market growth [#J]

  • Political/regulatory disruption (CQ51, CQ52): US IRA modifications or reversal under future administrations represents the single largest political disruption risk — Ørsted's US programme was built around IRA PTC economics; any rollback fundamentally changes project economics; European regulatory risk is lower but UK strike-price adequacy debates and German grid bottlenecks are real constraints [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The inversion case on barriers is that they are primarily temporal (regulatory queue, supply-chain access) rather than permanent (IP, network effects, brand switching costs) — a sufficiently capitalised entrant with a 5-7 year runway can build the permitting, supply-chain, and operational experience necessary to compete at Ørsted's level; BP and Equinor are demonstrating this in real-time; the knowledge-moat case depends on Ørsted converting its operational data into proprietary cost advantages faster than OEM digitalisation commoditises that data

§4 — Operator Quality + Foundation Resourcing

  • Verdict: Ørsted demonstrates genuine strategic ambition and transformation track record (DONG Energy coal-to-wind pivot is a landmark case study), but recent execution failures on the US programme and the 2023 impairment cycle raise serious questions about guidance credibility and capital-allocation discipline under the current leadership [#J]

  • Strategic ambition (RA1.CQ1): Ørsted's stated strategy is to be the world's leading developer and operator of offshore wind, with a medium-term installed capacity target of 35-38 GW by 2030 (pre-2023 target; subsequently revised to ~35 GW) and a long-run aspiration to be a green energy major — ambition is large, clearly articulated, and force-directional [#A]

  • Strategy fit (RA1.CQ2): Strategy is highly congruent with the macro decarbonisation trend and with Ørsted's core competencies in offshore wind development and O&M — there is no diversification into adjacent businesses (solar, onshore wind at scale) that would dilute the focus thesis; Power-to-X is an incremental adjacency [#J]

  • CEO calibre (RA2.CQ3): Mads Nipper was appointed January 2021, incoming from GRUNDFOS (pump manufacturer) — an unconventional appointment relative to sector norms; prior to Nipper, Henrik Poulsen had led the DONG-to-Ørsted transformation; Nipper's record in his first 4 years has been defined by the 2023 impairment crisis, US programme restructuring, and failure to deliver on financial guidance [#J]

  • CEO track record flag: The 2023 DKK 28.4bn impairment and concurrent dividend suspension under Nipper's watch represents a significant delivery failure; the key question is whether this was an unforeseeable macro shock (OEM cost inflation + rate rises) or a capital-allocation error (over-commitment to fixed-price contracts at inadequate risk premium) [#J]

  • Hiring + leadership team (RA2.CQ4): Ørsted has built a deep offshore wind technical team — Chief Technology Officer, Head of Portfolio Development, regional CEOs for US, Europe, APAC; however, US leadership turnover following the programme cancellations raises execution risk concerns [#I]

  • Resourcing (RA2.CQ5): Ørsted is well-resourced in engineering and project management terms — approximately 7,000 FTEs (2023), heavily concentrated in Denmark, UK, and Germany; R&D/innovation investment is below software-sector norms but appropriate for a capital-project-heavy infrastructure operator [#I]

  • Ant-colony quality: The operational workforce managing Hornsea 1 and 2 (world's largest offshore wind farms) demonstrates industrial-grade execution capability; resourcing quality at the construction management tier has been challenged by the US project failures [#A]

  • Culture + alignment (RA2.CQ6): Ørsted's culture is strongly mission-driven (leading offshore wind global ambition, sustainability KPIs integrated into pay) — a genuine differentiator for talent attraction in a sector competing for scarce offshore engineering talent; however, mission-alignment can mask performance-accountability gaps [#J]

  • Guidance posture (RA2.CQ7): ❌ Conservative guidance posture is clearly absent — Ørsted raised 2023 EPS guidance in early 2023 and then issued a profit warning in August 2023; the subsequent DKK 28.4bn impairment was not flagged to investors with adequate lead time; this is a guidance credibility failure vs peer benchmark (RWE, Iberdrola — both have maintained more stable guidance tracks) [#A]

  • Communication clarity (RA2.CQ8): Ørsted's investor communications are technically detailed and metrics-rich (ROCE per project, IRR by programme) but the 2023 crisis demonstrated that the depth of granularity can obscure rather than illuminate systemic risk exposures — investors were given project-level IRR data while portfolio-level correlation to OEM cost inflation was inadequately disclosed [#J]

  • Capital allocation — RONIC (RA3.CQ9): Ørsted's historical RONIC on completed offshore wind projects in the 2015-2022 vintage is approximately 10-14% equity IRR (per company-disclosed project economics) — respectable for regulated/contracted infrastructure, but below the 25-30%+ RONIC threshold that drives high-quality GENERATE scores; the 2022-2024 vintage is RONIC-negative at project level on cancelled US assets [#A]

  • Acts not talks (RA3.CQ10): The DONG Energy to Ørsted transformation (2009-2019 coal-to-wind pivot, shedding oil/gas/coal assets, listing as pure-play offshore wind in 2016) is one of the most credible "acts not talks" corporate transformations in European energy — a landmark positive signal [#J]

  • Acts vs recent record: The 2023 US programme failures partially invert this signal — commitment to US expansion at fixed-price contracts with inadequate contingency for inflation demonstrates strategic overreach; the walk-away decision itself is an "acts" signal, but the prior commitment decision was a capital-allocation error [#J]

  • Track record vs environments (RA3.CQ11): Ørsted's track record through the 2015-2020 period (strong build-out, rising EBITDA, DONG IPO success) was excellent; the 2021-2023 period constitutes the first serious test against a hostile environment, and the company failed relative to guidance — a negative signal on through-cycle delivery [#J]

  • Outlier standards (RA3.CQ12): Ørsted claims world-class offshore wind O&M standards — capacity factors on Hornsea 1 and 2 reportedly 40-46% (vs industry average ~35-40%), implying genuine operational excellence; the engineering and safety standards are widely cited as industry-leading [#A]

  • Shareholders first (RA3.CQ13): ❌ Mixed signal — the 2023 dividend suspension was the correct capital-preservation decision but followed years of growing DPS against a deteriorating balance sheet; dividend policy was used as a shareholder-comfort signal for too long; shareholders were not informed first but last [#J]

  • State ownership flag: The Danish State's majority ownership is non-standard vs global energy peer governance (RWE, Vattenfall are also partially state-owned; Iberdrola is fully listed; BP/Equinor have different profiles) — it constrains a hostile takeover premium and introduces political-economy considerations into capital allocation [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on operator quality is that the DONG-to-Ørsted transformation track record is now 10-15 years old and under previous leadership — the current CEO's tenure is characterised by the largest impairment in the company's history; the guidance credibility deficit, US programme miscalculation, and dividend suspension suggest that the operator-quality premium that underpinned Ørsted's valuation premium pre-2021 may have been partially eroded and not yet rebuilt; the Danish State majority ownership adds a further layer of non-commercial decision-making risk

§5 — Advantaged Business + SRCA Depth

  • Verdict: Ørsted possesses genuine structural competitive advantages — site exclusivity, O&M data depth, supply-chain relationships, and the world's largest offshore wind O&M book — but GENERATE-level outputs have deteriorated materially since 2022, and the case for gravitational SRCA improvement now rests heavily on CfD repricing and cost normalisation rather than mechanical competitive widening [#J]

MOAT (RA4)

  • Customer moat (CQ16): CfD + PPA contracts lock in offtake for 10-20 year terms with investment-grade counterparties (UK government, large corporates) — churn is structurally near-zero on the existing book; customer relationships are sticky by design and by contract [#A]

  • Customer moat depth: New customer moat (lease-award-to-CfD relationship with government auction bodies) is weaker — it's a competitive tender, not a repeat-purchase relationship [#J]

  • Production moat (CQ17): Seabed lease exclusivity is the single strongest production moat — once awarded a development zone, Ørsted has exclusive rights to develop that area; this cannot be replicated by competitors regardless of capital availability [#A]

  • Production moat geography: Hornsea , Borkum (Germany), Bornholm (Denmark) — prime offshore wind zones; these leases represent ~10-15 GW of potential additional capacity beyond current installations; genuine scarcity value [#A]

  • Scale moat (CQ18): Ørsted's 8-9 GW installed O&M base provides scale economies in: shared O&M vessel contracts, centralised monitoring infrastructure, O&M engineer deployment, supply-chain relationships — approximately 15-20% lower per-GW O&M cost vs a sub-1GW portfolio (estimated, vs company commentary) [#A]

  • Scale moat — supply chain: Multi-GW turbine procurement relationships with SGRE and Vestas provide preferential pricing, delivery priority, and warranty terms relative to sub-scale developers [#A]

  • Regulatory moat (CQ19): Deep relationships with UK Crown Estate, Danish Energy Agency, German BSH (maritime authorities), and Dutch RVO — these are not explicit regulatory advantages but translate into planning-process familiarity and relationship capital that accelerates permitting timelines vs newer entrants [#J]

  • Regulatory moat — US absence: Ørsted's US regulatory relationships are far shallower than European, which contributed to the US programme difficulties — a moat that is geography-specific, not globally portable [#A]

  • Culture moat (CQ20): DONG Energy alumni network and Ørsted's 30-year offshore engineering culture creates a talent flywheel — the company is disproportionately able to attract offshore wind engineers and project managers who want mission-alignment and career development in offshore wind [#J]

  • Culture moat risk: As oil majors (BP, Equinor) build offshore wind businesses, they compete for the same talent pool with higher total comp — culture moat is real but under pressure [#I]

  • High relative scale (CQ21): Ørsted is ~2x the installed capacity of the next Western developer — genuine relative-scale leadership; revenue (~DKK 76bn in 2023) is large vs sector peers, though not uncommon in European utilities context [#A]

  • Large realistic TAM (CQ22): Global offshore wind installed capacity target of 380 GW by 2030 (IEA Net Zero scenario) vs current ~75 GW implies 5x growth potential; even a declining market-share scenario (from 12% to 8% global share) implies Ørsted could grow installed base to 30+ GW — well within strategic planning assumptions; TAM is large and realistic [#J]

OPERATE (RA5)

  • High SCU (CQ23): Ørsted's operational capacity utilisation (wind farm availability) is approximately 95-97% across the fleet (company-reported) — vs industry benchmarks of 92-95%; this is above-peer-average operational utilisation [#A]

  • SCU evidence: Hornsea 1 (1.2 GW) reported capacity factors of ~40-46% against a design assumption of ~40% — within-range but not transformationally superior [#A]

  • Leading or non-inferior RSCU (CQ24): Ørsted's relative RSCU (relative-scale-corrected utilisation) is harder to assess without granular unit-economics data; the evidence does not clearly disprove non-inferiority — operational metrics are broadly at or above peer median; no evidence of systematic below-peer operational underperformance [#J]

  • RSCU caveat: The 2023 US construction-phase projects demonstrated below-peer cost execution vs Vattenfall/RWE's more conservative European pipeline positioning — construction-phase RSCU is weaker than O&M-phase RSCU [#A]

  • Pricing power utilised (CQ25): Ørsted does not utilise pricing power in a traditional sense — CfD prices are government-set; PPA prices are bilaterally negotiated; O&M prices are contractually determined at inception; pricing leverage exists at the O&M contract renegotiation stage (~every 3-5 years) but evidence of systematic above-inflation O&M repricing is thin [#J]

  • PPA pricing trajectory: New PPAs signed since 2023 reflect the repriced market — anecdotal evidence of PPA clearing prices 15-25% above 2020-2021 levels, improving economics on new-start projects [#I]

  • Scale economies shared (CQ26): Limited evidence of Ørsted explicitly sharing scale economies with end customers via pricing — the CfD structure means "savings" accrue to the government mechanism (lower consumer energy costs) rather than direct customer price reduction; O&M scale economies are retained as margin improvement [#J]

GENERATE (RA6)

  • Revenue growth 12-20% (CQ27): Ørsted's revenue growth has been volatile — DKK 61bn (2021), DKK 77bn (2022), DKK 76bn (2023) — approximately flat in nominal terms; installed capacity growth (GW additions) has been on a 10-15% pa trajectory, but revenue recognition is lumpy due to construction-phase accounting; consensus 2024-2026 organic revenue CAGR approximately 5-10% (post-US impairment) [#A]

§6 — Industry Evolution + Paradigm Fit

  • Verdict: Offshore wind is consolidating toward larger platforms with better cost structures, while policy repricing post-2022 is restoring project economics — Ørsted sits correctly positioned in the evolutionary direction but with a credibility deficit that means paradigm-fit benefits are not currently being priced in by the market [#J]

  • Evolution — consolidation: The offshore wind sector is consolidating at the developer tier — smaller players (Green Investment Group, Copenhagen Infrastructure Partners, Mainstream) are recycling capital into operating assets, ceding development activity to the large 6-8 platform developers; this net-positive for large incumbents [#A]

  • Evolution — OEM concentration: Turbine supply consolidation (SGRE troubles effectively removed a third turbine OEM option from the market) means developers face an even more concentrated supplier oligopoly in 2024-2026 than in 2019-2021 — negative for developer margins [#J]

  • Evolution — vertical integration: Limited evidence of developer vertical integration into turbine manufacturing; Ørsted's strategy remains asset-assembly (lease + develop + operate) not vertical integration into OEM — contrast with Equinor's minor interests in wind manufacturing; vertical integration is not a meaningful trend at the developer level [#I]

  • Evolution — technology disruption: Floating offshore wind represents the next-generation technology enabling access to deeper waters (50%+ of global offshore resource is in waters unsuitable for fixed-bottom foundations); Ørsted has limited FLOW capability — its pipeline is almost entirely fixed-bottom; FLOW moves to commercial scale c.2028-2035 [#A]

  • FLOW positioning: Equinor (Hywind Scotland), Principle Power, SBM Offshore are the current FLOW leaders; Ørsted is a follower in FLOW — a technology evolution mismatch if FLOW scales faster than consensus expects [#J]

  • Regulatory change: Post-2023 CfD repricing has restored project economics in UK (AR6 strike prices higher), Germany (repriced tender rounds), and partially in Denmark; the US Inflation Reduction Act remains intact as of 2026 but with political uncertainty under the Trump administration; regulatory trajectory is net-positive in Europe, uncertain in US [#A]

  • Pricing power direction: Margin trajectory is improving from 2024+ vintage projects (repriced CfDs, normalised supply chain) vs the 2021-2023 trough — but the pace of improvement is gradual; per-MWh development margin recovery to 2019-2020 levels is a 2025-2027 story, not immediate [#J]
