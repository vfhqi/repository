1. US OPO Regulatory Changes

The US organ procurement system is undergoing its most significant structural reform in decades. This is a direct, first-order tailwind for XVIVO.

Background. The US has 56 Organ Procurement Organisations (OPOs), each holding a geographic monopoly over organ procurement in their Donation Service Area . For years, OPOs operated with minimal accountability. CMS data showed wide variance in organ recovery and utilisation rates between the best and worst OPOs.

2020 Final Rule. CMS published a final rule in November 2020 revising OPO outcome measures, replacing self-reported data with objective, verifiable metrics (CDC death data). OPOs are now tiered:
- Tier 1: Top quartile performance -- automatic recertification.
- Tier 2: Middle performance -- conditional, subject to improvement plans.
- Tier 3: Bottom quartile -- face decertification.

January 2026 Proposed Rule. CMS published a further proposed rule (CMS-3409-P) in January 2026 to strengthen the implementation framework ahead of the first recertification cycle. Key provisions:
- OPOs must assess and improve performance on placement of organs from "medically complex" donors (older donors, donors with suboptimal health).
- New record-keeping requirements for research organs, including pancreata used for islet cell research, closing a loophole where viable organs were diverted to research.
- Tier 3 OPOs assigned only to underperforming DSAs face decertification. Higher-performing OPOs can then bid for those territories.
- 2026 performance reports (based on 2024 data) will be published Spring 2026. Recertification/decertification actions follow the final rule (expected late 2026).

Implications for XVIVO. The regulatory pressure creates demand-pull for organ preservation technology at multiple levels:
- OPOs facing decertification have strong incentive to increase recovery and utilisation rates. Perfusion technology (EVLP for lungs, hypothermic machine perfusion for kidneys/livers) directly enables this by salvaging marginal organs.
- The shift toward recovering organs from "medically complex" donors increases the proportion of organs that benefit from evaluation and reconditioning via perfusion -- exactly XVIVO's value proposition.
- Competition for OPO contracts (as failing OPOs are replaced) favours organisations that invest in best-in-class preservation technology.
- AOPO (the OPO trade body) has pushed back, arguing US OPOs are "strong" and the rule "threatens future success." This resistance is likely to be overridden given bipartisan congressional support for reform.

Assessment: This is a multi-year structural tailwind. Not a one-off event but an ongoing ratcheting of standards that will drive adoption of perfusion technology for years. Probability of sustained positive impact: High.

2. EVLP Adoption Curve

Where we are. EVLP remains in early stages of adoption despite being clinically available since 2015 in the US. Historical penetration was approximately 3% of US lung transplants (2015-2018 data). Recent data suggests meaningful acceleration, but from a very low base.

Two adoption models have emerged:
1. In-house EVLP: Large transplant centres operate their own XPS systems. Capital-intensive and requires dedicated staff. Limited to top-tier centres.
2. Centralised EVLP: Dedicated facilities (e.g., Lung Bioengineering, now part of the wider ecosystem) receive, evaluate, and recondition lungs on behalf of multiple transplant centres. Centralised EVLP usage grew from 35% to 56% of all EVLP cases between 2018 and 2022.

Growth evidence. Cleveland Clinic attributes 30-40% of its lung transplant volume (approximately 40-60 per year) to EVLP, and is on pace for 155 total lung transplants in 2025. Northwestern Medicine has also scaled volumes significantly using EVLP and XPS technology.

Barriers to further adoption:
- Cost. The XPS system and STEEN Solution consumables are expensive. Smaller centres struggle to justify the capital expenditure.
- Expertise. EVLP requires trained perfusionists and surgical teams. Workforce constraints limit expansion.
- Reimbursement. EVLP costs are bundled into the transplant DRG -- there is no separate CPT code for the perfusion procedure. This means centres absorb the perfusion cost but recoup it through the higher-value transplant DRG.

Market dynamics. In the US, only two EVLP systems are clinically available: XVIVO's XPS (with STEEN Solution) and TransMedics' OCS Lung. XVIVO claims its products are trusted by >90% of transplant clinics globally. The XPS uses a static, cold-perfusion approach (hypothermic), which is simpler and does not require continuous monitoring during transport. TransMedics' OCS is a portable, warm-perfusion system.

Assessment: EVLP is still in the first third of its adoption S-curve. The combination of OPO reform (increasing marginal organ supply), growing transplant volumes (lung transplants up 10% in 2024), and the shift toward centralised perfusion models should drive continued adoption. XVIVO's dominant position in static EVLP (>90% market trust) means it captures disproportionate share of this growth. Key watch: whether centralised models or in-house adoption drives the next leg of growth.

3. Heart Transplant Market Entry

This is the fulcrum driver for XVIVO's valuation re-rating. The heart market is larger than lung, and XVIVO's clinical data is strong. But execution risk is meaningful.

Clinical data -- European Multicenter Trial (NIHP2019).
- Randomised controlled trial comparing XVIVO Heart Assist Transport (hypothermic oxygenated perfusion) vs standard ice-based cold storage.
- 76% reduction in severe primary graft dysfunction * -- the most dangerous post-transplant complication for hearts.
- 92% one-year survival in XVIVO group vs 86% in control arm -- corresponding to six additional lives saved in the trial cohort.
- 38% reduction in severe complications** overall (33% vs 47%).
- 12-month follow-up results published in European Heart Journal (April 2026) and presented at ISHLT 2025 in Boston.
- This is strong, publication-grade data in a top-tier journal. Meaningful clinical differentiation.

US Regulatory Pathway (PRESERVE Trial).
- FDA approved XVIVO's Investigational Device Exemption for the PRESERVE trial.
- 141 patients enrolled across 14 leading US heart transplant centres. Enrollment completed five months ahead of schedule (November 2024) -- strong clinical demand signal.
- One-year follow-up data collection underway. Primary endpoints: 1-year survival and freedom from graft failure within 30 days.
- PMA submission targeted Q2 2026. However, XVIVO expects an FDA Advisory Panel meeting (expert panel), which adds at least 180 days.
- Realistic US launch timeline: H2 2027 at earliest (assumption: PMA filed mid-2026, panel Q4 2026, approval H1-H2 2027).

Continued Access Protocol .
- While awaiting PMA, XVIVO is running a CAP study (PRESERVE CAP) enrolling up to 60 additional patients across 26 US centres.
- First US patient enrolled July 2025.
- This maintains clinical momentum and builds the US user base ahead of commercial launch.

European CE Mark -- Delay.
- XVIVO Heart Assist Transport (the device) and the Perfusion Set have received CE marking.
- The perfusion solution (the liquid used during hypothermic perfusion) has been delayed 6-12 months. The notified body must seek scientific opinions from both EMA and another EU competent authority because the solution's composition classifies it as an ancillary medicinal substance within a medical device.
- The device cannot be used commercially without the solution. So the full European heart launch is pushed to H1-H2 2026 (from original H1 2025 target).
- Impact: European heart revenue will be negligible in 2025 and only begin ramping in late 2026 (assumption).

Heart market sizing.
- US heart transplants: 4,572 in 2024, roughly stable YoY.
- Current preservation: overwhelmingly cold storage (ice). TransMedics OCS Heart is the primary warm-perfusion alternative, but carries the baggage of the Scorpion Capital allegations.
- XVIVO's hypothermic approach is differentiated: no need for continuous monitoring during transport, potentially simpler logistics, and strong PGD reduction data.
- If XVIVO captures 30-50% of the US heart preservation market post-approval (assumption), at estimated ASP of $15-25K per case (assumption based on lung EVLP pricing), this represents $20-55M in incremental US revenue. European penetration would add further.

Assessment: Heart is a binary catalyst for the stock. The clinical data is strong. The regulatory pathway is long but progressing. The 6-12 month CE delay and the likely FDA panel meeting compress the timeline for revenue contribution. Key risk: FDA panel review adds uncertainty. Key watch: PRESERVE one-year data readout (expected H1 2026).

4. TransMedics Competitive Dynamics

TransMedics is XVIVO's most significant competitor. Its recent troubles create both opportunity and complexity.

Scorpion Capital Short Report (January 2025).
Titled "Walk Like An Egyptian," the 150+ page report alleged:
- The NOP operates as a "mafia-style" extortion scheme, using coercive tying to force centres into using TransMedics' OCS for all organs if they want access to the NOP logistics network.
- Fraudulent billing: overcharging hospitals for unnecessary charter flights.
- Organ trafficking: steering rejected organs to favoured centres in exchange for NOP participation. Allegations of kickbacks to surgeons.
- Off-label use of OCS devices beyond FDA-approved indications.
- NOP staffed with H1B visa holders unlicensed to practice medicine in the US.

Market impact:
- TMDX stock fell 13% immediately. Securities class actions filed by multiple law firms.
- TransMedics issued a robust denial, citing 7,000+ successful organ transplants. The company has not disclosed any formal DOJ or FBI investigation (as of search results), but the securities litigation is ongoing.

TransMedics financial performance (despite controversy):
- FY2025 revenue: $605.5M (+37% YoY). Still growing rapidly.
- Operating margins expanded to 18% (from 8.5% in 2024).
- First full year of positive cash flow ($152M).
- Material weakness identified in internal controls over financial reporting -- a red flag that lends credibility to governance concerns.

NOP model.
- TransMedics' NOP is a vertically integrated logistics platform: the company procures organs, perfuses them on its OCS devices, and transports them via its own charter flight network.
- NOP touches ~30% of US transplant volume.
- The bundled model is unique in transplantation -- no other company offers a comparable end-to-end service.
- Critics argue it creates dependency and anticompetitive dynamics. Supporters argue it dramatically increases organ availability.

Implications for XVIVO:
- Reputational opportunity. XVIVO's approach is fundamentally different: it sells devices and consumables to hospitals and OPOs, which run the perfusion themselves. No logistics empire, no procurement involvement, no charter flights. This positions XVIVO as the "clean" alternative -- pure medical technology, no vertically integrated conflicts of interest.
- Clinical positioning. XVIVO's hypothermic approach (cold perfusion) vs TransMedics' normothermic approach (warm perfusion) offers a genuine clinical choice. Hypothermic is simpler, does not require continuous monitoring, and has strong PGD data for heart.
- NOP vulnerability. If regulatory scrutiny or litigation constrains the NOP, transplant centres that relied on TransMedics' logistics may need alternative preservation technology. XVIVO is the obvious beneficiary.
- TMDX is not going away. $606M revenue, growing fast, FDA approvals for next-gen trials (ENHANCE Heart, DENOVO Lung). The Scorpion report has not destroyed the business. But it has created a persistent cloud that makes centre-level decision-makers think twice.

Assessment: TransMedics' troubles are a moderate tailwind for XVIVO, primarily through reputational differentiation and potential share-shifting at the margin. The NOP controversy has not collapsed TransMedics' business, but it creates a more receptive market for XVIVO's product-only model. Assumption: TMDX retains dominant market share in warm perfusion, but XVIVO gains incremental share in heart (new market) and potentially lung (switching).

5. Donor Organ Utilisation Rates

The gap between organs recovered and organs transplanted represents XVIVO's core addressable opportunity.

US discard rates (2023 OPTN/SRTR data):
- Kidneys: >20% discard rate. 4,038 left kidneys and 4,220 right kidneys recovered but not transplanted. This is the largest absolute waste pool.
- Lungs: 260 recovered but not transplanted. Lung discard rates have historically been high (estimated 80%+ of potential donor lungs are never recovered; of those recovered, a meaningful fraction are discarded). EVLP directly addresses this by enabling assessment and reconditioning.
- Hearts: 68 recovered but not transplanted. Heart discard is lower in absolute terms but the clinical stakes are highest (no dialysis equivalent for hearts).
- Livers: 1,056 recovered but not transplanted.
- Pancreata: 279 not transplanted.

Global context:
- Only 10% of global demand for organ transplantation is currently met.
- Global utilisation rate (percentage of recovered organs transplanted): median 98% across reporting countries, but this masks enormous variation. Turkey, for example, does not utilise 38% of its deceased donors.
- In 2024, 3,580 deceased donors globally remained unused.

Why organs are discarded:
- Organ quality concerns (age, ischaemia time, infection risk, anatomical issues).
- Logistical failures (transport time exceeding preservation window).
- Centre-level risk aversion (surgeons decline marginal organs rather than risk poor outcomes).
- Inadequate preservation technology to extend viability or assess quality.

XVIVO's role in closing the gap:
- Lungs: EVLP enables assessment and reconditioning of marginal donor lungs. Cleveland Clinic data shows 30-40% of transplants enabled by EVLP -- these are organs that would otherwise have been discarded.
- Hearts: Hypothermic oxygenated perfusion extends preservation time beyond the 4-6 hour ice window, enabling procurement from more distant donors.
- Kidneys: Machine perfusion (Kidney Assist Transport) reduces delayed graft function and enables better assessment of marginal kidneys.
- Livers: Liver Assist enables assessment and reconditioning of marginal livers, with European penetration now >50% in key markets.

Assessment: The organ waste problem is large, persistent, and politically salient. Every incremental percentage point improvement in utilisation translates directly into lives saved and transplant revenue. XVIVO's multi-organ platform is uniquely positioned to address this across all four major solid organs. This is the fundamental demand driver for the business.

6. Reimbursement Dynamics

Reimbursement is an enabler, not a barrier, for XVIVO -- but the structure matters.

US reimbursement (Medicare):
- Organ perfusion costs are bundled into the transplant DRG under Medicare Part A (Inpatient Prospective Payment System). There is no separate CPT code for EVLP or machine perfusion.
- The transplant DRG is an all-inclusive payment covering the entire hospital stay, surgery, devices, and supplies.
- Hospitals absorb the upfront cost of perfusion (XPS system, STEEN Solution consumables) but recover it within the DRG payment.
- The economic logic works when perfusion converts an otherwise-wasted organ into a transplanted organ: the DRG payment for a transplant ($100K-250K+) far exceeds the incremental cost of perfusion ($15-30K estimated, assumption).

European reimbursement:
- Varies by country. Some markets have dedicated reimbursement for machine perfusion; others bundle it similarly to the US.
- XVIVO has noted that longer contracts in Europe may delay the impact of price increases.
- Liver perfusion reimbursement is more established in Europe given higher penetration. XVIVO is investing in data generation to support reimbursement expansion for kidney perfusion.

Strategic implications:
- The bundled DRG model means XVIVO's pricing power is ultimately tied to the transplant centre's margin on the overall transplant procedure.
- As CMS pushes OPOs to increase utilisation (recovering more marginal organs), the incremental demand for perfusion evaluation should increase -- and each incremental transplant enabled by perfusion is profitable for the hospital within the DRG.
- A dedicated EVLP reimbursement code would be a significant catalyst, as it would remove the hospital's need to absorb perfusion costs within the DRG. This does not exist currently. XVIVO and industry groups have been advocating for it.
- TransMedics' NOP model sidesteps some of this by bundling procurement, perfusion, and transport into a single charge. This is part of what Scorpion Capital alleges is overcharging.

Assessment: Reimbursement is neutral-to-positive. The current bundled structure works because the economics of converting wasted organs into transplants are compelling. A dedicated perfusion CPT code would be upside. Key watch: any CMS moves toward dedicated perfusion reimbursement.
