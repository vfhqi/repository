KEY FINDINGS

  • Q1 2026 organic volume growth was +2.8%, reported volume +5.3% to 35.1m hl. Revenue grew +3.0% to DKK 20.7bn. Asia delivered +3.4% and CEEI +4.6% organically. Management maintained full-year organic operating profit guidance of 2-6%.
  • The CEEI +4.6% Q1 volume figure is heavily distorted by PepsiCo distribution expansion (Kazakhstan and Kyrgyzstan added Q1 2026). Organic beer volume in CEEI is almost certainly well below the headline. The meeting must force this decomposition.
  • Vietnam is growing from an easy base — Q1 2025 was weak across Southeast Asia. Carlsberg completed a USD 90m capacity expansion at Phu Bai to 6.0m hl. The question is whether demand is recovering or whether capacity is being absorbed by distribution push ahead of the India IPO-style re-rating narrative.
  • China is a structural drag. Management guided for market decline in 2025 (confirmed) and expected "slightly better but still declining" in 2026. Any Q1 2026 volume growth in China is share gain, not market expansion. This is a different quality of growth.
  • Britvic synergies are running ahead of plan: the target was raised from GBP 100m to GBP 110m after 30% was realised in 2025 (vs original 10-15% expectation). The meeting should test cumulative delivery through Q1 2026 and whether composite selling (beer + soft drinks) is generating measurable revenue upside, not just cost savings.
  • India IPO: Bloomberg reported in February 2026 that Carlsberg appointed three banks and commenced preparations for a ~$700m listing (~25% stake). The IPO is active process, not exploratory. Timing risk from April 2026 market volatility is the live question.
  • Commodity hedge policy targets 90% coverage at year-start for barley and aluminium. The 2027 position is the critical unknown — the meeting must quantify unhedged exposure and sensitivity to a Brent ±$20 scenario.
  • Poland trade-down is confirmed from public commentary. Eastern Europe consumer stress is real. This is a direct read-across to the KQ2 premium vs mainstream dynamic.
  • Heineken Q1 2026: total volume +1.2%, consolidated beer volume -0.2%, premium volume +5.8%. Carlsberg's +2.8% total organic volume outperformed Heineken on a headline basis. The attribution gap needs interrogating.
  • Leverage target: below 2.5x net debt/EBITDA "no later than end of 2027" before buyback resumption. At current trajectory, this is a late-2026 or H1 2027 event depending on India IPO proceeds (~DKK 5bn) and FCF generation.

1. Executive Summary

Meeting objective. The 30 April meeting occurs one day after Q1 2026 results. The CFO will be in explanation mode. The objective is not to restate what was in the release — it is to extract the information that sits behind the headline numbers and test whether the investment thesis is intact, accelerating, or quietly deteriorating.

Priority themes. Volume quality (KQ1) and commodity/margin resilience (KQ2) are co-equal priorities. Britvic capital allocation is the third axis because the synergy trajectory and leverage path determine when the buyback catalyst fires. Everything else is supporting information.

Top 3 priority questions.

  1. Strip CEEI Q1 volume of Pepsi distribution additions — what was organic beer volume in Central and Eastern Europe and India ex-Pepsi in Q1 2026, and what is the trend?
  2. What percentage of 2027 barley, aluminium, and energy COGS is hedged today, and at what levels relative to current spot? Quantify the COGS DKK impact of a Brent ±$20 scenario.
  3. What is the cumulative GBP synergy realisation from Britvic through Q1 2026, and what is the expected run-rate through year-end? Is the GBP 110m target still the ceiling or could it move higher?

2. Theme 1: Volume Drivers & Revenue Visibility (KQ1)

Q1 — CEEI ex-Pepsi: What is the underlying organic beer volume in CEEI in Q1 2026, stripping out the Kazakhstan and Kyrgyzstan PepsiCo distribution additions that went live in Q1?

Context. Carlsberg reported CEEI organic volume growth of +4.6% in Q1 2026. However, the PepsiCo bottling agreements for Kazakhstan and Kyrgyzstan came into effect in Q1 2026 and management itself flagged that Pepsi shipment buildups drove very strong CEEI growth in Q4 2025. This distortion almost certainly inflated the Q1 2026 CEEI headline. Without stripping it out, the +4.6% figure is not comparable to prior periods or peer benchmarks.

Good answer. Management confirms the Pepsi-related volume impact was, for example, 2-3 percentage points of the CEEI headline in Q1, with underlying beer volume running at +1-2%. They provide a clean organic beer volume series and confirm that Poland trade-down is stabilising. Confidence builds that the business is on an improving trajectory.

Bad answer. Management refuses to decompose the figure, describes Pepsi distribution as "part of the growth strategy" without quantifying, or implies organic beer volume in CEEI is broadly consistent with the headline. This signals either that beer volume momentum is weaker than presented, or that management is not prepared to discuss the granularity an informed investor needs.

Follow-up. What is the planned Pepsi expansion into additional CEEI markets in H2 2026, and how should we model the volume contribution versus organic beer volume growth going forward?

Q2 — Vietnam comp quality: How much of Asia's +3.4% Q1 2026 organic volume is attributable to Vietnam recovery on an easy Q1 2025 base versus genuine sustained demand recovery?

Context. Q1 2025 was weak across Southeast Asia. The Phu Bai capacity expansion to 6.0m hl was completed in 2025. Management flagged strong Huda mainstream volume and 1664 Blanc premium growth in Vietnam in the Q1 2026 release. Vietnam's beer market CAGR is projected at approximately 5% through 2032, but near-term the regulatory environment (drink-drive crackdowns) and cost of living pressures are real headwinds. The question is whether Q1 2026 Vietnam growth is base-effect or genuine demand acceleration.

Good answer. Management quantifies the comp benefit — for example, Vietnam Q1 2025 was down double-digit due to Decree 100 enforcement. Q1 2026 is partly comp recovery but two-year stack growth is positive, and on-trade reactivation rates confirm that the recovery has legs through Q2 and into the summer season.

Bad answer. Management conflates comp recovery with structural growth, provides no two-year stack data, or deflects to long-term market projections without addressing whether H2 2026 Vietnam demand is visible.

Follow-up. What is the current two-year stacked organic volume trend for Vietnam, and does Carlsberg expect Vietnam to return to pre-Decree 100 volumes by end of 2026?

Q3 — China actual volumes: In absolute hectoliter terms, what were Carlsberg China beer volumes in Q1 2026, and is market share gain being achieved through premiumisation or price/promotion?

Context. Management guided that the Chinese beer market would "decline again in 2025" and recover "slightly but remain negative." Any reported YoY growth in China in Q1 2026 is therefore either share gain in a declining market or base-effect from a weak Q1 2025. Premium growth in a volume-declining market can still be margin-dilutive if promotional intensity rises.

Good answer. Management confirms market share gained 30-50bp, supported by Wind Flower Snow Moon volume growth of mid-to-high single digits. Premium revenue per hl is rising. Promotional intensity relative to local brewers is stable or falling.

Bad answer. Management emphasises value share rather than volume share, or indicates that promotional competition from Tsingtao and CR Snow is intensifying.

Follow-up. What is the current state of retailer and on-trade inventory in China — is the destocking completed in H2 2024 sustaining, or is any re-stocking risk visible?

Q4 — H2 2026 volume visibility: What proportion of H2 2026 volume is effectively committed through forward contracts, key account agreements, or confirmed distribution listings, and is there any booking signal from on-trade accounts for the summer season?

Context. The Q1 2026 organic volume headline of +2.8% was in line with analyst estimates. Full-year guidance of 2-6% was maintained, not upgraded. The wide range suggests management has limited forward visibility beyond Q2. In Carlsberg's key European markets, on-trade volume is driven by large account agreements that are typically negotiated annually. Management should have visibility on H2 listed volume.

Good answer. Management confirms key account agreements for H2 in the UK/Ireland are in place with volume commitments broadly tracking FY2025 plus single-digit growth. Summer season bookings in Western Europe are progressing in line with expectations.

Bad answer. Management deflects to macroeconomic uncertainty and notes guidance is maintained but not narrowed. This signals lower forward visibility and makes the upper end of the 2-6% guidance range look optimistic.

Follow-up. Has the Britvic combined-category selling effort changed Carlsberg's share of key account wallet in the UK — are you winning incremental listings that you did not hold pre-acquisition?

Q5 — India pre-IPO volume dynamics: How much of Carlsberg India's current volume growth is driven by organic consumer demand versus distribution point expansion ahead of the IPO, and what is the realistic sustainable volume growth rate post-listing?

Context. Bloomberg reported in February 2026 that Carlsberg appointed three banks and began a ~$700m IPO process. Pre-IPO periods often see management teams accelerating distribution expansion and promotional activity to present the strongest possible growth trajectory. The sustainable post-IPO growth rate may be lower.

Good answer. Management confirms India organic volume growth is driven primarily by per-capita consumption increases and outlet productivity gains (volume per outlet rising). They provide per-outlet productivity data.

Bad answer. Management focuses on distribution point expansion (numeric outlet additions) without providing per-outlet productivity data.

Follow-up. Post-IPO, once Carlsberg retains approximately 75% of the India entity, can growth capex in India be partially funded by the listed subsidiary directly?

3. Theme 2: Margin Trajectory & Commodity Hedge (KQ2)

Q6 — 2027 hedge position: What percentage of Carlsberg's 2027 barley, aluminium, and energy COGS is hedged today, and at what price levels relative to current spot?

Context. Carlsberg's stated hedge policy targets 90% hedged for a given year at year-start. For 2026, the position is largely set. The strategic question is 2027. If Brent is currently elevated and spot commodity prices have moved materially, the cost at which Carlsberg locked 2027 hedges matters significantly to FY2027 EBIT. The 2025 Annual Report indicated malt/barley is approximately 40% of raw material costs, and raw materials are approximately 25% of COGS. Packaging (of which aluminium is a major component) is approximately 45% of COGS.

Good answer. Management confirms 2027 is approximately 50-60% hedged at this point and provides average hedge levels for barley — for example, approximately £160-180/tonne — representing a modest premium or discount to current spot. They can quantify the residual sensitivity: for every 10% move in unhedged commodity prices, EBIT impact is approximately DKK X00m.

Bad answer. Management provides no specific percentage or price levels, refers only to the policy framework without confirming where the 2027 book stands.

Follow-up. If Brent trades at $90+ for the remainder of 2026, what is the estimated COGS impact on FY2027 from oil-correlated inputs that fall outside the formal hedge book?

Q7 — Brent sensitivity: If Brent crude moves by plus or minus $20 from current levels, what is the quantified DKK COGS impact for FY2027?

Context. The current geopolitical environment (Iran conflict, supply disruption risk) has introduced Brent volatility as a material input cost risk for 2027 in multiple channels: energy costs at breweries, packaging film (oil-derived), logistics and freight, and — indirectly — consumer disposable income in energy-import-dependent markets like Vietnam and Eastern Europe. Carlsberg does not disaggregate oil-correlated costs in public filings, but the CFO can provide a directional sensitivity.

Good answer. Management confirms that a $20 increase in Brent translates to approximately DKK 100-200m of incremental COGS pressure in FY2027 on unhedged exposure. They disaggregate the channel: energy approximately X%, packaging film approximately Y%, logistics approximately Z%.

Bad answer. Management deflects to the hedging framework without providing DKK sensitivity figures, or claims that oil-correlated costs are too diffuse to quantify.

Follow-up. In markets where energy costs are pass-through to consumers (Eastern Europe, Vietnam), how quickly does Carlsberg's pricing mechanism respond — is there a lag between commodity cost inflation and price realisation?

Q8 — Consumer energy sensitivity: In oil/energy-cost-sensitive consumer markets — specifically Vietnam, Poland, and Romania — are there early signals of premium-to-mainstream trade-down driven by energy-inflation-induced disposable income compression?

Context. Poland trade-down is publicly documented in Carlsberg's commentary. Romania and other Central European markets are also energy-import-dependent. Vietnam's consumer is exposed to fuel cost inflation through transport and food costs. If Brent sustains at elevated levels, the second-order effect is consumer trade-down from premium to mainstream beer.

Good answer. Management provides specific data: in Poland, mainstream beer volumes are tracking approximately X% of the market in Q1 2026 versus Y% in Q1 2025, with premium share stable or rising in certain channels (off-trade) but declining in on-trade. They confirm ASP per hl in CEEI beer is stable or rising despite trade-down signals.

Bad answer. Management acknowledges Poland trade-down in general terms without providing data on magnitude, trend direction, or on-trade vs off-trade split.

Follow-up. What share of Polish beer volumes in Q1 2026 were mainstream versus premium, and how does that compare to Q1 2024?

Q9 — FY2026 margin bridge: Can you quantify the margin guidance bridge for FY2026 — specifically the contribution from (a) volume leverage, (b) Britvic synergies, (c) commodity tailwinds/headwinds, and (d) pricing?

Context. FY2025 organic operating profit grew +5%, landing at the upper end of guidance. FY2026 guidance is 2-6% organic operating profit growth — a wide range that management acknowledged reflects demand uncertainty. The bridge between the FY2025 base and FY2026 outcome needs to be decomposed to understand which components are at risk.

Good answer. Management provides a quantified bridge: volume leverage approximately +1-1.5%, Britvic synergies approximately +1-1.5% at operating profit level, commodity net tailwind approximately +0.5%, pricing approximately +0.5-1%, partially offset by reinvestment. The components sum to the guided range.

Follow-up. Which single item in the FY2026 EBIT bridge is most exposed to deterioration in the current macro environment — and at what level would that item trigger a guidance revision?

Q10 — Packaging mix and oil correlation: Is aluminium can mix as a share of total packaging increasing or decreasing in key markets, and what is the directional COGS implication given current aluminium spot versus the 2025 hedge book?

Context. Carlsberg's packaging COGS exposure is approximately 45% of total COGS, with aluminium cans a significant component. Aluminium is partially oil-correlated through the smelting energy cost. The net position for FY2026 is largely hedged, but FY2027 exposure is live.

Good answer. Management confirms can mix as a percentage of total packaging volume in key markets, confirms the current FY2026 aluminium hedge position and price, and provides directional commentary on whether can mix is rising or stable.

Follow-up. In the UK specifically, what is the post-Britvic can-to-glass-to-PET split across beer and soft drinks, and how does that compare to the pre-acquisition Carlsberg UK packaging mix?
