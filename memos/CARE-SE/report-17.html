KEY FINDINGS - Exposed — structurally: Careium is a SEK-reporting Nordic-welfare-tech operator with material non-SEK revenue across Norway , Denmark , Germany/Central Europe and the UK ; combined non-SEK revenue likely exceeds 55-60% of group, making FX translation a first-order P&L driver, not a footnote

  • Natural hedge partially present but incomplete: Nordic cost bases broadly match Nordic revenue geographies, but the Germany/Central Europe segment and the UK segment carry meaningful mismatches — overhead and centralised platform costs are SEK-denominated against local-currency revenue
  • Translation dominates; transaction exposure is secondary: Careium's contracts are typically multi-year public-sector welfare agreements invoiced in local currency; cross-currency cash-flow mismatches (true transaction exposure) are limited, but the SEK translation effect on reported EBITDA and EPS is substantial and recurring
  • Hedging policy is minimalist by disclosed evidence: Public disclosures do not evidence a structured financial-hedging programme with stated ratios, horizon, or instrument mix; the company appears to rely primarily on natural hedging and accept residual translation risk as unhedged — a common posture for Nordic-welfare-sector operators but materially below best practice for a company with 55%+ non-SEK revenue
  • SEK weakness has been a tailwind L3Y (2021-2023): A depreciating SEK inflated reported group revenue and EBITDA in translated terms; this tailwind is now reversing or turning neutral as SEK has stabilised/partially recovered in 2024-2025, representing a base-effect headwind going forward
  • Quantified sensitivity (headline): A 10% uniform appreciation of SEK vs all non-SEK currencies would reduce group revenue by approximately SEK 170-230m (est. 5-8% of group revenue) and EBITDA by SEK 25-45m (est. 4-9% of group EBITDA), with minimal hedge offset given the thin hedging programme — these are material numbers relative to an already thin EBITDA margin profile
  • Thesis risk: FX-flattered historicals + FX-headwind forward: Investors modelling 2022-2023 organic growth from reported revenue are at risk of attributing FX tailwind to operational momentum; conversely, a 2024-2026 SEK strengthening scenario strips 3-6% from reported revenue growth, making the EPS trajectory thesis more fragile than the headline reported numbers suggest
  • Transparency deficit: Careium's FX disclosure is below the standard of comparably-sized Nordic healthcare/welfare operators; no management-provided sensitivity table, no disclosed hedge ratio, no published cross-currency revenue split at sub-segment granularity — forcing analysts to triangulate from geographic segment revenue lines

§1 — Revenue by Currency

J-front verdict: Careium is a high-translation-exposure SEK reporter with 55-60% of revenue in non-SEK Nordic and European currencies; revenue-currency concentration is NOK/DKK-heavy, with growing EUR and residual GBP exposure — not diversified, diversely concentrated

Revenue geographic structure

  • Fragmented multi-Nordic: Careium operates across Sweden, Norway, Denmark, Germany and historically the UK; each geography bills in local currency — SEK, NOK, DKK, EUR, GBP respectively
  • Sweden : Largest single market by revenue historically; welfare-tech alarms, digital-care platform, Trygghetslarm contracts with Swedish municipalities; estimated 38-45% of FY2023 group revenue in SEK
  • Norway : Second-largest geography; Careium holds a strong position in Norwegian welfare-tech (Safemate brand and municipal alarm contracts); estimated 20-25% of group revenue in NOK
  • Denmark : Third geography; welfare alarm and digital-care services to Danish municipalities; estimated 10-15% of group revenue in DKK; DKK effectively pegged to EUR within ±2.25% band so DKK exposure behaves economically as near-EUR exposure
  • Germany / Central Europe : Growing segment; Careium has been expanding digital-care / IoT alarm solutions into German-speaking markets; estimated 10-15% of group revenue in EUR; this segment carries a higher operating cost in EUR relative to SEK overhead allocation
  • UK : Residual / declining post-restructuring; Careium reduced UK exposure following operational review; estimated sub-5% of group revenue; GBP exposure is the tail currency in the mix

§2 — Cost by Currency

J-front verdict: Cost base is partially naturally hedged — Swedish-domiciled overhead and platform R&D are SEK-denominated while field operations broadly match local-currency revenue — but centralised platform investment and M&A integration costs are SEK-fixed, creating a structural EUR/NOK/DKK margin sensitivity

COGS currency mix

  • COGS currency mix: Field-service delivery costs (alarm response, installation technicians, monitoring centre staff) are predominantly local-currency; a Norwegian field technician is paid in NOK, a Danish monitoring-centre operator in DKK — this is the natural-hedge layer and it functions reasonably for the field-delivery cost component
  • COGS natural-hedge ratio (field costs): Estimated 70-80% of country-level field COGS is in matching local currency; remainder is either EUR-denominated vendor/hardware supply (alarm hardware sourced from pan-European suppliers) or SEK-denominated allocation of group warehousing/logistics

Opex currency mix

  • Opex (SG&A) currency mix: Central group functions — executive management, group IT, platform engineering, finance, legal — are Stockholm-headquartered and predominantly SEK-denominated; these fixed SEK costs are allocated across all geographies as shared-service charges
  • Platform/R&D SEK concentration: Careium's digital-care platform (IoT, app, monitoring software) is developed in Sweden; R&D/capex on platform is SEK-denominated; when EUR or NOK revenue grows but platform costs remain SEK-denominated, a SEK appreciation compresses the effective contribution margin of non-SEK segments
  • Estimated opex natural-hedge ratio: Lower than COGS — approximately 50-60% of group opex in matching local currency; 40-50% in SEK (centralised overhead)

Capex currency mix

  • Capex currency mix: Hardware procurement (alarm devices, IoT sensors, installation equipment) is priced in EUR by European OEM suppliers regardless of deployment geography; this creates a systematic EUR input cost regardless of revenue currency — Norwegian NOK revenue pays EUR-priced hardware, creating a NOK/EUR mismatch at the capex level
  • Hardware EUR mismatch: Estimated EUR-denominated hardware procurement at 15-20% of total capex; most significant transaction-exposure point in the cost base

Natural hedge assessment

  • Net natural hedge quality — MIXED: Revenue is ~40% SEK; blended cost is ~42% SEK — broadly matched at the group level, which is superficially comforting; however, the EUR cost concentration (~22% of costs vs ~15% of revenue) is the key mismatch: EUR-denominated hardware procurement exceeds EUR-denominated revenue, meaning EUR appreciation creates a net cost headwind not offset by EUR revenue

Sceptical bullet

§3 — Translation Exposure

J-front verdict: Translation exposure is the primary FX mechanism for Careium — with 60% non-SEK revenue and limited financial hedging, the SEK/NOK, SEK/DKK, and SEK/EUR exchange rates are direct multipliers on reported group revenue, EBITDA, and EPS; L3Y the translation effect has been net positive but is turning adverse

Historical translation impact

  • FY2021 translation impact: SEK was relatively stable vs NOK and DKK; EUR/SEK range approximately 10.0-10.4; translation contribution to reported revenue approximately neutral to marginally positive (est. +1-2% on group revenue)
  • FY2022 translation impact: SEK depreciated sharply — EUR/SEK peaked ~10.9 in Q4 2022; NOK/SEK also unfavourable for SEK reporters; estimated translation tailwind +3-4% on reported group revenue vs a constant-FX base; EBITDA translation tailwind estimated +SEK 15-25m
  • FY2023 translation impact: SEK continued to be weak for much of 2023 (EUR/SEK 11.0-11.8 range mid-year); translation tailwind estimated +2-4% on reported group revenue; EBITDA tailwind estimated +SEK 10-20m; note SEK recovered in Q4 2023 as Riksbank tightened — creating an asymmetric tailwind H1 / partial reversal H2
  • L3Y average translation tailwind: Estimated +2-3% per year on reported revenue; +SEK 12-22m per year on reported EBITDA — material relative to group EBITDA which is estimated in the SEK 150-300m range

Translation mechanics

  • Reporting currency strength: SEK is the consolidation currency; when SEK weakens vs NOK/DKK/EUR/GBP, every unit of non-SEK P&L translates to more SEK — flattering reported group numbers
  • The translation reversal risk: 2024-2025 Riksbank rate trajectory (aggressive 2023 tightening, then pivoting to cuts in 2024) plus EUR/SEK moving back toward 11.0-11.3 by mid-2024 means the extreme 2022-2023 SEK weakness tailwind is rolling off; for FY2024 and FY2025, translation contribution is estimated close to neutral or marginally negative (-1-2% on revenue vs 2023 base)
  • Magnitude in stressed year: A 15% SEK appreciation (e.g. Riksbank divergence from ECB in rate-cutting pace) would create a translation headwind of approximately -9% on reported revenue (0.6 non-SEK × 15% = 9%) and -9% on reported EBITDA, assuming costs move symmetrically — a shock large enough to wipe multiple years of underlying growth in a single year

Sceptical bullet

  • Bearish read on translation stability: The apparent translation "stability" of L3Y (gradual drift rather than binary shock) is a feature of the 2021-2023 SEK depreciation being gradual; in a discontinuous SEK-appreciation scenario (e.g. flight-to-quality into SEK if Nordic geopolitical risk premium collapses, or surprise Riksbank tightening divergence), the full 60% non-SEK revenue exposure is simultaneously mark-to-market in a single reporting period; Careium has no disclosed translation-hedging programme (consistent with sector norm but not a comfort), so a binary-quarter translation shock goes directly to reported P&L with zero buffer; the EPS impact in a single-quarter binary shock could be 15-20% downside vs consensus

§4 — Transaction Exposure

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is secondary but not trivial — the EUR hardware procurement mismatch is the principal economic cross-currency cash-flow risk; pricing pass-through is structurally limited by public-sector multi-year contracts

Transaction exposure points

  • Primary transaction exposure — EUR hardware vs NOK/DKK revenue: Alarm devices and IoT hardware are procured in EUR (OEM supply chain); deployed into Norway and Denmark where revenue is in NOK and DKK; creates a real economic margin compression when EUR appreciates vs NOK/DKK — this is not a translation artefact but a real cash-flow mismatch
  • EUR hardware procurement quantum: Estimated EUR 8-15m annually in EUR-denominated hardware procurement (triangulated from capex line and gross margin structure); at NOKSEK/EURDKK current rates, a 10% EUR appreciation vs NOK/DKK creates approximately SEK 8-15m of real economic cost uplift with no natural revenue offset
  • Secondary transaction exposure — SEK-denominated platform costs vs non-SEK revenue: Group platform/R&D spend is ~SEK 50-80m annually; SEK-denominated in actual cash terms; when SEK appreciates, this cash cost becomes larger in EUR/NOK/DKK equivalent — compressing the real economic contribution of non-SEK segments, not just the translation; this is a mild but persistent transaction exposure

Pricing pass-through

  • Pricing pass-through — LOW: Careium's primary customers are Nordic/European municipal welfare agencies operating under multi-year public-sector procurement contracts; contract terms are typically 2-5 years with fixed or CPI-linked price escalators; ad-hoc FX-cost pass-through outside contractual escalation mechanisms is not feasible in the public-sector welfare context
  • CPI indexation partial hedge: Some contracts carry annual CPI or index-linked price adjustments — these provide partial compensation for inflationary cost increases (including imported EUR hardware inflation) but the adjustment frequency (annual) and basis (domestic CPI, not EUR import price index) means FX-driven hardware cost increases are only partially and lag-compensated
  • Cost pass-through to hardware suppliers — LIMITED: Careium is a price-taker from European OEM hardware suppliers; no evidence of supplier contracts with FX adjustment clauses; Careium absorbs EUR hardware cost volatility in gross margin

Magnitude vs translation

  • Transaction vs translation ratio: Translation exposure is materially larger in gross terms (~60% of revenue in non-SEK creates a large translation P&L multiplier); transaction exposure is smaller in quantum (~EUR 8-15m hardware + ~SEK 50-80m platform = combined SEK 130-200m of cross-currency cost exposure) but more economically real (does not reverse with subsequent FX moves unlike translation timing effects)
  • Duration: Transaction exposure is on a rolling basis — hardware procurement cycles are 12-24 months; platform costs are ongoing; unlike translation (which is a period-end accounting mechanism), transaction exposure is locked in at the point of contract/procurement

Sceptical bullet

  • Bearish read on transaction exposure: The public-sector contract pricing structure that limits pass-through is actually a structural amplifier — when EUR hardware costs spike (EUR appreciation vs NOK/DKK), Careium must absorb the margin hit in full for the remaining contract term (potentially 2-4 years) until the next procurement cycle allows repricing; a EUR +20% shock hitting mid-contract creates a sustained multi-year gross margin compression that the static sensitivity table (which assumes annual pass-through) will systematically understate; this is the mechanism by which a seemingly modest transaction exposure can punch above its headline weight in real EPS terms

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Hedging discipline is minimal by disclosed evidence — Careium's annual report disclosures confirm awareness of FX risk but evidence no structured financial-hedging programme with stated ratio, horizon, or named instruments; the company relies on natural hedging and accepts residual FX risk as unhedged

Disclosed hedging mandate

  • Disclosed mandate: Careium's annual report risk section acknowledges currency risk as a key financial risk; the disclosure is generic in form ("the Group is exposed to foreign currency risk through its operations in multiple currencies and uses natural hedging where possible") — no stated hedge ratio, no named instruments, no disclosed hedge horizon
  • Board-level mandate: No evidence in public filings of a formal board-approved hedging mandate with quantitative parameters; the Audit Committee risk section references FX as a monitored risk without operational hedging detail — below the disclosure standard of comparably complex European healthcare/welfare operators

Translation hedging

  • Translation hedging — ABSENT: Consistent with the European welfare-tech sector norm, Careium does not appear to hedge translation exposure; this is a defensible posture (translation hedging is expensive, imperfect in impact, and typically uneconomic for a long-duration operator) but means all translation risk flows directly to reported P&L

Transaction hedging

  • Transaction hedging — MINIMAL/UNCONFIRMED: No disclosure of forward contracts, FX options, FX collars, or natural-payment netting in any recent annual report reviewed; Careium does not report gains/losses on derivative instruments in its financial-instrument notes at a level that would indicate active financial hedging — the IFRS 9 derivative disclosures are either minimal or absent in the sections reviewed

Natural hedging quality

  • Natural hedging — PARTIAL: As assessed in §2, Careium achieves approximately 70-80% natural hedge in field COGS by geography; the structural EUR hardware procurement mismatch and SEK platform/R&D cost concentration are the natural-hedge breaks; management characterises the posture as "natural hedging where possible," which is accurate but masks the structural mismatch points

Hedge effectiveness L3Y

  • Hedge effectiveness — NOT MEASURABLE from public disclosures: Because Careium does not appear to run a structured financial-hedging programme, the conventional hedge-effectiveness ratio (hedged gain/loss vs underlying FX movement) is not calculable; the "effectiveness" of natural hedging can be assessed qualitatively — in FY2022-2023, the natural hedge partially offset but did not eliminate SEK-depreciation tailwind because cost bases were not 100% in non-SEK currencies; the asymmetric natural-hedge benefit (costs partly in SEK = costs rise less in SEK terms when SEK weakens = broader margins in translated terms) was real in the tailwind period and will be symmetrically adverse in a SEK-strengthening period

Hedge cost

  • Hedge cost — NOT INCURRED: Zero financial hedging = zero hedge carry cost; Careium saves on hedge programme costs but this is not the correct comparison — the relevant figure is the residual unhedged exposure value, which is large relative to EBITDA

Sector hedging principle assessment

  • Duration dimension: Careium has no financial-hedge duration — effectively zero; below sector norm for operators of comparable non-home-currency revenue exposure
  • Counter-cyclical vs pro-cyclical: Not applicable — no active financial hedging programme
  • Instrument mix: No instruments disclosed — lowest discipline tier on instrument-mix spectrum
  • Disclosed policy vs realised: Policy is minimally disclosed; realised position confirms minimal hedging activity (no derivative P&L to reconcile)
  • "Boring hedger" transparency spectrum: Careium is at the low-transparency end — language is vague, no quantitative disclosure, no quarterly hedge-position summary; this does not necessarily mean reckless management (natural-hedge reliance is legitimate for this sector) but it does mean investors cannot verify the residual risk quantum

Sceptical bullet

  • Bearish read on hedging discipline: The reliance on "natural hedging" as the primary risk control is credible in steady-state but creates a false confidence; natural hedging breaks in stress scenarios — if Careium's NOK-revenue contracts come up for renewal in a NOK-weakness environment, or if EUR hardware costs spike mid-contract, the natural hedge provides no protection; furthermore, the absence of a structured financial-hedging programme means management cannot provide investors with quantified residual exposure or hedge-adjusted sensitivity — creating an information gap that the market cannot properly price; in a risk-off Nordic scenario where SEK, NOK, and DKK all move simultaneously, Careium's lack of any financial-hedge buffer means the full unhedged exposure hits reported P&L in a single quarter with no smoothing

Hedge break point analysis

  • Hedge break point — NOT APPLICABLE in conventional sense: Because Careium runs no disclosed financial-hedging programme, there is no structured hedge that can "break" at a threshold FX move; the company is perpetually exposed to the full unhedged position; the relevant question is not "at what move does the hedge break" but "at what move does business viability degrade" — and that threshold is materially lower than for a hedged peer because there is no programme to exhaust first
  • Natural-hedge break point: The partial natural hedge (local-currency field costs) becomes less effective in a sustained SEK-appreciation scenario if Careium must raise SEK-equivalent overhead allocations to non-SEK segments to maintain group-level cost recovery; this creates a second-order margin squeeze that is not visible in simple sensitivity analysis

Second-order pricing pass-through assessment

  • Pass-through capacity — LOW and lagged: Municipal welfare contracts in Sweden, Norway, Denmark, and Germany are typically tendered on 3-5 year cycles with CPI-based annual escalation; FX-driven hardware cost increases are only partially absorbed by annual CPI riders (domestic CPI ≠ EUR import price; typical lag 12-18 months); full repricing requires new contract cycle — 2-5 year lag
  • Segment-specific ceiling: UK segment — small and declining; pass-through to UK NHS / local authority customers is similarly constrained by public-sector procurement rules; Germany — hardware cost is in EUR and revenue in EUR; net self-hedged on hardware; Sweden — SEK costs vs SEK revenue, pass-through less relevant

Empirical validation vs management disclosure

  • Empirical check — management disclosure absent: Careium does not publish a management FX sensitivity table, so there is no official figure to compare against the RESEARCHER estimate; the L3Y comparison is therefore: RESEARCHER estimate of FX contribution (+2-4% on revenue per year in 2022-2023) vs implied FX impact from constant-currency/reported-revenue delta in segment tables — broadly consistent in direction and order of magnitude; the risk is that the RESEARCHER estimate understates structural cost-side EUR exposure because internal cost allocations are not fully disclosed

Tail-currency exposures

  • DKK — tail risk if EUR/DKK peg breaks: DKK is pegged to EUR within ERM II ±2.25%; if ERM II stress (hypothetically if Denmark faced speculative attack), DKK could gap vs EUR and compound NOK/DKK simultaneous adverse move vs SEK; low-probability but non-zero given geopolitical tail scenarios; DKK exposure is 13% of revenue — not trivial if peg breaks
