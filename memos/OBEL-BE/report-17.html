2. KEY FINDINGS - EUR-domestic: Orange Belgium is structurally a near-pure EUR business. Roughly 95%+ of revenue is Belgian-origin, billed and collected in EUR; the residual arises from international roaming settlements and MVNO wholesale arrangements which carry a marginal USD component. FX is not a structural revenue-mix story here

  • Cost base is also predominantly EUR, creating natural hedge at the operational level. Network capex is substantially EUR-denominated (European vendor contracts with Ericsson/Nokia in EUR); labour is Belgian-EUR; spectrum fees are EUR-denominated regulatory charges. The principal non-EUR cost exposure is USD-linked international transit/interconnect and, most materially, handset procurement (largely USD-priced globally)

  • Transaction exposure concentrated in handset procurement USD — the single most important FX exposure line. Orange Belgium sources handsets via Orange Group's central procurement, which partially neutralises individual-OpCo USD risk through group-level contracts, but the precise pass-through/hedge allocation to OBEL specifically is opaque in standalone disclosures

  • Translation exposure is structurally low and shrinking. OBEL reports in EUR, earns predominantly in EUR; there is no material subsidiary P&L in another currency. Translation is not a meaningful earnings-volatility driver for this stock

  • Hedging policy is thin in public disclosure. Annual report FX disclosures are brief; management does not publish a hedging ratio, named hedge horizon, or effectiveness ratio. The stock sits at the lower end of FX-disclosure transparency for a listed European telecom — a structural opacity flag

  • Orange Group parent partially offsets exposure via group procurement and treasury centralisation — but the exact benefit to OBEL standalone is not carved out in OBEL's own filings, creating model uncertainty rather than economic risk

  • Sensitivity is small in absolute magnitude but meaningful relative to thin EBITDA-to-EPS conversion. A 10% USD appreciation against EUR adds an estimated €3-6m to handset/device COGS (0.2-0.5% of revenue; 1-2% of EBITDA) on a standalone basis after group procurement offsets; EPS impact ~1-3% for a sustained ±10% USD move

  • Macro FX outlook (N12-24M): EUR/USD near-range-bound consensus — modest EUR weakness vs USD is the consensus direction (EUR 1.05-1.10 vs USD range 2025-2026), representing a marginal cost headwind for OBEL's handset procurement; immaterial to thesis

  • Net thesis verdict: FX is NOT a load-bearing risk for the Orange Belgium thesis. EPS trajectory is overwhelmingly driven by domestic Belgian telecom fundamentals (ARPU, subscriber volume, wholesale cable access pricing, cost-efficiency programme). The primary FX-model discipline required is to not over-inflate or over-penalise handset-COGS for USD moves — the group procurement mechanism absorbs most of the raw exposure

⚡ Single outlier flag: Group procurement opacity means OBEL's USD handset exposure is probably hedged but OBEL's own filings provide no confirmation — the disclosure gap is the risk, not the underlying economics

Reporting currency: EUR (Belgian entity, Euronext Brussels listing, IFRS-reporting)

Revenue geography — what the filings say:
- Domestic Belgian: ~95-97% of total revenue — mobile service revenue, fixed broadband/TV, B2B ICT services, all billed in EUR to Belgian customers

§2 — Cost by Currency

J-front verdict: Cost base is also EUR-dominant (~85-90%), creating structural natural hedge; the primary mismatch is USD-priced handset/device procurement (~8-12% of total costs) — the single meaningful FX transaction exposure line

COGS currency mix:
- Network operations / interconnect (EUR ~90%): Belgian-operated network; Ericsson and Nokia vendor contracts in EUR (European framework); domestic energy in EUR; Belgian spectrum fees in EUR
- Handset/device procurement (USD-priced, ~8-12% of total COGS): handsets sourced through Orange Group central procurement, globally USD-priced at the wholesale level; OBEL participates in group master vendor agreements — the currency of settlement between OBEL and Orange Group purchasing entity may be EUR (intra-group re-invoicing in EUR), but the economic exposure at group level is USD; OBEL benefits from group scale negotiation but does not separately disclose its USD-component handset cost
- Content/roaming settlement costs (USD ~1-2%): international carrier costs, IOT/roaming clearing, some content rights in USD

Opex currency mix:
- Labour: 100% EUR — Belgian employees, EUR-denominated salaries, Belgian social security; largest single opex line
- IT systems/software (EUR ~80%, USD ~20%): core OSS/BSS platforms, cloud infrastructure (AWS, Azure, GCP — USD-priced); Orange Group IT procurement partially re-invoiced in EUR but underlying USD exposure at group level
- Marketing/distribution: 100% EUR
- Professional services: ~95% EUR (Belgian/European advisors)

Capex currency mix:
- Network capex (EUR ~85%): radio access and core network from European vendors (Ericsson, Nokia — EUR-contract framework for European telcos); civil works 100% EUR
- IT capex (EUR ~70%, USD ~30%): servers, cloud migration capex partially USD-priced; data centre equipment
- Spectrum: EUR — BIPT-regulated Belgian auctions, EUR

Natural hedge ratio assessment:
- Revenue EUR 95-97% vs cost EUR 85-90%: natural hedge is imperfect but reasonably close
- Net USD mismatch: revenue USD ~1-2% vs cost USD ~8-12% — OBEL is structurally SHORT USD (more USD costs than revenues); USD appreciation = margin headwind
- Net USD exposure (unhedged, pre-group procurement offset): estimated 6-10% of total cost base, or ~€40-70m annually at FY2024 scale (based on ~€600-650m total annual costs)

§3 — Translation Exposure

J-front verdict: Translation exposure is negligible — OBEL reports in EUR, earns >95% in EUR, holds no material foreign-currency subsidiaries; translation is not a P&L volatility driver of any note

Reported FX impact L3Y:
- FY2024: FX impact on revenue: estimated <€1m (<0.1% of revenue ~€1.4bn); FX impact on EBITDA: <€1m; EPS impact: de minimis
- FY2023: Comparable magnitude — roaming settlement currency fluctuations represent the entirety of translation noise
- FY2022: Comparable; slight USD-settlement effect from post-pandemic roaming recovery
- L3Y average translation impact: effectively zero to slightly positive/negative depending on EUR/USD direction; not a meaningful earnings line

Why translation is structurally absent:
- Single-country operator with EUR reporting: no foreign subsidiary P&L to translate; no material foreign-currency balance sheet items outside normal working capital
- No USD-denominated revenue stream of scale: the 1-2% USD revenue is immaterial even under a 20% EUR/USD move (~€2.8-5.6m revenue swing on €1.4bn base)
- No GBP, CHF, SEK, or EM currency exposure: pure Belgian geography eliminates cross-European translation risk that peers (e.g. Proximus's historical international operations, or Telenet's Libertad acquisitions) face

Translation in stress scenarios:
- 20% EUR depreciation vs USD: revenue translation gain ~€4-6m (USD roaming revenues worth more in EUR); cost translation loss ~€12-20m (USD handset/cloud costs worth more in EUR); net EBITDA impact: ~-€8 to -€14m (net headwind as USD-cost exposure > USD-revenue)
- This is transaction exposure dressed as translation in the handset-cost line; the mechanism matters (see §4)

Translation magnitude — average vs stressed year:
- Average year: ±€1-3m EBITDA from translation — below modelling materiality threshold
- Stressed year (±20% EUR/USD): ±€10-15m EBITDA net (mostly transaction, see §4)
- Translation-specific component: <€2m even in stress

Sceptical bullet — translation stability:
- Bearish read: apparent translation stability could mask a binary event — if Orange Group decides to restructure OBEL (potential merger with Telenet/Liberty Global; M&A with foreign-currency premium/discount effects; group treasury centralisation unwind), the FX dimension of the transaction could create one-time translation/retranslation impacts that dwarf the operating-year run-rate; management's "no translation exposure" narrative would be correct for steady-state but misleading ahead of corporate action

§4 — Transaction Exposure

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is real but modest — the primary vector is USD handset/device procurement cost; estimated €40-70m annual USD-cost exposure (pre-group-offset); materially manageable; pricing pass-through partial but present

Transaction-exposure points:
1. Handset/device procurement (PRIMARY): USD-priced globally; OBEL procures through Orange Group central purchasing; the economic USD exposure is real even if OBEL's internal invoicing is EUR. Group treasury likely hedges or absorbs the USD/EUR spread, but OBEL standalone does not disclose its hedge position
2. International transit/interconnect costs (SECONDARY): carrier costs for international voice/data traffic, partially USD-denominated; estimated €5-15m annual USD exposure (declining as voice traffic migrates to OTT)
3. Cloud infrastructure/software licences (TERTIARY): AWS, Azure, GCP infrastructure costs; USD-priced; estimated €10-20m USD exposure growing at ~15%pa as cloud migration accelerates
4. Content rights (MINIMAL): some streaming content in B2C bundles may include USD-priced wholesale content; small and partially pass-through

Magnitude vs translation exposure:
- Transaction exposure dominates translation ~10:1 in this business — transaction is where the real FX risk sits
- Total gross transaction USD exposure: ~€55-105m (sum of handset, transit, cloud, content) — wide range due to group procurement opacity
- Net of group procurement offsets: probably €20-50m effective exposure per year

Pricing pass-through:
- Handsets/devices: predominantly customer-priced (SIM-only + device financing); OBEL effectively passes handset cost to customer at time of sale (device instalments); USD handset price inflation partially passed through with 1-3 quarter lag; but competition in Belgian market limits ability to fully recover cost inflation without volume loss
- Cloud/IT: internal cost, no direct pass-through; flows through opex; partially recovered over time via B2B pricing
- Transit/interconnect: regulated/cost-based pricing floors in EU interconnect; limited pricing optionality

Cost pass-through assessment:
- Pass-through rate: ~50-70% for handsets (competitive market; Orange Belgium has ~25-26% mobile market share with price-competitive positioning — cannot fully pass through cost inflation vs Proximus and Telenet/BASE)
- Pass-through rate: ~0-20% for IT/cloud (internal cost; feeds EBITDA margin drag if unhedged)
- Economic margin impact of 10% USD appreciation: ~€5-10m EBITDA drag net of pass-through, pre-group-hedge offset

Sceptical bullet — transaction exposure:
- Bearish read: the assumption that Orange Group's central procurement effectively neutralises OBEL's USD handset exposure is load-bearing but unverifiable from OBEL's standalone filings. If Orange S.A. (parent, 52.9% shareholder) changes its intra-group transfer pricing methodology, renegotiates cost-allocation of group treasury services, or faces its own FX pressures that cause it to re-invoice more USD volatility downstream to OpCos, OBEL's effective USD transaction exposure could double or triple with no visible warning in OBEL's disclosed sensitivity figures. The group-buffer is an undisclosed mitigant, making it also an undisclosed fragility point

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Hedging policy disclosure is thin to absent in OBEL's standalone filings; the operative assumption is that hedging occurs at Orange Group treasury level; OBEL's own disclosed policy is insufficient to assess discipline with confidence

Hedging mandate (from OBEL annual report/20-F FX section):
- OBEL's annual reports include a boilerplate FX risk section in the notes to financial statements (IFRS 7 financial instruments risk management)
- Disclosure level: typically states "Orange Belgium is primarily exposed to EUR and does not consider foreign currency risk material to its operations" or equivalent — this is consistent with the factual EUR-dominance but understates transaction exposure
- No disclosed hedge ratio, hedge horizon, named instruments, or effectiveness statistics in OBEL's own filings — lowest tier of disclosure transparency for a listed European telecom

Group-level hedging (Orange S.A.):
- Orange Group treasury manages FX centrally; OpCos (including OBEL) participate in group-level hedging programmes
- Orange S.A. discloses group-level hedging in its own annual report (20-F as foreign private issuer): instruments include forward contracts and cross-currency swaps for material EUR/USD exposures at group level
- Group hedge horizon: typically 12-18M rolling for transaction exposures at group level
- OBEL's allocated share of group hedge benefit: not disclosed — opaque intra-group allocation

Translation hedging:
- Not applicable / not done: as OBEL reports in EUR with EUR-dominant operations, translation hedging would be a non-event; consistent with OBEL not disclosing any translation hedging instruments

Transaction hedging:
- Group-level: presumed present for handset procurement USD; Orange Group is a major global handset buyer (~€3-4bn device procurement annually); group-level hedging is economically rational and almost certainly occurs
- OBEL standalone: no disclosed financial hedging instruments in derivative note of standalone accounts
- Natural hedging reliance: this is the operative mechanism — management appears to rely on EUR dominance of the revenue/cost base as the primary risk management tool, supplemented by group treasury

Hedge effectiveness L3Y:
- Cannot assess effectiveness: no disclosed effectiveness ratios, no realised gain/loss on hedging instruments in OBEL's standalone accounts (consistent with no standalone derivative programme)
- Proxy: OBEL's EBITDA margin L3Y shows no material FX-driven variance — consistent with effective group-level absorption of USD shocks; but could also reflect EUR/USD stability during the period

Hedge cost / income recognition:
- OBEL standalone: nil — no derivative P&L line in standalone income statement
- Group level: Orange S.A. discloses hedging costs/gains in group consolidated accounts; not separately allocable to OBEL

Sceptical bullet — hedging discipline:
- Bearish read: the "group treasury absorbs it" narrative is functionally a black box from OBEL standalone investors' perspective. OBEL's minority shareholders (47.1% of shares are public float) are exposed to an FX risk management programme they cannot audit through OBEL's own filings. If the group's hedging programme is pro-cyclical (hedges placed reactively when USD is already moving) or under-hedged in duration (only 12M rolling vs a sustained 3-year USD strength scenario), OBEL's EBITDA would be impacted with no early-warning signal visible in OBEL's own disclosed positions. This is a governance/transparency gap as much as an FX-management gap

Sector-general FX hedging principles — applied to OBEL:

Applying the six sector-general principles from the template to OBEL:

  1. Hedge duration matters more than ratio:
    - OBEL: UNKNOWN — no standalone hedge duration disclosed; group-level assumed ~12M rolling
    - European telco sector norm: 12-18M rolling is typical for transaction hedging among listed European operators (Proximus, KPN, Telenet — European mid-cap telcos)
    - OBEL assessment: probably at or slightly below sector norm duration given reliance on group treasury rather than standalone programme

  2. Counter-cyclical vs pro-cyclical hedging:
    - OBEL: UNKNOWN — no public disclosure of hedging timing/strategy
    - Assessment: probably pro-cyclical by default (group treasury reactive) — no evidence of counter-cyclical strategy

  3. Instrument mix:
    - OBEL standalone: nil disclosed — no derivative instruments in OBEL's own financial statements
    - Group level (Orange S.A.): forwards + cross-currency swaps — standard, adequate instrument mix
    - OBEL standalone: below-average instrument transparency by sector standard

  4. Disclosed policy ≠ realised hedging:
    - OBEL: cannot cross-check — no disclosed effectiveness ratios, no standalone derivative P&L; realised gain/loss on hedges is not visible in OBEL's standalone accounts; FAILS this principle on transparency

  5. Natural hedging > financial hedging:
    - OBEL: strong natural hedge — EUR revenue vs EUR cost base >85%; this is structurally advantageous and represents the dominant risk-reduction mechanism

  6. The "boring hedger" transparency spectrum:
    - OBEL: low transparency — does not publish hedge positions, hedge horizon, effectiveness ratios, or sensitivity tables
    - Better-practice European telcos (e.g. KPN in Netherlands, Swisscom in Switzerland, Proximus in Belgium): all publish at minimum a management sensitivity table and note their hedge horizon and instruments
    - OBEL is toward the lower end of the transparency spectrum for a mid-cap European listed telecom
