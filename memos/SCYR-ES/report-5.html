[C] SCYR-ES | Triaging #5 | Earnings Delivery — LTM | 04-May-26

KEY FINDINGS

  • Q-turn is real and accelerating. The transformation from contractor to concession operator is producing measurable, compounding financial results across every key metric: OCF conversion exceeded 100% of EBITDA for the first time in FY2025, net profit ex-divestments rose 46%, recourse net debt fell to €59m, and Q1 2026 net profit confirmed +40% YoY continuation. This is not a future promise — it is a current delivery record.

  • IFRIC12 headline EBITDA is a false friend. The FY2024 EBITDA decline of -10% to €1,352m was an accounting artefact of the concession lifecycle model: EBITDA peaks during construction and declines during operations, while OCF does the opposite. Analysts who read the headline EBITDA without adjusting for this will systematically undervalue Sacyr at exactly the inflection point where the cash is at its peak. This creates a persistent perception gap — and a structural mispricing opportunity.

  • Colombia divestiture validates asset quality at 2.7x MOIC. The $1.565bn sale of three Colombian 4G toll-road concessions to Actis closed at 12% above Investor Day NAV estimates, confirming that Sacyr's concession book is worth materially more than book value. The proceeds both eliminate recourse debt and fund re-investment into the 2033 roadmap. Recycling capital at 2.7x MOIC in a single transaction is uncommon in European infrastructure.

  • Voreantis is the next value crystallisation event. The 49% minority stake sale in the Voreantis concessions vehicle — targeting €800m to potentially over €1bn depending on scope — is the single largest near-term catalyst. Delayed from 2025 to 2026, Santander and Deutsche Bank are appointed advisors. Completion in 2H 2026 would represent a further MOIC-accretive confirmation of NAV. Timeline slip is the primary execution risk.

  • E&I backlog record of €10.6bn at 71% P3-linked removes revenue visibility risk. The construction business, often the source of concern for investors in infrastructure groups, is structurally de-risked by its P3 contract composition. P3-linked work carries guaranteed payment mechanisms from government counterparties, eliminating fixed-price commodity-contract risk on the majority of the backlog. Total contracted backlog of €63.6bn (+15%) extends earnings visibility well into the next decade.

  • Management EPS guidance gap to consensus is extraordinary. Management guides €0.23 (2026) and €0.28 (2027) vs consensus c.€0.15 — a 50-87% gap. If management is directionally correct, the stock is materially mispriced. The gap reflects analyst difficulty modelling IFRIC12 dynamics, Voreantis optionality, and the Colombia recycling uplift. Q1 2026's +40% net profit print reduces the probability that management is guiding aggressively.

  • DBRS investment-grade (BBB low, Oct 2025) is a structural re-rating signal. First formal investment-grade rating from a global agency unlocks a new buyer base (insurance, pension, infrastructure funds with investment-grade mandates) and reduces the cost of corporate debt. Combined with recourse leverage at 0.18x, Sacyr's parent balance sheet is now demonstrably clean, removing a long-standing investor concern.

  • 93% of EBITDA from P3 assets by FY2025 — the business model pivot is complete. As recently as 2019, concessions were a minority of group earnings. The mix-shift is now complete, and the remaining 7% non-P3 exposure is primarily the E&I construction margin which is itself largely P3-linked. This level of recurring, government-backed cash flow does not attract a construction-sector multiple — it warrants infrastructure/utility pricing.

2. Earnings Delivery & Beat/Miss Analysis

Rating: Green

  • FY2024 revenue of €4,571m (-0.8% YoY) was slightly below prior year but the composition matters more than the aggregate. The decline was entirely driven by IFRIC12 construction roll-off: as Colombian and Uruguayan projects completed construction phases, the associated revenue recognition under the financial asset model ceased. This is a feature of the model's maturation, not a demand shortfall. Concessions and Water both grew their revenue lines in the period.

  • FY2024 EBITDA of €1,352m vs FY2023 €1,502m — the -10% headline masked divergent divisional trends. The Concessions division delivered EBITDA of €875m with an operating income of €1,340m (including concession-level financial income). E&I delivered modest construction EBITDA. The IFRIC12 effect on the consolidated EBITDA line overstated the degree of operational deterioration. Had the market adjusted for this accounting mechanics, FY2024 would have read as a broadly stable year operationally.

  • FY2024 net profit of €113m (+7.2% YoY) was the clean beat. Net profit growing in a year when EBITDA fell -10% is unusual and validates the IFRIC12 thesis: below-EBITDA items (financial income from concession receivables, debt amortisation, tax) moved favourably as assets transitioned from construction to operational phases. The net profit line is closer to the economic reality of the business than headline EBITDA in this accounting environment.

  • OCF of €1,294m (+53% YoY) in FY2024 was the year's most important data point. A 53% jump in operating cash flow when EBITDA fell 10% can only be explained by the IFRIC12 transition: concession assets in operational phase generate cash distributions that bypass the EBITDA line but flow directly into OCF. This 96% EBITDA conversion ratio was the highest in the company's history at that point, and it was the clearest confirmation that the Q-turn was underway.

  • FY2025 revenue of €4,660m (+2% YoY) was a modest recovery driven by E&I and Water acceleration. E&I revenue reached €2,971m (+8%), reflecting P3 project ramp-up. Water revenue was €307m (+25%), outperforming internal targets. Concessions revenue was a function of the Colombia partial divestiture timing, which removed three assets from the revenue base mid-year but added divestment proceeds.

  • FY2025 EBITDA of €1,358m (+0.4% YoY) was effectively flat but the mix improved significantly. E&I EBITDA rose 31% to €552m with margin stable at 4.8%. Water EBITDA grew 23% to €62m. Concessions-level EBITDA was affected by the Colombian asset exit but partially offset by new operational assets in Italy, Chile, and Paraguay. The flatness at group level conceals genuine improvement at divisional level once Colombia is excluded.

  • Net profit ex-divestments of €165m (+46% YoY) was the headline beat vs consensus. The consensus estimate was tracking approximately €130-140m for FY2025 net profit, based on trajectories from H1 2025 which showed €127m (+85% H1 vs H1). The full-year delivery at €165m was above the upper end of expectations. Including divestment gains from Colombia (the approximately €278m net proceeds being partly recognised as profit in H2 2025), the statutory net profit line was materially higher.

  • H1 2025 net profit of €127m (+85% YoY) was the mid-year inflection signal. The H1 delivery validated that the FY2024 trajectory was not a one-period phenomenon. OCF of €615m (+7% vs H1 2024) already tracked above the 2027 strategic plan run-rate on an annualised basis. By H1, the strategic plan's five-year OCF target was already being beaten on a twelve-month rolling basis.

  • 9M 2025 OCF of €890m (+11% YoY) confirmed the full-year trajectory. With three-quarter delivery 11% above the prior period and already at 65% of the FY2025 final figure, the full-year beat vs both consensus and prior year was locked. Net profit at 9M was already +81% YoY, with the Q4 divestment proceeds from Colombia adding further upside to the final print.

  • Q1 2026 net profit of €38m (+40% YoY) closes the loop on continuity. The +40% print is particularly important because it comes in a period with no divestment gains — it is entirely operational delivery. The Q1 2025 base was €27m, making the +40% calculation straightforward. EBITDA of €327m (+9%) and revenue of €1,116m (+5%) confirm that organic momentum is intact entering FY2026.

  • Construction EBITDA margin stability at 4.8-4.9% across FY2025 is better than feared. Infrastructure construction is inherently fixed-cost intensive and subject to project-specific margin volatility. The consistent margin delivery across multiple large P3 projects in diverse geographies (Italy, Chile, Colombia, Paraguay) suggests Sacyr's risk management and contract selection have improved materially from the 2015-2020 period when the business faced legacy project losses.

  • The Colombia divestiture uplift: 12% premium to book signals under-recognition in consolidated accounts. At book value, the three Colombian assets were carried at approximately €629m (per Capital Madrid reporting). The €1.565bn sale price (approximately €1.4bn at spot EUR/USD) implies a 2.2x price-to-book on the disposal, or 2.7x MOIC when accounting for the development cost basis. This premium is structurally important: it means the remaining concession book, carried at book value in the consolidated accounts, is likely worth materially more than reported.

  • The earnings delivery record across LTM is best characterised as a clean sweep. Revenue growing, EBITDA stable-to-growing on adjusted basis, net profit +46%, OCF beating EBITDA, balance sheet deleveraged to near-zero recourse leverage, and Q1 2026 confirming continuation. There are no meaningful misses in the period — the only negative headline was EBITDA -10% in FY2024, which was a false signal from the accounting model.

  • Recourse debt elimination trajectory deserves special note as a delivery milestone. Taking recourse net debt from €1,500m+ in 2019 to €59m in FY2025 without equity dilution is a compounding capital allocation story that most European infrastructure investors would struggle to replicate. The total deleveraging was approximately €1,450m over six years, funded by operating cash generation and asset recycling.

  • The Concessions division operating income of €1,340m (FY2024) vs €875m EBITDA reflects the IFRIC12 financial income component. Financial income on concession receivables (the asset recognised under the financial asset model of IFRIC12) is a P&L item above EBITDA that EBITDA analysis misses. The €465m gap between EBITDA and operating income in Concessions represents the contractual financial return embedded in the concession receivable, and it is cash-generative. This is why OCF/EBITDA conversion exceeds 100%.

3. Revenue Quality & Composition

Rating: Green

  • Revenue quality has fundamentally shifted over the last five years. In 2019, approximately 60% of group revenue came from construction activities with their associated fixed-price risk, schedule uncertainty, and margin volatility. By FY2025, 93% of EBITDA originates from P3/concession assets that carry government-backed, inflation-linked payment mechanisms. Revenue quality and earnings visibility have transformed even though the headline revenue number looks similar in absolute terms.

  • Concessions operating income grew +5% to an estimated €1,400m+ in FY2025. This is the metric to track, not EBITDA. Concession operating income includes the financial income on receivables (IFRIC12 financial asset return) that does not appear in EBITDA but is real contractual cash. The +5% growth in a year when Colombia assets were sold mid-year reflects the addition of new operational assets in Italy (Novara), Chile (desalination and reuse), and Paraguay (Asunción accesses).

  • E&I backlog of €10.6bn at year-end 2024, growing to €12.47bn by year-end 2025, provides 4+ years of construction revenue visibility. At a run-rate of approximately €2.7-3.0bn annual construction revenue, the current backlog covers 4-5 years of execution. This is at the high end of what European infrastructure groups typically carry, and it removes near-term revenue risk entirely. The 41% backlog growth in 2024 was driven by P3 awards, not commodity lump-sum work.

  • 71% P3-linked backlog composition is the key quality differentiator. P3 contracts link construction payment to concession operation — the contractor is typically also the concessionaire, meaning cost overruns on construction can be offset by operational contract terms, and the counterparty is a government or quasi-government body. The remaining 29% non-P3 backlog is predominantly infrastructure work in Spain, Italy, and Chile, not emerging-market speculative construction.

  • Total contracted backlog of €63.6bn (+15%) encompasses construction + concession operational revenues. The €63.6bn figure is the sum of all contracted future revenues: construction contracts (€12.47bn) plus the present value of concession operating revenues over remaining concession lives. This is the most comprehensive visibility indicator, and the +15% growth signals that new awards are outpacing revenue recognition, sustaining the growth trajectory through the current strategic plan period.

  • Water division revenue growth of +25% to €307m in FY2025 is a notable acceleration. Water had been a smaller, steady contributor at €245m (FY2024) and €227m (FY2023). The +25% growth in FY2025 reflects new desalination contract ramp-up in Chile (Antofagasta and Coquimbo) and water reuse project delivery in Spain. Water has the cleanest revenue quality profile: long-duration public utility contracts, municipal counterparties, inflation-linked tariffs. The margin expansion to 21.7% from 20.6% suggests operating leverage is kicking in.

  • LatAm concentration remains significant post-Colombia divestiture. Sacyr still operates three residual Colombian concessions, plus meaningful exposure to Chile (multiple roads and water assets), Peru, and Uruguay. After the $1.565bn Colombia sale, the proportion of EBITDA from LatAm declined but remains material at an estimated 25-30% of concessions EBITDA. Chile is the primary remaining concentration, with political and FX risk attached. The Chile-Italy-Spain diversification trend is structurally positive but not complete.

  • Italy has emerged as a key growth engine. The Novara Health and Science City concession awarded in 2025 follows several hospital and road awards in prior years. Italy's PPP market is one of Europe's most active, and Sacyr's establishment of a local operational track record creates a platform for further awards. The Italy exposure carries investment-grade government counterparty risk, which diversifies away from the LatAm FX risk pool.

  • Canada entry in Q1 2026 (Ontario Science Centre project) marks successful English-speaking market expansion. The 2024-2027 Strategic Plan prioritised North America and Australia as next-phase geographies. The Ontario award is the first concrete delivery of that strategy. Canada's P3 market — particularly in Ontario and British Columbia — is one of the world's most sophisticated and volume-rich, offering a pipeline that could sustain multiple awards per year for a well-capitalised concession developer of Sacyr's scale.

  • Spain and EU domestic exposure provides a stable, lower-risk revenue base. Spanish road and hospital concessions are long-duration, euro-denominated, and carry investment-grade sovereign counterparty risk. The DBRS BBB (low) rating makes Sacyr a credible bidder for the next wave of Spanish infrastructure privatisation and P3 programmes. EU structural funds continue to support infrastructure investment across Southern Europe, providing a macro tailwind.

  • Construction margin stability at 4.8-4.9% demonstrates revenue quality is not achieved at the cost of profitability. Some infrastructure groups accept low-margin construction contracts as loss-leaders to secure concession mandates. Sacyr's margin record suggests disciplined contract selection: the company is not buying revenue through margin destruction. The stable margin over multiple years and multiple geographies is a quality signal.

  • Revenue diversification across seven geographies (Spain, Italy, Chile, Colombia, Uruguay, Paraguay, now Canada/Australia) reduces single-event concentration risk. No single country or project now represents a dominant share of construction backlog. The concession portfolio is similarly dispersed. This diversification was not present five years ago when Colombia was the dominant growth driver.

  • P3 model creates self-reinforcing revenue quality. In a P3, the concessionaire earns its construction profit by delivering the project to specification, then earns its operational return from traffic, availability payments, or tariff revenues over the concession life. The interdependence aligns incentives, reduces costly disputes, and embeds the contractor/operator at the project level for 20-30 years. This is fundamentally different revenue quality from a single-contract EPC relationship.

4. Margin Delivery & Cost Dynamics

Rating: Green

  • Concessions EBITDA margin of approximately 65% is the earnings engine. The concessions division operates at a margin structure closer to a toll-road or utility operator than a contractor. Fixed-cost structures are low once construction is complete, and operating costs are primarily maintenance, management, and regulatory compliance. The €875m Concessions EBITDA on approximately €1,340m operating income implies a margin on operating income basis of approximately 65%, consistent with peer infrastructure concessionaire benchmarks.

  • Group EBITDA margin of 29.6% (FY2024) and approximately 29.1% (FY2025) is diluted by the construction business. The blended group margin reflects the mix of high-margin concessions (65%+ EBITDA margin) and lower-margin construction (4.8-4.9%). As the concessions contribution to group revenue grows, the blended margin will migrate upward. The mix-shift story is the central margin thesis: every percentage point of revenue mix-shift from construction to concessions lifts the group EBITDA margin by approximately 0.6 percentage points.

  • E&I EBITDA margin at 4.8-4.9% has been essentially flat for two years. This is exactly the right stability profile for a construction business: no margin compression suggesting cost control problems, no margin expansion suggesting unsustainable low-balling of bids. The 31% EBITDA growth in the division in FY2025 came from revenue volume (+8%), not margin expansion, which is the correct growth mode for a contractor.

  • Water EBITDA margin improvement to 21.7% (FY2025) from 20.6% (FY2024) is meaningful in a low-margin context. Water treatment operations have significant fixed costs (plant, infrastructure), so the incremental revenue from new contracts flows through to margin at a higher rate. The 110bp margin improvement on a revenue base growing +25% confirms positive operating leverage in the division. At scale, Water margins could approach the low-20s to mid-20s as Chilean desalination assets reach full operational run-rate.

  • IFRIC12 creates a unique margin dynamic that depresses reported EBITDA relative to economic earnings. Under IFRIC12, concession assets classified as financial assets recognise revenue during construction at cost-plus levels, then switch to financial income (interest on receivable) during operations. The transition reduces the revenue and EBITDA line in the P&L but generates superior economic cash flows. Analysts applying standard EV/EBITDA multiples to Sacyr's EBITDA will systematically undervalue the business unless they adjust for this bifurcation.

  • The OCF/EBITDA ratio above 1.0x is the margin model's ultimate validation. In normal businesses, OCF runs below EBITDA due to cash taxes, working capital movements, and maintenance capex. Sacyr's OCF exceeding EBITDA by definition means the concession portfolio is generating cash receipts above and beyond the accounting EBITDA recognition. The cash is real and growing. The accounting is conservative relative to the economics.

  • Concessions mix-shift is ongoing and structural. In FY2024, concessions contributed approximately 65% of group EBITDA. By FY2025, the 93% figure for P3 assets' share of EBITDA represents a step-change. The increase from approximately 65% to 93% partly reflects Colombia divestiture (removing mature P3 assets) and Italy operational ramp (adding new P3 EBITDA). The mix will continue to improve as Canada and Chile ramp through FY2026-2027.

  • Construction cost discipline is particularly impressive given the inflationary environment of 2022-2024. European construction costs rose substantially on labour, materials, and energy in 2022-2023. Sacyr maintained margin stability through the inflationary spike, which implies either strong contract pricing, hedging of materials costs, or formula-based escalation clauses in its P3 contracts. The absence of margin deterioration during peak inflation is a quality signal for the E&I division.

  • Concession-level operating costs are predictable and contractually managed. Road concessions face defined maintenance obligations (resurfacing schedules, safety upgrades) that are costed into the financial model at bid stage. Hospital concessions carry defined facilities management obligations. There are no uncontracted cost surprises in mature concession assets — the key cost uncertainty is in projects still under construction, and even there the P3 contractual structure limits Sacyr's downside via government payment mechanisms.

  • Group overhead is a modest drag on divisional margins. Sacyr's holding company costs, corporate finance, and group-level functions reduce the consolidated margin vs the sum-of-the-parts. As the concession portfolio grows, the overhead ratio as a percentage of total EBITDA will decline, providing additional incremental margin improvement over the strategic plan horizon without requiring any operational change at the divisional level.

  • Depreciation and amortisation dynamics differ sharply between divisions. Concession assets under the intangible asset model (IFRIC12) are amortised over concession life, producing a P&L charge but no cash outflow. Construction assets carry minimal D&A. The group D&A charge is therefore an accounting artefact of concession classification rather than a reflection of maintenance capital requirements. This further separates reported EBIT from economic earnings.

  • The thin net profit margin at approximately 2.5% of group revenue is a function of the financial structure, not operational performance. Net profit is after group interest costs (on the €59m recourse debt and the non-recourse project finance at asset level), D&A, and minorities. The concession minorities are significant as Sacyr typically co-invests with local partners and pension funds. The low reported net margin is misleading — the relevant metrics are OCF conversion, net profit growth, and asset-level equity returns.

5. Balance Sheet & Cash Flow Delivery

Rating: Green

  • Recourse net debt reduction from €146m (FY2024) to €59m (FY2025) is the culmination of a six-year deleveraging programme. From a peak of over €1,500m in 2019, the company has eliminated 96% of recourse leverage without diluting shareholders. This is exceptional capital discipline. At 0.18x net recourse debt to recourse EBITDA + distributions, the parent balance sheet carries negligible financial risk.

  • Non-recourse project finance of approximately €6.5bn is structurally ring-fenced at the concession entity level. Each concession is financed on a project-finance basis: the debt sits within the special purpose vehicle, has no recourse to the Sacyr parent, and is serviced from the concession's own revenues. Investors who compare the €6.5bn total debt figure to the parent equity are making an analytical error — the non-recourse debt is fully supported by long-duration contracted cash flows from government-backed concession revenues.

  • OCF of €1,294m (FY2024, +53% YoY) surpassing to €1,359m (FY2025, +5% YoY) is compounding at a sustainable rate. The 53% jump in FY2024 was a step-change as major Colombian and Uruguayan projects transitioned from construction to full operation. The +5% in FY2025 represents the ongoing growth from new operational assets. The trajectory — and the fact that FY2025 OCF exceeded both EBITDA and the 2027 strategic plan target two years early — establishes a high floor for OCF expectations.

  • 100%+ OCF conversion in FY2025 is structurally sustainable, not a one-off. The IFRIC12 financial asset model produces concession cash receipts that do not flow through EBITDA but do flow through OCF. As the proportion of operational-phase concession assets grows, this OCF premium above EBITDA will persist or grow. The structural argument for continued >100% conversion is strong as long as the concession portfolio keeps adding operational assets at a faster rate than construction-phase assets are added.

  • Colombia divestiture net proceeds of approximately €278m strengthened the balance sheet in H2 2025. The gross sale price of $1.565bn equates to approximately €1.4bn, but the net proceeds to Sacyr parent after non-recourse debt repayment at the project level, minority partner distributions, taxes, and transaction costs were substantially lower. The €278m estimate (based on press reports) represents real incremental equity value crystallised at 2.7x MOIC. These proceeds were used to reduce residual recourse debt, contributing to the €59m year-end figure.

  • Concession distributions from operational assets provide an annual cash flow floor independent of new project activity. The portfolio of mature concession assets (Spain, Italy, Chile, Uruguay, Paraguay) generates regular upward distributions to the Sacyr parent once project finance debt service is covered. These distributions are the sustainable operating cash engine. The Colombian assets, prior to their sale, were a major distribution contributor — their replacement by new Italian and Chilean assets ensures the distribution trajectory is maintained.

  • Voreantis is a €800m-€1,000m+ liquidity event in the balance sheet roadmap. Sacyr is seeking a 49% minority partner in Voreantis, its concessions holding vehicle, which consolidates highway, hospital, and other P3 assets across Spain, Italy, Chile, Colombia, and Uruguay. The sale of a minority stake at institutional infrastructure pricing would both crystallise NAV and provide capital for reinvestment into the 2033 roadmap. At €800m for 49%, the implied enterprise value of Voreantis would be approximately €1.6bn, providing a concrete reference point for the concession portfolio's NAV vs book.

  • Working capital dynamics are structurally favourable for a concession-dominated business. Concession operators receive payments from governments on fixed schedules (availability payments) or from users (traffic toll revenues). Unlike construction contractors, there is no large working capital float tied up in receivables from clients who are slow to certify progress payments. This structural difference explains why Sacyr's cash conversion has improved so dramatically as the mix has shifted.

  • Capex profile is dominated by equity investment in new concession projects. Sacyr's capital allocation is primarily the equity ticket for new P3 projects — the equity co-investment alongside the project finance debt. This capex is growth-oriented and discretionary, unlike the maintenance capex of a utility. The company has committed approximately €905m in equity toward the 2024-2027 target of €1bn, indicating the growth capex programme is on track to be fully deployed ahead of schedule.

  • The DBRS BBB (low) rating enables access to investment-grade bond markets for the first time. Prior to October 2025, Sacyr relied on bank financing and project finance for its debt needs, with no public bond market access on investment-grade terms. The new rating opens the European investment-grade bond market, where issuance costs are materially lower than bank lending and tenors can extend to 10-20 years. This is a structurally significant balance sheet development that will lower the future cost of capital.

  • Portfolio-level NAV of €5.1bn by 2027 (management target) vs current market cap implies significant value gap. If management's 2027 NAV target of €5.1bn is achievable — and the trajectory of OCF, divestiture pricing, and backlog growth suggest it is — the current market cap of approximately €4.0-4.5bn at €3.90-4.10 share price implies the stock is trading at or below NAV today, without pricing in any value of the construction business or the Water division. The market appears to be assigning zero or negative value to EI and Water, which is inconsistent with their profitability trajectories.

  • Q1 2026 OCF of €223m (+12% YoY) sustains the annual run-rate above €800m on a quarterly basis. Annualising Q1 2026 OCF gives a €892m run-rate, which is below the FY2025 €1,359m figure due to seasonality — OCF is typically backend-weighted as concession distributions concentrate in H2. The +12% growth is the relevant signal; the absolute quarterly figure understates the annual trajectory.
