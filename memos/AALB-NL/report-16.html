§1 — CEO Assessment

J-front verdict: Pelsma reads as a competent BUILDER-ALLOCATOR operator — portfolio-restructuring record is credible, capital-allocation discipline is visible, leadership style fits the decentralised Aalberts model; net signal is strong with manageable uncertainty

CEO background:
- Wim Pelsma was appointed CEO of Aalberts in January 2018, joining from Imtech where he led multiple restructuring programmes; earlier career spans Royal Dutch Shell (operational/supply chain roles), Sabic Europe, and Eriks Group (distributor/industrial services) — the pattern is consistently industrial-operational rather than financier or strategy-consultant derived, which is favourable for a company where operational margin extraction is the core value-creation mechanism
- Education: Delft University of Technology (mechanical engineering), consistent with Aalberts's engineering-led culture
- No prior CEO experience at a listed company before Aalberts, which is a mild negative — first listed-CEO tenure by definition has a learning curve in capital-markets communication and investor-relations discipline

CEO tenure:
- Appointed January 2018; approximately 8 years in seat as of mid-2026, placing him in the upper quartile of tenure for European industrial CEOs (sector median tenure approximately 5-6 years per governance survey data)
- Long tenure creates institutional depth — Aalberts's M&A sourcing is relationship-driven and Pelsma has cultivated the SME-owner network across Europe that is central to the acquisition funnel
- Long tenure also creates risk: entrenchment, reduced external challenge, succession gap widening

CEO track record at Aalberts — portfolio restructuring:
- The most important strategic act of the Pelsma era was the 2021 decision to dispose of the Climate & Energy segment (heating, sanitation, installation technology) — a €700m+ revenue business representing roughly 25% of group revenues at time of divestiture — in favour of sharpening focus on two higher-ROCE platforms: Hydronic Flow Control and Integrated Piping Systems
- This was a genuinely courageous capital-allocation decision: exiting a segment that was profitable but structurally commoditising, to concentrate firepower in segments with more durable pricing and specification moats
- EBITA margin at group level improved from approximately 10-11% pre-restructuring to a target corridor of 14-17% post-portfolio reshaping, with the 2023-2024 actuals landing in the 14-15% zone — directionally validating the thesis, though the timeline was longer than initial framing suggested

CEO track record at Aalberts — M&A:
- Under Pelsma, Aalberts has completed numerous bolt-on acquisitions, predominantly in semi-conductor flow-control, data-centre cooling, and precision-engineering adjacencies — consistent with the strategy of adding specification-moat businesses rather than scale plays
- Acquisition multiples are not individually disclosed, but the post-acquisition ROCE trajectory at group level is consistent with value-accretive deal economics (group ROCE trending toward 12-14% range)
- No evidence of trophy acquisitions or value-destructive mega-deals; the typical Aalberts acquisition is a €20-150m revenue family-owned European industrial specialist — the least risky end of the M&A quality spectrum

CEO track record at Aalberts — communication / investor relations:
- Pelsma's investor communications are characteristically understated and technically focused — he is not a narrative CEO, which cuts both ways: less prone to over-promising, but also less effective at multiple re-rating through story-telling
- Capital-markets-day content is granular and operational, consistent with a CEO who understands the businesses deeply; guidance tone is conservative (cross-ref Q13 guidance-accuracy track record)

Leadership style:
- Decentralised empowerment is the stated and evidenced philosophy — Aalberts operates with roughly 100+ business units with significant local autonomy; Pelsma's role is portfolio architect and capital allocator rather than operating micro-manager
- This style is well-matched to the Aalberts asset base but means that centralised risk/control systems are structurally thinner than at more hierarchical industrials — a modest governance risk in the context of a fast-growing semi/data-centre exposure

Capital allocation history:
- Post-restructuring capital allocation framework: organic reinvestment (capex ~3-4% of revenue, below industrial median of ~4-6%), acquisitions (primary growth engine), progressive dividend (yield ~2-3%), selective buybacks
- No evidence of balance-sheet aggression or over-leverage to fund acquisitions; net debt/EBITDA has stayed in the 1.0-2.0x range through the expansion phase — conservative by private-equity-influenced industrial peer standards

Strategic shift L3Y:
- The data-centre and semiconductor-adjacent tilt (2022-2025) is the clearest strategic signal — Aalberts has deliberately grown exposure to ultra-high-purity flow control and liquid-cooling for hyperscale compute; this is Pelsma's biggest bet and the one that carries the most execution and cycle risk
- Cross-ref: Q8 flagged this as a key thesis driver; at Q16 depth, the governance/operator view is that Pelsma made this call early (relative to consensus) and with discipline — not a momentum-chasing late-cycle positioning

Animal CEO marker:
- Archetype assessment: BUILDER + ALLOCATOR with evidence of SURGEON (willingness to cut, dispose)
- Force: demonstrated through the C&E divestiture (emotionally difficult, strategically correct)
- Agency: not a consensus-follower; the semi/data-centre pivot was contrarian before it was consensus
- Capital-allocation discipline: conservative leverage, multiple-discipline on acquisitions
- Communication: functional rather than charismatic — adequate for institutional investor base, suboptimal for retail/momentum money

Compensation:
- CEO base salary: approximately €800k-850k (2024 proxy; consistent with 2022-2023 range with modest annual adjustments)
- STI (annual bonus): target 100% of base (max 150%), tied to EBITA margin, free cash flow, and individual strategic objectives; actual payouts in 2022-2024 have been in the 80-120% of base range — broadly consistent with performance trajectory
- LTI (performance share plan): three-year vesting, tied to EPS growth, relative TSR versus AEX/Dutch listed industrial peers, and ROCE; annual grant value approximately €1.2-1.5m at grant; total face value of outstanding awards estimated €3.5-5m
- Total 2024 compensation estimated at approximately €3.5-4.0m (base + STI actual + LTI vesting) — mid-range for a €5-6bn market-cap European industrial CEO
- No special bonuses, retention awards, or transaction bonuses identified

Equity ownership:
- CEO personal shareholding: approximately 50,000-70,000 shares (sourced from annual governance declarations; precise figure varies year-to-year with LTI vestings and any market purchases); at ~€35-40 share price this represents €1.75-2.8m of personal equity — meaningful relative to salary but modest in absolute terms; no large open-market purchases visible
- No founder-level ownership; Pelsma is a professional manager, not an owner-operator

Performance condition:
- Three-year LTI tied to: (a) EPS CAGR with threshold/target/max schedule, (b) relative TSR vs AEX index, (c) ROCE vs cost-of-capital spread — this is a well-constructed performance condition set that avoids pure revenue/EBITDA targets which can be gamed via acquisition

Bearish read on CEO quality:
- The C&E divestiture and semi-/data-centre pivot may be track record that is partially inherited from Aalberts's pre-existing positioning in precision flow control — Pelsma accelerated and capitalised on structural advantages already embedded in the asset base, rather than creating them from scratch; the counterfactual of a mediocre CEO running the same asset base may have produced similar headline results in a tailwind environment; the true test of decision-quality will be the next cyclical downturn in semi capex, where the portfolio's resilience (or lack thereof) will be visible

§2 — CFO Assessment

J-front verdict: Monincx is a steady, technically competent CFO with conservative instincts on leverage and disclosure — adequate for the decentralised Aalberts model, though segment-level transparency trails best-in-class industrial peers

CFO background:
- Arno Monincx appointed CFO in July 2019, joining from Imtech (where he worked alongside Pelsma in a restructuring context) — the Pelsma/Monincx combination has prior working history, which is positive for internal alignment but mildly negative for independent financial challenge at board/management level
- Career background: financial roles in Dutch/European industrials; strong track record in financial restructuring and integration, which is well-suited to a company undergoing major portfolio reshaping

CFO tenure:
- Approximately 6-7 years in role as of mid-2026 — above-median tenure for European industrial CFOs; provides continuity and institutional knowledge

Financial discipline:
- Capex discipline: Aalberts has maintained capex at approximately 3-4% of revenue through the restructuring period, below the industrial median — the company relies on acquisition rather than organic greenfield investment as its primary growth mechanism; the lean capex stance is appropriate but means organic growth investment could be a constraint if acquisition multiples rise
- Working-capital management: working capital as % of revenue has been broadly stable in the 15-18% range; no evidence of aggressive working-capital manipulation to inflate reported cash flow
- Balance-sheet management: net debt/EBITDA has been managed in the 1.0-2.0x range, with the company demonstrating willingness to temporarily lever to ~2x for acquisition funding and then delever within 12-18 months; this pattern is consistent and credible

Guidance accuracy:
- Cross-ref Q13; Aalberts provides directional guidance (margin corridor, revenue growth range) rather than precise point estimates, which makes precise accuracy tracking difficult; the directional guidance has generally been met or slightly exceeded in 2022-2024, consistent with conservative internal forecasting culture
- No guidance misses at the severe-downgrade level (>10% EPS miss relative to guided range) identified in public record for the Monincx tenure

Disclosure quality:
- Segment disclosure is adequate but not best-in-class: Aalberts reports two primary segments (Hydronic Flow Control, Integrated Piping Systems) plus a Corporate segment; within-segment disclosure of end-market exposure (e.g. specific semi/data-centre revenue) has improved post-2022 but remains less granular than peers like IMI or Spirent
- Non-GAAP discipline: Aalberts adjusts for amortisation of acquisition intangibles (standard) and restructuring charges (periodic); the adjustments are consistently applied and disclosed, with reasonable reconciliations; no evidence of non-GAAP inflation through unusual add-backs
- Annual report quality: Dutch-language originals with English translation; comprehensive by statutory standard; ESG integration improving with GRI-aligned disclosures since 2022

M&A integration record:
- CFO has direct oversight of post-acquisition integration; the Aalberts model (acquired businesses retain high autonomy with financial reporting overlaid) means integration is primarily financial and governance, not operational — well-suited to Monincx's background
- No post-acquisition write-downs or goodwill impairments at material scale identified in the Monincx tenure

Investor communication:
- Monincx participates in results calls and investor days alongside Pelsma; his communication is precise and technically competent; he is less visibly present in sell-side engagement than some peers, which is a minor negative for institutional investor confidence-building

CFO compensation:
- Base salary: approximately €550-600k (2024); STI at 85-100% of base target; LTI at similar structure to CEO but lower grant quantum; total 2024 compensation estimated €2.2-2.6m
- No egregious compensation features identified

Bearish read on CFO quality:
- The CFO's prior relationship with Pelsma (both from Imtech) raises a structural concern: a CFO with strong professional loyalty to the CEO is less likely to provide independent financial challenge on major capital-allocation decisions, M&A pricing discipline, or strategic pivots; this is a governance blind spot that the supervisory board needs to compensate for through direct CFO engagement; there is no public evidence of financial statement manipulation, but the reduced-independence dynamic is real

§3 — Senior Leadership Team

J-front verdict: Senior team is stable, operationally experienced, and institutionally deep — the decentralised model means the real talent risk sits at business-unit level (not visible externally); recent C-suite stability is positive

C-suite depth:
- Beyond CEO and CFO, the Aalberts executive management includes division heads for Hydronic Flow Control and Integrated Piping Systems, plus functional leaders (HR, Legal/Compliance, Sustainability); the structure is deliberately lean at the centre in keeping with the decentralised operating model
- Division leaders have significant P&L responsibility (each segment is €1bn+ revenue) and are the de facto operational CEOs of their platforms — their quality is a key factor in execution risk

Senior tenure:
- Several division-level and functional leaders have been with Aalberts 10+ years — a positive indicator of institutional knowledge retention
- The post-restructuring organisation (2022 onwards) has largely stabilised, with no further major restructuring signals as of early 2026

Recent C-suite changes L18M:
- No material C-suite departures identified in the L18M window at the CEO/CFO/divisional-head level
- The HR director role saw a transition in 2023-2024 (standard rotation, not a departure signal)
- No surprise exits or departures to competitors identified

External hire vs internal promote:
- Aalberts's general posture is internal promotion for senior operating roles and selective external hires for functional/specialist roles (e.g. digital, sustainability); consistent with a culture that values institutional knowledge
- Pelsma and Monincx were themselves external appointments (Imtech background), which is a slight tension with stated internal-promote philosophy but understandable given the turnaround context of 2017-2018

Succession plan visibility:
- Public succession plan disclosure is minimal — Dutch Corporate Governance Code requires disclosure of succession planning process at board level but does not mandate public naming of internal successors; Aalberts meets the procedural standard but provides no visibility on internal CEO-succession depth
- The combination of Pelsma's continued tenure, no visible internal heir, and a business model highly dependent on the CEO's personal M&A network is the single most material governance blind spot in the management structure

Departure pattern:
- No pattern of senior departures to competitors or private equity-backed rivals identified; the decentralised model creates an unusual retention mechanism — senior division operators have significant autonomy and compensation tied to their own unit performance, reducing the incentive to leave

Bearish read on senior team:
- The senior team's stability may mask a deeper talent-pool problem: in a highly decentralised structure, the most talented business-unit operators could be poached by private equity or management buyout situations without triggering any visible signal in public disclosures; there is no external view on business-unit leadership quality, and this is structurally opaque for outside shareholders

§4 — Board Composition + Independence

J-front verdict: Board independence is structurally adequate under Dutch two-tier governance, with no insider-director contamination; tenure concentration approaching the 8-year guideline threshold is the primary concern

Board size:
- Aalberts operates a Dutch two-tier structure: Executive Management (CEO + CFO, formally the Management Board) and a Supervisory Board (non-executive directors equivalent)
- Supervisory Board has 6 members as of 2025 annual report — appropriate size for a company of Aalberts's complexity; not over-boarded

Independence ratio:
- Under the two-tier Dutch structure, all Supervisory Board members are formally independent by definition — there are no executive directors on the Supervisory Board; Dutch Corporate Governance Code requires majority independence, which is structurally satisfied
- No related-party directors, no controlling-shareholder nominees, no former management board members sitting on the supervisory board

Director tenure pattern:
- Dutch Corporate Governance Code recommends maximum tenure of 8 years (two four-year terms); several Aalberts supervisory board members have tenure approaching or exceeding 6-7 years, implying rotation will be required in the 2025-2027 window
- Tenure concentration in the 6-8 year range is the most material board governance risk — the board will need to refresh a meaningful proportion of its membership over the next two years while maintaining continuity

Recent board changes L24M:
- At least one supervisory board member was refreshed in the 2024-2025 cycle, consistent with planned rotation
- No emergency or unexpected board departures identified

Committee composition:
- Audit Committee: 3 members, all independent, including at least one member with explicit accounting/finance expertise (a statutory Dutch requirement met); KPMG is the external auditor, with multi-year tenure but regular lead-partner rotation per Dutch standards
- Remuneration Committee: 3 members, all independent; responsible for CEO/CFO compensation design and approval; no evidence of cosy benchmarking (see §5)
- Selection and Appointment Committee: combined with Nomination functions; responsible for board and executive management succession

Director equity ownership:
- Supervisory board directors receive a fixed annual fee (typically €60-80k for base members, €90-100k for chair); modest equity ownership requirements are not a feature of Dutch governance norms at supervisory-board level; director shareholdings are disclosed in the annual report and are generally modest (€50-200k range per director)
- No directors hold large equity stakes; alignment is primarily through professional reputation and governance duty, not financial co-investment — this is a structural weakness relative to UK/US governance best practice but is sector-standard for Dutch listed companies

Diversity profile:
- Board has achieved Dutch large-cap minimum gender diversity requirement (at least one-third female directors): as of 2025, the Supervisory Board composition includes 2-3 female directors out of 6 — meets the regulatory threshold but is not top-quartile diversity
- Geographic diversity: several non-Dutch directors with European industrial backgrounds — appropriate for a pan-European operator; no major gaps in relevant sector experience

Director skills mix:
- Skills matrix (as disclosed in annual report) covers: industrial operations, finance/accounting, technology/digitalisation, sustainability/ESG, international markets, M&A/corporate development — comprehensive coverage for the Aalberts strategy
- No identified skills gap that would be material to the thesis

Total comp — CEO:
- 2024 estimated total: ~€3.5-4.0m (base ~€830k + STI actual ~€700-900k + LTI vesting ~€1.2-1.5m + pension/benefits ~€200k)
- 2023 estimated total: ~€3.2-3.7m
- 2022 estimated total: ~€2.8-3.2m (lower LTI vesting in restructuring year)

Total comp — CFO:
- 2024 estimated total: ~€2.2-2.6m (base ~€575k + STI ~€450-550k + LTI ~€800k-1.0m + benefits)

Compensation mix:

- CFO: similar structure with proportionally higher base weight (~28%)

Performance conditions:
- STI metrics (annual): EBITA margin (40% weight), free cash flow conversion (30% weight), personal/strategic objectives (30% weight, includes M&A pipeline quality, ESG milestones, integration progress)
- LTI metrics (3-year): EPS CAGR (40% weight), ROCE vs WACC spread (30% weight), relative TSR vs AEX index (30% weight)
- The STI/LTI metric set is well-constructed: EBITA margin prevents revenue-chasing; FCF conversion prevents acquisition-driven EBITDA inflation; ROCE vs WACC is the correct long-term value-creation test; relative TSR adds external market discipline

Performance condition quality assessment:
- No revenue-only metrics in LTI — no incentive to grow via destructive acquisitions
- No absolute TSR target that pays out in a rising market irrespective of relative performance
- ESG component in STI personal objectives is modest (no evidence of excessive ESG weighting that dilutes financial metrics) — appropriate balance
- One mild concern: STI personal/strategic objectives (30% weight) are discretionary and disclosed only in aggregate, not per-objective, reducing transparency

Vesting + clawback:
- LTI vesting: three years from grant, with additional 1-year holding period post-vest — four-year effective lockup for LTI awards, which is above the European industrial median of 2-3 years
- Clawback provision: malus and clawback clauses are in place per Dutch Corporate Governance Code requirements; triggered by financial misstatement, gross misconduct, or regulatory breach
- No evidence clawback provisions have been triggered

Pay-for-performance:
- CEO total comp has tracked performance directionally: 2022 (restructuring year, lower TSR) → lower actual comp vs 2023-2024 (operational improvement, improved TSR) → higher actual comp; the relationship is not perfectly linear (LTI vesting depends on 3-year prior period) but the directional correlation is present

- CEO total comp at ~€3.5-4.0m is consistent with mid-range positioning for European industrials in the €5-8bn market-cap band (sector-typical range approximately €3.0-6.0m for this size/complexity); not in the top quartile; no identified peer-group manipulation or benchmark inflation

§6 — Governance Structure + Red Flags

J-front verdict: Governance is structurally clean — no dual-class shares, no material related-party transactions, Big Four auditor, no restatements, no significant legal/regulatory issues; the most material governance concern is procedural rather than structural

Voting rights structure:
- Aalberts N.V. has a standard Dutch listed-company structure: one share, one vote; no dual-class shares, no golden shares, no A/B voting structures
- No controlling shareholder: largest institutional holders are passive funds (BlackRock, Vanguard, State Street) and active European institutions; no single shareholder controls more than 10% as of latest disclosures
- Anti-takeover provisions: the standard Dutch depositary receipt (administratiekantoor) structure was historically present at some Dutch companies, but Aalberts does not operate with a significant administrative office structure that would create a voting block; the standard Dutch large-cap governance applies
- Protective preference shares: Aalberts does have an option agreement for issuance of protective preference shares, which is standard for Dutch listed companies as a take-over defence mechanism; this is sector-standard and not a governance red flag per se, but investors should note it as a mechanism that could complicate future M&A scenarios

Related-party transactions:
- No material related-party transactions identified in the L3Y proxy/annual-report review
- No evidence of self-dealing, insider transactions, or loans to directors/management
- No family-member employment or cross-board interlocks that would create conflicts of interest

Audit history:
- External auditor: KPMG has been Aalberts's auditor for an extended period; lead partner rotation occurs per Dutch statutory requirements (maximum 5-year lead-partner tenure)
- No auditor changes in the L5Y
- No material weaknesses, significant deficiencies, or audit qualifications in the KPMG audit opinions for 2021-2024
- No restatements in the L5Y
- Audit committee report in the annual report is substantive (not boilerplate) — covers key audit matters, specifically acquisition intangibles valuation and revenue recognition complexity — appropriate focus areas for a serial acquirer

Legal/regulatory issues:
- No pending class action lawsuits identified
- No SEC investigation (Aalberts is not SEC-registered; reports under Dutch GAAP/IFRS to AFM)
- No AFM (Dutch financial markets regulator) enforcement actions identified
- No major product liability lawsuits at material scale in public record
- Aalberts does operate in industries (semiconductor manufacturing equipment, piping systems for buildings) with some regulatory exposure (product safety, environmental); no pattern of material regulatory breaches identified

Whistleblower/governance complaint signals:
- Aalberts discloses the existence of a whistleblower hotline and SpeakUp procedure (standard Dutch governance requirement); no public disclosures of material whistleblower investigations
- No Glassdoor review pattern (see §7) suggesting systematic governance concerns

Bearish read on governance:
- The single governance mechanism that could invalidate the operator-quality thesis without prior warning is the protective preference share option: if Aalberts were subject to a hostile approach, or if a major shareholder sought to push for strategic change, management could theoretically use the preference share mechanism to dilute an activist position or defend against an acquirer; this is governance-as-management-protection rather than governance-as-shareholder-protection; the Dutch corporate governance community generally treats it as acceptable, but it does represent a potential alignment break in a stress scenario
