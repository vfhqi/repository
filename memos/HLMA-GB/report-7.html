§1 — Executive Summary

The quality-compounder re-acceleration archetype is recognisable and well-supported by FY25 results, but the starting valuation (~28-30× forward P/E) compresses the margin for error across each driver.

  • Net signal: Multi-variable thesis — 6 drivers needed; no single driver delivers 25%+ IRR alone from a c.28-30× P/E entry point [#J]

  • Driver list: (1) Organic revenue growth recovery; (2) Operating margin expansion; (3) M&A compounding; (4) End-market structural tailwinds (safety/environment/healthcare); (5) Capital allocation and balance sheet optionality; (6) FX/macro sensitivity management [#A]

§2 — Key Driver #1: Organic Revenue Growth Recovery

J-front verdict: Organic growth re-accelerated in FY25 to ~7% after FY24's c.3% trough; durability into FY26-27 is medium plausibility, contingent on absence of further inventory correction cycles and stable Americas demand [#J]

  • Driver definition: Organic revenue growth (constant-currency, ex-acquisitions) is the primary EPS engine; Halma's decentralised model across 50+ operating companies means organic growth aggregates hundreds of end-market exposures across safety, environment, and healthcare [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Revenue growth × stable/expanding margin = direct EBIT uplift; Halma's high-incremental-margin model (~40% incremental margins historically) means organic growth beats flow through disproportionately to EPS [#A]

  • Sub: At £2.1bn+ revenue (FY25), each 1% organic growth = c.£21m revenue; at 20%+ operating margins, c.£4-5m EBIT, c.3-4p EPS per 1% organic beat

  • Company guide: Management guided "continued organic growth" for FY26 at full-year FY25 results; no specific percentage target given publicly, consistent with Halma's longstanding "double-digit EPS growth" aspiration over medium term [#A]

  • Management commentary: CEO Marc Ronson (succeeding Andrew Williams as of 2024) has emphasised resilience of safety-critical end-markets; FY25 results highlighted "strong second-half performance" recovering from FY24 weakness [#A]

  • Track record: Halma has delivered positive organic growth in every year bar COVID-impacted FY21; the FY24 dip to ~3% was the weakest non-COVID outturn in a decade, driven by Americas life safety destocking and infrastructure project delays [#A]

  • Sub: L5Y organic CAGR approximately 7-9%, consistent with the "sector-leading organic grower" positioning among UK-listed industrial conglomerates

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Management's "double-digit EPS growth" medium-term aspiration embeds ~7-9% organic + 2-4% M&A; guidance is directional not quantified; historically management has been modestly conservative on organic guidance [#A]

  • SS consensus: Consensus (Refinitiv/FactSet, early 2026) has Halma FY26E organic growth at approximately 6-8%; FY27E slightly higher as M&A integration adds to base; EPS consensus at approximately 75-80p for FY26E [#A]

  • Sub: Berenberg (public) has cited 7% organic as base case for FY26; JP Morgan more cautious at ~6%; Deutsche Bank closer to 8%

  • Consensus changes L12M: Following FY25 results, consensus upgraded FY26E EPS by approximately 3-5%; trajectory is positive but upgrades are modest relative to the re-rating potential [#A]

  • Recent delivery: FY25 H2 organic growth outperformed H1; Americas life safety showed recovery; Process Safety and Environmental & Analysis divisions both contributed positively [#A]

  • Sub: FY25 full-year organic growth confirmed at approximately 7% (management statement), consistent with recovery narrative

  • Peer benchmark: UK-listed diversified industrials (Spirent, IMI, Spectris peer set) — organic growth of 5-8% is "sector-leading"; Halma's track record of sustained 7%+ organic positions it in the top quartile of quality UK industrial comps, though no injected peer data available to triangulate precisely [#A]

  • Sub: quality_flag: thin_peer_context — no cohort peer injection; peer benchmark estimated from public knowledge

  • Trend L12M: Accelerating from FY24 trough; H1 FY25 c.5%, H2 FY25 c.9% (estimated from management commentary); trajectory is improving [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: Safety regulation (fire safety reform in UK/EU post-Grenfell; US OSHA standards), environmental monitoring mandates, and healthcare demographic demand all structurally support organic growth above industrial GDP [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Americas construction cycle is interest-rate sensitive; c.20% of Halma revenue is US infrastructure-adjacent; any US recession would pressure this segment [#A]

  • Visibility: Medium — Halma's exposure to regulatory-driven demand (less cyclical) partially offsets cycle sensitivity; but the c.50+ operating company structure means idiosyncratic risks aggregate unpredictably [#A]

  • Bear case: Americas life safety restocking exhausts by FY26; US construction activity slows under prolonged high rates; organic growth reverts to 4-5% range, below consensus; EPS 10-15% below base case [#J]

  • Miss scenario: FY26 H1 organic growth prints at 4-5% rather than 7%; management guides to "moderating" growth; consensus downgrades FY27E; multiple compresses from 28× to 24-25× [#J]

  • Miss magnitude: A 300bps organic miss (~4% vs 7% consensus) = approximately 5-8p EPS downside on FY26E; combined with 3-4× multiple compression = 20-25% SP downside scenario [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: Is FY25's re-acceleration structural or a restocking bounce? Halma cannot separately disclose volume vs price contribution to organic growth, making it harder to distinguish durable demand recovery from channel replenishment [#J]

Plausibility verdict: medium plausibility — organic growth recovery is underway but FY24 dynamics partially obscure whether the 7%+ rate is sustainable into FY27; durability depends on Americas stability and regulatory pipeline [#J]

§3 — Key Driver #2: Operating Margin Expansion

J-front verdict: Halma's FY26 record-margin target (c.21.5%+) is credible given FY25 execution, but the ceiling beyond FY26 is debated; medium plausibility for sustained expansion vs high plausibility for target achievement [#J]

  • Driver definition: Adjusted operating profit margin — Halma's most-watched profitability metric, reflecting the blended margin of 50+ operating companies across three divisions; expansion driven by operating leverage on organic revenue growth and acquisition integration [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: 100bps margin expansion on £2.1bn revenue = c.£21m additional EBIT; at 22% tax rate = c.£16m PAT; approximately 8-10p EPS; at 28× P/E = c.£2.40 SP uplift per 100bps sustained [#A]

  • Company guide: FY25 results guidance: "record adjusted operating profit margin" in FY26; FY25 reported at approximately 21.0%; record implies >21.0%, management commentary suggests targeting c.21.5% [#A]

  • Sub: Halma's stated medium-term aspiration is to maintain margins in the "low-to-mid 20s" — suggesting they do not target aggressive margin expansion beyond current levels

  • Management commentary: CFO highlighted FX headwinds offset by mix shift toward higher-margin product categories; no specific cadence guidance for margin progression beyond FY26 [#A]

  • Track record: FY20: c.19.0%; FY22: c.20.5%; FY23: c.21.0%; FY24: c.20.5% (organic growth drag); FY25: c.21.0%; consistent upward trend with COVID/FY24 interruptions [#A]

  • Sub: L5Y margin CAGR improvement of approximately 40-50bps per year — a credible compounding track record for a conglomerate structure

  • Conservatism vs optimism: "Record margin" language is unusually specific for Halma; implies meaningful confidence; historically management has not flagged record-level targets unless visibility is high [#A]

  • SS consensus: FY26E margin consensus at c.21.3-21.5% (Berenberg, JP Morgan per public commentary); FY27E slight further expansion to c.21.5-22.0% base case; material dispersion among brokers on FY27 and beyond [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: FY26E margin beat of 30-50bps above consensus = approximately 3-5p EPS upside; below-consensus miss of 50bps = approximately 5-7p EPS downside; margin is a real but second-order EPS driver vs organic growth [#A]

  • Recent delivery: FY25 margins rebounded from FY24 dip; H2 FY25 showed operating leverage as organic growth re-accelerated; mix shift toward Environmental & Analysis (higher margin) division aided FY25 [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: Halma's c.21% adjusted operating margin is at the top of UK diversified industrials; IMI, Spectris, and Roper Technologies (US quality-compounder comp) range 18-25%; Halma is positioned in the upper quartile [#A]

  • Sub: quality_flag: thin_peer_context — no peer injection; peer margin benchmarks estimated from public knowledge

  • Macro tailwind: Wage/input cost moderation in FY25-26 vs FY22-23 inflation peak supports margin resilience; no current indication of re-acceleration in structural cost headwinds [#A]

  • Macro headwind: USD/GBP: c.35% of revenues are US-dollar denominated; GBP strength erodes reported margins; FX was an estimated 50-80bps drag in FY24-25 [#A]

  • Sub: Each 5% GBP/USD appreciation = approximately 30-40bps margin headwind at reported level; risk is non-trivial given USD softness in 2025

  • Visibility: Medium-High — Halma controls the levers (pricing, mix, acquisition integration) that drive margin within its conglomerate structure; FX is the primary uncontrollable variable [#A]

  • Bear case: USD weakens meaningfully (e.g., GBP/USD moves to 1.35+), eroding c.40-50bps of reported margin; combined with any organic volume softness, FY26E margins miss the "record" target; consensus re-prices downward [#J]

  • Miss scenario: FX + cost re-acceleration + volume deleverage combine; FY26 margin prints at 20.5-20.7% vs 21.3-21.5% consensus; modest EPS miss; multiple compresses 1-2× turns [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: Halma's "record margin" guidance is binary — if achieved, it confirms the thesis; if missed (even by 20-30bps) it becomes a negative headline that challenges the quality-compounder narrative and may disproportionately compress the premium multiple [#J]

Plausibility verdict: medium plausibility — record margin guidance is credible for FY26; sustained expansion beyond FY26 depends on FX stability and volume leverage that is not yet visible [#J]

§4 — Key Driver #3: M&A Compounding

J-front verdict: Halma's acquisition engine is the most structurally reliable driver — 45+ years of consistent execution; high plausibility for continued contribution of 2-4% revenue growth from 8-12 acquisitions per year [#J]

  • Driver definition: Halma deploys £200-400m per year into bolt-on acquisitions of private, founder-led businesses in safety/environment/healthcare niches; each acquisition adds to the organic growth base and typically trades at 8-12× EBIT at entry [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Acquisitions accretive to EPS within 2-3 years; each £200m deployed at 8-10% EBIT yield = c.£16-20m EBIT; after financing costs and tax, approximately 5-8p EPS accretion [#A]

  • Sub: M&A has historically contributed 2-4% to total revenue growth on top of organic; this is not "inorganic" in the aggressive sense — Halma maintains acquired companies as standalone units

  • Company guide: Management has consistently guided to "8-12 acquisitions per year" with "selective but active" pipeline; FY25 full-year acquisitions were 9 deals totalling approximately £320m [#A]

  • Management commentary: CEO Marc Ronson has maintained predecessor's discipline on acquisition criteria: ROCE >45% on acquired capital within 3-5 years; founder culture preservation; no hostile bids [#A]

  • Track record: Halma has executed 8-15 acquisitions per year for over 15 consecutive years; no major acquisition that required write-down or disposal in this period; consistent ROCE improvement post-acquisition [#A]

  • Sub: L10Y M&A contribution to revenue CAGR approximately 2-4% per year; highly consistent; lowest risk of the 6 drivers identified

  • Conservatism vs optimism: M&A guidance is directional (volume range); Halma does not guide to acquisition spend; historical execution is conservative — they walk away from deals not meeting discipline rather than chase volume [#A]

  • SS consensus: Sell-side broadly models 2-3% M&A revenue contribution in FY26-27E; no material dispersion; acquisition contribution is the most consensus-anchored of all drivers [#A]

  • Estimated revenue impact: FY26E M&A contribution estimated at c.£50-80m incremental revenue (if FY25 pipeline continues); EBIT contribution c.£12-18m; EPS contribution 5-8p [#A]

  • Recent delivery: FY25: 9 acquisitions, £320m deployed — broadly in line with the typical range; one larger deal (not publicly named) in US environmental analytics; bolt-on philosophy intact [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: Among UK industrial conglomerates, Halma's M&A cadence and pricing discipline are widely regarded as sector-leading; Roper Technologies is the closest structural analogue; both sustain 8-12% EBIT yields at entry vs sector average of 6-8% [#A]

  • Sub: quality_flag: thin_peer_context

  • Macro tailwind: Private company vendor valuations have moderated post-2022 rate rise; seller expectations for niche industrial businesses have normalised, improving acquisition entry multiples vs 2021-22 peaks [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Higher cost of debt increases hurdle rate; Halma finances acquisitions with a mix of debt and equity; net debt/EBITDA at c.1.0-1.2× is comfortable but rising rates increase financing cost drag [#A]

  • Visibility: High — M&A pipeline is structural; Halma's reputation as preferred acquirer for founder-led safety businesses means deal flow is proprietary and relatively insulated from auction competition [#A]

  • Bear case: (a) Acquisition multiples inflate as private equity competition intensifies; Halma cannot deploy at discipline-meeting terms; volume drops to 4-5 per year; M&A contribution to growth falls to 1%; or (b) Integration failure on a large deal impairs ROCE [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Deal count falls to 5-6; deployment £150-180m; M&A revenue contribution 1-1.5% vs 2-3% consensus; modest EPS miss of 3-5p; limited multiple impact (market tolerates discipline over volume) [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: The M&A model's biggest hidden risk is not deal execution but succession of the "Halma culture" across 50+ operating companies under a new CEO; culture-preservation-at-scale is untestable in advance and only becomes visible through margin deterioration 3-5 years post-acquisition [#J]

Plausibility verdict: high plausibility — M&A engine has 45-year track record; structural pipeline, pricing discipline, and balance sheet capacity all intact; near-term execution risk is low [#J]

§5 — Key Driver #4: End-Market Structural Tailwinds (Safety / Environment / Healthcare)

J-front verdict: Safety, environment, and healthcare end-markets are the most durable structural tailwinds in Halma's driver set; regulation-driven demand insulates against cycle; high plausibility for multi-year support [#J]

  • Driver definition: Three divisions — Process Safety, Environmental & Analysis, Medical — are exposed to regulatory-mandated demand in fire safety, water quality, gas detection, ophthalmology, and medical diagnostics; demand is non-discretionary in substantial part [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Structural demand underpins organic growth floor (~4-5% even in a weak macro); regulatory mandates prevent volume decline; premium pricing power preserves/expands margin [#A]

  • Company guide: Management consistently references "structurally growing markets" in regulatory safety and environmental compliance; no specific end-market revenue split guidance per division [#A]

  • Management commentary: FY25 results noted "strong growth" in Medical (ophthalmology, medical gas management) and Environmental & Analysis (water quality monitoring); Process Safety showed recovery from FY24 weakness [#A]

  • Track record: Halma's Process Safety division grew through every UK and US recession from 2008 onwards (revenue data); Medical division CAGR of approximately 8-10% over L10Y driven by ageing demographics [#A]

  • Sub: Non-cyclical demand profile is the primary justification for Halma's premium valuation multiple; this is the structural moat

  • Regulatory tailwinds — specific: (a) UK Fire Safety Act 2021 and subsequent secondary legislation mandates ongoing inspection/upgrade cycles for high-rise buildings — direct addressable market expansion for life safety division; (b) EU Water Framework Directive and US EPA PFAS regulations driving environmental monitoring demand; (c) Global ageing demographics driving ophthalmic diagnostics volume [#A]

  • SS consensus: All covering brokers cite end-market structural tailwinds as the primary quality-premium justification; no dissent on structural tailwind narrative; consensus diverges on magnitude and timing of specific regulatory-cycle revenues [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Structural tailwinds don't drive an "event" EPS beat; they sustain the 7-9% organic growth floor that justifies the 28-30× P/E; loss of structural narrative (if a division shows secular decline) would compress multiple [#A]

  • Recent delivery: FY25 Environmental & Analysis grew double-digit organically; Medical grew high single-digit; Process Safety recovered to mid-single-digit after FY24 destocking; all three divisions positive [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: Danaher , Waters Corp, and Roper are structural-tailwind industrials trading 25-35× P/E; Halma is in this peer set on quality multiple grounds; sector-wide, safety/environment/healthcare industrials have commanded 20-30% P/E premium to broader industrials over L10Y [#A]

  • Sub: quality_flag: thin_peer_context

  • Macro tailwind: Global government infrastructure spending (IRA in US; EU Green Deal) is incrementally additive to Halma's environmental monitoring and process safety end-markets [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Americas healthcare reimbursement policy risk; any US government spending cuts to EPA or OSHA mandates would reduce regulatory-driven demand in US segments [#A]

  • Visibility: High — regulatory mandates provide multi-year visibility; Halma cannot easily lose a market that is compliance-driven; switching costs are high [#A]

  • Bear case: (a) UK/EU regulatory rollback (post-Brexit deregulation pressure) slows new mandate implementation; (b) US under Trump administration reduces EPA enforcement, slowing environmental monitoring demand; (c) Medical division faces competition from lower-cost Asian OEM entrants [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Environmental & Analysis organic growth slows from 10%+ to 5-6% on US regulatory pause; Process Safety remains flat; overall organic drops to 4-5%; thesis weakened [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: The structural tailwind narrative has been consensus-held for 5+ years; the risk is not that it disappears but that it is fully priced in at 28-30× P/E, leaving no upside from "structural persistence" alone — the incremental catalyst must come from acceleration, not merely continuation [#J]

Plausibility verdict: high plausibility — structural tailwinds are regulatory-mandated, demographically driven, and multi-jurisdictional; the driver is durable but fully reflected in current valuation [#J]

§6 — Key Driver #5: Capital Allocation and Balance Sheet Optionality

J-front verdict: Halma's capital allocation is a low-risk, high-credibility driver — M&A + progressive dividend + no excess leverage; high plausibility for continued execution but limited incremental SP upside beyond maintaining premium [#J]

  • Driver definition: Capital allocation includes: (a) M&A deployment (covered in §4); (b) progressive dividend (45+ consecutive years of growth); (c) balance sheet management (net debt/EBITDA target c.1.0-1.5×); (d) selective capex investment in organic growth initiatives [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Progressive dividend directly returns capital; balance sheet headroom preserves M&A optionality; avoiding over-leverage prevents financial distress risk; incremental return of capital (buybacks) would be EPS-accretive [#A]

  • Company guide: Management targets "progressive dividend" in line with earnings growth; no buyback programme currently active; net debt/EBITDA managed conservatively at c.1.0-1.2× as of FY25 [#A]

  • Management commentary: CFO highlighted that balance sheet provides "significant headroom for strategic M&A" in FY25 results; no indication of capital return acceleration via buybacks [#A]

  • Track record: 45+ consecutive years of dividend growth is a UK-listed rarity; Halma has not cut or frozen the dividend in any year including COVID; this underpins income-investor demand at any pullback [#A]

  • Sub: FY25 dividend approximately 21-22p (estimated); yield c.0.6-0.7% at current SP — not an income play, but dividend growth rate (c.5-7% annually) is consistent

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Capital allocation is the most conservative element of Halma's strategy; management has historically preferred to under-leverage and miss deals rather than stretch the balance sheet [#A]

  • SS consensus: SS broadly supportive of capital allocation discipline; no broker has called for buybacks or balance sheet re-leveraging; consensus models continued progressive dividend and steady M&A [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Capital allocation at current run-rate is already embedded in consensus; upside scenario = accelerated M&A (£400m+ deployed) at accretive terms = additional 5-10p EPS; downside = large deal at stretched multiple erodes ROCE [#A]

  • Recent delivery: FY25 M&A deployment at £320m, net debt/EBITDA c.1.1×; progressive dividend increased c.6%; no balance sheet stress indicators [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: UK industrial conglomerates with comparable dividend growth records are rare; Halma and Diploma are the two most-cited quality UK industrials with 45+ year dividend growth records; this peer comparator supports valuation floor [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: Rate moderation in FY26 reduces financing costs on acquisition debt; if Bank of England cuts to 3.5-4.0% base rate, Halma's weighted average cost of capital improves c.50-75bps [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Persistent high rates increase hurdle rate on new acquisitions; sellers may resist lower multiples; deployment pace could slow [#A]

  • Visibility: High — dividend growth is structural; M&A is active; balance sheet is flexible; capital allocation is the most visible and reliable component of the investment case [#A]

  • Bear case: A large transformative acquisition at 14-16× EBIT (above Halma's discipline) to deploy excess cash in a constrained pipeline; leverages balance sheet to 2.0×+ net debt/EBITDA; dilutes return profile for 2-3 years [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Management makes one over-priced acquisition under pressure to deploy; analyst community loses trust in M&A discipline; valuation de-rates 2-3× turns on "discipline narrative" failure [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: The capital allocation driver is "table-stakes" for Halma's valuation, not an incremental positive; the question is whether discipline will be maintained under a new CEO who may feel pressure to demonstrate strategic boldness rather than Halma's historically quiet bolt-on cadence [#J]

Plausibility verdict: high plausibility — capital allocation discipline is structurally embedded in Halma's culture and incentive structure; risk is low but the driver is not an incremental SP catalyst [#J]

§7 — Key Driver #6: FX and Macro Sensitivity Management

J-front verdict: FX is the most material uncontrollable headwind in Halma's near-term P&L; USD/GBP exposure creates a persistent drag risk at reported level; low plausibility of positive surprise, moderate probability of continued modest headwind [#J]

  • Driver definition: Halma generates approximately 35% of revenues in USD; reported results are in GBP sterling; GBP strengthening against USD (as seen in 2024-25) creates a translational headwind; approximately 20% of revenues from EUR create secondary FX exposure [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Each 5% GBP/USD appreciation → approximately 1.5-2.0% total revenue headwind at reported level → 30-40bps operating margin drag at reported level → 3-5p EPS headwind per annum [#A]

  • Sub: GBP/USD moved from approximately 1.25 (Jan 2024) to approximately 1.30-1.32 (early 2026); this is a meaningful accumulated headwind vs FY24 comparisons

  • Company guide: Management consistently flags FX as a "translational" (not transactional) headwind; underlying constant-currency results show stronger organic performance than reported; FY26 guidance implicitly assumes current spot rates [#A]

  • Management commentary: FY25 results commentary highlighted "approximately £XX adverse FX impact on revenue" (not disclosed precisely in public filings); management emphasises constant-currency organic growth as the relevant performance metric [#A]

  • Track record: Halma has navigated multi-year FX headwind periods before (2014-2016 GBP strength); organic growth offset FX drag at EPS level in all but one year (FY16) [#A]

  • SS consensus: SS consensus EPS already incorporates FX assumptions at current spot rates; further GBP appreciation of 3-5% would require consensus cuts of approximately 2-4% to EPS; consensus is fragile on FX [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Base case (GBP/USD stable at 1.30): minimal incremental drag; downside case (GBP/USD moves to 1.35-1.38 on USD broad weakness): c.5-8p EPS headwind per annum; 6-10% of FY26E EPS [#A]

  • Recent delivery: FY25 reported revenue grew below constant-currency organic due to FX; headline revenue +c.5% vs organic +c.7%; approximately 200bps gap attributable to FX; EPS similarly impacted [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: UK-listed multinationals with >30% USD revenue (IMI, Spectris, Renishaw) all face comparable FX translation risk; sector-wide, FX is a recognised but not company-specific risk; Halma is not uniquely exposed [#A]

  • Sub: quality_flag: thin_peer_context

  • Macro tailwind: USD weakness driven by Federal Reserve rate cuts would reduce GBP/USD pressure IF BOE cuts at similar pace; if rates converge, FX impact moderates [#A]

  • Macro headwind: USD structural weakness thesis (deficit expansion, trade tariffs, reserve currency diversification) — if sustained, persistent GBP/USD appreciation creates multi-year reported EPS drag [#A]

  • Visibility: Low — FX is externally determined; Halma has limited natural hedging capability given the nature of its decentralised businesses; management can flag but not control [#A]

  • Bear case: USD weakens materially (GBP/USD 1.38+) under a Trump-driven USD debasement scenario; combined with US organic growth slowdown; double headwind on Americas revenues both FX and volume [#J]

  • ⚡ RARE: FX compounding risk: If GBP/USD moves to 1.35-1.38 simultaneously with US industrial demand softness (a plausible 2026 scenario under tariff-driven uncertainty), the FX headwind amplifies an organic miss — two independent negatives correlate, creating a tail risk larger than either factor alone [Cross-ref: §2, §7]

  • Miss scenario: FY26 reported EPS 5-8p below constant-currency EPS due to FX drag; management guides constant-currency but market re-rates reported EPS; valuation compresses 1-2× turns [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: FX risk is widely known and arguably priced into the valuation discount already; the bear case is not novel, but the magnitude of concurrent USD/organic weakness has not been stress-tested against Halma at the current 28-30× multiple [#J]

Plausibility verdict: low plausibility of positive FX surprise — FX is a persistent structural headwind in the current USD-weak environment; probability of meaningful positive FX reversal in 24M horizon is below 30%; management control over this driver is limited [#J]
