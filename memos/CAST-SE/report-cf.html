EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (BLUF) 22 findings

Section summary
  1. a major activist-driven strategic pivot toward profitability and share buybacks is underway, though severe fundamental headwinds in the Swedish office market constrain near-term EPS growth

Materiality of change

1.
Sweeping strategic pivot: the new "Back to Basics" strategy fundamentally shifts Castellum from growth-chasing to a strict 10% return-on-equity mandate, prioritising profitability over volume
2.
Aggressive capital reallocation: management cancelled the cash dividend for a SEK 3.4bn share buyback, stating "if we can't earn it, we will return it"
3.
Major asset divestments: Castellum sold nine properties to AP7 for SEK 5.6bn to deleverage and fund buybacks, increasing concentration in the challenging office sector

Activist presence

4.
Dominant shareholder intervention: Akelius Apartments increased its stake above 25%, forcing an extraordinary general meeting to overhaul the board due to a lack of confidence
5.
Leadership overhaul: the activist installed Ralf Spann as Chair and is aggressively pushing for former Akelius CEO Pål Ahlsén to become Castellum's new CEO
6.
Radical restructuring proposal: Roger Akelius has publicly advocated for splitting Castellum into three separate companies to improve strategic focus and shareholder value

Top 5-8 key drivers

7.
Share buyback execution (Top driver): front-loaded repurchases of ~24 million shares below NAV provide primary EPS support over N12M; HIGH plausibility
8.
Asset divestment pipeline (Mid-tier driver): further non-core sales above book value will fund buybacks, though execution risk remains high; MEDIUM plausibility
9.
Vacancy rate normalisation (Mid-tier driver): management's "leasing, leasing, leasing" focus must reverse a climbing vacancy rate that reached 12% in Q1 2026; LOW plausibility
10.
Head office cost-cutting (Mid-tier driver): a recent reorganisation aims to deliver SEK 50m in annualised savings, providing a modest margin buffer; HIGH plausibility
11.
Interest rate easing (Bottom driver): falling STIBOR and improved bond market access offer relief on financing costs and valuation yields; MEDIUM plausibility

Recent delivery signal

12.
Deteriorating organic performance: Q1 2026 revealed like-for-like net rents falling 2% and NOI dropping 6%, driven by persistent tenant weakness in regional Swedish offices
13.
Hollow leasing victories: while Q1 reported positive net leasing of SEK 82m, this was almost entirely dependent on a single SEK 72m Ericsson contract

Forward credibility

14.
Pragmatic management posture: the messaging signals an honest-tracker archetype, openly admitting that "things will get worse before getting better" in the office market
15.
Realistic ROE anchor: the strict 10% ROE hurdle rate demonstrates a credible shift toward disciplined capital allocation, though achieving it organically currently lacks visibility

Sell-side positioning

16.
Broadly sceptical consensus: the sell-side pool leans heavily cautious, maintaining Underweight or Hold ratings due to weak underlying fundamentals and rising vacancy
17.
Valuation ceiling narrative: analysts widely argue that despite a visually undemanding P/NAV discount, the lack of forward earnings growth limits multiple expansion potential

Fundamental peer position

Falling volume: Castellum's portfolio vacancy climbed 100 bps in Q1 2026 to 12%, reflecting broad tenant downsizing and delayed leasing decisions
3.
Pricing power eroding: like-for-like net rents declined 2% in Q1 2026, vastly underperforming historical inflation-linked growth averages for the sector
4.
Deteriorating sentiment: tenants are taking considerably longer to sign leases, heavily driven by geopolitical uncertainty and weak Swedish GDP growth
5.
Structural demand pulse: demand remains resilient for modern, central, ESG-certified offices, creating a widening performance gap against secondary suburban locations

Competition

6.
Rising intensity: fierce competition for tenants in Stockholm is forcing landlords to grant higher incentives and greater rent discounts to secure occupancy
7.
Improving competitor health: transaction volumes in Sweden rebounded 16% year-on-year to SEK 163 billion, improving broad market liquidity for property owners
8.
Fragmenting structure: foreign investor presence nearly doubled to 32% of transaction volume, intensifying the bidding landscape for prime Swedish assets

Substitute emerging: sub-leasing by tenants trying to shed excess space acts as a direct, lower-cost competitor to traditional landlords' direct offerings
10.
Accelerating competitor risk: shorter typical lease durations of 3 years in Sweden expose landlords to higher renegotiation risk than continental European peers

11.
Fast disruption pace: 57% of companies plan to decrease their portfolio size over the next three years to reduce structural real estate costs
12.
Accelerating technology disruption: artificial intelligence adoption is expected to reduce administrative headcounts, directly threatening long-term office footprint requirements for large corporate tenants
13.
Structural demand pulse: hybrid working models have structurally lowered daily office attendance, permanently altering the density and usage patterns of commercial properties
14.
Innovative new entrant: the shift towards flexible, co-working office space requires significant capital expenditure from traditional landlords to remain relevant to modern tenants

Regulation

15.
Rising compliance cost: stricter EU ESG mandates force heightened capital expenditure to prevent older office properties from becoming stranded assets
16.
New environmental regulation: 100% of new Fabege leases are "green", setting a mandatory sustainability baseline that Castellum must match to attract premium tenants
17.
Supportive political actor: the Swedish government's 2026 budget includes fiscal stimulus intended to boost household disposable income and overall economic growth
18.
Hostile political actor: geopolitical tensions and international trade restrictions create supply chain bottlenecks that continually drive up construction material costs

Supply

19.
Falling concentration: new construction starts have plummeted due to high financing costs, which should gradually constrain future office supply and support eventual recovery

Rising supply risk: a significant oversupply of logistics facilities from speculative building has driven up vacancy rates in specific regional sub-markets

Cycle

Through-cycle deteriorating: office yields expanded significantly over the past two years, triggering severe portfolio devaluations across the entire Nordic property sector

Trough behaviour: direct market prime yields are showing early signs of stabilisation, with Stockholm office yields compressing by 10 bps in early 2026

Falling predictability: the correlation between interest rate cuts and property valuation recovery is proving much weaker than in previous historical cycles

Macro

27.
Supportive macro shift: the Riksbank reduced the policy rate to 1.75%, easing the immediate interest burden on highly geared property companies like Castellum
28.
Adverse macro shift: Swedish GDP growth remains heavily subdued, slowing corporate expansion and delaying the recovery of white-collar employment

Rising inflation: Middle East geopolitical conflicts threaten to spark renewed energy inflation, complicating further central bank rate cuts across Europe

Wage pressure: rising unemployment in Sweden, approaching 8.3%, directly depresses office space absorption and overall commercial leasing velocity

Other side of the story: the anticipated macro recovery could be entirely derailed by a resurgence in inflation; if bond yields spike again, the highly leveraged Swedish property sector faces a devastating second wave of yield expansion and severe refinancing crises
