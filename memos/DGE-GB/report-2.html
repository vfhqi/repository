§1 — Brief Recap + Key Components Summary

  • Net verdict: [#J] Diageo is a business in genuine transition — leadership on its third CEO in two years, financials structurally weaker than FY18-22, but brand equity (Guinness, Don Julio) intact and destocking in key markets (LAC, US) is completing rather than beginning.
  • Leadership change: [#I] Debra Crew (appointed Jul-23, departed Jul-25) replaced by interim CFO Nik Jhangiani, then permanent CEO Sir Dave Lewis (from Jan-26, ex-Tesco); three CEO changes in under 24 months — no large-cap spirits peer matches this instability.
  • Strategy change: [#I] "Accelerate" programme launched May-25: $625m cost savings over three years, $3bn annual FCF target from FY26, North America route-to-market restructuring, decentralised market team model.
  • Financial profile deterioration: [#A] Organic net sales growth collapsed from +21.4% in FY22 to -1.4% in FY24; FY25 recovered to +1.7% organic, but FY26 guidance revised to -2% to -3% reflecting renewed US weakness, tariff headwind, and China baijiu pressure.
  • Demand — bright spots: [#I] Guinness global volume +14% in FY25 (lapping +5% prior year); Don Julio largest US spirits brand by retail value (~$2.6bn); India (United Spirits) EBITDA +20.5% YoY in FY25.
  • Demand — weak spots: [#I] ❌ Johnnie Walker organic net sales -10.6% in FY25; Casamigos -18% in FY24; US spirits Q3 FY26 organic -15.4%; North America organic -9.4% in Q3 FY26.
  • Disruption forces: [#A] GLP-1 acknowledged by management but assessed as modest vs macroeconomic pressure; no/low alcohol portfolio growing ~56% from a small base; RTDs a structural tailwind Diageo is positioned to capture.
  • Political/regulatory: [#I] US 10% tariff on UK imports (post Liberation Day Apr-25) costs ~$200m/year; Diageo mitigating ~50%; Trump signal on Scotch tariff removal (May-26) is a potential positive catalyst.
  • Leverage creep: [#A] ❌ Net debt/EBITDA at 3.4x at Jun-25, above stated 2.5-3.0x target; buyback programme suspended vs Pernod's continued commitment.
  • Fulcrum driver (CQ-C5): [#J] US spirits recovery timing is the single largest binary for FY27 EPS trajectory; Guinness momentum is the offsetting flywheel but cannot fully compensate at group level.
  • Destocking cycle (CQ-C1): [#A] LAC declared functionally complete; US advanced but Q3 FY26 weakness (-15.4%) suggests either renewed inventory adjustment or genuine consumption softening — not confirmed as cleared.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] Sequential CEO changes each reset execution credibility; Lewis inherits declining medium-term guidance, rising leverage, and no visibility on US volume inflection — three unknowns simultaneously.

§2 — Change in Leadership

  • Net verdict: [#J] Diageo has had three CEOs in under two years — unparalleled among large-cap spirits peers — and the permanent appointee (Dave Lewis, Jan-26) is an outsider with no spirits sector experience, deployed for operational turnaround skills.
  • Ivan Menezes legacy: [#I] Menezes died in office Jun-23 having led Diageo for a decade; the LAC channel overstocking problem was incubated under his watch during the post-COVID demand surge; FY22 (+21.4% organic) was the peak of his tenure.
  • Debra Crew (Jul-23 — Jul-25): [#I] First female CEO; prior roles at Reynolds American and BAT; launched the Accelerate programme but could not prevent the Nov-23 LAC profit warning, FY24 H1 miss, or deteriorating US trends; departed "by mutual agreement" after ~24 months.
  • Nik Jhangiani (interim CEO Jul-25 — Dec-25): [#I] CFO since 2024; steered FY25 full-year results (Aug-25) and communicated the Accelerate programme; returned to CFO role when Lewis took the helm; Deirdre Mahlan served as interim CFO during the bridging period.
  • Sir Dave Lewis (CEO from Jan-26): [#A] Chair of Haleon (2022-25), non-exec at PepsiCo; famous for leading Tesco's recovery from the 2014 accounting scandal via operational overhaul, margin rebuild, and supplier renegotiation; zero premium spirits sector experience.
  • Lewis mandate signals: [#A] The board has explicitly bought a cost-discipline and operational overhaul archetype, not a brand-building or M&A architect; this frames the next 24 months as margin and FCF recovery, not revenue acceleration — a significant strategic framing for EPS trajectory expectations.
  • Second-level management: [#I] North America route-to-market restructuring under Accelerate has produced regional management changes; standalone market teams (e.g., Southern Europe) created to sharpen local responsiveness; change fatigue is a genuine execution risk in mid-implementation.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] Lewis's Tesco recovery playbook (lean inventory, supplier pressure, margin engineering) may not transfer cleanly to a premiumisation business where consumer pull, not shelf efficiency, is the operating lever — sector fit is an untested hypothesis.

§3 — Change in Strategy / Structure

  • Net verdict: [#J] Accelerate is the most substantive strategic pivot since the 2015 efficiency programme; the logic (focus, cost, route-to-market fix) is sound but $625m savings over three years is modest relative to the $4bn+ operating profit base, and LAC is the key test of structural vs cyclical repair.
  • Accelerate programme overview: [#I] Launched May-25; $500m initial target raised to $625m over three years (FY26-28); savings to be reinvested in A&P rather than dropped to earnings; $3bn annual FCF commitment from FY26.
  • Cost saving mechanisms: [#A] A&P optimisation via AI-driven content production; supply chain and logistics efficiencies; organisational simplification reducing overhead layers; North America reorg creating fewer but larger market teams.
  • Portfolio rationalisation: [#I] Moving from a "50+ brand" model toward a focused core; 13 "billion-dollar" brands as anchor (Crew's Feb-25 language); Casamigos, Crown Royal, Baileys, Smirnoff form the mass-premium tier below Don Julio and Johnnie Walker.
  • Route-to-market — LAC fix: [#A] LAC failure was a distribution management failure — over-ordering incentivised by distributor economics; Diageo's fix included new inventory tracking technology, revised distributor agreements, and sell-through frequency increases; LAC returned to organic growth in FY25, suggesting the structural repair is operative.
  • Route-to-market — North America: [#I] New decentralised market team model with standalone US commercial teams by channel; designed to sharpen on-trade vs off-trade execution vs the historical unified structure.
  • India strategy: [#A] United Spirits (~55% Diageo economic interest) focused on premiumisation of mass-market consumers into Prestige & Above; INR 27,600 Cr annual revenue; EBITDA margin 17.8% (FY25), +181bps YoY; excise complexity at state level creates quarterly volatility.
  • China baijiu (Shui Jing Fang): [#I] ~60% stake in Sichuan Swellfun; FY26 Q1 shows double-digit organic declines; board member resigned amid executive turnover (Aug-25) — adds governance uncertainty to commercial weakness.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] Accelerate's reinvestment component means near-term reported margin benefit is limited; if US recovery is slower than assumed, savings may be redirected to earnings support — creating a treadmill rather than a flywheel.

§4 — Change in Priorities / Ambition

  • Net verdict: [#J] Priority framing has shifted from "premium growth at scale" (Menezes era) to "stabilise, fix execution, rebuild cash" — a significant derating of medium-term ambition that the market has partially priced but may not have fully internalised.
  • Medium-term guidance removal (Feb-25): [#A] Diageo withdrew its medium-term organic net sales growth guidance (previously 5-7% CAGR) citing US tariff uncertainty; this was a meaningful credibility reset — guidance withdrawal at major peers (Pernod also withdrew) is a sector-level signal, but the optics for Diageo were particularly poor given two prior guidance cuts.
  • FY26 guidance: [#I] Organic net sales -2% to -3% for full-year FY26; organic operating profit growth expected at low-to-mid-single-digit (margin benefit from cost savings); Q1 FY26 showed -2.2% organic, described as "not satisfied" by Jhangiani.
  • Lewis's "mass market" signal: [#A] Lewis articulated a "mass market" focus (Spirits Business, Feb-26) — signals intention to re-engage value-oriented consumers alongside premium; broadens the playing field but risks diluting the premium brand positioning that justifies valuation multiples.
  • Capital allocation shift: [#A] ❌ Share buyback effectively suspended (no new programme at FY25); dividend held flat in nominal terms at 103.48 cents (FY24 and FY25); contrasts with Pernod's continued buyback; cash generation prioritised for deleveraging toward 2.5-3.0x by FY28.
  • CMD signals: [#I] No formal Capital Markets Day scheduled in the near term; Lewis appointment (Jan-26) likely precedes a CMD update in H2 CY26 — a potential positive catalyst or negative reset event once Lewis has assessed the business.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] Lewis's operational and mass-market framing risks repositioning Diageo as an efficiency story rather than a brand compounding story — market re-rating to a lower P/E on that framing could offset any EPS recovery.

§5 — Financial Profile Change

  • Net verdict: [#J] The financial profile has materially deteriorated from peak FY22: organic growth collapsed, leverage rose above target, buyback suspended — but FCF generation remains real ($3bn target from FY26) and operating margin is being defended, making this a cash story not a growth story for the next 24 months.
  • Organic revenue growth track record: [#I] FY22: +21.4%; FY23: +6.5%; FY24: -1.4%; FY25: +1.7%; FY26 guidance: -2% to -3%; a ~23 percentage point peak-to-trough compression in four years — among the worst decelerations in large-cap consumer staples vs Pernod, Campari, or Brown-Forman peers.
  • Net sales absolute: [#I] FY25 net sales $20,245m; FY24 $20,269m; FY23 $20,555m; revenue base broadly stable in USD terms, masking operational deterioration in constant currency.
  • Operating profit: [#I] FY25 operating profit $4,335m; implied operating margin (~21%) is broadly resilient, reflecting management's ability to defend gross margin through price/mix — a genuine positive vs mass-market staples peers.
  • Leverage: [#A] ❌ Net debt/EBITDA at 3.4x at Jun-25 (above 2.5-3.0x target); target return to range by FY28; creep reflects weaker EBITDA denominator rather than dramatic debt expansion, but 3.4x at a cyclical trough is not a comfort position.
  • FCF conversion: [#A] Diageo is historically an excellent FCF converter (capex/sales ~5-6%, working capital discipline); the $3bn annual FCF target from FY26 is plausible if operating profit stabilises; maturation inventory (Scotch casks) does not impair near-term cash.
  • Dividend: [#I] Flat at 103.48 cents per share FY24 and FY25; the flat FY25 outcome signals management chose cash preservation over growing returns — a rational but psychologically negative signal for income investors.
  • Share buyback vs peers: [#A] ❌ Diageo's buyback paused; Pernod Ricard and Campari maintained returns programmes through the downcycle; Diageo's suspension reflects leverage constraint — a relative negative for total shareholder return in a flat-sales environment.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] The $3bn FCF target is achievable only if US volumes stabilise and Accelerate savings land on time; if Q3 FY26's -15% US organic run rate persists, FCF guidance becomes another downward revision — leverage stays elevated and dividend security becomes a conversation.

§6 — Value Chain: Demand

  • Net verdict: [#J] Demand is bifurcated — Guinness and Don Julio are genuine consumer-pull stories; Johnnie Walker, Casamigos, and Smirnoff face volume headwinds from category softness, downtrading, and competitive intensity; aggregate is flat-to-slightly-declining until US normalisation arrives.
  • US spirits market: [#A] The US is the epicentre of post-COVID destocking; Q3 FY26 US spirits organic -15.4% for Diageo — a material setback to the "recovery imminent" thesis; the five largest listed spirits producers hold ~$22bn in unsold aged inventory (Robb Report); not a clean destocking completion signal.
  • CQ-C1 — LAC destocking: [#A] LAC declared functionally complete; region returned to organic growth in FY25 after -23% H1 FY24 collapse; new inventory tracking systems and revised distributor agreements are the structural fix; the region is now cyclically recovering.
  • Guinness: [#I] ⚡ Guinness is Diageo's single brightest demand signal — UK on-trade #1 beer brand (one in nine pints poured), global volume +14% FY25 lapping +5% prior year; recruiting female and younger (18-34) drinkers at an accelerating rate (+11pp usage since prior year); Guinness 0.0 net sales doubled in FY24.
  • Don Julio: [#I] Largest-selling US spirits brand by retail value (~$2.6bn); Don Julio Reposado drove +41.9% net sales in FY24; #2 overall tequila by case volume, gaining on Patrón; luxury tequila consumer demonstrably less price-sensitive.
  • Casamigos: [#A] ❌ Net sales -18% in FY24; Blanco-tier positioning faces most intense tequila competition; RTD launch (Casamigos Margarita) is damage limitation; the decline from the 2017 $1bn acquisition price continues.
  • Johnnie Walker: [#A] ❌ Organic net sales -10.6% FY25, volume -3%; Scotch category under pressure from trade-down and US consumer preference shift toward tequila/bourbon; Blue Label was a positive performer, partially offsetting Red/Black weakness.
  • CQ-C4 — Flywheel or treadmill on JW: [#A] Brand equity not eroding structurally — age-stated expressions maintain premium credentials — but cyclical volumes are soft; flywheel thesis requires premium/ultra-premium expressions (Blue, Odyssey) to compensate for mass-end weakness.
  • CQ-C2 — China: [#A] Greater China weakness driven primarily by Shui Jing Fang baijiu (~3-4% of group); Q1 FY26 double-digit decline driven by "deep structural adjustment" in baijiu market — anti-extravagance policy suppressing business banquet consumption; no near-term recovery catalyst visible.
  • India: [#I] United Spirits revenue INR 27,600 Cr (FY25); Prestige & Above NSV +16.1% in Q3 FY25; EBITDA margin +181bps; India is the structural growth offset — but INR volatility and state excise complexity constrain earnings conversion.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] Guinness and Don Julio are insufficient to offset JW and US-general weakness at group level; if US volumes do not recover in H2 FY26-H1 FY27, Diageo posts negative organic growth for a third consecutive fiscal year — qualitative inflection risking further multiple de-rating.

§7 — Value Chain: Competition

  • Net verdict: [#J] Diageo faces a more intense competitive environment than at any point in the past decade — tequila overcrowded, Scotch under structural pressure, and Pernod is executing a recovery ahead of Diageo's timeline — relative performance risk is elevated.
  • Pernod Ricard: [#A] Direct head-to-head on Scotch (Chivas vs JW, Ballantine's vs Black Label) and gin (Beefeater vs Tanqueray); Pernod showed early return to organic sales growth in Apr-26 (The Drinks Business), ahead of Diageo's still-negative FY26 trajectory; Pernod's 2027-29 guidance of 3-6% sales growth implies faster recovery curve.
  • Brown-Forman (Jack Daniel's): [#I] Jack Daniel's competes directly with JW Black in the accessible premium tier; domestically-produced US whiskey avoids the 10% UK tariff that hits Scotch — a meaningful competitive cost asymmetry favouring Brown-Forman in the US market.
  • Bacardi (Patrón): [#I] Bacardi's private status means sustained tequila investment without short-term earnings pressure; Patrón is the #1 ultra-premium tequila by volume; Don Julio has closed the gap significantly but competes in the same super-premium tier — private company patience is a structural disadvantage for listed competitors.
  • Tequila competition — Casamigos tier: [#A] ❌ Casamigos is losing share in the Blanco ($30-50) tier to a proliferation of celebrity-backed brands; 200+ brand launches in tequila since 2018; at mass-premium price points the shelf is saturated; Don Julio's strength in ultra-premium ($60-100) is more defensible.
  • RTD competition: [#I] Diageo's branded RTD portfolio (Crown Royal Whisky Sour, Ketel One Espresso Martini, Casamigos Margarita) competes with AB InBev, Boston Beer, and White Claw in a channel where brand loyalty is lower and price sensitivity higher — distribution relationships matter more than brand premiumisation.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] Diageo's competitive moat has not disappeared, but the FY22 outperformance was partly a rising-tide phenomenon; Pernod's recovery trajectory and Brown-Forman's US tariff advantage both suggest relative pressure is unlikely to abate quickly.

§8 — Value Chain: Disruption

  • Net verdict: [#J] Disruption forces are real but incremental — GLP-1 is a monitoring risk not a current-year headwind; no/low alcohol is a channel Diageo is sensibly entering; RTDs are an opportunity; the more immediate disruptive pressure is cultural (lifestyle moderation) rather than pharmaceutical.
  • GLP-1 — current assessment: [#A] Diageo's own US research (FY25 disclosures) finds GLP-1s have "not yet shown significant disruption to spirits consumption"; the mechanism — reduced appetite-driven alcohol consumption — is plausible but undemonstrated at population scale; management classifies GLP-1 as a monitoring item.
  • GLP-1 — sell-side framing: [#A] Multiple sell-side notes specifically name Diageo in GLP-1 headwind analysis; in a scenario of 20-30% GLP-1 adoption among US spirits-drinking demographics, a 2-5% structural consumption headwind is a reasonable estimate — but a 10+ year tail, not a three-year issue.
  • No/low alcohol: [#I] Diageo's no/low portfolio grew ~56% in FY25, including Seedlip, Ritual Zero Proof (acquired Sep-24, #1 US no-alcohol spirit), Gordon's 0.0, Tanqueray 0.0, Guinness 0.0; the portfolio is a hedge against lifestyle moderation rather than a material revenue driver at current scale.
  • RTD cocktails: [#I] IWSR expects spirits-based RTDs to have the fastest growth rate through 2028; Diageo is well positioned with branded RTD extensions; spirits-based RTD growth is accretive to addressable market, not a substitution risk.
  • Cannabis substitution: [#I] US recreational cannabis legalisation expanding; management assesses alcohol and cannabis as more complementary than substituting among relevant cohorts; a 5-10 year uncertainty, not a near-term catalyst.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] Diageo's disruption response (no/low, RTD) is competent but not differentiated — every major spirits and beer company is executing a near-identical playbook; if lifestyle moderation accelerates faster than base-case, the no/low portfolio is insufficient to offset structural volume losses in core whisky and tequila.

§9 — Value Chain: Political / Regulatory

  • Net verdict: [#J] The regulatory environment has deteriorated on multiple axes simultaneously — US tariffs, India excise complexity, WHO health messaging escalation, UK duty increases — making this the most politically hostile environment Diageo has faced in a decade.
  • US tariffs — Scotch whisky: [#A] ❌ 10% tariff on UK imports (post Liberation Day, Apr-25); Diageo estimates ~$200m annual cost; mitigating ~50% through inventory management and supply chain adjustments; net impact ~$100m (~2.5% of group operating profit); Trump signal on Scotch tariff removal (May-26) is a meaningful potential positive catalyst.
  • US tariffs — broader spirits: [#I] 15% tariff on European imports (affects Tanqueray, Captain Morgan from EU sites); tariffs on Canadian spirits (Crown Royal produced in Canada); multi-country exposure requires a complex mitigation response.
  • UK alcohol duty: [#I] UK government duty escalator (duty above inflation annually) is a structural headwind for Guinness and UK-sold spirits; Scotland's minimum unit pricing (50p/unit) subject to regular review — a potential constraint on on-trade volume if raised.
  • India excise complexity: [#A] Each of 28+ Indian states sets its own excise duty, distribution rules, and retail licensing; excise policy shifts in key states (UP, Maharashtra, Telangana) can materially shift NSV in a single quarter; a recurring structural uncertainty in an otherwise strong growth market.
  • WHO and health messaging: [#I] WHO's 2023 statement that "no level of alcohol consumption is safe for health" is the most aggressive global health messaging ever directed at the category; 15+ countries are mandating health warning labels on alcohol packaging; a 10-20 year structural demand suppression concern.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] Tariff risk is bidirectional — the May-26 Scotch tariff removal signal is genuine but US political reversibility is high; a reversal to 25% tariffs (pre-Biden settlement level) would cost ~$500m annualised — a material downside scenario not fully off the table.

§10 — Value Chain: Supply Side

  • Net verdict: [#J] Diageo's supply side is structurally advantaged — 10m+ Scotch casks representing decades of irreplaceable competitive moat — but faces near-term headwinds from soft Scotch demand into an abundant inventory position and ongoing glass/logistics cost normalisation.
  • Scotch maturation — structural moat: [#I] ⚡ Diageo holds over 10 million casks of maturing Scotch — the world's largest maturing inventory; minimum 3 years (standard) to 25+ years (aged malts) to produce; this asset cannot be replicated in under a decade by any competitor, making aged expressions a structurally defensible margin pool.
  • Scotch whisky — current demand challenge: [#A] Despite the maturation moat, Scotch net sales fell ~5% overall in FY25; Johnnie Walker organic -10.6%; the casks are maturing into a soft demand market — supply advantage provides optionality for the premium recovery, not near-term EPS protection.
  • Agave — tequila supply cycle: [#A] Agave supply cycle runs 7-9 years from planting to harvest; the tequila boom 2017-22 drove extraordinary plantings now maturing into supply abundance; agave raw material prices have fallen sharply from 2022 peaks — a modest gross margin tailwind for Don Julio and Casamigos in FY26-27.
  • Grain neutral spirit and glass: [#I] GNS prices normalised post-2022 energy crisis (modest positive for Smirnoff, Gordon's, Captain Morgan); glass supply normalised after 2021-22 constraints — neither is a notable headwind.
  • Logistics and energy: [#I] Ocean freight rates (Red Sea disruption 2024) normalising; energy costs in Scotland elevated vs pre-2022 but moderating from peaks; both included within Accelerate savings targets.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] The Scotch maturation moat is a long-duration asset — genuinely valuable in 2030-40 — but provides zero comfort in a FY26-27 demand trough; agave cost tailwind is real but modest; supply-side advantages do not protect EPS when demand is weak.

§11 — Revenue / Demand Cycle

  • Net verdict: [#J] Diageo is in the late stage of the LAC destocking cycle but mid-cycle in the US — the critical distinction is that LAC was a channel-mechanics problem (now fixed) while US reflects both destocking and genuine demand softening, with no clear inflection date visible from current data.
  • CQ-C5 — Fulcrum driver for 18-36M EPS: [#J] US spirits volume normalisation is the single largest swing factor; North America ~35% of Diageo net sales; even a +5% organic recovery from a -10% base would add ~$150-200m to operating profit — material relative to current consensus EPS growth expectations.
  • US recovery timing scenarios: [#A] Base case: US organic growth turns positive in H2 FY27 on easy comparatives and normalised trade inventory. Bear case: weak US consumer and tariff-driven price increases (Diageo passing through ~50% of tariff cost) delay inflection to FY28. Bull case: tariff removal and inventory rebuild drive a sharp inflection in H2 FY26.
  • Global destocking context: [#A] Industry-wide $22bn inventory glut (five largest producers); LAC fix complete; US advanced but Q3 FY26 data (-15.4% US organic) suggests either a second inventory correction wave or underlying demand deterioration superimposed on destocking — a critical analytical fork current data cannot resolve.
  • Guinness demand cycle: [#I] Guinness is not a destocking story — it is consumer pull; UK on-trade demand is recurring (pub visits), not inventory-driven; international Guinness (US, Africa, SEA) is in an earlier growth phase; this demand stream is as structurally durable as any in Diageo's portfolio.
  • China cycle (CQ-C2): [#A] Baijiu recovery is H2 CY26 at earliest, materially dependent on policy reversal regarding business banquet suppression; Scotch in China (secondary exposure) affected by same caution around business entertaining; not within management's control to accelerate.
  • India secular trend: [#A] India is a counter-cyclical positive — Prestige & Above NSV +16.1% Q3 FY25; the risk is excise policy shifts (state-level unpredictability) rather than demand; India has absorbed some of the US/LAC drag in group FY25 numbers.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] The destocking cycle thesis has been advanced for three consecutive fiscal years by bulls; each year a new headwind reset the recovery timetable — LAC in FY24, US in FY25, US+tariffs+China in FY26; pattern recognition here should make investors highly sceptical of any near-term inflection narrative absent volume data confirmation.

§12 — Value Chain: Macroeconomic

  • Net verdict: [#J] The macro environment is the most significant aggregate headwind since 2009 — US consumer pressure, EM currency weakness, energy cost legacy, and tariffs create a simultaneous multi-market squeeze masking any underlying demand recovery.
  • USD/GBP sensitivity: [#A] Diageo reports in USD; Scotch whisky, Guinness, and UK operations generate significant GBP revenue; GBP appreciation is a reported headwind; FY25 FX was a material adverse factor on reported vs organic growth differential.
  • Emerging market currencies — LAC: [#A] ❌ BRL, MXN, CLP, ARS are highly volatile; the 2023 LAC volume issue was compounded by currency devaluation; Argentina inflation makes reported numbers particularly volatile; Diageo's LAC exposure is predominantly local-currency priced with limited natural hedging.
  • US consumer: [#A] ❌ Sub-$50k US household income cohort demonstrably under pressure; spirits price points up 15-20% since 2021; creating a two-speed market — ultra-premium (Don Julio Reposado at $70+) resilient, mid-premium (Casamigos Blanco, JW Black at $45) under volume pressure — maps exactly onto Diageo's winner/loser brand bifurcation.
  • UK consumer and pub recovery: [#I] UK real wages recovered from 2022-23 inflation peak; post-COVID pub return trend is durable; Guinness is the primary beneficiary — partially insulates this revenue stream from broader UK consumer weakness.
  • Inflation and price pass-through (CQ-C3): [#A] Diageo raised prices aggressively 2021-23; initial volume/price trade-off was favourable (pricing power demonstrated at ultra-premium); by FY24 consumer resistance in the mass-premium tier was clear; Don Julio demonstrates durable pricing power; JW Black and Casamigos are the litmus test — current volumes suggest mid-premium pricing power has limits.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] A macro recovery (US rate cuts, consumer confidence rebound) is the scenario that re-rates Diageo fastest — but that recovery has been "12 months away" since mid-2024 and Q3 FY26 US organic -15.4% continues to push the inflection date rightward.

§13 — Historical Track Record of Delivery

  • Net verdict: [#J] Diageo's credibility is at a historic low — three CEO transitions, two major guidance cuts in 12 months, and FY26 negative organic guidance after FY25 was framed as recovery; the re-rating requires earned trust through delivery, not communication.
  • FY14-FY22 clean track record: [#I] Diageo delivered consistent mid-single-digit organic growth through the Menezes decade; operating margin expanded from ~27% to ~29%; reliable FCF, annual dividend growth, and 2.5-3.0x leverage discipline — a genuinely strong 8-year delivery record.
  • The LAC guidance catastrophe: [#A] ❌ Nov-23 intra-year trading update: first major guidance breach in a decade; management initially described LAC as "an isolated issue" (The Drinks Business, Nov-23); H1 FY24 then showed -23% organic LAC — the two-step (minimise then miss again) is the most damaging pattern for institutional investor trust.
  • Management vs external causation: [#A] LAC overstocking was substantially a management execution failure (poor inventory visibility, distributor incentive misalignment), not a pure external demand shock; US destocking is more legitimately external (post-COVID normalisation); conflating both as external would be an error — the market has not confused them.
  • Crew's first year — net assessment: [#A] Delivered FY25 at +1.7% organic (technically an improvement); launched Accelerate; withdrew medium-term guidance (Feb-25); FY26 launched negatively at -2.2% Q1; the totality is stabilisation without recovery — insufficient to restore credibility.
  • Dave Lewis — inherited baseline: [#A] Lewis takes over with three consecutive years of organic growth below FY22, leverage above target, buyback suspended, and FY26 starting negative; the credibility restoration baseline is simply delivering stated FY26 guidance (-2% to -3%) and $3bn FCF — low relative to historical ambition but achievable.
  • Peer comparison on credibility: [#A] Pernod Ricard, Brown-Forman, and Rémy Cointreau have all gone through guidance resets on China/US; Diageo is not uniquely culpable but its higher profile and market cap make each miss more visible and more penalised in the multiple.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] The board has now reset the narrative twice (Crew's appointment, Lewis's appointment); the market has seen multiple "fresh start" reframings in 24 months; unless H2 FY27 delivers demonstrable US volume recovery (not just mix/price), credibility rebuilding remains theoretical.

Aggregate Weak Signals / Downside (❌)

  • US volumes accelerating lower in FY26: [#J] Q3 FY26 US spirits organic -15.4% is materially worse than the destocking narrative implies; if this reflects genuine demand destruction (consumer downtrading, lifestyle moderation), the inflection to positive growth recedes to FY28 or beyond — the core bull thesis breaks.
  • Leverage above target with constrained path to deleverage: [#A] ❌ At 3.4x net debt/EBITDA vs 2.5x target, balance sheet flexibility is constrained; buyback remains suspended; further EBITDA deterioration in FY26 pushes leverage toward 3.5-3.8x — a level at which rating agency discussions begin.
  • Third consecutive CEO in two years: [#J] ❌ Dave Lewis has no spirits sector experience; change fatigue from three CEO transitions in 24 months is a genuine execution risk; Accelerate mid-implementation leadership change risks slowing North America market team decision-making at a critical commercial juncture.
  • China baijiu — no policy catalyst visible: [#A] ❌ Greater China double-digit organic decline in FY26 Q1; Shui Jing Fang board member resignation adds governance noise; China exposure ~3-4% of group is modest but directionally negative with no visible recovery horizon.
  • Casamigos brand value erosion: [#A] ❌ Acquired for $1bn in 2017; net sales -18% in FY24; Blanco-tier tequila is the most competitive segment; a formal impairment or strategic review of Casamigos would be a negative headline and potential earnings risk.
