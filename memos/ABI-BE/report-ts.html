Section 2 – The 80/20 (bottom line up front) 8 findings

Section summary
  1. AB InBev offers a highly predictable earnings trajectory driven by structural pricing power in emerging markets and a repaired balance sheet that unlocks massive capital returns.
1.
Unrivaled brewing scale: The business generates revenue by manufacturing and distributing over 500 beverage brands across 150 countries, leveraging immense supply chain scale to achieve industry-leading profit margins
2.
Inflecting investment thesis: The core investment case rests on the company's transition from a decade-long deleveraging cycle to an era of accelerated shareholder returns. With net debt now below 3.0x EBITDA, the business has initiated a $6 billion share buyback program that provides a highly visible floor for multi-year earnings growth
3.
Critical margin driver: The single most important swing factor for forward earnings is the company's ability to execute price-mix increases in Latin America to offset volatile local currency depreciation and raw material inflation
4.
Resilient recent execution: Despite a challenging macro environment, the company delivered a robust 5.3% organic EBITDA increase in the first quarter of 2026, proving its ability to drive profit expansion through premiumization even when aggregate volumes remain flat
5.
Historic leverage reduction: Management has successfully deleveraged the balance sheet from a peak of 5.6x net debt to EBITDA following the SABMiller acquisition down to a sustainable 2.87x by the end of 2025
6.
Primary structural threat: Persistent structural volume declines in the Chinese on-premise market and potential stagnation in US market share recovery represent the most significant risks capable of breaking the compounding earnings algorithm
7.
Constructive first-look verdict: Yes, a deeper investigation is warranted because the current valuation discount fails to reflect the underlying cash generation and the newly unencumbered balance sheet

Section 3 – What the company does 13 findings

Section summary
  1. The company operates the most profitable platform in the global brewing industry, utilizing unmatched scale and an expanding digital B2B ecosystem to extract superior economics across its value chain.

Core business and segmentation

1.
Diversified product hierarchy: The company segments its vast portfolio into premium megabrands like Stella Artois and Corona, mainstream local champions, and a rapidly expanding Beyond Beer division that captures shifting consumer preferences
2.
Accelerating adjacent categories: Management has aggressively expanded into high-margin adjacent spaces, driving a 23% revenue increase in the Beyond Beer segment during 2025 led by spirits-based ready-to-drink brands like Cutwater
3.
Strategic moderation pivot: Responding to structural health trends, the company has heavily invested in no-alcohol and low-alcohol beverages, achieving a 34% revenue increase in its non-alcohol beer portfolio led by Corona Cero
4.
Digital ecosystem monetization: The business has fundamentally transformed its route-to-market through the BEES digital B2B platform, which now acts as a high-margin transactional marketplace capturing $1.1 billion in quarterly gross merchandise value from third-party products

Geographic footprint and profit pools

5.
Emerging market profit engine: The company derives approximately 70% of its EBITDA from emerging and developing markets, positioning it perfectly to capture the vast majority of projected global beer volume growth through 2029
6.
Latin American dominance: Operations in Central and South America, primarily executed through its 62% stake in Ambev, account for approximately 37% of the group's equity-adjusted beer profit, supported by an oligopolistic market structure that affords exceptional pricing power
7.
Consolidated value chain position: The company sits at the most lucrative node of the beverage value chain, utilizing its massive procurement scale to suppress input costs while leveraging its brand equity to command premium shelf space from retail customers
8.
Customer concentration dynamics: The customer base is highly fragmented globally but concentrated locally, spanning millions of independent bars and restaurants in emerging markets to consolidated mega-retailers in North America

Competitive advantages and industry structure

9.
Scale-driven cost moat: The company's durable competitive advantage is rooted in economies of scale across manufacturing, marketing, and distribution, which allows it to generate significantly higher operating margins than its closest peers Heineken and Carlsberg
10.
Consolidated mature markets: The global brewing industry is highly consolidated at the top, with AB InBev alone accounting for roughly 25% of global beer net revenue and an outsized 36% of global beer EBIT
11.
Fragmented premium competition: Despite top-level consolidation, the industry faces intense fragmentation at the premium edge, forcing the company to continually acquire or launch craft and specialty brands to defend market share against agile local entrants
12.
Other side of the story: The company's sheer size makes it practically impossible to grow through transformational M&A moving forward, forcing total reliance on organic volume growth in markets where consumer alcohol consumption per capita is structurally declining

Growth and margin recovery

1.
Margin recovery: The normalized EBITDA margin has steadily expanded, reaching approximately 35.7% in 2025, as relentless premiumization and stringent overhead management successfully offset severe input cost inflation

Balance sheet repair and latest results

7.

8.

Section 5 – The possible investment case 11 findings

Section summary
  1. The investment case significantly strengthens as the completed deleveraging cycle guarantees massive capital returns, while emerging market premiumization creates a predictable floor for organic earnings growth.

The capital return inflection

1.
Predictable share repurchases: The primary driver of forward EPS growth is the transition to aggressive capital returns, explicitly defined by a newly authorized $6 billion share buyback program spanning the next 24 months
2.
Capital return plausibility: Plausibility is exceptionally high because the balance sheet repair is already mathematically complete, meaning free cash flow is no longer trapped by mandatory debt amortization and can flow directly to equity holders
3.
Failure mechanism for capital returns: This driver would fail if an unexpected global macroeconomic shock forced the company to pause buybacks to defend its credit rating, or if management pursued another massive, debt-fueled acquisition against current stated strategy

Emerging market pricing and premiumization

4.
Megabrand price-mix engine: The second key driver touches the top line, where global megabrands like Stella Artois and Corona command immense pricing power, having grown revenue at a 10% compound annual rate since 2021 to represent 57% of total sales
5.
Pricing plausibility: Plausibility is high as the company has empirically demonstrated its ability to push price increases slightly ahead of inflation across Latin America over the past decade without destroying brand equity
6.
Failure mechanism for pricing: This driver breaks if severe local recessions in Brazil or Mexico force consumers to abruptly trade down to discount brands, collapsing the gross margin structure before the company can adjust its cost base

North American stabilization

7.
US market share recovery: The final earnings driver is the stabilization of the highly profitable North American segment, where brands like Michelob Ultra are consistently gaining share to offset the historical drag from the Bud Light controversy
8.
US recovery plausibility: Plausibility is medium; while Michelob Ultra is successfully expanding, the broader US beer industry remains in a structural low-single-digit volume decline due to demographic shifts and the rise of spirits
9.
Compound probability requirement: The bull case does not require all drivers to fire simultaneously; the sheer mathematical force of the $6 billion buyback guarantees EPS accretion even if organic volume growth in North America remains sluggish
10.
Other side of the story: If global beer volumes enter a terminal structural decline faster than premiumization can raise per-unit revenues, the resulting loss of manufacturing operating leverage will crush EBITDA margins regardless of the share count reduction

Section 7 – Key risks and the other side 9 findings

Section summary
  1. The risk profile is heavily weighted toward uncontrollable macroeconomic variables, specifically raw material inflation and emerging market currency volatility, which constantly threaten to erode hard-won margin gains.

Input cost inflation and FX volatility

1.
Aluminum supply chain exposure: The most material transmission mechanism for margin compression is the rising spot price of aluminum, compounded by the US Midwest premium, which directly inflates the cost of goods sold for canned beverages globally
2.
Transactional currency mismatches: Because approximately 50-55% of Ambev's cost of goods in Brazil are denominated in US dollars, any sudden devaluation of the Brazilian Real immediately crushes gross margins before local price increases can catch up
3.
Dividend taxation threat: A looming regulatory risk involves proposed changes to Brazilian tax law that could impose a 10% withholding tax on dividends, directly threatening the cash flow AB InBev extracts from its massive Ambev subsidiary

Structural volume declines

4.
Chinese market deterioration: Earnings face persistent headwinds from China, where government crackdowns on entertainment spending and weak consumer confidence have triggered structural volume declines in the highly profitable on-premise channels
5.
The critical failure mode: The single failure mode that would most damage the investment case is a simultaneous recession in the US and Mexico, which together represent the vast majority of the company's high-margin profit pool
6.
False friend signal: Record high nominal revenue in inflationary markets like Argentina acts as a dangerous "false friend," optically masking severe underlying volume deterioration and the destruction of real purchasing power
7.
Early warning monitor: Investors must closely monitor US Nielsen and Circana scanner data every four weeks, as any sudden deceleration in Michelob Ultra's growth would signal that the North American stabilization thesis is failing
8.
Other side of the story: The sheer geographic diversity of the company's profit pools creates a natural hedge, ensuring that localized recessions or currency shocks in one region are frequently offset by premiumization and growth in another

Section 8 – Management: what is changing 5 findings

Section summary
  1. Leadership is currently undergoing a planned, orderly generational transition that signals continuity in strategic execution rather than disruptive corporate restructuring.
1.
Board leadership transition: Chairman Martin J. Barrington will retire after the 2026 annual meeting due to age limits, with former Altria CEO William F. Gifford Jr. proposed to fill the restricted share director seat, signaling continued stability at the board level
2.
Human resources overhaul: Following a 30-year tenure, Chief People Officer Nelson Jamel stepped down and was replaced by Thiago Porto, reflecting a desire to modernize organizational design and improve employee engagement specifically across the Americas
3.
Regional executive reshuffle: The company executed a major swap of its top operational talent, moving Carlos Lisboa to CEO of Ambev while transitioning Jean Jereissati to lead the massive Middle Americas Zone, ensuring cross-pollination of best practices across crucial markets
4.
Leadership stability verdict: Aside from these orderly regional and board-level successions, the core C-suite under CEO Michel Doukeris remains highly stable with no material disruptions threatening the current strategic trajectory
