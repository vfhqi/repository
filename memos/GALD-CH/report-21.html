Methodology pre-staged:

  • Second-order: for any first-order finding in §2–§N, the second-order implication is the downstream effect on a different dimension — e.g., if market structure is more concentrated than consensus implies, the second-order effect is on pricing power, which flows through to EBIT margin trajectory
  • Third-order: third-order implications typically cross pillars — e.g., a regulatory tailwind that lifts near-term earnings (P&L pillar) may simultaneously raise ESG/governance risk (quality pillar) if it is perceived as regulatory capture
  • Cross-pillar: any KQ that resolves in a direction that is bullish on one pillar and bearish on another creates a cross-pillar tension; APM must explicitly arbitrate these tensions in the investment decision
  • Cross-stock: in a concentrated 5-15 position portfolio, a KQ resolution that changes the thesis for one position may have read-across implications for adjacent positions in the same sector or thematic
  • Cross-thematic: thematic exposures (e.g., AI capex cycle, energy transition, demographic shift) often create correlated KQ resolutions across multiple positions; surfacing these correlations is a second-order risk management input

M/M/O applications (pre-staged — ≥2 required by gate Q21-SO):

  • M/M/O application 1 (pre-staged): once the KQ is defined, the analyst should identify the actor most likely to drive the KQ toward the non-consensus resolution and apply the means/motive/opportunity test — does this actor have the resources (means), the economic incentive (motive), and the structural position (opportunity) to drive the outcome? If all three are present, the non-consensus resolution deserves a higher prior probability than consensus assigns
  • M/M/O application 2 (pre-staged): a second M/M/O application should target the incumbent — does the company at the centre of the case have means + motive + opportunity to suppress, delay, or reframe the KQ resolution in a way that flatters the bull case? Management has strong motive (compensation, reputation) and often has opportunity (information asymmetry, investor relations control); the means test is the binding constraint
  • M/M/O application 3 (pre-staged): where the KQ involves a regulatory or competitive actor, the M/M/O test should be applied to that actor — regulators with means (enforcement power) + motive (political mandate) + opportunity (jurisdictional reach) represent a non-diversifiable risk that should not be discounted simply because it has not materialised in the historical period

Additional edge-detection bullets:

  • Cross-roads exposure: the Q21 template explicitly requires surfacing cross-roads exposures — structural intersections between dimensions that are not visible when each dimension is mapped in isolation; e.g., a technology disruption vector that is currently subcritical may become critical if combined with a regulatory tailwind that removes incumbent pricing protection
  • Power-of-incentives: management compensation structures, sell-side fee relationships, and index inclusion incentives all create systematic biases in the landscape data; surfacing these incentive distortions is a second-order analytical task
  • Inversion: the most useful second-order technique for DD-stage KQs is inversion — instead of asking "what would make the bull case true?", ask "what would have to be true for the bull case to fail catastrophically?"; if the inversion conditions are plausible, they should be reflected in scenario analysis
  • Bear asymmetry: at DD stage in a concentrated long-only portfolio, the cost of false-positive bull cases significantly exceeds the cost of false-negative bear cases — second-order implications should be weighted toward downside scenario completeness

Sceptical bullet: What second-order implication is the analyst flinching from? Per the obstacle is the way — surface it explicitly. At DD stage, the most common flinch is the implication that the KQ resolution, even if favourable, does not actually support the 25%+ IRR target because the resolution is already in the price. If the bull case requires a non-consensus KQ resolution AND that resolution is not yet in the price, the case is strong. If either condition fails, the case is weaker than the bull memo implies. The analyst should explicitly test whether the KQ resolution is (a) non-consensus and (b) not yet priced — both conditions must hold for the KQ to be load-bearing for the return case
