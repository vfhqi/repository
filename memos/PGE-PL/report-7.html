§1 — Executive Summary

PGE-PL sits at the intersection of European energy transition and Polish political economy — a utility undergoing structural transformation under fiscal stress. The driver landscape is genuinely multi-variable rather than single-fulcrum.

  • Driver list: Six key drivers identified — (1) wholesale power price trajectory, (2) Baltica offshore wind execution, (3) coal asset transition and capacity market support, (4) regulated networks CAPEX return, (5) balance sheet / financing cost management, (6) Polish macro and FX [#J]
  • Impact ranking: Driver #1 (power prices) has highest 24M EPS sensitivity; Driver #2 (Baltica) has highest 36M+ upside optionality but limited 24M cash impact; Driver #3 (coal transition) is the largest downside risk to free cash flow [#A]

§2 — Key Driver #1: Wholesale Power Price Trajectory

  • J-front verdict: Medium plausibility for price stabilisation at levels supporting thesis returns; single highest-sensitivity variable in the model, but structurally exposed to European gas + renewables dynamic beyond PGE's control [#J]

  • Driver definition: PGE generates ~55-60TWh annually from a mixed fleet (coal ~50%, gas, renewables, nuclear aspirations); wholesale PLN/MWh baseload price directly sets generation margin for the merchant/semi-merchant book [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Every PLN 10/MWh change in realised power price translates to ~PLN 300-500m EBITDA delta (management-disclosed sensitivity; ~15-20% of FY2024 consensus EBITDA of ~PLN 8-9bn) [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Market applies ~6-8x EV/EBITDA to generation segment; price-driven EBITDA swing of PLN 1bn implies ~PLN 6-8bn EV change on ~PLN 25bn market cap — material multiple of current market cap at stress scenarios [#A]

  • Company guide: PGE hedges forward production partially (typically 50-70% of next 12M output hedged); FY2025 guidance implicitly embeds Polish forward curve of ~PLN 380-420/MWh; management has not provided explicit power price sensitivity curves in recent public disclosures [#I]

  • Management commentary: CEO commentary at FY2023 results indicated "normalisation of energy prices" as expected; characterised 2022-23 as exceptional; guided for sustainable margins at lower price levels [#I]
  • Track record: PGE has consistently lagged in hedging efficiency vs Western European peers (e.g., EDF, RWE hedge 80-90% of near-term output); Polish forward market liquidity is thinner, limiting hedge depth [#I]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Guidance appears neither aggressive nor conservative — it reflects the forward curve, which itself may be optimistic if European gas prices continue declining [#A]

  • SS consensus: Sell-side models broadly embed PLN 350-430/MWh for FY2025-26; range is wide and broker-dependent; no single dominant sensitivity disclosure available from public sources [#I]

  • Consensus changes L12M: Net downward revisions as 2023-peak power prices normalised; FY2025 EBITDA consensus cut ~10-15% over L12M per public PT revision trail [#I]

  • Estimated EPS impact: FY2025 consensus EPS ~PLN 1.5-2.0/share; a 10% power price miss vs consensus implies ~PLN 0.3-0.5/share EPS reduction (~20-30% EPS downside from single variable) [#A]

  • Recent delivery: FY2023 generation EBITDA came in below plan as power prices fell from 2022 highs; Q3-Q4 2023 realised prices weakened; FY2024 partial recovery on grid fees and networks but generation EBITDA under pressure [#I]

  • Peer benchmark: RWE, ENGIE, and CEZ all face similar power price normalisation; CEZ (closest Polish/CEE peer) guided lower EBITDA for FY2024-25 citing same dynamic; PGE's coal-heavy mix makes it more exposed than renewables-weighted Western peers [#I]

  • Trend L12M: Decelerating — Polish baseload forward prices fell ~30% from 2023 peak; partial stabilisation in Q1 2025 but structural European electricity market shift ongoing [#I]

  • Macro tailwind: Any European gas supply disruption or cold-weather demand spike provides upside; EU carbon price floor supports clean dark spreads for coal fleet [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Accelerating European renewables buildout (Germany, Spain, Poland itself) structurally compresses marginal cost of power over 5Y horizon; gas glut scenario adds asymmetric downside [#A]
  • Competitive dynamics: Polish grid is increasingly integrated with European market; price-setting mechanism shifting toward renewables marginal cost as penetration increases [#A]

  • Visibility: Low — macro/commodity-driven; essentially outside management control beyond hedging programme [#J]

  • Plausibility assessment: Price stabilisation at consensus levels (~PLN 380-420/MWh) is plausible if gas prices hold; structural renewables pressure is a 3-5Y concern not a 24M event [#A]

  • Key assumptions: (a) No European gas glut in 2025-26; (b) Polish renewable capacity additions don't front-run grid integration; (c) ETS price holds above €60/t supporting coal profitability [#A]
  • Bear case: Gas oversupply + warm winters + accelerated Polish solar buildout pushes baseload below PLN 300/MWh; generation EBITDA collapses 40%+ and leverage breaches covenant levels [#J]
  • Miss magnitude: PLN 2-3bn EBITDA miss in stress scenario; at 7x EV/EBITDA implies ~PLN 14-21bn EV destruction on ~PLN 25bn market cap — existential for equity [#A]

  • Red flags: Q4 2023 realised prices already below FY2024 forward curve at time of guidance; hedging book roll-forward risk as 2022-vintage hedges expire [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet: Management's hedging programme provides 12-18M buffer but does not address structural price compression; the 24M window is exactly when 2022-23 vintage hedges expire and PGE faces full market price exposure [#J]

Plausibility verdict: medium plausibility — stabilisation at consensus-embedded prices is plausible but non-trivial; commodity exposure is the weakest link in the model and largest single EPS risk

§3 — Key Driver #2: Baltica Offshore Wind Programme Execution

  • J-front verdict: Strategically the most important long-term value driver, but 24M impact is option value not earnings — FID confirmation and financing de-risking are the near-term price catalysts, not revenue [#J]

  • Driver definition: PGE's Baltica 2 (1.5GW, operational target ~2027) and Baltica 3 (1GW, ~2030+) offshore wind projects in the Baltic Sea; joint venture with Ørsted (50/50); total investment ~EUR 6-8bn combined [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Post-commissioning, Baltica 2 alone expected to contribute PLN 1.5-2.0bn EBITDA annually at ~EUR 400-500/MWh CfD strike; represents ~20-25% uplift to current EBITDA base [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Market re-rating from "coal utility in decline" to "renewables-transition compounder" is the multiple-expansion story; peers with offshore wind programmes trade at 1-2x EV/EBITDA premium to coal-heavy utilities [#A]

  • Company guide: FY2024 results commentary confirmed Baltica 2 FID targeted for 2025; Ørsted partnership maintained; Polish government Contract for Difference (CfD) auction round 3 allocations pending [#I]

  • Management commentary: Management consistently frames Baltica as the "strategic centrepiece" of the 2030 strategy; CEO repeatedly cited at CMD that CfD economics are "attractive and bankable" [#I]
  • Track record: FID timeline has slipped once already (original 2023-24 target pushed to 2025); offshore wind project delays are sector-norm (Ørsted itself has cancelled/delayed multiple projects globally) [#I]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Timeline guidance has a history of optimism bias in offshore wind sector broadly; Ørsted's own global project review (2023) showed cost inflation of 20-30% on pending projects [#I]

  • SS consensus: Sell-side broadly assigns option value to Baltica 2 in DCF models (~PLN 3-6/share NPV contribution) but with wide range; most brokers model FID by end-2025 and first power 2027-28 [#I]

  • Consensus changes L12M: Option value marks have been trimmed as Ørsted's global challenges became visible; some brokers reduced Baltica NPV contribution by 10-20% citing cost inflation and financing risk [#I]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Pre-revenue in 24M window; FID confirmation alone could trigger multiple expansion of 0.5-1.0x EV/EBITDA on the whole PGE group as market re-rates transition credibility [#A]

  • Recent delivery: No FID confirmed as of research date; Baltica project development costs expensed (~PLN 200-300m/yr); no negative technical surprises disclosed but supply chain costs elevated [#I]

  • Peer benchmark: Ørsted (50% JV partner) has faced severe cost inflation globally; UK/German offshore wind auctions saw project cancellations at lower strike prices than Baltica CfD; Polish CfD round 3 strikes reportedly higher than UK equivalents [#I]

  • Trend L12M: Stable-to-cautious — no negative project announcements but no FID catalyst delivered; market sentiment on offshore wind has deteriorated globally vs 2021-22 peak optimism [#I]

  • Macro tailwind: Polish government committed to 6GW offshore wind by 2030 under National Energy Policy; EU funding (REPowerEU) available for Baltic offshore development; grid connection investments accelerating [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Offshore wind supply chain inflation (turbines, foundations, installation vessels) remains elevated; interest rate environment increases project IRR hurdles; Ørsted's global financial stress creates JV governance risk [#A]
  • Competitive dynamics: Baltica 2 has first-mover advantage in Polish Baltic zone; limited direct competition for CfD allocations in the near term [#A]

  • Visibility: Medium — CfD economics visible, but FID depends on financing completion, Ørsted commitment confirmation, and supply chain cost lock [#J]

  • Plausibility assessment: FID by end-2025 is plausible but not high conviction given JV partner stress and cost environment; commissioning by 2027 requires flawless execution in a sector where delays are endemic [#A]

  • Key assumptions: (a) Ørsted does not exit JV or seek to renegotiate economics; (b) Polish CfD strike covers all-in project costs at current inflation; (c) financing banks provide non-recourse project debt at workable terms; (d) grid connection approval and permitting completed on schedule [#A]
  • Bear case: Ørsted exits or restructures JV post-FID uncertainty; PGE forced to either find new partner (diluting economics) or carry higher equity proportion (balance sheet stress); FID delayed to 2027+ and 24M share price catalyst evaporates [#J]
  • Miss magnitude: Option-value loss of PLN 3-5/share on ~PLN 10/share current price — significant; plus negative re-rating as "transition compounder" thesis is invalidated [#A]

  • Red flags: ⚡ Ørsted's decision in 2023 to write down USD 4bn in global offshore projects and its Q4 2023 profit warning create a genuine JV partner risk that is structurally different from normal project-execution risk [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: Baltica is the "story" that justifies a premium multiple, but it contributes zero cash in 24M and its JV partner is financially stressed; if the story cracks, PGE re-rates to a pure coal utility multiple, which is materially lower [#J]

Plausibility verdict: medium plausibility — FID confirmation by 2026 is achievable but dependent on Ørsted stability and financing markets; 24M earnings impact is immaterial, making this a re-rating driver rather than earnings driver

§4 — Key Driver #3: Coal Asset Transition and Capacity Market Support

  • J-front verdict: The most politically uncertain and downside-asymmetric driver; capacity market payments are the critical cash bridge for coal-to-renewables transition — their reduction or elimination would destroy the transition financing model [#J]

  • Driver definition: PGE operates ~8GW of coal-fired generation; these assets are loss-making or marginally profitable at spot power prices; capacity market payments (PLN 1.5-2.5bn/yr estimated) bridge the gap and fund capex transition [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Capacity market payments are near-pure EBITDA contribution (~PLN 1.5-2.0bn/yr); removal or reduction flows directly to earnings and constrains capex funding capacity [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Negative trigger scenario: EU state aid challenge to Polish capacity market payments forces restructuring; coal fleet becomes cash-drain rather than cash-bridge; balance sheet stress accelerates [#J]

  • Company guide: PGE guidance assumes capacity market payments continue through 2028 under existing Polish capacity market legislation; no explicit sensitivity for payment reduction provided [#I]

  • Management commentary: Management has consistently treated capacity market as "contracted and secured"; flagged EU review process as "ongoing but constructive" in investor presentations [#I]
  • Track record: Polish capacity market has been operational since 2021; payments have been made as contracted to date; EU State Aid approval for current mechanism received (2020); next review cycle in process [#I]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Management characterisation of EU review as "constructive" is optimistic; EU has tightened State Aid rules for fossil fuel capacity payments across member states since 2022 [#A]

  • SS consensus: Sell-side models assume capacity payments continue through ~2028 in base case; bear case scenarios typically show PLN 1-2bn EBITDA removal as a 2-sigma downside event [#I]

  • Consensus changes L12M: Limited consensus change on this driver specifically; generally treated as a "known known" in models rather than active upside/downside [#I]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Capacity market continuation = ~PLN 0.3-0.4/share EPS annually embedded in consensus; loss of payments = immediate ~20-25% EPS reduction, compounding through early transition years [#A]

  • Recent delivery: Capacity payments received as contracted in FY2023-24; no negative regulatory developments disclosed; EU review ongoing but no adverse ruling [#I]

  • Peer benchmark: Czech CEZ faces similar coal-capacity market dynamics; German utilities (RWE, Uniper) have largely exited coal or received explicit government transition payments; Polish mechanism is among the last major EU coal capacity support schemes [#I]

  • Trend L12M: Stable on the surface but EU regulatory pressure incrementally increasing; Poland's post-election government (Tusk coalition from late 2023) has stated faster clean energy transition ambitions which adds uncertainty to coal fleet longevity assumptions [#I]

  • Macro tailwind: None material — structural pressure against coal capacity support is one-directional [#A]

  • Macro headwind: EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism, REPowerEU coal phase-out commitments, and new Polish government's cleaner energy policy stance all directionally negative for coal capacity support longevity [#A]
  • Competitive dynamics: PGE is the dominant Polish coal generator; no competitive threat, but also no competitive protection from regulatory change [#A]

  • Visibility: Low-to-medium — contractually secure through ~2028 but subject to political/regulatory disruption that is difficult to price [#J]

  • Plausibility assessment: Continuation through 2026-27 is high plausibility given existing contracts; continuation beyond 2028 is medium-to-low plausibility given trajectory [#A]

  • Key assumptions: (a) EU does not reclassify existing capacity market contracts as impermissible State Aid; (b) new Polish government does not accelerate coal exit beyond contracted decommissioning schedule; (c) coal fleet remains technically capable and profitable enough to warrant continued operation [#A]
  • Bear case: EU challenges capacity market payments as incompatible with State Aid rules; PGE required to repay or restructure PLN 3-5bn of historical payments; simultaneous coal asset impairment of PLN 5-10bn; balance sheet crisis [#J]
  • Miss magnitude: Extreme scenario — PLN 10-15bn impairment + lost capacity payments = potential equity impairment; more likely scenario = gradual reduction adding PLN 0.5-1bn annual EBITDA pressure from 2026 onward [#A]

  • Red flags: New Polish government's stated climate ambitions and EU coal phase-out pressure creating two-sided squeeze not visible in consensus models [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet: Sell-side treats capacity market continuation as a low-risk assumption because contracts exist; the actual risk is regulatory/political discontinuity, which by definition doesn't show up in the contract schedule — exactly the kind of risk consensus systematically underprices in utility models [#J]

Plausibility verdict: low-medium plausibility on a sustained basis beyond 2027 — near-term continuity is high conviction, but 24M window includes the EU review cycle and new Polish government policy clarification, making tail risk non-trivial

§5 — Key Driver #4: Regulated Networks CAPEX Return (Distribution/Transmission)

  • J-front verdict: The most visible and defensible earnings driver; regulated WACC-based returns on a PLN 25-30bn RAB provide stable EBITDA floor — but WACC reset risk and regulatory formula changes are the underappreciated risk [#J]

  • Driver definition: PGE Dystrybucja (distribution network subsidiary) operates under Polish ERO (Urząd Regulacji Energetyki) regulatory framework; EBITDA ~PLN 3.5-4.0bn/yr (est. ~40-45% of group EBITDA); RAB-based return model [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Regulated EBITDA is formulaic (RAB × WACC + depreciation + opex allowance); capex invested increases RAB and future returns; relatively predictable 3-5Y earnings trajectory [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Provides valuation floor; regulated utilities trade at 1.0-1.3x RAB globally; PGE's networks RAB alone is ~PLN 25-30bn, providing meaningful downside protection to equity [#A]

  • Company guide: PGE has guided PLN 8-10bn capex into distribution networks through 2027 under regulatory investment plan; WACC for current tariff period set by ERO at ~7.5% (real); next tariff reset ~2026-27 [#I]

  • Management commentary: Management consistently emphasises network investment as "low risk, high visibility" earnings growth; guided network EBITDA growing ~5-7% annually through investable capex [#I]
  • Track record: Regulated networks division has been the earnings stabiliser during generation volatility; FY2023 networks EBITDA met plan despite generation miss; consistent delivery vs regulatory plan [#I]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Guidance is well-anchored in regulatory formula; conservatism is structural — upside limited but downside protected by contract-equivalent regulatory framework [#A]

  • SS consensus: Sell-side uniformly models networks as the stable base; EPS contribution from networks ~PLN 0.7-0.9/share annually; growth assumptions ~5-7%/yr — broadly aligned with regulatory plan [#I]

  • Consensus changes L12M: No material revisions to networks segment estimates; treated as de-risked in all models [#I]

  • Estimated EPS impact: PLN 3.5-4.0bn EBITDA contribution; at PLN 0.7-0.9/share EPS, represents ~40-50% of group consensus EPS — the anchor of the investment case [#A]

  • Recent delivery: Consistently delivered; Q3-Q4 2023 results showed networks EBITDA in-line; no underperformance signal visible [#I]

  • Peer benchmark: CEZ's regulated Czech distribution network similarly provides ~40-45% of group EBITDA and trades at a premium to merchant segments; Enel's Italian network contributes 50%+ of group EBITDA — higher network weighting correlates with higher valuation multiples across EU utility sector [#I]

  • Trend L12M: Stable and on-plan; network investment accelerating in line with EU grid modernisation requirements and EV integration [#I]

  • Macro tailwind: EU grid investment mandates (EU electricity market reform, smart meter rollout, EV charging infrastructure) drive structurally higher regulatory capex allowances through 2030 [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Polish ERO WACC reset in 2026-27 is the key risk; in a declining interest rate environment, real WACC may be cut, reducing returns on existing and incremental RAB [#A]
  • Competitive dynamics: Monopoly regulated asset — no competitive risk but also no growth above regulatory return [#A]

  • Visibility: High — regulatory formula is known; capex plan is filed and approved; next reset in ~18M [#J]

  • Plausibility assessment: High plausibility for 24M delivery; the WACC reset in 2026-27 is the single contingency that could modestly impair this driver [#A]

  • Key assumptions: (a) ERO maintains current regulatory WACC at or above ~7% real; (b) capex execution on network investment plan proceeds without material delays; (c) no retrospective regulatory disallowances [#A]
  • Bear case: ERO cuts WACC at next reset to ~6.0-6.5% (plausible in lower-rate environment); networks EBITDA growth slows to 2-3% vs guided 5-7%; anchor segment loses its growth premium [#A]
  • Miss magnitude: WACC cut of 100bp = ~PLN 250-300m EBITDA reduction on PLN 25-30bn RAB; modest but not catastrophic — preserves capital returns, reduces growth rate [#A]

  • Red flags: None material — this is the cleanest driver in the PGE model [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet: The regulated networks driver is genuinely low-risk, but it is also priced in — sell-side uniformly knows this and has modelled it; if Richard's edge requires mispricing, this driver won't provide it [#J]

Plausibility verdict: high plausibility — regulated formula provides visible 24M earnings floor; WACC reset is a modest risk but not a case-breaker; this driver underwrites the base case but does not generate alpha

§6 — Key Driver #5: Balance Sheet and Financing Cost Management

  • J-front verdict: Under-appreciated risk in consensus models; net debt/EBITDA approaching 3.0-3.5x on PLN 75-80bn capex plan creates covenant and credit rating risk that could constrain dividend, capex, and equity value simultaneously [#J]

  • Driver definition: PGE's PLN 75-80bn capex plan through 2030 (generation transition + networks + Baltica equity contribution) requires substantial external financing; net debt ~PLN 25-30bn at FY2023; credit rating BBB- (investment grade floor) [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Rising net debt increases interest expense; every 100bp increase in average debt cost on PLN 25-30bn net debt = ~PLN 250-300m EBITDA-to-net-income bridge cost; dividend capacity constrained by covenant headroom [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Leverage multiple expansion (re-rating from stress scenario) and dividend reinstatement/increase are the equity value levers; a credit downgrade to sub-investment grade would be a share price event of -20-30% based on sector precedents [#A]

  • Company guide: Management guided net debt/EBITDA "comfortable below 3.5x" through capex peak; FY2025-26 expected to be leverage peak before Baltica revenues commence; no explicit covenant headroom disclosure [#I]

  • Management commentary: CFO commentary emphasised EU Green Deal financing (green bonds, EIB facilities) as lower-cost capex funding; guided average debt cost ~3.5-4.5% on EUR-denominated facilities [#I]
  • Track record: PGE has maintained investment grade rating through previous capex cycles; however, current cycle is materially larger than any prior programme [#I]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Management's "comfortable below 3.5x" guidance assumes Baltica capex timing and power price trajectory both co-operate — a heroic joint assumption given independent risks on each [#A]

  • SS consensus: Sell-side generally models 3.0-3.3x net debt/EBITDA at capex peak (FY2026-27); most brokers treat this as manageable; few explicitly stress-test combined power price miss + capex overrun scenario [#I]

  • Consensus changes L12M: Modest upward revision to leverage estimates as capex costs inflated; no credit-rating concern explicitly raised in public broker research reviewed [#I]

  • Estimated EPS impact: A 50bp increase in average debt cost = ~PLN 130-150m additional interest = ~PLN 0.05-0.07/share EPS impact annually; manageable in isolation but additive to power price and capacity market pressures [#A]

  • Recent delivery: FY2023 net debt increased in line with capex plan; no refinancing stress or covenant breach disclosed; green bond issuance completed at competitive spreads [#I]

  • Peer benchmark: CEZ net debt/EBITDA ~2.0-2.5x (lower leverage, more conservative capex plan); Enel ~3.0-3.5x (comparable but lower country risk premium); PGE at 3.0-3.5x with higher political risk premium is at the tighter end of peer range [#I]

  • Trend L12M: Leverage slowly rising as expected; rate environment has improved modestly but refinancing risk on FY2025-27 maturities remains [#I]

  • Macro tailwind: ECB rate cuts (if materialise in 2025-26) reduce refinancing cost on EUR-denominated facilities; EU green bond demand robust, providing liquidity [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Polish zloty weakness vs EUR increases PLN-equivalent debt service cost on EUR-denominated borrowings; Polish NBP rate path independent of ECB, creating basis risk [#A]
  • Competitive dynamics: Not applicable — internal balance sheet management [#A]

  • Visibility: Medium — debt structure and maturity profile visible in filings; macro rate and FX factors create uncertainty [#J]

  • Plausibility assessment: Maintaining investment grade is medium plausibility given combined capex + power price risks; the scenario where leverage exceeds 3.5x is not a tail risk — it's a ~30-35% probability scenario given interdependencies [#A]

  • Key assumptions: (a) Power prices stabilise above PLN 380/MWh; (b) Baltica capex schedule stays on plan without cost overrun; (c) no forced accelerated coal asset write-downs requiring cash top-up; (d) ECB rates decline supports EUR debt refinancing [#A]
  • Bear case: Power price decline + Baltica cost overrun + coal impairment charge in same year (FY2026-27) pushes net debt/EBITDA above 4.0x; Moody's/S&P downgrade to BB; spread widening adds 100-150bp to refinancing costs; equity re-rates as distressed utility [#J]
  • Miss magnitude: Credit downgrade scenario = -20-30% share price impact based on comparable EU utility downgrades (Uniper 2022; EDP 2014); equity value destruction is non-linear above leverage thresholds [#A]

  • Red flags: FY2026-27 is the capex + leverage peak and coincides with the ERO WACC reset, EU capacity market review, and Baltica FID financing close — four simultaneous pinch-points [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet: The sell-side stress-tests drivers independently; the real risk is the joint scenario where power prices, coal market, and financing all disappoint simultaneously — the correlation matrix is positive, and consensus models treat these as independent events [#J]

Plausibility verdict: medium plausibility for investment grade maintenance — manageable in base case but the stress scenario is a 30%+ probability event, not a tail risk, given driver correlations

§7 — Key Driver #6: Polish Macro and PLN/EUR FX

  • J-front verdict: Second-order driver in isolation but amplifies all other drivers; PLN weakness adds to EUR-denominated debt service cost and reduces USD/EUR-priced commodity inputs' competitiveness — the FX tail is underweighted in consensus models [#J]
