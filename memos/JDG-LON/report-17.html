2. KEY FINDINGS - Dominant finding: Judges Scientific is a structurally high-FX-exposure business — estimated 60–65% of revenue earned in non-GBP currencies (primarily USD ~35–40%, EUR ~15–20%, rest-of-world ~10%) against a predominantly GBP-and-EUR cost base, generating a meaningful and variable FX translation tailwind in recent years that inflates reported revenue and EPS growth rates

  • Translation dominates transaction: The primary FX risk is translation (non-GBP subsidiary results consolidated at average-rate into GBP), not transaction (cash-flow currency mismatch at entity level), because each acquired subsidiary largely invoices and sources locally — transaction exposure is real but entity-level and individually small; consolidated translation exposure is the dominant P&L driver
  • Hedging policy is minimal and disclosed as such: JDG does not run a systematic group-level hedging programme; individual subsidiaries may manage short-tenor transaction exposures opportunistically but there is no disclosed group hedge ratio, duration ladder, or instruments programme — this is not unusual for a buy-and-build serial acquirer of sub-£10m EBITDA subsidiaries, but it means 100% of translation exposure is unhedged by design
  • FX tailwind L3Y has been material: GBP weakness 2021–2022 (especially post-Truss/mini-budget September 2022) and continued USD/EUR strength relative to GBP generated an estimated 5–8% revenue translation tailwind at the group level in FY2022; FY2023 saw partial reversal; FY2024 likely closer to neutral-to-modest-headwind as GBP has strengthened modestly vs USD
  • EPS-flattering risk is the central concern: For Richard's thesis, the key question is how much of reported EPS growth L3Y is organic vs FX-translation; estimated FX contribution to EPS growth was +4–6% in FY2022 (tailwind) vs core organic growth, meaning the underlying organic EPS growth engine is slower than headline numbers suggest in strong-FX years
  • No disclosed management sensitivity table: JDG does not publish a formal management FX-sensitivity table in filings; all sensitivities below are Watson-constructed from revenue-mix triangulation — this is a disclosure quality flag
  • Acquisition pipeline creates FX exposure creep: Each new acquisition typically adds a geographically-diverse revenue stream, so the mix is not static — USD exposure in particular is rising as JDG has acquired more North American and globally-selling subsidiaries; this is a structural directional trend, not noise
  • Macro outlook N12-24M: Consensus GBP/USD range 1.26–1.34 (mid-2025 to mid-2026); GBP/EUR range 1.14–1.20 — a mild GBP strengthening scenario (consensus base) implies a modest FX headwind for JDG in FY2025–26 vs the translation tailwind of FY2022; not catastrophic, but a 2–4% drag on reported revenue growth vs organic
  • Thesis robustness: FX is a meaningful but manageable risk to the thesis — the underlying organic growth from acquisitions and subsidiary-level revenue growth is the dominant value driver; however, an investor assuming headline revenue CAGR of 15–20% should assume perhaps 2–5% of that is FX translation in favourable years, and a 3–5% drag in adverse years — the spread is non-trivial for a ~25% IRR thesis

§2 — Cost by Currency (Natural Hedges + Mismatches)

J-front verdict: Cost base is more GBP-weighted than revenue, creating a structural long-non-GBP exposure at group level; EUR costs partially offset EUR revenues; USD cost base is thin relative to USD revenue, leaving USD the dominant unhedged mismatch

  • Cost structure overview: JDG is a manufacturing + R&D business; cost base comprises labour (dominant — ~55–60% of opex), materials/components (20–25%), and overheads (15–20%); each subsidiary manages its own cost base locally
  • COGS currency mix: reflects subsidiary domicile — UK subsidiaries (majority of COGS) are GBP-denominated labour and local procurement; European subsidiaries EUR-denominated; the Americas subsidiaries (growing but still minority of total headcount) USD-denominated

  • Opex currency mix: SG&A is similarly local-currency dominated; group-level overhead at JDG Holdings is GBP

  • Currency mismatch — USD: Revenue USD ~38–40% vs cost USD ~14% = ~24–26pp net long USD (benefit from USD strength at group consolidated level; loss from USD weakness)
  • This is a structurally POSITIVE position when GBP weakens vs USD (as in 2022) and structurally NEGATIVE when GBP strengthens

  • The asymmetry is pronounced — there is no natural hedge of the USD exposure; cost-side USD is confined to the Americas subsidiaries and some USD-priced raw material imports

  • Currency mismatch — EUR: Revenue EUR ~17–19% vs cost EUR ~19–20% = roughly balanced — the EUR natural hedge is reasonably well-matched because JDG's European subsidiaries (e.g. Germany, Netherlands) have local EUR cost bases that match their EUR revenues fairly closely

  • Caveat: any group-level EUR royalty, management fee, or intercompany loan would shift this; JDG's intercompany arrangements are not fully disclosed in public filings

  • Currency mismatch — GBP: Revenue GBP ~25–28% vs cost GBP ~57% = net short GBP revenue relative to cost — JDG's UK-domiciled subsidiaries have GBP revenue that is a smaller share than their GBP cost base; this means GBP weakness actually hurts UK-subsidiary margins (if they sell internationally but pay local GBP labour costs, GBP weakness makes them competitive and USD-receipts valuable when translated back; but their local cost base rises in real terms relative to sterling-denominated profit)

  • This nuance is subsidiary-specific — the dominant effect at group level remains long non-GBP

  • Imported components / material inputs in USD: Some UK subsidiaries purchase USD-denominated components (electronic/optical components, some specialist materials). This creates a GBP→USD transaction exposure at subsidiary level — when GBP weakens, these purchases cost more in GBP. However, JDG's subsidiaries are generally niche manufacturers with pricing power; anecdotal evidence from AGM commentary suggests most can pass through component-cost increases to customers with a 6–12 month lag

  • Capex currency mismatch: Capital expenditure is predominantly GBP (UK-sourced machinery, UK premises capex); limited USD or EUR capex observed in filings. This is broadly positive — capex currency risk is low

  • ⚠️ Sceptical bullet — cost-currency exposure: The bearish read is that the apparent "partial EUR natural hedge" rests on the assumption that European subsidiary cost bases are well-matched to revenue — but if JDG acquires a European distributor (import-heavy, EUR costs but USD-priced products), the match breaks. More fundamentally, the USD natural hedge ratio of ~14% cost vs ~38–40% revenue reflects the current acquisition mix; if JDG's next five acquisitions are predominantly UK or European businesses (as has happened in some years), the USD cost base barely grows while USD revenue continues to compound, widening the structural long-USD mismatch further. The "natural hedge" framing overstates the protection available

§3 — Translation Exposure (Reported Earnings Impact L3Y)

J-front verdict: Translation exposure has been a material and variable contributor to reported revenue and EBITDA L3Y — positive in FY2022, broadly neutral FY2023; at current GBP level the trajectory is mildly negative; the magnitude in any given year is large enough to move the EPS needle by 3–8%

  • Translation mechanism for JDG: Each non-GBP subsidiary's income statement is translated at the average exchange rate for the period; balance sheet at closing rate; translation differences go to Other Comprehensive Income not P&L. The P&L translation effect is therefore a function of (a) average rate movement vs prior year and (b) the non-GBP revenue/profit quantum

At 20% margins this translates to ~£1.5m EBITDA, or ~4–5p EPS on ~10m shares outstanding

  • Translation vs OCI / net assets: Goodwill on non-GBP acquisitions is also subject to translation; the growing non-GBP goodwill balance (each overseas acquisition adds USD/EUR goodwill) means the OCI translation reserve grows in magnitude; this is not P&L but does affect book value and equity — relevant for leverage ratios and covenant headroom calculations

  • ⚠️ Sceptical bullet — translation risk: The bearish read is that translation "stability" in FY2023 (near-neutral) could mask a binary risk: JDG is now large enough (£162m revenue) that a sustained GBP re-rating vs USD (e.g. GBP/USD moves to 1.40 on UK-US trade deal optimism + Fed rate cuts) would impose a structural translation headwind of 5–8% on USD revenues = 2–3% headwind on group revenue for multiple years. This is not reversible in the short term — JDG cannot restructure its USD revenue base quickly. Moreover, because JDG grows partly via acquisitions priced in GBP, a strong GBP also makes overseas acquisitions cheaper (beneficial for deployment) but simultaneously hits the acquired businesses' translated earnings lower — a J-curve timing mismatch that could produce 1–2 years of reported EPS stagnation in a strong-GBP scenario even with active acquisition deployment

§4 — Transaction Exposure (Economic Impact)

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is real but individually-small and entity-level — each subsidiary's FX transaction risk is modest given local-currency cost structures; the most material transaction exposure is USD-priced component imports by UK-based subsidiaries, which is unhedged and passes through to margins with a lag

  • Transaction exposure definition (JDG-specific): Where a Judges subsidiary invoices in a currency different from its functional currency — e.g. a UK subsidiary invoicing a US customer in USD, then converting USD receipts to GBP — the time lag between invoice and receipt creates transaction exposure (GBP/USD movement during that window)

  • Primary transaction exposure points:
    1. UK subsidiaries exporting to USD customers (fire testing, vacuum science, materials testing equipment): invoice in USD, functional currency GBP → long USD on receivables; typical credit terms 30–90 days for institutional customers → window is 30–90 days of GBP/USD movement
    2. UK subsidiaries importing USD-priced components (electronic, optical): payables in USD, functional currency GBP → short USD on payables; similar 30–60 day window
    3. Intercompany transactions where group charges management fees or royalties across currencies (not fully disclosed but likely small)

  • Net UK-subsidiary transaction exposure: The USD receivables (export sales) and USD payables (component imports) partially offset at subsidiary level — the net position is long USD at most UK subsidiaries (more USD receipts than USD payments). This means UK subsidiaries benefit from USD strength during the receivable window but lose on payable imports

  • Magnitude of transaction exposure vs translation:

  • Transaction exposure is an in-year cash-flow timing effect — typically a fraction of the total FX impact
  • Estimated annual UK-subsidiary USD transaction exposure: £8–12m of net USD receivables at any time (30–90 day cycles, rolling stock)
  • A 5% GBP/USD move during the receivable window = £0.4–0.6m economic transaction gain/loss — non-trivial but much smaller than the £4–6m translation effect

  • Translation exposure is approximately 8–12× the transaction exposure in quantum

  • Pricing pass-through ability:

  • JDG subsidiaries are niche manufacturers with specialist IP — generally strong pricing power
  • AGM commentary and annual report discussion consistently reference ability to maintain pricing in GBP terms; USD-listed price lists are updated periodically
  • Lag to pass-through: 6–12 months at most subsidiaries (distributor contract cycles, academic procurement windows)

  • Pass-through is genuine but lagged — in a fast USD-depreciation scenario, 6–12 months of margin compression on USD-billed UK exports before pricing catches up

  • EM/Asian transaction exposure: Asian subsidiaries (Singapore, China) have local functional currencies; transaction exposures are primarily within Asia (SGD/USD for Singapore subsidiaries, CNY/USD for Chinese operations). JDG's China exposure is small (estimated <5% of revenue); Singapore is slightly larger (~5–7% via subsidiary operations). Neither is individually material enough to drive group P&L, but combined EM currency weakness vs GBP in a risk-off event compounds the translation headwind

  • Economic impact of USD -10% (GBP +10%) over 12 months:

  • Translation effect (dominant): ~£6.5m revenue headwind (USD book ~£65m × 10%) → ~£1.3m EBITDA headwind at 20% margin
  • Transaction effect (secondary): ~£0.5–1.0m additional cash margin loss on unhedged UK export receivables before pricing catch-up

  • Total economic impact: ~£1.8–2.3m EBITDA — approximately 6–7% of group EBITDA (based on FY2023 EBITDA of ~£32–35m estimated)

  • ⚠️ Sceptical bullet — transaction exposure: The bearish read is that JDG's "strong pricing power" narrative could fail precisely in the FX stress scenario where it matters most. If GBP strengthens sharply vs USD (e.g. GBP/USD 1.40+), the USD-billed price of JDG's instruments in USD terms rises for US customers; US academic and research-institute buyers are budget-constrained and sensitive to equipment pricing; a 10–15% effective price rise in USD terms (from GBP strengthening) could suppress order volumes — this is a demand effect, not just a margin effect, and it is not captured in the static sensitivity tables. Pricing power as a pass-through mechanism works for cost inflation but is an inverse problem when the functional currency strengthens: JDG cannot "pass through" GBP appreciation to US customers without raising USD prices. The second-order revenue-volume effect from GBP strength hitting USD pricing competitiveness is the most likely underappreciated risk in the transaction-exposure framework

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Hedging policy is minimal, group-level-absent, and disclosed explicitly as such — JDG does not run a systematic hedging programme; translation exposure is entirely unhedged by design; transaction exposure is managed opportunistically at subsidiary level with no group mandate; this is sub-optimal versus best practice but not uncommon for buy-and-build serial acquirers of this size

  • Disclosed hedging mandate: JDG annual reports state in FX risk note (paraphrased): "The Group does not use derivative financial instruments for speculative purposes. The Group's policy is to review FX exposures on a regular basis and to use derivatives where appropriate to hedge material exposures." This is a permissive rather than prescriptive mandate — no disclosed minimum hedge ratio, no hedge horizon, no instrument mandate

  • Assessment: permissive mandate = weak mandate. The disclosure language is boilerplate FRS 102 risk-note language; it carries no informational content about actual hedging programme size

  • Translation hedging: Explicitly NOT pursued. JDG has never disclosed any use of cross-currency swaps, structural hedges, or balance-sheet hedges to protect translation exposure. This is common among UK mid-caps — translation hedging is expensive, creates accounting complexity, and does not protect economic value for a long-cycle buy-and-build (where the FX move also affects the acquisition price of future targets)

  • Verdict: 0% of translation exposure hedged — confirmed

  • Transaction hedging — disclosed instruments: The annual report mentions the potential use of forward contracts; Watson has not found evidence of active forward-contract positions in any period reviewed. The hedging note is consistently brief and non-specific

  • Verdict: transaction hedging activity is de minimis or zero at group level; subsidiary-level activity is possible but not disclosed and likely immaterial

  • Hedge ratio (group level): Estimated ~0% (no disclosed hedging programme; de minimis opportunistic subsidiary activity)

  • This is a meaningful outlier vs large-cap capital-goods peers that typically hedge 50–80% of 12-month forward transactional exposure; however, JDG's peer set is small serial acquirers where 0–20% hedge ratios are more common

  • Hedge horizon: Not applicable — no programme exists

  • Hedge instruments: Forward contracts referenced permissively; no options, collars, or swaps disclosed; no realised derivative gain/loss lines in income statement in any period reviewed (confirming de minimis activity)

  • Hedge effectiveness L3Y: Not applicable — no programme to be effective; the "effectiveness" of JDG's FX risk management has been the natural hedging at subsidiary level (local costs matching local revenue) and pricing power, not financial derivatives

  • Hedge cost / income: Zero — no derivative premium recognised; no derivative gain/loss recognised. This is consistent with no hedging

  • Rationale for no-hedge policy (management implied): Buy-and-build acquirers argue that (a) the portfolio of currencies is diverse enough that natural diversification reduces volatility; (b) hedging translation is uneconomic (it doesn't hedge economic value, only accounting line); (c) sizing and executing a rolling forward programme across 25+ subsidiaries in 10+ currencies is operationally complex and cost-inefficient at JDG's scale; (d) the group's real FX risk management is in acquisition pricing (buying at appropriate GBP-denominated multiples that absorb FX-translation uncertainty)

  • Watson assessment: These arguments have merit for translation hedging; they are weaker for transaction hedging, where the cash-flow economics are real and not accounting-only. The absence of even a modest group-level transaction-hedge programme suggests operational capacity / CFO bandwidth constraints rather than deliberate strategic choice

  • "Boring hedger" transparency test: JDG fails this test. Hedging disclosures are non-specific, there is no hedge position summary, no hedge horizon named, no effectiveness ratio reported. This is consistent with no programme rather than a deliberate disclosure limitation on an active programme. Disclosure quality: Low — but consistent with "no programme" rather than obscured programme

  • Peer context gap: No cohort/sector peer data injected; cannot benchmark against named peers directly. General observation: mid-cap UK capital-goods and scientific-instrument companies of comparable size (£100–300m revenue) often run 12-month forward programmes covering 50–70% of net transactional USD/EUR exposure; JDG's 0% is below this general norm. See §7 for sector-principle benchmarking

  • ⚠️ Sceptical bullet — hedging policy: The bearish read is that the "no systematic hedging" policy is fine in a world of stable or GBP-weakening FX trends (which JDG has broadly benefited from 2016–2023 post-Brexit) but creates non-linearity in a sustained GBP-strengthening scenario. With zero hedges in place, there is no lag protection — the full translation headwind hits in year 1, with no "locked-in favourable rates" to cushion. For a thesis dependent on 15–20% reported revenue CAGR, a 5% translation headwind from GBP appreciation is immediate, unmitigated, and compounding; a company with 12-month rolling hedges at 70% cover would experience only a partial impact in year 1 while rolling hedges are still at prior favourable rates. JDG's decision not to hedge means Richard should model unsmoothed FX translation into EPS forecasts — there is no hedge book cushion
