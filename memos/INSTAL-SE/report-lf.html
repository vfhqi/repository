EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (BLUF)

Board composition demonstrates robust independent oversight, with six out of seven directors independent of the company and its major shareholders, representing an 85.71% independence ratio that exceeds the 50.00% minimum standard recommended by the Swedish Corporate Governance Code. Johnny Alvarsson was appointed Chairman on 31 July 2025 to replace Sjöstrand upon his executive return, bringing extensive corporate leadership experience as CEO of Swedish industrial group Beijer Alma. However, Per Sjöstrand serves as the sole non-independent executive director due to his role as President and CEO, and his dual role as major shareholder and director risks compromising independent oversight of executive performance. The Audit Committee operates with three independent directors and held six formal meetings in 2025 with 100.00% attendance from all members, ensuring rigorous oversight of risks and financial reporting. Board member profiles provide critical sector expertise, including Camilla Öberg who leads the Audit Committee with CFO experience from Swegro, Per Leopoldsson who brings installation sector knowledge from his prior CFO role at Bravida, and Carina Edblad with over thirty years of construction management experience as CEO of Thomas Betong.

Cultural indicators reveal highly resilient entrepreneurial strength, with Instalco achieving a strong eNPS of 31 in 2025 that outperforms peer Bravida's weak eNPS of 14 in 2025 and 11 in 2024. Group-wide employee turnover rose to 15.80% in 2025 from 15.30% in 2024, sitting below Bravida's 20.40% in 2025 but exceeding Burkhalter's 14.50% in 2024. Acquired local firms retain their original brands, local customer relationships, and distinct corporate identities, with extreme local accountability meaning local branch managers retain full profit and loss responsibility, aligning the structure with the Lifco decentralized archetype that grants small average units of 30 employees high operational freedom. Expert network feedback validates that Instalco secures more high-performing subsidiaries from the outset compared to traditional centralized peers like Bravida, with acquired firms requiring significantly less central support, maintaining high decision velocity without dense corporate layers, and enabling higher operating margins than peers.

Insider transactions demonstrate profound alignment, with Per Sjöstrand holding exactly 22,999,835 shares representing 8.56% of total votes and capital as of 31 March 2026, making him the largest individual shareholder and substantiating an exceptional alignment advantage over traditional non-founder corporate peers. Sjöstrand increased his holdings by 42,000 shares in Q4 2025, with this open-market purchase occurring directly after he resumed the CEO role, signaling strong conviction during the executive transition. Swedish Country Manager Johan Larsson holds an impressive 1,656,610 shares, representing significant skin-in-the-game at the operational management level. Former CEO Robin Boheman held zero shares as of 31 December 2025, though he retained 100,000 warrants of the 2023/2026 series and 50,000 warrants of 2024/2027.

Sjöstrand's most fragile leadership trait is diluted focus due to his extreme overboarding, with six concurrent chairmanships representing a severe time-allocation risk compared to the standard Swedish corporate governance code norm of two to three. His board roles at Eltel and Knowit risk distracting his leadership during Instalco's critical operational recovery, representing the primary risk that Sjöstrand cannot sustain this dual board and executive workload and potentially stalling the turnaround. However, Sjöstrand's Q4 2025 purchase of 42,000 shares validates his public commitment to remain until margins hit 8.00%, and his focus on margin-over-volume matches operational reality as seen in Finland's Q1 2026 margin performance. The primary cultural friction emerges from Sjöstrand's shift to a more hands-on follow-up that diverges from Instalco's historical hands-off decentralized philosophy, potentially causing subsidiary managers to lose their entrepreneurial motivation.
