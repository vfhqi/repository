§1 — Executive Summary

MTLN-GB is a dual-segment Greek conglomerate (energy + aluminium) with an unusual internal vertical: captive cheap power feeding an energy-intensive metals business. The investment case is materially more complex than a pure-play utility or a pure-play metals name, which explains both the valuation discount and the multi-driver risk profile.

  • Driver list: Seven drivers identified: (1) Greek RES capacity build-out, (2) Aluminium energy cost advantage, (3) European electrification demand pull, (4) International energy segment expansion (M&A / greenfield), (5) Power price normalisation upside, (6) Capital discipline and deleveraging, (7) Re-rating toward European utility/integrated peers [#J]

  • Impact ranking: Stack-ranked by estimated 24M share price impact — RES expansion > Aluminium economics > Electrification demand > International expansion > Power price normalisation > Capital discipline > Re-rating [#A]

§2 — Key Driver #1: Greek RES Capacity Build-Out

  • J-front verdict: Highest-conviction driver — management has largest Greek private RES pipeline, regulatory tailwinds are real, and execution track record to date is credible [#J]

  • Driver definition: Metlen is Greece's largest private renewable energy developer; pipeline spans solar, wind, and hybrid storage across Greek territory; commissioned capacity generates regulated/contracted or merchant revenue [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: New MW commissioned → incremental EBITDA at high operating leverage → EBIT growth → EPS accretion, amplified by relatively low incremental capex once grid-connected [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Revenue visibility from PPAs + contracted capacity supports DCF re-rating; capacity additions compress per-unit cost base; peer-relative EV/EBITDA expands as RES mix grows [#A]

  • Company guide: Management has guided toward multi-GW pipeline with phased commissioning through 2026-2028; specific GW targets have been articulated in investor presentations (pipeline reportedly >4 GW across development stages) [#A]

  • Management commentary: Consistently bullish on Greek RES; frames Greece as having among the best solar irradiation and wind resources in Europe with structurally underbuilt grid capacity [#A]

  • Track record: Positive — Metlen/MYTILINEOS (predecessor entity) commissioned capacity ahead of initial guidance in several recent years; no evidence of material project write-offs [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Moderate optimism risk — pipeline GW figures include early-stage projects where grid connection is not yet secured; effective commissioned capacity is a subset of headline pipeline [#A]

  • SS consensus: Broadly supportive — most covering analysts flag Greek RES as the primary EBITDA growth engine; Eurobank Equities, Piraeus Securities among Greek brokers most consistently positive [#A]

  • Consensus changes L12M: Upgraded — 2024 Greek grid reform (simplification of licensing procedures) accelerated timeline expectations; consensus 2025-2026 RES EBITDA estimates moved up [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Each 500 MW of commissioned solar/wind capacity at ~€50-70/MWh blended capture price generates c.€50-80M incremental EBITDA; at a 25-30% tax and minority rate, EPS uplift per 500 MW tranche c.€0.15-0.25 (base-rate anchor: European utility peers generate €80-120M EBITDA per 500 MW in mature markets; Greek costs lower, so margin per MW potentially higher) [#A]

  • Recent delivery: On track — 2024 full-year results showed RES segment revenue and EBITDA growth; commissioning milestones broadly met [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: Accelerating — installed RES capacity has roughly doubled over 2021-2024 period; trajectory consistent with guidance [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: Strong — Greece's REPowerEU commitments, EU Green Deal industrial targets, and rising grid electrification demand all create structural demand pull for Greek renewable capacity [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Grid bottleneck — ADMIE (Greek TSO) connection queue remains a real constraint; project delays caused by grid unavailability are the primary execution risk [#J]

  • Competitive dynamics: Increasing competition — European utilities (Enel, Iberdrola, RWE) have been active in Greek RES tenders; local advantage (relationships, land rights, permitting knowledge) provides Metlen an edge but does not eliminate competitive pressure [#A]

  • Visibility: Medium-high — contracted/PPA portion of revenue provides multi-year visibility; merchant tail exposure introduces price risk [#A]

  • Bear case: Grid connection delays push commissioning into 2027-2028; merchant power price falls below PPA floor economics; construction cost inflation erodes project IRRs [#J]

  • Miss scenario: 500 MW of planned 2025-2026 commissioned capacity slips 12 months; EBITDA miss c.€50-80M vs consensus; 5-8% EPS downside at consolidated level [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: Moderate — painful but not thesis-ending; RES story is a multi-year compounder, one year's slip is recoverable [#J]

  • Red flags: Grid connection approvals — any evidence of ADMIE processing delays or moratorium on new connections would be a material warning signal [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: Pipeline GW figures are management-disclosed and development-stage heavy; actual commissioned capacity in 24 months may be 30-40% of headline pipeline — a consistent pattern across European developers [#J]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — Greek RES is the best-evidenced growth driver; key assumption is grid connection queue clears at management-projected pace

§3 — Key Driver #2: Aluminium Energy Cost Advantage

  • J-front verdict: Credible but fragile — Metlen's aluminium margin is structurally dependent on captive power pricing; any repricing of internal energy transfer or external power market intervention collapses the Metals segment's competitive advantage [#J]

  • Driver definition: Aluminium smelting is extremely energy-intensive (c.13-15 MWh/t); Metlen's Aluminium of Greece smelter benefits from captive/self-generated power at costs below market, creating a structural margin advantage vs European peers dependent on grid power [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Lower power cost per tonne → higher aluminium EBITDA margin → EPS uplift; LME aluminium price also directly scales revenue on fixed-cost base [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Metals segment contributes c.30-40% of consolidated EBITDA; sustained aluminium profitability supports overall EBITDA floor; EV/EBITDA re-rating possible if metals earnings quality improves [#A]

  • Company guide: Management has highlighted the captive power advantage as a core competitive moat in investor materials; has guided for aluminium operations to remain cash-generative through the cycle [#A]

  • Management commentary: Emphasises moat language — "lowest-cost European producer" framing appears in investor presentations; credible given Greek power mix [#A]

  • Track record: Consistent — Aluminium of Greece has remained operationally profitable through multiple LME cycles; did not require smelter curtailments seen at European peers (e.g. Slovalco, Dunkirk) during 2022 energy crisis [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Guidance is realistic but assumes energy cost advantage persists — the key assumption (captive power below market) is structural but not immutable [#J]

  • SS consensus: Mixed — bulls emphasise cost leadership; bears flag LME aluminium range-bound at $2,200-2,500/t and global overcapacity from Chinese production; Axia Capital and Eurobank Equities generally supportive of the metals segment long-term [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: LME aluminium moves €100/t → consolidated EBITDA impact c.€25-40M (Aluminium of Greece capacity c.180,000 t/year); EPS impact c.€0.08-0.12 per €100/t LME swing (base-rate anchor: per-tonne LME sensitivity typical for integrated producers in 180-200kt range) [#A]

  • Recent delivery: Mixed 2024 — LME aluminium was range-bound; metals segment EBITDA broadly flat year-over-year; no material deterioration but no upside surprise [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: Stable, not improving — aluminium EBITDA has been managed defensively; the segment is a cash generator, not a growth engine in its own right [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: Energy transition demand — aluminium is a key input for EV bodies, solar panel frames, grid wiring; structural demand tailwind over 5-10 year horizon [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Chinese overcapacity — China's aluminium production has grown materially; excess Chinese exports are structurally depressing global LME prices; European producers face sustained price pressure [#J]

  • Competitive dynamics: Deteriorating for European peers — most European smelters are high-cost vs Chinese and Middle Eastern production; Metlen is best-positioned among European producers but is not immune [#A]

  • Visibility: Low on LME price; medium on cost base — cost advantage is structural; revenue is market-price dependent [#A]

  • Bear case: LME aluminium falls to $1,900-2,000/t on China dumping acceleration; energy cost advantage narrows due to Greek power market liberalisation; Metals segment turns EBITDA-negative [#J]

  • Miss scenario: LME -15% from current levels; power cost premium rises 20% from grid market reform; Metals EBITDA -30% vs consensus; consolidated EPS impact c.-10% [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: Material but not acute — if Metals goes to breakeven rather than loss-making, RES segment can partially offset [#J]

  • Red flags: Any Greek energy market reform that narrows the captive power cost advantage vs. spot grid power would be an immediate negative signal [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The captive power moat is real but backward-looking; as Greek RES becomes cheaper and grid power costs fall across Europe, the differential advantage of captive power may structurally compress — the moat may be self-dissolving [#J]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility on cost advantage persistence over 24 months; medium plausibility on LME price support — key assumption is LME holds above $2,200/t

§4 — Key Driver #3: European Electrification Demand Pull

  • J-front verdict: Structural tailwind clearly real — European electricity demand is inflecting upward from data centres, EVs, and heat pumps; Greek grid is a beneficiary as a net exporter and RES developer [#J]

  • Driver definition: European power demand is expected to grow materially through 2030+ from EV adoption, industrial electrification, and data centre growth; rising aggregate demand supports power prices and RES PPA pricing [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Higher aggregate electricity demand → tighter supply/demand balance in European power markets → higher achieved power prices for uncontracted/merchant RES capacity → EBITDA uplift in Metlen's Energy segment [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Sustained higher power prices compress the discount rate used in RES project DCFs; forward PPA pricing improves for new contracts → higher project valuations → EV/EBITDA re-rating [#A]

  • Company guide: Management has cited European demand growth as a central assumption in its RES pipeline economics; embedded in project IRR assumptions [#A]

  • Management commentary: Constructive — management frames Greek RES as ideally positioned to serve growing European demand via interconnectors (the Greece-Italy interconnector is a key piece of infrastructure) [#A]

  • Track record: Tailwind confirmed by data — European power demand bottomed post-2022 energy crisis and is recovering; IEA projects European electricity demand growth of 2-3% CAGR through 2030 [#A]

  • SS consensus: Broadly positive — this is not a controversial view in energy sector coverage; consensus that demand growth supports power prices [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Each €5/MWh improvement in achieved power price for Metlen's contracted/merchant generation (c.1,500-2,000 GWh/year blended) → €7.5-10M EBITDA uplift; at 24M forward, >3,000 GWh projected → €15-20M sensitivity per €5/MWh (base-rate anchor: Iberdrola, Enel guided similar per-MWh sensitivities for Mediterranean generation assets) [#A]

  • Recent delivery: Mixed — European power prices were volatile in 2024; Greek achieved prices were above European average due to island-effect and grid constraints [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: Strong and multi-year — EU AI data centre buildout, EV adoption curves, heat pump mandates all create durable demand pull; not a cyclical tailwind [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Solar cannibalisation — rapid RES build across Europe depresses midday power prices (the "solar duck curve" effect); Greek irradiation means this is particularly relevant for solar-heavy Metlen portfolio [#J]

  • Competitive dynamics: Increasing RES supply partially offsets demand tailwind — net effect on power prices depends on pace of demand growth vs supply growth; current projections suggest balanced-to-tight through 2027 [#A]

  • Visibility: High on structural direction; low on specific price levels — the demand tailwind is structural; the exact power price path is not [#A]

  • Bear case: European economic slowdown compresses industrial power demand; RES supply build outpaces demand growth; power prices revert to €40-50/MWh; merchant revenue disappoints [#J]

  • Miss magnitude: Meaningful but not catastrophic — contracted capacity partially insulates EBITDA; merchant tail is the exposed portion [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: Electrification demand tailwind is widely priced into European utility valuations; Metlen's ability to capture incremental upside above embedded consensus is unclear [#J]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility as a structural floor under power prices; medium plausibility as an active EPS upside driver above consensus

§5 — Key Driver #4: International Energy Segment Expansion (M&A / Greenfield)

  • J-front verdict: Optionality value rather than earnings driver — international ambitions are real but execution risk is high; M&A history in utility sectors is poor for value creation at scale [#J]

  • Driver definition: Metlen has articulated ambitions to expand its energy business beyond Greece into Southeast Europe, Middle East, and potentially beyond; includes both greenfield RES development and M&A targets [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Acquired or developed international assets → incremental EBITDA at Group level → EPS accretion IF deal economics are disciplined and integration succeeds [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Successful international execution demonstrates scalability of the Greek RES model; multiple expansion possible if markets assign "growth platform" premium [#A]

  • Company guide: Management has made international expansion a stated strategic priority; specific targets in Balkans (Serbia, North Macedonia), Romania, and Egypt have been referenced [#A]

  • Management commentary: Ambitious but vague on specifics — public disclosures on international pipeline are less precise than on Greek RES; pipeline MW figures less well-documented [#A]

  • Track record: Limited history on international — Metlen's core competency is Greek; international is relatively new; track record is thin [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Optimism risk is high — management commentary on international has been aspirational; no delivered international assets at scale visible in financials yet [#J]

  • SS consensus: Sceptical-to-neutral — sell-side coverage generally treats international as upside optionality, not base case; few brokers model material international EBITDA contribution in 2025-2026 estimates [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: If 500 MW of international RES is commissioned by 2027 at similar economics to Greece → c.€40-60M EBITDA → c.€0.10-0.15 EPS uplift; but commissioning probability in this window is medium-low given current pipeline maturity [#A]

  • Recent delivery: Minimal — international segment has not contributed material EBITDA in most recent reporting periods; pipeline is still pre-commissioning [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: Balkan and MENA RES demand is strong — SEE countries have significant RES deficit relative to EU targets; Egypt's green hydrogen ambitions create demand for large-scale RES [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Execution complexity — political risk in MENA and Balkans, permitting complexity, currency risk, off-taker credit risk all materially complicate international economics vs. Greece [#J]

  • Competitive dynamics: International markets are more competitive — Metlen faces larger balance sheets from Enel, TotalEnergies, Masdar in its target markets [#A]

  • Visibility: Low — least visible of all drivers; most dependent on deal flow and external factors [#J]

  • Bear case: International expansion consumes capital and management attention without generating material EBITDA in the 24M window; M&A target overpaid for; dilutive equity issuance required [#J]

  • Miss magnitude: Moderate if capital-light miss; severe if dilutive acquisition — the tail risk is a large M&A deal that destroys value [#A]

  • Red flags: Any announcement of a large international acquisition at >12× EBITDA multiple with significant equity consideration would be a material negative signal [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: International expansion as a stated strategic priority without delivered assets is a classic management aspiration discount; the 24M window is almost certainly too short for international assets to move the consolidated EPS needle [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility as a 24M EPS contributor — international expansion is real optionality but not base-case earnings; key assumption is capital discipline

§6 — Key Driver #5: Power Price Normalisation Upside

  • J-front verdict: Cyclical rather than structural — Greek and European power price trajectory is the key earnings sensitivity for the Energy segment's merchant capacity; direction and timing are uncertain [#J]

  • Driver definition: Post-2022-energy-crisis power prices partially normalised downward; a re-acceleration of power prices toward or above pre-normalisation levels would provide EBITDA upside for uncontracted generation capacity [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Higher Greek/European power prices → higher achieved revenue per MWh for merchant generation → EBITDA uplift → EPS accretion without incremental capex [#A]

  • Company guide: Management does not provide explicit power price guidance (market convention); does provide sensitivity disclosures (typically €X EBITDA per €Y/MWh move) [#A]

  • Track record: Volatile — Greek power prices have ranged from <€50/MWh to >€300/MWh over 2020-2024; normalised range is €70-110/MWh; merchant profitability highly sensitive to position in this range [#A]

  • SS consensus: Base case assumes €70-90/MWh Greek baseload for 2025-2026; some bulls argue for €100-120/MWh on tight gas supply; bears cite rapid RES build compressing prices [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Each €10/MWh on merchant exposure (estimated 1,500-2,500 GWh merchant in current portfolio) → €15-25M EBITDA → c.€0.05-0.08 EPS; significant if power prices move €30-40/MWh in either direction [#A]

  • Recent delivery: Greek power prices were €70-90/MWh for most of 2024 — in line with base case but not delivering upside vs consensus [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: Geopolitical risk premium — any recurrence of gas supply disruption (Russia-Ukraine, LNG bottlenecks) would reprice European power upward [#A]

  • Macro headwind: RES cannibalisation + gas storage normalisation — European gas storage has recovered from 2022 lows; RES supply build structurally compresses power prices at the margin [#J]

  • Visibility: Low — power prices are market-determined and highly volatile; least visible of all drivers [#J]

  • Bear case: European power prices fall to €50-60/MWh on RES oversupply + warm winters; merchant EBITDA -30-40% vs consensus; consolidated EPS -8-12% [#J]

  • Miss magnitude: Moderate — Power price miss is not thesis-ending but is material to 2025-2026 EPS delivery [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: Merchant power price upside is a commodity call layered on top of the equity investment case; Richard should be cautious about sizing up on a stock where a meaningful portion of EPS depends on power price forecasting [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility for power price upside above consensus — structurally compressed near-term; upside is geopolitically contingent

§7 — Key Driver #6: Capital Discipline and Deleveraging

  • J-front verdict: Necessary but not sufficient — Metlen carries elevated leverage from aggressive RES capex; disciplined deployment and progressive deleveraging are preconditions for re-rating rather than standalone drivers [#J]

  • Driver definition: Metlen's RES growth program is capex-intensive; net debt has risen materially as a % of EBITDA; disciplined capital allocation (maintaining leverage below 3× net debt/EBITDA) and cash conversion would unlock a valuation re-rating [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Lower interest cost from deleveraging → higher free cash flow per share → EPS accretion; also reduces equity risk premium demanded by market [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Leverage reduction → multiple expansion (European utilities historically trade at premium to highly-levered peers by 1-2× EV/EBITDA); also unlocks dividend / buyback capacity [#A]

  • Company guide: Management has guided to maintaining leverage within stated parameters; specific net debt/EBITDA target varies by reporting period but the company has indicated 2.5-3.0× is the operating range [#A]

  • Management commentary: Capital discipline messaging has been consistent but tested — aggressive RES pipeline investment is structurally in tension with near-term leverage reduction [#J]

  • Track record: Mixed — leverage has risen with capex acceleration; free cash flow generation has been partly reinvested rather than used for debt reduction [#A]

  • SS consensus: Noted as a monitoring item — most covering analysts flag leverage as a risk rather than a near-term positive catalyst; deleveraging is a 2026-2027 story on most models [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Each 0.5× reduction in net debt/EBITDA (at current EBITDA c.€800-900M) → c.€400-450M debt reduction → at 5% average cost of debt → c.€20-22M interest saving → c.€0.05-0.07 EPS uplift [#A]

  • Recent delivery: Leverage stable to slightly rising — not deteriorating but not on visible deleveraging path in most recent results [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Higher-for-longer interest rates — European refinancing costs remain elevated vs 2020-2021; interest burden is higher than modelled at project inception for some assets [#A]

  • Visibility: Medium — management has capital allocation framework; execution is more transparent than M&A-driven drivers [#A]

  • Bear case: Leverage rises toward 3.5-4× on overaggressive international expansion capex; credit rating pressure; higher refinancing costs further compress EPS [#J]

  • Miss magnitude: Moderate — leverage overshoot would not create an immediate crisis but would suppress the re-rating thesis [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: Deleveraging is a derivative of the RES commissioning story — if commissioning is on time and EBITDA grows as planned, leverage naturally falls; this driver is not independent of drivers 1 and 3 [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — deleveraging is a function of RES delivery and capital discipline; not independently controllable; key assumption is capex does not balloon on international

§8 — Key Driver #7: Re-Rating Toward European Utility / Integrated Peer Premium

  • J-front verdict: Low plausibility as a 24M standalone driver — structural discount for Greek domicile, dual-segment complexity, and liquidity constraints is unlikely to close materially absent a step-change in index inclusion or institutional awareness [#J]

  • Driver definition: Metlen trades at a discount to European integrated energy peers (Enel, Iberdrola, RWE, EDP) on both EV/EBITDA and P/E; re-rating would occur if the market assigns a higher multiple to the RES-heavy portfolio [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Multiple expansion is not an EPS driver per se — it is a valuation driver; however, a higher multiple lowers the hurdle rate for accretive acquisitions and reduces cost of equity [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Pure re-rating from 6-7× EV/EBITDA to 8-9× (European integrated peer range) → 25-40% SP upside without any EPS change; the magnitude is the reason this driver is included despite low plausibility [#A]

  • Company guide: Management has targeted premium European peer comparisons in investor presentations; London listing (LSE dual listing achieved 2023) was explicitly intended to improve valuation by attracting European/UK institutional capital [#A]

  • Management commentary: Acknowledges discount; believes it is unjustified — management narrative is that the discount is information-gap driven; LSE listing was the catalytic action [#A]

  • Track record: Discount has persisted post-LSE listing — the LSE listing expanded the investor universe but has not materially closed the valuation gap to European peers [#J]

  • SS consensus: Sceptical on near-term re-rating — brokers note the dual-segment complexity and Greek country risk as structural discount factors [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Multiple re-rating is not an EPS event; but at current EPS c.€1.50-1.80 (approximate), a 25% SP re-rating from pure multiple expansion would represent significant IRR contribution [#A]

  • Structural discount factors: (1) Greek country risk; (2) dual-segment (energy + metals) complexity vs pure-play peers; (3) lower free float vs large-cap European utilities; (4) relatively thin English-language analyst coverage [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: ESG flows — growing RES mix improves ESG screen scores; could gradually expand institutional investor universe [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Greek risk premium is sticky — European sovereign and corporate risk premia for Greek names are structurally above Western European peers; this is unlikely to change in 24M [#J]

  • Visibility: Very low — re-rating is a market sentiment event; not manageable or predictable [#J]

  • Bear case: Multiple compression rather than expansion; if metals segment earnings deteriorate, market may de-rate the energy segment to reflect conglomerate discount [#J]

  • Miss magnitude: Symmetric risk — no re-rating is the base case; de-rating is possible if conglomerate complexity increases [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: ⚡ RARE: The LSE dual listing thesis has been in market for 2+ years and has not generated the promised re-rating; the failure of a deliberate management action to close the discount is itself a signal that structural discount factors are more durable than management acknowledges [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Low plausibility as an active 24M share price driver — re-rating is conditional on multiple structural discount factors resolving simultaneously

§9 — AGGREGATE WEAK SIGNALS / DOWNSIDE (❌)

  • ❌ Re-rating has failed to materialise: LSE listing completed 2023; discount to European integrated peers persists; management's primary re-rating catalytic action has not delivered. Base rate: dual-listed European industrial stocks typically require 3-5 years before full institutional re-rating is observed. [Cross-ref: §8] [#J]

  • ❌ International execution unproven: International energy expansion is a stated strategic priority with zero material EBITDA delivered in the financial statements to date; unproven at scale. Peer base rate: European utility international expansions have c.40-50% capital destruction rate in first 5 years. [Cross-ref: §5] [#J]

  • ❌ LME aluminium range-bound: LME aluminium has been range-bound at $2,200-2,500/t for most of 2023-2024; Chinese overcapacity structurally constrains upside; Metals segment EBITDA is flat, not growing. Peer base rate: European aluminium producers have not seen material LME-driven EBITDA growth in 6+ quarters. [Cross-ref: §3] [#A]

  • ❌ Leverage elevated and not visibly declining: Net debt/EBITDA stable-to-rising despite EBITDA growth; deleveraging story is a 2026-2027 event at best; interest burden is a near-term EPS headwind. [Cross-ref: §7] [#A]

  • ❌ Greek power price normalisation complete: Power prices are at or near what most analysts assume as base case (€70-90/MWh); limited upside from current levels; downside risk to RES cannibalisation is real. [Cross-ref: §6] [#J]

§10 — Driver Synthesis + Sceptical Synthesis

  • J-front verdict: Strongest bearish case is the Greek regulatory meta-risk — a single regulatory intervention (windfall levy, NOME-style supply obligation, or grid access restriction) would simultaneously damage drivers 1, 2, 3, 5, 6, and 7 [#J]

  • Driver dependency: High correlation across drivers 1, 2, 5, and 6 — all are directly sensitive to Greek/European power market conditions; failure of a single policy assumption (energy market structure) simultaneously undermines four of seven drivers [#J]

  • Driver 6 (deleveraging) is fully derivative: Not independent — deleveraging only occurs if RES commissioning (Driver 1) delivers EBITDA on schedule; treating it as a separate driver overstates the number of independent positive bets [#A]
