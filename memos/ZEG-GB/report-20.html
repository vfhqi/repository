3. THEME §1 — REVENUE DRIVERS + VISIBILITY

J-front verdict

  • Uncertain-and-load-bearing: Revenue visibility is low — Spain mobile market is promotional and ZEG has limited public disclosure on forward ARPU/volume split post-unlimited plan repricing

Q1.1 — ARPU trajectory

  • Question: "Walk us through the ARPU bridge from H2-24 to your internal H2-25 target — volume, price, and mix separately"
  • Expected answer: Management cites modest ARPU recovery driven by premium tier upsell + postpaid mix shift, guided by internal NPS uplift
  • Good answer: Specific numeric bridge with named levers; acknowledges promotional headwinds but quantifies containment
  • Bad answer: Deflects to "market conditions difficult"; references only top-line revenue without unit decomposition
  • Follow-up probe: "What percentage of your base is still on legacy low-ARPU plans migrated from the Vodafone Spain era?"
  • Diagnostic note: Fluent numeric answer = bull; vague market-conditions deflection = bear; acknowledging legacy plan tail but citing scheduled migration dates = false-friend watch (migration dates can slip)

Q1.2 — Churn / retention

  • Question: "What is your blended postpaid churn today vs your 12M target, and which customer segment is driving the gap?"
  • Expected answer: Churn modestly elevated post-brand transition, stabilising; B2C mass-market segment is the pressure point
  • Good answer: Specific churn figure by segment; distinguishes voluntary vs involuntary; cites retention intervention already underway
  • Bad answer: References only blended churn, won't decompose; claims churn is "in line with plan" without disclosing the plan
  • Follow-up probe: "At what churn rate does your EBITDA synergy model break, and how much buffer do you have today?"
  • Diagnostic note: If churn buffer is <50bps vs model, bull case is fragile — cross-ref Q19 bear-case failure mode (customer attrition post-brand migration)

Q1.3 — Pricing power

  • Question: "In the last price increase test you ran, what was the elasticity — how much volume did you lose per unit of ARPU gained?"
  • Expected answer: Management has run at least one test; elasticity better than Spanish market average due to network quality delta
  • Good answer: Cites specific test data; shows elasticity <1 (inelastic); references competitor response as manageable
  • Bad answer: No test has been run; or elasticity data refused citing commercial sensitivity
  • Follow-up probe: "How does that elasticity compare to what Vodafone Spain observed pre-acquisition?"
  • Diagnostic note: No elasticity data = management flying blind on pricing = bear; better-than-market elasticity with data = strong bull signal on ARPU recovery thesis

Q1.4 — B2B + fixed revenue visibility

  • Question: "What share of revenue is B2B or fixed, and what is the contract-weighted average remaining duration?"
  • Expected answer: B2B ~15-20% of revenue; average contract duration 18-24M; provides some visibility floor
  • Good answer: Gives weighted-average duration + % of B2B revenue under multi-year contract; identifies renewal cliff years
  • Bad answer: Cannot quantify contract duration; B2B described qualitatively only
  • Follow-up probe: "Do you have any B2B contract renewals >€10M coming up in the next 18 months — and what's your win-rate assumption?"
  • Diagnostic note: Undisclosed B2B renewal cliff = hidden churn risk not in consensus model

Q1.5 — End-market segmentation

  • Question: "What is your current postpaid/prepaid revenue split, and what is the 3Y target mix?"
  • Expected answer: Prepaid declining, postpaid growing; 3Y target ~80% postpaid by revenue
  • Good answer: Specific split + target; quantifies ARPU uplift from mix shift with timeline
  • Bad answer: Mix not disclosed; or acknowledges prepaid base is structurally sticky and hard to migrate
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the cost of acquiring a prepaid-to-postpaid upgrade vs a net-new postpaid customer?"
  • Diagnostic note: Prepaid stickiness = ARPU recovery slower than model; cross-ref Q19 ACH matrix on revenue timing

Sceptical bullet: Fluent ARPU and churn answers may reflect rehearsed investor-relations framing rather than actual operational confidence — the follow-up probes on elasticity data and churn buffer are designed to distinguish prepared narrative from operational clarity

4. THEME §2 — MARGIN TRAJECTORY + LEVERS

J-front verdict

  • High uncertainty: EBITDA margin trajectory is the most contested element of ZEG's investment case — cost-out pacing, network investment needs, and content cost evolution are all opaque

Q2.1 — EBITDA synergy phasing

  • Question: "Against your public synergy target, what percentage has been contracted vs run-rate delivered as of today?"
  • Expected answer: ~30-40% contracted/delivered at this stage; majority backend-loaded to 2025-2026 cost-out programme
  • Good answer: Specific contracted vs run-rate split; identifies which synergy buckets are ahead vs behind; names the largest single delivered item
  • Bad answer: Refuses to split contracted vs run-rate; cites "on track" without numeric decomposition
  • Follow-up probe: "Which synergy bucket has surprised you most — positively or negatively — vs your Day 1 model?"
  • Diagnostic note: Backend-loading with no delivered run-rate is a yellow flag — cost programmes slip; cross-ref Q19 ACH on synergy delivery risk

Q2.2 — GM driver + network cost trajectory

  • Question: "Walk us through your network cost per subscriber trajectory — what drives it down, and what risks push it up?"
  • Expected answer: Network cost/sub declining via vendor renegotiation + fibre overbuild efficiency; risk is spectrum renewal cost
  • Good answer: Quantified cost/sub trend with specific vendor contract milestones; spectrum renewal cost flagged and sized
  • Bad answer: Only references "operational efficiencies" without unit economics; spectrum risk not addressed
  • Follow-up probe: "When does your primary network vendor contract come up for renegotiation, and what is the risk that the renegotiation goes against you?"
  • Diagnostic note: Vendor renegotiation risk not in consensus = potential margin surprise — either positive (if renegotiated well) or negative (if vendor has pricing power)

Q2.3 — Capex pacing

  • Question: "What is your capex intensity target at steady state vs today, and what triggers the step-down?"
  • Expected answer: Capex intensity currently elevated at ~15-18% of revenue; steady-state target ~12-14% post-network integration
  • Good answer: Specific trigger milestones (e.g. network cutover completion date); quantified steady-state range with confidence interval
  • Bad answer: Refuses to commit to a capex intensity target; describes capex as "market-dependent"
  • Follow-up probe: "If the network integration slips 12 months, what is the capex overshoot in absolute euros?"
  • Diagnostic note: No capex intensity target = FCF model unreliable; integration slip = direct FCF haircut — cross-ref Q19 pre-mortem on FCF timing

Q2.4 — Content cost evolution

  • Question: "What is your content cost as a percentage of revenue today, and what is the trajectory over 3 years?"
  • Expected answer: Content costs modest relative to cable peers (~5-8% of revenue); declining as legacy Vodafone content deals expire
  • Good answer: Specific % today + trajectory; names the largest content deal expiry date and expected re-negotiation outcome
  • Bad answer: Cannot quantify content cost; or reveals content cost is growing due to competitive necessity
  • Follow-up probe: "Is there any scenario where you need to add a sports rights package to defend churn — and what would that cost?"
  • Diagnostic note: Forced sports rights addition would blow margin model — false-friend risk if management downplays competitive necessity

Q2.5 — Op leverage + FX pass-through

  • Question: "At what revenue growth rate does your EBITDA margin expand — and what is the fixed-cost leverage ratio you're working with?"
  • Expected answer: Fixed cost base ~60% of OpEx; above mid-single-digit revenue growth, margin expands meaningfully
  • Good answer: Specific fixed/variable cost split; growth rate threshold for margin expansion named; FX exposure (€ cost vs € revenue — largely domestic, so limited)
  • Bad answer: Can't quantify operating leverage; describes cost structure as "flexible" without data
  • Follow-up probe: "If revenue is flat for 12 months, what happens to your EBITDA margin — give us the worst case?"
  • Diagnostic note: Flat-revenue EBITDA margin stress test is the false-friend crystalliser — if margin collapses on flat revenue, bull case requires growth that may not materialise

5. THEME §3 — CAPITAL ALLOCATION + BALANCE SHEET

J-front verdict

  • Critical path item: Leverage reduction is the gating condition for ZEG re-rating — FCF conversion, debt maturity management, and dividend optionality are the three sub-questions that matter most

Q3.1 — Leverage target + FCF inflection

  • Question: "What is your internal target leverage ratio and the specific year you expect to reach it — not the public guidance range?"
  • Expected answer: Target ~3.0-3.5x net debt/EBITDA; internal expectation H2-2026 or 2027
  • Good answer: Specific year + scenario dependency (e.g. "assuming synergy delivery on schedule and no material churn deterioration")
  • Bad answer: Refuses to commit beyond public guidance range; or the year is beyond 2027 (extended leverage risk)
  • Follow-up probe: "What single event would push that timeline out by 12 months, and how likely do you assess it?"
  • Diagnostic note: Leverage timeline >2027 = equity story structurally weaker; cross-ref Q19 ACH on refinancing cliff risk

Q3.2 — Debt maturity management

  • Question: "Walk us through your debt maturity profile — what maturities hit in the next 36 months and what is your refinancing plan?"
  • Expected answer: Primary maturities 2026-2027; refinancing plan involves pre-emptive capital markets execution at 12-18M horizon
  • Good answer: Specific maturity dates + sizes; management has already begun lender dialogue; margin/covenants on refinancing quantified
  • Bad answer: "We'll address maturities when they come"; or management reveals covenants are already tight
  • Follow-up probe: "If credit markets tighten materially in 2025-2026, do you have a Plan B — asset sale, equity issuance, or covenant waiver negotiation?"
  • Diagnostic note: No Plan B = binary leverage risk; asset-sale optionality = bull; equity issuance Plan B = dilution risk for current holders

Q3.3 — Dividend policy

  • Question: "At what FCF/leverage threshold does a dividend become possible, and have you stress-tested that against downside scenarios?"
  • Expected answer: Dividend not on the agenda until leverage <3.5x and FCF yield >8%; no stress test result disclosed
  • Good answer: Specific threshold; acknowledges dividend is secondary to deleveraging; stress test confirms threshold is achievable in base case
  • Bad answer: Management hints at earlier dividend as stakeholder-pleaser; or stress test not run
  • Follow-up probe: "Has the board discussed whether a share buyback or dividend is the preferred mechanism — and what is the current board preference?"
  • Diagnostic note: Premature dividend signalling = management prioritising optics over balance sheet = bear; buyback preference = management believes stock is cheap = modestly bullish

Q3.4 — Working capital management

  • Question: "What is your working capital cycle today vs your steady-state target — and what is the cash release opportunity?"
  • Expected answer: Working capital improved post-acquisition as legacy Vodafone processes streamlined; modest cash release opportunity (~€20-50M)
  • Good answer: Specific days-payable / days-receivable data; quantified cash release with timeline; no working capital one-off assumed in model
  • Bad answer: Working capital described as "normalised"; no data on underlying cycle
  • Follow-up probe: "Are there any working capital timing items in your 2024-2025 reported FCF that we should strip out for underlying FCF calculation?"
  • Diagnostic note: Hidden working capital timing flatters reported FCF — crucial for true FCF yield calculation

Q3.5 — M&A philosophy

  • Question: "Given the balance sheet, is any M&A possible in the next 36 months — and what would you actually want to buy?"
  • Expected answer: M&A off the table until leverage <3.5x; wishlist is fibre infrastructure or small Spanish MVNO consolidation
  • Good answer: Clear "no M&A until X" threshold; any stated appetite is modest bolt-on, not transformational
  • Bad answer: Management hints at "strategic opportunities being evaluated"; refuses to commit to M&A moratorium
  • Follow-up probe: "If a fibre wholesale asset came to market at an attractive price in 2025, would the board consider it even at current leverage?"
  • Diagnostic note: M&A appetite at current leverage = management prioritises scale over balance sheet repair = significant bear signal

6. THEME §4 — STRATEGY + COMPETITIVE POSITION

J-front verdict

  • Contested moat: ZEG's competitive position in Spain is mobile-led with limited fixed infrastructure ownership — strategic durability depends on network quality delta and brand repositioning success, both unproven at scale

Q4.1 — Win condition

  • Question: "In 3 years, what does Zegona look like if the strategy has fully worked — define 'success' in unit-economic terms?"
  • Expected answer: 30%+ EBITDA margin, postpaid market share stabilised, leverage <3x, brand repositioned as value-for-money premium
  • Good answer: Specific metric targets with ownership; distinguishes "what we control" from "what requires market cooperation"
  • Bad answer: Strategy described in competitive positioning language without unit-economic anchors
  • Follow-up probe: "Which of those success metrics is most at risk, and what is the current trend?"
  • Diagnostic note: Management that defines success with owned metrics vs externally-dependent metrics = better operator archetype

Q4.2 — Competitive response

  • Question: "How has MásMóvil or Orange Spain responded to your pricing actions in the last 6 months, and has that surprised you?"
  • Expected answer: MásMóvil aggressive on price but unable to match network quality; Orange rational; no material market disruption
  • Good answer: Specific competitor response named; management has a stated competitive reaction function
  • Bad answer: "Market is competitive" without specific competitor characterisation; or competitor response has been more aggressive than expected
  • Follow-up probe: "If MásMóvil drops price by 10% across all tiers next quarter, what is your response — match, absorb, or re-position?"
  • Diagnostic note: No stated competitive reaction function = strategy is reactive not proactive = bear signal on operator quality

Q4.3 — Regulatory positioning

  • Question: "What is your current relationship with CNMC, and are there any regulatory decisions in the next 18 months that could materially impact your economics?"
  • Expected answer: Constructive relationship; key risk is wholesale access pricing decision (likely H2-2025 or 2026)
  • Good answer: Specific regulatory calendar with management's assessment of outcome probabilities; no material adverse surprise expected
  • Bad answer: Regulatory risk dismissed; or management reveals an undisclosed CNMC investigation
  • Follow-up probe: "If the wholesale access pricing decision goes against you, what is the EBITDA impact — and have you modelled it?"
  • Diagnostic note: Undisclosed regulatory risk = false-friend; management with a specific regulatory impact model = operationally credible

Q4.4 — Moat trajectory

  • Question: "In 5 years, what will prevent a new entrant or a restructured competitor from taking 5 points of your market share?"
  • Expected answer: Network investment + brand repositioning creates switching cost; spectrum holdings provide barrier; fibre partnership deepens infrastructure moat
  • Good answer: Specific durable advantage named + evidence it is being actively built (capex allocation, NPS data, net adds trend)
  • Bad answer: "Our network is good" without comparative data; or no specific moat-building investment cited
  • Follow-up probe: "What is your network NPS vs the Spanish market average today — and what is the gap vs 12 months ago?"
  • Diagnostic note: Narrowing NPS gap = moat building on track; stagnant or widening gap = competitive position eroding despite cost investment

7. THEME §5 — MANAGEMENT QUALITY + OPERATOR TRACK RECORD

J-front verdict

  • Credibility unproven at scale: ZEG management (Eamonn O'Hare / team) has a track record from Virgin Media Spain but this is the first execution at this complexity level — operator archetype assessment is critical

Q5.1 — Operator track record

  • Question: "What is the single biggest operational mistake you've made since acquiring Vodafone Spain, and what did you change?"
  • Expected answer: Honest acknowledgement of integration execution delay or customer communication misstep; specific corrective action named
  • Good answer: Specific, owned, lesson-demonstrated — not a humblebrag disguised as a failure
  • Bad answer: "We've executed well overall" with no specific mistake; or mistake cited is trivial relative to deal complexity
  • Follow-up probe: "How has that change affected your integration timeline or synergy phasing?"
  • Diagnostic note: Inability to cite a real mistake = management communication style is defensive/investor-relations-optimised = bear for information quality going forward

Q5.2 — Sandbagging discipline

  • Question: "Looking back at your public guidance for 2024, what came in ahead of your internal model and what came in behind?"
  • Expected answer: Some beats, some misses; specific items named; overall honest characterisation of forecast accuracy
  • Good answer: Management distinguishes between what they controlled vs external factors; internal model vs public guidance gap acknowledged
  • Bad answer: "We hit our guidance" without internal model comparison; or management claims perfect foresight
  • Follow-up probe: "Do you guide conservatively vs your internal model — and by how many percentage points typically?"
  • Diagnostic note: Sandbagging habit = earnings upside potential; "guidance = internal model" = no cushion = downside risk if miss occurs

Q5.3 — Power of incentives (MANDATORY per Q20-MMO gate)

  • Question: "Walk us through the compensation structure — what metrics determine your annual bonus, and what are the equity vesting conditions?"
  • Expected answer: EBITDA + FCF + leverage ratio drive annual bonus; equity vests over 3-4 years with market cap or TSR condition
  • Good answer: Specific metric weightings; equity cliff that aligns with 2-3Y investment horizon; no short-term earnings gaming incentive
  • Bad answer: EBITDA-only bonus with no FCF gate = incentive to sacrifice cash conversion; or large near-term vest = management may be thinking about exit, not build
  • Follow-up probe: "Is there any scenario where hitting your bonus metrics creates a tension with what's best for long-term equity holders?"
  • Diagnostic note: Concept A core probe — compensation structure predicts behaviour more reliably than stated strategy; misaligned incentives = structural execution risk

Q5.4 — Culture archetype

  • Question: "How do you make big resource-allocation decisions — what is the process and who is in the room?"
  • Expected answer: Small core team + board; data-driven with specific financial return hurdles; CEO has final call but challenges are surfaced
  • Good answer: Clear decision rights; evidence of dissent being welcomed; financial return threshold named (e.g. ">15% IRR or we don't proceed")
  • Bad answer: Decision process described vaguely; or CEO describes a consensus-led process that implies slow/political capital allocation
  • Follow-up probe: "Can you give me an example of a resource-allocation decision you've reversed or stopped in the last 12 months?"
  • Diagnostic note: Reversals = intellectual flexibility = good operator; no reversals = either nothing went wrong (lucky) or culture doesn't surface bad news (bear)

Q5.5 — Communication clarity

  • Question: "What metric do you track most obsessively — the one number that, if it moved against you, would most worry you?"
  • Expected answer: Postpaid churn rate or ARPU; real-time tracking; clear articulation of why that metric is the leading indicator
  • Good answer: Specific metric + current value + trend + action threshold — reveals how management actually runs the business
  • Bad answer: Generic "we watch all our KPIs"; or metric named is a lagging indicator (e.g. annual revenue)
  • Follow-up probe: "What is that metric currently — and is it moving in the right direction?"
  • Diagnostic note: ⚡ This is the WH Smith probe — the single most unconventional question in the set; management's immediate metric reveals true operating priorities vs stated strategy

8. THEME §6 — RISK + DOWNSIDE (INCL. PRE-MORTEM CROSS-REF)

J-front verdict

  • Three plausible failure modes: (1) Churn spike post-brand migration destroying revenue; (2) Leverage refinancing risk if FCF misses; (3) Competitive price war preventing ARPU recovery — Q19 ACH matrix aligns to all three

Q6.1 — Bear-case probe: churn vector

  • Question: "If churn accelerates by 200bps above plan for 6 consecutive months — what triggers that scenario and how do you detect it early?"
  • Expected answer: Early warning via weekly NPS data + social listening; 200bps would be visible within 4-6 weeks; management has a pre-planned retention playbook
  • Good answer: Specific trigger (e.g. competitor price action of specific magnitude); retention intervention already staged and costed
  • Bad answer: 200bps scenario described as "very unlikely"; no pre-staged retention playbook
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the retention offer cost per subscriber in your playbook — and at what ARPU does the retention spend destroy value?"
  • Diagnostic note: Cross-ref Q19 ACH — churn acceleration is the single highest-plausibility failure mode; management with no pre-staged playbook = operational immaturity

Q6.2 — Failure mode test: refinancing cliff

  • Question: "Walk me through your worst-case refinancing scenario — what does the waterfall look like if credit markets are closed in your primary refinancing window?"
  • Expected answer: Fallback includes revolving credit facility + possible asset monetisation; worst-case equity raise at dilutive price not ruled out
  • Good answer: Specific liquidity buffer quantified; management can articulate the sequence of fallback options with clear decision triggers
  • Bad answer: "We don't expect to need an equity raise"; scenario not modelled; or liquidity buffer not quantified
  • Follow-up probe: "What asset could you sell quickly and at what EBITDA multiple, if you needed liquidity in 90 days?"
  • Diagnostic note: Cross-ref Q19 ACH — leverage/refinancing risk is second-highest plausibility failure mode; no sellable asset = binary equity raise risk

Q6.3 — Pricing fragility

  • Question: "At what point does competitive pricing pressure force you to cut ARPU — and what is the EBITDA sensitivity per €1 of ARPU loss?"
  • Expected answer: €1 ARPU loss = ~€X M EBITDA (management should be able to give this instantly); competitive threshold is a 15-20% market price decline
  • Good answer: Instant numeric answer; competitive threshold identified; management has modelled and has a stated response
  • Bad answer: Cannot give the EBITDA sensitivity per €1 ARPU; or threshold is lower than analyst expected (more fragile)
  • Follow-up probe: "Has any competitor in the last 12 months come close to that threshold — and what did you do?"
  • Diagnostic note: Cannot answer EBITDA/ARPU sensitivity instantly = management doesn't run the business on unit economics = operator quality concern

Q6.4 — False-friend probe

  • Question: "Is there any metric in your public reporting that looks better than the underlying reality — something where the headline number is misleading?"
  • Expected answer: This question is designed to surface candour — a good operator will name something; a less candid one will deflect
  • Good answer: Specific item named (e.g. "our reported EBITDA includes a one-off synergy cost-avoidance that won't repeat") — demonstrates intellectual honesty
  • Bad answer: "Our reporting is transparent" with no specific item cited
  • Follow-up probe: "Is there anything in your EBITDA adjustments that a sceptical analyst should strip out for underlying performance assessment?"
  • Diagnostic note: ⚡ This is the most uncomfortably direct question in the set — management's willingness to answer candidly is itself a management quality signal (per Concept A: what management is incentivised to withhold is most valuable to learn)

Q6.5 — Regulatory exposure

  • Question: "What is your single largest unmodelled regulatory risk, and what is the management's internal probability assessment?"
  • Expected answer: Wholesale access pricing or spectrum renewal cost; internal probability of adverse outcome <30%; sized at €X M EBITDA impact
  • Good answer: Specific risk named; probability + impact both quantified; mitigation strategy outlined
  • Bad answer: "Regulatory environment is stable" without specifics; or management reveals they haven't modelled the impact
  • Follow-up probe: "If the CNMC makes a decision adverse to your wholesale terms in 2025, what is your response — litigate, absorb, or reprice?"
  • Diagnostic note: Cross-ref Q19 ACH — regulatory exposure is a moderate-probability, high-impact risk not well-covered in consensus

Q19 ACH diagnostic items NOT covered by questions above:

  • Spectrum renewal auction timing and cost — partially covered in Q4.3 + Q2.2 but no dedicated spectrum cost question; consider adding
  • Technology disruption (satellite broadband penetration in Spain urban/rural) — not covered; low probability but non-zero
  • Management team key-person risk (CEO departure scenario) — not covered; add if board composition/succession is accessible

Sceptical bullet: The false-friend probe (Q6.4) and the leverage waterfall question (Q6.2) are the two questions management is most likely to give scripted non-answers on — if both produce deflection, the risk management/disclosure culture is poorer than the bull case requires

9. CROSS-CUTTING META + NON-OBVIOUS QUESTIONS

J-front verdict

  • Unconventional questions are load-bearing here: the questions management hasn't been asked by sell-side are more diagnostic than any standard metric question — §8 contains the three highest-signal probes

Non-obvious angle #1:

  • Question: "If Vodafone Group called you tomorrow and offered to buy back the Spanish business at your acquisition price — would you sell?"
  • Context: This surfaces management's true conviction in the strategy vs deal-maker mentality
  • Good answer signature: Immediate "no — here's why the value isn't realised yet" with specific milestone cited
  • Bad answer signature: Hedging; "it depends on the price"; reveals optionality thinking rather than builder thinking
  • Diagnostic note: Concept A — management incentives on exit vs build; if CEO is a deal-maker archetype rather than operator archetype, this answer is the tell

Non-obvious angle #2:

  • Question: "What is the most surprising piece of feedback from Spanish customers in the last 6 months — something that changed how you run the business?"
  • Context: WH Smith probe pattern — unconventional question surfaces non-consensus customer insight
  • Good answer signature: Specific, unexpected insight that has driven a product or pricing change; demonstrates customer-centricity
  • Bad answer signature: "Customers want better network and lower prices" — generic, unrehearsed mediocrity
  • Diagnostic note: Operator quality signal — the best operators process customer feedback in ways that generate proprietary insight; the worst operators have NPS dashboards but no behavioural response

Non-obvious angle #3 (⚡):

  • Question: "What decision have you changed your mind on most significantly in the last 12 months — specifically about how Spaniards buy telecom services?"
  • Context: Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 3 — multiple truths coexist; this question invites the "and yet" answer that reveals genuine learning vs confirmation-bias management
  • Good answer signature: Specific belief revision with named consequence for strategy (e.g. "we thought prepaid-to-postpaid migration would be faster — it isn't — here's what we've adjusted")
  • Bad answer signature: "Our original thesis has been validated"
  • Diagnostic note: Management that can cite a genuine belief revision = high-quality epistemic culture = better operator signal; zero belief revision in a complex integration = overconfidence or communication-management mode

Cross-roads question (Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 1):

  • Question: "What question do investors ask that you find most frustrating — and why?"
  • Context: Surfaces the gap between management's self-perception and investor concerns
  • Good answer signature: Names a specific question; explains why it misunderstands the business; teaches the analyst something about the actual driver
  • Bad answer signature: "All questions are fair" — management is performing rather than engaging

Diagnostic crystalliser:

  • Question: "If your 2026 EBITDA comes in 15% below your current model — what is the most likely cause, and what early indicator would have flagged it 12 months earlier?"
  • Context: Tests management's own bear-case thinking; the early indicator named reveals the metric they track internally
  • Good answer: Specific cause + specific early indicator with current trend cited
  • Bad answer: Deflects; or names a macro factor they can't control (avoids internal accountability)

Multiple-truths probe (Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 3):

  • Question: "The bull case on Zegona is obvious. What is the bear case that keeps YOU up at night — the one you think the market underestimates?"
  • Context: Invites management to simultaneously acknowledge the bull case and articulate the bear case — prevents either/or framing
  • Good answer: Specific, internal bear case that is more specific than public risk factors; shows they've stress-tested their own thesis
  • Bad answer: Recites standard risk factors; or deflects to macro (FX, Spanish GDP) rather than company-specific

Stewing trigger:

  • Question: "What are the 3 things you wish you had known before signing the Vodafone Spain acquisition agreement?"
  • Context: Retrospective question designed to surface hidden integration risks that are not yet resolved
  • Good answer: 3 specific items named; management has addressed or is addressing each; no items are still unresolved surprises
  • Bad answer: Only 1-2 items cited (management is editing); or one of the items is still unresolved with no clear action

Sceptical bullet — questions NOT included (Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 1 — go to the most-different question):

The analyst has NOT asked the following questions, which may be the most diagnostic:

  • "At what stock price would you sell your personal equity position?" — feels inappropriate but directly tests CEO conviction vs financial self-interest
  • "Which of your direct reports do you trust least, and what does that person do?" — organisational weak-link detection; too personal for most meetings but the answer is the most diagnostic operator quality signal available
  • "What will Vodafone Spain be worth in 5 years to a trade buyer — give me a number?" — forces management to anchor to a terminal value, surfacing whether they're running a business or managing an exit

These questions surface the most uncomfortable truths — the fact they feel uncomfortable to ask is the reason they should be on the list per Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 1
