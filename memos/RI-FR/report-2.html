§1 — Brief Recap + Key Components Summary

  • Net verdict: [#J] RI-FR is in a multi-front contraction — US destocking, China cognac collapse, and a Brown-Forman merger that distracted and then failed — with a credible but unproven cost efficiency plan as the primary near-term lever.
  • Leadership change: [#I] Alexandre Ricard remains Chairman and CEO; no group CEO change occurred. The "Nicolas Krantz" appointment was to Wyborowa Pernod Ricard and Central & Eastern Europe from January 2026 — a regional role, not a group succession. The Cognac division head was replaced (Neusch → Morizot) in May 2025.
  • Strategy shift: [#A] The Transform & Accelerate programme (FY23–25) completed, replaced by the €1bn Fit for Future operational efficiency programme (FY26–29); one-third of savings targeted for FY26. Brand portfolio pruning active — Imperial Blue sold for €412.6m in December 2025.
  • Priority change: [#A] CEO messaging pivoted from growth-led medium-term framework (+4% to +7%) to a "transition year" narrative for FY26, with recovery skewed to H2 FY26 and medium-term guidance of +3% to +6% reinstated only for FY27–29.
  • Financial profile: [#I] FY25 organic net sales -3.0%; FY26 9M organic -3% to -4% (revised downward in April 2026 on Middle East conflict escalation); margin expanded +64bps in FY25 driven by cost discipline; net debt/EBITDA 3.3x — above comfort zone, targeting sub-3x by FY29.
  • Demand — US destocking: [#A] US channel fell -6% in FY25 and -12% in Q3 FY26 organically; spirits industry faces a $22bn global inventory glut post-pandemic; management guides normalisation skewed to H2 FY26 but remains dependent on distributor confidence.
  • Demand — India: [#A] ⚡ India is RI's largest market by volume, second-largest by value — Q3 FY26 India +11% YTD — and is exploring an IPO of the Indian subsidiary (process begun April 2026, subsequently paused to prioritise debt reduction). India offers a genuine structural growth vector.
  • Demand — China: [#I] China -21% in FY25, -7% further in Q3 FY26. Martell duty-free suspended under China's anti-dumping response to EU EV tariffs (October 2024); RI won a minimum-price exemption from the headline 30.6% tariff, but actual recovery remains weak.
  • Competition: [#A] Brown-Forman merger talks confirmed March 2026, terminated April 2026 without agreement — a major M&A distraction that resolved cleanly but signals RI's awareness of scale disadvantage vs Diageo.
  • Disruption: [#A] No/low alcohol, RTD cocktails, and GLP-1 drugs compound category-level volume pressure on vodka and gin specifically — Absolut exposed; Jameson Irish whiskey partially insulated by cocktail relevance.
  • Political/regulatory: [#I] US 15% tariff on EU spirits (Irish whiskey ~15%, Scotch 10% post-relief) adds price friction on imported spirits at retail; EU health labelling rules advancing.
  • Macro: [#I] USD/EUR and INR/EUR are the two most material FX exposures; INR depreciation partially offsets India growth in group P&L; USD weakness amplifies reported revenue headwinds.
  • Track record: [#J] RI has systematically missed its own medium-term growth framework over FY23–FY26; two consecutive years of organic revenue decline raises the question of whether recovery to +3–6% is achievable without China and US normalising simultaneously.

§2 — Change in Leadership

  • Net verdict: [#J] No group CEO change has occurred — the premise of a "Nicolas Krantz" group succession is factually incorrect. Ricard family control remains intact through Alexandre Ricard; leadership risk is internal continuity rather than external disruption.
  • Chairman/CEO: [#I] Alexandre Ricard has been Chairman and CEO since February 2015; he presented both FY25 (August 2025) and H1 FY26 (February 2026) results in person; no succession announcement has been made.
  • Regional mis-attribution: [#I] Nicolas Krantz was appointed CEO of Wyborowa Pernod Ricard and Pernod Ricard Central & Eastern Europe from 1 January 2026; previously CEO of Corby Spirit and Wine (Canada) for five years, where he led the company through pandemic and oversaw RTD segment growth.
  • North America CEO: [#I] Conor McQuaid appointed CEO Pernod Ricard North America (USA and Canada) effective 1 January 2024; this is a significant hire given US is ~28% of group sales and in deepest distress.
  • Cognac division head: [#I] Philippe Neusch replaced as head of the Martell cognac division in May 2025 after Martell's collapse drove ~90% of the group's H1 FY25 organic sales decline. Replaced by François-Xavier Morizot, previously head of champagne (Mumm and Perrier-Jouët), who now runs both divisions.
  • Cognac leadership read-across: [#A] Merging the cognac and champagne division heads signals cost discipline at the divisional level and possibly a strategic demotion of Martell's standalone priority — either a belt-tightening response to China or a structural repositioning of the brand's weight within the portfolio.
  • Board composition: [#I] The Ricard family remains the controlling shareholder block; the board includes independent directors but family influence on strategic decisions (e.g., the Brown-Forman merger termination) is structurally significant.
  • M&A distraction: [#A] Merger talks with Brown-Forman were confirmed 26 March 2026 and terminated 28 April 2026 — a 33-day public M&A process that produced no deal and absorbed management bandwidth during the critical Q4 FY26 operating period.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] If family control insulates CEO tenure regardless of delivery, then the absence of a CEO change despite three years of declining organic sales may signal structural governance rigidity — the leadership change signal the market normally receives is suppressed.

§3 — Change in Strategy/Structure

  • Net verdict: [#J] Strategy has pivoted from growth-led expansion (Transform & Accelerate, FY23–25) to cost-and-cash preservation (Fit for Future, FY26–29) — a defensive repositioning that reflects the external environment but reduces medium-term optionality.
  • Fit for Future programme: [#I] The €1bn operational efficiency programme spans FY26–29; one-third (~€333m) targeted for FY26; focus areas include a leaner organisational structure, AI-driven digital transformation, and working capital discipline.
  • Previous programme completion: [#I] The €900m efficiency programme (FY23–25) was completed in FY25; contributed to the +64bps organic operating margin expansion that year; the FY25 FCF of €1.1bn (+18% YoY) is the clearest evidence of real execution.
  • Structure costs: [#I] Structure costs fell -10% in H1 FY26 driven by the Fit for Future operating model rollout; strategic investments revised to ~€750m (from a higher prior level).
  • Portfolio pruning: [#I] Imperial Blue (Indian whisky) sold to Tilaknagar Industries in December 2025 for €412.6m; the brand was a volume driver but low-margin; disposal improves mix and reduces complexity in India's excise-complex state-by-state market.
  • Brand prioritisation: [#A] Core "Strategic International Brands" (Jameson, Absolut, Chivas Regal, Ballantine's, Martell, Beefeater, Malibu) receive the bulk of A&P; A&P ratio maintained at ~16% of sales even as sales fall — this is a deliberate choice to protect brand desirability over short-term profit.
  • India strategic intensification: [#A] RI India revenue grew +11% in the first nine months of FY26; the planned IPO of the Indian subsidiary — announced April 2026, subsequently paused to prioritise debt reduction — signals a structural recognition that India is undervalued within the group.
  • India distillery investment: [#I] €200m committed to build RI's largest Asian distillery in Nagpur, Maharashtra, to support Longitude 77 (Indian single malt) exports and Seagram's portfolio scale.
  • Seagrams export push: [#I] RI India plans to expand Seagram's portfolio exports from 40 to 70 markets, targeting the Indian diaspora; Royal Stag and Blenders Pride identified as the key vehicles.
  • US recovery strategy: [#A] Conor McQuaid's North America brief focuses on distributor relationship repair, SKU rationalisation, and selective pricing discipline — a reset from the aggressive pricing-over-volume strategy that exacerbated destocking in FY24.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] A cost-efficiency programme that delivers margin via structure cuts rather than volume recovery is only durable if demand returns — if China and US normalisation is delayed beyond FY27, the Fit for Future savings are absorbed by operating deleverage, not incremental margin.

§4 — Change in Priorities/Ambition

  • Net verdict: [#J] The CEO's stated priorities have de-prioritised top-line growth ambition in favour of cost discipline, cash generation, and balance sheet repair — a rational response to the environment but a meaningful step-down from the FY23 peak-of-cycle messaging.
  • FY25 messaging (August 2025): [#I] Alexandre Ricard's FY25 headline: "Steering through a challenging environment with agility, discipline and strategic conviction." Volume recovery cited (+2% group volumes, three consecutive semesters of growth) but organic revenue still -3.0%.
  • H1 FY26 messaging (February 2026): [#I] "FY26 is a transition year" — emphasis on improving trends skewed to H2; three priorities stated: brand desirability, organisational efficiency, cash generation. No return to specific medium-term growth targets for FY26.
  • Medium-term framework reinstated: [#I] Organic net sales guidance of +3% to +6% p.a. on average is reinstated for FY27–29, supported by the €1bn efficiencies. This is a moderated version of the prior +4% to +7% framework.
  • Q3 FY26 (April 2026): [#I] FY26 full-year guidance revised to organic net sales -3% to -4% (from a flat expectation) citing escalating Middle East conflict; volumes returned to growth (+4%) in Q3 but US and China remain contractionary.
  • A&P discipline: [#I] A&P ratio held at ~16% of sales despite revenue decline — management explicitly prioritised brand investment over short-term margin defence.
  • Dividend: [#I] Dividend held flat at €4.70/share for FY25 — a signal of balance sheet confidence but also of earnings pressure (cover is thinning relative to the FY23 peak).
  • Capital allocation shift: [#A] Strategic investment envelope reduced from higher levels to ~€750m; capex discipline reflects cash generation priority; the €200m Nagpur distillery represents the single largest ring-fenced growth spend.
  • Brown-Forman episode read-through: [#A] Entering and then exiting merger talks within 33 days suggests the deal economics were unattractive to one or both sides, but the fact RI was at the table signals management's recognition that organic growth alone may not close the valuation gap vs Diageo.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] If the "transition year" narrative stretches from FY26 into FY27 — as it could if China duty-free remains disrupted and US normalisation is slow — then the medium-term +3–6% guidance becomes a forward promise rather than a near-term catalyst, and the market discount may deepen.

§5 — Financial Profile Change

  • Net verdict: [#J] RI's financial profile has deteriorated from peak-cycle (FY23: +10% organic, 28%+ EBIT margin) to a structurally pressured state — revenue declining, leverage above target, and FCF the lone bright spot, which is not sufficient to re-rate the equity.
  • Revenue trajectory: [#I] FY22 +17% organic, FY23 +10% organic, FY24 -2% organic, FY25 -3% organic, FY26 guided -3% to -4% organic. Three consecutive years of organic decline is atypical for a premium spirits company; by comparison, ❌ Diageo delivered flat to slightly declining, but did not sustain three consecutive years of decline.
  • Operating margin: [#A] FY25 organic operating margin +64bps — driven by cost discipline (Fit for Future early wins) rather than volume leverage; this inverts the normal premium spirits margin profile which relies on mix and operating leverage.
  • EBITDA absolute: [#A] Organic margin expansion does not prevent absolute EBITDA erosion when revenues fall 3%+; the absolute profit pool is shrinking even as the percentage margin holds.
  • FCF: [#I] FY25 FCF €1.1bn, +18% YoY — driven by strong operating working capital management; this is a genuine positive and the clearest evidence of Fit for Future early execution.
  • Leverage: [#I] Net debt/EBITDA 3.3x as of FY25 end; target is below 3.0x by FY29; ❌ this is elevated relative to Diageo (~2.8–3.0x) and significantly above Campari (~3.5x but shrinking). The gap vs Diageo is narrow but directionally unfavourable.
  • Imperial Blue disposal: [#I] The €412.6m Imperial Blue sale proceeds provide balance sheet headroom; combined with the India IPO pause (prioritising debt reduction), management is signalling leverage reduction as a 12-month priority.
  • Peer FCF yield comparison: [#A] At current share price levels (-39% from 52W high), RI trades on a compressed multiple that implies meaningful FCF yield uplift on a normalised earnings basis — but normalisation is multi-year away.
  • A&P as % of sales: [#I] ~16% of sales maintained; this is sector-normal (Diageo ~16-17%); holding this during revenue decline means A&P absolute spend is falling in line with revenue — a managed reduction rather than a brand investment increase.
  • Dividend cover: [#A] Flat €4.70/share dividend vs declining earnings = thinning cover; not yet at risk but watch if FY26 FCF delivery misses the FY25 €1.1bn benchmark.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] ❌ Three consecutive years of organic revenue decline with net debt/EBITDA at 3.3x and a dividend that absorbs a growing share of FCF is a fragile combination — if China recovery extends into FY28, leverage targeting becomes a binding constraint on investment and capital return.

§6 — Value Chain: Demand

  • Net verdict: [#J] Demand is bifurcated: India is structurally growing and partially compensates, but the two largest profit pools — US and China — remain contractionary in FY26, and the recovery timeline for both is credibly H2 FY26 at the earliest but more likely FY27.
  • US channel — scale: [#I] The US is approximately 28% of RI group sales and the primary profit pool for Jameson, Absolut, and Malibu; US organic sales fell -6% in FY25 and -12% in Q3 FY26 — the second consecutive year of accelerating decline.
  • US destocking mechanism: [#A] Post-pandemic over-ordering by distributors and retailers created a $22bn global spirits inventory glut; US wholesalers right-sized holdings across FY24 and FY25; RI management signals the worst is behind but Q3 FY26 data (-12%) has not confirmed a floor.
  • US spirits volumes: [#A] Industry-wide spirits volume down ~5% in 2024 at retail; vodka specifically projected -5.15% for full year 2024; RI's US portfolio (Absolut, Jameson, Malibu) is concentrated in the highest-destocked categories.
  • India — structural opportunity: [#A] ⚡ India is RI's largest market by volume and second largest by value; revenue grew +11% in the first nine months of FY26; India contributes ~12% of global group revenue; structural drivers include a growing middle class, urbanisation, and premiumisation from Indian Made Foreign Liquor to international brands.
  • Seagram's dominance: [#I] Royal Stag and Blenders Pride are leading Indian whisky brands by volume; Seagram's portfolio covers all major Indian whisky price tiers; Longitude 77 (Indian single malt, 2023 launch) opens the premium single malt segment which IMFL brands historically could not address.
  • India premiumisation: [#I] Pernod Ricard India aims to triple revenue by 2034; this implies ~10% CAGR sustained, consistent with recent actuals; state-by-state excise complexity is the primary friction limiting the pace of premiumisation.
  • China — Martell cognac: [#I] China ~10-12% of group sales; Martell accounts for the majority of China exposure; China sales -21% in FY25 (down from -26% in Q1 FY25 and -27% in H1 FY25); duty-free suspension is the proximate cause, not structural demand collapse.
  • China duty-free pathway: [#A] The provisional anti-dumping duties on EU brandy came into force October 2024; RI won a minimum-price exemption from the headline 30.6% tariff; duty-free channel recovery was projected for October–December 2025 but has not yet fully materialised given ongoing geopolitical tensions.
  • European premiumisation: [#I] European volumes relatively resilient — Ballantine's and Chivas performing credibly in Europe; Q3 FY26 rest-of-world excluding US and China grew +5%; this underscores that the problem is geographic, not brand-structural.
  • Global Travel Retail: [#I] GTR grew +11% in Q3 FY26 with traveller numbers remaining strong ahead of pre-COVID levels; GTR is a meaningful channel for Martell, Chivas, and Absolut and provides partial China proxy exposure via Chinese travellers.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] India's volume scale (the metric it leads on) does not straightforwardly translate to margin leadership; Indian whisky margins are lower than Scotch, cognac, or premium vodka, so India's compensation for US and China erosion is partial rather than full at the profit line.

§7 — Value Chain: Competition

  • Net verdict: [#J] RI faces structurally disadvantaged head-to-head competition in most of its largest categories; Diageo's scale in Scotch and Johnnie Walker, Brown-Forman's dominance in bourbon and Jameson's fastest-growing sub-category, and Tito's in vodka all create share headwinds.
  • Diageo — Scotch: [#A] Ballantine's vs Johnnie Walker (Diageo); Johnnie Walker is the world's number-one Scotch brand and significantly outsells Ballantine's at the global level; Diageo's Scotch scale advantage in production, distribution, and brand equity is substantial.
  • Diageo — North America: [#A] Captain Morgan (Diageo) vs Malibu ; both are flavoured/spiced rum/coconut brands competing for the same US on-trade and casual occasion; Captain Morgan retains share leadership in the rum-adjacent segment.
  • Brown-Forman — bourbon vs Irish whiskey: [#A] Jack Daniel's is the world's best-selling American whiskey; Jameson competes in the "smooth/approachable whiskey" adjacent space and is taking US share — particularly among cocktail occasions — at the expense of Jack Daniel's rather than bourbon per se; RI's terminated merger with BF would have merged these two competing growth brands.
  • Tito's Handmade Vodka: [#A] Tito's has captured ~14.4% US vodka market share (11.6m cases) — domestically positioned, lower price point perception, no tariff exposure; directly competes with Absolut's US volume base; ❌ Absolut dropped 4% to 2.68m cases in the US in 2024 while Tito's grew.
  • Craft distillers — US: [#A] More than 10,000 US craft distilleries now operate; concentrated impact in gin and small-batch whiskey; Beefeater and Jameson face increasing local competition in bartender and craft-cocktail occasions.
  • Rémy Cointreau — cognac: [#I] Rémy Martin and Martell are the two major standalone cognac competitors in China; both suffered from the same anti-dumping headwinds; Rémy Cointreau's greater concentration in China makes its pain more acute, but also means its recovery, when it comes, will be larger.
  • Heaven Hill — premium spirits: [#A] Heaven Hill (Evan Williams, Elijah Craig) competes below Jameson's price point and benefits from bourbon's continued US consumer preference trend; no direct head-to-head but absorbs some of the whiskey drinker who trades down from premium imported Irish.
  • Diageo delivery comparison: [#A] Both Diageo and RI sustained organic revenue declines in FY25; Diageo delivered -0.6% organic in FY24, RI delivered -2%; in FY25 Diageo delivered flat to slightly declining, RI -3%; RI has underperformed Diageo on organic revenue delivery across two consecutive fiscal years.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] If the Brown-Forman merger had completed, the combined entity's Jack Daniel's + Jameson Irish whiskey combination would have created the world's most powerful whiskey portfolio — the termination restores both companies' competitive positions but leaves RI without the bourbon exposure that is currently the US on-trade growth driver.

§8 — Value Chain: Disruption

  • Net verdict: [#J] Structural disruption from no/low alcohol, RTDs, and GLP-1 drugs is real but category-specific — vodka and gin are most exposed; Jameson Irish whiskey is the most insulated RI brand; the near-term destocking problem is cyclical, but disruption compounds it.
  • No/low alcohol trend: [#A] The sober-curious and moderation movement has gone mainstream; IWSR reports strong growth in no-alcohol categories through 2024, with RTD no-alcohol the fastest-growing sub-segment; this disproportionately affects occasions where Absolut and Beefeater have historically performed (vodka-sodas, G&Ts).
  • Absolut vulnerability: [#A] ❌ Absolut is the most structurally exposed RI brand to no/low alcohol and RTD cocktail disruption; vodka category projected -5.15% decline in US in 2024; Absolut at 11.9m cases in FY24 is -8% from its 2023 peak of 13.0m cases; the brand has rebounded modestly (+0.5% in 2024) but is not in structural growth.
  • RTD cocktails: [#A] Spirits-based RTDs grew ~20% in 2025 while overall spirits revenue fell -4.3%; this represents category disruption — consumers migrating from buying bottles to buying pre-made serves at lower price points per occasion; RI has some RTD exposure (Malibu RTDs, Olmeca Altos tequila cocktails) but is not a category leader.
  • RTD cannibalisation mechanism: [#A] RTD growth is partly cannibalising premium spirit bottle purchases — the same consumer who would have bought a bottle of Absolut for home cocktails now buys a can instead; this reduces average transaction value and depresses spirits volume even as RTD spirits-base volumes grow.
  • GLP-1 drugs — mechanism: [#A] GLP-1 receptor agonists (Ozempic, Wegovy) reduce dopamine response to alcohol, slow gastric emptying (higher blood alcohol effect), and clinically reduce alcohol consumption by 23–41% in users; 39% of UK GLP-1 users report drinking less since starting treatment.
  • GLP-1 — current scale: [#A] GLP-1 penetration is still sub-10% of the adult population in most markets; the near-term volume impact is modest but the directional risk is real, particularly for the casual occasion drinker who is also the target of Absolut and Malibu marketing.
  • Jameson cocktail resilience: [#I] Jameson's Irish whiskey growth is partially driven by cocktail culture (Jameson and ginger, Jameson and Coke); this occasion is somewhat insulated from GLP-1 effects as it skews to social/occasion drinking rather than habitual home consumption.
  • Bourbon trend vs Irish whiskey: [#A] The US bourbon renaissance (Wild Turkey, Buffalo Trace, Woodford Reserve) captures the premiumising whiskey drinker who might otherwise upgrade within Irish whiskey; Jameson's cocktail/casual positioning limits its ability to trade consumers up into ultra-premium at the same pace as bourbon.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] Disruption arguments for spirits have been made before (craft beer, wine as substitute, cannabis) and spirits proved durable; the GLP-1 effect may reduce excess consumption rather than eliminate moderate social drinking — but the combination of RTD, no/low, and GLP-1 represents an unusually broad multi-vector disruption with no obvious single brand offset.

§9 — Value Chain: Political/Regulatory

  • Net verdict: [#J] RI faces an unusually complex and adversarial regulatory environment in its three most important markets simultaneously — US tariffs on EU spirits, China anti-dumping duties on cognac, and India's state-level excise fragmentation — a coincidence of headwinds without historical precedent in recent years.
  • US tariffs — Irish whiskey: [#I] Irish whiskey (Republic of Ireland origin) currently faces 15% US tariff under Trump's Section 122 tariffs applied from April 2, 2025; Scotch whisky faces 10% after partial relief; EU spirits more broadly face 15%. Jameson is primarily produced in Ireland.
  • US tariff impact: [#A] The 15% tariff on Irish whiskey adds ~€12–15 per 9L case at import cost; this erodes distributor margins and may force price increases at retail, further suppressing volume in an already destocking channel. Scotch whisky tariff at 10% is costing the industry ~£20m/month in lost UK exports.
  • Scotch tariff differential: [#A] Scotch at 10% vs Irish at 15% creates a competitive distortion favouring Diageo's Scotch portfolio (Johnnie Walker) over RI's Jameson at the wholesale level; if the differential persists, it could shift bartender/buyer preference in blended and accessible whiskey segments.
  • China — cognac anti-dumping: [#I] China's anti-dumping investigation on EU brandy (launched January 2024) imposed provisional duties from October 2024; final duties set at 30.6% for Martell, up to 39% for other producers; RI won a minimum-price exemption allowing Martell to avoid the tariff if sold above a floor price.
  • China exemption mechanism: [#A] The minimum-price exemption is commercially real but operationally complex — it limits Martell's ability to participate in promotional pricing in Chinese duty-free and on-trade; the channel most impacted is duty-free, where cognac competes on gift-giving occasions.
  • China resolution timeline: [#A] The anti-dumping investigation was extended three months in Q2 2025; resolution was projected for April 2025, then October–December 2025; ongoing geopolitical tension between EU and China on EV tariffs means a full resolution is not assured in FY26.
  • India — excise complexity: [#I] India's spirits market is regulated state-by-state; each of 28 states sets its own excise duty, licensing regime, and price approval; this creates 28 effective sub-markets with different margin profiles, listing requirements, and channel structures.
  • India — IMFL/imported split: [#A] International spirits (Scotch, cognac, imported vodka) face higher state excise duties in India than Indian Made Foreign Liquor ; RI's growth engine is IMFL (Seagram's portfolio) rather than international imports — this is structurally favourable for tax burden but limits premium international brand penetration.
  • EU health labelling: [#I] EU alcohol health labelling proposals (calorie content, health warnings on labels) are advancing; the timeline for full implementation spans 2026–2028; near-term impact is modest but long-term changes to front-of-pack information for alcohol may deter casual occasion purchases.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] If US and China political tensions reverse — a US-EU tariff deal reducing spirits duties, a China-EU trade resolution removing cognac anti-dumping — the regulatory drag that explains much of RI's current underperformance would lift simultaneously with the underlying demand cycle, creating a more powerful recovery than the current consensus pricing implies.

§10 — Value Chain: Supply Side

  • Net verdict: [#J] Supply side is not a near-term constraint — the industry has too much maturing inventory, not too little — but the capital-intensive, time-locked nature of Scotch and cognac production creates strategic rigidity that limits RI's ability to pivot quickly if category dynamics shift.
  • Scotch whisky maturation: [#I] Ballantine's and Chivas Regal (including Royal Salute) are blended Scotch whiskies requiring substantial aged whisky inventory; Scotch must be matured for a minimum of 3 years, with premium expressions 12–25+ years; RI holds extensive maturing whisky inventory on its Scottish balance sheet.
  • Industry inventory glut: [#A] The global spirits industry is sitting on a $22bn inventory overhang post-pandemic boom; Suntory closed the Jim Beam distillery for at least a year; Diageo halted whiskey production at Texas and Tennessee facilities; RI is similarly managing maturing inventory above target levels — this is a cash drag but not a near-term shortage risk.
  • Irish whiskey — Midleton Distillery: [#I] Jameson is produced at Midleton, County Cork, Ireland's largest distillery; capacity expansion has been ongoing; post-COVID demand boom led to volume commitments that now sit ahead of near-term consumption run-rate.
  • Cognac — Martell Châtellerault: [#I] Martell produces cognac in the Charente region; cognac requires grape spirit matured in Limousin oak; the duty-free suspension in China directly reduces shipment volumes but does not affect the production cycle, leaving maturing inventory building.
  • Packaging and glass: [#A] Premium spirit bottle design is a key brand asset; glass supply has normalised after COVID disruptions; input costs (glass, cork, carton) are broadly stable in 2025-26.
  • Grain spirits — Absolut: [#I] Absolut vodka is produced from winter wheat in Åhus, Sweden; grain and energy costs are the primary input variables; European grain prices have moderated since 2022 peak, reducing input cost pressure; Absolut's COGS profile is comparatively asset-light versus aged spirit categories.
  • Currency risk in production: [#A] GBP/EUR (Scotch production in GBP, reporting in EUR), EUR (cognac and vodka production costs in EUR): RI's reporting currency matches most of its production currency, limiting structural currency mismatch in COGS.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] The maturing inventory overhang is a balance sheet item that constrains FCF and inflates working capital; if Scotch and cognac demand recovery is slower than the 2-3 year consensus, RI faces a multi-year period of holding excess inventory while continuing to fund production — a real cash drag not fully priced.

§11 — Revenue/Demand Cycle

  • Net verdict: [#J] RI is not at the trough of the cycle — Q3 FY26 US data (-12%) and full-year guidance revision (-3% to -4%) confirm destocking has not yet cleared; the question is whether H2 FY26 delivers the management-promised inflection or whether the cycle extends into FY27.
  • US destocking — current state: [#A] Management signals the worst is "likely behind" but Q3 FY26 US -12% contradicts a completed destocking narrative; the volume recovery (+4% in Q3) is at the group level driven by emerging markets and GTR, masking continuing US and China pressure.
  • Destocking mechanism: [#A] Distributor inventory levels normalise when retailers re-order matches consumption velocity; US spirits consumption was tracking modestly positive in 2024 (consumer demand) even as wholesale shipments declined — the gap between consumption and shipments is the destocking measure; this gap was estimated at 6-9 months of excess inventory in mid-2025.
  • US normalisation timeline: [#A] Consensus (management + sell-side) expects US distributor normalisation by H2 FY26 (October 2025–June 2026 period); if correct, RI's FY27 US revenues could rebound 5–8% on a normalised base. The April 2026 Middle East conflict-driven guidance revision suggests H2 FY26 inflection is now less certain.
  • India — secular or cyclical?: [#A] India's +6% (FY24) and +11% (9M FY26) growth is structurally supported: demographic tailwinds, expanding middle class, premiumisation from IMFL to premium Indian whisky and imported spirits; this is not a post-pandemic bounce; the IPO process (paused for debt reasons) is a management signal of structural confidence.
  • China — when does recovery begin?: [#A] The minimum-price exemption from anti-dumping duties is operational but the duty-free channel remains disrupted; management projected October–December 2025 duty-free resumption; Q3 FY26 data (-7% for China) shows no recovery yet; the most realistic China recovery timeline is FY27 at the earliest.
  • China structural vs cyclical: [#A] China premium spirits consumption is structurally positive (growing middle class, gifting culture, hospitality sector recovery post-COVID); the current decline is primarily driven by the anti-dumping regulatory barrier and channel disruption, not structural demand loss — this is a key distinction vs Absolut's more structural US challenges.
  • European demand: [#I] European markets are performing reasonably well; Ballantine's and Chivas broadly stable; GTR +11% in Q3 FY26 provides partial upside; European consumer discretionary resilience in spirits is a positive offset.
  • Cycle position read: [#A] Based on: destocking in progress in US, China regulatory disruption not resolved, Middle East conflict adding near-term consumer caution — RI is likely 2-3 quarters from a confirmed trough at the group revenue level; the H2 FY26 skew is management's best-case, not a certainty.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] If US normalisation is H2 FY26 but China recovery is FY27 and Europe remains stable, the aggregate recovery still produces only modest group organic growth in FY27 — the bullish scenario requires both US and China to normalise within 12 months of each other, which is not a given.

§12 — Value Chain: Macroeconomic
