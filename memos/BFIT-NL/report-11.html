[C] Basic-Fit N.V. — ESA — Value Chain Map @ 02-May-26

Executive Summary

Basic-Fit operates as a pure-play low-cost gym operator sitting at the consumer-facing end of a multi-stage fitness industry value chain. The company's model is deliberately asset-light at the ownership level — 100% leased real estate, third-party equipment procurement, outsourced digital content distribution — yet operationally dense, with ~1,500 clubs requiring sustained capital allocation for fit-out, maintenance, and technology. Its value proposition is simple: maximum gym access at minimum price, delivered through a standardised club format scaled aggressively across five continental European markets. The business earns its margin not through premium positioning but through membership volume and occupancy leverage on a largely fixed lease cost base. #A

The value chain position Basic-Fit occupies — branded gym operator and direct membership aggregator — is structurally advantageous in one critical respect: it controls the customer relationship entirely. There is no intermediary between the company and the end member. This gives Basic-Fit pricing authority, retention levers (app, premium tier, corporate), and direct data on usage patterns. What it does not control is the upstream cost stack: rent (the dominant expense and balance sheet liability), equipment vendors (Life Fitness, Matrix, Technogym — all independently scaled businesses with their own pricing power), and the underlying real estate market that governs site availability and lease economics. #A The 100% leased model means Basic-Fit's cost of capital is structurally embedded in the lease liability, not the P&L in an obvious way — a source of both financial flexibility and analytical opacity for investors. #J

Competitive positioning is sound but not exceptional at ESA stage. Basic-Fit is the largest low-cost gym operator on the continent by club count, giving it procurement scale over smaller national chains and a brand recognition advantage in core markets (Netherlands, Belgium, France). But the barriers to entry in low-cost fitness are structurally thin: no proprietary technology, no owned real estate, no exclusive supplier relationships, no regulatory moat. The premium tier (€33-38/month, reportedly 50% of new joiners) is encouraging and suggests members are willing to pay for incremental features — most likely the Basic-Fit Live app and wider access — but it is not yet clear whether this represents a sustainable pricing step-up or a temporary cohort effect from post-COVID re-engagement. PureGym, McFit, and Fitness Park all compete on similar value propositions and are capable of replicating format innovations. #J

Margin structure is characterised by high operating leverage: a large fixed lease cost base means incremental members are highly profitable but occupancy shortfalls rapidly erode EBITDA. The company's expansion into Spain represents both the largest near-term growth opportunity and the highest execution risk, given lower brand awareness, a less mature low-cost gym culture, and more fragmented real estate availability. Switching costs for individual members are low (monthly contracts are standard), but habitual usage and club proximity create practical stickiness that is better than the contractual terms suggest. Overall position sustainability is rated GREEN-YELLOW: the scale advantage and brand in core markets are real, but the lease-liability-heavy balance sheet, thin moat, and competitive exposure in expansion markets prevent a higher rating.

1. End-to-End Value Chain (15 stages)

The fitness industry value chain begins with physical infrastructure and ends with a recurring consumer health behaviour. Basic-Fit sits firmly in the middle-to-downstream segment — it is an aggregator and operator, not a manufacturer or landlord. Understanding the stages upstream and downstream of its position is essential to assessing where margin is captured, where power resides, and where vulnerability concentrates.

Stage 1: Commercial Real Estate Development. Property developers and REITs build and own the retail/commercial floorspace that gyms lease. Developer economics are driven by yield, not gym performance. Basic-Fit has no ownership stake here; it is a tenant. In tight urban markets (Amsterdam, Paris inner suburbs), landlord power is elevated. In secondary retail (suburban retail parks, former anchor tenant units) — where Basic-Fit disproportionately locates — landlord power is more balanced. Lease duration typically 10-15 years; Basic-Fit's entire fixed asset base is effectively rented from this stage. #A

Stage 2: Site Identification and Leasing. Before a club opens, Basic-Fit's property and development team assesses catchment areas, footfall data, competitor proximity, and lease economics. This is a proprietary capability: market-by-market site knowledge, landlord relationships, and the ability to underwrite lease terms against a membership density model. At scale (~1,500 clubs), this becomes a compounding advantage over smaller operators who lack the portfolio data to underwrite risk precisely. Internal function, not outsourced. #A

Stage 3: Gym Equipment Manufacturing. Life Fitness , Matrix (US/Taiwan), and Technogym (Italy) are the primary global gym equipment OEMs. They supply cardio machines, strength equipment, and connected fitness hardware. These companies have moderate pricing power — particularly Technogym, which positions as premium — but face competition from each other and from lower-cost Asian OEMs entering the market. Basic-Fit procures from multiple vendors and likely negotiates volume-based pricing. No proprietary equipment; Basic-Fit is a price-taker at this stage, though its scale gives it leverage. #A

Stage 4: Equipment Procurement and Fit-Out. Basic-Fit configures each club to a standardised layout: cardio floor, strength zone, functional training area. Fit-out also includes locker rooms, reception infrastructure, and digital access control. Capital cost per new club is approximately €1.5-2.5m (management estimates vary). Equipment is depreciated over the asset life; fit-out costs are capitalised. This is where Basic-Fit's capex is concentrated, and where the economics of expansion are most sensitive to build-cost inflation. #A

Stage 5: Digital Technology and Software. Club management systems (access control, membership admin, booking), CRM platforms, and the Basic-Fit Live app sit here. The app is an internally developed product (or contracted build) providing on-demand fitness content and live streaming — the primary differentiator of the premium membership tier. Software stack is not proprietary in a deep sense (no unique algorithmic asset), but the app is a genuine membership feature and retention lever. Investment in digital capability has been modest relative to pureplay digital fitness players. #A

Stage 6: Content Production (Digital Fitness). Basic-Fit Live requires ongoing fitness content — instructor-led classes, workouts, nutritional guidance — produced either in-house or via contracted trainers. This is a low-to-moderate cost layer but strategically important: content quality drives premium tier uptake and retention. Basic-Fit competes here not with Peloton or Les Mills globally, but with what a member could access freely via YouTube or Apple Fitness+. Content differentiation is a work in progress. #J

Stage 7: Club Operations and Staffing. Individual clubs are operated with minimal staffing — the low-cost model is predicated on near-unmanned operations (digital check-in, no front desk sales, self-serve). Staff costs are a small fraction of revenue relative to traditional gym operators. This is a genuine structural cost advantage. However, it also means member experience is almost entirely product-driven (equipment quality, cleanliness, layout) rather than service-driven. #A

Stage 8: Membership Sales and Marketing. Basic-Fit acquires members primarily through digital marketing (search, social, affiliate) and physical signage/walk-past at club locations. There is no commissioned sales force (another low-cost model advantage). B2B/corporate channel (estimated 5-10% of revenue) involves direct sales to employers and benefits platforms — a structurally different channel requiring account management capability. #A

Stage 9: Membership Administration and Billing. Monthly direct debit collection, contract management, cancellation processing, and fraud prevention. Operationally routine but competitively relevant: frictionless join/cancel processes reduce churn hesitation but also reduce lock-in. Basic-Fit's model relies on volume and low churn rates sustained by habit, not contractual lock-in. #A

Stage 10: Corporate/B2B Channel. Employers, health insurers, and employee benefits platforms (e.g., Alleo, edenred-adjacent) aggregate gym memberships and resell as employee perks. Basic-Fit participates as a supply-side gym network. Revenue per member through this channel is likely lower (discounted or subsidised rates), but customer acquisition cost is also lower. Estimated 5-10% of revenue — not dominant but growing as corporate wellness spend increases post-COVID. #A

Stage 11: Health Insurance / Government Subsidy Layer. In the Netherlands specifically, some health insurers partially reimburse gym membership as preventive health expenditure. In France, government schemes (Pass'Sport) subsidise youth fitness access. These schemes are intermittent and policy-dependent, but represent a demand-side subsidy that reduces member price sensitivity and drives incremental membership. Basic-Fit benefits disproportionately as the most accessible low-cost option. #A

Stage 12: Personal Training and Ancillary Services. Personal trainers operate in or around Basic-Fit clubs (often as independent contractors or via PT platform apps). Basic-Fit does not directly employ PTs at scale but may collect floor-access fees. Supplement retail, protein vending, and tanning booths are additional revenue streams in some clubs. Ancillary revenue is small as a proportion of total but high-margin at the incremental level. #I

Stage 13: Member Engagement and Retention. The ongoing relationship between a gym operator and its member base — driven by app engagement, class booking, usage tracking, and targeted communication. Basic-Fit's digital infrastructure (app, access data) gives it visibility into member usage patterns that can be deployed for churn prediction and intervention. This is an underdeveloped capability relative to what the data asset theoretically permits. #J

Stage 14: Exit / Churn. Members cancel for reasons including price sensitivity, relocation, injury, or competitive switching. At monthly contracts, churn is a continuous operational reality. Basic-Fit does not publicly disclose churn rates by market, making retention analysis difficult from the outside. Proxy: member count growth vs. club count growth gives a rough read on net member economics per club. #A

Stage 15: Secondary / Resale Markets (Emerging). Gym membership resale platforms (e.g., ClassPass-adjacent models) exist at the margin in European markets. Not material to Basic-Fit today, but directionally relevant: if aggregator platforms grow, they could mediate the consumer-operator relationship and erode Basic-Fit's direct customer ownership. Low probability near-term but a structural watch item. #J

2. Basic-Fit's Position in the Value Chain (15 dimensions)

Basic-Fit's value chain position is best characterised as a high-volume, low-price branded aggregator of physical fitness access, with direct consumer ownership, significant lease-driven operating leverage, and moderate but not deep competitive differentiation. The following dimensions map the specifics of that position against competitive alternatives and industry economics.

Dimension 1: Market Position by Scale. Basic-Fit is the largest low-cost gym operator in continental Europe by club count (~1,500 clubs as of early 2026). This is a genuine competitive asset: scale confers procurement leverage with equipment OEMs, brand recognition in established markets, and management bandwidth to execute rapid new club openings. No single continental competitor matches this footprint. PureGym is the closest comparable but remains predominantly UK-focused. McFit and Fitness Park are national-level players without Basic-Fit's geographic diversification. #A

Dimension 2: Real Estate Model. 100% leased portfolio with zero owned assets is the foundational operating choice. Advantages: capital efficiency (no equity tied up in property), flexibility to exit underperforming markets, lower depreciation burden. Disadvantages: lease liabilities are the dominant balance sheet item (IFRS 16 treatment makes leverage look elevated), rent inflation is an unhedged cost exposure, and landlords capture property value appreciation. Basic-Fit is permanently a tenant, not a landowner. In a rising rent environment, this is a structural cost headwind. #A

Dimension 3: Pricing Architecture. Two-tier pricing: standard (approximately €20-25/month) and premium (approximately €33-38/month). Premium tier includes Basic-Fit Live app access, multi-club access across the network, and potentially other features. Reported 50% of new joiners taking premium is, if sustained, a significant ARPU (average revenue per user) step-up: it implies blended ARPU of approximately €27-30/month vs. a pure-standard model at €22/month. This is a meaningful revenue density improvement on a fixed lease cost base. Whether it is structural or a post-launch cohort effect from post-COVID re-engagement requires monitoring. #J

Dimension 4: Geographic Spread and Revenue Mix. Five markets: Netherlands (most mature, highest margin), Belgium, Luxembourg, France (largest by club count, still scaling), Spain (youngest, highest growth drag). Geographic diversification reduces single-market regulatory or macro risk but complicates margin analysis. France and Spain are EBITDA drag markets in the near term as new clubs ramp toward maturity. The Netherlands and Belgium are cash-generative cores funding expansion. Management's ability to sustain this internal cross-subsidy is a key financial watch item. #A

Dimension 5: Lease Liability and Financial Structure. Under IFRS 16, Basic-Fit's operating lease commitments are capitalised on balance sheet as right-of-use assets offset by lease liabilities. This creates a debt-heavy balance sheet appearance. Net debt (including lease liabilities) is the correct lens for leverage assessment. Basic-Fit's true financial risk is the fixed rental commitment across ~1,500 leases — a real obligation that persists whether members join or not. In a severe membership decline scenario, the lease structure creates significant downside. In a growth scenario, it is exactly the right structure. #A

Dimension 6: Digital Product (Basic-Fit Live). The app is the primary product differentiator enabling the premium tier. Content includes on-demand and live-streamed classes, workout planning, nutrition guidance. Competitive context: this positions Basic-Fit above pure-access operators (no app) but well below dedicated digital fitness players (Peloton, Les Mills On Demand, Apple Fitness+) in content depth. The strategic value is not defeating digital players but retaining members who would otherwise supplement gym membership with external content. #J

Dimension 7: Equipment Procurement and Standardisation. Multi-vendor strategy (Life Fitness, Matrix, Technogym) provides procurement flexibility. Standardised club layout reduces fit-out cost and operational complexity. No proprietary equipment means no R&D cost but also no equipment-based differentiation — members can find equivalent machines at PureGym or McFit. Equipment quality is table stakes, not a moat. Maintenance costs and equipment refresh cycles are a recurring capex obligation that Basic-Fit cannot escape. #A

Dimension 8: Staffing Model. Near-unmanned club operations is one of the most powerful structural cost advantages in the model. A traditional premium gym might have 5-10 FTE per location; Basic-Fit operates with substantially fewer. This translates directly into a lower breakeven membership level per club and a higher EBITDA margin at maturity. The trade-off is a member experience that is entirely product-dependent — when equipment breaks or cleanliness deteriorates, there is no staff layer to compensate. #A

Dimension 9: Switching Costs and Member Stickiness. Contractual switching costs are low: monthly contracts with no long lock-in periods are standard. Practical switching costs are higher: proximity to home/office is the primary gym selection driver, and Basic-Fit's network density (particularly in the Netherlands and Belgium) means the nearest alternative may be materially less convenient. Habit formation is the deepest retention mechanism — members who attend regularly stop evaluating alternatives. The app adds a marginal switching cost for premium members. Overall: low-medium switching costs, below-average for consumer subscription businesses. #J

Dimension 10: B2B/Corporate Channel. Estimated 5-10% of revenue through employer and benefits platform partnerships. This channel provides lower-CAC member acquisition and predictable revenue, but typically at discounted membership rates. Strategic value lies in its potential to grow: European corporate wellness budgets are rising post-COVID, and gym access is increasingly reimbursed or subsidised. Basic-Fit's network breadth makes it an attractive corporate partner — a single contract can cover employees across multiple countries. Under-disclosed and likely under-monetised at present. #J

Dimension 11: Club Maturity Curve and Cohort Economics. New clubs require approximately 12-18 months to reach maturity (management targets ~1,500 members per club at maturity). During the ramp-up period, the club generates negative or break-even EBITDA against a fixed lease cost. The quality of Basic-Fit's reported EBITDA is therefore heavily dependent on the proportion of mature vs. ramping clubs in any given period. Rapid expansion (as in France/Spain) dilutes reported margins but does not impair the underlying economics of mature clubs. Distinguishing the two is critical to fundamental analysis. #J

Dimension 12: Brand and Consumer Trust. Basic-Fit has high brand recognition in the Netherlands and Belgium, moderate in France, low in Spain. Brand equity in low-cost fitness is primarily a function of club density and marketing spend — it is not deeply differentiated (no personality or aspirational positioning). This is adequate for a low-cost model but means the brand provides limited pricing protection in competitive markets. In Spain, Basic-Fit is building brand from scratch in a market where local chains (Altafit, GO fit) have established presence. #A

Dimension 13: Technology and Data Infrastructure. Basic-Fit collects member access data, app usage, and billing data across ~1,500 clubs. The analytical potential of this dataset — churn prediction, capacity optimisation, content personalisation, corporate upsell — is significant and likely under-exploited. Investment in data analytics and personalisation represents an asymmetric opportunity: low capital cost relative to the potential for ARPU improvement and churn reduction. Monitoring whether management is investing meaningfully here is a forward-looking ESA flag. #J

Dimension 14: Capital Allocation Discipline. The primary capital allocation decision is the pace of new club openings. Too fast: dilutes near-term margins, strains working capital, risks quality control in new markets. Too slow: cedes expansion runway to competitors and delays maturity curve benefits. Management has guided to a moderated expansion pace in recent periods, reflecting macro caution. Discipline in site selection (rent economics, catchment density, competitor proximity) is the most important capital allocation lever available to management. #J

Dimension 15: Regulatory and Labour Environment. Gym operations are lightly regulated in most European markets. Key regulatory exposures: labour law (minimum wage increases affect the small but non-zero staffing cost), data privacy (GDPR applies to membership and usage data), and building/health safety regulations. No sector-specific regulatory moat or threat identified at ESA stage. Brexit has minimal impact (Basic-Fit has no UK presence). #I

3. Key Risks & Opportunities (10 bullets)

Basic-Fit's risk/opportunity profile at ESA stage is dominated by the interplay between its lease cost base, its expansion pace, and the sustainability of the premium tier ARPU uplift. The company is not in existential risk territory, but it is operating with a structurally leveraged balance sheet in a competitive, low-moat sector — which makes execution quality and macro sensitivity more consequential than in a higher-moat compounder. The following are the most material items for an ESA-stage investor to interrogate.

Risk 1: Lease Cost Inflation. #A Rental costs are the largest single cost item and are substantially fixed for the duration of each lease. In markets where landlords have pricing power (dense urban locations, prime retail parks), rent review clauses can impose material cost step-ups. Basic-Fit's French and Spanish expansion is occurring in a commercial real estate environment where rental levels remain elevated. A scenario where rent inflation outpaces ARPU growth structurally compresses unit economics, particularly for newer clubs that have not yet reached full membership density.

Risk 2: Competitive Encroachment in Expansion Markets. #J France and Spain are the primary growth markets, but neither is competitively clear. In France, Fitness Park, Keep Cool, and national independents compete aggressively on price. In Spain, GO fit, Altafit, and VivaGym have established network density. Basic-Fit's scale advantage is meaningful in the Netherlands and Belgium but does not transfer automatically to new geographies. The risk of building out a large French/Spanish lease portfolio into a structurally competitive market — where unit economics never reach Dutch maturity levels — is the primary bear case on the expansion thesis.

Risk 3: Premium Tier Durability. #J The reported 50% new joiner premium take-up is a key ARPU driver. But this metric may reflect a launch-effect cohort — price-insensitive or digitally engaged early adopters — rather than the steady-state behaviour of the broader member base. If premium penetration normalises at 20-30% as the member cohort shifts toward more price-sensitive consumers, ARPU assumptions embedded in consensus estimates may be too high. This is a high-priority question for DD Phase 1: disaggregate premium penetration by market, cohort vintage, and club maturity.

Risk 4: Macro/Consumer Spending Downturn. #A Gym membership is a discretionary spend item, though Basic-Fit's price point (€20-38/month) is at the lower end of the consumer discretionary spectrum. In a recession scenario, low-cost gym operators historically outperform premium competitors as members trade down — evidence from the 2008-09 and COVID periods supports this. However, net member growth can still slow or reverse under severe income pressure. The lease cost base does not flex with membership decline; a 10% membership reduction could produce a disproportionate EBITDA contraction.

Risk 5: Digital Fitness Disintermediation. #J Peloton, Apple Fitness+, and free content (YouTube, TikTok) represent structural alternatives to physical gym attendance, particularly for cardio and flexibility-focused members. Basic-Fit's response — the Live app and premium tier — is the right strategic direction but is competing with platforms with vastly greater content investment and technology sophistication. The scenario to watch: if a generation of younger members normalises home-based fitness and treats the gym as an occasional supplement rather than primary workout destination, Basic-Fit's structural volume assumptions may be too optimistic for the long term.

Risk 6: Interest Rate and Refinancing Exposure. #A Basic-Fit's lease liability is the economic equivalent of long-duration debt. Rising interest rates affect the present value of future lease commitments and, if the company needs to refinance corporate debt facilities, will increase financing costs. The balance sheet profile — high lease liabilities, meaningful corporate debt — means the cost of capital is sensitive to the rate environment. Not a near-term existential risk, but relevant to valuation and the pace of internally funded expansion.

Opportunity 1: Premium Tier Penetration Upside. #J If 50% new-joiner premium take-up is sustained or grows — and if it is driven by genuine app value rather than promotional pricing — the ARPU improvement is structurally significant. Each percentage point increase in premium penetration across a ~2.5-3m member base adds several million euros in near-zero-marginal-cost revenue. The financial model sensitivity to blended ARPU is high; management's ability to maintain and grow the premium mix is the single largest near-term earnings driver that is controllable and not macro-dependent.

Opportunity 2: Club Maturity Curve Upside in France/Spain. #A The drag from ramping clubs in France and Spain is real and will reverse. As the club cohort matures — driven by membership density growth and local brand recognition — the margin contribution from these markets will improve without additional capex. Investors who can accurately model the maturity curve (and distinguish structural margin from ramp-drag) will be better positioned to value the business at the right point in the expansion cycle. This is a classic compounding operator pattern where near-term reported margins understate the medium-term earnings power.

Opportunity 3: Corporate Wellness Channel Growth. #J The B2B/corporate channel (estimated 5-10% of revenue) is under-discussed and likely under-developed. European employer wellness budgets are rising, and health insurers in several markets (Netherlands, Germany, France) are increasingly subsidising gym access. Basic-Fit's multi-country network is a structural advantage for corporate deals that span geographies. If this channel grows from 5-10% to 15-20% of revenue over a 3-5 year horizon, it adds a predictable, lower-churn revenue layer that improves earnings quality.

Opportunity 4: Data Monetisation and Operational Analytics. #J Basic-Fit has a large, granular dataset of member behaviour across ~1,500 clubs. Systematic investment in churn prediction, dynamic capacity management, and personalised engagement (via the app) could reduce churn, improve retention, and reduce CAC over time. This is not a near-term catalyst — it requires sustained product and data investment — but it represents an asymmetric opportunity in a business where small improvements in retention rates have outsized EBITDA impact due to the fixed cost base. Whether management has the capability and appetite to build this is a DD-level question.
