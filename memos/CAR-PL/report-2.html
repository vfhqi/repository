The Supervisory Board underwent significant refreshment at the June 2025 AGM, adding five new members (Witold Kmieciak, Kamilla Spark, Zofia Dzik, Łukasz Dziekoński, and Zoya Gyurova) to modernize corporate governance.

Management and Board Transitions

Inter Cars SA has recently navigated significant leadership adjustments, reflecting its ongoing evolution from a family-run business into a highly structured corporate enterprise. The company has systematically strengthened its executive layer while overhauling its oversight bodies to support long-term strategic growth and international expansion.

Management Board Expansion

The most notable executive shift occurred on January 1, 2024, when Wojciech Aleksandrowicz was officially appointed to the Management Board for its current joint four-year term. With this appointment, the Supervisory Board formally expanded the Management Board from four to five active members. Aleksandrowicz, who has been with the Inter Cars Group since 2004, assumed the critical role of Managing Director for Logistics and IT. He joins a stable executive team led by President Maciej Oleksowicz, alongside Vice President Krzysztof Soszyński, and Members Wojciech Twaróg and Piotr Zamora.

Supervisory Board Overhaul

At the oversight level, the Supervisory Board experienced sweeping changes following the passing of long-serving member Piotr Płoszajski in September 2024. During the Annual General Meeting on June 10, 2025, the company significantly refreshed its governance body. While Chairman Andrzej Oliszewski and member Radosław Kudła were re-appointed for new terms, the assembly simultaneously brought in a slate of new oversight figures. The newly appointed members include Witold Kmieciak, Kamilla Spark, Zofia Dzik, Łukasz Dziekoński, and Zoya Gyurova. This broadened composition introduces diverse professional backgrounds to the Supervisory Board, aligning the firm with modernized corporate governance demands.

Organizational Structure and Corporate Culture

From an operational perspective, the company's culture has deliberately decentralized to support its growing international footprint. While the headquarters in Poland drives top-level corporate standards, Inter Cars currently operates 18 separate branches across Europe as independent entities managed by local country directors. This operational flexibility allows local managers to run their respective segments according to regional market demands.

However, this regional decentralization is counterbalanced by a stiffer, more formalized corporate reporting structure at the group level. Decision-making protocols have moved away from the informal direct-access culture of the company's early days toward rigid corporate hierarchies. Strategic responsibilities are also distinctly bifurcated between the founding generation and the current executive team. Founder Krzysztof Oleksowicz, now acting formally as an advisor, focuses primarily on five- to ten-year long-term strategic planning. Meanwhile, President Maciej Oleksowicz and the expanded Management Board are responsible for managing day-to-day corporate operations and executing current-year business development initiatives.

Corporate Governance and Policy Updates

To support its rapid scale and ensure alignment between executive performance and shareholder value, Inter Cars SA has instituted sweeping modifications to its corporate governance frameworks, remuneration structures, and foundational governing documents. These changes reflect a mature approach to corporate oversight, emphasizing long-term financial targets and sustainability.

Evolution of the Remuneration Policy

The Company's compensation framework has undergone systematic refinement to enhance transparency and directly link executive pay to consolidated financial performance. The foundation of this system is the Remuneration Policy, originally adopted by the Ordinary General Meeting in June 2020 and substantially amended on May 23, 2024. The policy dictates that executive compensation is split into fixed baseline salaries and variable cash bonuses tied to the audited consolidated net profit of the Inter Cars Group.

Further policy amendments were enacted during the Annual General Meeting on June 10, 2025, specifically to accommodate the integration of equity-based compensation. Under the revised rules, the variable cash remuneration is generally capped at 85% of the total compensation received by a Management Board member in a given year. However, to prevent excessive total compensation when equity incentives are triggered, the AGM established a stricter limit. In years where a Management Board member obtains the right to acquire Company shares under the new Incentive Programme, the cash bonus portion is restricted to a maximum of 75% of their total remuneration.

2025–2027 Management Incentive Schemes

A cornerstone of the June 2025 corporate governance overhaul was the adoption of a comprehensive long-term incentive program for key managers and executives. The program aims to motivate participants to maximize the Group's consolidated financial results over a three-year performance cycle spanning from 2025 through 2027. The estimated total cost of implementing these programs over the vesting period is PLN 85 million.

The granting of entitlements under this scheme is strictly contingent upon fulfilling designated loyalty conditions and achieving aggressive minimum thresholds for both consolidated EBITDA and sales revenue. The performance targets established for each tranche are as follows:

2025: Minimum EBITDA of PLN 1,447.3 million and revenues of at least PLN 21,739.4 million.

2026: Minimum EBITDA of PLN 1,633.9 million and revenues of at least PLN 23,913.4 million.

2027: Minimum EBITDA of PLN 1,843.4 million and revenues of at least PLN 26,304.7 million.

Additional Tranche: Granted if the cumulative 2025–2027 EBITDA reaches at least PLN 4,924.6 million and cumulative revenue hits at least PLN 71,957.5 million.

Participants who meet these criteria will receive rights entitling them to acquire either newly issued shares or treasury shares. The acquisition price for these shares is set favorably at a 5% discount to the arithmetic mean of the volume-weighted average daily prices over the one-month period immediately preceding the June 2025 AGM.

To facilitate the share-based compensation, the June 2025 AGM authorized a conditional share capital increase of up to PLN 500,000. This allows for the issuance of up to 250,000 Series H ordinary bearer shares, specifically allocated for holders of subscription warrants with pre-emptive rights disapplied. Concurrently, the AGM also authorized the Management Board to execute a share buy-back program running through December 31, 2028, permitting the acquisition of up to 2.83% of the Company's total share capital. To finance these repurchases, PLN 320 million was transferred from dividend-eligible reserve capital into a dedicated reserve fund.

Amendments to the Articles of Association

Inter Cars SA has executed several strategic amendments to its Articles of Association over recent periods to modernize operational procedures and comply with evolving regulatory standards. During the Extraordinary General Meeting in October 2022, the assembly updated internal information-sharing protocols, requiring the Management Board to systematically provide the Supervisory Board with requisite operational data to align with the Commercial Companies Code.

Governance structures regarding ESG compliance were subsequently updated at the June 2025 AGM. Shareholders voted to amend Article 14.2.3 of the Articles of Association, officially empowering the Supervisory Board to appoint an audit firm specifically tasked with auditing the Company's sustainability reports.

The most profound structural modifications occurred during the EGM held on November 4, 2025. To optimize operational efficiency, shareholders approved the relocation of the Company's registered office from Warsaw to Swobodnia, located in the Zakroczym municipality. Furthermore, the EGM expanded the permissible geographic locations for future General Meetings, allowing assemblies to be convened not only at the new headquarters but also in Warsaw, Cząstków Mazowiecki, Poznań, or Wrocław. Finally, the Articles were updated to align the formal scope of the Company's business activities with the newly implemented Polish Classification of Activities (PKD 2025) standards.

Strategic and Operational Shifts

Inter Cars SA has positioned itself as the fastest-growing distributor in the European independent aftermarket through a deliberate strategy of organic expansion and profound operational upgrades. The company's strategic framework relies on capitalizing on market fragmentation to consolidate its leadership while simultaneously future-proofing its supply chain against rising operational costs and labor shortages. By prioritizing internal efficiency and scalable distribution models, the Group aims to maintain its double-digit growth trajectory across an evolving continental landscape.

Supply Chain and Logistics Automation

A cornerstone of Inter Cars' operational shift is its aggressive investment in warehouse robotization and logistics infrastructure, managed primarily through its specialized subsidiary, ILS Sp. z o.o. To mitigate rising operating costs and ensure rapid delivery capabilities, the company has partnered with Exotec to deploy advanced Skypod automated storage and retrieval systems.

At the European Logistics and Development Centre in Zakroczym, the company is executing a multi-phase expansion valued at approximately PLN 235 million. The facility utilizes over 200 autonomous robots capable of handling up to 100,000 stock keeping units, which represents over 20% of the entire Zakroczym inventory. This modular racking system reaches heights of 11 meters and allows four orders to be processed simultaneously at a single station, significantly increasing warehouse throughput. While the final stages of the Zakroczym automation are slated for completion in early 2026, management has recognized that the facility has reached its optimal scaling limits. Consequently, the Group will cease further expansion at Zakroczym to avoid operational inefficiencies, pivoting instead to a new regional warehouse project in Stęszew, near Poznań, to better serve Western Poland and German markets.

Concurrently, the company has heavily invested in the ILS Balkan automated warehouse in Brasov, Romania. This fully automated, build-to-suit facility represents a capital expenditure of roughly PLN 250 million, including an estimated EUR 50 million dedicated solely to robotics hardware. The Skypod system in Brasov is designed to handle approximately 70% of all internal warehouse processes, with human operators managing the remaining 30%. The transition to this new facility was executed as the lease on the older Brasov warehouse expired, fundamentally enhancing distribution efficiency across the broader Balkan region.

Geographic Market Expansion

Rather than relying on mergers and acquisitions, Inter Cars utilizes an organic franchise and partnership model to consolidate the fragmented European aftermarket. The Group identifies established local entrepreneurs, integrating them into its comprehensive "one-stop-shop" ecosystem that provides access to nearly 6 million parts, garage equipment, and technical know-how. This strategy has yielded rapid footprint growth, with the company opening up to 34 new branches in foreign subsidiaries over recent 12-month periods.

While Central and Eastern Europe remains the high-margin growth engine, Inter Cars is strategically penetrating Western European markets. The company recently launched operations in Belgium and Austria, alongside a calculated expansion into Germany. In Germany, the firm achieved double-digit growth after implementing a new IT system and carefully migrating customers, signaling a methodical approach to scaling in highly competitive and mature territories.

Adapting to Aftermarket Dynamics

Inter Cars is proactively maneuvering through structural changes in the global automotive sector. Management views the independent aftermarket as a resilient "safe harbor" during economic downturns, as consumers delay new vehicle purchases and opt to repair older cars. Although electric vehicles are gradually gaining share, the current European fleet remains heavily dominated by combustion and hybrid engines, ensuring sustained demand for traditional spare parts throughout the coming decade.

Furthermore, the company is preparing for the zero-emission transition by incorporating electric vehicle offerings into its portfolio, such as securing the authorized dealership for BYD electric trucks in Poland through its subsidiary Armatus. To capture digital demand, Inter Cars continues to scale its Motointegrator platform, a B2C application that seamlessly redirects individual drivers requiring repairs to Inter Cars' B2B garage partners, effectively generating service leads while reinforcing business-to-business loyalty.

Financial Strategy and Capital Allocation

Inter Cars SA's financial strategy focuses on balancing aggressive top-line revenue growth with the substantial capital requirements of its expanding logistics and automation footprint. The firm's overarching capital management objective is to maintain a sound credit rating and safe capital ratios while funding its extensive international operations.

Share Capital Structure and Debt Management

The company's share capital remains highly stable, consisting of 14,168,100 ordinary bearer shares across Series A through F. These shares carry a par value of PLN 2 each, bringing the total share capital to PLN 28.3 million. To finance its operational scale without heavily diluting equity, the Group relies on a combination of retained earnings, bank loans, and leases. Consequently, intensive capital expenditures have moderately increased the company's leverage. The Group's estimated net debt-to-EBITDA ratio climbed to 2.29 by the end of 2024, representing a 26% increase compared to the end of 2023. Management attributes this rising debt directly to the utilization of credit facilities for the expansion and robotization of the Zakroczym and Brasov logistics centers.

Dividend Policy and Profit Allocation

In April 2024, the Management Board formally adopted a dividend policy covering the 2023–2025 period, which permits a maximum dividend payout of up to 60% of the Group's consolidated net profit. However, actual payouts are strictly calibrated against the firm's intensive investment plans and existing debt covenants.

For the 2024 financial year, Inter Cars generated a preliminary estimated consolidated net profit of approximately PLN 720 million. In May 2025, the Management Board proposed a highly conservative allocation of the parent company's standalone 2024 net profit of PLN 402.7 million. Specifically, the proposal allocates only PLN 20.1 million to dividends, translating to a modest PLN 1.42 per share. The vast majority of the profit, amounting to PLN 382.6 million, is designated to be transferred into the reserve capital. This heavy retention of earnings intentionally suppresses short-term shareholder yields to guarantee sufficient internal liquidity for maintaining a business growth trajectory that outpaces the broader market.

Financial Performance and Margin Dynamics

The Group's operational financial performance has been characterized by resilient top-line expansion, with 2024 preliminary consolidated revenue reaching PLN 19.47 billion, an 8% year-over-year increase. Despite this revenue growth, the company has navigated significant profitability headwinds. A primary driver of gross margin pressure throughout 2024 was the strengthening of the Polish zloty against the euro. Because Inter Cars purchases a vast majority of its inventory in euros, a strong zloty creates natural downward pressure on domestic retail prices, forcing the company to pass lower purchasing costs onto customers to remain competitive.

Beyond currency dynamics, profitability was squeezed by escalating operating expenses, particularly wage inflation and short-term costs related to logistics optimization. By the third quarter of 2025, the ratio of sales and administrative costs to revenue had risen slightly to 14.3%, up from 14.0% in the prior year. Management noted that this temporary cost inflation was caused by the rapid accumulation of logistics expenditures designed to accelerate the completion of automated warehouses. Despite these pressures, the company's strategic scale and supplier leverage enabled a successful stabilization of its gross margin on goods sold, which recovered to 29.9% in the third quarter of 2025 after eliminating exchange rate differences.
