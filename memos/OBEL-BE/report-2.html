[W][C] Orange Belgium SA (OBEL-BE) — IG Query #2: Change Forces

Date: 04-May-26 | Source: Claude native research | Role: RESEARCHER | Stage: IG

1. Key Findings

  • The VOO acquisition (closed June 2023, 100% ownership completed May 2024 for €110m additional consideration) is the singular structural event — it transformed OBEL from a pure mobile challenger into Belgium's only fully-integrated FMC operator outside Proximus. The flywheel test is passing: €47.5m synergies delivered in 2024 against an €85m run-rate target now expected by year four (original guidance: year seven). This is a genuine positive surprise, not a management narrative.

  • EBITDAaL growth has been exceptional by European telco standards: +10.1% in 2024, +4.0% in 2025, with 2026 guidance of +3.5%. The growth rate is decelerating as the step-change synergy capture phase matures, but starting from a structurally higher margin base. Organic cash flow inflected sharply: €90.8m in 2025 vs €41.8m in 2024. This is the key financial signal — the treadmill is becoming a flywheel on the cost side.

  • ❌ Revenue is declining: total revenues -1.5% in 2025 (H1 2025: €962.7m). Fixed voice erosion is structural and irreversible; DIGI Belgium's December 2024 entry is compressing mobile ARPU at the margin even with a sub-1% market share (91,000 subscribers end-2025). OBEL is earning its EBITDA improvement through cost discipline and synergies, not top-line growth. This is the central risk — how long can cost-led improvement run before revenue attrition dominates?

  • The FMC bundle thesis is early but credible: 300,000+ "Love" convergent customers already, with the VOO cable base now fully absorbed. FMC ARPU in Europe consistently runs 15-25% above single-play equivalents. Belgium is structurally oligopolistic (three MNOs pre-DIGI; Proximus + Telenet/Orange post-VOO on fixed) which supports pricing power in converged bundles once integration is complete.

  • ❌ Zero dividend for 2024 and 2025. Board declined to propose a dividend for 2025 to "preserve cash for future capital requirements." No reinstatement guidance offered. Net debt/EBITDAaL remains elevated at ~3.5x post-VOO (net debt ~€1.82bn as of end-2025). This is the primary capital allocation red flag for any yield-seeking investor — though for a change-thesis framing, it is the correct decision.

  • The parent relationship (Orange SA owns ~69.6% of OBEL; Nethys holds 11.08% after the VOO share-swap completion) creates a governance overhang. Orange SA attempted a squeeze-out in 2021 at €22/share that failed when Polygon rejected the price and minority investors organised via Deminor. This leaves a residual parent-interest misalignment risk: Orange SA may prioritise upstream cash extraction (via management fees, intra-group loan interest) over minority investor returns.

  • 🚩 RARE: The Proximus-Orange Belgium MOU (signed July 2025) for joint fibre rollout in Wallonia's medium-to-low-density areas is an industry-level signal. Two competitors co-investing in passive infrastructure to reduce capex is structurally bullish for the entire Belgian market — it reduces the risk of a prolonged capex war and signals regulatory acceptance of network sharing. This is genuinely uncommon in European telco history outside Scandinavia.

  • Management (CEO Xavier Pichon, in seat since Sept 2020 with a BCG TMT transformation background and prior Orange Group CFO experience) has executed well on the VOO integration — synergies ahead of schedule is the primary evidence. The "LeadTheFuture" strategy is credible. However, OBEL's change thesis depends almost entirely on VOO integration success, leaving limited strategic optionality if integration stalls.

  • The EPS trajectory is investable on a base-case assumption: EPS of €0.33 in 2024 (vs ~breakeven 2023), analyst consensus ~€0.79 for next year. If capex normalises as guided (~€360m in 2026 vs €376m in 2025), and organic cash flow continues to build, there is a credible path to dividend reinstatement by 2027 and an EPS CAGR of 20%+ from the 2024 base through 2027 — well above the Goldilocks 15% threshold. Whether the market is pricing this in is the key valuation question this memo does not resolve.

  • On the flywheel-vs-treadmill test: OBEL is transitioning. The mobile-only phase (pre-VOO) was a classic treadmill — capex intensity, subscriber competition with Proximus/Telenet, yield compression. Post-VOO, the converged infrastructure creates genuine switching cost uplift. The verdict is: early flywheel, not confirmed — the transition needs 2-3 more years of ARPU evidence and deleveraging to be declared.

3. Change in Leadership

Rating: YELLOW — Pichon installed in 2020 with transformation mandate, but leadership continuity means this is not a fresh-appointment catalyst.

  • Xavier Pichon has been CEO since September 2020, appointed directly from Boston Consulting Group's global TMT Practice where he focused on "large-scale telco transformations." His prior career included CFO of Orange France and Group Head of Investor Relations at Orange SA — giving him unusual visibility into both the operational and parent-relationship dimensions of running a listed subsidiary. (Orange Belgium corporate site)

  • The VOO acquisition was strategically architected under Pichon's tenure — he championed the deal thesis that Belgium's fixed-mobile convergence opportunity was under-exploited, and secured Orange SA backing for the €1.8bn enterprise value transaction. The decision to move the synergy timeline from seven years to four is consistent with a CEO who had credibility with the parent to accelerate investment.

  • The executive committee assembled under Pichon is operationally oriented. Antoine Chouc has maintained financial discipline during the VOO integration period; Werner De Laet (Chief Enterprise Innovation Officer) reflects the B2B build-out ambition; Christophe Dujardin (Chief Consumer Officer) is running the FMC convergence commercial push. The team is functionally complete for the current strategic phase. (Orange Belgium corporate site)

  • There has been no recent CEO change or anticipated succession discussion in public domain. This is neither a catalyst (no fresh eyes) nor a risk (experienced operator with skin in the game on VOO integration). By peer standards — Elisa (stable), KPN (stable), Swisscom (stable) — OBEL's management continuity is in line with the sector norm for settled European incumbents.

  • The "#LeadTheFuture" strategic plan provides a clear public accountability framework: (1) nationwide gigabit network leadership, (2) customer experience excellence, (3) responsible company. The gigabit leadership claim is now technically accurate following the HFC upgrade completion. The customer experience pillar is harder to verify externally but the 300,000 Love subscriber milestone suggests commercial execution is on track.

  • Governance: Nethys (the Walloon public utility holding company) received two board seats and veto rights over certain VOO-related matters as part of the May 2024 share-swap completion. This creates a second governance dimension beyond Orange SA — a regionally embedded public-sector shareholder with local political accountability. The risk is that VOO brand commitments (Nethys' preference is to maintain VOO's regional identity in Wallonia) constrain OBEL's ability to fully rationalise the VOO commercial infrastructure. (GlobeNewswire, May 2024)

  • Pichon's BCG background is a distinguishing feature vs the typical European telco CEO who is an engineering-track lifer. BCG's TMT Practice methodology tends to favour structural analysis, margin focus, and portfolio rationalization over revenue growth maximisation — which explains the aggressive synergy acceleration and willingness to let revenue decline while protecting EBITDA. This is the right playbook for 2024-2026 but may need to evolve once synergies are exhausted.

  • By the A-F grading standard, Pichon's track record at OBEL rates B — above median European telco CEO in terms of strategic execution and M&A integration, but not top decile (no transformative innovation, no exceptional revenue re-rate). The management team does not yet have the commercial hunger of Tele2's 2016-2020 leadership which built a genuine challenger flywheel in Sweden.

  • The failed Orange SA squeeze-out attempt (2021) created a degree of tension between Pichon's operational role and the parent's financial objectives. He is simultaneously accountable to Orange SA (which wants cash upstream) and minority shareholders (who want reinstatement of dividends and independent value creation). This dual-accountability tension is an ongoing governance constraint on his strategic freedom.

  • Pichon has not hedged on dividend guidance — he has consistently stated that dividend reinstatement requires deleveraging progress, without providing a specific timeline. This honesty is positive (no false promises) but also signals that management does not expect reinstatement until net leverage falls meaningfully from current 3.5x levels.

  • On the Proximus MOU (July 2025, announced post-period), Pichon's team negotiated a co-investment structure for Wallonia fibre in medium-to-low-density areas. This is a strategic signal that OBEL has concluded the standalone capex case for deep rural FTTH does not make economic sense — a pragmatic and intellectually honest acknowledgement of network economics. It reduces long-term capex risk but also signals a ceiling on OBEL's network leadership ambitions in rural Belgium.

  • Base rate comparison: Among the European telco sub-€5bn market cap peer group, the percentage with CEO tenure >3 years who have simultaneously executed a major M&A integration and maintained market share is below 30%. Pichon is in that minority. Qualitative edge is real but modest.

4. Change in Strategy / Structure

Rating: GREEN — The VOO acquisition is a genuine structural transformation, not a bolt-on. The change from mobile-only challenger to converged FMC operator is the most significant strategic repositioning in OBEL's history.

  • Pre-VOO, Orange Belgium was structurally disadvantaged in Belgium: the only major market (alongside the UK) where it lacked a fixed network, forcing MVNO dependence on Proximus' copper/fibre infrastructure for broadband. This constrained ARPU potential (no bundle pricing) and elevated churn (no switching cost). The VOO acquisition closed that structural gap in a single transaction. (Orange Belgium newsroom, June 2023)

  • The deal terms: €1.35bn for 75% minus one share of VOO SA at an enterprise value of €1.8bn (100% basis), representing 9.5x pre-synergy EBITDA and 6.5x post-synergy EBITDA. Financed via an Orange SA intra-group loan — i.e., OBEL's balance sheet absorbed the debt, not Orange SA's. This is the mechanism behind the leverage spike to ~3.5x net debt/EBITDAaL. The remaining 25% plus one share was held by Nethys until May 2024, when Orange Belgium acquired it for €110m funded by issuing new OBEL shares to Nethys (hence Nethys' 11.08% OBEL stake). (GlobeNewswire, May 2024; Orange Belgium press release, June 2023)

  • VOO's strategic contribution: ~1 million cable broadband subscribers in Wallonia and six Brussels municipalities; HFC network covering ~45% of Belgian households (Wallonia focus); a TV platform; and approximately 500,000 MVNO mobile subscribers who were migrated onto Orange Belgium's own MNO network post-acquisition. The MVNO-to-MNO migration was the primary near-term synergy source — eliminating wholesale access cost on those subscribers was the single biggest cost reduction item and the key driver of the €47.5m 2024 synergy delivery. (GlobeNewswire, February 2025)

  • The HFC network has now been upgraded to gigabit speeds across 95% of coverage — the first operator in Belgium to achieve nationwide gigabit capability. This was completed six months ahead of schedule. The upgrade technology is Distributed Access Architecture , the same path taken by Liberty Global and Comcast in their DOCSIS evolution. The long-term roadmap includes FTTP migration for approximately two-thirds of the Wallonia footprint by 2040, with HFC retained for one-third (the economics of deep rural FTTP don't justify full replacement). (Orange Belgium press releases)

  • The FMC commercial strategy branded as "Love" (Love Duo = internet + mobile; Love Trio = internet + TV + mobile) reached 300,000 subscribers — a milestone announced as significant by OBEL. For context, Belgium has ~5 million households. At 300,000 Love customers, OBEL's FMC penetration of its addressable fixed base is meaningful but early-stage. The conversion rate of VOO broadband subscribers to FMC bundles is the key commercial conversion metric — not disclosed granularly in public reporting.

  • A strategic decision was taken to maintain the VOO brand distinctly in Wallonia (a commitment partly embedded in Nethys' governance rights) while integrating the operational infrastructure. This creates a dual-brand architecture: Orange in Flanders and nationally on mobile; VOO in Wallonia on fixed. This is structurally more complex than a clean single-brand integration but respects Belgian linguistic and political sensitivities. The risk is customer confusion and sub-optimal cross-selling efficiency.

  • The Proximus mobile network-sharing agreement (RAN sharing, signed 2022) is now in conclusion phase — eCAPEX is expected to decline by ~€16m in 2026 partly as RAN sharing capex winds down. This is a one-off tailwind to cash generation in 2026 that should not be extrapolated into the medium term. (Orange Belgium FY2025 results, February 2026)

  • The Proximus-Orange Belgium MOU on Wallonia fibre (July 2025) represents the second phase of co-investment logic: having already shared RAN, the two operators are now discussing shared passive fibre infrastructure for medium-to-low-density areas. This is structurally significant — if approved by the Belgian Competition Authority and BIPT, it would reduce OBEL's standalone capex requirement in rural Wallonia by a material amount, improving long-term FCF generation. No financial terms disclosed; regulatory approval expected at earliest Q4 2025. (GlobeNewswire, July 2025)

  • The B2B enterprise strategy is the third leg of the structural repositioning. Orange Belgium is deploying a unified go-to-market for SME, multinational, and public sector clients — leveraging the convergence of fixed and mobile assets to offer ICT services beyond basic connectivity. Enterprise/ICT is the European telco growth engine at the group level (Orange Group's enterprise segment consistently outgrows consumer), and OBEL's enterprise revenue is expected to grow in the 4-5% CAGR range to 2031 per Mordor Intelligence estimates.

  • The strategic structure is now fundamentally more defensible than three years ago. Pre-VOO OBEL was a mono-product mobile challenger with no pricing power. Post-VOO OBEL has three infrastructure layers (mobile MNO, cable HFC, and 5G spectrum including the maximum available 3.6GHz band), FMC bundle capability, and a growing enterprise segment. The Red Queen treadmill dynamic (run harder, stay still) is breaking — but only if ARPU per converged customer is rising, which requires longer observation.

5. Change in Priorities / Ambition

Rating: YELLOW — The stated priorities (deleveraging, synergy realisation, FMC growth) are operationally clear and being executed. The ambition is constrained by the debt burden — this is a consolidation/repair phase, not an expansionary phase.

  • The stated strategic priorities for 2025-2026 are: (1) complete VOO operational integration; (2) deliver €85m synergy run-rate by year four (i.e., 2027); (3) deleverage to enable eventual dividend reinstatement; (4) grow FMC subscriber base; (5) develop enterprise/B2B. This is an internally consistent, sequenced set of priorities — there is no distraction risk or strategic overreach.

  • The deleveraging ambition is concrete: from ~3.5x net debt/EBITDAaL toward an implied ~2.5x or below before dividend reinstatement becomes possible. The maths require a combination of EBITDA growth (guided +3.5% in 2026) and moderate debt amortisation. At the current trajectory — net debt declining from €1.90bn to €1.82bn in 2025 (-4.7%) — the deleveraging path to 2.5x is achievable by 2027-2028 if EBITDA continues to grow. (GlobeNewswire, February 2026; GuruFocus)

  • The capex ambition is disciplined: eCAPEX guidance of ~€360m in 2026, down from €376m in 2025. This represents a structural capex reduction as the HFC gigabit upgrade completes and RAN sharing winds down. The Proximus MOU, if implemented, would further reduce future capex in Wallonia's rural fibre build. European telco sector capex is expected to fall 24% in aggregate for 2025-2027 vs 2020-2024 — OBEL's trajectory is consistent with this sector direction. (Scope Ratings; Orange Belgium guidance)

  • The dividend ambition is explicitly zero for 2024 and 2025. Management's language is "preserve cash for future capital requirements" — a conservative framing that does not promise a 2026 reinstatement. Analyst consensus EPS forecast of ~€0.79 for next year implies meaningful earnings growth, but without dividend guidance, income-oriented investors remain on the sidelines. This is a deliberate choice that depresses the current valuation multiple.

  • The FMC ambition is quantified at 300,000 Love subscribers (achieved) but there is no public target for 2026-2027. Given OBEL's combined broadband base now exceeds 1 million subscribers, the implicit conversion headroom to FMC is large — but the conversion rate depends on ARPU uplift incentives and the effectiveness of cross-selling from the VOO base to Orange mobile (and vice versa).

  • On network ambition: the LeadTheFuture plan commits to nationwide gigabit coverage — already achieved on HFC. The next phase is 5G densification (OBEL obtained maximum 5G spectrum in 2022 auction) and progressive FTTP upgrades in Wallonia through 2040. The ambition is infrastructure leadership rather than service innovation — consistent with the sector's general movement toward network quality as the primary differentiator post-convergence.

  • By peer comparison, OBEL's stated ambition is more modest than KPN (Netherlands), which is pushing FTTH to near-national coverage while simultaneously growing enterprise ICT revenue in double-digits. OBEL's priority sequencing (deleverage first, grow second) is financially prudent but means it is unlikely to be among the top-performing European telcos in total return terms during the 2025-2026 phase. The opportunity for an investor lies in the 2027-2028 re-rating once dividends restart and debt concerns recede.

  • There is no M&A ambition evident in current management communication. OBEL has completed the VOO integration and absorbed the full VOO SA (100% stake). The Nethys put/call mechanism on Nethys' OBEL shares (residual from the 2024 share-swap) creates a future complexity but is not a near-term strategic event.

  • Management's ambition on DIGI response is explicitly non-aggressive: OBEL is rationalising the VOO brand to streamline costs (a defensive efficiency move) rather than launching price cuts to pre-empt DIGI. This is the rational response given DIGI's sub-1% market share after its first year — the disruption risk is real in the medium term but not yet material enough to justify destroying margins in a pre-emptive price war.

6. Financial Profile

Rating: YELLOW — The EBITDA trajectory is strong and above European peer average; the revenue line and leverage position are the twin weaknesses.

  • FY2024 financials: total revenue €1,993.7m (approximately; up 1.7% 2024 vs 2023), EBITDAaL €544.3m (+10.1% YoY), EPS €0.33 (from ~breakeven in 2023), eCAPEX ~€376m for 2025 with 2024 within similar range. FY2025 financials: revenue declined ~1.5%, EBITDAaL €566.1m (+4.0% YoY), organic free cash flow €90.8m (vs €41.8m in 2024). 2026 guidance: EBITDAaL +~3.5%, eCAPEX ~€360m. (GlobeNewswire, February 2025 and February 2026; Investing.com)

  • EBITDAaL margin: with 2025 EBITDAaL of €566.1m on revenues that declined to approximately €1.96bn (applying the -1.5% to the 2024 base), the margin is approximately 28.9%. This compares to Swisscom (~40% EBITDA margin), KPN (~43%), and Elisa (~35.8% in 2025). OBEL is below the European telco quality tier on margin — C grade by sector standards — but the margin expansion trajectory (approaching 29% from ~24% pre-VOO) is one of the strongest in the peer set.

  • ❌ Net leverage: net debt ~€1.82bn at end-2025 vs EBITDAaL €566.1m = net leverage ~3.2x (improving from ~3.5x at end-2024). Still above the 2.5x level most European regulators and rating agencies consider appropriate for investment-grade telecom. This is the primary reason dividends remain suspended and the stock trades at a discount to its converged peers. European sector average net leverage is tracking toward ~2.5x by 2027 per Scope Ratings — OBEL is above average but improving.

  • Free cash flow inflection is the most positive signal: organic FCF of €90.8m in 2025 vs €41.8m in 2024 — a 117% increase. This reflects the operational leverage from synergies falling to the FCF line as capex stabilises. If eCAPEX declines to €360m in 2026 and EBITDAaL grows by 3.5%, adjusted operating cash flow (EBITDAaL minus eCAPEX) improves by approximately €25-30m, putting organic FCF in the €110-120m range. Lease payments, interest on the Orange SA intra-group loan, and tax payments are the key deductions that determine net FCF available for debt repayment.

  • The intra-group loan interest cost is a critical financial item not fully visible in public reporting. OBEL borrowed from Orange SA to fund the VOO acquisition — the interest rate and repayment terms are governed by an intra-group agreement. This creates an earnings transfer from OBEL to Orange SA that reduces minority shareholder returns. The 2.07x debt-to-equity ratio (149% above the 10-year median of 0.83x) reflects the structural leverage transformation post-VOO. (GuruFocus)

  • VOO synergies: €47.5m delivered in 2024 against the €85m run-rate target by year four (2027). The largest synergy source was MVNO-to-MNO migration (eliminating wholesale mobile cost for VOO's 500,000 subscribers). Remaining synergy capture through 2027 includes network infrastructure optimisation, IT systems rationalisation, procurement and scale benefits, and commercial cross-selling. The acceleration from the original seven-year timeline to four years is a significant positive surprise — it implies better-than-expected operational compatibility between the Orange mobile network and VOO's cable infrastructure.

  • Revenue mix: Orange Belgium's revenue breakdown is roughly 60% mobile services, 25% fixed services (broadband + TV + voice), and 15% equipment/other. Fixed-voice revenue is in structural decline (OTT substitution). Fixed broadband and TV revenues benefit from the VOO acquisition. Mobile services revenue faces DIGI pricing pressure at the margin. Equipment revenue is volatile and not a value-creation driver.

  • Capital returns: the last dividend was paid more than four years ago (50 euro cents per share). No dividend is proposed for 2024 or 2025. Analyst consensus EPS forecast of ~€0.79 for next year (2026) implies earnings per share growth of ~140% from the 2024 base of €0.33. If this materialises, and if management chooses to reinstate a 30-40% payout ratio, the implied dividend yield at current price would be material. The market is not pricing this in given the uncertainty.

  • By peer comparison on EPS growth: OBEL's 2023-2026 EPS CAGR (from near-zero to potentially €0.79) is among the highest in the European telco sector — but starts from a deeply depressed base. The relative growth rate is high; the absolute level of earnings per share and the starting dividend yield of zero are structurally unattractive compared to Elisa (consistent dividend yield ~5%), Swisscom (dividend yield ~5.5%), and KPN (returning capital via buybacks). OBEL is a pure EPS recovery play, not a yield play.

  • 🚩 RARE: The combination of +10.1% EBITDAaL growth in 2024 followed by +4.0% in 2025 in a market with declining revenue is extremely unusual in European telecom. The only comparable recent example is Liberty Global's European subsidiaries following MVNO-to-MNO conversions. This level of operating leverage in a declining-revenue environment signals that the synergy capture is structurally real, not accounting-driven.

7. Value Chain — Demand

Rating: YELLOW — Broadband and mobile demand fundamentals are solid; fixed-voice in structural decline; the FMC bundle is the new demand driver.

  • Belgian broadband penetration is among the highest in Europe at approximately 90% of households, limiting subscriber volume growth upside. The demand opportunity is ARPU migration — from single-play to FMC bundles, and from copper/legacy cable to gigabit-capable services — rather than subscriber acquisition. This is a mature market demand profile, consistent with Elisa (Finland) and Swisscom (Switzerland) peers.

  • FMC bundle demand is the clearest positive demand signal. Orange Belgium's 300,000 Love customers (combining fixed broadband + mobile) represent a growing cohort where churn is demonstrably lower than single-play. European telco data consistently shows FMC churn rates of 1.0-1.5% per month vs 2.0-2.5% for mobile-only — a 100+ basis point churn advantage that directly enhances customer lifetime value. OBEL has not disclosed Belgium-specific FMC churn data, but the European pattern should apply.

  • Mobile data demand is growing structurally (driven by streaming, gaming, and enterprise mobility), but price per GB in Belgium is being rapidly compressed. Pre-DIGI, Belgium was one of the most expensive mobile markets in Europe: Proximus at €16.6/GB, Orange at €13.5/GB, vs France at €0.23/GB. DIGI's entry at €5/month for 15GB (effectively €0.33/GB) has begun repricing expectations even if its subscriber base remains sub-1%. (Belgium Telecoms Market Report 2024, GlobalNewswire)

  • Fixed broadband demand remains robust. Belgium's cable penetration is high — VOO's HFC network passes approximately 45% of Belgian households, predominantly in Wallonia. Broadband ARPU in Belgium is structurally elevated vs European average, partly because Belgium's legacy analogue cable monopoly created a high-ARPU fixed internet culture. Post-gigabit upgrade, OBEL/VOO can justify premium tier pricing. The question is whether the premium is sustainable given DIGI's planned broadband entry (€10/month for 500Mbps).

  • Business/enterprise demand: Belgian enterprise IT and connectivity spending is growing at ~4-5% CAGR. Orange Belgium's positioning as a converged operator with both fixed and mobile capabilities for enterprise is a genuine demand opportunity — multi-site enterprise clients increasingly want a single provider for both fixed LAN connectivity and mobile workforce management. OBEL's "unified go-to-market" for SME, multinational, and public sector clients is the correct demand-side response.

  • TV/content demand is structurally declining in Belgium — OTT substitution from Netflix, Amazon Prime, and Disney+ is reducing the value proposition of traditional linear TV. VOO had significant TV subscriber relationships in Wallonia. Maintaining TV ARPU against OTT competition is one of the structural headwinds OBEL must navigate as part of the FMC bundle management.

  • The population and demographic base (Belgium: ~11.6 million population, ~5 million households) is relatively stable and structurally constructive for recurring telecom revenues. Belgium's GDP per capita (~€44,000) supports premium ARPU pricing. Unlike emerging markets, there is no subscriber volume growth story here — all demand value comes from ARPU mix shift and churn reduction.

  • IoT and machine-to-machine (M2M) connectivity represents a nascent demand tailwind for OBEL's enterprise segment. Belgium's industrial base (chemicals, logistics, automotive supply) creates meaningful IoT connectivity demand. Orange Group's enterprise IoT platform can be deployed through OBEL, creating a potential high-margin revenue layer above base connectivity. No OBEL-specific IoT revenue data is in the public domain.

8. Value Chain — Competition

Rating: ORANGE — Three-player market with DIGI as new entrant; structural consolidation is the medium-term direction but near-term pricing pressure is intensifying.

  • The Belgian mobile market has three MNOs: Proximus (incumbent, ~43% mobile share), Orange Belgium (~25%), and Telenet (with its base3 MNO, ~32%). DIGI Belgium launched as the fourth MNO in December 2024, ending the first year with 91,000 subscribers (sub-1% market share). The market is structurally an oligopoly with DIGI as the disruptive entrant — analogous to DIGI's entry into France (France's SFR/Iliad comparison), Spain (MasMovil entry), or DIGI Romania (where it now holds double-digit share). (Belga News Agency; Telecompaper)

  • ❌ DIGI is a genuine long-term competitive threat, not a rounding error. DIGI Romania grew from zero to 25%+ share over 10 years with the identical playbook — extreme low-price entry, minimal marketing, self-install, digital-only distribution. Belgium's market structure (high absolute pricing, sophisticated broadband infrastructure, technically literate urban population) is fertile ground for DIGI's model. The 91,000 subscriber base after one year is modest by DIGI's own historical standards, but the key question is the trajectory: DIGI Belgium ended 2025 with 91,000 subscribers but has publicly signalled plans to extend into fixed broadband at €10/month for 500Mbps — this is the threat that could structurally reprice the fixed market.

  • Proximus remains the incumbent competitor with full national coverage, FTTH in Flanders (through Unifiber JV), the SFR-equivalent brand recognition, and the natural churn beneficiary from DIGI's disruption (customers who leave OBEL or Telenet may default to Proximus as the established quality brand). Proximus responded to DIGI by raising data allowances without price cuts — a rational defensive posture that suggests it is not prepared to price-match DIGI.

  • Telenet/Liberty Global is the structural wildcard. Telenet operates in Flanders with an HFC cable network comparable to VOO's Wallonia network. Post-VOO, OBEL and Telenet are now the two cable operators operating in different geographic zones — Wallonia (OBEL/VOO) vs Flanders (Telenet). This creates an implicit duopoly structure in cable where head-to-head competition is limited by geography. However, Telenet in April 2025 launched TADAAM mobile (unlimited plans at €25/month including 5G) — signalling that the cross-sell from cable to mobile is competitive terrain.

  • Market consolidation: the Belgian market went from four players (Proximus, Orange, Telenet, MVNO/VOO) to effectively three MNOs plus one cable-FMC operator per region. Post-DIGI entry it returns to four MNOs. European telco history suggests that markets sustainably settle into three MNO structures — four-player markets tend to have protracted margin compression until one player exits or is acquired (DIGI Belgium is unlikely to exit quickly given parent DIGI's financial backing).

  • 🚩 RARE: The Proximus-Orange Belgium MOU on Wallonia fibre (July 2025) represents a structural de-escalation of the capex competition in rural fixed. If approved by competition authorities, this is one of only a handful of examples globally where two MNO competitors have agreed to co-invest passively in a contested geographic market. The economic logic (rural fibre economics do not support full duplication) is sound; the regulatory precedent is being set. This would reduce OBEL's 2028-2040 capex burden in rural Wallonia and improve returns on deployed capital.

  • ARPU dynamics: Belgian mobile ARPU is among the highest in Europe on a per-GB basis — the €13.5/GB OBEL average compares to €0.23/GB in France. This pricing premium is structurally at risk from DIGI.
