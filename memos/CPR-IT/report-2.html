§1 — Brief Recap + Key Components Summary

  • Net verdict: [#J] Campari enters a period of structural reset: new CEO, accelerated portfolio disposal, 200bps cost programme, and a deleveraging arc that completed a year early — but organic growth of +2.4% in both FY24 and FY25 is unspectacular for a premium spirits platform.
  • Leadership reset: [#I] Three CEOs in twelve months (Kunze-Concewitz exit → Fantacchiotti five-month tenure → Hunt from January 2025); Hunt brings US commercial depth but no prior CEO track record at a listed European spirits group.
  • Portfolio rationalisation: [#I] Hunt signals disposal of up to 30 of 72 brands; Cinzano, Averna, Zedda Piras, Frattina sold in 2025 for a combined ~€210m; Courvoisier integration proceeds amid a deep cognac cycle downturn.
  • Cost programme: [#I] 200bps improvement in cost incidence on net sales by 2027; ~500 job cuts (~10% of workforce) announced February 2025; programme framed as "focus, simplification and cost containment."
  • Leverage resolved ahead of schedule: [#A] Net debt/EBITDA reached 2.5x at December 2025, down from 3.6x peak (September 2024), one year ahead of plan; driven by brand disposals (~€210m proceeds) and FCF conversion of ~73%.
  • Aperol flywheel still turning: [#A] Aperol grew from €25m (2003) to >€750m (2025); US penetration +11% in 2024; remains the fulcrum asset but growth moderated to +1% in 2025 on flat US market and destocking headwinds.
  • Tariff headwind materialised: [#I] Campari estimated €25m EBIT impact from US tariffs in 2025 (actual impact €11m after mitigation); Q1 2025 organic sales -4.2%, US -11%; full-year FY25 organic growth recovered to +2.4%.
  • Destocking not fully cleared: [#A] Grand Marnier -14% for 9M 2025; Wild Turkey bounced +14% in Q3 2025 but SKYY persists negative; cognac cycle at nadir with Courvoisier acquired at cycle peak for $1.17bn.
  • Garavoglia tax seizure: [#I] October 2025: Italian police seized €1.3bn of holding-company shares (17% of market cap) in alleged exit-tax fraud probe; chairman Luca Garavoglia under formal investigation; operational business ring-fenced but governance overhang material.
  • Macro crosscurrents: [#A] EUR/USD headwind on US revenues (Americas = ~45% of sales); spirits market declining 2-3% in volume in core US; GLP-1 adoption and no/low alcohol trend structurally reduce addressable volume.
  • Margin recovery underway: [#I] Adjusted EBITDA margin expanded to 25.7% in FY25 (€785m adjusted EBITDA, +7.6% reported); EBIT-adjusted grew +5.4% organically, recovering from FY24 marginal dip.
  • M&A moratorium declared: [#I] Hunt explicitly stated no acquisitions at this stage; full attention on executing the restructuring and portfolio simplification; M&A pause removes overhang risk and confirms capital discipline.

§2 — Change in Leadership

  • Net verdict: [#J] The leadership sequence (Kunze-Concewitz → Fantacchiotti → Hunt) compressed eighteen months of instability into the CEO role; Hunt is the right profile for the US intensity play but his early mandate is operational, not growth-expansionary.
  • Outgoing CEO legacy: [#I] Bob Kunze-Concewitz served 18 years (CEO from 2007), grew net sales ~3x, oversaw 17 acquisitions totalling ~€3bn, and grew Aperol 12-fold; the Aperol flywheel is his primary legacy asset.
  • Courvoisier ($1.17bn, cognac cycle peak, April 2024) is the contested final act — deal committed under his watch, closed under Fantacchiotti.
  • Fantacchiotti tenure: [#I] Appointed April 2024, resigned September 2024 citing personal reasons; served fewer than five months; no strategic imprint left beyond confirming the Courvoisier integration would proceed.
  • Interim co-CEOs Paolo Marchesini and Fabio Di Fede (General Counsel) held the seat from September–January 2025 — a three-and-a-half month gap at executive level.
  • Simon Hunt — profile: [#I] Named CEO December 2024, effective January 15, 2025; >30 years spirits industry experience; CEO of William Grant & Sons (2016-2020); career arc spans Diageo, Allied Domecq, Pernod Ricard, William Grant; Board describes him as bringing "proven US market expertise."
  • Hunt — early strategic signals: [#A] Within months of appointment: confirmed restructuring programme (200bps cost target), declared M&A moratorium, signalled disposal of up to 30 brands, coined "fewer, bigger bets" as strategic doctrine.
  • Quote (Global Drinks Intel, 2025): "I'm leaning more towards disposing of brands than acquiring at this stage."
  • CFO continuity: [#I] Paolo Marchesini retained as Chief Financial and Operating Officer; continuity on financial function provides stability through the restructuring; no CFO-level change risk flagged.
  • Board — Garavoglia overhang: [#A] Chairman Luca Garavoglia (family controller since 1994) is under formal investigation for alleged fraudulent tax returns related to €5.3bn of undeclared capital gains (2018-2020 restructuring); €1.3bn share seizure October 2025.
  • Governance risk is non-trivial: chairman investigation + controlling family seizure = potential board instability if proceedings escalate.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] Hunt's "fewer, bigger bets" rhetoric is strategically sound but untested in execution; William Grant & Sons is a private company with different capital allocation pressures; his ability to manage listed-company activist tensions, a governance investigation, and a cognac cycle downturn simultaneously is unproven.

§3 — Change in Strategy/Structure

  • Net verdict: [#J] The shift from Kunze-Concewitz's acquisition-led brand accumulation to Hunt's disposal-led simplification is the most significant strategic pivot in Campari's modern era — directionally correct but creates near-term revenue attrition (~3% of sales disposed).
  • "Fewer, bigger bets" doctrine: [#I] Formally articulated by Hunt in early 2025; prioritises Aperol, Campari, Wild Turkey, Grand Marnier, Espolón, Courvoisier — six core platforms — with remaining ~30+ brands under review.
  • Portfolio disposals executed 2025: [#I] Cinzano (vermouth/sparkling) and Frattina sold to Caffo Group; Averna (amaro) and Zedda Piras sold to Illva Saronno (December 2025); total proceeds ~€210m.
  • Combined ~3% of net sales disposed; proceeds directed to debt reduction and contributed to ahead-of-plan deleveraging.
  • US strategic intensification: [#A] Americas generated ~47% of group sales in 2025; Hunt's US commercial depth is explicitly the rationale for his appointment; priority is Aperol US penetration growth and Wild Turkey revival.
  • Campari US on-premise grew +8% in FY25 vs flat total US; priority brands +15% in tequila (Espolón) and +15% in Aperol on-premise — selective strength behind headline flat US number.
  • Courvoisier integration strategy: [#A] Courvoisier acquired April 2024 ($1.17bn including maturing inventory $410m); integration proceeding under Hunt; brand contributed €99m in sales post-acquisition with "early signs of momentum in China" and ongoing investment in US and UK.
  • ❌ Acquired at cognac cycle peak — China import volumes fell ~24% in 2024; China now charges up to 34.9% duties on EU brandy from July 2025 with minimum price compliance carve-out.
  • Route-to-market investment: [#I] Campari launched RARE Division for super-premium and above spirits; expanded direct-to-market footprint (23 in-market companies covering ~93% of sales); Australian production facility and co-packing business disposed to concentrate on core European and US manufacturing.
  • Cost structure reset: [#I] 200bps improvement in cost incidence on net sales by 2027; ~€50m+ annualised savings implied at current revenue base; ~500 redundancies (~10% of workforce); programme announced February 2025 alongside FY24 results.
  • Aperol capacity doubled: [#I] New Aperol bottling line at Novi Ligure plant (€75m investment); production capacity doubled to meet long-term US demand growth — committed capital signal on conviction in Aperol flywheel continuing.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] Disposing of 30 brands and restructuring simultaneously while managing a governance crisis and a cognac downturn is operationally complex; execution risk on the simplification programme is higher than the clean "focus" narrative implies.

§4 — Change in Priorities/Ambition

  • Net verdict: [#J] Hunt's stated priorities represent a material de-ambition relative to Kunze-Concewitz's acquisition-led compound growth story — right for balance sheet health but implies consensus needs to lower multi-year top-line expectations.
  • Priority #1 — US focus: [#I] Hunt's consistent message across H1 2025 and FY25 earnings calls is that the US opportunity for Aperol is the primary growth engine; penetration still low relative to European markets; on-premise seeding strategy explicit.
  • Priority #2 — Cost and margin: [#I] The 200bps margin improvement by 2027 is the central financial commitment; EBITDA margin of 25.7% in FY25 is the starting point; peer Diageo runs ~31% adjusted EBIT margin; ❌ Campari's margin structurally trails the mega-cap peers.
  • Priority #3 — Deleveraging complete; returns now: [#A] 2.5x net debt/EBITDA achieved December 2025, one year ahead of plan; Hunt signalled step-up in dividend payout to "further enhance shareholder returns while retaining financial flexibility"; buyback not ruled out but not yet committed.
  • Priority #4 — Portfolio simplification: [#I] "30 brands potentially for sale" signal (Il Sole 24 Ore interview); discussions ongoing; Hunt: "we don't have to sell anything — we are choosing to see if we can see sensible value."
  • M&A moratorium: [#I] Explicit signal to avoid acquisitions while restructuring in progress; duration not formally specified but consistent across multiple communications through 2025.
  • Tone vs Kunze-Concewitz era: [#A] Kunze-Concewitz was acquisition-expansionary and portfolio-accumulating (17 deals, €3bn invested); Hunt is consolidating, cost-focused, and US-commercial; the shift from brand-builder to operator/fixer is the defining tonal change.
  • CMD / medium-term targets: [#I] No formal Capital Markets Day held under Hunt as of May 2026; absence of a CMD with quantified medium-term targets is a transparency gap vs peers (Diageo provides medium-term organic growth guidance ranges; Pernod Ricard provides margin expansion targets).
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] "Fewer, bigger bets" without quantified medium-term targets allows management to manage expectations selectively; the absence of a CMD is cover rather than clarity; consensus medium-term EPS estimates may remain too optimistic if US flat performance and destocking persist into FY26.

§5 — Financial Profile Change

  • Net verdict: [#J] The financial trajectory is improving — leverage solved, margin recovering, FCF strong — but organic growth of +2.4% in both FY24 and FY25 is at the low end of quality premium spirits peers; the interest cost tailwind is the most mechanical near-term EPS lever.
  • Revenue growth — FY22-25: [#I] FY23: double-digit organic growth (third consecutive year); FY24: +2.4% organic (net sales €3.07bn); FY25: +2.4% organic (net sales €3.05bn); reported flat to modest growth due to FX and disposals.
  • Peer context: Diageo organic -1.4% in FY24 (June year-end); Pernod Ricard organic -1% H1 FY25; Campari's +2.4% outperforms both mega-cap peers on top line but is below its own L5Y historical pace.
  • EBITDA margin: [#I] FY25 adjusted EBITDA €785m, margin 25.7%; FY24 EBITDA-adjusted €733m, margin ~23.9%; FY23 margin was higher at ~26%+ before the Courvoisier dilution.
  • 200bps programme implies ~27-28% EBITDA margin by 2027 — still below Diageo's ~32% and Remy Cointreau's stated target of ~33%, but directionally improving.
  • Leverage: [#A] Peak 3.6x net debt/EBITDA (September 2024, post-Courvoisier close); year-end 2025: 2.5x (net financial debt €1,958m); achieved one year ahead of 2026 target; driven by €419m net debt reduction from FCF + ~€210m disposal proceeds.
  • FCF conversion: [#I] ~73% FCF conversion rate stated by management; capital allocation priority has shifted from M&A to deleveraging and now to shareholder returns including dividend step-up.
  • Interest cost lever: [#A] At 3.6x leverage on €733m EBITDA, interest costs were running ~€100-120m+ annually; as leverage falls to 2.5x and refinancing occurs at moderating rates, each 50bps improvement in average cost = ~€10-15m EBIT benefit; this is the most mechanical FY26-27 EPS lever requiring no additional execution.
  • Capex: [#I] €75m invested in Aperol Novi Ligure capacity doubling (2024); ongoing capex normalises post-expansion; no major capex commitments flagged under Hunt strategy.
  • EPS trajectory: [#A] Interest cost reduction + 200bps margin programme + Courvoisier annualisation (€99m partial-year = ~€150m+ annualised) = three additive EPS levers for FY26-27; partially offset by disposal revenue attrition (~3% of sales) and tariff headwind (~€11m actual FY25).
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] FY25 +2.4% organic replicating FY24's +2.4% suggests the transition year story may extend — if US tariff and destocking headwinds persist into FY26, the consensus EPS recovery timeline elongates.

§6 — Value Chain — Demand

  • Net verdict (CQ-C1/C5 addressed): [#J] Destocking is not uniformly resolved — Aperol is past the trough and accelerating (especially US), but Grand Marnier and SKYY remain in contraction; the fulcrum for EPS recovery is Aperol US penetration + interest cost reduction, not a broad industry volume recovery.
  • Aperol demand — US: [#A] US Aperol +11% in FY24; on-premise +15% in FY25; production capacity doubled (Novi Ligure) — committed capital confirms confidence; penetration still far below European saturation levels.
  • ⚡ RARE: Aperol grew from €25m (2003) to >€750m (2025) — a 22-year 17% CAGR in a category Campari itself created; the aperitivo occasion is still expanding in US, Australia, and Latin American seeding markets.
  • Aperol demand — Europe: [#A] Aperol +1% overall in FY25 (resilient given market context); Italy -4% in FY24 (tough comp); Germany +5% in FY24; Europe is maturing — US is the incremental growth engine for the brand.
  • Aperol RTD canned spritz launched — extending occasion reach and reducing preparation friction for retail and out-of-home consumption.
  • Grand Marnier destocking (CQ-C1): [#A] Grand Marnier -12% in Q1 2025, -14% for 9M 2025; decline driven by logistic delays, US destocking, and pricing focus in competitive orange liqueur category; stabilising in Q3 2025 (-1% in Q3 vs -14% YTD) — base effects turning.
  • ❌ Grand Marnier remains in contraction through Q3 2025 — destocking not yet complete; recovery is stabilisation, not volume inflection.
  • Wild Turkey destocking (CQ-C1): [#A] Wild Turkey affected by tariff-driven destocking and logistic delays in Q1 2025; recovered to +14% in Q3 2025 — the cleanest destocking recovery in the portfolio.
  • SKYY Vodka: [#A] SKYY -8% in Q1 2025; structural category challenge in US vodka (declining share to tequila and RTDs); less a destocking story, more a category allocation question.
  • Cognac/Courvoisier cycle (CQ-C2): [#A] Global cognac export volumes fell ~24% to Far East in 2024; China's 34.9% duties (from July 2025) create structural headwind; Courvoisier contributing €99m in partial-year sales with "early signs of momentum" in China; full recovery timeline uncertain.
  • LVMH/Hennessy -12% spirits 9M 2025; Pernod/Martell -25% China H1 2025; Remy -19% cognac division — sector-wide cycle trough, not Campari-specific.
  • ❌ Courvoisier acquired at $1.17bn at cycle peak; the acquisition multiple has expanded materially in real terms since closing.
  • Consumer trading-down: [#I] US spirits market down 2-3% in volume; de-premiumisation visible in vodka and rum; tequila and aperitifs maintain premiumisation; Campari portfolio skews towards the more resilient segments.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] Aperol US penetration is the bull case engine — if the US aperitivo occasion proves more seasonal (summer-led) than structural, the penetration runway is narrower than the €25m→€750m European trajectory suggests.

§7 — Value Chain — Competition

  • Net verdict: [#J] Campari occupies a structurally differentiated position via the aperitivo category and the Aperol brand monopoly on the orange spritz occasion; competitive pressure is real in bourbon and cognac but the core Aperol franchise has low substitution risk.
  • Diageo competition: [#I] Diageo competes in gin (Tanqueray vs Campari's Bulldog), cream liqueur (Baileys), and vodka (Ciroc/Smirnoff vs SKYY); Diageo organic growth was -1.4% in FY24 — weaker than Campari on top line.
  • Diageo's EBIT margin (~31%) remains structurally above Campari's 25.7%; scale advantage in distribution and marketing budget relative to Campari.
  • Pernod Ricard competition: [#I] Pernod competes in liqueurs (Cointreau vs Grand Marnier) and gin (Beefeater vs Bulldog); Pernod organic -1% in H1 FY25; also a major cognac player (Martell) with China exposure headwind matching Campari's Courvoisier pain.
  • Remy Cointreau: [#A] Most direct cognac peer to Courvoisier; Remy's cognac division down ~19% in H1 2025; estimated €65m tariff blow (mitigated); withdrew sales targets — peer signal that cognac cycle pain is deep and duration uncertain.
  • Cointreau (Remy) is a direct competitor to Grand Marnier in premium orange liqueur — a contested space where Grand Marnier's pricing strategy has cost it volume share.
  • US craft distillers: [#I] Wild Turkey competes in premium American whiskey against Woodford Reserve (Brown-Forman), Maker's Mark (Beam Suntory), and a proliferation of craft bourbon brands; Wild Turkey's "Russell's Reserve" sub-brand targets the premium tier.
  • Private label / own label: [#I] Aperol faces no meaningful private-label risk — the aperitivo occasion is brand-defined; Grand Marnier and SKYY face more substitution risk from lower-priced alternatives in their respective categories.
  • Aperitivo category competition: [#A] Externally, St-Germain (Bacardi), Select (Diageo), and Luxardo Bitter all compete in Italian bitters; none have Aperol's scale or occasion ownership; the spritz variant space (Hugo Spritz, Limoncello Spritz) is growing but not brand-captured by Campari.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] Aperol's spritz occasion is a cocktail category Campari invented — but cocktail occasions can migrate; the Hugo Spritz (elderflower/prosecco variant) is growing in Southern Europe as an alternative aperitivo, and Campari does not own that formulation.

§8 — Value Chain — Disruption

  • Net verdict: [#J] The Aperol brand is unusual in that the primary disruption vectors (RTD, no/low alcohol) are either manageable (Aperol RTD canned spritz launched) or slow-moving (GLP-1); the Aperol Spritz format is structurally RTD-compatible, which is advantageous relative to straight spirits.
  • RTD cocktails: [#A] RTD spirits grew +26.8% to $2.8bn in the US in 2023; RTDs the only major alcohol category to grow in volume in 2023 (+2%); Campari launched Aperol canned spritz to capture this occasion — format extension rather than category threat.
  • Wild Turkey RTD pre-mixed bourbon cocktails also in market; Campari is responding to RTD as an opportunity, not purely a defensive play.
  • No/low alcohol: [#I] NA spirits growing double-digit but from a small base (<0.5% of spirits volume); NA Aperol-style products (e.g., Lyre's Italian Orange) exist but lack the brand equity and occasion association of Aperol; low direct disruption risk to Aperol in the near term.
  • Aperol Spritz as cocktail trend: [#A] Aperol Spritz is the embodiment of the aperitivo trend driving Campari's growth — the cocktail occasion expanding in the US is the bull-case driver, not a headwind; the spritz category is an enabler of Aperol penetration, not a competitive threat.
  • De-seasonalisation (ski season activations at Aspen; Aperol Après Ski collection) explicitly attempts to extend the occasion beyond summer — directionally correct, execution risk remains.
  • GLP-1 headwinds: [#A] 44% of GLP-1 users drink less after starting treatment; 82% retain reduced drinking habits even after stopping; EY estimates GLP-1 adoption structurally reduces alcohol cravings in a meaningful minority of users.
  • ❌ GLP-1 adoption is a slow-burn structural headwind affecting the entire spirits sector; Aperol's low ABV (~11%) and social/occasion framing may make it more resilient than straight spirits, but the headwind is real and cumulative.
  • THC/cannabis beverages: [#I] Hemp-derived THC beverages growing in US states where legal; most relevant to beer category; low near-term materiality for Campari's premium spirits positioning.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] Aperol RTD canned spritz is a logical extension but competes in a crowded RTD shelf set where Campari's brand power is lower than in on-premise; if RTD margin is structurally below the on-premise draught format, mix shift could dilute group profitability as format penetration grows.

§9 — Value Chain — Political/Regulatory

  • Net verdict: [#J] The tariff environment is the most acute near-term political risk — Campari quantified a €25m EBIT impact (actual €11m after mitigation in 2025) and faces ongoing uncertainty; the Garavoglia tax investigation is the most idiosyncratic governance risk in the cohort.
  • US tariffs on European spirits: [#A] Trump administration imposed 15% tariff on European goods; Campari guided €25m potential EBIT impact at 15%; actual FY25 impact was €11m due to mitigating actions (pricing, supply chain adjustments).
  • ❌ Campari's Italian manufacturing base (Aperol, Campari Bitter) means all European exports to the US face tariff exposure; Wild Turkey (Kentucky) and Espolón (Mexico) are US/NAFTA-manufactured and not directly tariff-hit.
  • 200% tariff scenario (threatened by Trump on European alcohol) would be highly disruptive — Campari has not publicly quantified this scenario.
  • Cognac/brandy tariffs : [#I] Courvoisier (French cognac) would face any US tariff on European spirits; cognac category already in deep cycle trough — an incremental tariff would compound existing pain.
  • China tariffs on EU brandy: [#I] China imposed up to 34.9% duties on EU brandy from July 2025 with minimum-price compliance escape; Courvoisier would benefit from pricing compliance but this limits pricing flexibility in China.
  • Italy regulatory/tax — Garavoglia: [#A] Chairman Luca Garavoglia and holding company Lagfin under investigation for €5.3bn of undeclared capital gains (2018-2020 restructuring); €1.3bn in holding-company shares seized October 2025; Campari's operating entity incorporated in the Netherlands post-2019 restructuring — the tax dispute relates to the 2019 migration, not current operations.
  • Operational business ring-fenced but chairman under formal investigation creates board instability risk at a critical strategic inflection point.
  • EU-US trade negotiations: [#I] European spirits industry lobbied for 0% tariffs as part of EU-US deal framework (July 2025); spirits excluded from early deal frameworks; outcome unresolved as of May 2026.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] The Garavoglia investigation creates an unquantifiable governance risk; if proceedings escalate to the operating-company level or if the chairman is required to step down, the controlling family's oversight of Campari's board would be disrupted at a critical strategic inflection point.

§10 — Value Chain — Supply Side

  • Net verdict: [#J] Campari's supply side is well-managed with no acute commodity crisis; the Aperol capacity doubling (€75m) is the primary capital commitment and positions the brand for US demand; glass packaging costs have moderated from 2022-23 peaks.
  • Aperol production: [#I] New bottling line at Novi Ligure (northwest Italy) commissioned 2024; production capacity doubled; €75m investment; supply is no longer a constraint on US market development.
  • Grape spirits and botanicals: [#I] Campari Bitter uses proprietary herbs and citrus; Aperol uses gentian, rhubarb, cinchona, and orange — proprietary botanical blends with controlled sourcing; not agave-dependent.
  • Agave (Espolón tequila): [#I] Agave cost cycle peaked 2022-23 and has been declining; Espolón benefits from lower agave input costs in FY25; tequila on-premise +15% in US showing demand strength despite prior cost pressures.
  • Glass packaging: [#A] Glass costs surged 20-30% in 2022-23 as energy costs rose; European energy costs moderating in 2024-25 as European energy market normalised; glass cost normalisation is a positive input cost tailwind for Campari's Italian-manufactured portfolio.
  • Italian manufacturing base: [#I] 23 production facilities globally; core aperitif production in Italy (Sesto San Giovanni for Campari, Novi Ligure for Aperol); UK (Bulldog gin), US (Wild Turkey, Lawrenceburg Kentucky), Mexico (Espolón); Australian production facility disposed in 2025.
  • Cognac maturing inventory: [#A] Courvoisier acquisition included $410m of maturing inventory — a significant balance sheet item representing long-dated natural assets; aging cycle means supply of older expressions is fixed and declining; working capital consideration distinct from operational supply management.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] Italian manufacturing concentration for core aperitifs creates geographic supply risk; any Italian industrial action, energy cost spike, or regulatory disruption at Novi Ligure or Sesto San Giovanni would impact the highest-margin brands.

§11 — Revenue/Demand Cycle

  • Net verdict (CQ-C1/C5): [#J] The spirits destocking cycle is at different stages by brand: Aperol is past the trough and in volume recovery; Grand Marnier is stabilising (not yet recovering); Wild Turkey has bounced; SKYY remains structurally challenged; Courvoisier/cognac is at or near cycle nadir.
  • Aperol cycle position: [#A] Aperol +5% in FY24, +1% in FY25 (both above flat spirits market); US +11% in FY24 and +15% on-premise in FY25; production capacity doubled — all signals confirm ongoing penetration phase, not destocking recovery.
  • Volume normalisation is not the right frame for Aperol — it is still penetrating a new market; the question is speed of US occasion adoption.
  • Grand Marnier cycle (CQ-C1/CQ-C3): [#A] -14% for 9M 2025; stabilising (Q3 2025: -1%); the brand was hit by simultaneous destocking, pricing focus (Campari raised prices), and category competition.
  • CQ-C3 (pricing power): Grand Marnier raises prices → volume falls; soft read on pricing power in orange liqueur — heritage premium positioning is being tested against price elasticity in a competitive category.
  • Wild Turkey: [#A] Destocking appears completed by Q3 2025 (+14% in quarter); bourbon category has structural premiumisation tailwinds; Wild Turkey "Russell's Reserve" positioning at premium tier provides mix uplift.
  • SKYY Vodka: [#I] Structural category headwind (US vodka losing share to tequila and RTDs); less a cycle story, more a strategic question about whether Campari should continue investing in the brand.
  • Courvoisier/cognac cycle: [#A] Cognac exports to China -24% in 2024; Hennessy -12% spirits 9M 2025; Martell -25% in China H1 2025; Remy -19% — all indicators point to a cycle trough in 2025 with early China recovery signs.
  • ❌ Courvoisier acquired at $1.17bn at cycle peak; current cognac downturn means the acquisition multiple has expanded materially since closing.
  • Fulcrum driver — 18-36M EPS (CQ-C5): [#A] Two compounding levers: (1) Aperol US penetration rate — on-premise +15% suggests the occasion is taking hold; each percentage point of US penetration equates to significant incremental volume given the addressable market size; (2) Interest expense normalisation — at 2.5x leverage with declining rates, interest cost reduction flows directly to EPS with no additional execution required.
  • Sceptical counter: [#J] The fulcrum thesis depends on Aperol US penetration sustaining beyond the initial spritz novelty phase; if the Aperol Spritz is a US trend rather than a US habit, the penetration curve flattens and the EPS recovery relies disproportionately on the mechanical interest cost lever.

§12 — Value Chain — Macroeconomic

  • Net verdict: [#J] The macro environment is the most complex it has been for Campari in a decade: EUR/USD headwind on US revenues, challenged Italian domestic consumer, tariff uncertainty — partially offset by moderating glass/energy costs and declining European interest rates.
  • EUR/USD FX: [#A] Americas = ~45-47% of Campari's net sales; Campari invoices and translates in USD; EUR/USD at ~1.10-1.13 in 2025 (stronger euro vs 2022 lows) creates a negative translation effect on reported results.
  • Industry rule of thumb for spirits with ~50% USD exposure: ~1-2% of reported growth impacted per 5% EUR/USD movement; Campari's reported FY25 sales of €3.05bn were flat vs FY24's €3.07bn despite +2.4% organic — FX translation is visibly real.
  • Italian consumer: [#A] Italy -4% in FY24 (CPR's domestic market); Italian real wages flat-to-slightly-positive in 2025; inflation moderating; Italian consumer not in acute distress but premium spirits are discretionary purchases.
  • Emerging market exposure: [#I] Brazil and Mexico are seeding markets for Aperol growing double-digit from low bases; no material EM revenue concentration risk; Australia a meaningful historical market (production facility disposed 2025).
  • Energy costs: [#A] Italian energy costs (natural gas) elevated 2022-23; moderating in 2024-25 as European energy market normalised; meaningful positive for Italian-manufactured products (Aperol, Campari Bitter) where production energy is a material input cost.
  • Interest rate environment: [#A] ECB rate cycle peaked ~2023-24 and is now declining; Campari's debt is a mix of bonds and credit facilities; each refinancing at lower rates reduces interest expense — the deleveraging achievement is amplified by falling base rates, making the interest cost lever doubly powerful.
  • Global spirits market volume: [#I] IWSR data: US spirits volume -2-3% in 2024 (first sustained multi-year decline in decades); European spirits market flat-to-marginally negative; post-COVID demand normalisation and structural factors (sober-curiosity, GLP-1, generational change) are the primary drivers.
