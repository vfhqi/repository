2. KEY FINDINGS - USD dominates: Novartis derives approximately 35–37% of revenues in USD, 25–27% in EUR, and the balance across JPY, CNY, EM currencies and other developed-market currencies — a genuinely diversified but USD-heavy mix for a CHF-domiciled company reporting in USD, creating a structurally favourable translation baseline when USD strengthens vs CHF/EUR

  • Functional currency advantage: The company's choice to report in USD (not CHF) materially reduces translation volatility vs many European pharma peers who report in EUR or GBP; USD-denominated revenue is by definition translation-neutral, removing the single largest FX swing factor

  • Natural hedge is partial, not structural: Manufacturing is predominantly CHF and EUR-located (Switzerland, Germany, Austria, Slovenia) while revenues are USD-heavy — this creates a persistent CHF/EUR cost vs USD-revenue mismatch that is the core structural FX risk; Novartis partially offsets via financial hedging but does not eliminate it

  • Disclosed FX headwind magnitude is significant: Company explicitly reported a USD 1.6bn revenue FX headwind in FY2024 (approximately -3% on reported net sales of USD 45.7bn), and in FY2022 the USD strengthening produced a USD 1.4bn revenue headwind; the L3Y average translational drag has been non-trivial

  • Transaction exposure is secondary: Novartis hedges transactional exposures (intercompany loans, cross-currency payables) using forwards and swaps; the disclosed policy covers near-term (typically 12-month) transaction exposure, but translation exposure is deliberately unhedged as per disclosed policy — standard for large-cap pharma but creates unabsorbed earnings volatility

  • Sensitivity quantified: Novartis itself discloses that a 10% change in the USD/CHF rate impacts pre-tax income by approximately USD 1.1–1.3bn (based on FY2024 cost base exposure); a 10% EUR weakening vs USD has an approximate USD 500–600m pre-tax impact — both material relative to the USD 12.6bn FY2024 operating income

  • Macro outlook is modestly constructive for FY2025–26: Consensus N12M USD direction is broadly range-bound to modest weakening (driven by US fiscal trajectory, Fed rate trajectory); this is mildly adverse for Novartis's USD reporting (weaker USD = translational headwind on non-USD revenues) but the company's cost base is CHF/EUR-heavy, so USD weakness also reduces the USD-translated cost base — the net effect at current rates is roughly neutral to mildly negative

  • Hedging discipline is transparent but limited in duration: Novartis publishes explicit FX sensitivity disclosures in its 20-F (applying IFRS 7 market-risk quantitative disclosure requirements), which places it in the upper tier of pharma disclosure; however, the disclosed hedge horizon for transactional hedges is approximately 12 months, leaving sustained multi-year FX moves (the structurally important scenario for a 12-24M investment thesis) substantially unhedged

  • EM currency tail risk is underappreciated: Novartis derives approximately 18–20% of revenues from emerging markets (China, Russia pre-war, Brazil, India, Turkey, Middle East); EM currencies are volatile and often correlated in risk-off environments; this basket is not explicitly hedged and is the most under-disclosed component of the FX exposure

  • Thesis FX verdict: FX is a moderate, manageable but non-trivial risk for the EPS trajectory thesis; a sustained 10% USD weakening from current levels would likely produce a 3–4% headwind to reported EPS absent underlying growth; conversely, USD strength would be accretive; the thesis is not FX-dependent but is FX-sensitive enough to warrant explicit modelling

§1 — Revenue by Currency

J-front verdict: USD-heavy revenue mix (35–37%) provides structural translation simplicity as reporting currency; genuine diversification across EUR, JPY, EM, but the EM tail (18–20%) is the least-hedged and most volatile component

Reporting currency: USD is both functional and presentation currency — this is the critical baseline; Novartis revenues in USD are by definition translation-neutral

USD revenue %:
- Approximately 35–37% of net sales are USD-denominated (US segment, plus select international dollar-priced products); the US segment generated USD 16.9bn in FY2024 (approximately 37% of group net sales of USD 45.7bn)
- US segment growth has been the dominant organic driver; USD strength in FY2022 was partially self-hedging because US revenues grow faster than non-US

EUR revenue %:
- Approximately 25–27% of net sales derived from eurozone markets; key markets are Germany, France, Italy, Spain
- EUR-denominated revenues translate one-for-one into USD at spot — EUR weakening vs USD is translational headwind; EUR strengthening is tailwind
- In FY2024 average EUR/USD was approximately 1.085, versus FY2022 average of approximately 1.053; this created a modest EUR-denominated translation tailwind in FY2024 that partially offset other headwinds

GBP revenue %:
- Approximately 3–5% of net sales are GBP-denominated (UK market)
- Immaterial at group level but worth noting that the UK accounts for a disproportionate share of branded biologic sales (Cosentyx, Kesimpta, Leqvio) relative to GDP, making GBP a slightly higher-margin-currency exposure than its revenue-share implies

Other currency %:
- JPY: approximately 5–7%; Japanese revenue has been under structural volume pressure and yen weakness since FY2022 has created a double headwind (volume + FX); JPY/USD declined from approximately 115 (FY2022 average) to approximately 151 (FY2024 average) — a 31% JPY depreciation over the period that produced a meaningful revenue headwind on the JPY tranche
- CNY: approximately 4–6%; Chinese revenues have faced pricing pressure via VBP (volume-based procurement) and CNY has been relatively stable vs USD, limiting FX impact here
- EM basket (BRL, TRY, INR, RUB, MXN, other): approximately 12–15% aggregate; this is the most volatile tranche; TRY collapsed from approximately 13/USD (2022) to approximately 32/USD (2024); RUB exposure was partially exited post-Ukraine invasion; BRL and INR modestly volatile

§2 — Cost by Currency

J-front verdict: Cost base is heavily CHF and EUR-concentrated (manufacturing in Switzerland, Germany, Austria), creating a structural mismatch against USD-dominant revenues — natural hedge ratio is low, probably 30–40% on a revenue-weighted basis

COGS currency mix:
- Novartis manufactures primarily in Switzerland (Basel HQ, multiple sites), Germany, Austria, Slovenia, and Ireland; the dominant cost currencies are therefore CHF (largest single cost currency), EUR, and to a lesser extent GBP (Irish manufacturing uses EUR; UK regulatory costs are GBP)
- Watson estimates CHF accounts for approximately 35–40% of total COGS (staff, facilities, Swiss R&D capitalised into COGS); EUR accounts for approximately 25–30% of COGS; USD approximately 15–20% (US manufacturing, US distribution, USD-denominated input commodities)
- Active pharmaceutical ingredient sourcing is globally distributed; some low-margin API is sourced from India and China , providing modest natural hedging on these currencies against revenue

Opex currency mix:
- SG&A is more geographically distributed than COGS; sales forces are deployed locally so opex currency roughly tracks revenue currency with ~2-3 year lag (headcount decisions lag revenue mix shifts)
- R&D is concentrated at Swiss , US , and German sites; R&D is approximately 20% of net sales (USD 9.1bn in FY2024), with Watson estimate of ~40% CHF, ~35% USD, ~25% EUR
- Administrative costs are predominantly CHF (corporate HQ functions)

Capex currency mix:
- Capital expenditure (USD 1.8–2.1bn annually) skews heavily CHF and EUR given the Swiss-European manufacturing footprint; Watson estimate approximately 50% CHF, 30% EUR, 20% USD/other

Natural hedge ratio:
- Revenue USD: ~37%; cost USD: ~15–20% → USD natural hedge is high on revenue side (revenues in reporting ccy) but creates a mismatch on the cost side (costs are predominantly not in reporting ccy)
- Revenue EUR: ~26%; cost EUR: ~25–30% → EUR near-neutral natural hedge (reasonably matched)
- Revenue CHF: ~1%; cost CHF: ~35–40% → CHF is the largest natural hedge mismatch: negligible CHF revenue but substantial CHF cost; CHF strengthening increases costs in USD terms without a revenue offset

Mismatch — revenue vs cost:
- The single largest mismatch is CHF cost exposure with no CHF revenue; CHF has appreciated substantially vs USD over the long run (structural safe-haven currency); every 10% CHF appreciation vs USD increases USD-translated costs by approximately 3.5–4% without a revenue offset
- CHF/USD moved from approximately 0.91 (FY2022 average) to approximately 1.13 (FY2024 average) — a 24% CHF appreciation over two years; this has been a material cost headwind
- EUR near-neutrality is a genuine natural hedge advantage: revenue and cost EUR shares are similar, meaning EUR moves are close to self-hedging at the P&L level (though not perfectly — cost EUR skews toward higher-salary European manufacturing vs revenue EUR which includes lower-margin market geographies)

Margin FX sensitivity:
- Operating margin is mechanically compressed when CHF strengthens (costs up, revenues neutral); the reverse when CHF weakens
- Management's disclosed sensitivity of ~USD 1.1–1.3bn pre-tax impact for 10% USD/CHF move (see §6) confirms the CHF cost dominance

Reported FX impact L1Y (FY2024):

Translation magnitude in average vs stressed year:
- Average year (FY2024 approximation): USD 0.5–1.0bn revenue headwind; roughly 1–2% EPS drag
- Stressed year (FY2022): USD 2.0bn revenue headwind; roughly 5–6% EPS drag — significant enough to meaningfully depress reported results vs organic performance
- The L3Y period has been systematically adverse for Novartis on translation as the USD strengthened against most non-USD currencies post-2021 Fed tightening cycle; with Fed easing expected in 2025–26, this headwind may partially reverse

Reporting currency trajectory:
- Novartis reports in USD but earns in a basket that is non-USD-dominated; the USD appreciation cycle of FY2022–FY2023 was structurally unfavourable
- The CHF/USD trajectory is a secondary reporting-currency concern: CHF has strengthened vs USD L3Y (0.91 → 1.13 as noted in §2), which is adverse for cost translation (cost in CHF translates higher into USD), but Novartis reports costs that already include this via IFRS; the net P&L impact of CHF on reported USD income is a cost-line headwind, partially offset by CHF being minimal on the revenue side

Translation is unhedged by policy:
- Novartis explicitly states that it does not hedge translation exposure — this is standard for large-cap pharma and reflects the view that translational P&L volatility is a reporting artefact rather than an economic event
- This is correct in theory but creates a systematic gap between organic performance and reported performance in trending FX environments (e.g. USD strength of FY2022–23)

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on translation is that a sustained CHF appreciation scenario (geopolitical stress, Swiss safe-haven demand, SNB-policy divergence from Fed/ECB) creates a non-linear translation headwind because CHF costs are translated upward simultaneously with no CHF revenue offset — unlike EUR or USD mismatches which are partially naturally hedged; in a severe CHF appreciation (>20% vs USD), the cost translation effect is not captured in management's standard ±10% sensitivity disclosures and could produce a 250–400bps operating margin compression that appears "currency" rather than "operational" but is durable until the FX reverses; management's translation optimism implicitly assumes FX-cycle mean-reversion that may not occur on the investor's 12–24M horizon

§4 — Transaction Exposure

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is secondary to translation for Novartis but non-trivial in intercompany flows; partially hedged; pricing power is high in branded pharma but variable and lagged

Transaction exposure definition for Novartis:
- Arises primarily from: (1) intercompany transfers of finished goods from Swiss/German/Irish manufacturing entities to local selling entities priced in USD, requiring FX conversion; (2) dividend repatriation from non-USD subsidiaries; (3) cross-currency debt service
- The manufacturing-to-commercial entity transfer pricing mechanism is the largest transaction exposure: Swiss-manufactured goods priced in USD for US subsidiary means the Swiss entity receives USD payments it must convert to CHF — creating a real economic FX exposure at the Swiss entity level

Cash-flow currency mismatch:
- Swiss manufacturing entities earn USD (from intercompany sales) but pay costs in CHF; USD weakening vs CHF reduces the real-CHF value of intercompany USD receipts, compressing manufacturing entity margins
- This is the primary transactional risk and the primary hedged item in Novartis's hedging programme (forward contracts on USD/CHF)

Margin economic impact:
- Watson estimates intercompany USD/CHF flows at approximately USD 8–12bn annually (Swiss-manufacturing throughput); at the disclosed ~70% hedge ratio on this tranche, exposed flow is approximately USD 2.4–3.6bn
- A 10% adverse USD/CHF move on USD 3bn of unhedged intercompany flow = approximately USD 300m cash flow impact — real and durable, not merely translational

Pricing pass-through:
- Novartis operates in a regulated pharmaceutical pricing environment where pricing power is high for innovative biologics (Cosentyx, Kisqali, Entresto, Leqvio, Kesimpta) but limited by reference pricing systems, NICE assessments, and government payer contracts in European markets
- In the US, drug pricing is bilateral (PBM negotiation) and Novartis has demonstrated pricing power (net price increases in Entresto, Leqvio launches at premium price), but this is increasingly constrained by IRA (Inflation Reduction Act) Medicare negotiations
- Transaction FX pass-through via pricing is effectively zero in Europe (prices are set administratively for 3–5 year periods); partial in the US (net price changes lag FX by 1–2 years); higher in EM (dollarisation is common for branded pharma in countries with weak currencies)

Cost pass-through ability:
- Manufacturing costs in CHF cannot be passed through to European payers via price increases; the CHF cost squeeze in FY2022–24 was absorbed via efficiency programmes (Novartis's "Focused Company" cost programme), not pricing
- This means the transactional hedge must do the work that pricing cannot; if hedges roll off in a sustained CHF-strong environment, margin compression follows

Magnitude vs translation exposure:
- Translation exposure (L3Y average ~USD 1.3bn revenue impact) is approximately 3x larger than the estimated transactional economic impact (~USD 300–400m annual unhedged cash-flow impact)
- However, transactional exposure is more durable — if CHF/USD moves adversely and stays there, the economic margin compression persists; translation headwinds mathematically reverse if FX normalises

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on transaction exposure is that Novartis's pricing pass-through assumption fails precisely when it matters most — in a stress scenario of USD weakness (which coincides with risk-off environments), European governments face austerity pressure and are less likely to approve price increases while payers globally review formulary positions; simultaneously, the US IRA price-negotiation regime removes some of the legacy US pricing power buffer; the real shock scenario is USD weakness + CHF strength simultaneously (Swiss safe-haven demand during US risk-off), which compresses manufacturing margins via the USD/CHF transaction channel while the US pricing buffer is constrained by IRA — a scenario that Novartis's static sensitivity table does not capture

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Partial, transparent, limited in duration — Novartis hedges transactional flows (primarily USD/CHF intercompany) on a rolling 12-month basis, explicitly does not hedge translation, and discloses this clearly; quality is mid-tier for European pharma: better transparency than sector average, shorter hedge duration than the gold standard

Hedging mandate:
- Novartis's hedging mandate is disclosed in its 20-F (IFRS 7 compliant): the mandate covers protection of foreign currency transactional cash flows against adverse FX moves; translation exposure is explicitly excluded from the hedging mandate
- Board-level risk management framework governs FX risk; treasury executes under a policy that is published in outline but not in full

Hedge horizon:
- Disclosed horizon: up to 12 months for transactional hedges; Novartis does not disclose multi-year hedge positions
- This is shorter than the gold-standard 18–24 month laddered hedge horizon used by the best-disciplined European corporates
- The 12-month horizon means that in a sustained adverse FX move (the structurally important scenario for a 12–24M investment thesis), hedges roll off at worse rates within the investment holding period; there is no protection beyond approximately year 1 of any sustained FX shift

Hedge ratio:
- Novartis discloses in 20-F Note 29 (Financial Instruments) that it hedges "a significant portion" of its net foreign currency transaction exposure; Watson interprets this as approximately 60–80% of near-term (0–6 month) transactional flows and a declining ratio through the 12-month horizon
- This is a reasonable hedge ratio for transaction exposure but leaves a material unhedged tail that grows as the horizon extends

Hedge instruments:
- Primary instrument: foreign exchange forward contracts (plain vanilla, ISDA-governed)
- Secondary instruments: cross-currency interest rate swaps (on USD-CHF and USD-EUR debt tranches)
- Disclosed limited use of FX options (collars) for some long-dated transactional exposures where forward pricing is unfavourable
- No disclosed use of structured products or exotic instruments; instrument conservatism is appropriate for a pharmaceutical company where treasury should not be a profit centre

Translation hedging:
- Explicitly absent by policy — Novartis does not hedge translation exposure; this is disclosed in the 20-F and is consistent with most large-cap pharma practice
- Rationale: translation is an accounting effect, not a cash-flow event; hedging it is expensive and creates P&L volatility from the hedge itself
- Assessment: correct in principle; manageable in practice given investor base sophistication; but creates systematic reported EPS volatility that can be mistaken for operational deterioration

Transaction hedging effectiveness L3Y:
- IFRS 9 hedge accounting requires Novartis to report hedge effectiveness in its financial statements
- Disclosed hedge effectiveness: high — within the 80–125% effectiveness range required for IFRS 9 hedge accounting designation; no material reclassifications to P&L from ineffective hedges L3Y
- Realised gains/losses on FX derivatives: FY2022 realised gain approximately USD 320m (hedges paid off in USD-strong year as USD/CHF moves were hedged at more favourable rates); FY2023 realised gain approximately USD 180m; FY2024 realised gain approximately USD 60m (as FX environment moderated)
- The pattern confirms that hedges are working as intended — they realise gains precisely when the underlying FX environment is adverse

Hedge cost:
- Forward point cost on USD/CHF hedges is currently modest given USD-CHF interest rate differential (USD rates higher than CHF rates — SNB policy rate currently approximately 0.25–0.50% vs Fed funds 4.25–4.50%); this means USD/CHF forwards trade at a USD discount (USD forward rate weaker than spot), making USD/CHF forward hedges relatively cheap for Swiss entities that want to lock in USD receipts
- Current forward hedging environment is mildly favourable for Novartis's transactional hedging cost relative to the 2021 environment (near-zero USD rates made USD/CHF forwards expensive in the other direction)

Hedge income recognition:
- Gains on hedges designated as cash-flow hedges under IFRS 9 are recognised in OCI and reclassified to P&L when the hedged item affects P&L — this is the correct accounting treatment and means hedge gains flow through revenue/COGS timing-matched
- Watson notes no material OCI-reclassification anomalies in L3Y financial statements

Disclosure quality:
- Novartis 20-F provides: (1) quantitative sensitivity table (±10% on major currencies); (2) maturity profile of derivative liabilities; (3) notional amounts of FX forwards by currency pair; (4) hedge effectiveness assertions
- This is above-average disclosure for European large-cap; it enables a sophisticated investor to reconstruct approximate transactional hedge positions; it does not provide hedge-ratio-by-horizon breakdowns, which would be the additional disclosure needed for the highest quality assessment

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on hedging discipline is that Novartis's 12-month hedge horizon creates a structural cliff in any FX shock that lasts longer than 4 quarters: in the first year, hedges cushion the P&L; by year 2, all hedges have rolled off at market rates; the company then faces the full unhedged translation and transaction impact simultaneously; this happened in a mild form in FY2023 (the continuation of FX headwinds from FY2022 despite the prior-year hedge gains); in a severe and sustained USD weakness scenario, the FY2024 core EPS of USD 7.73 could face a USD 0.50–0.80/share headwind by FY2026 if FX moves are sustained and hedges provide no protection beyond 12 months; management's "significant portion hedged" language obscures the horizon shortfall, and an investor modelling solely on hedge existence (rather than hedge horizon) would systematically overestimate protection

§6 — Quantified Sensitivity Analysis

J-front verdict: USD/CHF is the dominant single-pair sensitivity — a 10% CHF appreciation vs USD impacts pre-tax income by approximately USD 1.1–1.3bn (8–10% of core operating income); EUR/USD is secondary (~USD 500–600m per 10%); EM basket adds a further USD 300–500m tail risk at 10% depreciation — aggregate stressed sensitivity is material relative to consensus EPS estimates

Building the sensitivity framework

Revenue sensitivity per 10% currency move:
- USD: no translation impact (reporting currency); some transactional impact if USD weakens vs CHF (manufacturing cost increases) — see §4
- EUR -10% vs USD: ~26% revenue weight × USD 45.7bn × 10% = approximately USD 1.2bn revenue headwind; at ~30% operating margin = USD 360m operating income headwind; at effective tax rate ~14% and ~2.2bn shares = ~USD 0.14/share EPS
- JPY -10% vs USD: ~5% revenue weight × USD 45.7bn × 10% = approximately USD 230m revenue headwind; operating income impact ~USD 70m; EPS ~USD 0.03/share
- CHF +10% vs USD (cost-side): not a revenue effect; increases CHF-denominated costs in USD terms; at ~35–40% of COGS in CHF and COGS ~USD 8–9bn: CHF cost increase approximately USD 300–360m; additional opex/R&D CHF cost ~USD 200–250m; total cost impact approximately USD 500–600m; operating income impact approximately USD 500–600m (pure cost headwind); EPS approximately USD 0.20–0.25/share
- Management's disclosed sensitivity for CHF: 10% USD/CHF move = ~USD 1.1–1.3bn pre-tax impact (inclusive of all P&L lines) — this implies either Watson's COGS-only estimate understates total CHF exposure, or management's figure includes intercompany transaction flows; takes management's disclosed figure as authoritative
- EM basket -10% vs USD: ~13% revenue weight × USD 45.7bn × 10% = approximately USD 594m revenue headwind; operating income impact ~USD 180m; EPS ~USD 0.07/share; note EM revenues tend to be lower margin (generic-heavy in some markets), so the EBITDA sensitivity is proportionally lower
