§2 — Key Driver #1: AI & Cloud / Optical-IP Networking (Infinera-Led)

Nokia completed the Infinera acquisition on 28 February 2025 for $2.3bn, creating the leading pure-play optical networking portfolio at a time when AI infrastructure demand is driving unprecedented data centre interconnect spending.

  • Definition: Optical Networks and IP Routing (combined under Network Infrastructure segment) serving hyperscalers, cloud providers, submarine cable operators, and carriers building AI-capable backhaul and data centre interconnect. #I
  • Mechanism to EPS/SP: Optical/IP carries ~45%+ gross margins; incremental revenue at scale is highly EPS-accretive due to operating leverage; management raised FY-26 optical/IP combined growth guidance to 18-20% CAGR vs prior 10-12%, implying €700m+ incremental revenue at high margins. #A
  • Q1-26 delivery: Net sales from AI & Cloud customers grew 49% YoY; total Optical Networks net sales grew 20% YoY; AI & Cloud accounted for 8% of group sales; €1bn orders from AI & Cloud in the quarter alone. #I
  • Comparable operating profit: Group comparable operating profit increased 54% YoY in Q1-26 to ~€560m; Network Infrastructure is the primary driver, with operating margin expanding materially. #I
  • Infinera synergy path: Nokia targets >€200m net comparable operating profit synergies by 2027 with gradual ramp; >10% comparable EPS accretion in 2027; Q1-26 already showing Nokia ranking #1 in optical market share for first time. #I
  • Technology differentiation: Infinera's photonic integrated circuits combined with Nokia's DSP expertise creates a vertically integrated optical platform; competitors (Ciena, Lumentum, Coriant) lack this combined scale. #A
  • Hyperscaler capex context: Aggregate hyperscaler capex expectations jumped from $540bn to over $700bn between Nov-25 and early 2026; Nokia raised its addressable market CAGR estimate for AI & Cloud to 27% (2025-2028) from prior 16%. #I
  • Market share signal: Nokia reached #1 optical market share in Q4-25, displacing Ciena; this is a genuine structural shift enabled by Infinera scale plus AI demand acceleration. #A
  • Peer benchmark: Ciena (pure-play optical) trades at premium multiples; Nokia's optical business, if valued on a sum-of-parts basis, could alone justify current group enterprise value; this creates re-rating potential if Nokia explicitly separates optical/IP reporting. #A
  • New product cadence: Nokia launched coherent optical pluggable transceivers and transponder modules for campus, metro, and regional data centre networks; sampling mid-2027, GA later 2027; next product cycle already visible. #I
  • Bear case on D1: Hyperscaler capex announcements have already been well-telegraphed; if AI build cycle peaks in late 2026/early 2027, optical orders could normalise rapidly; Nokia's revenue recognition lags orders by 6-12 months, so downside would appear in 2027-28 financials. #J
  • ❌ Concentration risk: 8% of group sales from AI & Cloud in Q1-26; growing fast but still relatively small in absolute terms; market may be pricing future state, not current run-rate, creating earnings disappointment risk if trajectory slows. #A

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — AI infrastructure capex is multi-year, hyperscaler commitments are public and binding, and Nokia's market share gain to #1 in optical is confirmed; key assumption is that hyperscaler build programmes maintain 2026-27 pace without delay or concentration risk from a single large customer reducing orders.

§3 — Key Driver #2: Mobile Networks RAN Recovery + 5G-Advanced Cycle

Mobile Networks (formerly the largest Nokia segment by revenue) went through a severe cycle trough in 2023-24 driven by India Jio rollout completion, AT&T loss to Ericsson, and broad carrier capex digestion. Recovery is now underway but contested.

  • Definition: Radio Access Networks hardware, software, and services including 5G macro base stations, Massive MIMO radios, and baseband units; sold primarily to MNOs globally with Nokia holding ~16-18% global RAN share ex-China. #I
  • Trough context: Nokia's mobile business declined 25% YoY in Q2-24 driven primarily by India Jio rollout completion; full year 2024 Mobile Networks revenue fell materially following the AT&T loss (Ericsson $14bn deal, 2023). #I
  • Recovery signals: Nokia regained ~18,000 net base station sites on a net basis since start of 2024; Mobile Networks grew 6% in Q4-25 and was flat for full year 2025 vs a market that declined 2%; T-Mobile multi-year extension signed for baseband and radio technologies supporting 5G SA. #I
  • North America recovery: North American net sales grew double digits in Q4-24; T-Mobile is a long-standing Nokia RAN partner and the T-Mobile/Nokia AI-RAN Innovation Center provides sticky, multi-year commercial ties. #I
  • AT&T overhang: AT&T is structurally Ericsson territory for the foreseeable future; Nokia cannot recapture this volume in the 24-month horizon; this caps US market share recovery at partial. #J
  • 5G-Advanced timing: Nokia's AI-RAN is targeted for field trials in Q4-26 and commercial availability end-2027; this is a 2028+ revenue driver, not a 2026-27 catalyst; operators are still assessing 5G-Advanced business cases. #I
  • Carrier capex headwinds: Verizon has signalled significant capex cuts for 2026; Dell'Oro projects global RAN to remain "mostly stable" in 2026, not recover to prior peaks; this means Nokia's RAN growth depends on market share gains, not market expansion. #A
  • Market structure: Top 5 RAN vendors hold 96% of market (Huawei, Ericsson, Nokia, ZTE, Samsung); Nokia at 17.6% global share in 2024, down from 19.5% in 2023 despite Huawei displacement narrative — actual share loss in an adverse market. #I
  • India follow-on: Nokia won a multi-billion multi-year extension from Bharti Airtel in November 2024 covering 4G modernisation and 5G deployment across key Indian cities/states; AirScale portfolio, Massive MIMO radios, ReefShark SoC included. #I
  • Gross margin trajectory: Mobile Networks gross margin improved 490bps in Q3-24 to 45.7%; 2028 target is 48-50% Mobile Infrastructure gross margin. #A
  • Bear case on D2: RAN market remains structurally challenged; Nokia's share is declining even in a Huawei-restricted environment; AI-RAN is late-2027 commercially, too slow for 24M horizon; Verizon capex cuts would re-accelerate mobile revenue decline. #J
  • ❌ Structural share erosion: Nokia RAN share fell from 19.5% to 17.6% in 2024 despite Huawei bans — this is a negative data point that contradicts the Huawei displacement narrative and implies competitive pressure from Samsung and Ericsson that is underappreciated. #J

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — stabilisation is confirmed but meaningful recovery depends on market share gains in a flat-to-declining RAN market; the AT&T loss is structural and the 5G-Advanced upgrade cycle is a 2028+ story.

§4 — Key Driver #3: Nokia Technologies Licensing Stability + Automotive/IoT Expansion

Nokia Technologies is a €1.3bn+ annual run-rate business with high margins, low capital intensity, and a contracted revenue profile locked through 2030. It is Nokia's most distinctive and often least-understood asset.

  • Definition: SEP (standard-essential patent) licensing across wireless standards (2G-5G), encompassing smartphones, consumer electronics, automotive connectivity, IoT devices; revenue is royalty-based, largely independent of telecom equipment cycles. #I
  • Scale: Contracted recurring net sales run-rate of ~€1.3bn annually; >€800m of contracted recurring revenue secured annually through to 2030; Nokia has declared 7,000+ patent families as essential to 5G. #I
  • Smartphone renewal cycle completion: Nokia concluded its smartphone patent licence renewal cycle (2021-2024) with agreements with Apple, Samsung, Huawei, OPPO, vivo, Honor; this removes a major uncertainty overhang that caused Nokia to miss 2023 guidance targets. #J
  • Revenue quality: Licensing revenue carries near-100% gross margin; contributes disproportionately to group EBIT; the contracted-through-2030 structure provides multi-year earnings floor visibility that is rare in the telecom equipment sector. #A
  • Automotive expansion: Nokia has licensed WLAN technology to five major automakers including Mercedes; four Chinese OEMs licensed in the automotive sector; automotive is a structurally growing licensing pool as connected vehicles multiply. #I
  • IoT and consumer electronics expansion: 2025 saw new agreements with three consumer electronics companies; Amazon patent deal for video technologies in streaming services and devices (March 2025); IoT as a device category is in early innings of licensing. #I
  • Peer benchmark: Qualcomm derives ~$9bn annual licensing revenue from a comparable SEP portfolio; Nokia at €1.3bn is subscale by comparison but the trajectory (automotive expansion, IoT) mirrors Qualcomm's historical licensing growth arc. #A
  • Earnings anchor function: Technologies segment provides a €1.0-1.3bn gross margin contribution regardless of equipment cycle; this is the structural argument for Nokia's resilience floor vs pure-play equipment vendors (Ericsson has no equivalent in this form). #A
  • Litigation risk: SEP licensing is subject to ongoing challenge; hold-out by licensees (OPPO, vivo disputes in prior years) can delay or reduce revenue; EU SEP regulation discussions remain an overhang; legal costs are material. #J
  • Expansion limits: The total addressable licensing base is defined by deployed wireless devices; growth beyond smartphones requires expanding into new device categories each of which requires litigation or negotiation; this is inherently lumpy. #A
  • Bear case on D3: EU regulatory reform of SEP licensing (if enacted) could reduce royalty rates structurally; a major holdout licensee (e.g., a Chinese OEM post-geopolitical deterioration) could cause a repeat of the 2023 miss; IoT licensing build-out is slower than Nokia's public commentary suggests. #J

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — smartphone renewal cycle is complete, contracted revenues are locked through 2030, and automotive/IoT expansion is incremental upside not priced in; key assumption is that EU SEP regulatory environment does not materially impair royalty rates before 2028.

§5 — Key Driver #4: Cost Savings + Margin Expansion (2028 Strategic Targets)

Nokia launched a major cost reduction programme in 2023, targeting ~€1.2bn gross savings by 2026. Phase 2 targets an additional €400m gross recurring savings in 2026, with central costs falling €200m by 2028.

  • Definition: Structured headcount, real estate, procurement, and central function cost reduction programme initiated in late 2023; the programme is the primary lever for operating margin recovery. #I
  • 2024 delivery: The programme delivered tangible results: comparable gross margin improved 450bps in Q2-24 and 490bps in Q3-24 YoY; Q2-25 gross margin stabilised at 44.7% (flat YoY) following the initial improvement, suggesting base effects have normalised. #I
  • 2026 target: Nokia targets comparable operating profit of €2.0-2.5bn for 2026, with Q1-26 tracking "somewhat above the midpoint"; free cash flow conversion of 55-75% with 65-75% targeted longer-term. #I
  • 2028 financial targets: Comparable operating profit of €2.7-3.2bn by 2028; Network Infrastructure operating margin 13-17%; Mobile Infrastructure gross margin 48-50%; central operating expenses reduced from €350m to €150m run-rate (€200m reduction). #I
  • Upside path: The €2.7-3.2bn 2028 OP target implies roughly 35-60% growth from the 2025 base of €2.0bn; the midpoint (€3.0bn) would represent a step-change earnings base that could justify a sustained re-rating if cash conversion is strong. #A
  • Capital allocation: AGM Apr-26 authorised €0.14/share dividend (FY-25 basis) and 550m share repurchase valid through Oct-27; buyback adds EPS accretion layer to operating improvement story. #I
  • Structural cost base: Segment reorganisation into two primary segments (Network Infrastructure and Mobile Infrastructure) from Jan-26 is designed to reduce central cost duplication; CSS segment elimination into Mobile Infrastructure is specifically targeting margin dilution removal. #A
  • FCF quality: FY-25 FCF of €1.5bn (72% conversion) is strong relative to telecom equipment peers; higher FCF conversion supports the buyback and dividend programme without requiring external financing. #A
  • Peer benchmark: Ericsson targets 15-18% EBITA margins in steady state; Nokia's current group operating margin is ~10-11%; the 2028 target implies Ericsson-parity, which would be a genuine competitive re-rating argument if achieved. #A
  • Bear case on D4: Cost savings are structurally harder after the first wave; headcount reductions carry restructuring costs that clip near-term FCF; the 2028 targets require revenue growth (particularly in optical/IP) to materialise alongside cost savings — if revenue growth disappoints, the margin targets become unachievable through cost alone. #J
  • ❌ Cost savings vs reinvestment tension: Nokia is simultaneously targeting cost savings AND accelerating R&D investment for AI-RAN and optical next-gen products (€2bn annual R&D); this creates a tension — cost savings may be partially offset by required reinvestment. #A

Plausibility verdict: Medium-high plausibility — the cost programme has already delivered material gross margin improvement, FCF conversion is strong, and 2026 targets are well-defined; key assumption is that operating leverage from optical/IP revenue growth flows through the P&L without being reinvested fully into R&D and SG&A.

§6 — Key Driver #5: Huawei Displacement (EU + Global Restricted Markets)

The European Huawei rip-and-replace programme represents a potential multi-year revenue opportunity estimated at €2.0-2.5bn annually by Nokia's CEO. Delivery has been slower than anticipated.

  • Definition: Replacement of Huawei and ZTE equipment in European and other restricted-market networks (UK, Sweden, Australia, some Indian layers) with Nokia, Ericsson, or Samsung equipment; driven by national security legislation. #I
  • Market opportunity size: Nokia CEO Hotard estimates Huawei's directly competing European business at €2.0-2.5bn annually; Chinese vendors still hold 33-40% of European RAN market per Ericsson's CEO (implying a very large total displacement opportunity). #I
  • Germany — most advanced market: Core network Huawei/ZTE removal mandated by end of 2026; RAN management system replacement mandated by end of 2029; Nokia has replaced Huawei at 3,000+ Deutsche Telekom 5G sites; Deutsche Telekom explicitly cited Nokia as "as good as or better than Huawei" — a commercial validation. #I
  • Deutsche Telekom Open RAN programme: DT plans Europe's largest Open RAN deployment post-Nokia success; timeline slipped from end-2026 to 2027, but commitment is intact; Nokia is the primary beneficiary. #A
  • Vodafone Germany: Adopting a RAN management platform that removes Huawei's controller by 2029; retaining Huawei in access layer short-term; Nokia positioned for longer-term access layer displacement. #I
  • UK precedent: UK government mandated Huawei removal from core networks by end of 2023 (achieved); RAN deadline is 2027; Nokia and Ericsson are the primary beneficiaries of BT/EE and Vodafone UK displacement. #A
  • Counter-data point — share erosion: Nokia's global RAN market share fell from 19.5% (2023) to 17.6% (2024) despite the Huawei displacement narrative — actual share is declining even in the theoretically favourable environment; Samsung is a structural competitor gaining in Europe. #J
  • Pace risk: European carriers have been slow to act; German telcos received deadline extensions; political will has softened in some markets; actual displacement volumes are arriving later than Nokia guided. #J
  • Nokia vs Ericsson share of displacement: The displacement opportunity is shared with Ericsson and, increasingly, Samsung; Nokia is not the guaranteed winner of all Huawei sites being replaced; Open RAN architectures may also enable new vendors. #A
  • ⚡ RARE signal: Europe holding 33-40% Huawei RAN share is a genuinely extraordinary concentration given the security legislation; if displacement accelerates (e.g., under geopolitical escalation scenarios), the order pipeline could materially exceed current model assumptions. #J
  • Bear case on D5: Displacement timelines continue slipping; European carriers negotiate stranded asset compensation and delay execution; Samsung wins a disproportionate share of sites; Nokia's 2024 share erosion continues despite legislative tailwinds. #J

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — the legislative framework is real and Nokia has concrete wins (DT 3,000+ sites), but delivery pace is slow, share data is deteriorating, and the opportunity is contested.

§7 — Key Driver #6: India 5G / Airtel Multi-Billion Extension

India was Nokia's largest single mobile network headwind in 2024 (Jio completion drove -25% QoQ mobile revenue) and is now a medium-term recovery story through the Airtel multi-billion contract.

  • Definition: Revenue from India's two 5G operators (Airtel and Jio) for RAN equipment deployment; India is a high-volume, lower-margin market but represents significant absolute revenue due to the scale of network deployment. #I
  • Jio drag: Reliance Jio completed its first-wave 5G rollout in H1-24, removing a major revenue pillar for Nokia; Nokia's mobile business fell 25% in Q2-24 primarily due to this completion; no new significant Jio contract has been announced. #I
  • Airtel recovery: Nokia won a multi-billion multi-year extension from Bharti Airtel in November 2024 covering 4G modernisation and 5G deployment across key Indian cities/states; AirScale portfolio, Massive MIMO radios, ReefShark SoC, and MantaRay NMS are included; this restores a material India revenue stream from 2025 onwards. #I
  • India 5G subscriber penetration: India's 5G penetration reached ~42% across both operators by July 2025 (Jio ~210m 5G subscribers, Airtel ~153m); continued network densification and FWA expansion drive ongoing RAN capex. #I
  • Fixed Wireless Access : Airtel awarded Nokia additional contracts in Feb-25 for 5G FWA device expansion; FWA is a significant incremental use case in India that drives radio unit deployments beyond subscriber handsets. #I
  • Nokia bargaining position: Indian operators (Jio, Vodafone India, Airtel) historically drive hard on pricing; Nokia's India wins are noted as "bargain deals" by analysts — volume drivers for market share but margin-dilutive vs Nokia's European/North American contracts. #A
  • India market competitive dynamics: Nokia competes with Ericsson, Samsung, and Chinese vendors (in non-5G layers) in India; pricing pressure is intense; India revenue is important for volumes/overhead absorption but is not a premium-margin driver. #A
  • Jio Tier-2 expansion risk: If Jio re-enters a second-wave deployment for tier-2/3 city 5G densification, Nokia could benefit — but Jio uses a mix of Nokia, Ericsson, and Samsung and there is no confirmed Nokia contract for wave 2. #A
  • Bear case on D6: India remains a low-margin, high-uncertainty market; Jio's second wave has not materialised; if Airtel capex is constrained by spectrum payments or ARPU pressure, the multi-billion Airtel contract could be back-ended or phased more slowly than assumed. #J
  • ❌ Jio absence: The largest India operator by 5G subscriber count (Jio, 210m) has no confirmed Nokia contract for wave 2; Nokia's India recovery is Airtel-dependent, which creates single-customer concentration risk within India. #A

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — Airtel contract is confirmed and multi-year, providing baseline India revenue recovery; but Jio absence and Indian operator pricing discipline cap the margin impact.

§8 — Driver Synthesis + Sceptical Synthesis

Nokia's six identified drivers are not independent — they share overlapping assumptions, carry correlation risk, and their combined plausibility depends on whether the company has successfully pivoted from telecom-equipment-cyclical to infrastructure-for-AI.

  • Two-speed structure is the central analytical tension: D1 (optical/AI) is accelerating while D2 (RAN recovery) is stalled; D3 (licensing) is stable; D4 (cost savings) is additive; D5 and D6 are incremental optionality. The bull case requires D1 to keep compounding AND D2 to recover; the bear case only needs D1 to peak-roll. #J
  • Correlation risk — hyperscaler capex is the master variable: D1 (optical/AI demand), D4 (revenue leverage enabling margin expansion), and market multiple (re-rating from 8x to 26x EBITDA) are all simultaneously dependent on hyperscaler capex holding. A single coordinated hyperscaler capex reduction announcement would hit all three simultaneously. #J
