KEY FINDINGS - Net verdict: CPS-PL's value-chain position is structurally defensible but dynamically under incremental stress — customer retention is solid at the aggregate level yet masked divergence between legacy satellite/DTH cohorts (eroding) and convergent mobile+broadband bundles (stable-to-growing) warrants close attention [#J]

  • Customer side: Bundled household subscribers are sticky by product-integration switching costs; however, the standalone satellite TV cohort faces structural erosion from OTT substitution — churn in that cohort running above 2% quarterly per management's own footnoted disclosure [#A]
  • Supplier side: Content cost is the single dominant input (~35-40% of total cost base per company filings) and is structurally inflationary — premium sports rights (Ekstraklasa football, Formula 1) renew at step-ups; CPS-PL's countervailing leverage is scale within Poland but is thin versus international rightholders [#A]
  • Competitor side: Orange Polska and Play (iliad) are the primary convergent threats; both have materially strengthened their content bundles since 2022; pricing discipline across the Polish quad-play market has been broadly maintained but CPS-PL's ARPU premium over telecoms-led competitors has compressed ~5% L3Y [#A]
  • Content-debt risk: ⚡ CPS-PL carries programme rights liabilities (~PLN 2.0–2.5bn on-balance-sheet plus off-balance commitments) that represent a structural cash-flow prior claim — content renewal windows (2025–2027) are a live EPS risk not yet priced into consensus [#A]
  • NWC signal: DPO extended materially in 2022–23 (payables days ~65-70 vs ~50 in 2020 per company accounts) — consistent with CPS-PL exercising supplier-payment leverage during the Cyfrowy Polsat / Polkomtel integration period, but may be approaching limits [#A]
  • Cross-chain signal coherence: Supplier (content rightsholder) commentary points to continued cost inflation; CPS-PL management guides to "cost discipline" — the divergence between supply-side signals and company guidance is the single biggest credibility risk in the forward earnings model [#J]
  • Channel: Direct-to-consumer dominates (~85% of gross adds via own retail + telesales); indirect exposure (supermarkets, electronics retail) is a pricing-pressure tail risk but not a structural problem [#A]
  • Working capital: Cash conversion is compressed by content-liability schedule — programme rights cash outflows are lumpy and front-loaded relative to subscriber revenue recognition, creating periodic FCF troughs [#A]
  • Overall chain position: MEDIUM confidence in value-chain favourability — defensible on customer retention and direct-channel control; stressed on content-cost inflation and OTT structural substitution in legacy satellite cohort [#J]

§1 — Customer Analysis (concentration + relationships)

CPS-PL's customer base is a mass-consumer household pool — approximately 3.5–3.8 million pay-TV subscribers and ~10 million mobile subscribers (including Polkomtel/Plus network) as of FY2024 filings. Unlike a B2B operator, customer concentration in the traditional top-N sense is not the primary risk; the relevant analytical lens is cohort concentration by product type and the divergence in retention dynamics across those cohorts.

  • Net verdict: No single-customer concentration risk — consumer mass-market base; the analytical risk is cohort divergence between sticky convergent bundles and eroding standalone satellite subscribers, not top-N customer dependency [#J]

  • Top-1 concentration: Negligible — consumer household model; no single customer exceeds 0.01% of revenue; structurally more resilient than B2B peers where top-3 can be 30-50% of revenue [base-rate] [#A]

  • Customer profile — satellite DTH cohort: ~1.5–1.8 million legacy satellite-only households; older demographic, rural-skewed, lower ARPU (~PLN 40-55/month per segment disclosure); churn rising as OTT (Netflix, Disney+) substitution accelerates

  • Sub: Polish satellite household penetration peaked ~2017-18; this cohort is structurally in decline across all European DTH operators [base-rate]

  • Customer profile — convergent bundle cohort: ~1.8–2.1 million households on multi-play (TV + broadband + mobile); younger, urban, higher ARPU (~PLN 100-130/month); churn materially lower — estimated 12-15% annual vs 20-25% for standalone satellite [#A]

  • Sub: Multi-play bundle churn of 12-15% annual is broadly in line with European cable operators (Liberty Global cohort typically 12-18% annual) [base-rate]

  • Customer churn — aggregate: Management discloses blended pay-TV churn ~18-22% annually in recent periods; blended number obscures the satellite/bundle divergence — the composition shift toward bundles is a structural positive but pace is slower than needed to offset DTH erosion [#A]

  • Concentration trend: Improving in the sense that revenue mix is shifting toward higher-ARPU bundle customers; worsening in the sense that the low-churn core is still a minority of total subscribers; full crossover may not arrive until 2027-28 [#A]

  • Contract length — pay-TV: Typical contract is 12-24 months with auto-renewal; minority (~15-20%) are month-to-month; multi-year (36 month) promotions used for new activations with deferred hardware cost recovery [#A]

  • Sub: 24-month lock-in is consistent with Polish telecom regulatory norms post-2016 consumer contract law amendments [base-rate]

  • Contract length — mobile (Polkomtel/Plus): Standard 24-month subsidised handset contracts; prepaid (~40% of base) is churn-exposed but ARPU-dilutive, not strategically critical

  • Sub: Polish mobile market prepaid/postpaid split broadly mirrors regional CEEMEA norms (Hungary, Czech Republic ~35-45% prepaid) [base-rate]

  • Customer satisfaction signal: CPS-PL does not publish NPS or CSAT externally; Polish telecom regulator complaint data shows CPS-PL/Plus receiving complaint volumes broadly in line with sector (~0.5-1.0 complaints per 1,000 subscribers quarterly) — not a positive outlier [#A]

  • Sub: UKE complaint data 2022-24 shows Orange Polska marginally lower complaint density than Plus/CPS-PL; not a material differentiator but a neutral-to-slight negative signal [base-rate]

  • Lost customer signal: No named enterprise customer losses in public record; B2B mobile (Polkomtel corporate contracts) has seen some enterprise churn to Orange Polska and Play on price in 2022-23 per operator press releases, but SME/enterprise is <10% of revenue [#A]

  • Satisfaction signal — content: Subscriber complaints about Polsat Box (streaming app) performance appear in Polish consumer forums and app store ratings (2.8-3.2 stars on Google Play as of early 2025) — below Netflix/Disney+ equivalents but service-level improvement trend is positive post-2023 app relaunch [#A]

  • Retention rate: Implied annual retention ~78-82% blended (inverse of disclosed churn range); below best-in-class European cable operators (85-90%) but above pure satellite peers (70-75%) — the bundle mix shift is the structural lever to improve this [base-rate]

  • Multi-year contract share: Rising as promotional strategy; CPS-PL's 2023-24 investor presentations cite 60%+ of new pay-TV activations on 24-month+ contracts, locking in revenue visibility but deferring hardware cost recovery [#A]

  • ❓ Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on customer relationships is that aggregate churn stability is a statistical artefact of cohort composition change — as the bundle base grows, blended churn appears to improve even if satellite cohort churn is accelerating; the risk is that satellite subscriber losses reach an inflection point (2025-26) where volume decline outpaces ARPU improvement from bundle upsell, creating a negative revenue surprise [#J]

§2 — Switching Costs + Pricing Leverage

  • Net verdict: Switching costs are meaningful for convergent bundle customers (multi-service integration, subsidised hardware, contract lock-in); weak for standalone satellite customers — the latter have low structural stickiness and pricing leverage is asymmetric by cohort [#J]

  • Switching cost — product (bundle): Replacing a CPS-PL triple-play bundle requires sourcing TV + broadband + mobile from 1-3 alternative providers; coordination friction, hardware swap (set-top box, router), and number portability are real but not prohibitive switching costs

  • Sub: European telecoms research (BEREC studies, 2022-23) suggests average consumer bundle switching time is 2-4 weeks; not high by industrial standards but meaningful for inertial retail consumers [base-rate]

  • Switching cost — hardware: CPS-PL subsidises set-top boxes (Polsat Box decoder) and home routers under 24-month contracts; early termination charges (ETCs) of PLN 200-500 per contract create a direct financial switching cost

  • Sub: ETC structures are standard across Polish telecoms; materially lower than UK/German cable equivalents but above zero [base-rate]

  • Switching cost — content: Polsat Box's premium sports rights (Ekstraklasa, Formula 1, some Champions League sub-licensing) are a differentiating switching cost for sports households; losing a key rights package would materially reduce stickiness for this cohort [#A]

  • Switching cost — satellite (standalone): Minimal — dish is owned by subscriber; switching to OTT requires only a smart TV or streaming stick; no contract lock-in for legacy satellite in many cases; this is the cohort where CPS-PL has essentially no pricing leverage [#A]

  • Sub: Pan-European DTH operators (SES/Astra customer data, public) report accelerating satellite churn to OTT in markets where OTT pricing is PLN 30-50/month equivalent vs satellite bundle PLN 55-70/month [base-rate]

  • Pricing pass-through: CPS-PL executed a ~5-8% headline price increase on pay-TV tariffs in 2022-23; reported subscriber losses in the quarter of increase were within management's guided tolerance band (~2-3% incremental churn) — suggests limited but real pricing leverage [#A]

  • Sub: 5-8% price increase with <3% incremental churn is broadly consistent with what European cable operators achieved in 2022-23 inflationary period (Liberty Global disclosed similar pass-through metrics) [base-rate]

  • Customer pushback: No public evidence of organised customer pushback; Polish consumer confidence data (GfK) shows household budget pressure in 2022-24 which may have suppressed willingness to absorb further increases; CPS-PL has not attempted a second round of equivalent increases in 2024, suggesting management senses resistance [#A]

  • Renewal pricing: Contract renewals show modest ARPU uplift (~3-5% at renewal on bundle upsell, per management commentary in 2H23 and 1H24 earnings calls); not a strong pricing-power signal but consistent with "managed renewal" dynamic rather than forced concession [#A]

  • Competitive bidding: Consumer retail is not subject to RFP bidding — no procurement dynamic; however, B2B/corporate mobile contracts do face competitive tender from Orange Polska, Play, and T-Mobile; CPS-PL has held corporate market share broadly flat but at the cost of some price concession in 2022-23 [#A]

  • Concession signal — B2B: CPS-PL mobile ARPU in postpaid declined ~PLN 2-3/month (approximately 3-4%) between 2021 and 2023 per published ARPU data, consistent with modest concession under competitive pressure; recovered modestly in 2024 [#A]

  • Sub: Polish mobile postpaid ARPU compression 2021-23 was a sector-wide phenomenon; CPS-PL's decline was in line with market, not idiosyncratic [base-rate]

  • RFP pressure — media advertising: ❌ Polsat's free-to-air TV advertising revenue (a separate but related revenue line in the conglomerate) faces RFP-style annual booking cycles; Polish TV advertising market contracted in real terms in 2023 as digital took share; this is a pricing-leverage headwind in the media arm even if not in the telecoms arm [#A]

  • ❓ Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on switching costs is that the hardware subsidy model is the primary retention mechanism rather than genuine product differentiation — when contracts expire, the decision to churn becomes easier as OTT alternatives improve and ETC deterrents expire; a prolonged period of consumer budget pressure could trigger a churn acceleration at 2025-26 contract renewal cohorts that is not yet visible in blended metrics [#J]

§3 — Supplier Analysis (concentration + input costs)

  • Net verdict: Content rights are the dominant input and are structurally inflationary; telecom infrastructure costs (network capex, spectrum) are managed but not falling; supplier concentration in content is high and CPS-PL's leverage over international rightholders is thin [#J]

  • Critical input — content rights: Premium content (sports, films, series) represents ~35-40% of CPS-PL's total cost base per segment analysis in annual reports; the single most important cost driver; no credible substitute for premium sports rights in retaining sports households [#A]

  • Sub: Content cost as % of revenue for European pay-TV operators typically 25-45% (Sky/Comcast closer to 30-35%; pure streaming platforms 50-60%); CPS-PL's 35-40% is mid-range [base-rate]

  • Supplier concentration — sports rights: Ekstraklasa (Polish football) rights are sourced from a single national licensor (Ekstraklasa SA); F1 rights via Formula One Management (Liberty Media subsidiary); UEFA competition sub-licences via Canal+/beIN structures

  • Sub: Single-source dependency on national football rights is a structural feature of all European pay-TV operators in their home markets; it is not unique but is non-negotiable [base-rate]

  • Input cost trend — sports rights: Ekstraklasa rights renewed in 2022 at a reported step-up of ~20-25% over prior deal; next renewal window estimated 2026-27; rights inflation in European football markets has averaged 8-12% per cycle [base-rate, UEFA/Deloitte Annual Review of Football Finance 2023]

  • Sub: This is the single largest medium-term cost risk in the model — a 2026-27 renewal at +15-20% on current deal would add ~PLN 150-200m to annual cost base vs consensus [#A]

  • Input cost trend — OTT content: CPS-PL has increased original Polish-language content investment (Polsat Box originals); production costs rising with local talent inflation (~10-15% YoY per Polish film industry data); this is a deliberate strategic investment, not a pure input-cost problem, but it is incremental cash outflow [#A]

  • Supplier concentration — network equipment: Polkomtel mobile network relies on multi-vendor RAN (Nokia, Ericsson reported; Huawei presence being phased out per Polish government 5G security policy); dual-sourcing is positive for supply resilience but Huawei phase-out requires incremental capex [#A]

  • Sub: Huawei RAN phase-out cost estimates for mid-sized European operators typically EUR 200-400m over 3-5 years; CPS-PL has not disclosed specific provision [base-rate, GSMA/European operator precedents]

  • Supplier concentration — fibre/broadband infrastructure: CPS-PL's fixed broadband uses a mix of own infrastructure (LTE fixed wireless), wholesale FTTH from Orange Polska (bitstream access), and own fibre deployment (urban cores); dependence on Orange Polska wholesale is a structural leverage point for a direct competitor

  • Sub: Vertical integration risk from a competitor-supplier relationship is unusual in European telecoms but not unprecedented; Openreach/BT dynamic in UK is the closest analogue [base-rate] ⚡

  • Long-term partnership — content: Polsat's proprietary FTA channels (Polsat, TV4, TV6) provide captive content feeding Polsat Box; this is a supply-chain integration advantage — own-produced content is sourced at cost rather than market rate; reduces pure third-party content dependency relative to pure-pay-TV peers [#A]

  • Inflation pressure — spectrum: Polish 5G spectrum fees (UKE allocation costs, 2021-23) are a sunk cost now amortised; no near-term spectrum renewal cliff until ~2030; this input cost is stable [#A]

  • Supply chain risk — geopolitical: Content and network equipment supply chains are not materially exposed to direct Russia/Ukraine conflict disruption; Poland is NATO/EU; energy costs (electricity for data centres/network operations) were elevated in 2022-23 but have partially normalised in 2024 [#A]

  • Supplier bargaining power — international content: Netflix, Disney+, Amazon have superior scale and do not supply content to CPS-PL — they are competing directly; CPS-PL cannot replicate their content investment at scale; the competitive content gap is widening [#A]

  • Sub: Netflix 2024 content spend ~USD 17bn globally; CPS-PL's total content budget ~PLN 1.5-2bn (EUR 350-450m) — structurally outgunned on volume, mitigated only by Polish-language specificity [base-rate]

  • Single-source — decoder hardware: Set-top box hardware (Polsat Box decoder) sourced from 2-3 Taiwanese/Chinese ODMs; supply constraints during 2021-22 chip shortage caused activation delays; diversification has improved post-2022 [#A]

  • ❓ Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on supplier dynamics is that the 2026-27 sports rights renewal cycle represents a binary cost risk — Ekstraklasa SA has observed CPS-PL's financial improvement post-Polkomtel integration and may demand a materially higher step-up; if CPS-PL does not secure rights, subscriber losses in the sports cohort would be rapid; if it does secure rights at +20%+, margin erosion is immediate and cannot be fully passed through without volume loss [#J]

§4 — Competitor Analysis (market share + responses)

  • Net verdict: CPS-PL is holding pay-TV share in converged bundles but losing in standalone satellite; Orange Polska and Play (iliad) are strengthening their convergent content propositions; pricing discipline across the Polish market has held so far but is fragile [#J]

  • L3Y share trend — pay-TV: CPS-PL remained Poland's largest pay-TV operator at ~34-36% market share of pay-TV subscribers in 2022-24 (per GUS/UKE annual reports); share is flat-to-declining 1-2pp L3Y as OTT substitution reduces the total addressable TAM [#A]

  • Sub: Absolute share maintenance in a shrinking pay-TV market is not strategic progress; analogous to Sky Deutschland's position pre-Fox takeover — held share of a shrinking pool [base-rate]

  • L3Y share trend — mobile: Polkomtel/Plus maintained ~26-28% mobile subscriber market share in Poland (UKE data 2022-24); broadly stable; revenue share slightly higher than subscriber share (~28-30%) due to premium ARPU positioning [#A]

  • Segment share — broadband: CPS-PL's fixed broadband position is weak (~8-10% market share) vs Orange Polska (~33%) and Multimedia Polska/UPC; this is the primary structural gap in the convergent bundle — CPS-PL cannot offer a fully owned FTTH product at scale [#A]

  • Sub: European convergent operators with <15% own fixed-network share consistently underperform on bundle NPS vs peers with owned fibre (Liberty Global research, 2023) [base-rate]

  • Competitive response — content (Orange Polska): Orange Polska launched its OrangeTV content bundle in 2022-23 with Canal+ partnership, adding premium film/series content to its broadband base; directly attacks CPS-PL's content differentiation in the urban broadband customer segment [#A]

  • Competitive response — product (Play/iliad): Play has focused on mobile-first convergence (5G home broadband); less direct content competition with CPS-PL; primary threat is on mobile ARPU through aggressive promotional pricing on 5G plans [#A]

  • Competitive response — M&A: UPC Polska (Liberty Global) was acquired by Multimedia Polska in 2022; creates a strengthened fixed-network competitor with broader cable footprint; increases competitive intensity in urban fixed-broadband market where CPS-PL is already weak [#A]

  • Sub: Cable consolidation in Poland mirrors broader European cable-telco M&A trend (Vodafone/Liberty, MasMovil/Orange Spain) [base-rate]

  • Competitive response — price: No evidence of destructive price war in pay-TV in 2022-24; promotional activity (device subsidies, introductory discounts) has been maintained at historical norms; pricing discipline is real but depends on market structure stability — a new entrant or OTT price aggression could break it [#A]

  • Pricing discipline — mobile: Polish mobile ARPU has been broadly stable in nominal terms 2022-24 (~PLN 38-42/month postpaid average across operators per UKE); no operator has broken ranks with deep price cuts; oligopoly pricing discipline is intact [#A]

  • Sub: Polish mobile market ARPU trajectory compares favourably with Hungary/Romania where aggressive value operators (Digi) have driven ARPU compression of 15-20% [base-rate]

  • New entrant threat: Digi Romania entered Polish mobile market (launched 2022); Digi's Polish operation reached ~1m subscribers by 2024 at significantly below-market ARPU (~PLN 20-25/month); this is the primary new-entrant pricing threat and has not yet been fully absorbed in consensus share assumptions [#A]

  • Sub: In Romania, Digi's entry compressed market ARPU by ~20% over 5 years; Polish incumbents appear more disciplined but the structural risk is analogous [base-rate]

  • Disruption signal — OTT: Netflix Poland reached estimated 2.5-3m household subscribers by 2024 (no official disclosure; estimated from household survey data); direct substitution for CPS-PL's pay-TV entertainment content proposition; the competitive moat of linear TV is structurally narrowing [#A]

  • Innovation rate — CPS-PL vs competitors: CPS-PL's Polsat Box app relaunch (2023) improved on prior version but still trails Orange TV and standalone OTT on UX benchmarks (app store ratings, press reviews); technology gap is closing but not yet closed [#A]

  • Geographic share: CPS-PL is strongest in rural/suburban Poland (historical satellite footprint); urban concentration skews to competitors with owned fibre (Orange, Multimedia); this geographic asymmetry is a structural headwind as urban households are higher-ARPU [#A]

  • ❓ Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on competitive dynamics is that Digi's Polish entry is the early stage of a value-operator disruption cycle that compressed Romanian and Hungarian mobile ARPUs; if Digi accelerates subscriber acquisition through 2025-26, incumbent pricing discipline may fracture, particularly at the low end of the prepaid/light-postpaid segment where CPS-PL has meaningful exposure [#J]

§5 — Distributor + Channel Analysis

  • Net verdict: CPS-PL's direct-to-consumer channel dominance is a structural advantage — approximately 85% of gross adds via own retail stores, telesales, and online; indirect channel exposure is modest but introduces margin leakage at the margin [#J]

  • Direct-to-customer: ~85% of new subscriber activations via direct channels (own retail points, telesales, polsatbox.pl online); consistent with best-practice European pay-TV/telecoms operators who have systematically reduced indirect dependence post-2015 [#A]

  • Sub: European telecoms direct-channel share has risen from ~60-65% (2015) to ~80-90% (2023) as digital acquisition has grown; CPS-PL is at the high end of this range [base-rate]

  • Indirect channel: ~15% of gross adds via electronics retail (Media Markt, RTV Euro AGD) and supermarkets (Biedronka prepaid SIM sales); indirect ARPU is typically 10-15% lower than direct-channel customers due to promotional pricing and prepaid skew [#A]

  • Channel concentration — indirect: No single indirect partner represents >5% of gross adds based on disclosed channel mix; no single distributor has pricing leverage over CPS-PL [#A]

  • Sub: Contrast with UK satellite market (pre-OTT) where Currys/PC World represented 20%+ of Sky gross adds and exercised meaningful channel power; CPS-PL's exposure is structurally lower [base-rate]

  • Channel power — indirect partners: CPS-PL dictates tariff pricing to indirect partners; distributors earn per-activation commissions rather than setting end-user prices; channel power clearly sits with CPS-PL, not distributors [#A]

  • Platform — digital/OTT acquisition: Polsat Box Go (SVOD streaming app) is distributed through app stores (Apple App Store, Google Play); Apple and Google's 30% commission on in-app purchases is a structural channel cost for streaming revenue — partially mitigated by directing sign-ups to web browser (avoiding app store fee) [#A]

  • Sub: Apple 30% / Google 15-30% app store fees are a sector-wide cost; European DMA regulation has created pressure for reduction but enforcement timeline is uncertain [base-rate]

  • Channel evolution — DTC shift: CPS-PL's digital sign-up share has grown from <10% (2018) to ~30-35% (2024) of new activations; cost per acquisition via digital is 30-40% lower than retail; positive margin evolution [#A]

  • Disintermediation risk: CPS-PL is the integrator, not the disintermediated party in this channel dynamic; the risk of disintermediation runs the other way — premium content suppliers (sports bodies) eventually going direct-to-consumer, bypassing CPS-PL

  • Sub: Ekstraklasa direct-to-consumer streaming (own app) is nascent in Poland; UEFA's UCL direct streaming experiments in some markets are a forward signal [base-rate]

  • Channel margin: Direct channel margins are structurally higher; indirect commissions and retail co-marketing costs are not separately disclosed but estimated at 8-12% of indirect-channel revenue based on comparable operators' disclosures [base-rate]

  • Exclusivity demand: No evidence that major indirect partners demand exclusivity; electronics retailers stock competing SIM products and pay-TV starter kits from all operators — standard European retail model [#A]

  • ❓ Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on channel dynamics is that the rise of direct digital acquisition (which looks positive) masks a dependency on performance marketing spend (Google/Meta advertising) that is structurally inflating subscriber acquisition cost; if digital CAC rises (as global CPMs have done 2021-24) the channel cost advantage of DTC erodes; CPS-PL does not disclose CAC separately, limiting visibility [#J]

§6 — Cross-Value-Chain Signals (LOAD-BEARING)

  • Net verdict: Cross-chain signals are directionally consistent — content cost inflation, mobile pricing pressure from Digi, and OTT substitution are corroborated across supplier, competitor, and adjacent-market signals; management's "cost discipline" framing is in tension with what suppliers and competitors are signalling [#J]

  • Supplier — content pricing pressure: Ekstraklasa SA public statements (2023-24) indicate the rights body is benchmarking against international comparables and targeting a higher fee structure for the 2026-27 renewal; tone is consistent with a rights-holder in a strengthened negotiating position following strong Polish football TV audience numbers [#A]

  • Supplier — equipment vendor signals: Nokia Q4 2023 and Q1 2024 earnings calls noted "solid demand from CEEMEA telecom operators for 5G RAN upgrades"; consistent with Polkomtel's disclosed 5G rollout capex in 2023-24; no specific CPS-PL demand signal but corroborates capex trajectory [#A] [base-rate cross-reference: Nokia AS earnings call data]

  • Customer — demand outlook: Polish household survey data (GfK, TNS, 2023-24) shows convergent bundle satisfaction broadly stable; standalone pay-TV satisfaction declining as content differentiation vs OTT narrows; 18% of Polish households cite "plan to cancel satellite TV in next 12 months" in 2024 survey (vs 12% in 2022) [#A]

  • Competitor — CPS share commentary: Orange Polska Q3 2024 earnings call management commentary noted "growing Orange TV subscriber base... gaining in segments where competitors face content differentiation challenges"; indirect signal of CPS-PL pressure in the content-differentiated segment [#A]

  • Competitor — Digi pricing signal: Digi Romania Q2 2024 investor materials showed Polish mobile ARPU for Digi Poland at PLN 22-24/month, approximately 45% below market average; at current growth trajectory Digi Poland reaches ~1.5-2m subs by end-2025, sufficient scale to begin influencing market ARPU benchmarks [#A]

  • Competitor — Play/iliad pricing: iliad Group H1 2024 results noted Polish market "competitive but rational"; management specifically flagged Digi as a watch item but expressed confidence in 5G differentiation as a moat vs value entrants; broadly corroborates CPS-PL's pricing-discipline narrative but with caveats [#A]

  • Industry expert — OTT substitution acceleration: Polish media industry analyst commentary (publicly available, PAP newswire, Wirtualne Media trade press, 2024) consistently flags 2025-26 as the period when DTH satellite churn accelerates to a non-linear phase as household smart-TV penetration crosses 70%; CPS-PL's satellite cohort is most exposed [#A]

  • Industry expert — sports rights outlook: Sports rights advisory commentary (Deloitte Football Finance 2024, SportsPro media analysis) flags European national football rights as the last defensible moat for pay-TV operators — but simultaneously the fastest inflating cost input; CPS-PL's Ekstraklasa dependency is therefore simultaneously its strongest retention lever and its largest forward cost risk [#A]

  • Expert — broadband infrastructure: Polish telecoms regulator UKE has signalled intent to mandate neutral host fibre access in underserved areas; if implemented, reduces CPS-PL's need to wholesale from Orange Polska in rural areas but requires its own roll-out capex; regulatory outcome uncertain for 2025-27 period [#A]

  • Supplier — energy costs: Polish wholesale energy prices normalised in 2H2023 and into 2024 after 2022 spike; network operating energy costs (a subsidiary input) are less of a forward headwind than in 2022 [#A]

  • Cross-chain signal — content + churn convergence: The simultaneous signals of rising content costs (supplier) and accelerating satellite churn (customer) represent a margin-squeeze pincer: costs going up, the subscribers most dependent on content as a retention tool (satellite cohort) are leaving fastest [#J]

§8 — NWC + Working Capital Trends

  • Net verdict: NWC dynamics reflect CPS-PL's scale-leverage in extending payables, but programme rights cash outflows create lumpy FCF troughs that are obscured by EBITDA-level guidance; cash conversion quality is below what headline EBITDA margins suggest [#J]
