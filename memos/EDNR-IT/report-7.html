  • Management commentary: Post-Eaton, management has emphasised cooperation with CPUC and legislative engagement; there is no public acknowledgement of compact erosion risk from management [#A]

  • Track record: Strong historically — California has maintained constructive utility regulation for 30+ years; even post-PG&E bankruptcy, CPUC restructured (not abandoned) the regulatory compact; inverse condemnation has been a recurring issue but not resolved adversely at the structural level [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Management optimism on regulatory compact durability may be real or performative; post-Eaton fire political dynamics in California are genuinely more adversarial toward utilities than at any point in the past decade [#J]

  • SS consensus: Public commentary suggests sell-side broadly models regulatory compact as intact; few analysts are pricing material compact erosion as a base case; this may represent a systemic optimism bias [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: ⚡ The regulatory compact risk may be systematically underweighted because sell-side analysts model it as binary-intact when it is actually a gradual erosion variable — California's political economy in 2025-2026 is materially different from 2015-2019, and this may not be adequately captured in forward estimates [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility for compact durability — California's electrification dependency preserves the utility model in aggregate, but the Eaton fire aftermath has introduced genuine regulatory adversarialism risk not present in prior cycles; key assumption is CPUC maintains constructive cost recovery stance [#J]
