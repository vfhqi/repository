§4 — Accounting + Governance Red Flags

J-front verdict: Accounting quality is mixed-to-concerning — non-GAAP adjustments are material, pension obligations are structurally underweighted in bears' analyses, and the governance history from the bribery episode is a persistent reputational liability [#J]

  • Non-GAAP gap — EBITDA adjustments: Telia reports "Adjusted EBITDA" excluding restructuring charges that have been incurred in every reporting year since 2018; restructuring charges ranging SEK 1-3bn annually are excluded from the adjusted metric used for dividend coverage and leverage ratios [#A]
  • Peer anchor: European telecom peers (BT, Vodafone, Orange) similarly adjust for restructuring, but Telia's restructuring charges as % of reported EBITDA (approximately 5-8% annually) are at the higher end of the peer range [#A]
  • ❌ Recurring restructuring: if restructuring is genuinely recurring (8 consecutive years), it should be treated as an operating cost; the adjusted EBITDA overstates normalised earnings by an estimated SEK 1-3bn annually [#A]
  • Capitalisation — aggressive: Telia capitalises subscriber acquisition costs and network software under IFRS 15/16; bears argue the capitalisation policies allow earnings smoothing [#A]
  • IFRS 16 lease obligations: right-of-use assets and lease liabilities added approximately SEK 20-25bn to the balance sheet on IFRS 16 adoption; this is disclosed but not always included in bears' leverage calculations [#A]
  • Pension obligations — underappreciated liability: defined benefit pension obligations in Sweden and Finland (legacy) total approximately SEK 8-12bn net liability; at current discount rates, this is understated relative to a rising-rate reversal scenario [#A]
  • SocGen flag: SocGen's adjusted net debt including pensions and leases suggests leverage of 2.5-3.0x vs reported ~2.0x (See §3 SocGen comment) [#A]
  • Related-party — historical CIS entities: residual obligations and legal proceedings from CIS market exits (Uzbekistan, Tajikistan, Kazakhstan) include contingent liabilities not fully resolved; as of 2024-2025 filings, certain tax and regulatory claims in exited markets remain open [#A]
  • Quantification difficulty: exact contingent liability from CIS exits is not fully determinable from public filings; bears argue this is a hidden option against equity value [#I]
  • Auditor — PwC continuity: PwC has audited Telia for multiple consecutive years; no recent auditor change flagged; no material weakness disclosures in recent filings [#A]
  • Bear nuance: long-tenure auditors in Scandinavian governance tradition are common and not inherently a red flag; PwC's role in the bribery period has been subject to some Nordic press scrutiny without formal findings [#A]
  • Revenue recognition — roaming and wholesale: wholesale and roaming revenue recognition under multi-year contracts carries some timing flexibility; no specific allegation of manipulation found, but bears note that contract-modification revenue is a common telecom smoothing vector [#A]
  • Board independence — improved post-bribery: Board composition has been substantially refreshed since 2017-2019; majority state ownership (Swedish government ~37%) means board composition reflects state priorities, which can conflict with shareholder value optimisation [#A]
  • State ownership bear angle: the Swedish state as controlling shareholder prioritises employment stability, network coverage obligations, and political optics over pure capital efficiency — this is a structural governance headwind for returns [#J]
  • Management comp structure — conservative: CEO compensation is modest by European telecom standards; performance linkage is partly tied to ESG metrics and employee satisfaction rather than pure TSR/FCF [#A]
  • Bear angle: compensation not sufficiently equity-linked may reduce urgency on capital allocation decisions [#I]
  • NWC liability — receivables and payables: working capital has been broadly stable; no pattern of aggressive payables stretching or receivables acceleration identified in public filings [#A]
  • Legal proceedings — ongoing: beyond CIS, Telia has periodic consumer regulatory disputes in Sweden and Lithuania; no material pending lawsuit significantly above what is provisioned [#A]
  • Dividend — declared vs FCF: the dividend (approximately SEK 2.0-2.2 per share, ~6-7% yield) requires approximately SEK 4-5bn of FCF annually; reported FCF after leases is tight at SEK 4-6bn in recent years, implying a coverage ratio of approximately 1.0-1.2x — thin by European telecom peer standards [#A]
  • Peer anchor: European telecom sector average FCF dividend coverage approximately 1.3-1.6x; Telia's coverage is at the lower quartile [#A]

Bull rebuttal (sceptical bullet): The accounting at Telia is IFRS-compliant, audited, and disclosed; the restructuring charges are flagged clearly; the pension liabilities are in the notes; the non-GAAP adjustments are industry-standard; bears have been calling for a dividend cut since 2019 and been wrong — the company has managed FCF to cover the dividend consistently [#J]

§6 — Counter-Arguments to Bull Case

J-front verdict: The strongest bear rebuttal to the Telia bull case is that the "turnaround / simplification" thesis has been running for 5+ years without producing growth; the yield thesis is precarious given thin FCF coverage [#J]

  • Bull thesis #1 — dividend yield of 6-7% is attractive and sustainable: the payout supports a total-return case for patient investors [#J]
  • Bear rebuttal #1: FCF dividend coverage of 1.0-1.2x is structurally thin; any EBITDA shortfall of 5-8% eliminates coverage headroom; the 2019 cut happened at similar coverage ratios [#A]
  • Evidence supporting rebuttal: European telecom peers with sub-1.3x FCF coverage (Vodafone, BT) have all cut or restructured dividends in the last 5 years [#A]

  • Track record evidence: Telia cut the dividend once (2019); management guidance on "sustainable dividend" has been repeated — the same language used before the 2019 cut [#A]

  • Bull thesis #2 — simplification/asset disposal unlocks value: selling non-core assets (Finland, Norway) concentrates the portfolio on the higher-quality Swedish market [#J]

  • Bear rebuttal #2: the disposals removed the growth markets along with the headache markets; Sweden is the highest-quality but also the most mature and slowest-growing; concentration in Sweden is concentration in a low-growth business [#A]

  • Competitive evidence: Sweden's mobile market is a three-player oligopoly (Telia, Tele2, Telenor) with rational pricing; however, fixed broadband faces growing FWA and fibre-from-Tele2/broadband-challengers competition that is accelerating fixed-line cord-cutting [#A]

  • Bull thesis #3 — cost-cutting programme drives margin expansion: multi-year operational efficiency programme should structurally reduce cost base [#J]

  • Bear rebuttal #3: the cost programme has consistently delivered below the front-end promise; in every year 2020-2024, actual EBITDA margin improvement was below initial annual guidance [#A]
  • Track record evidence: European telecom cost programmes historically deliver 60-70% of promised savings within the announced timeframe (Citi research base rate); Telia's track record is in the lower half of this range [#A]

  • Macro evidence: Swedish wage inflation and union agreements constrain headcount cost reduction pace; Telia is a significant employer with political sensitivity [#A]

  • Bull thesis #4 — 5G investment cycle peaks and FCF recovers: capex intensity should decline post-5G rollout, improving FCF conversion [#J]

  • Bear rebuttal #4: 5G capex is not a one-time cycle — fibre-to-the-premise (FttP) rollout obligations in Sweden remain ongoing; regulatory requirements and competitive necessity mean capex intensity does not normalise to pre-5G levels [#A]
  • Competitive evidence: Telenor and Tele2 are also investing in fibre and 5G; the competitive capex cycle is industry-wide and self-reinforcing [#A]

  • Evidence quantified: Telia's capex as % of revenue approximately 18-20% vs European telecom peer median ~16%; the gap has persisted for 3+ years, not converging [#A]

  • Bull thesis #5 — valuation at 5-6x EV/EBITDA is cheap vs history: Telia trades at a discount to European telecom peer median of 6-7x [#J]

  • Bear rebuttal #5: the discount is structural and deserved; Telia has lower growth, higher governance risk premium (bribery legacy), higher pension obligations, and state-ownership capital allocation constraints vs the comparator set [#A]

  • Bear rebuttal #5b: if EBITDA declines 5-8% (Exane bear scenario), the 5-6x EV/EBITDA multiple applied to a lower EBITDA base produces a flat-to-down equity value even as the multiple appears "stable" [#A]

  • Bull thesis #6 — ESG improvement story: Telia has invested heavily in ESG credentials post-bribery and is positioned as a reformed governance leader [#J]

  • Bear rebuttal #6: ESG improvement does not drive EPS or FCF; institutional ESG frameworks have not materially rerated Telia's multiple; the governance premium from state ownership (which prioritises political objectives) is a structural drag that ESG credentials do not offset [#A]

Bull rebuttal (sceptical bullet): The bear rebuttals are strongest on the dividend coverage and cost-programme track record; they are weakest on the 5G capex argument (capex has been declining from 2022 peak) and on the simplification argument (Sweden IS demonstrably higher-margin than the divested assets were); the multiple-discount argument is two-sided — it also means limited downside rerating risk [#J]

§9 — Risk Assessment + Sceptical Synthesis (cross-cutting)

J-front verdict: The single most dangerous bear scenario is a dividend cut triggered by an EBITDA shortfall, which would remove the only reliable return driver and potentially trigger a multiple compression from 5-6x to 4-5x EV/EBITDA simultaneously [#J]

  • Downside scenario — base bear: EBITDA declines 5-8% over 2 years as Swedish fixed-line accelerates, mobile ARPU softens, and cost savings disappoint; dividend cut of 25-30% required; stock re-rates to 4.5-5.0x EV/EBITDA [#J]
  • EPS downside: EBITDA decline of 5-8% flows to EBIT decline of 10-15% (operating leverage); EPS potentially down 20-30% [#A]
  • Margin compression: Adjusted EBITDA margin (currently approximately 35-38%) compresses to 32-35% range [#A]
  • Valuation re-rate: at 4.5x EV/EBITDA on a lower EBITDA base, equity value declines approximately 25-40% [#A]

  • Fair value downside: bear case fair value approximately SEK 19-23 vs current SEK 27-32 range — implied downside of approximately 25-40% [#A]

  • Downside scenario — severe bear: EBITDA declines 10-15% over 3 years; dividend eliminated or near-zero; leverage rises above 3.0x; re-rating to 3.5-4.0x EV/EBITDA [#J]

  • Probability assessment — severe bear: approximately 10-15%; requires both revenue acceleration and cost programme failure simultaneously [#A]

  • Trigger conditions: Swedish recession, FWA substitution accelerating faster than expected, 5G capex obligations extended by regulator [#A]

  • Probability — bear correct (base scenario): approximately 30-40% over an 18-36M horizon; the structural concerns are real but the timeline is uncertain [#J]

  • Probability — bear partially correct: approximately 50-60%; meaning some combination of dividend pressure, below-guidance EBITDA, and multiple compression is the most likely outcome [#J]

  • Single largest risk: dividend cut catalyst — this is the event that would simultaneously destroy the income return thesis and likely trigger a re-rating; without the yield, Telia at 5-6x EV/EBITDA in a zero-growth business has no investment case [#J]

  • Reverse-engineered bear — what has to be true for bears to be right:

  • Swedish mobile ARPU declines >3% annually for 2+ consecutive years [#I]
  • Fixed-line revenue decline accelerates beyond -8% annually [#I]
  • Cost programme delivers less than 50% of guided savings [#I]

  • Any two of these three occurring simultaneously makes the dividend cut probability approximately 60-70% [#I]
