§1 — Industry Overview + Value Chain Economics

Centrica sits at the intersection of energy commodity trading, regulated network services, and consumer-facing energy services — three very different economic sub-industries bundled under one listed entity.

  • Industry size: The UK domestic energy supply market (retail gas + electricity) is sized at approximately £80-90bn in annual consumer spend at 2023 prices; the energy services sub-market (boiler/appliance repair + installation, heat pumps, EV charging) adds a further ~£4-6bn addressable annually [#A]

  • Growth trajectory: The UK retail energy market is flat-to-declining in volumetric terms (gas demand declining structurally with insulation and heat-pump transition); energy services TAM grows low-single-digit pa, broadly GDP-inline, with heat pump installation representing the key structural acceleration option [#A]

  • Sub: UK gas consumption has fallen ~20% over the past decade; electricity consumption flat; no volume growth tailwind in core retail

  • Sub: Heat pump market could grow 3-5x by 2030 under the UK government's trajectory, but is currently very small (~50k units/year installed nationally)

  • Structural driver: Energy transition legislation (UK Net Zero 2050) is the dominant structural shaper, driving shift from gas servicing toward electricity, heat pumps, smart meters, and EV charging; creates secular opportunity and secular threat simultaneously for a gas-legacy player like Centrica [#A]

  • Geographic split: Centrica is ~75% UK, ~15% Ireland (Bord Gáis), ~10% North America (Direct Energy operations, largely divested 2021); post-Direct Energy, the business is substantially a UK + Ireland entity [#A]

  • Fragmentation: UK retail energy supply is moderately concentrated at household level (6-7 major suppliers hold ~75% accounts), but experienced extreme fragmentation entry and subsequent exit cycle 2015-2022 (over 30 small suppliers entered; majority failed during the 2021-22 energy crisis) [#A]

  • Consolidation: Post-2022, the market is re-consolidating; ~30 small supplier failures reverted account books to British Gas and Octopus; survivor set is tighter; structural consolidation is net-positive for incumbents but offset by Ofgem price-cap suppression of returns [#J]

  • Value chain map: Centrica spans multiple stages (see table below); power/margin concentration varies sharply by stage [#A]

§2 — Competitive Landscape + Industry Structure

  • Competitive verdict: The UK energy supply market is moderately concentrated but structurally contested; Centrica is the incumbent scale leader by customer accounts but has ceded ground to Octopus since 2020; industry structure is net-negative relative to the BB#2 TC4 ideal [#J]

  • Competitor tier — Global leaders: No global-scale energy retail champions; the industry is nationally fragmented; UK-specific scale leaders are Centrica/British Gas (~6.9m domestic supply accounts), Octopus Energy (~6m+ accounts), E.ON UK (~4.5m), EDF Energy (~3m), OVO Energy (~4m) [#A]

  • Competitor tier — Mid-market: Shell Energy Retail (~1.5m, post-First Utility acquisition), So Energy (acquired by Shell), Utilita (~800k); these are smaller but growing in niches [#A]

  • Competitor tier — Niche/disruptive: Octopus is the most important entrant — venture-backed (Masayoshi Son/Softbank invested ~£630m at £4.5bn valuation in 2023), technology-led (Kraken platform), growing internationally by licensing their operating system; Octopus is a genuinely disruptive archetype, not a vanilla entrant [#J]

  • Competitive dimension — Scale-cost: Centrica has genuine scale in UK domestic services (engineer workforce ~12,000 engineers, largest private-sector boiler installation/repair network in UK); this scale in engineers creates a real cost-per-job advantage vs smaller entrants [#A]

  • Competitive dimension — Technology: Centrica's technology platform is a structural weak point vs Octopus's Kraken; British Gas is mid-legacy-transformation (ongoing IT modernisation programme); Octopus can on-board accounts, manage tariffs, and handle customer interactions at ~30-40% lower cost per account vs legacy platform operators per industry commentary [#A]

  • Industry rank: British Gas is the largest UK domestic supplier by customer account count (approximately 6.9m accounts as of 2023/24); however, Octopus is closing the gap and may have drawn level or ahead on pure energy supply accounts by early 2026 [#A]

  • Top 5 share: Top-5 UK domestic suppliers hold approximately 75-80% of household energy accounts; reasonably concentrated; however, Ofgem's regulatory cap suppresses the pricing power that concentration would normally deliver [#A]

  • Distribution curve: Market share distribution is moderately steep (British Gas 25%, Octopus 20%, E.ON 15%, OVO 14%, EDF 10% approximate); the long tail accounts for ~15%; steepness is moderate rather than dramatic [#A]

  • Share stability: British Gas has lost meaningful share over the past decade (from ~45% market share in 2015 to ~25% today); share has stabilised somewhat post-2022 energy crisis as smaller rivals failed, but the 10-year trajectory is clearly negative [#J]

  • New entrant share: Octopus Energy is the single most significant share-taking competitor, growing from ~300k accounts in 2018 to ~6m by 2024; this is a structural secular customer transfer, not a cyclical temporary blip [#J]

  • Concentrating gravitational pull: Does share consolidate to the most advantaged? Partially: the 2022 crisis eliminated the weakest players; but the accounts did not all flow back to British Gas — Octopus was a primary beneficiary; share is consolidating but toward a technology-advantaged challenger, not the incumbent [#J]

  • Competitor attitudes — Benign-ness: Competitor attitudes are mixed to negative; Octopus is explicitly targeting growth and market leadership, licensing its Kraken platform internationally, and is a growth-oriented operator; OVO has similarly been aggressive in pricing; this is not a benign-to-rational oligopoly [#J]

  • MOL stability: Retail supply margins are regulated to the Ofgem price cap level, limiting above-normal profits; service margins are more stable (30-40% gross margin range) but face growing competition; blended MOL stability is structurally below the BB#2 TC4 ideal [#A]

  • Shark attack risk: Material — Octopus Energy has demonstrated willingness to compete aggressively on price, technology, and product innovation; if British Gas showed weakness (customer service failures, pricing misstep), Octopus has the appetite and financial backing to accelerate share gains [#J]

  • Volume vs ROCE: British Gas historically has prioritised customer retention and ROCE over volume-chasing in recent years under O'Shea; the explicit strategy has been to stop chasing low-quality, low-margin accounts and focus on higher-value service relationships; this is the right capital allocation instinct but structurally shrinks the addressable account base [#J]

  • Competitiveness trends: Net negative on competitive position in retail supply (secular share loss to Octopus); net more stable in services (engineer moat intact); B2B solutions under moderate competitive pressure [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on industry structure is that the favourable concentration dynamic (top-5 control 75-80%) is structurally undermined by the regulator; Ofgem has consistently intervened whenever incumbent margins moved toward normal profitability; the concentration ratio is therefore deceptive — it does not translate to pricing power in the way a consumer goods or software market concentration would; additionally, Octopus's platform licensing model (Kraken serving 50m+ accounts globally) creates a structural technology-cost advantage that compounds with scale, ultimately threatening the incumbent economics of British Gas at an accelerating pace [#J]

§3 — Barriers to Entry, Exit + Disruption Forces

  • Barriers verdict: Barriers in retail supply are moderate-low (regulation is the primary barrier, not proprietary advantage); barriers in services are higher (engineer network, brand, customer relationships); in aggregate, barriers are below the BB#2 TC3 ideal and declining in supply [#J]

  • Capital requirement: Low-to-moderate for pure retail energy supply (no generation assets required); Ofgem licencing requires credit collateral and financial standing, which eliminated many small players in 2021-22; this is a temporary barrier not a structural moat [#A]

  • Scale economy: In services (engineer deployment), scale matters significantly — British Gas's ~12,000-engineer network creates a meaningful per-job cost advantage; national coverage + call-centre routing efficiency is hard to replicate at sub-1m customer scale [#A]

  • Brand: British Gas brand is one of the UK's most recognised consumer brands; awareness is near-universal; this drives retention and new-customer acquisition in services; in supply, the brand has been damaged by billing and service failures (widely reported 2012-2018 period) [#A]

  • Sub: Net Promoter Score recovery under O'Shea has been tracked and reported as improved, though independently unverified against peers in public data

  • Switching cost: Services customers are sticky — annual service agreements with auto-renewal create switching friction; Centrica reports ~85% average retention on service agreements; this is a genuine lock-in mechanism [#A]

  • Sub: Energy supply switching is near-frictionless (one-click digital switching since 2019 Ofgem rule changes); supply switching cost is essentially zero

  • Regulatory barrier: Ofgem licencing requirements post-2022 have effectively raised the bar for new entrants; balance sheet requirements, hedging obligations, and Supplier of Last Resort (SoLR) duties now require materially more capital; this helps incumbents but also constrains their own return profile [#A]

  • Technology barrier: Technology is becoming a barrier against Centrica rather than for it; Octopus's Kraken platform processes accounts at a fraction of legacy-system cost; if Centrica's IT modernisation programme (ongoing multi-year investment) lags, the technology gap widens [#J]

  • Distribution barrier: British Gas's engineer distribution network is a genuine distribution barrier in services; replicating a 12,000-engineer national deployment network with same-/next-day booking capability requires years of recruitment and training [#A]

  • Lock-in mechanism: Services auto-renewal contracts are the primary lock-in; bundling (combining supply + services + smart home on one account) creates additional stickiness; Centrica's "Connected Home" strategy explicitly targets this bundling lock-in [#A]

  • Structural exit: Post-2022 energy crisis, 30+ smaller UK suppliers have exited; the market is effectively self-cleansing at the small-scale end; no major incumbent exit is underway [#A]

  • Disruption force — Technology platform: Octopus Kraken is the most material single disruption force; a licensed SaaS operating platform that runs energy supply at half the cost per account of legacy infrastructure; if Kraken achieves 30-40 licensed utilities globally (currently ~50m accounts on platform), the cost-per-account differential compresses legacy players' economics permanently [#J]

  • Disruption force — Decarbonisation mandate: UK government Heat Pump targets (600k/year by 2028 from ~70k today), smart meter roll-out obligations, and EV charging proliferation all require Centrica to transform its service workforce and product range within a compressed timeline; execution failure = stranded legacy asset base [#A]

  • Innovative entrant: Octopus Energy is the paradigmatic innovative entrant; technology-first, VC-backed, international platform ambition; has disrupted the market more in 6 years than all other challengers combined [#J]

  • Vanilla entrant: Shell Energy Retail (acquired First Utility), bp Pulse (EV charging), Total Energies (expanding UK retail) are vanilla/adjacency entrants; less threatening than Octopus but adding competitive surface area [#A]

  • Adjacency entry: Big Tech adjacencies are early-stage but plausible — Amazon (Alexa home energy management), Google (Nest smart thermostat) have strategic footholds in connected-home infrastructure that could extend to energy services management [#A]

  • Substitute: District heating networks are a partial substitute for gas boiler servicing in new-build and some retrofit scenarios; community energy schemes represent a small but growing substitute; the most structural substitute is the heat pump (replaces gas boiler entirely — eliminates the recurring boiler-service agreement revenue) [#J]

  • Disintermediation: Peer-to-peer energy trading (local generation + battery storage) and community energy platforms represent a nascent disintermediation risk; currently under 1% of the market but directionally moving toward consumer self-supply [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The inversion case on barriers is that Centrica's most cited barriers (brand, engineer network, service contract lock-in) are all under coherent assault simultaneously: Octopus is winning on brand perception among younger demographics, is training and hiring engineers (50k workforce by 2030 ambition stated), and is offering bundle-and-lock service contracts at digital-native cost structures; the engineer-network moat may prove less durable than assumed if Octopus replicates it over a 5-7 year horizon at lower unit cost; the disruption risk is not a point-in-time event but a slow structural compression [#J]
