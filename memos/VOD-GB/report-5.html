[W] VOD-GB (Vodafone Group plc) — Earnings & Fundamental Delivery: LTM Analysis [C] @ 29-Apr-26

KEY FINDINGS

- Germany drag materially worse than headlines suggest. Germany service revenue fell 5.0% in FY25, with EBITDAaL down 12.6%, driven by the MDU TV law change that cost Vodafone approximately 3 million TV subscribers; the underlying mobile and broadband trends are also weak, with 1 million mobile customers lost over the year and 102,000 fewer broadband customers.

- Group results in-line but flattered by Africa and FX/disposal mix. Organic service revenue growth of 5.1% looked solid but was heavily subsidised by Africa (Vodacom organic service revenue +11.3%), obscuring a deteriorating European core; the Germany drag was described as "offset" by the rest, but the quality of offset matters.

- Three UK merger creates genuine optionality but is pre-revenue-synergy stage. The £700m per annum synergy target by year 5 is credible in structure, and network integration has started faster than expected; however, the merger only completed 31 May 2025, meaning FY25 numbers carry zero synergy benefit and FY26 captures only a partial year.

- FCF of €2.5bn with €3.7bn returned to shareholders looks generous — requires scrutiny. The buyback was funded by disposal proceeds (Spain, Italy, Vantage Towers stakes), not by operating cash generation; going forward, with disposal proceeds exhausted, the sustainability of capital returns depends on Germany stabilising.

- Balance sheet materially improved post-disposals; leverage at 2.0x is below the 2.25-2.75x target range. This is a genuine positive, providing strategic optionality, but it is a one-time improvement from asset sales rather than operating deleveraging.

- Germany has shown its first signs of TV customer stabilisation (Q1 FY26: +28,000 cable subscribers), offering a potential inflection signal, but is too early to call a durable trend. Management's "turnaround market" framing has been consistent — but credibility requires quarters of sustained delivery, not one quarter.

- FY26 guidance upgraded in November 2025 to the upper end of the range (EBITDAaL €11.3-11.6bn, FCF €2.4-2.6bn), with Q3 FY26 reaffirmed. This trajectory — and Barclays' upgrade to Overweight — suggests growing market confidence in the Della Valle transformation thesis.

1. Executive Summary

Overall Earnings Quality & Forward Outlook Rating: YELLOW

- In-line delivery on key metrics. FY25 results announced 20 May 2025 were described by management as having achieved all financial guidance for the year. Organic service revenue growth of 5.1% (guidance: approximately 5%) and adjusted EBITDAaL of €10.9bn (guidance: approximately €10.7-11.0bn) were both within or at the top of communicated ranges.

- Revenue beat was marginal; EBITDA was at the high end of expectations. The consensus had been expecting broadly similar numbers given the Q3 FY25 trading update (February 2025) had already flagged the Germany trajectory. The absence of a positive surprise limited the upside share price reaction in isolation.

- Germany was the dominant negative surprise in H2. The magnitude of the MDU TV law impact (−7.5pp on Germany EBITDAaL) was partially known but the breadth of knock-on effects to broadband net adds and mobile retention was worse than some analysts had modelled.

- Guidance for FY26 was set constructively. Initial FY26 guidance at announcement was EBITDAaL €11.0-€11.3bn and FCF €2.6-€2.8bn (pre-Three UK completion). Post Three UK merger completion (31 May 2025), guidance was revised to incorporate the combined entity: EBITDAaL €11.3-11.6bn, FCF €2.4-2.6bn. The slight FCF reduction reflected elevated Three UK capex.

- Analyst tone improved materially post-results and through FY26. Barclays upgraded to Overweight (price target 120p). By H1 FY26 (November 2025), management tone was more confident, with Germany returning to growth in Q2 FY26. Morgan Stanley remained at Hold; Goldman Sachs retained a Sell.

- Share price rose approximately 8% on the FY25 day. This was partly a relief rally — Germany had not been worse than feared — rather than genuine positive surprise.

  • Dividend rebased to 4.5 eurocents per share (from 9.0 eurocents), reflecting the reshaped, smaller Group post Spain and Italy disposals. The cut was well-telegraphed and the market had already priced it in.

- The transformation narrative is progressing. Della Valle's "customers, simplicity, growth" agenda is generating visible structural changes: portfolio focused from 21 to 14 markets, headcount reduced, Vantage Towers monetised, Spain and Italy exited. The question is whether the operating metrics follow strategy.

- Q&A tone post-results (May 2025) was cautiously positive. Management resisted providing detailed Germany recovery timelines but said mobile churn was "the lowest in four years" and the TV customer loss rate was already stabilising.

- The key forward earnings risk is Germany execution speed, not Africa or Other Europe. The bear thesis is that Germany remains structurally impaired — not merely cyclically pressured — by cable competition from Deutsche Telekom's open-access network.

2. Company Description & Context

Rating: YELLOW

  • Core business post-disposals. Vodafone Group plc is a multinational telecommunications operator headquartered in London. Following the 2024 sales of Vodafone Italy (to Swisscom) and Vodafone Spain (to Zegona), the Group now operates across Europe (Germany, UK via Three UK merger, Portugal, Ireland, Greece, Romania, Czech Republic, Albania) and Africa (via Vodacom, a separately listed subsidiary with operations in South Africa, Tanzania, DRC, Mozambique, Kenya, Ethiopia, Egypt, and others including Vodafone Ghana). Türkiye is a minority-owned associate.

  • Revenue profile. Total FY25 revenue: €37.4bn. Service revenue: €30.8bn. The Group operates across mobile and fixed (cable/fibre), B2C and B2B. Germany has historically been the largest single market (~33% of Group revenues) and its structural position is the dominant valuation debate.

- Financial profile. Adjusted EBITDAaL €10.9bn; FCF €2.5bn; net debt €22.4bn; leverage 2.0x (below the new 2.25-2.75x target range). This capital structure is materially cleaner than three years ago, when leverage was ~3.3x.

- Market position. In Germany, Vodafone operates the country's second-largest cable network and a significant mobile network. Post Three UK merger, VodafoneThree becomes the largest mobile operator in the UK by subscriber base (28.8 million mobile customers). Africa/Vodacom is the dominant operator in South Africa and a significant challenger in multiple sub-Saharan markets.

  • L3Y revenue performance. Reported revenue has contracted in EUR terms due to the Italy and Spain disposals. Organic service revenue growth: FY23 approximately +5%, FY24 +6.3%, FY25 +5.1%. The organic trajectory shows a slight deceleration but remains constructive. On a like-for-like basis (excluding disposed entities), the continuing operations are growing.

- L3Y EPS trajectory. Reported EPS is distorted by large disposal gains (FY24) and impairments (FY22/23). Adjusted basic EPS (continuing operations, constant currency) has been depressed and is not a clean long-term trend signal given the portfolio transformation.

  • L3Y margin trajectory. Adjusted EBITDAaL margin has hovered around 28-30% on a group basis, with Germany dragging since FY23 when the MDU law change began to bite. The margin improvement story for FY26 onwards depends on Germany stabilising and Three UK synergies flowing through.

- Key strategic initiatives. (1) Three UK merger integration — the largest structural event; (2) Germany mobile and cable turnaround; (3) Africa data and financial services growth via Vodacom; (4) Vantage Towers monetisation — now largely complete at 50:50 ownership; (5) Group simplification — headcount reduction, shared services rationalisation, technology modernisation.

  • CEO Della Valle took over in April 2023 having served as CFO since 2018. She has deep institutional knowledge of Vodafone and has driven the disposals programme with clear strategic logic. Market credibility has improved over her tenure, though investors remain sceptical on the German recovery timeline.

  • Vantage Towers. Vodafone has progressively sold down its stake — most recently selling a further 10% for €1.3bn in March 2026, reaching the 50:50 joint ownership structure with GIP/KKR that was the original deal target. Total Vantage proceeds to date: €6.6bn, used primarily for deleveraging and buybacks.

3. Earnings Delivery & Beat/Miss Analysis

Rating: YELLOW

- Group service revenue organic growth: 5.1% vs guidance ~5%. A marginal beat at the top line, driven by Africa and Other Europe outperforming. Germany was the principal drag and was within the range that had been communicated at Q3 FY25 update.

- Adjusted EBITDAaL: €10.9bn. This was at or near the top of communicated guidance. Organic EBITDAaL growth of 2.5% was better than some feared given the Germany deterioration, because Other Europe and Africa contributed meaningfully.

- Revenue by geography. Germany: service revenue −5.0% (7.5pp impact from MDU TV law, remainder from mobile and broadband weakness). UK: carried pre-merger Vodafone UK numbers, showing modest organic growth. Africa/Vodacom: organic service revenue growth +11.3%, an outperformer. Other Europe (Portugal, Ireland, Greece, Romania, Czech Republic, Albania): collectively positive organic growth, partially offsetting Germany.

- EPS. Adjusted EPS is distorted by the portfolio transformation. Reported EPS was impacted by disposal accounting. The company does not guide to reported EPS; management focuses on adjusted EBITDAaL and FCF as the operative metrics.

- FCF: €2.5bn. In line with guidance. However, contextually important: this excludes significant capex associated with the Three UK transaction (accelerated network deployment c.€1.5bn proforma), which will flow into FY26 numbers as the combined entity invests in integration.

- Prior period guidance delivery pattern. Vodafone has a reasonable but not exceptional track record of guidance delivery. FY24 EBITDAaL guidance was met. The H1 FY25 results had already flagged Germany TV subscriber losses were larger than originally expected, requiring a recalibration mid-year. FY25 full-year delivery was within the recalibrated range, not the original range.

  • Germany EBITDAaL: −12.6%. This is the most important single earnings quality signal. A double-digit EBITDA decline in the group's largest market is not in-line in any qualitative sense. The reported reason (MDU TV regulation) is genuine and verifiable — Vodafone lost approximately 3 million TV subscribers as a direct result of legislation that ended bulk TV contracts in multi-dwelling units from July 2024. However, the underlying mobile and broadband trajectory (−1 million mobile customers, −102,000 broadband) reflects competitive weakness beyond the regulatory event.

- Beats were not driven by one-off items. Africa's outperformance is structural (M-Pesa growth, data penetration, currency recovery in Egypt). Other Europe's solid performance reflects pricing discipline and relatively lower competitive intensity vs Germany. The Germany miss is operationally real.

- Quality concern: Germany generates ~33% of Group adjusted FCF. A market generating one-third of cash that is declining EBITDAaL by double digits is a genuine medium-term risk to Group FCF sustainability. The bear case is not unreasonable on this metric.

  • Sceptical interpretation. The full-year "in-line" framing obscures the fact that Germany — the anchor of the investment case — is in structural trouble. The organic group numbers are held up by Africa and a beneficial denominator effect (Spain/Italy excluded). Strip out Africa and Turkey and European Vodafone (ex-UK) barely covers its cost of capital.

4. Revenue & Organic Growth Analysis

Rating: YELLOW

- Total reported revenue: €37.4bn (+2.0% reported). Organic growth is a better lens given Spain and Italy were included in FY24 comparatives. Organic service revenue growth of 5.1% is the headline number management uses.

- Africa: the standout performer. Vodacom/Africa organic service revenue growth was +11.3% in FY25, accelerating through the year (Q4 FY25: +13.5%). This reflects: (1) Egyptian Pound currency normalisation post devaluation; (2) M-Pesa revenue growth +10% to €427.9m (representing 27.6% of Vodacom's service revenue); (3) strong data penetration across sub-Saharan Africa; and (4) South Africa service revenue growing solidly. Africa is the genuine growth engine of the Group.

- Germany service revenue: −5.0%. This is the most significant organic revenue miss in absolute terms. The MDU TV regulation change (effective July 2024) removed 3pp of Germany revenue growth alone. Underlying mobile service revenue was also negative due to competitive pressure from Deutsche Telekom and customer losses. The important forward question is when the TV subscriber loss cycle completes (losses were already slowing by FY25 year-end) and when the mobile base stabilises.

- UK (Vodafone UK standalone, pre-Three merger). Organic growth was modest positive before Three UK completion on 31 May 2025. Post-merger, UK becomes a significantly larger operation — but synergies are deferred to future periods.

- Other Europe. Portugal, Ireland, Greece, Romania, Czech Republic, and Albania collectively delivered positive organic growth, providing a meaningful offset to Germany. These markets have lower competitive intensity and benefit from Vodafone's fibre rollout and pricing discipline.

- Pricing vs volume decomposition. Across European markets, growth was broadly price-driven: Vodafone has implemented above-inflation price increases in most markets (CPI + 3% type mechanisms), while volume (subscriber) trends have been negative in Germany. In Africa, growth is volume-driven (data adoption, financial services users) with pricing disciplined by the competitive environment.

- Market share signals. In Germany: continued share loss in mobile (−1m customers over the year), broadband net adds negative. This is the key bear case support — Vodafone is not just suffering a regulatory headwind, it is losing market share in a structurally competitive cable market. In UK post-merger, VodafoneThree is the largest network by subscriber count but faces a market recovery phase.

- Forward revenue guidance. For FY26, Vodafone guided organic service revenue growth of approximately 3-5%, including Three UK (whose customers are now in the base). Q1 FY26 delivered 5.5% organic growth, and Q3 FY26 delivered 6.5%, with guidance maintained. Germany returned to growth in Q2 FY26 — a critical milestone.

- Sceptical interpretation. The 5.1% group organic growth figure is flattered by Africa (high single-digit weighting, double-digit growth). European Vodafone (ex-Germany, ex-UK) grew, but Germany alone was strongly negative. The headline organic number is not representative of the European core health. Investors should decompose this carefully before drawing a "growing telco" conclusion.

5. Margin Profile & Quality

Rating: YELLOW

  • Group adjusted EBITDAaL margin (FY25): approximately 29.2% of total revenue (€10.9bn / €37.4bn). On a service revenue basis (more relevant for telcos): approximately 35.4%.

- L3Y margin trajectory. FY23: ~30%+ margin (pre-Germany deterioration). FY24: approximately 29.5% (first full year of MDU TV impact beginning to flow). FY25: approximately 29.2%. The trend is modestly declining at group level, driven entirely by Germany. Other Europe and Africa are either stable or improving.

- Germany margin collapse: −12.6% EBITDAaL on −5% service revenue. This represents aggressive negative operating leverage. The high fixed-cost structure of a cable network means revenue loss falls disproportionately to EBITDA. Germany's cable network carries high depreciation and maintenance costs regardless of subscriber count.

  • Three UK integration capex. Post-merger, the combined entity (VodafoneThree) faces elevated capex — approximately €1.5bn proforma above prior standalone levels — as the network is integrated, MOCN deployed, and spectrum combined. This will weigh on depreciation and hence EBITDAaL margin for FY26 and FY27, though cost synergies (£700m run-rate by year 5) are intended to more than offset.

- Adjusted vs reported margins. Vodafone takes significant non-GAAP adjustments: restructuring charges (headcount reduction costs), disposal gains, spectrum and licence impairments, and integration costs. The gap between adjusted and reported EBIT has historically been meaningful. In FY25, with Spain/Italy disposals generating accounting gains and integration costs for Three UK beginning, the gap is again elevated.

  • Cost savings programme. Management committed to €1bn+ in cost savings across the Group over FY24-FY26. Progress has been on track, with shared services consolidation, technology rationalisation, and headcount reductions contributing. This is a structural margin support.

- Operating leverage. Outside Germany, there is some operating leverage as revenue grows against a broadly fixed cost base. Africa's improving EBITDA margin reflects scale leverage as data revenue grows. In European markets ex-Germany, margin discipline is solid.

- One-off costs. FY25 contained ongoing restructuring charges related to the simplification programme. These are non-recurring in management's view but have been recurring in practice for several years — a standard sceptical observation for any ongoing transformation.

- Sceptical interpretation. The margin story depends almost entirely on Germany recovering. If Germany EBITDAaL continues to decline — or stabilises at the new depressed level — Group margins face structural compression because Germany was the highest-margin market in the European portfolio. The cost savings programme is real but partially offsets what is being lost in Germany.

6. Cash Flow & Capital Allocation

Rating: YELLOW

- Adjusted FCF: €2.5bn (FY25). This is the operative metric for assessing cash generation capability. It represents operating cash flow less capex less interest, on a continuing operations basis.

  • Cash conversion. FCF of €2.5bn against EBITDAaL of €10.9bn implies a FCF conversion of approximately 23%. This is below the levels a structurally healthy telco should generate and reflects: high interest costs on the legacy debt stack, elevated capex (particularly network modernisation across Europe and Africa), and working capital movements related to the transformation programme.

  • Capex. Group capex has been running at €5-6bn per annum (approximately 15-16% of revenue). Post Three UK merger, the combined UK entity is investing approximately €1.5bn above pre-merger UK levels, which will suppress FCF in FY26. Management has guided FY26 FCF at €2.4-2.6bn, implying capex discipline even with Three UK integration.

- Net debt: €22.4bn at FY25 year end. Leverage ratio: 2.0x net debt / EBITDAaL. This is below the new target range of 2.25-2.75x, having improved dramatically from approximately 3.3x two years ago. The improvement is primarily disposal-driven (Italy, Spain proceeds, Vantage Towers stake sales of €6.6bn cumulative).

  • Capital returns in FY25. Total returns to shareholders: €3.7bn. This comprised: dividend (4.5 eurocents per share, rebased from 9.0 eurocents); share buyback of €2.0bn (2.4 billion shares repurchased since March 2024). The buyback was funded from disposal proceeds, not from operating FCF — a critical distinction.

  • New buyback announced. A new €2.0bn buyback programme was launched at FY25 results, with an initial tranche of €0.5bn. This signals confidence but again should be read in the context of available liquidity from Vantage Towers proceeds, not as evidence of underlying FCF strength.

  • Dividend sustainability. The rebased 4.5 eurocent dividend costs approximately €1.2bn annually at current share count. Against FCF guidance of €2.4-2.6bn (FY26), this is covered approximately 2x. However, if FCF disappoints (Germany declines further, Three UK integration capex exceeds guidance), dividend coverage narrows. FY26 dividend is expected to grow 2.5%.

  • Vantage Towers monetisation. The March 2026 sale of a further 10% stake for €1.3bn (total €6.6bn realised) is a clean balance sheet event. The 50:50 ownership structure is now the target — further disposal is not part of the immediate plan.

  • Sceptical interpretation. Strip out the disposal windfall and the underlying FCF generation is modest relative to the enterprise value. The buyback is financed by asset sales, not by earnings power. The leverage improvement is real but not repeatable — there are no more Italy-scale assets to sell. Future deleveraging must come from earnings growth, which requires Germany to cooperate.

7. Management Guidance & Forward Outlook

Rating: YELLOW-GREEN

- FY26 initial guidance (May 2025, pre-Three UK completion). EBITDAaL: €11.0-11.3bn; FCF: €2.6-2.8bn. This was set before the Three UK completion on 31 May 2025.

- FY26 revised guidance (post-Three UK merger, November 2025). EBITDAaL: €11.3-11.6bn; FCF: €2.4-2.6bn. The EBITDAaL increase reflects Three UK consolidation (adding scale), while the FCF reduction reflects elevated integration capex. This is a structurally sound trade: more EBITDA now, more FCF from FY27 onwards as synergies flow.

- Q3 FY26 guidance reaffirmed (early 2026). Management confirmed delivery of the upper end of the FY26 guidance ranges, with Q3 FY26 organic service revenue growth of 6.5%, ahead of the FY26 guidance rate of approximately 5%.

- Germany inflection. The most critical forward milestone: Germany returned to growth in Q2 FY26 (first time in approximately 4-5 years), driven by completion of the MDU TV law change impact cycle and growing wholesale revenue. Q1 FY26 TV adds were positive for the first time in nearly a decade (+28,000 cable subscribers). These are early but genuine positive datapoints.

- Three UK synergy milestones. Target: £700m per annum cost and capex synergies by year 5 (i.e., by approximately FY30). FCF accretion expected from FY29 onwards. Near-term integration milestones: 500 MOCN-enabled sites live by August 2025, targeting 10,000+ by March 2026. Early wins on spectrum sharing have delivered 20-40% 4G speed improvements for Three customers.

- Africa growth trajectory. Vodacom's 2030 targets include reaching 260 million users and significantly diversifying revenue. M-Pesa's 10%+ annual growth rate and VodaPay's 11.9 million users provide a structural growth platform. This is the most visible, durable growth component of the Group.

  • Dividend policy. 4.5 eurocents per share for FY25, with FY26 guided to grow 2.5% to approximately 4.6 eurocents. The "ambition to grow dividends over time" framing is deliberately non-committal — management wants to rebuild confidence before making stronger capital return commitments.

- Medium-term financial algorithm. Management has communicated an ambition for mid-single-digit organic service revenue growth, EBITDAaL growth above service revenue growth (operating leverage), and FCF growing faster than EBITDAaL (capex normalisation). This framework requires Germany stabilisation and Three UK synergy delivery — both of which are credible but not yet proven.

- Sceptical interpretation. The revised FY26 guidance (lower FCF vs original) reveals the cost of the Three UK merger in the near term. Management's upgrade to "upper end" at H1 FY26 is positive, but the track record of Vodafone guidance delivery over three years includes one mid-year Germany recalibration, which somewhat undermines the guidance precision. The 5-year synergy timeline for Three UK is long enough that it cannot be tested in the near term.

8. Strategic Execution & Operational Progress

Rating: YELLOW-GREEN

- Three UK merger: fast start on integration. The merger completed 31 May 2025, ahead of some regulatory scenario timelines. Within months, MOCN technology was being deployed at scale: 500 sites live by August 2025, with 10,000 targeted by March 2026. Seven million Three customers experienced average 20% boost in 4G speeds immediately post-merger. This is a genuine operational win.

- Three UK synergy delivery: too early to assess. The £700m annual synergy target (year 5) has not yet begun to flow in numbers. The framework is credible — network sharing removes duplicate infrastructure costs, spectrum combination removes capex requirements, and cost rationalisation across overlapping functions is real. The risk is delivery timeline slippage.

- National roaming. Vodafone has a long-term national roaming agreement that management described as "a material contributor to growth," with full run-rate expected by H2 FY26. This provides an additional revenue stream and leverages the combined network asset.

- Germany turnaround: stabilising, not yet recovering. The critical signals: mobile churn is now "single digit, the lowest in four years"; TV customer losses are decelerating and turned positive in Q1 FY26 (first positive adds in nearly a decade). These are the right leading indicators. The lagging indicator (service revenue and EBITDAaL) turned positive in Q2 FY26, as noted.

- German cable competitive dynamics. The structural risk is Deutsche Telekom's open-access fibre rollout, which is progressively making Vodafone's cable proposition less differentiated. Vodafone's Germany business model depends on the cable network maintaining adequate ARPU and subscriber economics — and the competitive environment (DT, 1&1, and new fibre ISPs) is intensifying. This is not a cyclical issue; it is a structural one.

  • Africa / Vodacom. Management is executing well: 260 million user target by 2030, financial services growing double-digit, M-Pesa representing 27.6% of Vodacom service revenue. The Vodacom separately listed structure means Vodafone consolidates the revenues but shareholders capture the value at a partial discount.

- Portfolio simplification. The exit from Italy (Swisscom deal) and Spain (Zegona deal) has substantially reduced geographic complexity. The Group is now in 14 markets (down from 21). Organisational simplification is underway, with shared services, technology platforms, and management layers reduced.

  • Vantage Towers. The 50:50 joint venture with GIP/KKR is now the stable structure. Vodafone retains meaningful economic benefit from the tower assets (valued at €16bn enterprise value in the original 2022 transaction) without the operational complexity of full ownership. The €6.6bn of cumulative proceeds have been a major balance sheet repair tool.

- Della Valle track record. Under her leadership since April 2023: two major market exits (Italy, Spain), Vantage Towers monetisation, Three UK merger, buyback programme, dividend rebasing and clarity. This is a high-velocity transformation agenda executed in approximately two years. The open question is whether the operational (Germany) results validate the strategic choices.

9. Industry / Sector Context

Rating: YELLOW

- European telecom sector: flat to modest growth. The IDC analysis of Europe's top 5 telcos (BT, Deutsche Telekom, Orange, Telefónica, Vodafone) shows CY24 top-line growth was −2.2% to +3.4%, reflecting a broadly flat competitive environment with pockets of pricing-driven improvement. This is the context in which Vodafone's 5.1% organic service revenue growth (including Africa) looks relatively strong — but it is partially a comparison set effect.

- Deutsche Telekom: the benchmark. DTE has delivered EBITDAaL margins of approximately 43% (significantly above Vodafone's ~29-30%) and has been consistently the best operator in Europe by capital allocation discipline and network quality. DTE's T-Mobile US provides a high-growth earnings driver that Vodafone lacks. The comparison makes Vodafone's Germany challenges more stark — DTE is winning market share at Vodafone's expense.

  • BT Group. BT's EBITDAaL margin approximately 40%, focused on UK fibre (Openreach) and enterprise. BT's strategic exit from legacy copper (announced March 2025) is structurally positive — it accelerates fibre adoption and improves network quality. BT and VodafoneThree will compete intensely in UK mobile (EE vs VodafoneThree).

  • Orange. Similar scale to Telefónica, focused on Europe and Africa. Orange's Africa operations provide a structural parallel to Vodacom, though M-Pesa and Orange Money compete directly in several markets. Orange has been more defensive, with less portfolio transformation than Vodafone.

  • Telefónica. Spain/LATAM focused. The sale of Vodafone Spain to Zegona indirectly reflects Telefónica's competitive intensity in Spain. TEF has been more cautious on structural reform and leverage.

  • Telecom Italia. The Swisscom acquisition of Vodafone Italy coincides with TIM's network separation and Vivendi's ongoing battles. TIM remains deeply restructuring-phase — a very different investment thesis from Vodafone.

  • Telia. Focused on Nordics/Baltics — a smaller, more defensive peer. Lower leverage and more dividend-stable, but minimal growth.

- Sector-wide trends. (1) European telecoms are implementing price increases (CPI+x mechanisms) broadly, providing a revenue tailwind for FY25-FY26 despite volume maturity. (2) Fibre rollout is a capex-intensive but structurally necessary investment across all operators. (3) AI and digital services are emerging revenue opportunities, but material revenue contributions remain 2-3 years away. (4) Infrastructure monetisation (tower sales, fibre JVs) has been a sector-wide theme that Vodafone has played more aggressively than peers.

- Macro context. European economic conditions in CY25 were sluggish — low GDP growth across Germany, France, and the UK particularly. This translates to modest consumer spending and enterprise IT budget pressure, relevant to Vodafone's business connectivity revenues. However, mobile and broadband are relatively non-cyclical, and Vodafone's pricing has been maintained above inflation in most markets.

10. Sceptical / Bearish Interpretation

Rating: ORANGE

- Germany is the case against Vodafone, in full. The bear thesis: Germany generates approximately one-third of Group adjusted FCF, is in structural — not just regulatory — decline, faces an increasingly well-resourced competitor (Deutsche Telekom's open-access fibre erodes cable superiority), and has lost 1 million mobile customers in the past year. The MDU TV regulation is a convenient one-off explanation, but the underlying mobile and broadband trends (both negative subscriber adds) existed before the TV law change.

- The "turnaround market" is in its third year of decline. Vodafone first labelled Germany a "turnaround market" in 2022. It is now 2025 and Germany EBITDAaL is down 12.6% in the most recent year. The definition of turnaround that produces three consecutive years of deterioration needs scrutiny.

  • Cash returns are asset sale proceeds in disguise. The €3.7bn returned to shareholders in FY25, and the new €2.0bn buyback, are explicitly funded by Spain/Italy disposal proceeds and Vantage Towers stake sales. Once these are exhausted, operating FCF of €2.5bn must sustain: dividends (~€1.2bn), interest on €22.4bn debt stack, and any further buybacks. The structural FCF is adequate but not generous.

- Three UK integration is a multi-year optionality story, not a near-term earnings driver. FCF accretion is guided from FY29. Investors buying Vodafone for Three UK synergies are paying today for cash flows arriving in approximately five years, in a sector that typically trades on near-term metrics.

  • Working capital and non-cash items. Vodafone's adjusted EBITDAaL to reported FCF gap (€10.9bn EBITDAaL, €2.5bn FCF) implies approximately €8.4bn of cash absorbed by interest, taxes, capex, and working capital. This is partially structural (interest on €22.4bn debt, capex on a large physical network) but the magnitude is a genuine quality concern. Interest cover is adequate but not comfortable.

- Earnings quality: persistent gap between adjusted and reported. Restructuring charges, disposal accounting, integration costs, and spectrum amortisation are recurrent features of Vodafone's P&L, blurring the true underlying earnings trajectory.
