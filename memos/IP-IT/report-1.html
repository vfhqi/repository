BLUF — Key Findings [#J]

  • Overall judgement: Interpump is a high-quality serial-acquirer compounder with a genuine Water Jetting moat, but post-goodwill ROIC (~8.8%) is at best barely WACC-covering — the investment case depends entirely on the pre-goodwill engine (ROIC ~20%+) continuing to generate enough organic return to offset acquisition goodwill dilution. [#J]
  • Hydraulics downturn is deep and ongoing: FY2024 Hydraulics revenue fell 13.9% yoy; Q3 2025 showed the first organic recovery (+3.4%) — the cycle has turned but is not yet confirmed; Water Jetting is the margin cushion, not Hydraulics. [#J]
  • Water Jetting is the crown jewel: ~32% of revenue but structurally higher gross margins (~45%+), driven by aftermarket/consumables model — this division has genuine installed-base moat characteristics. [#J]
  • Succession risk is lower than commonly cited: Montipò remains Executive Chairman; Fabio Marasi (CEO since Apr-23) is an internal appointment with direct operational history across three Interpump subsidiaries — not a blank-sheet external hire. [#J]
  • M&A discipline is a real question: 51 acquisitions over ~30 years produced revenue CAGR ~12% and EPS CAGR ~16% since FY2011; however average FCF/EBITDA conversion ~50% over 2016-23 is ❌ below specialty industrial peers (Alfa Laval ~60-65%; Parker Hannifin adjusted ~70%+). [#J/#A]
  • Capital employed expanding into the downturn: Capital employed rose €2,371m → €2,496m between end-2023 and end-2024 even as EBIT fell 21%; ROCE declined from 18.1% to 13.5% — this compression is a watchpoint. [#A]
  • Electrification of hydraulics is structural opportunity, not threat: Interpump's pivot to electro-hydraulic pumps (permanent magnet motors, variable displacement, smart IoT) positions it to benefit from the $11.8bn (2024) electro-hydraulic market growing at 6.6% CAGR to 2033. [#A]
  • Valuation: Currently ~9x EV/EBITDA (2.0x EV/Revenue LTM) — not obviously cheap for a name in hydraulic cyclical trough; thesis requires confidence that Water Jetting moat + M&A engine will deliver re-rating on recovery. [#J]
  • Sceptic's view: Post-goodwill ROIC barely covering WACC, FCF conversion structurally below peers, CEO compensation up 20%+ while earnings fell 20%+ — none of these are disqualifying alone, but collectively they are watchpoints. [#J]

§2 Company Details

§2.1 Revenue Model [#I]

  • Business model: Design, manufacture, and sell hydraulic power components (Hydraulics segment) and high-pressure pumping systems (Water Jetting segment) to OEM and aftermarket customers globally.
  • Hydraulics revenue driver: Primarily OEM-driven — sold into original equipment manufacturers making agricultural machinery, construction equipment, material handling equipment, forestry equipment. Revenue linked to new equipment build rates (cyclical). [#I]
  • Water Jetting revenue driver: More aftermarket-driven — pumps sold to industrial users for cleaning, surface preparation, food processing; consumables, seals, replacement nozzles drive recurring aftermarket revenue. [#I]
  • Geographic balance: ~57% Europe, ~27% North America, ~13% Asia-Pacific, ~7% RoW — well diversified across geographies and end-markets. [#I]
  • Pricing: Niche/specialty positioning allows price premium vs commodity hydraulic peers; Water Jetting in particular has proprietary ceramic piston technology (original Montipò innovation from 1977) that underpins premium pricing. [#I]

§2.2 Moat Assessment [#A/#J]

  • Water Jetting moat (genuine): Proprietary ceramic piston pump technology, installed base requiring Interpump-specific consumables, brand recognition in high-pressure cleaning globally — fits the installed-base aftermarket moat template well. [#J]
  • Hydraulics moat (weaker/narrower): Scale advantages in power take-offs (Muncie Power Products — world's largest PTO manufacturer) and gearboxes (Reggiana Riduttori); proprietary valve designs (Walvoil); but overall Hydraulics is more commoditised and OEM-exposed. [#A]
  • Serial acquirer value creation: 51 acquisitions over ~30 years, applying Interpump's operational rigour and distribution to acquired businesses — this is itself a competitive advantage if capital discipline is maintained. [#J]
  • Sceptic: The Hydraulics moat is fragmented across many product families; no single Hydraulics sub-unit has Water Jetting's clean aftermarket flywheel. The "moat" in Hydraulics is really just scale + niche. [#J]

§2.3 M&A History [#I]

  • Era 1 — Water Jetting foundation (1977-1996): Organic build, IPO 1996; ceramic piston pump technology core.
  • Era 2 — Hydraulics entry (1998-2014): PZB (Italy), Hydrocar, Muncie Power Products (USA — world's largest PTO), Walvoil (hydraulic valves, 2014). Established global hydraulics footprint.
  • Era 3 — Scale-building (2015-2021): Bertoli (flow processing, 2015); Reggiana Riduttori (gearboxes, 2019); Transtecno (gearmotors, 2020); White Drive Motors & Steering (orbital motors, Poland/US/Germany, 2021).
  • Era 4 — Supply chain + niche (2022-2025): Eurofluid (hydraulic manifolds, 2022); Mouldtech (iron casting, India, 2023); I.Mec (mechanical screens, 2023); Waikato (milking systems, NZ, 2023); YRP Flow Technologies (Apr-24); Alltube (May-24); Alfa Valvole (Jun-24); Nuova SM (Jun-25).
  • M&A pace: ~11 disclosed acquisitions in recent Tracxn data (larger deals only); company discloses 51 total investments and acquisitions. Average acquisition consideration ~$52m (Tracxn data). [#I]
  • Revenue CAGR since FY2011: 12% total; EPS CAGR ~16.2% — leverage slightly positive. [#A]
  • Net debt/EBITDA: Rose to ~1.66x in 2014 M&A peak; declined to ~1.13x by recent reporting, demonstrating management of leverage through the acquisition cycle. [#A]

§2.4 Recent Delivery [#A/#J]

  • FY2024 context: Revenue -7.2% to €2,078m; organic decline -9%; Hydraulics -13.9% (cyclical normalisation from post-COVID inventory build); Water Jetting +10.8% (structural demand, not cyclical). [#I]
  • EBITDA resilience: 22.0% despite significant revenue headwind — management held cost structure effectively; Hydraulics segment margins compressed but not dramatically. [#A]
  • FCF record (2024): €205m FCF — highest ever, per company communications; reflects working capital normalisation as Hydraulics de-stocked. [#I]
  • 1H 2025: Q1-25 revenues -4.5% yoy (organic -8.1%); Q2-25 +1.0% yoy (organic -1.3%) — sequential improvement. Q3-25 organic +2.3% (Hydraulics +3.4%) — confirms trough passed. [#A]
  • FY2025 guidance: Organic revenue -5% to +1%; EBITDA margin 22-22.5%. [#I]
  • Sceptic: FY2025 guidance range still includes a -5% organic scenario; the recovery is nascent and not confirmed. [#J]

§3 Industry Context

§3.1 Hydraulics Industry Structure [#I]

  • Global hydraulics market size: ~$50-60bn total addressable market; mobile hydraulics (agriculture, construction, material handling) dominates.
  • Market structure: Oligopoly at the top — Parker Hannifin (#1 globally), Bosch Rexroth (#2, private), Eaton Hydraulics (#3), Danfoss Power Solutions — then fragmented mid-tier. Interpump is a leading independent European/global mid-tier player.
  • Interpump's niche: Power take-offs (dominant global position via Muncie), hydraulic cylinders, valves (Walvoil), gearboxes (Reggiana Riduttori), orbital motors (White Drive) — product breadth across the hydraulic powertrain rather than domination of a single category.
  • Cycle dynamics: Mobile hydraulics is highly cyclical — tied to agricultural/construction equipment production. 2024 saw sharp destocking after 2022-23 over-ordering. Recovery in 2025 is consistent with typical 18-24 month cycle.

§3.2 Water Jetting / High-Pressure Pumps [#I]

  • Market size: Smaller, more specialised — high-pressure water jetting is a niche within broader industrial cleaning; Interpump is a top-3 global player.
  • Key competitors: KMT (Kennametal subsidiary), Flow International — smaller field of direct competitors; Interpump's Water Jetting segment (Hammelmann, Interpump Pumps, Giant Industries) has genuine scale advantage.
  • Aftermarket intensity: High-pressure pumps require regular seal, nozzle, and piston replacement — consumables revenue is recurring and margin-accretive. This is the key differentiator vs Hydraulics.
  • End-market diversification: Industrial cleaning, food/beverage, shipbuilding, surface preparation, municipal water, oil & gas cleaning — less cyclical than mobile hydraulics.

§3.3 Macro / Non-Macro Change Forces [#A]

  • Electrification of mobile equipment: Agricultural and construction machinery OEMs (CNH, Deere, Volvo CE) are electrifying. Transition = opportunity for electro-hydraulic hybrid systems (Interpump is positioning) but also long-term risk if full electrification eliminates hydraulic powertrain need. [#A]
  • Defence spending ramp: European defence budget expansion (NATO 2%+ targets) drives demand for military vehicles with hydraulic systems — modest incremental positive for Interpump's Hydraulics segment. [#A]
  • Food safety / water regulation: Water Jetting benefits from increasing hygiene regulations in food processing and industrial cleaning; structural, non-cyclical growth driver. [#A]
  • India/Asia manufacturing growth: Interpump's Mouldtech (India) acquisition and geographic diversification positions it for Asia-Pacific infrastructure/agriculture capex cycle. [#A]
  • Sceptic: Full electrification risk in agriculture/construction is a 2030+ structural headwind; not imminent but warrants monitoring as EV adoption curves for heavy equipment steepen. [#J]

§4.3 Value Chain Analysis [#A]

Demand side:

  • Hydraulics demand drivers: Agricultural machinery (Deere, CNH, AGCO), construction equipment (Volvo CE, Liebherr, Manitowoc), material handling (Jungheinrich, Kion Group), forestry (Ponsse). All tie to end-market capex cycles — highly cyclical.
  • Water Jetting demand drivers: Industrial facilities (chemicals, food processing, pharma), shipyards, municipal water infrastructure. Driven by regulatory compliance, hygiene standards, maintenance cycles — less cyclical.
  • Geographic demand: North America and Europe remain Interpump's largest markets (combined ~70% of revenue); Asia-Pacific growing (India, China, SE Asia) but from lower base.

Value chain positioning:

  • Components → Systems → Aftermarket: Interpump operates across all three positions in Water Jetting (proprietary components, integrated pump systems, spare parts/consumables). In Hydraulics, primarily Components and Systems, with limited aftermarket pull-through vs Water Jetting.
  • Margin concentration: Water Jetting's ~45%+ gross margin vs Hydraulics ~35-38% estimated confirms that aftermarket/consumables concentration in Water Jetting drives the premium. [#A/estimated]
  • Upstream integration: Mouldtech (India casting) and Eurofluid (hydraulic manifold blocks) acquisitions represent upstream integration into core components — reduces supply chain risk, supports margin. [#A]

Competition by segment:

  • Hydraulics: Parker Hannifin and Bosch Rexroth dominate globally; Eaton, Danfoss, and Interpump are second-tier large players. Interpump's competitive position is strongest in PTO (via Muncie) and gearboxes (Reggiana Riduttori) — niches where it has genuine leadership.
  • Water Jetting: KMT (Kennametal), Flow International — Interpump is likely #1 or #2 globally. Competitive advantages are entrenched; new entrants face the installed-base replacement barrier.

Flywheel vs Treadmill assessment:

  • Water Jetting = Flywheel: OEM win creates installed base → aftermarket pulls through → funds R&D into next-generation pump → enables next OEM win. This is a classic high-quality compounder flywheel. [#J] ⚡ RARE
  • Hydraulics = Treadmill with pockets of flywheel: Most Hydraulics sub-units are OEM-driven treadmills requiring constant new business wins; but Muncie PTO (dominant market position creates de facto recurring revenue from PTO maintenance/replacement) has flywheel elements. [#J]

§4.4 Industry Structure [#A]

  • Fragmented markets with scale advantages: Both hydraulics and water jetting are fragmented at the long tail; Interpump's strategy of acquiring niche leaders creates scale advantages in each sub-niche.
  • Barriers to entry: High in Water Jetting (ceramic piston IP, installed base, global service network); moderate in Hydraulics (engineering relationships with OEMs, qualification processes, tooling investment); low at commodity hydraulic end.
  • Switching costs: Significant in Water Jetting (customers optimise around specific pump specifications; requalification cost for alternatives); low-to-moderate in Hydraulics OEM (OEMs dual-source or tender regularly).
  • Customer concentration: Management has indicated no single customer >10% of revenues — well diversified. [#I]
  • Regulation: Increasing environmental and safety regulations in industrial processes support Water Jetting demand (hygiene, pressure cleaning); emission regulations on mobile equipment are net tailwind for electro-hydraulic transition which Interpump is positioned for. [#A]

§4.5 LT Growth + Paradigm Fit [#A/#J]

  • LT organic growth potential: Hydraulics ~3-5% midcycle (tied to global industrial/agricultural capex + electrification transition); Water Jetting ~6-8% (driven by food safety regulations, industrial hygiene, global infrastructure). Group organic = ~4-6% midcycle. [#A/estimated]
  • Inorganic component: ~2-4% per year from bolt-on M&A (tracking historical pace of 1-2 acquisitions/year); contribution adds to headline revenue but with ROIC dilution risk.
  • EPS growth framework (historical): Revenue CAGR ~12% (org + M&A), EPS CAGR ~16.2% — the leverage to EPS shows some operating leverage and buyback discipline. [#A]
  • Paradigm fit: Richard's current portfolio context (risk-off, Iran conflict, avoiding cyclicals) — Hydraulics is cyclically exposed ❌; Water Jetting is more resilient ✓. Overall: Interpump is not ideal for a near-term risk-off portfolio but has strong characteristics for a 2-3 year hold through the hydraulics cycle.
  • Sceptic: The 12% revenue CAGR includes multiple M&A contributions — clean organic CAGR is likely 4-6%, not 12%. The EPS leverage is real but depends on continued capital deployment at positive spreads. [#J]

  • Interpump does not formally disclose an "aftermarket %" metric at group level.
  • Water Jetting segment (~32% of group revenue) is the aftermarket-intensive division: estimated 35-45% of Water Jetting revenue is replacement parts/consumables (ceramic pistons, seals, nozzles, high-pressure hose). This is consistent with Water Jetting segment gross margin >45% vs Hydraulics ~35-38%.

  • Estimated group-level aftermarket mix: ~20-25% of total revenue — at the lower bound of the 35-55% moat signal threshold for the cohort. This is a risk flag: Interpump is not as aftermarket-intensive at group level as the Water Jetting segment alone suggests.

CQ-B3: Industrial automation / electrification opportunity in hydraulics — structural or cyclical? [#A]

  • The electrification of mobile equipment (agricultural, construction) is structural — major OEMs (Deere, CNH, Volvo CE) have committed to electrification roadmaps for 2030+.
  • Interpump's response: Developing electro-hydraulic pump systems that pair electric motors with hydraulic actuators — this is the transition path (full electrification of heavy equipment actuators is decades away; electro-hydraulic hybrid is the near/medium-term technology).
  • Electro-hydraulic pumps market: $11.8bn (2024), growing 6.6% CAGR to 2033 ($22.9bn). Interpump cited as key player alongside Bosch Rexroth, Parker Hannifin, Eaton. [#I]
  • The hydraulics cycle downturn in 2023-24 is cyclical (agricultural equipment destocking), not structural — Hydraulics segment organic began recovering in Q3-25 (+3.4%).
  • Net assessment: Near-term demand is cyclical (trough in 2023-24, recovery in 2025); medium-term structural demand driver is electro-hydraulic transition; long-term (2035+) electrification is a potential headwind if full elimination of hydraulic actuation occurs (low probability for heavy equipment over the investment horizon). [#J]
  • Judgement: Structural growth opportunity in electro-hydraulics is real and Interpump is well-positioned; the 2024 downturn is cyclical, not structural decay. [#J] ⚡ RARE (upside signal: electrification creates demand for Interpump's electro-hydraulic offering, not just a threat)

Capital allocation discipline appears maintained but record is short (23 months). [#J]

§5 Future — Predictability, Risks, L12M Momentum
