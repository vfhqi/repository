§1 — Brief Recap + Key Components Summary

  • Net verdict: [#J] RCO is at a multi-year inflection point: FY25 marks the trough of a -33% revenue decline, but dual-market recovery (US + China) is slower and less certain than management's sequencing implies.
  • CEO change: [#I] Éric Vallat resigned Apr-25 after presiding over three consecutive years of guidance cuts; Franck Marilly (Chanel/Shiseido background) took office Jun-25, signalling a clean-slate reset rather than continuity.
  • FY2030 guidance withdrawn: [#I] The original FY2030 target (high-single-digit organic CAGR, COP margin expansion) was formally withdrawn at FY25 results Jun-25, replaced by a near-term transformation plan; long-run ambition now unanchored.
  • Revenue destruction L3Y: [#A] Group revenue fell from a peak ~€1.46bn (FY23) to €984.6m (FY25), a decline of ~33%; Rémy Martin cognac segment declined ~-40% peak-to-trough — worse than Diageo Scotch (-8% same period) or Pernod Ricard group (-14%).
  • Margin compression severe: [#A] COP margin collapsed from ~30%+ (FY22 peak) to 22.0% in FY25; net debt/EBITDA rose from 0.84x (FY23) to 2.40x (FY25), approaching levels requiring active financial management.
  • US cognac destocking largely complete: [#A] US distributor inventory overhang worked down through FY25; FY26 Q3 (Oct-Dec 25) showed four consecutive quarters of positive US depletions; US tariff (15% from Aug-25) adds a new headwind.
  • China remains structurally challenged: [#A] APAC revenues still declining double-digits in FY26 9M; China anti-dumping tariffs (32.2%) imposed Jul-25, though RCO secured minimum-price undertaking exemption; underlying gifting demand not recovered.
  • Tariff dual shock partially mitigated: [#I] US tariff impact revised down from €45m to €20m after EU-US deal (15% rate, Aug-25); China tariff partially bypassed via price undertaking; combined net COP impact ~€20m for FY26.
  • Cost restructuring overdelivering: [#A] FY25 cost savings €85m vs €50m target; cumulative two-year savings ~€230m (55% structural); total cost base -12% vs FY23.
  • Competitive position narrowing: [#A] Hennessy gained US share during the downturn (+2% depletions 2024 when RCO declined sharply), widening an already-dominant gap; RCO's premium-only strategy has constrained volume recovery options.
  • Structural demand risks real: [#I] US Black American consumers historically ~40% of US cognac consumption — trading down and migrating to whiskey/tequila; GLP-1 drug adoption adds incremental drag to high-occasion spirits consumption.
  • Sceptic close: [#J] Every recovery indicator has been present in the narrative since H1 FY24 and has repeatedly failed to materialise on time — the fulcrum question is whether FY26 recovery is a genuine inflection or a further false dawn with tariff-amplified downside.

§2 — Change in Leadership

  • Net verdict: [#J] The CEO change is the most significant structural event at RCO in a decade — Vallat's departure signals the family board's loss of patience with the premiumisation-at-all-costs approach; Marilly represents a luxury brand-building reset rather than a spirits operations fix.
  • Vallat resignation: [#I] Éric Vallat resigned Apr-9-25, acknowledged by the board immediately; tenure 2019–2025 spanned the post-COVID boom and the subsequent three-year implosion; his "patience through the cycle" narrative proved untenable with FY25 revenue at €984m vs FY23 peak €1.46bn.
  • Vallat's defining call — refusing to discount Rémy Martin VSOP to clear US overhang — directly prolonged the US destocking cycle relative to peers.
  • Three consecutive guidance reductions in FY24–FY25 appear to have been the proximate cause of board intervention.
  • Franck Marilly appointment: [#I] Marilly formally assumed CEO role Jun-25; background: 17 years at Chanel EMEA (fragrance/beauty), plus Unilever and Shiseido; no prior spirits category experience.
  • His luxury brand-building profile is directionally consistent with RCO's ultra-premium aspiration but raises execution risk in a distribution-intensive spirits business.
  • Marilly's first public framing: "offensive transformation plan" + additional cost reduction, suggesting he is not treating the portfolio as a pure organic growth story near-term.
  • Strategic roadmap reception: [#A] Market reception to Marilly's Nov-25 strategic roadmap was positive, suggesting investors view the directional change as credible; key question unanswered is whether a new FY-2030-equivalent financial anchor will be issued.
  • CFO continuity: [#I] Luca Marotta retained as CFO, providing financial continuity through the leadership transition; Marotta has been the main interface with debt markets as leverage has risen to 2.40x.
  • Family ownership dynamics: [#I] The Cointreau and Hériard-Dubreuil families retain control (~72% of capital/voting); family's willingness to replace Vallat signals accountability alignment, but also means the premium strategy core is unlikely to be abandoned — the families built Louis XIII.
  • Family board control is a constraint on M&A optionality; a trade buyer would face structural ownership overhang.
  • ❌ Spirits inexperience: [#J] Marilly's luxury credentials are strong but his complete absence of spirits distribution or three-tier US market experience is a risk for the complex US recovery task; Vallat at least brought direct spirits operating background.

§3 — Change in Strategy / Structure

  • Net verdict: [#J] The strategic direction has shifted from Vallat's patience-and-premiumisation to Marilly's offensive reset, but the core portfolio bet (cognac concentration, ultra-premium focus) remains unchanged — execution speed and geographic diversification credibility are the open questions.
  • FY2030 guidance withdrawal: [#I] At the Jun-25 annual results, RCO formally withdrew its 2029/30 long-run targets (high-single-digit organic revenue CAGR, COP margin expansion); this removes the investment case's quantitative anchor.
  • Withdrawal attributed to tariff uncertainty and unresolved China recovery timeline; no replacement long-run framework announced at FY25 results.
  • Marilly's Nov-25 strategic roadmap provided a new near-term operational framework but left multi-year financial targets open.
  • Premiumisation refocus retained: [#A] Both Vallat's FY2030 plan and Marilly's successor framework maintain the ultra-premium positioning at the top of the Rémy Martin range and Louis XIII; no mass-market pivot contemplated.
  • Validation: Louis XIII showed relative resilience in China's digital/e-commerce channels (+10% growth cited in FY25 calls) even as mid-premium cognac collapsed.
  • Risk: premiumisation concentrates revenue in high-ticket, high-occasion gifting occasions — precisely the occasions compressed by China anti-corruption and US consumer trading down.
  • Geographic diversification: [#I] Marilly's plan explicitly targets "opening up to new territories" as partial mitigation for US/China dual exposure; EMEA and travel retail called out.
  • EMEA returned to growth in FY26 Q3, driven by Cointreau and Metaxa; but these brands are small relative to the Rémy Martin cognac segment revenue weight.
  • Cost structure reset: [#A] Cumulative €230m in cost savings over FY24-25 (55% structural, -12% total cost base vs FY23); further €50m+ cost reduction announced under Marilly.
  • Structural nature of savings (not temporary marketing deferrals) suggests the organisation is genuinely smaller — positive for margin optionality when revenue returns.
  • Channel strategy — direct-to-consumer build: [#I] Louis XIII brand model shifting toward direct sales and e-commerce in China; described as "breaking with industry conventions."
  • Direct sales reduce distributor dependency and protect pricing integrity — strategically sensible but at small current scale relative to group revenues.
  • Sceptic close: [#J] Geographic diversification into EMEA and travel retail is structurally insufficient to offset dual US/China risk — Cointreau and Metaxa cannot move the group revenue needle in the near term; the strategy remains China/US-levered with a more attractive narrative around it.

§4 — Change in Priorities / Ambition

  • Net verdict: [#J] The priority shift from growth-and-scale to stabilise-and-rebuild is credible given financial reality, but the removal of quantitative long-run targets makes the investment thesis harder to underwrite; Marilly must re-anchor ambition before the stock re-rates.
  • Vallat's "patience" era: [#I] Throughout FY24 and FY25, Vallat's messaging centred on cycle patience — the view that US destocking would complete by H2 FY25 and China would normalise; both proved too optimistic in timing.
  • Key FY24 earnings language: "transitional year," "trajectory to FY2030 intact"; that language was retired by FY25 results.
  • Guidance credibility destroyed: [#A] RCO cut its FY25 guidance three times in-year (from ~-10% to ~-18% organic revenue); peer Diageo cut guidance twice in the comparable period; Pernod Ricard once. RCO's guidance track record is the weakest in the cohort.
  • Pattern: guidance set optimistically at year-start, deteriorated through quarters, cut at Q2/Q3 updates — consistent across FY24 and FY25.
  • New priorities under Marilly: [#I] Four stated priorities: (1) brand desirability investment; (2) market recovery in US/China; (3) geographic diversification; (4) cost discipline.
  • Notably absent from public framing: a new EPS target or medium-term EBITDA margin anchor; ambition is deliberately left vague at this transitional stage.
  • FY26 guidance: [#A] RCO guided organic COP decline of mid-single digits to mid-teens for FY26 (very wide range reflecting uncertainty); after tariff deal revision, mid-single-digits organic COP decline is the central case.
  • Sales guidance: stable to low-single-digit organic growth for FY26 — the first non-decline year since FY23.
  • Capital allocation — eaux-de-vie: [#I] Strategic capex in eaux-de-vie inventories (10-year aging cycle requirement) maintained even during the downturn; these are balance sheet assets (~€120m+ purchased at peak) that must be supported regardless of near-term demand.
  • Sceptic close: [#J] Marilly's wide guidance range for FY26 COP signals he does not yet have confident visibility into demand recovery in either key market; "offensive transformation" language without quantified targets is a holding pattern, not a genuine strategic pivot.

§5 — Financial Profile Change

  • Net verdict: [#J] RCO's financial profile has deteriorated faster than any peer in the spirits cohort; revenue -33% from peak, COP margin -800-900bps, leverage rising to 2.4x — the model needs US/China dual recovery to prevent further deterioration; no obvious remaining self-help lever at scale.
  • Revenue trajectory: [#A] FY22 peak ~€1.46bn → FY25 €984.6m; approximate CAGR of -12%/year for three years.
  • ❌ Vs peers: Diageo group revenues declined ~-5% cumulatively same period; Pernod Ricard ~-8%; Campari essentially flat. RCO's revenue compression is 4-6× worse than cohort median.
  • COP margin: [#A] Peak COP margin ~30%+ (FY22); FY25 COP margin 22.0% on €217m COP.
  • Cost cuts of €85m in FY25 (vs €50m target) partially cushioned; without overdelivery on costs, COP margin would have printed 19-20%.
  • Peer context: Diageo EBIT margin ~30%; Pernod Ricard ~26%; Campari ~23%. ❌ RCO at 22% has fallen below Campari for the first time in recent history.
  • Net debt / EBITDA: [#A] 2.40x at Mar-25 vs 1.68x at Mar-24 and 0.84x at Mar-23; pace of leverage increase (+1.56x in two years) is the steepest in the peer group.
  • At €675m net debt, the company is not in distress territory but has limited financial flexibility for investment or M&A.
  • Interest costs rising as fixed-rate bonds roll and floating-rate debt reprices; FY26 interest burden tracking higher year-on-year.
  • ROCE: [#A] 10.3% at Mar-25, down -5.2pp year-on-year; declining return on an expanding asset base (eaux-de-vie inventory bought at peak demand) while revenues compress.
  • FCF and dividend: [#I] FCF has been thin/negative across FY24-FY25; dividend maintained in FY25 despite earnings compression, creating further balance sheet drag.
  • Dividend sustainability: family ownership culture of maintaining dividends, but with leverage at 2.4x, covenant pressure may eventually force reassessment.
  • FY26 early read: [#A] FY26 H1 (to Sep-25) and 9M data indicate revenue returning to growth (guided stable to low-single-digit organic); COP still declining organically due to tariff headwinds and reinvestment.
  • ⚡ Cost overdelivery magnitude: [#A] €85m delivered vs €50m targeted in FY25 (70% beat); cumulative €230m over two years represents genuine structural action — no comparable cost management outperformance found in the peer cohort in the same period.
  • Sceptic close: [#J] Even if FY26 marks the revenue trough, the path back to peak COP margins requires simultaneous US volume recovery, China value recovery, and tariff normalisation — three conditions that have never yet co-existed; the probability of all three aligning in FY27-28 is lower than consensus implies.

§6 — Value Chain: Demand

  • Net verdict: [#J] US cognac destocking is substantively complete but consumer migration to whiskey/tequila is a structural headwind layered beneath the cyclical recovery; China demand recovery is delayed by anti-corruption cultural reset and consumer confidence collapse — making RCO's dual-market recovery timeline optimistic.
  • CQ-C1 — US destocking status: [#A] US distributor inventory overhang from the post-COVID surge (2021-peak at $2.1bn US imports) worked down through FY25; by Q3 FY26 (Oct-Dec 25), four consecutive quarters of positive US depletions confirmed.
  • Base effect is flattering the trend: comps become progressively harder from Q2 FY27 onward once easy comparisons expire.
  • BNIC data: US cognac import values fell from $2.1bn peak (2022) to $1.2bn (2023/24); structural consumption has declined, not just distributor inventory.
  • CQ-C2 — China recovery timeline: [#A] China/APAC revenue still down double-digits in FY26 9M (to Dec-25); anti-corruption crackdown compressed gifting occasions structurally rather than cyclically.
  • Management language shifted from "China recovery H2 FY26" to "gradual" without a specific timeline; no firm recovery inflection date provided at FY26 H1 results.
  • Government austerity messaging re-intensified in 2025, deepening the corporate gifting freeze beyond what the tariff situation alone would imply.
  • US structural demand concern: [#A] US Black American consumers — historically ~40% of US cognac category volume — are trading down to VS/VSOP from XO/premium, and migrating to American whiskey and tequila for non-celebratory occasions.
  • Cognac sales volume in US down ~41% since 2021 peak (BNIC/FEVS); even Hennessy is navigating a structurally smaller US cognac market, not simply a temporary inventory blip.
  • ❌ RCO's premium-only strategy (refusing to aggressively compete at VS/VSOP) has cost it relative volume share vs Hennessy, which maintained broader price-tier coverage.
  • European demand: [#I] EMEA returned to growth in FY26 Q3, driven by Cointreau and Metaxa; Rémy Martin cognac in Europe growing modestly but off a small base.
  • European travel retail recovering; Louis XIII benefiting from Asian tourist spend in Paris and London airports.
  • China gifting — baijiu dynamic: [#A] Premium baijiu (Kweichow Moutai) facing its own anti-gifting headwind in China, with >80% of retailers reporting decline in business gifting; cognac and baijiu compete for the same shrinking formal occasion market.
  • Baijiu is pivoting to domestic casual occasions (camping, post-work socialising) rather than directly substituting cognac on taste; the gifting occasion itself is compressing for both.
  • Sceptic close: [#J] The US destocking recovery is real but masks structural consumer migration — Hennessy is capturing disproportionate residual cognac volume; RCO's premium-only stance may mean it recovers value before volume but misses the first leg of the category volume recovery entirely.

§7 — Value Chain: Competition

  • Net verdict: [#J] Hennessy's structural dominance widened materially during the downturn; RCO is the most exposed large player by a significant margin, and within-category competition intensifies even as the category itself contracts.
  • Competitive structure: [#I] Four players share ~80% of global cognac market by volume: Hennessy , Martell (Pernod Ricard), Courvoisier (Beam Suntory), Rémy Martin .
  • Global volumes (2023): Hennessy 6.9m cases; Martell 2.3m cases; Rémy Martin estimated 1.5-1.8m cases based on public revenue data.
  • Hennessy — #1 player: [#A] Hennessy holds 62.3% of US cognac market volume (2024) and was the only top-5 brand to post positive US depletions (+2%) in 2024; LVMH's scale provides marketing leverage, distributor priority, and balance sheet to invest through cycles.
  • Hennessy's VS/VSOP price-tier breadth is its structural advantage vs RCO's premium-concentrated portfolio: Hennessy absorbs trading-down within the category; RCO cannot without a strategy reversal.
  • ❌ Hennessy reported 6% global sales growth in 2026 within LVMH results, diverging sharply from RCO's continued weakness at group level.
  • Martell (Pernod Ricard): [#A] Martell is RCO's closest premium competitor in China; Pernod's China cognac sales fell ~25% H1 FY25, confirming the China demand collapse is category-wide, not RCO-specific.
  • Pernod Ricard's scale and broader spirits portfolio gives it cross-subsidisation capacity; Martell faces the same China challenge but from a stronger parent financial position.
  • Craft and challenger cognac: [#I] Small-batch craft cognac (CAMUS, Bache-Gabrielsen) taking marginal share in EMEA on provenance and direct-sales narratives; not yet material at global scale but relevant for RCO's higher-margin EMEA channel.
  • D'Ussé (celebrity premium): [#I] D'Ussé — the celebrity-endorsed premium cognac that surged post-2020 — fell 18% in 2024 to 525k cases; its collapse signals that the "premium celebrity" demand wave that lifted all cognac boats is fully deflated.
  • Sceptic close: [#J] Hennessy's ability to grow share in a declining category is the most dangerous competitive signal — it implies the category recovery, when it comes, may accrue disproportionately to #1; RCO's premium concentration provides limited structural defence against this dynamic.

§8 — Value Chain: Disruption

  • Net verdict: [#J] Disruption risks for RCO operate across three concurrent vectors — US category occasion migration, GLP-1 behavioural effects, and China substitution — none of which are resolved by a cyclical recovery alone; individually manageable, cumulatively significant.
  • US whiskey and tequila migration: [#A] American whiskey and tequila have captured celebration/occasion share historically owned by cognac among younger US Black consumers; migration reflects both price normalisation and flavour preference shifts.
  • Premium tequila ($25+ bottles) grew >12% in 2023-24 even as cognac fell; American craft whiskey growing double digits; both categories are taking on-premise share from cognac.
  • ❌ This is not a temporary trade-down — surveys indicate younger US cohorts (21-35) have lower stated preference for cognac as a category vs their predecessors; a structural cohort replacement problem.
  • GLP-1 weight-loss drugs: [#A] GLP-1 drugs (Ozempic/Wegovy analogues) reduce alcohol consumption by 40-75% in users via dopamine suppression; third-party data indicates 50% reduction in drinking occasions among GLP-1 patients.
  • Spirits consumption falls ~40% among GLP-1 users who reduce alcohol; within spirits, high-occasion premium formats (cognac, single malt) are disproportionately exposed because they index to celebratory occasions reduced first.
  • Current US GLP-1 penetration ~7-10m users and growing; the demand drag is small today but directionally adverse and compounding over time.
  • No/low alcohol: [#I] No/low alcohol beer and wine growing double digits; non-alcoholic spirits emerging but not yet displacing premium cognac at scale; trend more relevant for casual occasion spirits than for high-occasion gifting formats like Louis XIII.
  • ⚡ US cognac volume decline — historically anomalous: [#A] US cognac shipments down 41% since 2021 peak; the pace and persistence is historically anomalous relative to prior US cognac cycles (post-2008 recovery took 2-3 years; this cycle is now 3+ years with structural characteristics); either structural break or extraordinary recovery opportunity depending on the thesis adopted.
  • China baijiu dynamic: [#A] Baijiu and cognac share the formal Chinese gifting occasion; government anti-gifting crackdowns suppress both simultaneously; no direct flavour/product substitution between the two, but they compete for the same shrinking gifting budget in corporate settings.
  • Sceptic close: [#J] GLP-1, no/low alcohol, and occasion migration are individually small effects but all directionally aligned against high-occasion premium spirits; their combined impact on secular demand growth has not been fully priced into the recovery narrative at any spirits company.

§9 — Value Chain: Political / Regulatory

  • Net verdict: [#J] The political/regulatory environment is the single most binary risk factor for RCO in the 18-month forward window; both the US tariff situation and the China anti-dumping regime carry scenario tails that could add €40-60m incremental COP drag beyond current guidance if diplomatic conditions deteriorate.
  • US tariffs — current state: [#A] US imposed 25% tariff on EU goods initially (Apr-25); subsequent EU-US deal (Aug-25) settled at 15% base rate on EU goods including cognac.
  • RCO's estimated net COP impact revised down from €45m to €20m after the deal; the company is absorbing part via pricing and mitigating via investment reallocation.
  • ❌ Spirits trade bodies (DISCUS in US, SPIRITS EUROPE in EU) explicitly pushed for full cognac exclusion; the 15% compromise is better than 25% but is not zero — every unit of tariff is a direct COP headwind into already-weak demand.
  • Risk tail: the 15% rate is a temporary truce, not a binding agreement; any re-escalation (US elections, EU retaliation) would reopen the cost-impact discussion immediately.
  • China anti-dumping tariffs: [#A] China imposed 32.2% anti-dumping duties on EU brandy from Jul-5-25 (final ruling, five-year term); Hennessy, RCO, and Pernod Ricard secured minimum-price undertaking exemptions — exempt if they sell at or above MOFCOM-agreed floor prices.
  • The exemption provides meaningful protection for RCO's China business; the floor price constraint simultaneously limits promotional flexibility and volume recovery speed.
  • The tariffs are formally a retaliation against EU EV duties on China; their duration is geopolitically contingent, not commercially resolved.
  • EU filed WTO challenge against China's brandy tariffs (DS631); resolution timescales typically 2-4 years and uncertain.
  • French manufacturing and regulatory cost: [#I] RCO's Cognac operations are France-based; French labour costs rose materially 2022-24; structural savings have partly absorbed this but margin recovery requires revenue recovery and cost-base stability simultaneously.
  • EU alcohol regulation: [#I] EU Health Union initiative has explored minimum unit pricing and advertising restrictions; France has resisted aggressive implementation; no material near-term regulatory change but directional pressure on spirits advertising budgets.
  • China anti-corruption structural impact: [#I] Xi Jinping's ongoing anti-corruption campaigns (intensified 2022-25) have structurally compressed corporate gifting spending; this is policy-driven demand destruction that cannot be resolved by distribution or pricing actions at the company level.
  • Sceptic close: [#J] The China tariff exemption and US tariff reduction are genuine positive developments but both are conditional and revocable; the geopolitical foundation of both remains unstable; a single diplomatic escalation event could reverse months of financial guidance revision within hours.

§10 — Value Chain: Supply Side

  • Net verdict: [#J] RCO's supply side is paradoxically a structural strength in the long run (owned vineyards, strategic eaux-de-vie inventory) but a source of near-term financial drag and strategic inflexibility in a demand downturn of this depth.
  • Aging cycle rigidity: [#I] Cognac requires minimum 2-year oak aging; XO requires 10 years minimum; Louis XIII requires decades of aging in Tierçon barrels — production decisions today reflect demand expectations a decade hence.
  • This supply rigidity means RCO cannot rapidly adjust eaux-de-vie purchases without either creating future supply shortfalls or further stressing the balance sheet with idle stock.
  • Strategic eaux-de-vie purchases: [#A] RCO invested €120m+ in strategic eaux-de-vie stock purchases at near-peak market prices; these sit on the balance sheet as fixed assets generating no revenue during the downturn, contributing directly to ROCE decline (10.3% FY25 vs 18%+ at peak).
  • The strategic logic is sound (preserving future production quality) but the timing of purchases at peak prices is unfortunate against the subsequent demand collapse.
  • 2024 harvest — poor conditions: [#I] The 2024 Charentes vintage was severely impacted by high rainfall and mildew pressure; grape production fell ~35% from expected levels.
  • Irony: during maximum demand depression, a supply disruption emerges — when/if demand recovers strongly in 2028-30, the 2024 vintage shortfall will create a production constraint precisely when it is least wanted.
  • Grande Champagne vineyard ownership: [#I] RCO owns vineyards in the premier cru Grande Champagne appellation — the highest-quality cognac terroir; provides long-run differentiation but is expensive to maintain during a downturn.
  • Charentes industry rationalisation: [#A] The Charentes region entered the downturn with excess production relative to demand; BNIC data confirms vineyard area is actively being restructured downward as growers exit the market under low-price pressure.
  • Long-term industry supply rationalisation is constructive for RCO's future pricing environment but takes 3-5 years to play through the system.
  • Sceptic close: [#J] The supply side is RCO's structural competitive moat in the long run but a balance sheet burden in the short run; the 2024 vintage shortfall adds a supply uncertainty tail at precisely the moment when demand recovery visibility is most needed for the investment case.

§11 — Revenue / Demand Cycle

  • Net verdict: [#J] Cognac is the most cyclical category in the spirits cohort; RCO is positioned at what management calls the trough, but the dual-driver nature of the cycle (US distributor + China gifting) means recovery is structurally more complex and slower than a single-market destocking cycle; the FY26 guidance of stable to low-single-digit revenue growth is a meaningful step but insufficient to underwrite a re-rating.
  • Cycle anatomy: [#A] The cognac cycle follows a specific pattern: post-event demand surge → distributor overstocking → demand normalisation → distributor destocking → 12-18 month gap between depletions and shipments recovery.
  • The 2021-2023 US surge was unusually large (fiscal stimulus + on-premise reopening + celebrity culture); the subsequent destocking has therefore been unusually deep (-33% revenue for RCO vs typical cycle of -10-15% for 1-2 years).
  • US cycle position: [#A] The consensus view (shared by RCO management) is that US distributor destocking is complete as of H2 FY25; FY26 Q3 (Oct-Dec 25) confirmed four consecutive quarters of positive US depletions.
  • Base effect is flattering: comps become harder from Q2 FY27 onward; the underlying depletions growth rate (ex-base effect) is modest, not explosive.
  • The BNIC/FEVS data shows US import values at $1.2bn in 2023/24 vs $2.1bn peak — structural consumption has declined; the recovery ceiling may be well below the prior peak.
  • China cycle position: [#A] China is structurally more challenged than the US: demand compression driven by (a) anti-corruption policy suppressing gifting occasions, (b) slower economic growth reducing aspirational consumption, (c) EU-China trade tensions, and (d) property market wealth destruction.
  • Management moved from "China recovery H2 FY26" to "gradual" without a date; the China/APAC segment was still declining double-digits as of FY26 Q3.
  • There is no historical precedent for the speed of China cognac recovery following an anti-corruption demand collapse of this magnitude and duration.
  • Dual-cycle simultaneity risk: [#A] The fact that US destocking and China demand collapse occurred simultaneously is the core reason RCO's revenue decline has been so much more severe than peers with geographic diversification.
  • ❌ The probability that US and China both recover strongly in the same 18-month window — the implicit recovery thesis — is low based on historical precedent for dual-market simultaneous recovery.
  • FY26 guidance: [#I] Organic revenue: stable to low-single-digit growth; COP organic: mid-single-digit decline (central) to mid-teens (downside). Wide range signals limited management conviction on the demand recovery timing.
  • Sceptic close: [#J] Even in a bull case where US returns to +5% volume growth and China stabilises, RCO's path back to FY23 revenue levels is a 3-5 year journey given structural consumer shifts in both markets; the stock's valuation must be assessed against that elongated timeline and the uncertainty around it.

§12 — Value Chain: Macroeconomic

  • Net verdict: [#J] Macro headwinds are compounding the cycle — USD weakness vs EUR, CNY area consumer confidence weakness, and French operational cost inflation all net negative for RCO's reported financial delivery; the FX environment is uniformly unfavourable across both key markets.
  • USD / EUR: [#A] RCO generates ~40%+ of revenue in USD (US market) and reports in EUR; EUR strength in 2025 has been a direct drag on reported revenue when USD revenues translate.
  • Every 10% USD depreciation vs EUR reduces RCO reported revenue by approximately 4-5% holding volume constant.
  • US tariff discussions have added volatility to USD/EUR expectations; trade uncertainty typically strengthens EUR as a haven, which is doubly negative for RCO.
  • CNY / EUR: [#I] China revenue is partially CNY-denominated; CNY has been relatively stable vs EUR, but Chinese consumer confidence and household wealth effects (property market deflation 2023-25) are suppressing luxury spirits demand independently of spot FX.
  • Property market wealth destruction in China directly reduces the consumer propensity to spend on cognac gifting — a non-FX demand headwind with no easy macro policy fix.
  • French manufacturing cost: [#A] Cognac production costs in France include labour (French minimum wage CPI-linked, rose sharply 2022-24) and energy; cost savings programme has partially offset inflation but structural cost inflation in France continues at a rate above European peers.
  • Interest rates: [#I] RCO's €675m net debt is partly floating-rate; ECB rate cuts from mid-2024 onward provide a modest tailwind on interest costs; not a material swing factor but marginally helpful at the current leverage level.
  • China macro — broader context: [#A] China GDP growing at ~4-5% officially but youth unemployment (reported >20%) and property sector deflation are suppressing middle-class consumer spending; cognac is a beneficiary of Chinese wealth effects on the upswing and amplified victim on the downswing.
  • Travel retail macro: [#I] Global travel retail has normalised post-COVID; Chinese outbound tourism recovering but not yet at 2019 levels; travel retail is a growth channel for Louis XIII in EMEA airports where Asian tourist spend concentrates.
  • Sceptic close: [#J] The macro environment (weak USD, suppressed CNY consumer confidence, high French costs) is uniformly unfavourable for RCO's reported financial delivery in FY26; even if operational trends improve organically, the macro translation effect may suppress reported numbers and investor perception of the recovery quality.

§13 — Historical Track Record of Delivery
