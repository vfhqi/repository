3. PART A — FAILURE NARRATIVES

§2 — SCENARIO 1: Oil-Price Shock / IOC Capex Retrenchment

Failure narrative: Brent falls to and sustains below $65-70/bbl — the empirically observed threshold at which major IOCs reactivate capex-deferral playbooks. Subsea project FIDs (Final Investment Decisions) stall, contract awards decelerate, and DOFG's order intake falls 30-40% within two quarters. Transmission mechanism: IOC capex → FID rate → contract awards → DOFG revenue backlog → EPS, with a 6-18 month lag at each stage meaning the EPS impact lands 12-24 months after the capex decision, precisely within the 18M-3Y hold window.

Early warning signals:
- Brent spot sustained below $70 for ≥6 weeks accompanied by futures curve contango
- IOC capex-guidance reductions at quarterly earnings (any 2 of Equinor, Shell, BP, TotalEnergies)
- Rystad / Wood Mackenzie FID-pipeline data showing ≥15% YoY decline in sanctioned deepwater projects
- DOFG contract award press releases going quiet for ≥2 consecutive quarters
- Peer day-rate bid spreads widening on tender submissions (vessel utilisation softening)

Plausibility: High plausibility — peer-precedent base rate is unambiguous; 2014-16 and 2019-20 cycles both saw offshore service revenues drop 30-50% within 18 months of sustained $60-70/bbl Brent; 2022-24 recovery has been narrated as structurally different but the underlying mechanism (IOC capex = discretionary at the margin) is unchanged

M/M/O test:
- Means ✓ — macro oil-price shock is exogenous and fully within range of historical volatility
- Motive ✓ — IOCs have explicit shareholder-return frameworks prioritising buybacks over capex when IRR thresholds fall
- Opportunity ✓ — DOFG's revenue mix is concentrated in project-cycle work (not maintenance) which is first-cut
- All three legs aligned — full failure scenario, not watch-only

Disconfirming evidence: Multi-year take-or-pay contracts with high cancellation penalties already locked in covering ≥60% of 2026-27 revenue would invalidate this; if NOC (Equinor, Petrobras) commitments are structurally ring-fenced from IOC capex discretion, failure probability is reduced (partial disconfirmation only — NOC capex is also oil-price sensitive with 12-18M delay)

Monitoring checklist:
- Brent 6-week rolling average vs. $70 threshold
- DOFG book-to-bill ratio quarterly (warn below 0.9x)
- Equinor Norwegian Continental Shelf capex guidance (DOFG's home market)
- Global deepwater FID count per quarter (Rystad public data)
- Fleet utilisation rate for comparable DSV/construction vessels

Interaction with other failure modes: Directly correlated with Scenario 3 (day-rate compression) and Scenario 7 (FALSE FRIEND backlog plateau); if oil price shock triggers capex retrenchment, scenarios 1+3+7 cascade sequentially within 18 months

Sceptical bullet: What would invalidate this? Contractual revenue protection covering ≥70% of 2026-28 revenue at locked day-rates — without that anchor, this failure mode is the baseline capex-cycle hypothesis, not an outlier

§3 — SCENARIO 2: Norwegian Continental Shelf Customer Concentration

Failure narrative: DOFG's revenue base is heavily concentrated on the Norwegian Continental Shelf with Equinor as the dominant single customer; if Equinor reduces its subsea maintenance/modification programme (due to mature-field economics, carbon transition capex re-allocation, or internal restructuring), DOFG loses the demand anchor that has historically provided utilisation floor. Transmission mechanism: Equinor programme spend → DOFG utilisation floor → day-rate discipline in NCS market → EBITDA margin → EPS.

Early warning signals:
- Equinor NCS operational capex guidance cut by ≥10% YoY
- Equinor announcing accelerated decommissioning schedules for mature NCS fields
- DOFG revenue concentration — if top-3 customers exceed 60% of revenue, any single programme cut is material
- Competitive tendering re-emergence for contracts previously awarded on preferred-supplier basis
- Equinor public statements on energy transition capital reallocation toward renewables

Plausibility: Medium plausibility — NCS is a structurally advantaged market (regulatory stability, long-field lives, Equinor's domestic political mandate) but customer concentration risk is real; peer analogue is Subsea 7's exposure to Petrobras concentration in the Brazil cycle of 2014-16, where one customer's programme changes triggered a 40% revenue revision

M/M/O test:
- Means ✓ — Equinor can restructure its NCS programme unilaterally
- Motive ✓ — energy transition pressure and shareholder returns create motive for capex rationalisation
- Opportunity ✓ — contract renewal cycles provide natural re-allocation moments
- All three legs — full failure scenario

Disconfirming evidence: Long-term preferred-supplier framework agreements with Equinor extending through 2028+ would partially disconfirm; structural NCS regulatory requirements for ongoing field maintenance (not discretionary) provide partial floor — but maintenance-only revenue at floor rates generates insufficient EBITDA to justify current equity valuation

Monitoring checklist:
- Equinor NCS capex line in quarterly reports
- DOFG disclosed customer concentration (annual report)
- NCS licence renewal and development activity (Norwegian Petroleum Directorate public data)
- Competitive bid activity on historically sole-sourced contracts
- Equinor renewable capex allocation percentage

Interaction: Correlated with Scenario 1 (if oil price falls, Equinor's NCS investment thesis weakens simultaneously); partially independent of competitive/structural scenarios (NCS has high barriers to entry, limiting new entrant risk)

Sceptical bullet: The disconfirming evidence here is contractual lock-in — if the analyst cannot produce evidence of multi-year framework agreements with Equinor at defined volume/rate floors, this scenario cannot be dismissed

§4 — SCENARIO 3: Day-Rate Compression — Cycle-Top Reversal

Failure narrative: Current day-rate levels in the DSV/construction vessel market reflect cyclically tight supply (cold-stacked vessel attrition 2016-2021, newbuild hesitation) rather than structurally improved pricing power; as demand softens and/or cold-stacked capacity reactivates, day-rates compress 15-25% from current levels, delivering operating leverage in reverse — EBITDA margins compress disproportionately because fixed costs (vessel OpEx, crew, maintenance class) are largely invariant. Transmission: day-rate → revenue per vessel day → EBITDA per vessel day → aggregate EBITDA → EPS.

Early warning signals:
- Day-rate bid spreads widening on newbuild or reactivated-vessel tenders
- Any cold-stacked vessel reactivation announcements by peers (Subsea 7, Saipem, Technip Energies)
- DOFG disclosed average day-rate moving average declining QoQ
- Vessel utilisation rates softening from >90% toward 80-85% threshold
- New entrant vessel capacity entering NCS/North Sea market (flag-of-convenience vessels on European-spec projects)

Plausibility: High plausibility — day-rate cycle-top reversion is the most well-documented failure mode in offshore services; base rate across 1996-2024 shows multiple cycles with 20-40% peak-to-trough day-rate moves; current consensus narrative is "this time structurally tighter" but same narrative was active in 2012-2014 before the 2015-16 collapse

M/M/O test:
- Means ✓ — capacity reactivation is capital-light relative to newbuild; peers have financial incentive
- Motive ✓ — elevated day-rates create economic incentive for capacity reactivation
- Opportunity ✓ — timing coincides with 2026-28 project FID pipeline maturing demand
- All three legs — full failure scenario

Disconfirming evidence: If fleet-age data shows cold-stacked vessels are genuinely beyond economic reactivation (>15 years, major class survey due, corrosion), supply-side reactivation is structurally constrained; if newbuild orderbook is genuinely near-zero and lead times are 3+ years, supply response is delayed beyond hold period — both would partially disconfirm; Watson has not seen this data, flags as DD data priority

Monitoring checklist:
- Clarkson / Bassoe Offshore cold-stacked vessel count and age profile (public)
- Industry day-rate indices for North Sea/NCS DSV class
- Peer vessel fleet additions / reactivations
- DOFG contract rate disclosures (where available)
- Newbuild orderbook at major yards (South Korean / Chinese)

Interaction: This is the direct mechanistic link between Scenario 1 (capex retrenchment) and EBITDA outcomes; also feeds into Scenario 8 (FALSE FRIEND margin expansion that reverses); tightly correlated cluster with Scenarios 1, 2, 7, 8

Sceptical bullet: The "structurally tighter supply" narrative has been the consensus view at every prior cycle top; what specific fleet-age and reactivation-cost data makes this cycle different — and is that data in the investment case?

§5 — SCENARIO 4: Operating-Leverage Inversion / Cost Inflation

Failure narrative: DOFG's EBITDA margin expansion narrative is premised on positive operating leverage — revenue growing faster than the largely-fixed cost base of vessel operations. Cost inflation (crew wages, fuel, maintenance classification costs, insurance) accelerates above revenue growth, inverting the operating leverage thesis. Norwegian crew wages (among the highest globally) and North Sea maritime labour market tightness create structural cost pressure that is not hedged by contract escalation clauses. Transmission: input cost inflation → EBITDA margin compression → EPS miss vs. consensus.

Early warning signals:
- Norwegian maritime wage settlements (annual, public) exceeding CPI by >2pp
- Fuel costs (LSMGO/VLSFO) sustained >10% above prior-year average without vessel day-rate escalation clauses
- DOFG disclosed EBITDA margin declining QoQ despite stable/growing revenue
- Class-survey timing concentration — if multiple vessels are due class simultaneously, one-off capex inflates operating cost
- Maintenance CapEx guided higher by management (often a leading signal of operating cost pressure deferred to CapEx)

Plausibility: Medium plausibility — Norwegian labour costs are structurally elevated vs. global peers (20-30% premium estimated from public wage data), and CBA renegotiations occur regularly; however, DOFG likely has escalation clauses in major Equinor framework contracts that partially pass through costs — the key unknown is clause coverage

M/M/O test:
- Means ✓ — exogenous cost inflation is outside DOFG's control
- Motive N/A — cost inflation is a macro condition, not an intentional act
- Opportunity ✓ — contract structures without full pass-through create vulnerability window
- Two legs (Means + Opportunity) — downgrade to watch-only unless analyst confirms absence of cost pass-through clauses

Disconfirming evidence: If DOFG's Equinor framework agreements include full cost-escalation clauses indexed to Norwegian maritime wages and fuel, this scenario is materially disconfirmed on the margin compression mechanism (though revenue growth story could still be impaired separately)

Monitoring checklist:
- Annual Norwegian maritime/offshore CBA settlement outcomes
- DOFG's EBITDA-margin trend relative to revenue growth (operating leverage ratio)
- Fuel cost per vessel-day (estimated from public fuel indices + vessel class specs)
- Vessel class-survey schedule (annual report fleet section)
- Peer (Subsea 7, DOF Subsea) cost-per-vessel-day disclosures

Interaction: Partially correlated with Scenario 3 (day-rate compression + cost inflation is the lethal combination); interacts with Scenario 9 (R&D/greentech underinvestment as cost is cut to protect margin)

Sceptical bullet: Operating-leverage thesis requires costs to be genuinely fixed; if Norwegian maritime labour costs are structurally rising 4-6% annually, DOFG needs 5-8% annual revenue growth just to hold margin — is that baked into consensus EPS?

Failure narrative: DOFG's operational turnaround (if thesis-dependent on a specific management team) is disrupted by departure of CEO or CFO; offshore services companies with concentrated operational knowledge in a small leadership team face material execution risk at succession because relationships with major customers (Equinor preferred-supplier dynamic) are partially personal/reputational. Plausibility: Low-to-medium plausibility as standalone failure mode — management departure is a real risk in any mid-cap but is rarely the primary EPS transmission mechanism unless the company is in active operational turnaround where execution is leadership-dependent; without specific intelligence on DOFG's current management stability, base rate for mid-cap Norwegian CEO departure within any 3-year window is ~25-30% (general corporate governance data)

M/M/O test:

- Two-of-three unless compensation/tenure data confirms Motive — watch-only

Disconfirming evidence: Long-dated equity incentive structures with continued-service requirements through 2027+ would reduce this risk materially; evidence of deep second-tier management bench (deputy CEO, operational directors) providing continuity

Monitoring checklist:

- LinkedIn tenure signals for C-suite and VPs

Interaction: Partially correlated with Scenario 6 (M&A integration); in isolation, low interaction with macro scenarios

Failure narrative: DOFG pursues acquisitive growth (vessel acquisition, competitor consolidation, or subsea-services bolt-on) at cycle-top valuations, overpaying because the offshore services market consensus is bullish; integration costs, fleet-overlap inefficiencies, or cultural/operational mismatch impair EBITDA margins and distract management from organic execution. Early warning signals:

Plausibility: Medium plausibility — offshore services sector has a poor historical record on M&A; Technip-FMC (2017) and Saipem's multiple restructurings are well-documented failure anchors; cycle-top M&A (2012-2014 vintage) consistently destroyed value; probability elevated if DOFG is generating strong cash flow and management is incentivised on growth metrics

M/M/O test:
- Means ✓ — cash generation and debt markets provide acquisition capacity
- Motive ✓ — management incentive structures often include scale/revenue targets
- Opportunity ✓ — cycle top creates willing sellers and lender appetite
- All three legs — full failure scenario

Monitoring checklist:

Interaction: Correlated with Scenario 5 (management distraction); can interact with Scenario 1 (post-acquisition leverage leaves balance sheet exposed to capex downcycle)

§8 — SCENARIO 7 (FALSE FRIEND #1): Backlog Growth as Cycle-Top Signal

Failure narrative (FALSE FRIEND CLASS): Order backlog grows strongly, providing consensus with a "revenue visibility" narrative that is interpreted as confirming thesis execution; however, backlog growth at cycle top reflects project FIDs sanctioned under elevated oil price assumptions that are 12-18 months behind current market conditions. When those FIDs are re-evaluated or deferred, cancellation options are exercised, and apparent revenue visibility collapses suddenly. The dangerous FALSE FRIEND dynamic: backlog growth looks like confirmation but is actually a lagging indicator of cycle-peak sanctioning that front-runs a demand cliff. Transmission: apparent backlog → consensus revenue/EPS upgrades → share price appreciation → then FID cancellations → sharp consensus EPS downgrades → de-rating.

Early warning signals:
- Backlog growth driven by letters of intent / conditional awards rather than fully-executed contracts
- Increasing proportion of backlog from NOC counterparties with weaker contractual commitments
- Book-to-bill ratio elevated but awards concentrated in 1-2 large multi-year contracts (concentration risk within backlog)
- FID sanctioning rate for projects not yet in backlog declining (forward-looking signal)
- Oil price declining while backlog number remains stable (dissociation between leading and lagging indicators)

Plausibility: High plausibility as FALSE FRIEND failure class — this mechanism operated precisely in 2013-2015 in offshore services; Subsea 7 and TechnipFMC both reported record backlogs in Q3 2014 while the capex cycle was already turning; the lag between cycle peak and backlog recognition was 9-18 months; consensus was still upgrading EPS as the cycle peaked

M/M/O test:
- Means ✓ — backlog reporting conventions allow inclusion of conditional awards
- Motive ✓ — management has incentive to present backlog positively (compensation, credit metrics)
- Opportunity ✓ — project accounting and backlog definition vary and are not audited on the same basis as revenue
- All three legs — full FALSE FRIEND scenario

Disconfirming evidence: If backlog is exclusively composed of fully-executed, take-or-pay contracts with high early-termination penalties, backlog-as-lagging-indicator risk is reduced; if cancellation penalty data shows ≥20% of contract value as termination fee, conversion risk is lower — but these contractual specifics are rarely in public disclosure

Monitoring checklist:
- Backlog quality: letter-of-intent vs. firm-contract breakdown (management disclosure)
- Cancellation-penalty disclosures in annual report notes
- Backlog-to-revenue conversion ratio (how much of opening backlog converts to recognised revenue each year)
- Peer backlog-revision announcements (industry-wide signal)
- FID pipeline data for projects not yet in backlog

Interaction: This is the gateway FALSE FRIEND scenario — if Scenario 7 triggers (backlog misread), it cascades into Scenarios 1, 2, and 3 within 12-18 months; it is the most dangerous because it generates analyst overconfidence precisely when deterioration is accelerating

Sceptical bullet: The disconfirming evidence is contractual quality of backlog — and if that contractual quality is opaque in public filings, the inability to disconfirm is itself a warning signal

§9 — SCENARIO 8 (FALSE FRIEND #2): Margin Expansion from CapEx Deferral / R&D Underinvestment

Failure narrative (FALSE FRIEND CLASS): DOFG's EBITDA margin improves, which is interpreted as operating-leverage confirmation; however, the margin improvement is driven by deferral of vessel-maintenance capex, reduction in R&D/technology investment, and fleet-renewal avoidance — all of which improve near-term EBITDA at the cost of future competitive position and vessel regulatory compliance. The False Friend: EBITDA margin expansion looks like thesis confirmation but is actually a balance-sheet-consuming, future-competitiveness-impairing short-term optimisation. Transmission: deferred capex/R&D → immediate EBITDA improvement → then catch-up capex spike + competitive capability gap → FCF collapse + day-rate discount vs. peers → multi-year EPS impairment.

Early warning signals:
- Maintenance capex declining as a percentage of revenue for 2+ consecutive years
- Vessel age profile rising without corresponding new-vessel commitment
- Technology/digital capability gap vs. peers widening (subsea robotics, autonomous inspection, carbon-reporting tooling)
- Class-survey deferrals (vessels operating on extended survey periods — a direct capex-deferral signal)
- EBITDA margin expanding while FCF conversion declines or is flat (capex suppression elevates EBITDA but FCF should converge — if it doesn't, capex is being capitalised or deferred)

Plausibility: Medium-to-high plausibility — capex deferral as margin-management mechanism is well-documented in offshore services; Siem Offshore, Solstad Offshore, and multiple smaller NCS operators used this exact mechanism 2015-2020 before it generated balance-sheet crises; the risk is elevated in a cyclical upturn because management may prioritise reporting clean EBITDA margins to support equity re-rating

M/M/O test:
- Means ✓ — maintenance capex deferral is within management discretion
- Motive ✓ — equity compensation linked to EBITDA margin or EV/EBITDA multiple provides direct incentive
- Opportunity ✓ — vessels can typically operate 12-24 months beyond scheduled maintenance without immediate regulatory breach
- All three legs — full FALSE FRIEND scenario

Disconfirming evidence: If DOFG's capex-to-revenue ratio is stable or rising (not compressing), capex-deferral signal is absent; if annual report fleet section shows all class surveys current with no deferrals, near-term risk is reduced; FCF conversion in line with EBITDA conversion would also reduce the signal

Monitoring checklist:
- Maintenance capex / revenue ratio annually
- Fleet class-survey schedule (annual report)
- R&D expense as % of revenue (year-on-year)
- FCF vs. EBITDA conversion ratio
- Peer comparison of capex intensity (Subsea 7, DOF Subsea if public data available)

Interaction: Directly correlated with Scenario 4 (cost inflation — deferred maintenance catches up as a cost spike); interacts with Scenario 3 (vessel-quality gap leads to day-rate discounts vs. better-maintained competitor fleet)

Sceptical bullet: EBITDA margin expansion in offshore services is most credible when accompanied by rising maintenance capex intensity — if margins expand AND capex intensity declines simultaneously, the analyst is looking at a False Friend by definition

§10 — SCENARIO 9: Technology Disruption / Autonomous Subsea Systems

Failure narrative: Autonomous underwater vehicles (AUVs), remotely operated vehicle technology advances, and AI-driven subsea inspection systems reduce the vessel-time required for inspection, maintenance, and repair work — DOFG's core service category; over a 3-5 year horizon, vessel-day demand per unit of subsea infrastructure maintained declines, structurally impairing utilisation assumptions underpinning the investment thesis. Transmission: AUV/autonomous IMR adoption → vessel-day demand reduction per installed subsea base → utilisation ceiling decline → day-rate ceiling compression → long-run EPS impairment.

Early warning signals:
- IOC/NOC announcements of AUV-based IMR trials on NCS (Equinor has publicly disclosed digital subsea programmes)
- Technology-company subsea robotics funding rounds at scale (>$50M Series B/C)
- DOFG or peer day-rate plateauing even as conventional IMR demand appears stable (utilisation efficiency gains already baking in)
- Equinor / Shell publicising vessel-day productivity improvements in IMR operations
- Academic/industry white papers on NCS autonomous inspection standards (regulatory pathway for AUV-based certification)

Plausibility: Low-to-medium plausibility within the 18M-3Y hold window — technology substitution in subsea IMR is real but the regulatory certification cycle (DNV GL, Health and Safety Executive approval for autonomous IMR on critical subsea infrastructure) creates a 5-10 year adoption lag from current technology capability; vessel-day demand will not compress materially within 3 years; but the risk is that the market begins discounting the long-term structural threat within the hold period, impairing the exit multiple

M/M/O test:
- Means ✓ — technology capability is advancing
- Motive ✓ — IOCs have cost-reduction incentive to adopt
- Opportunity Partial — regulatory certification timeline limits near-term opportunity
- Two-of-three (regulatory constraint limits Opportunity) — watch-only for 18M-3Y hold

Disconfirming evidence: Long regulatory certification cycles for autonomous IMR on safety-critical NCS infrastructure provide a structural disconfirmation of near-term technology substitution; if current DOFG IMR contracts are for work where AUV substitution is most difficult (complex intervention, light well intervention) rather than simple inspection, risk is further reduced

Monitoring checklist:
- Norwegian Petroleum Safety Authority (Ptil) and DNV GL regulatory guidance on AUV IMR certification
- Equinor / Aker BP technology announcements on autonomous subsea
- Subsea technology conference proceedings (Underwater Technology Conference, Subsea Tieback)
- Technology capex in peer DOFG and Subsea 7 annual reports
- Patent filings by ROV/AUV competitors in key IMR task categories

Interaction: Low interaction with short-cycle macro scenarios (1, 2, 3); medium interaction with Scenario 8 (if DOFG underinvests in its own technology, competitor AUV capability widens relative gap faster)

Sceptical bullet: Technology disruption of offshore services is a real long-run risk that the offshore services consensus has dismissed repeatedly — the question is whether it is within the 3-year hold window or beyond; if it is beyond, it is an exit-multiple risk rather than an EPS risk

§11 — SCENARIO 10: Competitive New Entrant / Asian Flag-of-Convenience Vessels

Failure narrative: A new entrant operating under Asian flag-of-convenience registration (lower crew cost) or a European competitor reactivating cold-stacked capacity enters the NCS/North Sea DSV market at below-market rates to win utilisation; existing contractual barriers (Jones Act equivalent does not apply in NCS; NORSOK standards create some barrier but are not absolute) are lower than consensus assumes; DOFG loses tenders and/or is forced to concede day-rate, with utilisation declining from >90% toward 80-85% — a threshold at which offshore services EBITDA leverage turns sharply negative. Transmission: new entrant capacity → DOFG utilisation decline → day-rate pressure → negative operating leverage → EBITDA compression → EPS miss.

Early warning signals:
- Asian or Southern European DSV flagging announcements for North Sea deployment
- NCS IMR tenders with unusually low awards relative to prior rounds
- DOFG losing tenders to non-traditional competitors
- Vessel-tracking data showing non-NCS vessels entering North Sea patterns (AIS data, public)
- Equinor procurement announcements broadening approved-vendor lists

Plausibility: Low-to-medium plausibility — NCS has genuine regulatory and certification barriers (NORSOK, NOROG, Equinor vendor qualification) that take 12-24 months for new entrants to navigate; however, the 2022-24 cycle upturn has incentivised international operators to pursue NCS qualification; base rate from 2005-2010 NCS upturn shows 2-3 new entrant vessels per cycle, sufficient to add 5-8% capacity at the margin

M/M/O test:
- Means ✓ — vessel supply is available globally
- Motive ✓ — elevated NCS day-rates are attractive to international operators
- Opportunity Partial — regulatory barriers slow but do not prevent entry
- Two-and-a-half legs — elevated watch-only, not full failure scenario within 18M; full scenario risk at 3Y horizon

Disconfirming evidence: If NCS qualification requirements are genuinely 24+ months and Equinor's vendor qualification list is closed, near-term competitive entry is constrained; if DOFG's vessel fleet has specific NCS capability (dynamic positioning class, North Sea winterisation, NORSOK-certified equipment) not easily replicated, competitive moat is real

Monitoring checklist:
- NCS vessel traffic / AIS tracking for non-DOFG DSV activity
- Equinor and Aker BP approved-vendor announcements
- DNV vessel class register for NCS-qualified DSV additions
- Day-rate tender outcomes vs. prior rounds
- Competitor press releases on NCS market entry intent

Interaction: Correlated with Scenario 3 (day-rate compression) — new entrant is one of several mechanisms that could trigger Scenario 3; low interaction with macro scenarios 1 and 2

Sceptical bullet: The NCS regulatory moat is real but not absolute — the disconfirming test is whether Equinor's procurement actively maintains or actively opens its vendor qualification list

§12 — SCENARIO 11: Leverage / Balance Sheet Stress in Downcycle

Failure narrative: DOFG carries vessel-secured debt with covenant structures tied to vessel utilisation, day-rate levels, or EBITDA coverage ratios; in a cyclical downturn (triggered by Scenarios 1, 3, or 7), covenant breach triggers a forced refinancing at distressed terms, accelerates amortisation schedules, or limits operational discretion (dividend suspension, capex freeze); equity value is disproportionately impaired by balance-sheet stress even in a moderate demand decline. Transmission: revenue/EBITDA decline → covenant breach → forced refinancing cost → EPS impairment + equity dilution risk from equity cure provisions.

Early warning signals:
- Net debt/EBITDA approaching covenant threshold (typically 3.5-4.5x for offshore services vessel-secured debt)
- Revolving credit facility utilisation rising without corresponding asset-backed collateral
- Vessel impairment test results approaching book value (disclosure in annual report note)
- Rating agency outlook revision to negative
- Bond/credit spread widening on any listed DOFG debt instruments

Plausibility: Medium plausibility — offshore services companies with vessel-secured debt have historically been vulnerable to this mechanism (2016-2018 restructuring wave in NCS was largely covenant-driven); risk is elevated if DOFG has not fully deleveraged from prior cycle; partially mitigated if current cash generation has reduced leverage toward 1-2x range

M/M/O test:
- Means ✓ — leverage exists (to be confirmed by analyst from balance sheet)
- Motive N/A — covenant breach is a structural mechanism, not intentional
