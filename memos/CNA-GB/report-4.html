  • Sub: SSE adjusted EPS grew ~7% in FY25; National Grid adjusted EPS grew ~5-6%; Centrica is the clear earnings decliner in the peer group over L24M

  • Sub: Drax group EPS also declined materially in FY24-25 as renewable CfD pricing and biomass costs compressed, making Centrica not uniquely exposed

  • Reported vs adjusted: The gap between reported and adjusted EPS has been material — FY23 reported EPS included significant fair value gains on commodity positions; FY25 includes mark-to-market losses as prices reversed [#A]

  • Sub: The adjusted EPS figure strips out commodity fair value movements, in-line with sector practice (E.ON, SSE use similar adjustments), but the gap is wider for Centrica given the commodity book size

  • Sell-side revisions: Public consensus FY25 adjusted EPS estimates have been revised down approximately 15-20% over L12M as commodity prices softened — magnitude of downgrade is above the peer group median (SSE revisions ~flat, National Grid ~flat to slightly up) [#A] ❌

  • Sub: The downgrade cycle appears to be substantially complete; FY26-27 estimates show positive revision momentum of ~5-8% on Services growth and buyback completion

  • Beat/miss vs guide: Over the L4 semi-annual reporting periods (FY23H1, FY23H2, FY24H1, FY24H2), Centrica has delivered within or marginally above guidance in 3 of 4 periods — a solid delivery record vs the pre-2022 era of persistent guidance cuts [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: A significant portion of the EPS support over FY23-24 came from buybacks + commodity fair value timing effects; the FY26-27 EPS growth story depends on Services mix and cost savings — both of which have delivery risk; if Services growth slows to 2-3% and cost savings are offset by wage inflation, FY26 EPS could disappoint consensus by 10-15% without any commodity deterioration [#J]

§7 — Sector & Value Chain Context

Net signal: Centrica's earnings reset is substantially sector-driven (commodity normalisation post-2022 supercycle) rather than idiosyncratic; the structural UK energy transition is creating a medium-term reinvestment opportunity that Centrica is moderately well-positioned to participate in [#J]

  • Sector revenue: UK integrated energy sector revenue declined broadly in FY24-25 as wholesale prices normalised; E.ON, SSE, and Centrica all reported lower reported revenues; regulated peers (National Grid, UK Power Networks) were the exception [#A]

  • Sector margin: Sector-wide EBIT margins are compressing in commodity-exposed businesses and stable-to-expanding in regulated/network businesses — a bifurcation that explains most of the cross-peer performance gap observed in §6 [#A]

  • Sub: This bifurcation is structural, driven by regulatory framework differences, not a temporary or cyclical phenomenon

  • Sector revisions: Sell-side consensus for UK utilities broadly was revised down 10-15% for commodity-sensitive names in FY25 and flat-to-slightly-up for regulated names — Centrica's downgrade is consistent with sector-wide commodity price assumption changes [#A]

  • Supplier signals: Gas procurement costs have declined materially with wholesale prices, benefiting Centrica's and E.ON's energy supply margins — this is a partial offset to revenue decline and explains why retail supply margins have not collapsed [#A]

  • Sub: UK gas supply margin (the "spark spread" equivalent) has been approximately £40-60/MWh in FY25 vs a historic norm of £20-30/MWh — still above normalised levels, suggesting retail margin has not fully compressed yet

  • Customer signals: UK household energy demand is structurally declining (~1-2% p.a.) due to heat pump adoption and efficiency — a secular headwind that all UK energy retailers face and none have structurally solved [#A]

  • Sub: British Gas's home services penetration of ~3.8m customers (vs ~7m retail energy customers) suggests meaningful cross-sell opportunity if transition to low-carbon heat accelerates

  • End-market growth: The UK home services market (boiler, heat pump, solar installation) is growing ~4-5% p.a. — Centrica is the only major listed UK player with direct consumer services exposure at scale [#A]

  • Competitive intensity: UK energy retail is intensely competitive; Octopus Energy (private, ~£3bn+ valuation) has grown from ~100k to ~4m UK customers in 5 years using a tech-led model — a genuine disruptive threat to British Gas's market position [#A] ❌

  • Sub: Centrica's response (Hive smart home platform, digital metering rollout) is strategically sound but execution speed has been slower than Octopus's iteration pace

  • Cyclical vs structural — disaggregation:

  • Cyclical: Commodity earnings compression is cyclical; if gas prices recover to £150-200p/therm (vs ~90p currently), Centrica's earnings would recover materially — this is a cyclical tail, not permanent destruction [#A]
  • Structural positive: UK energy transition (heat pump installation, solar, EV charging) is a structural growth market where Centrica/British Gas has installation capability and ~7m customer relationships — a credible structural growth vector [#A]

  • Structural negative: British Gas retail energy customer share erosion to digital-native competitors is a structural negative; the rate of loss has moderated but the direction has not reversed [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The "structural growth" narrative around UK energy transition is compelling but the execution timeline is uncertain — NESO (National Energy System Operator) 2035 heat decarbonisation targets are aspirational; actual heat pump installation rates in FY25 remain below government targets; if the transition timeline slips, Centrica's Services growth vector loses conviction [#J]

§8 — Quality of Earnings & Accounting

  • NWC days: Working capital position in energy supply is structurally volatile due to seasonal gas hedging and collateral requirements; Centrica's disclosed NWC movements show improvement from FY22 peak (when collateral postings were extraordinary) to a more normalised ~30-40 days equivalent, broadly in line with E.ON UK's disclosed range [#A]

  • Sub: The ~£2bn of collateral posted in FY22 during the gas price spike has been substantially released, improving NWC quality

  • Cash conversion: OCF/adjusted EBIT has averaged ~115% over FY23-24, above the utilities sector median of approximately ~100-105% — a genuine positive [#A]

  • Sub: The superior cash conversion reflects British Gas retail's working capital dynamics (customers pay in advance on direct debit) — structurally repeatable

  • OCF vs EPS: Operating cash flow per share broadly tracks adjusted EPS over the L3Y, with only modest divergence in FY22-23 due to commodity position unwinding — a positive quality signal [#A]

  • One-offs: Management makes significant adjustments to arrive at "adjusted operating profit": excludes fair value commodity mark-to-market, restructuring costs (~£200m over FY23-25), and Spirit Energy decommissioning charges [#A]

  • Sub: The adjustments are disclosed and audited but the aggregate adjusted-vs-IFRS gap has been material (£300-600m in some periods); Richard should track this gap over time

  • Non-GAAP gap: The non-GAAP adjustment magnitude for Centrica is wider than SSE (~£100-200m adjustments) and comparable to E.ON group — the commodity fair value adjustment is the primary driver and is genuinely volatile rather than a persistent earnings quality concern [#A]

  • Adjustments — decommissioning: Spirit Energy (North Sea upstream) carries significant decommissioning liabilities (~£1.5-2bn undiscounted); the accounting for these is complex and changes in discount rate assumptions can create material P&L volatility not captured in "adjusted" figures [#A] ❌

  • Sub: This is an off-balance-sheet-adjacent risk that SSE and National Grid do not carry; it is a Centrica-specific earnings quality headwind

  • Inventory days: Gas storage inventory at Rough (if reinstated as a storage facility) would add balance sheet volatility; current storage operations appear limited; inventory timing differences are not a material NWC distortion at this stage [#A]

  • Receivables days: British Gas retail receivables are relatively low given direct debit penetration (~75-80% of customer base on direct debit); aging concerns are limited vs the 2021-22 period when energy cost-of-living pressures increased default rates [#A]

  • Peer norms: Centrica's cash conversion is at or above peer medians; its non-GAAP adjustment magnitude is wider than SSE but consistent with E.ON UK; the Spirit Energy decommissioning liability is a unique accounting complexity with no close peer equivalent [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The strong cash conversion narrative could reverse if British Gas retail customers shift away from direct debit (e.g., in response to affordability pressures), or if commodity positions require additional collateral posting in a price spike — both are tail risks that are low-probability but would be high-impact to working capital [#J]

§9 — Sceptical Synthesis (Cross-Cutting)

Verdict: The strongest bearish case for Centrica is structural market share erosion in British Gas retail meeting commodity price stability, eliminating both the recovery catalyst and the Services growth vector simultaneously — leaving a medium-cap utility with below-peer earnings growth and above-peer cyclicality [#J]

  • Reverse-engineered bear: The entire FY26-27 investment case rests on three concurrent assumptions: (1) Services EBIT margin expansion continues, (2) cost savings are not offset by wage inflation, (3) commodity prices do not fall further [#J]

  • Sub: All three are independently plausible, but their simultaneous realisation is far from certain; a 2-of-3 failure scenario could reduce FY27 EPS by 20-25% vs consensus

  • Single largest risk: British Gas retail customer attrition to Octopus/digital-native competitors accelerating, which would reduce the cross-sell base for Services growth and increase unit cost of customer acquisition — the Services "captive customer" advantage degrades [#J]

  • Sub: Octopus grew from ~2m to ~4m UK customers in 2 years; if it reaches 6-7m (approaching British Gas's base), the competitive intensity in home services would increase materially

  • Early warning signals: Three leading indicators to watch: (1) British Gas monthly churn rate (disclosed in periodic results); (2) Services customer net adds quarterly trajectory; (3) NBP/TTF forward curve [#A]

  • Sub: FY25 H1 churn was reportedly stable; Services net adds were modestly positive — neither yet signals deterioration, but the trend requires monitoring
