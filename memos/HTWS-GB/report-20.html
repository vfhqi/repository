2. KEY FINDINGS — §1

§ 1 — Executive Summary: Top-3 Most-Diagnostic Questions

  • DIAGNOSTIC: The three questions that most sharply discriminate bull from bear are (1) tenancy-ratio trajectory by market — specifically whether Ghana/Tanzania are inflecting or stalling; (2) the true all-in cost of debt re-financing in 2025-27 against a structurally higher USD rate environment; and (3) whether the pipeline of MNO co-location demand is contractually committed or merely indicative

  • DIAGNOSTIC: Tenancy-ratio inflection is the single most load-bearing variable — each 0.1x improvement on a ~6,000-tower estate at $1,000-1,500 average incremental revenue per tenancy is a ~$6-9m annualised EBITDA tailwind; management's answer on cadence here discriminates the "5yr FCF compounder" from the "capital sink" hypothesis

  • DIAGNOSTIC: Refinancing cost is structurally underappreciated by consensus — HTWS carries substantial USD-denominated tower-company bonds issued at sub-7% coupons; refinancing risk in 2025-2028 at current USD rates is a silent P&L lever that management will not volunteer unless pushed; CFO's answer cadence on this is itself signal

  • DIAGNOSTIC: Currency translation and devaluation remain the most frequent bear-case trigger — Nigerian Naira, Ghanaian Cedi, and Tanzanian Shilling have all devalued materially since 2021; the question of whether USD-denominated contracts or escalator clauses fully neutralise this, or merely defer the pain, is the crux

  • CONTEXT: Regulatory and spectrum dynamics in South Africa / Oman / Madagascar are less consensus-tested — HTWS has expanded into markets where regulatory frameworks for passive infrastructure sharing are still maturing; management's read on country-by-country regulatory posture is less rehearsed and therefore more diagnostic

  • SCEPTICAL OPENER: The questions the analyst most wants NOT to ask are the ones probing whether management's market-entry ROIC assumptions for newer markets (DC, Oman, Madagascar) have already been revised downward post-entry — Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 1: go directly there

3. THEME §1 — Revenue Drivers + Visibility

J-front verdict

  • VERDICT: Revenue-side uncertainty is moderate-to-high — contracted tenancy backlog provides a floor, but conversion timing and MNO capex cycles make near-term tenancy ratio improvement difficult to model with conviction; this is the most important theme

Q1 — Tenancy ratio inflection cadence

Question: "In Ghana and Tanzania specifically, at what point in 2025-2026 do you expect tenancy ratios to inflect above 1.7x — and what are the two or three preconditions that need to be true for that inflection to happen on schedule?"

  • Expected answer: Management will cite MNO network rollout timelines, spectrum assignment completions, and existing signed co-location agreements as the preconditions; they will likely express confidence in H2 2025 or 2026 inflection
  • Good answer signature: Specific, contract-anchored, names the MNO counterparties and spectrum bands, and quantifies the committed pipeline in towers or sites
  • Bad answer signature: Vague reference to "continued strong demand" without naming contracts, timelines, or counterparties; hedging on MNO spending decisions
  • Follow-up probe: "Of the co-location agreements signed in the last 12 months in those two markets, what percentage have a committed installation date within 18 months?"
  • Diagnostic note: A specific, contract-anchored answer supports the bull case (visible EBITDA step-up); a vague or hedged answer is the strongest single indicator for the bear case ("tenancy plateau"); cross-references Q19 ACH pre-mortem Item #1 — tenancy ratio stall in core markets

Q2 — Volume vs price split in lease escalators

Question: "For FY2025, can you split your expected service revenue growth between (a) volume — new tenancies — and (b) price — escalators on existing leases? What percentage of leases carry a USD or USD-linked escalator?"

  • Expected answer: CFO will indicate a mix of ~60-70% volume-driven and ~30-40% price/escalator; most leases in USD or USD-linked markets (South Africa, Oman) will carry CPI or fixed escalators; management will likely say >70% of revenue is USD-denominated or USD-indexed
  • Good answer signature: Precise split with specific escalator percentages; clear separation of hard-currency vs local-currency exposure by market
  • Bad answer signature: Conflation of USD-denominated revenue with USD-PROTECTED revenue; inability to split volume vs price growth with specificity
  • Follow-up probe: "For the portion of leases NOT in USD or USD-linked currencies, do escalators fully cover local CPI — or is there a real-terms revenue erosion risk in high-inflation markets?"
  • Diagnostic note: If >30% of leases carry local-currency escalators that underrun CPI, the "currency-neutral growth" narrative is partially false; this probe directly tests Q19 ACH matrix false-friend hypothesis (headline growth masks real-terms erosion)

Q3 — Customer concentration and MNO counterparty risk

Question: "What percentage of group revenue comes from your top two MNO customers — and if either reduced capex by 30% for 12 months, what is the direct revenue impact and what contractual protections exist?"

  • Expected answer: Top-2 likely represents 40-55% of revenue; management will cite take-or-pay structures and long-term MSAs as protection; they will note that revenue is not at-risk even if MNO capex slows because existing tenancies are contracted
  • Good answer signature: Quantified concentration figure + explicit reference to take-or-pay floors with minimum committed revenues over the contract term
  • Bad answer signature: Deflection toward "we have long-term contracts" without quantifying the floor; inability to distinguish between contracted recurring revenue and contingent new-business pipeline
  • Follow-up probe: "Have any top-5 MNO customers invoked force majeure, re-negotiated, or exited a contract in the last 36 months?"
  • Diagnostic note: Customer concentration with genuine take-or-pay floors is a moat characteristic; concentration without hard floors is a hidden churn vector; cross-refs Q19 ACH Item #3 — MNO consolidation/MVNO substitution

Q4 — Forward order book quality

Question: "How large is the signed-but-not-yet-installed tenancy pipeline today, and what is the average historical lag between signing and revenue recognition — and has that lag lengthened in the last 18 months?"

  • Expected answer: Pipeline of several hundred signed tenancies awaiting installation; lag of 3-9 months typical; management may acknowledge that permitting delays or MNO equipment supply chain issues have extended lag in some markets
  • Good answer signature: Specific pipeline figure, average lag quantified, and explicit acknowledgement of where lag has extended with a credible explanation
  • Bad answer signature: No specific pipeline figure; lag characterised as "normal" without data; denial of any extension in installation timelines
  • Follow-up probe: "In which specific markets has the signing-to-installation lag extended, and what remediation steps have been taken?"
  • Diagnostic note: Elongating lag without explanation signals either MNO commitment quality deterioration or operational execution issues; both are bear-case relevant

Sceptical bullet: A fluent answer from management on tenancy pipeline and escalator structure may reflect a well-rehearsed IR narrative rather than operational confidence — probe for the specific number of sites where installation has been delayed >12 months; the non-answer to THAT sub-question is the most diagnostic signal

4. THEME §2 — Margin Trajectory + Levers

J-front verdict

  • VERDICT: EBITDA margin trajectory is the most mechanically predictable part of the investment case — but the power loss cost structure and fuel cost exposure are underweighted in consensus models

Q5 — Gross margin drivers: fuel / power cost trajectory

Question: "Ground lease costs and power/fuel together represent the majority of your direct costs — can you break down the 2024 cost-per-tower for each, and what is your expectation for each by end-2026?"

  • Expected answer: Ground lease costs are largely fixed and predictable (escalating with local CPI); fuel costs are the volatile element, driven by diesel pricing, grid reliability, and hybrid/solar penetration; management will likely cite cost-per-tower reduction from energy-transition capex
  • Good answer signature: Specific cost-per-tower for fuel (likely $3,000-8,000/tower/year depending on grid reliability) with a credible glide path to reduction via solar/hybrid rollout, with a % of tower estate already hybrid/solar
  • Bad answer signature: Aggregate cost figures without disaggregation; vague reference to "energy efficiency initiatives" without a cost-per-tower anchor
  • Follow-up probe: "In the markets with the lowest grid reliability (highest diesel dependency), what is the payback period on your hybrid/solar capex, and when does it become economic vs continued diesel?"
  • Diagnostic note: If fuel cost reduction is the margin lever, the question is whether the capex to achieve it has already been committed; unspent energy-transition capex is an underappreciated source of future FCF compression

Q6 — Operating leverage: what does the next 1,000 tenancies look like in margin terms?

Question: "If you add 1,000 net new tenancies in 2025, what is the incremental EBITDA margin on those tenancies — and how does that compare to the blended portfolio margin today?"

  • Expected answer: Incremental tenancy margin should be structurally higher than blended margin (classic tower-company operating leverage) because tower infrastructure costs are largely fixed; management will likely quote incremental margins of 70-80%+
  • Good answer signature: Specific incremental margin figure, broken down by market type (existing-tower co-location vs new-build), with a clear distinction between the two
  • Bad answer signature: Inability to disaggregate incremental from blended margin; conflation of gross and EBITDA margin on incremental tenancies
  • Follow-up probe: "What proportion of the 2025 new tenancy expectation comes from co-locations on existing towers vs new-build deployments — and what is the margin differential?"
  • Diagnostic note: If management cannot or will not provide incremental margin data, it raises the question of whether new-build capex is being deployed in markets where co-location economics are weaker than implied; cross-refs Q19 ACH Item #5 — margin dilution from new-market entry

Q7 — FX pass-through and real EBITDA margin

Question: "Reported EBITDA in USD has been growing, but local-currency devaluations in Nigeria and Ghana have been severe — can you quantify the FX headwind to reported EBITDA in 2023 and 2024, and what does the same analysis look like at current FX rates for 2025?"

  • Expected answer: CFO will acknowledge meaningful FX headwind (likely $20-40m+ on a reported EBITDA of ~$400-450m); will point to USD-denominated or USD-linked revenue contracts as the offset; will note that NGN devaluation was a specific 2023-24 event rather than a recurring drag
  • Good answer signature: Explicit quantification of FX headwind by year and by market; clear explanation of the contractual USD-linkage that offsets translation exposure at the revenue line
  • Bad answer signature: Deflection to "we are largely USD-denominated" without quantifying the residual local-currency exposure; inability or unwillingness to put a dollar figure on the 2023-24 FX drag
  • Follow-up probe: "If the Naira depreciates a further 20% from current levels, what is the mechanical EBITDA impact in reported USD — and do your contracts re-set immediately or with a lag?"
  • Diagnostic note: This is the most direct test of whether "USD-denominated" revenue is operationally real or contractually nominal; the false-friend hypothesis (USD contracts with local enforcement gaps) is tested here

Q8 — Capex pacing: maintenance vs growth, and any deferral?

Question: "In 2024, how much of your capex was (a) maintenance/lifecycle, (b) new-build, (c) energy transition/hybrid — and has any maintenance capex been deferred to manage near-term FCF?"

  • Expected answer: Management will provide a capex bucket split; will likely deny any systematic maintenance deferral; energy transition capex is likely growing as a share of total
  • Good answer signature: Specific three-way split with dollar figures; explicit statement on maintenance capex per tower vs industry norms; clear articulation of the energy-transition capex programme timeline
  • Bad answer signature: Inability to split the three buckets; defensive response to the deferral question; any indication that "maintenance" is defined narrowly to manage the FCF narrative
  • Follow-up probe: "What is your target maintenance capex per tower per year, and how does that compare to peers — and has the average age of your tower estate changed in the last three years?"
  • Diagnostic note: Maintenance capex deferral is a classic FCF-flattering short-term tactic in capital-intensive businesses; the age-of-estate question is the diagnostic cross-check

5. THEME §3 — Capital Allocation + Balance Sheet

J-front verdict

  • VERDICT: Balance sheet risk is the most underdiscussed component of the Helios investment case — leverage at ~5x EBITDA with USD-denominated bonds maturing in 2026-2028 creates a refinancing exposure that the equity story depends on being benign; this is BB#2 RA3 territory and deserves maximum scrutiny

Q9 — Refinancing timeline, cost, and rate sensitivity

Question: "Walk me through your debt maturity profile over 2025-2028 — what volume of bonds need to be refinanced, at what coupon, and what is your current assumption for the refinancing rate on each tranche?"

  • Expected answer: CFO will outline bond maturities (HTWS had ~$750m 7% 2025 notes and ~$650m 7.875% 2026 notes in prior filings); will reference either already-completed refinancing activity or a stated refinancing plan; will likely indicate that market conditions are manageable
  • Good answer signature: Specific maturity dates and volumes; realistic current-market coupon assumption for each tranche; explicit sensitivity — "if refinancing rate is X% rather than Y%, the annual interest cost increases by $Z"
  • Bad answer signature: Vague comfort ("we have good banking relationships"); inability to quantify the rate differential between existing and likely refinancing coupons; dismissal of refinancing as a material concern
  • Follow-up probe: "If you refinanced all maturing debt today at current USD high-yield market rates, what is the incremental annual interest expense vs your current run-rate — and what does that do to your EPS trajectory?"
  • Diagnostic note: This is the single most important CFO-specific question; a 2-3% rate increase on $1.4bn of bonds is $28-42m of additional annual interest — equivalent to wiping out 1-2 years of EBITDA growth; cross-refs Q19 ACH Item #2 — refinancing shock

Q10 — Leverage target and deleveraging path

Question: "What is your target net leverage ratio over the next 3 years — and what is the specific path to get there? Is it organic EBITDA growth, asset disposals, or equity?"

  • Expected answer: Management will cite a target of ~4-4.5x net debt/EBITDA; will indicate that organic EBITDA growth is the primary deleveraging mechanism; will likely rule out equity issuance as dilutive
  • Good answer signature: Specific leverage target with a year-by-year glide path; explicit sensitivity on what happens to leverage if EBITDA comes in 10% below budget
  • Bad answer signature: Target leverage stated without a credible path; reliance on "EBITDA growth will take care of it" without specifics; any reference to leverage ratios that use adjusted EBITDA with large add-backs
  • Follow-up probe: "If you're at [current leverage] and EBITDA grows 10% in 2025, but interest expense also increases due to refinancing, what does leverage look like on a net basis — does it actually improve?"
  • Diagnostic note: RONIC discipline question — are management deploying capital in a leverage-neutral or leverage-increasing manner? Cross-refs false-friend hypothesis: "EBITDA compounding with no FCF conversion"

Q11 — M&A philosophy and return thresholds

Question: "For a tower acquisition at 15x EBITDA in a new market, what minimum tenancy ratio and annual tenancy-ratio improvement would you need to see to clear your investment hurdle — and what is that hurdle?"

  • Expected answer: Management will cite a mid-to-high-teens IRR hurdle; will indicate that entry tenancy ratios of ~1.2-1.3x with a credible path to 2.0x+ over 5-7 years are typical deal parameters; will likely reference prior acquisitions as benchmarks
  • Good answer signature: Specific IRR or ROIC hurdle stated clearly; sensitivity to entry multiple vs tenancy ratio; explicit reference to how the last acquisition has tracked vs its entry model
  • Bad answer signature: Vague "value-accretive" language; refusal to name the IRR hurdle; no reference to how prior acquisitions have tracked vs underwriting assumptions
  • Follow-up probe: "For the [most recent acquisition], what was the underwritten tenancy ratio improvement vs what has actually been achieved — and if there is a gap, what caused it?"
  • Diagnostic note: The most diagnostic M&A question is not about the next deal — it's about whether the last deal tracked its model; management that has clean track records on post-acquisition execution are qualitatively differentiated from those who habitually underperform entry models

6. THEME §4 — Strategy + Competitive Position

J-front verdict

  • VERDICT: Helios's competitive moat in core markets is genuine but geographically bounded — the question is whether expansion into DC/Oman/Madagascar creates value at group level or dilutes management focus and ROIC at the margin

Q12 — Adjacency expansion: data centres

Question: "You've entered the data centre market in some African markets — what is the strategic logic, and how do you prevent this from being a capital-intensive distraction from your core tower business?"

  • Expected answer: Management will frame DC as a natural extension of their power infrastructure expertise and fibre/connectivity position; will argue it serves the same MNO/enterprise customers; will likely characterise it as a small, high-return adjacency
  • Good answer signature: Clear articulation of the specific customer overlap; DC capex as a proportion of total capex (should be small); explicit return hurdle for DC investments separate from tower returns
  • Bad answer signature: Vague "digital infrastructure" framing without specific DC economics; inability to articulate how DC competes with hyperscaler infrastructure; any suggestion that DC is being pursued primarily for re-rating optics
  • Follow-up probe: "What EBITDA does your DC portfolio generate today, and what return on invested capital are you achieving vs the tower portfolio?"
  • Diagnostic note: The false-friend hypothesis here is "optionality premium with no current economics"; if DC represents capital deployment at sub-tower ROIC while management attention is consumed, it is a net negative at DD depth

Q13 — Competitive response: MNO in-sourcing risk

Question: "In markets where you have >60% tower market share, is there any evidence that MNOs are considering building their own towers rather than leasing — and what would change their calculus?"

  • Expected answer: Management will argue that the economics of tower ownership are clearly inferior for MNOs at current spectrum/capex intensities; will cite the global trend toward MNO tower divestment as evidence of structural demand
  • Good answer signature: Specific analysis of MNO build-vs-buy economics with a cost-per-tower comparison; reference to any contracts that explicitly prevent MNOs from building competing infrastructure in specific markets
  • Bad answer signature: Dismissal of in-sourcing risk as "not a real threat" without engaging with the economics; no reference to what triggers a change in MNO build-vs-buy calculation
  • Follow-up probe: "Has any MNO in any of your markets announced a new tower-building programme in the last 24 months — and if so, how have you responded?"
  • Diagnostic note: MNO in-sourcing is the most underappreciated long-term threat to the tower-company moat; the question is whether the moat is structural (regulation, economics, scale) or contingent (MNO balance sheets currently constrained)

7. THEME §5 — Management Quality + Operator Track Record

J-front verdict

  • VERDICT: Management credibility is mixed — the CEO's operational track record on tower expansion is solid, but the capital markets track record (leverage management, FX hedging, bond issuance timing) is less clearly differentiated from a "riding the sector tailwind" profile; the CFO tenure and incentive alignment deserve scrutiny

Q14 — Sandbagging discipline: guidance vs delivery

Question: "In each of the last four years, how has your full-year EBITDA guidance at the start of the year compared to actual outturn — and what were the one or two items that most frequently caused you to miss or beat?"

  • Expected answer: Management will likely characterise guidance as conservative and outturn as typically at the upper end or slightly above; will cite FX and country-level operational items as the main variance drivers
  • Good answer signature: Honest quantification of guidance vs outturn by year; explicit identification of recurring miss factors rather than blaming unique/one-off items each time
  • Bad answer signature: Deflection to "we guided a range and hit the range" without examining directional bias; any pattern of consistently blaming FX as the miss driver without explaining why guidance didn't incorporate FX sensitivity
  • Follow-up probe: "Have you ever had to revise guidance downward mid-year — and if so, was the market given sufficient advance notice, or was the revision concentrated close to period end?"
  • Diagnostic note: Mid-year guidance revision pattern is the single best behavioural indicator of whether management guides conservatively or optimistically; the bear case on management quality is: "perpetual positive guidance with negative FX surprise as the exit"

Q15 — Power of incentives: CEO/CFO compensation structure

Question: "What percentage of your personal annual compensation is tied to (a) EBITDA delivery, (b) FCF delivery, (c) tenancy ratio improvement, and (d) leverage reduction — and has that mix changed in the last two years?"

  • Expected answer: Management may be somewhat guarded; will likely cite a combination of EBITDA and total shareholder return metrics; FCF and leverage may be secondary metrics
  • Good answer signature: Specific percentages across multiple metrics; explicit inclusion of FCF or leverage in the LTI structure; evidence that metric weights have been reviewed post-FY2023 FX pressure
  • Bad answer signature: Vague "shareholder value" framing; heavy weighting to EBITDA vs FCF (incentivises EBITDA-maximising capex decisions that destroy FCF); absence of leverage metric in a highly-levered company
  • Follow-up probe: "If you hit your EBITDA target but miss FCF due to higher interest costs and working capital outflows, does your compensation still pay out at target?"
  • Diagnostic note (Power of Incentives — Concept A): EBITDA-only compensation structures in levered businesses are dangerous — they incentivise EBITDA growth that may be FCF-destructive; the answer here is load-bearing for the investment thesis

Q16 — Crisis playbook: the 2023 Nigeria Naira devaluation response

Question: "When the Naira devalued sharply in mid-2023, walk me through the specific decisions you made — what contracts did you renegotiate, what costs did you cut, and what did you learn about your hedging programme's adequacy?"

  • Expected answer: Management will outline the USD-linked contract structure that provided protection; may acknowledge some residual local-currency cost exposure; will reference hedging improvements post-event
  • Good answer signature: Specific, decision-level narrative with dollar impacts; honest acknowledgement of what the hedging programme did NOT protect; concrete changes made to the programme post-event
  • Bad answer signature: Scripted narrative that emphasises protection without quantifying the residual damage; inability to name specific decisions made vs prior plan; suggestion that the USD-contract structure fully neutralised the devaluation
  • Follow-up probe: "In the 12 months following the Naira devaluation, did your Nigeria EBITDA in USD terms increase, decrease, or stay flat — and can you disaggregate the organic vs FX component?"
  • Diagnostic note: Crisis response quality is the most diagnostic management-quality test available; the 2023 Naira event is a live, auditable case study; management that can narrate it with specificity and self-awareness score higher on operator quality

8. THEME §6 — Risk + Downside (Pre-mortem Cross-ref)

J-front verdict

  • VERDICT: The highest-plausibility failure modes are FX/refinancing in combination — a simultaneous Naira/Cedi devaluation AND a 200bp refinancing spread widening would likely require an equity raise; neither individually is priced as a severe tail risk by consensus

Q17 — Bear-case probe: FX + refinancing combined stress

Question: "Stress-test for me: Naira -30%, Cedi -25%, USD high-yield spreads +200bp — what does that do to your (a) reported EBITDA, (b) net debt/EBITDA, and (c) FCF?"

  • Expected answer: Management will likely acknowledge the scenario is severe; will point to USD-contract protections at the EBITDA level; will acknowledge the interest expense impact of spread widening; will likely argue that covenant headroom remains adequate
  • Good answer signature: Specific numerical output for each of the three impact lines; explicit reference to covenant tests and headroom under the stress scenario; a clear statement of what management actions would be triggered
  • Bad answer signature: Refusal to engage with the combined stress; any suggestion that "our contracts protect us" without quantifying the residual exposure; inability to state the covenant test levels
  • Follow-up probe: "In that stress scenario, at what point does the board consider an equity raise — and what is the threshold metric that would trigger that conversation?"
  • Diagnostic note: The equity raise trigger question is the most important risk question in the memo; if management cannot or will not answer it, the investor must assume the threshold is opaque and therefore un-hedgeable

Q18 — Regulatory exposure: tower-sharing mandates

Question: "In your markets, are any regulators considering mandatory tower-sharing or wholesale access regulations that could compel you to offer capacity at regulated rates — and how are you positioning for that risk?"

  • Expected answer: Management will note that mandatory sharing regulations are present in several markets (South Africa's ICASA framework, various SSA regulatory reviews); will argue that regulated sharing prices have not been materially below market to date; will indicate active engagement with regulators
  • Good answer signature: Market-by-market assessment of regulatory risk; specific reference to any current proceedings; clear articulation of the economic impact if regulated rates are imposed
  • Bad answer signature: Dismissal of regulatory risk as "not a current concern"; inability to name the specific regulatory proceedings underway; any suggestion that tower companies are immune to price regulation because they are "passive infrastructure"
  • Follow-up probe: "If a regulator imposed a 15% reduction in the rates you can charge for co-location in one of your top-three markets, what is the EBITDA impact and what contractual or operational response is available?"
  • Diagnostic note: Regulatory pricing risk is the least-modelled downside scenario in sell-side coverage; it is also a scenario where management's lobbying effectiveness is a genuine moat differentiator

Q19 — False-friend probe: reported metrics vs economic reality

⚡ NON-OBVIOUS ANGLE — this question is not in the consensus sell-side interview bank

Question: "If you stripped out all non-cash adjustments — including straight-lining of ground lease escalators, D&A on right-of-use assets, and capitalised maintenance — what does your cash P&L look like, and how different is it from reported EBITDA?"

  • Expected answer: CFO will be somewhat guarded; will acknowledge IFRS 16 right-of-use asset treatment; may note that adjusted EBITDA includes lease costs as an add-back; will defend reported metrics as industry-standard
  • Good answer signature: Willingness to walk through the specific reconciliation items; specific dollar figure for the difference between reported EBITDA and a cash P&L; acknowledgement that some adjustments are defensible (e.g. IFRS 16) and others are more discretionary
  • Bad answer signature: Refusal to engage with the cash P&L concept; defensive reference to "industry standard" metrics without acknowledging that industry standards in tower companies have historically been designed to flatter optics
  • Follow-up probe: "What does unlevered free cash flow per tower look like — after all capex, including maintenance — and how has that trended over three years?"
  • Diagnostic note: Tower-company adjusted EBITDA metrics have been the subject of significant controversy in the sector (cf. SBA Communications, Crown Castle debates on adjusted vs unadjusted FCF); the false-friend hypothesis is that HTWS's EV/EBITDA appears cheap because the EBITDA denominator is flattered

- The ACH item for "MNO consolidation reducing counterparty count below diversification threshold" is touched in Q3 but not stressed as a standalone failure mode
- The ACH item for "management team departure / succession risk" is not directly addressed — the §5 questions probe current management quality but not bench depth

9. §8 — Cross-Cutting Meta + Non-Obvious Questions

J-front verdict

  • VERDICT: The questions management is LEAST prepared for are the ones that cross-cut reported metrics with cash economics — any question that requires the CFO to reconcile reported EBITDA with unlevered FCF per tower will surface the most diagnostic information

Non-obvious angles

⚡ OUTLIER Q20 — "The 20% capex cut" question (WH Smith archetype)

  • Question: "If your board instructed you to cut capex by 20% starting tomorrow — no new-builds for 12 months — which projects would you kill first, and what would that do to your FY2025 tenancy ratio?"
  • Why non-obvious: This question is never asked in consensus meetings; it forces management to reveal the NPV hierarchy of their capex programme and implicitly discloses which projects are marginal vs core
  • Diagnostic note: If management struggles to answer — or if the answer reveals that a large proportion of capex is non-discretionary (maintenance) — it tells the analyst that FCF is structurally lower than the growth narrative implies

⚡ OUTLIER Q21 — "The single metric that would most worry you" probe

  • Question: "What single internal metric, if it moved materially against you over the next 90 days, would most concern you — and what is the current trend on that metric?"
  • Why non-obvious: Management has a tendency to discuss metrics they control; this probe asks for the metric they FEAR — which reveals the dog they aren't whistling
  • Expected answer: Most likely they'll say tenancy ratio or ARPU; a surprisingly candid answer might be a refinancing metric or a country-specific regulatory indicator

  • Diagnostic note: If the answer is immediately fluent and generic, it's rehearsed; if the CEO or CFO pauses and says something specific and unexpected, that pause and that specificity is the most valuable data point in the meeting

  • Multiple-truths probe (Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 3): "You've articulated a compelling case for why USD-denominated contracts protect you from FX risk AND why organic tenancy growth drives margin expansion — but is there a world where both of those things are true AND you still don't generate meaningful equity-level FCF for the next three years? Walk me through that scenario."

  • Good answer signature: Management engages with the scenario directly, quantifies the conditions, and articulates why they believe it won't materialise — while acknowledging it as a genuine scenario

  • Bad answer signature: Dismissal or redirection to the bull case; inability to articulate the scenario coherently; any suggestion that the two positives are incompatible with FCF stagnation

  • Edge-detection probe: New market ROIC post-entry review: "For the markets you entered in the last four years — specifically DC and Oman — have your actual returns to date been above or below your entry underwriting models? And if below, by how much and what caused the variance?"

  • Diagnostic note: This is the most uncomfortable question for management to answer honestly; a specific, quantified answer with a credible explanation of variance drivers is high management quality; deflection is a bear signal

  • Incentive probe — equity vesting: "What percentage of your personal equity compensation vests in the next 18 months — and is any of it subject to accelerated vesting triggers?"

  • Diagnostic note (Concept A): Vesting cliffs in the next 18 months create incentives to manage near-term reported metrics aggressively; concentrated near-term vesting in a CFO is a signal to watch working capital and non-cash adjustment choices closely
