[W][C] Swisscom AG (SCMN-CH) — Business Description

Stage: IG (Ideas Generation) | Source: Claude/Watson [C] | Date: 2026-05-10
Cohort: EU Converged Telecoms/Cable Incumbents — Liberty Global (LBTYK) / Proximus (PROX-BE) / Swisscom (SCMN-CH)
Role: RESEARCHER | Model: claude-sonnet-4-6

KEY FINDINGS [#J]

  • Switzerland is a premium utility, not a growth story: Swiss operations generate ~CHF 7.9bn revenue at ~43% EBITDAaL margin — among the highest in EU telecoms — but organic revenue is declining (-1.4% in 2025) and mobile ARPU eroding ~2-3% annually. The franchise protects cash, not growth. [#J]
  • Fastweb+Vodafone is a transformational bet on Italian convergence, not yet proven: EUR 8bn was paid for the #1 Italian mobile operator (by connections). The combined entity generated EUR 7.29bn revenue in FY2025 — down 1.1% pro forma, with EBITDA broadly flat. EUR 95m synergies achieved vs EUR 600m target by 2029; a 6x gap to close. Classic massive-corporate-change setup: credibility window is 2026-2028. [#J]
  • Leverage elevated but manageable — state dividend is the binding constraint: Net debt/EBITDAaL of 2.4x at YE2025 declining to ~2.3x in 2026. State ownership (51%) locks in the CHF 26-27/share dividend (~CHF 1.35bn/yr), leaving limited FCF for debt repayment after capex of CHF 3.0-3.1bn. FCF cover of dividend ~1.4x in 2026 — tight relative to EU peers but not distressed. [#J]
  • Alt-net threat in Switzerland is real but overstated near-term: Swiss Open Fiber (Sunrise+Salt JV) is building a 1.5m household rival FTTH network. Swisscom FTTH coverage of 56% (targeting 60% by 2026) still leads, but broadband share is already eroding (-1.6pp in 2025 to 47%). The regulatory protection of no mandatory wholesale fibre access is Swisscom's critical structural advantage — any change would be a material derating event. [#J]
  • Italian convergence thesis has genuine optionality but Italian market economics are structurally challenged: Italy is Europe's most price-competitive telecom market — Iliad's entry permanently lowered the floor. FMC penetration in Italy (~38% in 2023) is well below Belgium/Spain (>50%), giving a runway, but margin expansion requires synergy delivery and B2C ARPU stabilisation. [#J]
  • ⚡ RARE — Swisscom holds structurally the highest telco EBITDAaL margin in Western Europe at ~43% (Swiss operations), sustained by market duopoly economics and regulatory protection. No mandatory fibre wholesale, high Swiss ARPU, limited competitive entry. This is a genuine pricing-power observation, not monopoly legacy. [#J]
  • 25%+ IRR thesis is not available at current pricing: SCMN trades ~CHF 500 (implied ~8x EV/EBITDAaL on 2026E). Consensus EPS growth of ~9% YoY, a 5.3% dividend yield, and moderate FCF expansion. A total return of 8-12% is plausible; 25%+ requires synergy acceleration beyond guidance and a re-rating — both non-trivial. [#J]
  • The animal CEO test is equivocal: Aeschlimann executed the Vodafone Italia deal decisively but comes from a network/IT background rather than commercial/M&A. The integration challenge — customer base stabilisation + synergy delivery + capex discipline — requires a different skill set. Watch Q2 2026 earnings for first evidence of Fastweb+Vodafone revenue stabilisation. [#J]
  • Key asymmetry: Downside is a dividend cut if Italian losses widen and Swiss FCF erodes — management has guided CHF 27/share for 2026 but explicitly conditioned on financial targets. Upside is Italian profitability inflection + Swiss IT services becoming a visible growth engine. Neither is priced in; this is a binary-outcome stock at current leverage. [#J]

§1 — Business Overview & Structure

Verdict: Swisscom is a high-quality incumbent telco with a bifurcated identity: a cash-generative Swiss fortress business funding an ambitious Italian transformation play via Fastweb+Vodafone. Structure complexity is elevated post-acquisition. [#J]

  • Structure: Four reportable segments — Switzerland Residential, Switzerland Business (incl. IT services), Fastweb Italy (post-Jan 2025 consolidation), and Other/Eliminations. [#I]
  • Switzerland Residential: consumer mobile, broadband, TV, fixed-voice — primarily under the "blue" brand and secondary brand "Wingo". [#I]
  • Switzerland Business: enterprise and SME connectivity, cloud, managed IT, cybersecurity, UC — branded Swisscom Business; IT services arm embedded here. [#I]

  • Fastweb Italy: post-31-Dec-2024 acquisition of Vodafone Italia; branded "Fastweb + Vodafone" — combined fixed (5.7m lines) + mobile (20m connections) operator in Italy. [#I]

  • Scale (FY2025 actuals): [#I]

  • Group revenue: CHF 15,048m (+36.6% reported vs 2024; -2.0% pro forma on constant perimeter).
  • Group EBITDAaL: CHF 4,984m (+22.6% reported; -1.2% pro forma).
  • Operating FCF: CHF 1,920m (+9.6%).

  • Swiss subscribers: 6.42m mobile lines (+2.2%), 1.94m broadband (-1.6%), 1.47m TV (-2.1%).

  • Revenue segmental split (FY2025, approximate): [#A]

  • Switzerland total: ~CHF 7,868m (52% of group); telecom services ~CHF 5,148m + IT services CHF 1,215m + other.
  • Fastweb Italy: ~EUR 7,291m (~CHF 7,180m equivalent) — approximately 48% of group revenue by value.

  • Group is now roughly 50/50 Switzerland vs Italy — a dramatic structural shift from 2024 when Switzerland was >90% of revenue. [#A]

  • Ownership — the 51% state constraint: [#I]

  • Swiss Confederation holds 51.0% of Swisscom shares. Free float ~49%.
  • State receives ~CHF 690m/year in dividends — politically non-negotiable income stream for federal budget. [#A]

  • Swiss Parliament explicitly rejected mandatory wholesale fibre access regulation (22-19 in Council of States) — a state-owned company benefiting from a regulatory reprieve creates political risk if government stance reverses. [#A]

  • Sceptical observation: The reported 36.6% revenue growth is entirely acquisition-driven. On a comparable basis, the group shrank -2.0%. The headline number flatters a business in aggregate organic decline. [#J]

§2 — Business Model & Economics

Verdict: Switzerland is a high-margin, FCF-generative treadmill — not a flywheel. IT services is the only structural growth engine in Switzerland. Italy has flywheel potential but is pre-inflection. [#J]

Swiss Residential — ARPU & Convergence

  • ARPU trajectory (L3Y): Mobile ARPU declining ~2-3% annually — driven by brand mix shift (Wingo secondary brand cannibalising main brand) and family plan discounting. [#A]
  • Management described wireline ARPU as "very stable" in 2025 — partially offsets mobile erosion. [#I]
  • 2022-2025 mobile ARPU compression: approximately -6-8% cumulative — this is structural, not demand-pulse; Switzerland's high-ARPU starting point (among highest in Europe, ~CHF 40+ vs EU median EUR 15-18) masks the erosion rate. [#A]

  • Pricing action: Swisscom raised certain service prices by 1.8-8.1% in 2025 in response to energy/inflation costs; largely accepted given Swiss disposable incomes. One-off offset, not a structural lever. [#A]

  • Fixed+mobile convergence: "Blue" integrated brand covers 48% of mobile subscribers and 80% of fixed broadband customers in Residential segment. [#I]

  • Convergence measurably reduces churn: Swisscom cites loyalty as a key metric; bundle churn is typically 40-60% lower than standalone in EU empirical data. [#A]

  • ❌ Broadband RGU loss (-1.6% to 1.94m in 2025): convergence has NOT stopped fixed-line decline; alt-net pressure from Swiss Open Fiber (Sunrise+Salt JV) and municipal cable networks is biting. [#J]

  • Incumbent dilemma test — Switzerland: Swisscom is investing ~CHF 1.7bn/yr in Swiss capex to maintain network leadership but market share is declining across all fixed metrics. Adapting via investment, not via pricing or product innovation. Treadmill dynamics. [#J]

Fastweb Italy — Transition Year Economics

  • FY2025 Fastweb+Vodafone (pro forma full year): [#I]
  • Revenue: EUR 7,291m (-1.1% YoY pro forma).
  • EBITDAaL: EUR 1,687m (+0.1% YoY) — resilient margin despite revenue pressure.
  • B2C revenue: EUR 3,328m (-2.9% YoY) — mobile price pressure dominant.
  • B2B revenue: EUR 3,237m (-0.5% YoY) — more resilient; ICT/cloud/cybersecurity growing +5.4% to EUR 844m.

  • Wholesale revenue: EUR 722m (+5.4% YoY); ultrabroadband lines to other operators: 1,126k (+24.4%).

  • Market position Italy: [#I]

  • Mobile: #1 by connections at ~20m (vs Wind Tre ~19m, TIM ~16m, Iliad ~11.5m).
  • Fixed broadband: #2 at 5.7m lines (vs TIM #1 at ~7.25m).

  • FTTH coverage: 56% of Italian territory at YE2025 (up 15pp YoY — strong build pace).

  • Synergy delivery vs target: [#A]

  • Achieved: EUR 95m in 2025 — driven by SIM migration to Vodafone network (near-complete) + Vodafone Group services insourcing.
  • Target: EUR 600m annual run-rate by 2029; EUR 200m guided for 2026.
  • Gap: 6x current run-rate vs long-term ambition. First year is easiest (network consolidation); headcount/procurement synergies are harder. [#A]

  • ❌ Revenue synergies (FMC cross-sell) have NOT materialised in 2025 — B2C revenue declined -2.9% — suggesting customer base is destabilising post-merger rather than growing. [#J]

  • Italian economics vs EU peers: [#A]

  • EBITDAaL margin: EUR 1,687m / EUR 7,291m = ~23% — well below Swiss segment (~43%) and EU telco median (~35-38%). [#A]
  • Italian structural economics (Iliad-driven ARPU floor, 4-player competition) are the root cause, not operational underperformance. [#A]

IT Services / Swisscom IT Services (B2B Switzerland)

  • Revenue: CHF 1,215m in FY2025 (+2.0% YoY) — positive divergence from total Swiss segment decline of -1.4%. [#I]
  • Portfolio: Cloud, cybersecurity, managed services, SAP solutions, UC, IoT, 5G MPN. New "Beem" network-level cybersecurity: 38,000 users across 770 enterprise locations by YE2025. [#I]
  • Margin: Not separately disclosed; embedded in Swiss Business segment. IT services now ~50% of B2B revenue as legacy voice declines. [#A]
  • Flywheel test: IT services + connectivity bundle creates switching costs for enterprise customers — managed IT relationship stickier than a connectivity contract alone. But Swisscom competes against global cloud providers (AWS, Azure) and Swiss IT integrators. Not yet a flywheel — more a defensive bundle with data sovereignty moat. [#J]

Capex Intensity

  • Swiss capex: ~CHF 1.7bn in 2025 (FTTH rollout + 5G). [#I]
  • Italian capex: EUR 1,478m (EUR 1,331m adjusted, -5% YoY) in 2025 — driven by FTTH expansion (+15pp coverage to 56%) and RAN investment. [#I]
  • Group capex intensity: ~CHF 3.0-3.1bn guidance for 2026 vs CHF 14.7-14.9bn revenue = ~20-21% — broadly in line with EU integrated telco median (~20-22%). [#A]

  • Capex peak: Swiss FTTH rollout (targeting 60% FTTH by 2026, 75-80% by 2030) drives capex through 2027. Italy capex declining after 2025 adjusted figures; RAN sharing deal with TIM (15,500 sites/operator by 2028) could reduce Italian mobile capex EUR 200-300m from 2026. Group capex trajectory should decline after 2027 if Swiss fibre hits coverage targets. [#A]

  • Sceptical observation: IT services growing at 2% (CHF 24m/year) is insufficient to offset telecom services declining at 2.3% (-CHF 120m/year) in Switzerland. Italy's B2C revenue decline of -2.9% in 2025 suggests the integration is destroying rather than creating customer value near-term. [#J]

§3 — Competitive Dynamics

Verdict: Switzerland is a benign duopoly with a persistent structural threat from alt-net overbuild. Italy is a four-player price war with limited margin expansion scope — even as a #1/#2 operator. [#J]

Switzerland — Incumbent vs Challengers

  • Market structure: Effective duopoly — Swisscom (~47% broadband, ~52% postpaid mobile) and Sunrise (~27-28% broadband, ~30% postpaid mobile). Salt distant third (~4% fixed broadband, ~20% mobile). Cable/regional ISPs (Quickline, municipal) ~13% combined. [#I]
  • Market share trends (FY2025): [#I]
  • Postpaid mobile: Swisscom 52% (-1.2pp YoY) — steady erosion.
  • Broadband: Swisscom 47% (-1.6pp YoY) — accelerating erosion as alt-net FTTH activates.
  • TV: Swisscom 41% (-0.6pp YoY) — slower erosion.

  • All three metrics deteriorating — trend is structural, not cyclical. [#A]

  • Sunrise post-Liberty Global spin-off: Re-listed on SIX November 2024 (valued ~$3.26bn). As an independent operator, cleaner capital structure and management incentives focused purely on Swiss market share gain. LG's strategic priorities no longer dilute Sunrise management attention — this structurally changes the competitive dynamic. [#A]

  • Swiss Open Fiber JV (Sunrise+Salt): targeting 1.5m FTTH households — a credible alt-net overbuild threat. Swiss FibreCo (separate entity linked to Swiss Fibre Net) also accelerating FTTH rollout with Sunrise and Salt as anchor tenants. [#I]

  • ❌ Alt-net overbuild in Switzerland is accelerating — not a "potential" threat but an active overbuild already impacting Swisscom broadband share. The 1.5m household JV target alone covers ~35% of the households Swisscom has NOT yet passed with FTTH. [#A]

  • Salt: Sky entered Swiss market via Salt distribution agreement (2025) — adds content/bundle complexity for Swisscom TV. Salt's fibre JV with Sunrise makes it a more credible fixed competitor in Swiss Open Fiber coverage areas. [#I]

  • Regulatory environment (ComCom): [#I]

  • No mandatory wholesale access to Swisscom's FTTH network — Parliament voted this down (22-19). This is Swisscom's most important regulatory protection.
  • Copper-only LLU (local loop unbundling) mandated at cost-oriented prices, but copper is being decommissioned.
  • Regulatory disputes ongoing: Swisscom fined CHF 18m in a fibre-optic cable architecture dispute (point-to-multipoint vs point-to-point) — required cable modifications by Dec 2025. [#A]
  • ❌ Regulatory risk: a future mandate for wholesale fibre access (if re-tabled) would remove Swisscom's infrastructure monopoly advantage and compress margins materially. [#J]

Italy — Four-Player Price War

  • Market structure: Four national MNOs — TIM, Fastweb+Vodafone, Wind Tre, Iliad. Fastweb+Vodafone is now #1 mobile (20m connections) and #2 fixed (5.7m vs TIM's ~7.25m). [#I]
  • Competitive dynamics: Iliad's presence (11.5m subscribers, ultra-low-price positioning) has permanently depressed Italian mobile ARPU. FMC penetration in Italy (38% in 2023) is structurally lower than comparables — Belgium 50%+, Spain 50%+. [#A]
  • TIM: Losing fixed subscribers but retaining B2B enterprise relationships. OpenFiber wholesale network gives TIM a legacy structural advantage in wholesale-dependent FTTH access. [#I]
  • RAN sharing — Fastweb+Vodafone + TIM: Agreement reached Jan 2026 for RAN sharing targeting ~15,500 mobile sites per operator by 2028. If executed, would reduce Italian capex requirements materially — positive FCF lever despite revenue headwinds. [#A]

  • ⚡ RARE — Italy has a new #1 mobile operator by connections (Fastweb+Vodafone at 20m) vs Wind Tre's ~19m, TIM's ~16m, and Iliad's ~11.5m. This scale advantage in mobile is a genuine structural asset for convergence cross-sell — but ARPU from this base is among the lowest in EU. [#I]

  • Sceptical observation: Fastweb+Vodafone is #1 mobile in Italy by connections, not by revenue or ARPU. Italy's structural economics — chronic low ARPU, four competing MNOs, persistent promotional intensity — mean scale leadership doesn't automatically translate to margin leadership. [#J]

Verdict: Swisscom's Swiss business generates premium FCF; Italy is consuming capital in a transition year. The dividend is politically and contractually quasi-mandated — this binds capital allocation in ways a fully private telco wouldn't tolerate. [#J]

  • Swiss EBITDAaL: CHF 3,362m in FY2025 (+1.0% YoY; -0.8% adjusted) — remarkable stability for a business with -1.4% revenue. Cost discipline is genuine. [#A]
  • Italian EBITDAaL: EUR 1,687m (~CHF 1,620m) — ~23% margin vs Swiss ~43%. Italy is currently a 23% margin business inside a 33% margin group. [#A]
  • Group EBITDAaL margin: CHF 4,984m / CHF 15,048m = 33.1% — below Swisscom's pre-Italy 38-40% range. This dilution is the direct consequence of Vodafone Italia consolidation. [#A]
  • Q1 2026 update: EBITDAaL CHF 1,288m (+0.8% vs consensus CHF 1,257m — beat). Revenue CHF 3,610m (-4.1% vs consensus CHF 3,650m — slight miss). Operating FCF CHF 594m (+23% beat vs consensus CHF 483m — capex CHF 82m below forecast). Full-year 2026 guidance maintained. [#I]

Balance Sheet & Leverage

  • Net debt YE2025: CHF ~15.9bn including lease liabilities; leverage 2.4x EBITDAaL. [#I]
  • Leverage trajectory: Guided to ~2.3x at YE2026; management committed to deleveraging through FCF generation + synergy delivery. [#I]
  • EU telco peer leverage: EU integrated telco median ~2.0-2.5x (DT ~2.7x, Orange ~1.8x, Proximus ~1.9x). Swisscom at 2.4x is in the upper half but not an outlier. However, given transition risk in Italy, higher than the structural earnings quality of the Swiss business alone would suggest as appropriate. [#A]
  • Average interest rate: 1.86% average cost of debt (well-staggered maturity profile). CHF borrower with EUR asset — natural currency hedge via Fastweb's EUR revenues. [#I]

Dividend Policy

  • 2025 dividend: CHF 26/share (approved AGM March 2026). [#I]
  • 2026 plan: CHF 27/share — explicitly conditioned on meeting financial targets. [#I]
  • Total dividend cost: ~CHF 1.35bn/year (51.8m shares × CHF 26). Swiss Confederation receives ~CHF 690m/year. [#A]
  • FCF cover: CHF 1,920m operating FCF vs CHF 1,350m dividend = 1.42x cover (2025). For 2026: FCF ~CHF 2,000m vs ~CHF 1,400m dividend = ~1.43x. Adequate but not comfortable — a 15% FCF miss challenges dividend sustainability. [#A]
  • ❌ Payout ratio vs EU peers: 91% reported payout ratio — among the highest in EU telecoms. Deutsche Telekom ~40%, Orange ~65-70%. Swisscom's payout leaves minimal retained capital for debt reduction or strategic reinvestment. The state dividend obligation effectively forces this. [#J]
  • ⚡ RARE — The dividend yield of 5.3% at current price (~CHF 500) is backstopped by a sovereign (Swiss Confederation) that will politically resist a cut — this creates an unusual yield floor not common in private-sector telcos. Risk is not zero but state ownership makes a cut politically costly. [#J]

Capex Cycle

  • Swiss capex: ~CHF 1.7bn per year through 2027 (FTTH 60% by 2026, 75-80% by 2030); begins declining post-2027 as rollout matures. [#A]
  • Italian capex: EUR 1,331m (adjusted) in 2025. RAN sharing deal with TIM should reduce Italian mobile capex materially from 2026-2028 — potentially EUR 200-300m capex saving. [#A]
  • Peak capex view: 2025 likely peak capex year at ~CHF 3.1bn. 2026 guidance CHF 3.0-3.1bn (flat). Capex should start declining from 2027 as Swiss FTTH matures + Italian RAN sharing delivers — positive FCF inflection story from 2027 onward, but conditional. [#A]

FX / Currency

  • Reporting currency: CHF (safe-haven, typically appreciating). Fastweb revenues EUR-denominated (~48% of group). [#I]
  • FX drag: Strong CHF environment (CHF/EUR below parity) structurally drags Italian EBITDAaL contribution when translated into CHF. EUR investor receives CHF dividends — CHF strength is a tailwind for EUR-based investors. [#A]

  • Hedging: Natural hedge via CHF debt + EUR-denominated Italian assets. Average interest rate 1.86% reflects low Swiss borrowing costs — cross-currency advantage is real. [#A]

  • Sceptical observation: FCF cover of 1.42x is dependent on (a) Italian EBITDA not falling further, (b) Swiss FCF not deteriorating from alt-net competition, and (c) capex executing at plan. Any two of these going wrong simultaneously challenges dividend sustainability and accelerates leverage. [#J]

§5 — Management & Capital Allocation

Verdict: Aeschlimann made a bold, defensible strategic call on Italy; the execution test starts now. State ownership creates an unusual incentive structure — management must serve the sovereign's income needs as well as shareholders'. [#J]

CEO — Christoph Aeschlimann

  • Background: Joined Swisscom 2019 as Head of IT, Network and Infrastructure. Appointed CEO June 2022. Network/technology-first career profile — not a commercial or M&A-native leader. [#I]
  • Track record (2022-2025):
  • Orchestrated and closed the EUR 8bn Vodafone Italia acquisition (announced March 2024, closed December 2024) within 9 months — material strategic ambition and execution speed. [#A]
  • Swiss operational discipline maintained: Swiss EBITDAaL +1% in FY2025 despite -1.4% revenue — cost management is strong. [#A]
  • AI positioning: FastwebAI Suite (Italy B2B) and Beem cybersecurity (Switzerland) show innovation intent; pre-revenue material at group level. [#I]
  • Animal CEO test: [#J]
  • Positive: closed EUR 8bn deal; synergy delivery in 2025 (EUR 95m vs EUR 60m original target) beat plan.
  • Negative: B2C revenue in Italy declined -2.9% in 2025 — integration disturbed customer base; no evidence of proactive commercial aggression in Italy post-acquisition.
  • Verdict: Capable operator, unproven integrator. Italian commercial turnaround requires a different skill set than Aeschlimann's background. [#J]

Fastweb Acquisition — Strategic Rationale & Capital Deployed

  • Strategic rationale: [#A]
  • Fastweb (Swisscom-owned since 2007) had high-quality fixed network but lacked mobile at scale (~3.8m mobile customers via Wind Tre MVNO).
  • Vodafone Italia brought 22,000 mobile sites, 20m mobile customers, and enterprise relationships.
  • Combined entity = Italy's #1 converged operator — a genuine convergence play in a market with low FMC penetration. [#A]
  • EUR 8bn acquisition price implies ~4.7x EV/EBITDAaL (pre-synergies) — expensive for a business in a structurally challenged market. [#A]
  • Capital deployed: EUR 8bn equity consideration (debt/cash free) + EUR 176m integration costs booked in 2024 + ongoing integration capex. [#I]
  • Integration progress: Legal merger complete (Jan 2026). SIM migration near-complete. Brand consolidation underway. First synergy delivery ahead of schedule (EUR 95m vs EUR 60m plan). [#I]
  • Credibility test (massive-corporate-change model): First year synergy beat is a positive signal. Real test is 2026-2027: EUR 200m synergy target + B2C revenue stabilisation. If B2C revenue still declining at >-3% YoY in H1 2026, investment case materially weakens. [#J]

State Ownership Constraint on Capital Allocation

  • 51% Swiss Confederation stake — practical implications: [#A]
  • State receives ~CHF 690m/year in dividends — politically non-negotiable for federal budget.
  • Swisscom cannot do a meaningful buyback program without committing to an equivalent dividend floor.
  • M&A risk is politically constrained — the Italy deal was itself a bold exception requiring implicit state support. [#A]
  • Labour/restructuring: state ownership limits aggressive Italian headcount restructuring. [#A]

  • ❌ Capital allocation sub-optimal: 91% payout ratio maintained by quasi-political obligation — minimal retained capital for debt repayment or organic reinvestment. A fully private operator in the same position would cut the dividend and accelerate deleveraging. [#J]

  • Sceptical observation: If the Italian transformation thesis fails, management has limited financial flexibility to respond — dividend quasi-fixed, leverage already 2.4x, equity issuance would dilute the Confederation's stake. The downside scenario is more constrained than for a private-sector telco. [#J]

§6 — Growth Outlook & Optionality

Verdict: Swiss organic growth is structurally limited to IT services (2-3% annually); Italy offers the only material EBITDA inflection story, but requires 3-5 years of flawless execution to be visible in group numbers. 25%+ IRR is not the base case. [#J]

Switzerland — Mature Market with IT Services Optionality

  • Revenue trajectory: Swiss organic revenue likely to decline -1 to -2% annually for 2026-2028, driven by telecom services erosion partially offset by IT services growth. [#A]
  • Mobile ARPU: -2-3% annual structural decline. Price rises of 1.8-8.1% in 2025 partially offset in 2026 but are a one-off, not a trend. [#A]
  • Fixed broadband: volume loss of -1.6%/year as Swiss Open Fiber overbuild activates. Not fast enough to create a step-change, but cumulative. [#A]
  • IT services: CHF 1,215m growing at 2-5% annually adds CHF 25-60m revenue/year — insufficient to offset CHF 100-150m annual telecom erosion. [#A]
  • EBITDA: Swiss EBITDAaL likely broadly flat in 2026-2028 (cost cuts offset revenue decline). Not a growth franchise. [#J]

Fastweb Italy — The Growth Optionality

  • Base case (realistic): [#A]
  • 2026: B2C revenue stabilisation as post-merger customer churn abates; synergy delivery EUR 200m (per guidance).
  • 2027-2028: Net revenue growth if FMC penetration rises from 38% toward 50%+ (Belgium/Spain comps); synergy run-rate builds to EUR 400m.
  • 2029: EUR 600m synergy run-rate — equivalent to ~EUR 600m additional EBITDA vs pre-synergy baseline.
  • The optionality calculation: [#A]
  • If EUR 600m synergies delivered by 2029 and Italian EBITDAaL rises from EUR 1,687m to ~EUR 2,287m (~+35%), Italian EBITDA margin reaches ~31% — closer to EU integrated telco norms.
  • At 5-6x EV/EBITDA (Italian telco range), Italian business would be worth EUR 11-14bn — vs EUR 8bn acquisition price. That is the bull case for re-rating.
  • Requires: no revenue headwind, no Italian regulatory intervention, capex declining per RAN sharing plan. Multiple assumptions required simultaneously. [#A]
  • Transformation play test: Italy is early-stage Q-turn. 2025 data (synergy beat + revenue still declining) is consistent with a company that has begun the transformation but not demonstrated commercial inflection. The Q-turn, if it occurs, becomes visible in H2 2026 data. [#J]

Cloud/IT Services — Differentiation Potential

  • Swiss IT services moat: Swiss enterprises have structural preference for Swiss-domiciled cloud providers (data sovereignty, FINMA compliance for financial services). [#A]
  • Creates a niche moat unavailable to AWS/Azure — but addressable market in Switzerland is capped at ~CHF 1-2bn total IT services opportunity for a telco. Not transformational at group level. [#A]
  • FastwebAI Suite (Italy B2B): AI platform for Italian enterprise — too early to assess financial materiality. B2B ICT growing at +5.4% in Italy is the most positive single data point in the Italian P&L. [#I]

Is a 25%+ IRR Thesis Available?

  • Current setup (~CHF 500 share price): [#A]
  • Dividend yield: CHF 26/CHF 500 = 5.2% (growing to CHF 27 = 5.4% in 2026).
  • Consensus EPS growth: ~9% YoY (CHF 24.57 → CHF 26.81 for 2026E, 19 analysts).
  • Consensus rating: "Sell" — 3 Buy, 10 Sell, 6 Hold. Average 12-month price target CHF 559.73.
  • Total return baseline: ~14-16% including dividend + EPS growth if no re-rating.
  • For 25%+ IRR: requires (a) significant multiple re-rating from ~8x to 10x+ EV/EBITDA OR (b) synergy delivery materially ahead of schedule + Swiss outperformance. Neither is base case. [#J]

  • ❌ The 25%+ IRR case is not available at current pricing without a material asymmetric event (Italian inflection, M&A, or multiple re-rating). [#J]

  • Sceptical observation: IT services growth is consistently overplayed as a "cloud transformation" narrative by management. CHF 1,215m growing at 2% is not a meaningful needle-mover for a CHF 7.9bn Swiss revenue base. Italy needs to inflect — and it hasn't yet. [#J]

§7 — Key Risks

Verdict: Primary risk is binary — Italian integration either delivers or fails. Swiss franchise risks are slower-burn but cumulative. State ownership constrains management response optionality in both scenarios. [#J]

Risk 1 — Italian Integration Failure (HIGH PROBABILITY OF PARTIAL; MODERATE-HIGH OF FULL)

  • Mechanism: EUR 600m synergy target (by 2029) not achieved; B2C revenue continues declining, compressing Italian EBITDA rather than expanding it. [#I]
