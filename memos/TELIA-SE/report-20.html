3. §1 — EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (TOP-3 MOST-DIAGNOSTIC)

  • VERDICT: Top-3 diagnostics crystallise around pricing power durability, cost-programme permanence, and the cash-flow bridge sustaining the dividend; fluent answers on all three simultaneously would be the bull-confirming signal
  • Top diagnostic Q1 (Revenue): ARPU trajectory in Swedish consumer + B2B — does pricing hold as bundling matures or does it revert toward volume competition?
  • Top diagnostic Q2 (Margin): Cost programme granularity — permanent vs transient savings, sensitivity to volume, and whether efficiency gains are being reinvested or flowing to shareholders
  • Top diagnostic Q3 (Capital): FCF-to-dividend bridge — what internal stress-test does management run on dividend coverage, and at what leverage / FCF level does the payout policy inflect?
  • SIGNPOST — Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 1 (go to the most-different question): The question the analyst might avoid is "What have you communicated to shareholders that you now believe was wrong?" — uncomfortable, but the answer (or the dodge) is maximally diagnostic of communication honesty
  • Sceptical bullet: Fluent answers from a recently-appointed CEO on specific operational metrics may reflect prepared talking points rather than deep operational conviction — probe for spontaneous numerical specificity, not rehearsed narrative

4. §2 — THEME 1: REVENUE DRIVERS + VISIBILITY

  • VERDICT: Revenue-side uncertainty is ELEVATED — Swedish consumer ARPU post-price-increase cycle, B2B contract renewal timing, and Finland/Estonia mix shift are all partially unresolved in public filings

Q1 — Swedish Consumer ARPU Sustainability

  • Question: "Post the 2023-24 price increase cycle, what is your current ARPU trend in Swedish consumer mobile, and how much runway remains for further above-inflation increases?"
  • Expected answer: Management guides to low-single-digit ARPU growth driven by upsell to higher-tier plans and bundled broadband-mobile; expects 1-2 further price adjustment opportunities through 2026
  • Good answer signature: Quantified ARPU bridge (volume mix + pricing + upsell) with specific churn elasticity observed during 2023-24 increases
  • Bad answer signature: Narrative-only; "customer value" framing without quantified ARPU split; inability to state observed churn response to price increases
  • Follow-up probe: "What was the net churn response, in basis points, to the most recent price increase, and how does it compare to your internal elasticity model?"
  • Diagnostic note: Confirms bull hypothesis (durable pricing power in oligopolistic Swedish market) if ARPU growth >CPI with sub-50bp churn uplift; confirms bear hypothesis if churn response exceeded model predictions or if management is reluctant to quantify

Q2 — B2B Revenue Composition + Attach Rate

  • Question: "What proportion of your Swedish B2B revenue is now multi-product (connectivity + IT services + IoT), and what is the ARPU differential between single-product and multi-product customers?"
  • Expected answer: Multi-product customers represent ~30-40% of B2B revenue, with ARPU 20-35% above single-product; growth is being driven by SME segment upsell rather than large enterprise wins
  • Good answer signature: Specific ARPU differential stated; evidence of improving retention in multi-product cohort; named internal metric tracked at board level
  • Bad answer signature: "We're seeing good momentum in our B2B portfolio" without quantification; conflation of hardware revenue with services ARPU
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the revenue-at-risk on the top-10 B2B enterprise contracts renewing in the next 18 months?"
  • Diagnostic note (ACH cross-ref): Bull case requires B2B to be an ARPU expansion engine, not just a volume play; bad answer shifts weight to false-friend hypothesis (headline growth masking commoditising services mix)

Q3 — Nordic vs Baltic Revenue Mix Trajectory

  • Question: "Given the ongoing Swedish-market focus, are you deliberately allowing the Baltic contribution to grow as a share of group EBITDA — and if so, what is the target mix in 3 years?"
  • Expected answer: Baltic operations are high-margin, growing, and strategically retained; management comfortable with Baltic share moving from ~15% to ~20% of group EBITDA as organic growth compounds
  • Good answer signature: Explicit EBITDA margin differential cited (Baltics structurally higher-margin); management can articulate why Baltic competitive position is durable
  • Bad answer signature: Vague reference to "diversified portfolio"; unwillingness to state a 3-year mix target; hedging on Baltic competitive intensity from Estonian/Latvian rivals
  • Follow-up probe: "Is the Baltic segment's higher EBITDA margin structural (market structure, cost base) or cyclical (FX, lower capex vintage)?"
  • Diagnostic note: Material under-valuation of Baltic assets is a known bull-case pillar; management's willingness to articulate it clearly is signal of investor-alignment

Q4 — Finland Exit / Remaining Exposure

  • Question: "Following the Finland divestiture process, what residual revenue/cost exposure does Telia retain to Finnish market dynamics, and has the exit fully de-risked the P&L?"
  • Expected answer: Finnish exit fully completed or in final completion stages; residual exposure limited to transition-service agreements of fixed duration; no meaningful P&L risk
  • Good answer signature: Specific TSA duration and cost; confirmation of no earn-out contingencies that could swing reported results
  • Bad answer signature: Ongoing TSA complexity not quantified; earn-out provisions referencing Finnish market performance still live; management deflects to "we're working through the transition"
  • Follow-up probe: "Are there any indemnity or warranty claims outstanding from the Finland disposal that could generate P&L charges in 2025-26?"
  • Diagnostic note: Residual Finland exposure is a false-friend risk — the headline divestiture creates bull-case narrative but TSA/earn-out tail could erode the clean story

Sceptical bullet: A fluent answer on Swedish ARPU from a CEO who has been in role <18 months may be based on briefing packs rather than operational intuition — probe for an instance where the ARPU outcome surprised management in the last 12 months; the surprise is more diagnostic than the guidance

5. §3 — THEME 2: MARGIN TRAJECTORY + LEVERS

  • VERDICT: Margin trajectory is the HIGHEST-uncertainty pillar — cost programme permanence vs revenue-mix headwinds is unresolved; the EBITDAAL margin is arithmetically tight against the 3-4% growth guidance

Q5 — Cost Programme Permanence

  • Question: "Of the SEK 2-3bn cost savings guided through 2026, what fraction is structural (permanent unit-cost reduction) vs temporary (vacancy freeze, project deferral)?"
  • Expected answer: Management guides ~60-70% structural (procurement renegotiations, headcount reduction, network sharing efficiencies) and acknowledges ~30-40% is subject to reinvestment as revenue growth returns
  • Good answer signature: Specific cost lines by category; evidence that structural savings are already embedded in run-rate (not dependent on future actions); savings are independently auditable against prior year headcount/opex filings
  • Bad answer signature: "We're confident in our cost programme" without structural/temporary split; inability to reconcile prior-year cost base to current guidance without one-off items
  • Follow-up probe : "If Swedish consumer revenue came in 3% below plan in 2025, which cost lines flex down automatically and which are sticky?"
  • Diagnostic note: Bull case requires ≥60% structural savings; if management can't cleanly split, the operating leverage thesis is compromised — shifts weight to bear hypothesis (margin dependent on revenue trajectory, not cost-base reset)

Q6 — Network Sharing Economics

  • Question: "What is the quantified EBITDA benefit from the Net4Mobility network-sharing agreement per year, and how does this change if one party's traffic growth significantly diverges?"
  • Expected answer: Net4Mobility delivers SEK 400-700m annual cost avoidance; traffic-divergence risk is managed by contractual true-up mechanisms reviewed every 2-3 years
  • Good answer signature: Specific annual SEK benefit stated; true-up mechanism explained; management understands the contract risk (not just the benefit)
  • Bad answer signature: "Network sharing is strategically important" without quantified benefit; uncertainty about true-up mechanism suggests management has not stress-tested the economics
  • Follow-up probe: "At what traffic-share divergence threshold does the economics of Net4Mobility turn unfavourable for Telia vs standalone deployment?"
  • Diagnostic note (GM driver / Op leverage): Network sharing is a structural cost lever — if economics are not well-understood by management, the margin model is less durable than priced in

Q7 — Capex Trajectory and Fibre Completion

  • Question: "You're targeting declining capex intensity post-2025 as the Swedish fibre rollout matures — at what completion percentage does capex intensity drop, and by how many capex SEKbn?"
  • Expected answer: Swedish fibre >80% complete by end-2025; capex intensity declines 1-2pp of revenue from 2026 as rollout transitions to maintenance; absolute capex falls SEK 1-2bn by 2027
  • Good answer signature: Specific completion percentage stated; absolute capex bridge provided (not just % intensity); acknowledgement of maintenance capex run-rate
  • Bad answer signature: "We expect capex to normalise" without specificity; conflation of fibre rollout capex with 5G spectrum investment that could offset the reduction
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the spectrum outlay embedded in your 2025-27 capex guidance, and could an unexpected spectrum auction accelerate cash outflows?"
  • Diagnostic note (Capex pacing): The FCF uplift thesis post-fibre depends entirely on capex actually declining — management's specificity here is a key bull/bear discriminant

Q8 — Mix Shift Headwinds (Wholesale vs Retail)

  • Question: "Is the wholesale/MVNO revenue growing faster than retail in Sweden — and if so, how does that mix shift affect blended EBITDA margins over a 3-year horizon?"
  • Expected answer: Wholesale/MVNO is a modest mix headwind — ~2-3pp lower margin than retail — but management sees it as strategic (network utilisation) and caps its P&L drag
  • Good answer signature: EBITDA margin differential between wholesale and retail quantified; management has a stated ceiling on wholesale share; evidence that this trade-off is actively managed
  • Bad answer signature: Cannot state wholesale margin differential; frames mix shift as purely strategic without acknowledging margin drag; MVNO share growing without a stated cap
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the EBITDA margin of your wholesale segment relative to consumer retail — is it above or below 25%?"
  • Diagnostic note (Mix shift): Material mix shift toward lower-margin wholesale is a latent false-friend — headline revenue grows, EBITDA per unit declines

Sceptical bullet: Management has a strong incentive to present cost savings as structural (supports EBITDA guide, protects dividend) — test the permanence claim by asking for the single largest cost line where savings are most at risk, and whether it has been achieved through renegotiations already signed vs negotiations still ongoing

6. §4 — THEME 3: CAPITAL ALLOCATION + BALANCE SHEET

  • VERDICT: Capital allocation is the VALUATION-ANCHOR theme — the dividend commitment is load-bearing for the investment case and the FCF bridge is arithmetically tight; questions here are highest-stakes

Q9 — Dividend Sustainability Bridge

  • Question: "Can you walk the cash flow bridge from EBITDAAL to ordinary dividend coverage — what is your internal floor FCF for the dividend to be safe, and what is 2025E FCF vs that floor?"
  • Expected answer: Dividend covered 1.3-1.5x by equity FCF; internal floor is ~SEK 5bn equity FCF; 2025E is tracking above floor even in a downside scenario
  • Good answer signature: Explicit FCF bridge provided (EBITDAAL → D&A tax adj → interest → capex → working capital → FCF); floor stated as absolute SEK figure not a payout ratio
  • Bad answer signature: Payout ratio framing only; inability to walk the bridge; reference to "adjusted" FCF that excludes items without justification; deflection to leverage ratio
  • Follow-up probe: "In your internal stress-test, what is the 2025E FCF in a scenario where Swedish consumer revenue is 5% below plan?"
  • Diagnostic note (Dividend policy): A management team that can fluently walk the FCF bridge with sensitivity analysis is exhibiting genuine conviction; a management team that retreats to payout-ratio language is not — the latter is a material warning signal

Q10 — Leverage Target and Debt Structure

  • Question: "You're guiding to 2.0-2.5x net debt / EBITDAAL — what drives the timing of reaching that target, and are there covenants or refinancing events that could constrain shareholder returns in 2025-26?"
  • Expected answer: Leverage at ~2.4x end-2024; on track to 2.0-2.5x by end-2026 driven by EBITDA growth + selective disposal proceeds; no binding covenant at current operating range
  • Good answer signature: Leverage trajectory stated quarter by quarter; specific refinancing events called out (maturity wall, floating-rate exposure); covenant headroom quantified
  • Bad answer signature: "We're committed to our leverage target" without trajectory; inability to identify maturity events in 2025-26; vague on floating-rate exposure given rate environment
  • Follow-up probe (Leverage target): "What proportion of your debt is floating-rate, and at current rates, what is the annual interest cost sensitivity per 50bp move?"
  • Diagnostic note (RONIC discipline): Tight leverage with refinancing risk in a high-rate environment is a credibility risk — misalignment between stated leverage target and actual debt structure is a false-friend signal

Q11 — M&A Appetite vs Organic Focus

  • Question: "Post the disposal programme, is Telia a net acquirer or net vendor over the next 3 years, and what is the minimum RONIC hurdle for any in-market M&A?"
  • Expected answer: Management guides to organic focus; bolt-on M&A limited to in-footprint fibre/tower assets; RONIC hurdle ≥8-9% (above WACC); no large transformational M&A contemplated
  • Good answer signature: RONIC hurdle stated explicitly; named criteria for in-market bolt-on (e.g. fibre overbuild avoidance, tower consolidation); commitment to organic FCF generation as primary capital return mechanism
  • Bad answer signature: "We remain open to value-creating M&A" without a RONIC hurdle; reference to "strategic optionality" that implies appetite for transformational deals; no acknowledgement of prior M&A value-destruction history
  • Follow-up probe (M&A philosophy): "Looking at Telia's M&A track record over 2015-2022, what specific decisions would you not repeat, and why?"
  • Diagnostic note (RONIC discipline): A willingness to critique prior M&A is a credibility marker; deflection or revisionism is a warning signal on capital discipline

Sceptical bullet: FCF framing in Telia's investor communications has historically used "equity free cash flow" definitions that exclude lease payments and restructuring — probe whether the CEO/CFO can articulate the gap between reported equity FCF and pure unlevered cash generation, and whether dividend coverage looks different on the stricter basis

7. §5 — THEME 4: STRATEGY + COMPETITIVE POSITION

  • VERDICT: Strategic positioning is PARTIALLY RESOLVED — Swedish oligopoly structure is durable but 5G monetisation path is unclear; B2B strategy is under-articulated in public communications

Q12 — 5G Monetisation Timeline

  • Question: "Swedish 5G standalone coverage is approaching broad availability — what specific revenue lines are you expecting to monetise from 5G in 2025-27, and on what timeline do those revenues appear in EBITDA?"
  • Expected answer: 5G monetisation primarily from B2B (private networks, IoT) rather than consumer ARPU uplift; consumer benefit is retention/churn reduction rather than pricing uplift; B2B 5G revenue visible in 2026-27 at low-single-digit SEK bn
  • Good answer signature: Revenue pathway stated with timing; named B2B verticals (manufacturing, logistics, public sector); acknowledgement that consumer 5G monetisation is slower than original investment case
  • Bad answer signature: "5G creates significant value" narrative; inability to bridge from coverage rollout to specific revenue events; conflation of consumer and B2B 5G economics
  • Follow-up probe (Win condition): "Name one specific enterprise customer where Telia has won a private 5G contract — what did that contract look like economically?"
  • Diagnostic note (Moat trajectory): Failure to articulate specific 5G revenue wins by 2025 is a material warning signal — the investment case increasingly depends on 5G ARPU or B2B revenue emerging; if management can't point to named examples, the bull case is thinning

Q13 — Competitive Response to Fibre Overbuild

  • Question: "Where Telia's fibre network faces overbuild from Telenor/Open Infra/local operators, what is your observed churn response, and how does your ARPU hold in overbuild zones vs non-overlapping zones?"
  • Expected answer: Overbuild affects ~20-25% of Swedish fibre footprint; churn in overbuild zones is 50-150bp higher; ARPU differential is modest but present; Telia's response is service quality differentiation rather than price concession
  • Good answer signature: Churn differential quantified; ARPU differential stated; specific competitive response named (bundle loyalty, speed tier advantage, content package)
  • Bad answer signature: "We're very comfortable with our competitive position" without quantification; inability to identify overbuild geography or its financial impact
  • Follow-up probe (Competitive response): "Are you matching competitor introductory pricing in overbuild zones or holding on price — and what is the cost of each strategy in churn terms?"
  • Diagnostic note (Disruption risk): Fibre overbuild is the most direct near-term structural risk to consumer ARPU — management's ability to quantify the impact vs their response is a key bull/bear discriminant

Sceptical bullet: Telia's historical strategic communications have often described 5G and fibre as dual competitive advantages — probe whether the economic reality of dual-infrastructure investment (5G spectrum cost + fibre rollout) is actually compressing the FCF generation that was supposed to follow from "infrastructure monetisation" by 2025

8. §6 — THEME 5: MANAGEMENT QUALITY + OPERATOR TRACK RECORD

  • VERDICT: Management quality is a HIGH-UNCERTAINTY pillar — CEO Allison Kirk is <18 months in role with limited public track record on Nordic telecom P&L; incentive structure probing is load-bearing

Q14 — CEO Incentive Structure (POWER OF INCENTIVES — MMO)

  • Question: "What is the weighting of EBITDAAL growth vs FCF vs TSR in your short-term and long-term incentive plans — and has the weighting changed since you joined in 2023?"
  • Expected answer: LTI weighted 40-50% TSR, 30-40% FCF/ROCE, 10-20% ESG/NPS; STI weighted toward EBITDAAL and revenue; weighting adjusted by Kirk to increase FCF weighting vs prior EBITDAAL-only structure
  • Good answer signature: Explicit percentage weights stated; acknowledgement of prior incentive structure's weaknesses (EBITDAAL-focused = potential margin-over-growth bias); specific change made with rationale
  • Bad answer signature: "My incentives are fully aligned with shareholders" without specifics; deflection to annual report remuneration section; inability to state own LTI performance conditions
  • Follow-up probe (Power of incentives): "If EBITDAAL grows 4% but FCF is flat due to working capital, does that constitute a good year for your bonus?"
  • Diagnostic note (Concept A — incentive structure predicts behaviour): LTI weighted heavily to EBITDAAL incentivises margin management over cash generation — detecting a misalignment here is a material bear-case input

Q15 — Sandbagging Discipline + Prior Guidance Track Record

  • Question: "Over the last 8 quarters, Telia has missed/met/exceeded consensus on revenue and EBITDA — where has guidance been most systematically wrong, and why?"
  • Expected answer: Management acknowledges specific instances where guidance underestimated cost inflation (2022-23) or overestimated Finnish recovery (2022); frames current guidance as more conservative as a result of those misses
  • Good answer signature: Specific prior guidance errors named (not just "market conditions"); structural change to guidance process articulated; willingness to say "we were wrong on X"
  • Bad answer signature: "Our guidance philosophy is to give the market confidence"; inability to identify prior guidance misses; attribution of all prior misses to external factors
  • Follow-up probe (Sandbagging discipline): "Is your current 3-4% EBITDAAL growth guide a central case or a floor — and what probability do you internally assign to exceeding it?"
  • Diagnostic note (Operator track record): Sandbagging (guiding low to beat consistently) is a positive signal for an investor seeking predictable EPS trajectory; inability to distinguish between floor and central-case guidance is a warning signal

Q16 — Crisis Playbook + Culture Under Pressure

  • Question: "What was the hardest operating decision you made in your first 18 months at Telia — and what would you do differently?"
  • Expected answer: A specific decision is named (e.g. headcount programme, Baltic investment allocation, a specific customer contract decision); genuine self-criticism articulated; not a prepared answer
  • Good answer signature: Spontaneous specificity — names a real decision with a quantified outcome; demonstrates willingness to learn publicly
  • Bad answer signature: Generic "the hardest thing is driving cultural change at scale" answer; no specific decision named; turns the answer into a leadership narrative
  • Follow-up probe (Crisis playbook): "If Swedish consumer broadband market share declined 2pp in the next 12 months, what would your response be — and have you pre-planned that response?"
  • Diagnostic note (Culture archetype): The quality of the self-critical answer is one of the highest-signal management assessment inputs — a prepared generic answer vs a spontaneous specific answer is highly discriminating

Sceptical bullet: A CEO with <18 months in role who answers management-quality questions fluently may be communicating a vision rather than a track record — probe for a decision where the outcome was different from the intention, and how the organisation responded; this cannot be prepared

9. §7 — THEME 6: RISK + DOWNSIDE (PRE-MORTEM CROSS-REF)

  • VERDICT: Downside-risk questions are load-bearing given the valuation tension between FCF yield attractiveness and the arithmetically tight dividend/deleveraging/growth triangle

Q17 — Bear-Case FCF Scenario

  • Question: "In a scenario where Swedish consumer ARPU is flat (not growing), fibre overbuild worsens, and spectrum costs come earlier than planned — what is the FCF impact in 2025-26, and does the dividend still hold?"
  • Expected answer: Management has run this scenario internally; FCF declines SEK 1-2bn vs base case; dividend still covered at 1.1x but with no margin for SBB; leverage climbs toward 2.5x ceiling
  • Good answer signature: Scenario has been internally modelled; specific FCF impact stated; dividend coverage at the floor is above 1.0x even in this scenario; management names the variable most likely to trigger a rethink
  • Bad answer signature: "We're comfortable with our guidance range" without scenario modelling; refusal to engage with the bear-case construct; "the dividend is a priority" without quantification
  • Follow-up probe (Bear-case probe): "What is the single most-likely event, in your view, that would cause you to reconsider the dividend level — and how far away from that event are you today?"
  • Diagnostic note (ACH pre-mortem cross-ref): The bear case for Telia is FCF compression forcing a dividend cut — a management team that has genuinely stress-tested this is a higher-quality operator; one that refuses the scenario is signalling either overconfidence or information withholding

Q18 — Regulatory Exposure (Security / EU Vendor)

  • Question: "How do Swedish or EU regulatory requirements on network security (Chinese vendor restrictions, NIS2 compliance) affect your capex plan and timeline — and what is the quantified cost?"
  • Expected answer: Ericsson-heavy network already compliant; NIS2 incremental cost is SEK 200-400m over 2024-26; no material risk to capex guidance from regulatory changes in current environment
  • Good answer signature: Specific vendor exposure stated; compliance cost quantified and already embedded in guidance; management has identified the single largest regulatory risk beyond NIS2
  • Bad answer signature: "We're fully compliant with all applicable regulations" without quantification; inability to state vendor mix; no acknowledgement of evolving EU security rules as a potential capex wildcard
  • Follow-up probe (Regulatory exposure): "Is there any scenario under current EU discussions on network resilience that would require you to accelerate infrastructure spend beyond your current plan?"
  • Diagnostic note (Regulatory positioning): Regulatory capex wildcards are a known false-friend risk — headline capex guidance may be overstated as conservative, or understated if compliance costs are not embedded

Q19 — Customer Churn Vector (B2B Enterprise)

  • Question: "What is the average remaining contract duration of your top-20 B2B enterprise accounts, and are any of those accounts in active competitive review today?"
  • Expected answer: Average B2B enterprise contract length is 2-3 years; 15-20% of top-20 accounts in renewal cycle in 2025-26; no single account represents >3% of B2B revenue
  • Good answer signature: Specific contract duration data; concentration risk addressed; management can name the competitive dynamic in at-risk renewals without compromising confidentiality
  • Bad answer signature: "Our B2B relationships are very long-term and sticky" without duration data; refusal to engage with concentration question; inability to identify a competitive renewal situation
  • Follow-up probe (Customer churn vector): "In the last 12 months, which was the largest B2B contract you lost, and what was the primary reason?"
  • Diagnostic note (False-friend probe): B2B enterprise contract concentration with short remaining durations is a latent revenue-cliff risk not visible in headline revenue trends

Sceptical bullet on Q19 ACH coverage gaps: The questions above do NOT explicitly test (a) whether the Telia Towers monetisation creates an ongoing rental cost on the P&L that compresses EBITDA over time, (b) whether the Swedish pension liability has been fully marked to current rates, or (c) whether the TV/media segment carries impairment risk from streaming disruption — these should be added if the meeting agenda permits a longer session; flag to APM as uncovered ACH items

10. §8 — CROSS-CUTTING META + NON-OBVIOUS QUESTIONS

  • VERDICT: Non-obvious questioning angles cluster around the incentive structure / organisational-design gaps, the towers-rental drag, and the internal measurement of whether the strategy is working — questions management would not volunteer on

⚡ Non-Obvious Q-A — The Towers Rental Drag

  • Non-obvious angle: Telia sold Telia Towers in 2021 and now leases back tower access — this creates a perpetual and potentially CPI-escalating cost that is often embedded in reported D&A rather than EBITDAAL, inflating the EBITDAAL figure relative to true economics
  • Question: "What is your annual tower lease cost following the Telia Towers disposal, what is the escalation clause, and how would EBITDA (pre-IFRS 16) compare to your reported EBITDAAL if towers were still owned?"
  • Expected answer: Annual tower lease cost ~SEK 1.5-2.5bn; CPI-escalated; EBITDAAL-to-EBITDA gap is explicitly acknowledged and disclosed; management is transparent that EBITDAAL inflates the headline vs pre-IFRS 16 economics
  • Good answer signature: The specific annual lease cost stated; escalation clause described; management can reconcile EBITDAAL to pre-IFRS 16 EBITDA clearly and without discomfort
  • Bad answer signature: Deflection to "this is disclosed in the notes"; inability to state the annual tower lease cost from memory; discomfort with the question
  • Follow-up probe: "If you were to re-acquire tower access rights today at current market rates, would the economics be better or worse than the 2021 sale price implied?"
  • Diagnostic note: This question goes to the heart of whether EBITDAAL growth is genuine economic improvement or partially a metric-engineering artefact from the sale-leaseback structure — a management team that understands and embraces this question is exhibiting genuine financial transparency

⚡ Non-Obvious Q-B — The "What Metric Keeps You Up At Night" Probe

  • Cross-roads question (WH Smith archetype): "What single KPI, not in your public guidance framework, do you track most closely as a leading indicator of whether the strategy is working — and what is the current trend?"
  • Expected answer: A genuinely non-consensus internal metric (e.g. NPS trajectory in fibre overbuild zones, B2B attach rate per new logo, internal employee NPS as a leading culture indicator)
  • Good answer signature: Spontaneous, specific metric named that is NOT a headline reported figure; management can state the current trend without preparation; it is a metric they care about, not one that flatters
  • Bad answer signature: Falls back to NPS (already public) or revenue/EBITDAAL (guidance metrics); unable to name a metric that would worry them
  • Follow-up probe: "And if that metric moved 10% against you over the next 6 months, what would be your first operational response?"
  • Diagnostic note: This question has maximum diagnostic power for operator quality — it cannot be prepared for without genuine operational depth; a spontaneous, honest answer is highly bull-confirmatory; a retreated-to-guidance answer is a material warning signal

⚡ Non-Obvious Q-C — The "Multiple Truths" Probe (Concept B CA3)

  • Multiple-truths probe: "I want to understand both sides — the bull case for Telia is strong FCF yield + oligopoly pricing + dividend durability. The bear case is that FCF is overstated by IFRS 16, competitive intensity from overbuild is underestimated, and 5G monetisation is delayed by 3-5 years vs original expectations. Which part of the bear case do you think the market underestimates?"
  • Expected answer (diagnostic): Management engages with the bear case and nominates the most plausible near-term risk — the answer itself discriminates between a management team that has genuinely stress-tested the investment case vs one that will defend the bull case under all conditions
  • Good answer signature: Management volunteers the one bear-case item they are MOST worried about; it is specific; it is not "macro uncertainty"
  • Bad answer signature: "We're confident in our strategy and the bear case doesn't reflect the underlying strength" — a full deflection is the worst possible answer because it signals either that management has not stress-tested, or that they have and won't disclose
