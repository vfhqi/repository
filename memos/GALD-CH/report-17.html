KEY FINDINGS - Concentrated USD/EUR bilateral mismatch: Galderma generates ~46% of revenue in USD/USD-linked currencies but reports in USD — however cost base carries a meaningful CHF and EUR component (HQ overhead, R&D, manufacturing in Switzerland and Europe), creating a structural cost-currency mismatch that is not fully visible from revenue mix alone

  • Translation exposure is the primary mechanism: ~54% of revenue originates outside North America, primarily Europe, Asia-Pacific and EM; a USD-strengthening scenario (e.g. USD +10% broad trade-weighted) creates a c.CHF 160-200m revenue headwind and c.CHF 30-50m EBITDA drag at current scale — moderately significant but not thesis-breaking at moderate move magnitudes

  • Transaction exposure is partially mitigated by natural hedging in manufacturing: key manufacturing hubs in Sweden (Bioglan), France, Switzerland, and the US provide some natural matching, but the higher-value dermatology biologics (Nemolizumab pipeline cost base is USD-heavy) vs. predominantly ex-US revenue skew creates a transaction net-short position on USD at the EBITDA margin level

  • Hedging policy is disclosed but thin on specifics: Galderma discloses that it hedges "significant" transaction exposures via forward contracts for 6-12 month horizons; the company does NOT hedge translation exposure (standard for a company of its size and listing); hedge ratios, notional amounts, and effectiveness metrics are partially disclosed in the IFRS 7 note but not presented in a management-authored sensitivity table format that matches the precision of larger-cap peers

  • Sensitivity: base case CHF/USD move: at a 10% USD appreciation vs the EUR/CHF basket, Galderma's reported revenue would decline c.2.5-3.5% on a constant-currency basis vs reported basis, and reported EBITDA margin would compress c.30-50bps — manageable but not trivial for a margin-expansion thesis

  • FX was a modest tailwind in 2023 and turned neutral-to-slight headwind in 2024: constant-currency revenue growth outpaced reported growth in 2022 (FX headwind) and the gap closed in 2023-2024; the regime shift in USD/EUR-CHF dynamics since mid-2024 (EUR/CHF appreciation, USD softening) has moved from headwind to a tentative tailwind in H2 2024 and 2025 trending

  • Macro N12-24M outlook: USD softening = net tailwind for Galderma: consensus FX forecasts (Bloomberg composite, May 2026) point to mild USD depreciation vs EUR and neutral-to-weak USD vs CHF, which would mechanically boost reported revenue from ex-US operations when translated back into USD — a modest but real tailwind to reported numbers vs organic

  • Single largest FX risk: a sharp USD re-strengthening scenario driven by US fiscal stress or flight-to-quality event would compress reported ex-US revenue materially; combined with CHF strength (typical safe-haven correlation), this double-move scenario could create c.5-7% reported revenue headwind — the scenario where both translation AND transaction work adversely simultaneously

  • Peer gap is the key QC caveat: no cohort or sector peer data was injected; comparative hedging-discipline grading relative to named dermatology/aesthetics peers (Revance, Evolus, Ipsen, Recordati) is not possible in this run — flagged explicitly in QC

§1 — Revenue by Currency

J-front verdict: Revenue is geographically diversified across three blocs (Americas ~46%, EMEA ~35%, APAC ~19%) but reported in USD, creating meaningful translation exposure to EUR, CHF, and Asian currencies with a mild long-USD structural tilt

Revenue by Currency — Analytical Bullets

  • Reporting currency: USD — Galderma reports consolidated financials in USD despite Swiss listing (GALD-CH on SIX) and Swiss HQ; this means all non-USD revenue blocs are subject to translation into USD for reporting purposes; no economic reason to suspect the USD-reporting choice itself creates artificial exposure, but it does mean USD strength consistently flatters/depresses reported numbers vs economic reality

  • Geographic revenue structure (FY2024): Galderma's three segments (Injectable Aesthetics, Dermatological Skincare, Therapeutic Dermatology) have different geographic mixes — Injectable Aesthetics is most North America-weighted; Therapeutic Dermatology (Nemolizumab, Toctino) carries a heavier EU/global mix; Skincare (Cetaphil, Benzac) is most EM/APAC-weighted

  • Approximate revenue-currency split (derived from geographic revenue disclosures and segment product positioning):

  • USD revenue %: ~44-46% (US direct + USD-invoiced EM markets)

  • Mix trend L3Y: North America share has been gradually increasing as Injectable Aesthetics (US Dysport market, Sculptra US) grew faster than Skincare EM; this implies reporting-currency share has risen slightly — a modest structural improvement in translation risk, but offset by the fact that North America is also where cost inflation (labour, clinical trials) is highest

§2 — Cost by Currency

J-front verdict: Cost base is structurally CHF and EUR-heavy (HQ in Zug, major manufacturing in Europe), creating a net short-USD position on costs vs revenues — costs partially offset USD revenue strength but amplify USD weakness scenarios for margins

Cost by Currency — Analytical Bullets

  • CHF as the primary cost-heavy non-revenue currency: Galderma's HQ is in Zug, Switzerland; senior management, finance, legal, regulatory affairs, and a significant portion of R&D administration are CHF-denominated; CHF strength (USD weakness) directly inflates USD-reported overhead costs without a matching revenue offset — the textbook "cost-currency mismatch"

  • EUR manufacturing and COGS: key fill-finish and formulation sites are located in France (Sophia Antipolis heritage from former L'Oréal / Nestlé era), Sweden (Bioglan acquisition), and Germany (distribution); COGS for European products are primarily EUR — partially matched by EUR revenue but with a broader EUR cost footprint than EUR revenue (Europe produces for global export)

  • USD COGS: North America manufacturing includes botulinum toxin (Dysport) US bulk processing and some fill-finish; US clinical trial costs are USD; North America accounts for an estimated 35-40% of COGS, aligned with revenue, creating reasonable natural hedging in the largest single market

  • Opex currency mix:

  • CHF: ~18-22% of total opex (HQ, group functions, R&D administration)
  • EUR: ~25-30% of total opex (European commercial operations, EU manufacturing overhead)
  • USD: ~35-40% of total opex (North America commercial, US-based R&D programmes, Nemolizumab clinical costs)

  • Other: ~10-15% (APAC commercial, LatAm, other)

  • Capex currency: primarily EUR and CHF (European manufacturing investment, IT infrastructure in Switzerland); a minority USD capex (US commercial infrastructure); capex currency mismatch is less material on an annual basis but relevant for multi-year expansion plans

  • Natural hedge ratio (rough estimate):

  • USD revenue ~44-46% vs USD cost ~35-40% → net long USD on revenue vs costs: ~5-10pp surplus; USD strength benefits margins modestly
  • EUR revenue ~22-25% vs EUR cost ~25-30% → net short EUR slightly: EUR strength marginally hurts margins

  • CHF revenue ~2-3% vs CHF cost ~18-22% → severe CHF mismatch: CHF strength directly hurts margins; this is the most exposed bilateral pair

  • The CHF mismatch is the structurally under-appreciated exposure: a CHF +10% move vs USD on a revenue base of ~$3.5-4bn with c.20% of costs in CHF and only 2-3% of revenue in CHF implies a c.USD 55-70m EBITDA headwind from cost inflation alone — equivalent to c.150-200bps of EBITDA margin compression at current scale

§3 — Translation Exposure

J-front verdict: Translation exposure is moderate and L3Y has averaged a c.1-1.5pp annual revenue drag; the 2022 spike (c.3-4pp headwind) illustrates the vulnerability in sustained USD-strength cycles; translation normalises over cycles but is not negligible

Translation Exposure — Analytical Bullets

  • Reported FX impact L1Y (FY2024): constant-currency revenue growth ~8-9%; reported revenue growth ~8-9%; FX delta approximately flat to –0.5pp — translation was effectively neutral in FY2024 as USD ended the year close to its 2023 average vs the EUR/CHF basket

  • Reported FX impact FY2023: constant-currency growth ~10-11%; reported ~10-11%; FX approximately neutral — 2023 was a year where EUR recovered vs 2022 lows, partially reversing the 2022 translation headwind

  • Reported FX impact FY2022: the most significant recent translation year; Galderma (pre-IPO, reported as private entity) experienced c.3-4pp revenue drag as USD strengthened sharply (DXY +15% in 2022); this was the stressed-year data point; EBITDA margin was compressed c.30-50bps purely from translation

  • Reporting currency trajectory: USD has been in a mild structural softening trend since late 2022 peak (DXY from ~114 peak Oct 2022 to ~100-105 range by 2025); if this trend continues, translation becomes a tailwind in 2025-2026 — reported numbers would exceed constant-currency growth for the first time in several years on a sustained basis

  • Translation does NOT affect Galderma's cash generation in economic terms — but it does affect: (1) reported EPS (directly), (2) net debt/EBITDA leverage ratio if EBITDA is translated lower, and (3) investor perception of growth quality; for Richard's 18-24M EPS horizon, translation is the mechanism most likely to cause reported EPS to miss consensus without any organic deterioration

  • Balance sheet translation: Galderma carries net assets denominated in EUR and CHF; currency movements affect equity through the other comprehensive income line via the currency translation adjustment reserve; this is a P&L bypass but affects book value and, at stressed levels, could mechanically affect covenant calculations if leverage covenants reference USD-reported EBITDA

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on translation is that the FY2022 stress scenario could recur and be amplified if: (1) Galderma's APAC revenue mix grows as planned (adding more non-USD translation exposure), and (2) a concurrent USD re-strengthening shock occurs at a point when consensus forecasts have modelled flat-FX revenue growth; the consensus habitually under-hedges translation risk in models because management guidance is typically given at constant currency — a 3-4pp translation drag in a year when the consensus models +10% revenue growth would produce a reported +6-7% — a significant negative surprise without any operational deterioration

§4 — Transaction Exposure

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is partially naturally hedged (US revenue vs US cost roughly matched) but the CHF cost / non-CHF revenue mismatch creates a structural transaction-short-CHF position that is economically real and not eliminated by forward hedging

Transaction Exposure — Analytical Bullets

  • Transaction exposure definition applied here: economic cash-flow impact where Galderma receives revenue in currency A but must pay costs in currency B — the conversion at the prevailing spot rate creates a margin impact that is real and not reversed by subsequent FX moves (unlike translation)

  • Primary transaction exposure pairs:

  • EUR COGS / EUR revenue: partially matched — European manufacturing produces for European markets; residual mismatch exists where European production is exported (e.g. European-produced Cetaphil exported to EM)
  • CHF overhead / non-CHF revenue: this is the core structural transaction exposure — HQ costs in CHF must be paid out of consolidated USD cash flows; CHF appreciation vs USD raises real costs denominated in CHF when paid in USD terms

  • USD R&D / non-USD revenue: Nemolizumab Phase 3 and ongoing pipeline spend is predominantly USD (US clinical trial sites, US regulatory costs); this is funded from USD revenues and USD balance sheet — naturally matched; however, if USD weakens while pipeline cost inflation runs hot, the margin squeeze is real

  • Natural hedge assessment — transaction level:

  • North America : revenue USD ~44-46% vs cost USD ~36-40% — net long USD by ~5-8pp; USD strength = margin benefit
  • CHF: the most exposed bilateral; CHF revenue ~2-3% vs CHF cost ~14-18% — net short CHF by ~12-15pp; every 10% CHF appreciation vs USD costs Galderma c.USD 55-70m pre-tax (approximately 150-180bps EBITDA margin)

  • EUR: mild net short (costs slightly exceed revenue); EUR appreciation marginally hurts but magnitude is modest

  • Magnitude vs translation — comparison:

  • Translation: primarily a revenue line item; reverses over cycles; does not affect cash flow

  • Transaction (CHF mismatch): affects EBITDA and cash flow directly; does NOT reverse — CHF wages do not fall back when CHF depreciates; this asymmetry means the transaction exposure to CHF is more durable and arguably more insidious than the translation exposure

  • Pricing pass-through:

  • Injectable Aesthetics (Dysport, Sculptra): pricing is relatively inelastic and premium; some limited ability to pass CHF-driven cost increases through to distributors over 12-24 months; NOT immediate
  • Dermatological Skincare (Cetaphil): consumer-facing pricing — high price sensitivity; pass-through is slow and partial (estimated 30-50% pass-through of cost inflation over 18-24M); EM markets have additional FX constraint where local currency weakness limits absolute price increases

  • Therapeutic Dermatology (emerging): reimbursed market pricing in Europe/Japan — pass-through is effectively zero over a 3-year horizon; prices are set by health authorities and not responsive to Galderma's input cost FX

  • Cost pass-through ability assessment: mixed — high in Aesthetics (US/Europe premium), low in Skincare EM, effectively zero in Therapeutics — weighted average pass-through is probably 30-40% of a CHF-driven cost shock over 18-24M

  • Implied transaction exposure magnitude (illustrative):

  • CHF +10% vs USD, all else equal: EBITDA impact c. –USD 55-70m (–150 to –180bps margin)
  • EUR +10% vs USD, all else equal: EBITDA impact c. –USD 15-25m (–40 to –60bps margin), partially offset by EUR revenue translation gain
  • USD +10% vs EM basket: EBITDA impact c. +USD 20-35m (cost reduction > revenue loss at current mix)

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on transaction exposure is that the pricing-power assumption in Aesthetics (the segment where pass-through appears highest) is fragile specifically when a CHF shock coincides with a broader consumer-spending slowdown — in a risk-off macro environment, the very scenario that drives CHF strength (safe-haven demand) also suppresses consumer discretionary spending on elective aesthetics procedures; Galderma's pricing power in the channel most relied upon to absorb CHF cost shocks degrades precisely when it is most needed; the timing correlation is adverse, not neutral

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Hedging policy is disclosed as transaction-focused, forward-contract-based, with a 6-12 month horizon — functional but thin duration; translation exposure explicitly unhedged; hedge ratio and notional not quantified in investor-accessible format

Hedging Policy Assessment — Analytical Bullets

  • Hedging mandate (disclosed): Galderma's Annual Report (Notes to Financial Statements, financial risk management section) states that the Group hedges "significant foreign currency transaction exposures" — the language is appropriately conservative but lacks specificity on threshold, ratio, and horizon that would allow independent verification

  • Translation hedging: explicitly NOT hedged — consistent with standard practice for large-cap multinationals (hedging translation is expensive, accounting-complex under IFRS, and creates own mark-to-market volatility); the decision not to hedge translation is correct treasury practice but means the full revenue/EBITDA translation impact flows through reported numbers

  • Transaction hedging — disclosed instruments: forward foreign exchange contracts are the primary instrument; the IFRS 7 note in the 2024 Annual Report discloses derivative financial instruments including FX forwards (typically net-settled); options are not prominently disclosed (possibly used but not the primary tool)

  • Hedge horizon: the company discloses hedging over "the next 6 to 12 months" for identified transaction exposures — this is a short-to-mid horizon by sector standards; a 12-month maximum means that a sustained FX shock beyond 12 months is entirely unhedged; compare to best practice (18-24M rolling horizon with a laddered structure)

  • Hedge ratio: NOT explicitly stated in public disclosures in percentage terms; IFRS 7 disclosure shows the fair value of outstanding derivatives and the notional amounts; triangulating from the notional disclosure against estimated annual transaction exposure suggests a hedge ratio of approximately 50-70% of near-term (0-6M) identified exposures, declining to perhaps 20-40% for 6-12M exposures — these are researcher estimates, not disclosed figures

  • Hedge effectiveness L3Y:

  • IFRS 9 hedge accounting requires effectiveness testing; Galderma uses cash-flow hedge accounting for qualifying hedges — the OCI recycling treatment means effective hedges are recognised in OCI until the hedged item affects profit/loss
  • Hedge ineffectiveness amounts disclosed in the income statement (gains/losses on ineffective portion) have been small in FY2022-2024 — suggesting the designated hedges have been technically effective within the 80-125% effectiveness corridor

  • However: technical IFRS effectiveness ≠ economic completeness; hedges cover a fraction of total exposure, so a portion of FX-driven P&L impact is always unhedged

  • Hedge cost / income:

  • Forward contracts carry an implicit cost equal to the forward points (interest rate differential) — in the 2022-2024 period, USD rates were higher than EUR/CHF rates, meaning USD/EUR and USD/CHF forward contracts carried a cost to the hedger (selling USD forward to buy EUR/CHF was negative carry given USD premium)
  • This hedge carry cost reduces the economic efficiency of the hedging programme; in 2022-2024, this cost was meaningful given the wide USD-EUR rate differential

  • As rate differentials normalise (USD rates declining, EUR rates stable-to-declining), hedge carry cost should reduce

  • CHF-specific hedging: the CHF cost base is a structural cash cost (wages, rent, utilities) rather than a transactional flow — structural costs are harder to hedge via forwards (you would need to hedge an indefinite series of monthly CHF cash outflows); the company likely hedges a portion of near-term CHF overhead commitments but cannot hedge the structural competitiveness of being a Swiss-HQ company

  • Hedge roll-off risk: the disclosed 6-12M horizon means that in a sustained FX move (e.g. a 24-36M USD weakness or CHF strength cycle), hedges roll off progressively at worse rates; the "hedge cliff" occurs at month 13 when all remaining unhedged exposure becomes spot-rate dependent; this is a material risk in a sustained-shock scenario

  • Counterparty concentration: not disclosed explicitly; standard practice for a CHF-listed company is to use Swiss and European banking counterparties (likely UBS, Credit Suisse/UBS post-merger, BNP, Deutsche Bank, HSBC); no disclosure of concentration risk; no evidence of concern but also no evidence of active counterparty diversification policy

  • Disclosure quality assessment vs sector principles:

  • Hedge ratio: not disclosed (opaque)
  • Hedge horizon: disclosed (6-12M — disclosed but short)
  • Instrument mix: forwards primary (disclosed, narrow)
  • Effectiveness metrics: IFRS 9 note present (technically adequate)
  • Natural hedge disclosure: not quantified (absent)
  • Counter-cyclical vs pro-cyclical: not disclosed (unknown)

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on hedging discipline is that the combination of (1) non-disclosed hedge ratio, (2) 6-12M maximum horizon, and (3) CHF structural cost not amenable to financial hedging means the hedging programme is best characterised as a "timing smoother" rather than a genuine risk-reduction mechanism; in a scenario where USD depreciates 15-20% over 18 months (consistent with USD bear cycles), the first 6-12 months of hedging buys time but the second year of the move is fully unhedged — and the CHF structural costs never get hedged at all; the hedge provides false confidence in a sustained-shock scenario

§6 — Quantified Sensitivity Analysis

J-front verdict: A 10% broad USD depreciation generates approximately a 2.5-3.5pp revenue headwind on reported numbers and c.30-50bps EBITDA margin drag from translation; the more insidious and durable risk is CHF strength driving structural cost inflation — a 10% CHF appreciation generates c.150-180bps EBITDA margin compression through transaction exposure that financial hedging only partially and temporarily offsets

Sensitivity Analysis — Core Mechanics

  • Revenue sensitivity — translation channel:
  • Non-USD revenue share ~54-56%; a 10% uniform depreciation of all non-USD currencies vs USD reduces reported revenue by c.5.4-5.6pp (mechanical translation)
  • However, currencies do not move uniformly; the EUR (22-25% of revenue) is the single largest variable — EUR ±10% = ±2.2-2.5pp revenue impact
  • CNY/APAC basket (14-16% of revenue): CNY ±10% = ±1.4-1.6pp revenue impact
  • GBP (2-3% of revenue): GBP ±10% = ±0.2-0.3pp — minor

  • CHF revenue (2-3%): CHF ±10% = ±0.2-0.3pp on revenue — minor, but CHF matters enormously on cost side

  • EBITDA sensitivity — translation channel:

  • Translation affects EBITDA less than proportionally to revenue because costs are also partially in the same currencies
  • EUR revenue 22-25% vs EUR cost 26-30% → net EUR position is marginally short; EUR depreciation (vs USD) = small EBITDA benefit on balance (cost savings slightly exceed revenue loss in translation terms)

  • CNY/APAC: long revenue vs cost → CNY depreciation = EBITDA translation hit (revenue loss > cost saving)

  • EBITDA sensitivity — transaction channel:

  • CHF appreciation is the load-bearing transaction risk: CHF +10% → c.USD 55-70m EBITDA headwind (–150 to –180bps at c.USD 3.8bn revenue and ~28% EBITDA margin base)
  • EUR strength: mild headwind (costs > revenue in EUR on a net basis) — EUR +10% → c.USD 15-25m EBITDA drag (–40 to –60bps)
  • USD strength: mild EBITDA tailwind — net long USD in operations; USD +10% vs broad basket → c.+USD 20-35m EBITDA benefit (+50 to +90bps) through lower relative cost base
