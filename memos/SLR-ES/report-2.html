§8 — Value chain — Disruption

J-front verdict: Technology disruption is a net positive for Solaria's cost structure (module cost deflation) but introduces a potential negative via battery storage requirements changing the revenue model [#J]

  • Technology disruption — modules: Chinese solar panel prices fell ~50% over L18M (2023-2024) — Longi, Jinko, Trina pricing to ~€0.09-0.12/Wp vs ~€0.20-0.25/Wp in 2022 [#A]
  • Benefit to Solaria: lower CAPEX per MW on new projects (~15-20% CAPEX reduction) — EPC cost per MW now ~€500-600k vs ~€700-800k in 2022

  • Risk: margin compression on EPC for peers; for Solaria as pure IPP, the benefit flows directly to project IRR improvement

  • Technology disruption — battery storage : Grid operators are increasingly requiring storage to be co-located with solar to qualify for grid connection — this is an emerging regulatory-technical requirement in Spain [#A]

  • Impact: BESS adds ~20-30% to project CAPEX; changes the revenue model (adds capacity market and balancing revenue but reduces pure solar merchant exposure)

  • Solaria's current pipeline disclosure does not comprehensively address BESS integration — a potential gap vs market requirement

  • New entrant — innovative: Agrivoltaic and floating solar specialists are creating new use cases for land that Solaria's current land bank may not support [#A]

  • Near-term irrelevance: agrivoltaic is <1% of Spanish market; floating solar non-existent at scale in Spain

  • Disruption pace: Module cost deflation appears to be moderating after 2024 trough; further 20-30% decline possible over 2025-2027 but the step-change from 2022-2024 is largely played out [#A]

  • Incumbent response: Solaria has not publicly disclosed a BESS integration strategy — management has mentioned storage as "complementary" but no project-level BESS disclosure [#I] ❌

  • Peer contrast: Grenergy has explicitly disclosed hybrid solar+storage projects in pipeline; Iberdrola has integrated BESS into its solar development programme

  • Disruption cost: BESS integration cost risk: if grid connection regulations tighten to require co-located storage, Solaria's existing pipeline CAPEX estimates would need upward revision of 20-30% [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish disruption read is that Solaria is moving slower than peers on BESS integration; grid operators' storage requirements will tighten faster than expected (Spain has a 2030 interconnection + storage mandate framework); and the company's pipeline economics were modelled without storage, meaning IRR degradation risk is real if storage becomes a prerequisite for grid connection [#J]

§9 — Value chain — Political and regulatory

J-front verdict: Spanish regulatory risk is the single most material external risk to the investment case — retroactive intervention has already occurred once and the political environment remains volatile [#J]

  • Regulatory shift — retroactive tax: Spain imposed a "windfall profits" energy tax in late 2021/2022 that applied retroactively to renewable energy revenues above a cap (~€67/MWh threshold) — directly reduced Solaria's reported earnings in 2022-2023 [#A] ❌
  • Legal status: challenged by Solaria and other IPPs; Spanish Supreme Court review ongoing; outcome uncertain but even partial reinstatement would be a positive catalyst

  • Peer context: Italy imposed similar windfall tax; France did not; UK's EPL (Energy Profits Levy) applied to fossil fuels not renewables — Spain's renewables-specific windfall tax is more aggressive than most EU peer markets

  • Regulatory shift — NECP (National Energy and Climate Plan): Spain's updated NECP targets 74% renewable electricity by 2030 (from ~60% in 2024) — structurally supportive of solar development [#A]

  • But: the gap between NECP targets and grid connection capacity is large; REE (Red Eléctrica de España) grid investment lags renewable pipeline by 3-5 years

  • Political actor — Spanish government: Coalition government (PSOE/Sumar) is renewables-supportive in principle but has used renewable sector as a fiscal transfer mechanism — windfall tax reflects this dynamic [#A]

  • N3Y risk: upcoming Spanish elections (next scheduled 2027 unless snap election) could shift the policy pendulum; PP (centre-right opposition) has historically been less interventionist on energy but more permissive of fossil fuel incumbents

  • Geography — Spain concentration: ~90%+ of Solaria's operational revenue is Spain-sourced — unique concentration risk vs pan-European peers [#A] ❌

  • Peer anchor: Encavis operated across Germany, Netherlands, Italy, Denmark; Neoen operates across France, Australia, Scandinavia — geographic diversification materially reduces regulatory concentration risk

  • Tariff/subsidy: Spain's RECORE (renewable energy support scheme) provides floor pricing for pre-2013 plants; newer projects operate merchant or PPA — no subsidy dependency for post-2018 development [#I]

  • This is actually a structural positive: no subsidy cliff risk on the growth pipeline

  • Environmental regulation: Spain's permitting process includes environmental impact assessments that have been a source of project delays — local community opposition to large-scale solar in Extremadura and Castilla-La Mancha is rising [#A]

  • Delay impact: 6-24 months of additional timeline from EIA challenges; several Solaria projects have faced this

  • Trade policy: EU tariff on Chinese solar panels (AD/CVD investigation ongoing 2024-2025) could raise panel costs ~10-15% if implemented — partially reversing the module cost deflation benefit [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish regulatory read is that Spain's coalition government has demonstrated willingness to retroactively intervene in energy markets; a second windfall tax iteration cannot be ruled out if Spanish power prices re-spike (plausible on AI data-centre demand surge); and EU AD/CVD tariffs on Chinese panels would raise Solaria's construction CAPEX precisely when the pipeline is scaling fastest — a double regulatory headwind scenario [#J]

§10 — Value chain — Supply side

J-front verdict: Supply-side conditions are favourable — module oversupply has structurally lowered CAPEX inputs, and EPC contractor capacity in Spain is ample — but concentration in Chinese module supply creates geopolitical vulnerability [#J]

  • Supplier economics: Chinese solar panel manufacturers (Longi, Jinko, Trina, Canadian Solar) are operating at negative-to-breakeven margins — panel pricing below cash cost of production for some manufacturers in 2024 [#A]

  • Implication for Solaria: near-term CAPEX deflation benefit is real but relies on Chinese overcapacity persisting; any Chinese supply rationalisation or EU tariff implementation reverses this

  • Concentration — modules: ~80-85% of global solar module supply is Chinese-manufactured; Solaria's construction programme is dependent on this supply chain [#A] ❌

  • Peer anchor: this is industry-wide, not Solaria-specific; Iberdrola, Encavis, and all European solar developers face identical concentration risk — but risk is real and systemically under-priced

  • Supplier health: Chinese solar manufacturers are financially stressed — multiple second-tier manufacturers (Yingli, etc.) have filed for restructuring in recent years [#I]

  • Top-tier (Longi, Jinko, Trina) remain solvent but margin-compressed; supply availability is not currently a risk

  • EPC contractor supply: Spain has abundant EPC contractors for utility-scale solar (ACCIONA Engineering, ACS, Abengoa components, independent regional contractors) [#A]

  • Labour market tightness: skilled electrical/civil workers are increasingly scarce in high-activity solar regions; labour cost inflation in Extremadura/Andalucia running +8-12% per annum (2023-2024 data)

  • Vertical integration: Solaria is not vertically integrated — no module manufacturing, no EPC in-house; pure developer-operator model [#I]

  • This limits upside capture on the full value chain but also limits supply-side operational risk

  • Backward integration: No backward integration signalled; Solaria's strategic preference is to remain asset-light on construction [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish supply-side read is that Chinese module prices will partially recover as manufacturers rationalise capacity; EU AD/CVD tariffs could accelerate this; combined with labour cost inflation in Spanish construction, CAPEX per MW on 2025-2026 pipeline builds could run 15-25% above plan — directly compressing project IRRs below the hurdle rate at which pipeline was originally sanctioned [#J]

  • Cyclicality — business model: Solaria's revenue has two overlapping cycles: (1) the project development/construction cycle (3-7 years per project) and (2) the Spanish wholesale electricity price cycle (2-4 year periods) [#A]

  • These cycles currently diverge: Solaria is in mid-to-early build cycle on capacity (positive for medium-term EPS) while Spanish power prices are in post-spike normalisation (negative for near-term EPS)

  • Cycle stage — capacity: ~1.3 GW operational, ~0.5-0.7 GW under construction, ~4-5 GW in development/permitting — the company is in early-to-mid execution of the 6 GW programme [#A]

  • Peak CAPEX phase: 2025-2026; EPS inflection expected 2026-2028 as operational MW base compounds
