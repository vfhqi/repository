KEY FINDINGS Elia is among Europe's most compelling regulated infrastructure compounders at a structural inflection point — the energy transition mandates rapid RAB growth, and Elia holds monopoly franchises in two of the continent's most investment-intensive grids.

  • Franchise quality: Elia holds two legally protected monopoly franchises — Belgian high-voltage grid and 80.4% of 50Hertz (one of Germany's four TSOs) — delivering a near-zero-churn customer base and regulatory tariff certainty across multi-year reset periods. No competitor can enter either market; the moat is perpetual by legislation.

  • RAB growth velocity — exceptional vs peers: Group RAB reached €22.6bn at end-2025 (up 22.5% year-on-year) and is guided >20% average annual growth to 2028 under a €31.6bn capex plan — the fastest RAB compounding among all listed European TSO peers. Terna (Italy) runs c.€16bn RAB at c.6% CAGR; REE (Spain) c.€10bn at c.4% CAGR; Elia's growth trajectory is 3–5x the sector median.

  • Earnings quality — mechanistic compounder: Adjusted net profit grew 32.1% to €556.6m in FY25; EBITDA +17.2% to €1,794.5m; FY26 guidance €690–740m (+28% YoY at midpoint). EPS compounding is directly mechanistic: RAB × allowed equity return = regulated profit, with no demand-cycle, no pricing risk, no customer churn exposure.

  • German regulatory uncertainty — binary risk: BNetzA published a December 2025 draft for the new RP5 framework from 2029, transitioning to a dynamic cost-plus WACC model. Outcome is unresolved until autumn 2026: WACC above current 2.3% base is a positive catalyst; WACC below Elia's cost of equity is a material earnings headwind on 50Hertz's rapidly growing RAB (~60% of group profit).

  • Belgian regulatory cycle: constructive draft for 2028–2031: CREG April 2026 draft proposes max total incentive of 3.75% (vs 2.43% prior) targeting regulated ROE of 8.37% in 2028 — higher than current period, partially offsetting the lower risk-free rate floor of 1.0%.

  • CEO transition — continuity with calibre question: Bernard Gustin (former Brussels Airlines CEO, ex-Board Chairman, effective January 2025) brings cross-industry credentials but has not previously managed a multi-billion-euro infrastructure capex programme as executive CEO — execution calibre at this scale is unproven. 50Hertz's Stefan Kapferer (Deputy CEO + CTO from Oct 2025) provides operational counterweight.

  • Capital intensity — persistent equity dilution: The €31.6bn capex plan requires sustained equity injections; a €2.2bn raise (rights issue + PIPE) was completed H1 2025. Remaining equity funding gap of €1.8–2.3bn through 2028; management prefers non-dilutive options (hybrid bonds, minority stake sales); April 2026 €900m hybrid at 4.625% demonstrates capacity.

  • Thematic fit — direct pure-play on grid hardening: ENTSO-E TYNDP 2025 calls for >100,000km new transmission lines and €477–584bn European grid investment by 2030–2040. Elia's capex plan alone (€31.6bn) represents ~5% of total European grid investment need — unusually concentrated exposure to the structural tailwind.

§1 — COMPANY OVERVIEW & STRUCTURE

Elia Group is a large-cap Belgian-listed regulated utility with an unusually high-velocity RAB growth profile — more accurately described as a regulated infrastructure compounder than a static utility.

Elia Group SA/NV is a Belgian holding company owning: (1) Elia Transmission Belgium — Belgium's sole high-voltage electricity TSO; and (2) 80.4% of Eurogrid International, which owns 100% of 50Hertz Transmission GmbH — one of Germany's four TSOs.

  • Listing: Euronext Brussels (ELI.BR); ISIN BE0003822393; cross-listed Vienna; included in BEL 20
  • Market cap: c.€12.7bn (post-2025 equity raise); share count c.87–109m
  • Legal structure: Elia Group (listed HoldCo) → 100% ETB + 100% Eurogrid International → 80% Eurogrid GmbH → 100% 50Hertz. KfW (German state bank) holds 20% of 50Hertz on behalf of the German Federal Government
  • Reference shareholder: Publi-T cooperative (Belgian municipalities) controls ~44.8% of Elia Group voting rights via NextGrid Holding
  • Operations: Over 19,000 km of high-voltage transmission infrastructure; serves approximately 30 million end users across Belgium and northeastern Germany; regulated activities generate approximately 98% of group EBITDA.
  • Employees: c.4,500 across both operating subsidiaries

Executive Management — 2025 Reorganisation

  • Bernard Gustin (CEO, eff. 15-Jan-25): Previously Chairman of Elia Group Board; ex-Brussels Airlines CEO; cross-industry background. Elected Chairman of 50Hertz supervisory board August 2025.
  • Marco Nix (CFO, eff. 1-Apr-25): Promoted from decade-long tenure as 50Hertz CFO. Deep German regulatory and capex financing expertise at group level.

§2 — REVENUE MODEL & REGULATORY FRAMEWORK

Elia's revenue model is structurally simple and almost entirely de-risked from demand, pricing, and competitive forces — RAB growth translates mechanistically into regulated profit.

Belgium — CREG Tariff Framework

  • Regulatory model: Cost-plus; CREG (Commission for Electricity and Gas Regulation) approves a 4-year tariff in advance covering allowed depreciation, operating costs, and regulated equity return
  • 2024–2027 tariff: Approved November 2023; ~77% cumulative rise over 2020–2023 tariffs, reflecting the €6.4bn Belgian investment plan; tariff uplift is mechanically flowing through P&L as capex converts to RAB
  • Allowed equity return mechanism: Tied to 10-year Belgian government bond rate (OLO10Y); tariff assumes OLO10Y of 3.27%; Belgian gearing target 40% equity / 60% debt
  • Pass-throughs: Balancing costs, network losses, system services, offshore levies — largely passed through to grid users, protecting ETB net margin
  • CREG April 2026 draft for 2028–2031: Draft removes historical distinction between existing and new RAB; lowers risk-free rate floor to 1.0% (from 1.68%); raises maximum total incentive to 3.75% (from 2.43%). CREG calculation: regulated ROE after corporate tax 8.37% in 2028, descending to 8.08% by 2031. Bernstein assumes OLO of 3.5%: baseline ROE 5.9% + 2.0% incentive capture + 0.3% MOG incentives = ~8.2% total ROE.
  • Political risk: Belgian transmission tariffs already among EU's highest; FEBELIEC (industrial federation) has formally objected to 77% tariff rise; regulatory reset in 2028 could face political headwinds if industrial competitiveness remains a policy concern.

Germany (50Hertz) — BNetzA Framework

  • Current framework (4th Regulatory Period, 2024–2028): Cost-plus incentive system; revenue allowance based on 2021 cost base + efficiency factors + inflation
  • 50Hertz Xind = 100%: No BNetzA efficiency deduction applied — confirms 50Hertz is at the regulatory efficiency frontier. Significant positive signal.
  • Floating base rate (new assets from 2024): Pre-2024 assets earn 2.3% equity base rate. Post-2024 assets earn a floating rate linked to market rates — driving the 42.6% adjusted net profit growth at 50Hertz in FY2025.
  • BNetzA December 2025 draft for RP5 (2029+): Fundamental revision from five-year revenue-cap to dynamic annual cost-plus approach; harmonises onshore and offshore under uniform WACC-based remuneration; December 2025 draft did NOT retain explicit ROE adder advocated by TSOs — disappointing to sector; final decision scheduled autumn 2026. Key improvement: arithmetic average return spread (vs prior geometric/arithmetic mix) drives higher WACC value. Gearing maintained 60% debt / 40% equity.
  • Jefferies forecast: Consolidated ROE rises from 7.9% (2023) to 8.9% (2030E); ETB stable 7.2–7.8%; 50Hertz above 9%.

Revenue Mechanics — How Value Is Created

  • Core mechanic: RAB × allowed equity return rate = regulated equity income. Each €1bn of commissioned capex earns ~€40–50m of incremental annual earnings at 4–5% WACC.
  • Capex = earnings-accretive: Unlike most businesses, capex is earnings-accretive at Elia. Risk: assets must be commissioned to earn; construction-in-progress does not earn.
  • Incentive upside: Both CREG and BNetzA include outperformance mechanisms; cost delivery below allowed amount is partially retained.
  • Customer moat: All electricity generators, all distribution operators, and all large industrials are legally mandated grid users. No elasticity of demand — transmission is essential infrastructure with no substitutes. Revenue certainty is effectively a regulatory toll.

§3 — INDUSTRY CONTEXT & COMPETITIVE STRUCTURE

European electricity transmission is in a once-in-generation mandatory build-out cycle; Elia is positioned at the intersection of the two most investment-intensive TSO geographies on the continent.

Structural Tailwinds — Policy-Mandated Investment

  • ENTSO-E TYNDP 2024: >100,000km new transmission lines needed; total grid investment requirement €477–584bn by 2030–2040; European TSOs face external funding needs c.€250bn by 2030 alone. This is a statutory, permanently-backstopped investment supercycle.
  • Belgian offshore wind: National NECP targets 5.8 GW offshore wind by 2030 (from c.2.3 GW); virtually all new capacity connects via Elia's grid and Princess Elisabeth Island
  • German Energiewende (50Hertz zone): Germany targets 50 GW offshore by 2030 (from c.8 GW); 50Hertz's Baltic/North Sea zone is the primary expansion area; north-to-south HVDC corridors are structurally mandated
  • Data centre demand surge: European data centres consumed 96 TWh in 2024; expected +150% to 236 TWh by 2035; Belgium and Germany are both tier-1 data centre markets; grid connection queues 2–10 years across EU markets.
  • Electrification: EV penetration, heat pump rollout, industrial decarbonisation all increase grid stress independent of renewables integration

Competitive Structure — Natural Monopoly

  • Legal monopoly: Elia is the sole licensed TSO in Belgium; 50Hertz operates in defined geographic northeast Germany with no zone overlap against the other three German TSOs (TenneT, Amprion, TransnetBW)
  • New entrant impossibility: Capital cost of replicating national grid is prohibitive; EU Directive 2019/944 mandates ownership unbundling — legally prohibits competitors
  • 50Hertz geographic advantage: 50Hertz's zone covers ~30% of Germany's onshore wind capacity (wind-rich coastal/northern regions) — structurally highest mandatory grid investment density among the four German TSOs, supporting the outsized capex programme.
  • Only "competition" is regulatory: The sole competitive dimension is demonstrating to CREG and BNetzA that investments are prudent and should be included in RAB. Regulatory disallowance is the only value-destroying mechanism.

Disruption Assessment — Low Near-Term Risk

  • DER/VPPs: Operate at distribution level (below TSO voltage); no structural threat to high-voltage transmission. Even at full scale, DERs reduce grid peak demand (helpful) but do not substitute bulk transmission.
  • HVDC technology learning curve: Ongoing improvement reduces cost per km — actually supportive; Elia/50Hertz are leading European HVDC deployers and benefit from lower costs.
  • Princess Elisabeth Island: Elia IS the infrastructure disruptor — world's first artificial offshore energy island; no peer equivalent in European TSO universe.
  • Long-range risk (post-2035): If distributed generation and demand-side flexibility scale dramatically, some ENTSO-E scenarios suggest lower transmission investment need — minimal relevance to 2025–2030 thesis.

§4 — FOUNDATION QUALITY

Elia scores strongly on franchise quality, value chain protection, and industry structure; the RONIC-vs-WACC question on legacy 50Hertz assets is the primary quality caveat.

Operator Quality

  • Bernard Gustin (CEO, Jan-25): Aviation/logistics background (Brussels Airlines, Lineas) + 8 years on Elia Board as Chairman. Test over 2025–2028: can Gustin maintain capital execution velocity Peeters established at the €5bn+/year level? Non-utility background is the primary uncertainty.
  • Chris Peeters legacy (CEO 2012–2023): Transformed Elia from Belgian-only TSO to European champion; consolidated 50Hertz stake from 60% to 80.4%; grew group RAB from ~€3bn to ~€14.5bn over 11 years; launched offshore wind strategy. A-tier European utility CEO track record.
  • Stefan Kapferer (50Hertz CEO + Elia Deputy CEO + CTO): Former BNetzA President — unique embedded regulatory intelligence at the exact point of maximum risk (2029 NEST reform). Genuinely rare structural moat in navigating the single most important regulatory review for the investment thesis.

MOAT — Regulatory Franchise

Elia's moat is among the most durable available in European equities. The legally mandated exclusive franchise — protected by EU Directive 2019/944 and Belgian/German national law — cannot expire, be competed away, or be disrupted by technology within any foreseeable investment horizon. The moat's only "renegotiation" is periodic regulatory reset where the return rate (not the franchise itself) is adjusted.

RONIC vs WACC — Key Value Creation Question

  • Belgium: Allowed RoE c.5–6% (OLO10Y + spread) broadly in line with cost of equity → value creation broadly neutral on new Belgian investment
  • Germany legacy (pre-2024 assets): Base equity return rate 2.3% — materially below any reasonable estimate of Elia's cost of equity (~7–9%). Investing at 2.3% vs ~8% cost of equity is value-dilutive in a strict accounting sense. This is the principal quality caveat on the 50Hertz earnings contribution.
  • Germany new assets (post-2024): Floating base rate linked to market rates — improving meaningfully; if RP5 WACC model sets allowed equity return at 4–5%, German business could approach value-neutral or marginally value-creative territory. This is the key upside scenario.
  • FCF: Deeply negative in current capex cycle — entirely expected; this is a RAB/earnings compounding story, not an FCF yield story.

Supply Chain — Live Constraint

  • Lead times: Large power transformers and HVDC cables now at 2–4 year lead times; all four German TSOs plus UK/Dutch/Danish/Belgian TSOs simultaneously ramping capex — equipment manufacturing capacity near fully booked to 2028–2030.
  • Mitigation: Elia/50Hertz have adopted early reservation/frame agreement strategies; contracted ~€4.6bn in cable deals with NKT and Prysmian; secured Ostwind 4 HVDC system with GE Vernova/Drydocks World consortium; Deutsche Bank: ~60% of medium-term capex requirements contracted, 50% fully committed.
  • Permitting risk: Ventilus (Belgium) has faced ~2-year permitting delay vs original schedule; public consultation only began Nov 2025 for 2029–2030 target; >50% of ENTSO-E TYNDP 2024 projects still lack permits as of 2025.

Princess Elisabeth Island — World's First Artificial Energy Island

  • Located 45km off Belgian coast; designed to integrate 3.5 GW offshore wind; hub for future hybrid interconnectors (Nautilus UK-Belgium, TritonLink Denmark).
  • Progress: 11 of 23 concrete caissons installed at end-2025; €650m EIB green credit facility + €47.5m EU grant secured.
  • HVDC component suspended: Belgian government and Elia paused HVDC contract signing due to extreme supply chain price spikes — ~3-year delay for DC portion and Nautilus interconnector. 60% of wind zone achievable via ongoing AC infrastructure. Completion target delayed to end-2026.

Germany (50Hertz) — HVDC and Baltic
