§2 — Track Record at Hellenic Telecoms

J-front verdict: Demonstrably delivered on the core restructuring mandate — revenue stabilised after deep crisis decline, margins improved materially, debt reduced, dividends reinstated — execution is real but heavily macro-assisted post-2015 Greek recovery

Tenure at Hellenic Telecoms

  • Tenure at Hellenic Telecoms: Appointed CEO 2010; as of May 2026, approximately 15–16 years — one of the longest-tenured CEO incumbencies at any major European telco

Macro Context at Appointment

  • Revenue at appointment (~2010): OTE Group revenue approximately €3.9–4.1bn (2010 reported); Greek macro in acute deterioration, Romania subsidiary under pressure, workforce rationalisation beginning
  • Revenue current (~2024–25): OTE Group revenue approximately €3.5–3.7bn — optically lower than appointment but the composition has transformed: Romania operations sold (2014), legacy fixed-line decline offset by mobile/broadband growth, ARPU and margin quality substantially higher; like-for-like domestic Greek business has stabilised and grown modestly in nominal terms post-2016

Margin Trajectory

  • Margin at appointment: EBITDA margin approximately 32–34% (2010, blended group including Romania under pressure)
  • Margin current (~2024): EBITDA margin approximately 38–42% depending on reporting treatment — a structural improvement of 6–10 percentage points over the tenure, driven by: workforce headcount reduction (from ~20,000+ to ~10,000–11,000 in Greece over the decade), network investment rationalisation, fibre upgrade cycle improving mix, and exit from the lower-margin Romanian fixed-line drag
  • The margin expansion story is genuine — it predates the post-2020 Greek economic recovery and reflects deliberate operational decisions rather than pure revenue tailwind

Strategic Shifts Executed

  • Romania exit (2014): Sale of Romtelecom and Cosmote Romania to Deutsche Telekom — simplified the group to a Greece-pure-play (plus minor Balkan residuals); rationale was capital recycling and focus; at the time sell-side reception was mixed (some concern about growth foregone), in retrospect a clean disposal that removed a margin drag and recycled capital into Greek network investment
  • Fibre/VDSL network build (2015–2020): Substantial investment in vectored VDSL and subsequent FTTH rollout — competitive positioning response to alternative operators; this was a critical strategic commitment executed on a multi-year basis, which is the kind of sustained capital-allocation discipline that differentiates operators from administrators
  • Workforce restructuring (2012–2018): Multiple voluntary exit programmes, reduction of Greek headcount by approximately 40–50% from peak; politically difficult in Greek context — executed without catastrophic industrial action, a meaningful management accomplishment
  • 5G spectrum + network modernisation (2020–2024): OTE secured 5G spectrum in Greece and launched commercial 5G services; co-investment models with regulators/government in fibre deployment

M&A

  • M&A executed: Romania exit is the primary M&A event; domestic M&A limited — no transformational acquisitions during tenure; Tsamaz is not a deal-maker by archetype
  • M&A integration outcome: N/A for acquisitions; Romania disposal clean and value-realising in retrospect
  • Capital allocation history: Dividend reinstated post-crisis lows; progressive dividend growth from approximately €0.10/share (2014) to €0.60–0.65/share (2023–24 range); share buyback programme initiated 2018, extended/repeated subsequently — the buyback + dividend combination has been the primary capital return vehicle; debt has been managed down from elevated crisis-era levels to a modest net debt / EBITDA (approximately 1.0–1.2x by 2023–24)

TSR vs Sector Since Appointment

  • TSR vs peers since appointment (2010–2025): This is contextually complex — OTE's TSR from 2010 lows through 2025 is strongly positive (stock approximately €2–3 in 2012 crisis trough, €15–17 range by 2024–25), reflecting both Greek macro recovery and operational delivery; vs. broader European telecom sector (which has been a weak relative performer over the same period), OTE has been a notable outperformer, particularly 2016–2024; vs. Deutsche Telekom itself over the same period, OTE has roughly tracked or modestly outperformed on a total-return basis — not embarrassing for a subsidiary-level entity operating through a sovereign debt crisis

Crisis Handling

  • Crisis handling — Greek sovereign debt crisis (2010–2015): This is Tsamaz's most stress-tested period; OTE navigated: forced restructuring of Greek government bond holdings, pension obligations, regulatory uncertainty, currency crisis risk (pre-bailout), wholesale revenue collapse from Greek enterprise customers; the company did not go insolvent, maintained DT's support, and preserved network quality — a genuine operational accomplishment under duress
