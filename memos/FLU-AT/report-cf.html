Outliers worth noting 3 findings

1.
Severely restricted free float liquidity: The public free float has collapsed to under 6.0% of total equity, driven by Airports Group Europe S.a.r.l's accumulation of a 44.0% blockholder stake and the syndicated 40.0% holding by Vienna and Lower Austria provinces. This concentration ranks among the lowest free floats of any publicly traded European airport, creating severe liquidity constraints and limiting institutional investor access. The extreme ownership concentration effectively insulates management from capital market discipline and shareholder activism, while simultaneously restricting price discovery mechanisms. Peer comparisons reveal that Fraport maintains approximately 31% free float, while Zurich Airport trades with roughly 35% public ownership, making FWAG an extreme statistical outlier in European airport governance structures
2.
Massive strategic asset derecognition: Management executed a €55.9 million non-cash write-off of the third runway project assets in FY2025, representing an exceptional one-time balance sheet adjustment driven by the formal project abandonment decision rather than operational impairment
3.
Negative gearing and extreme net liquidity position: FWAG operates with a negative gearing ratio of -24.0% and maintains net cash of €407.1 million as of Q1 2026, representing a -1.0x Net Debt/EBITDA leverage position. This places the company in the statistically anomalous position of being a net lender rather than a net borrower, contradicting the typical airport sector capital structure where peers like Fraport carry 6.9x Net Debt/EBITDA and Groupe ADP maintains 3.7x leverage. The zero-leverage posture provides exceptional financial flexibility and eliminates refinancing risk, but simultaneously raises questions about suboptimal capital structure and potential underutilization of tax-efficient debt financing. Industry base rates suggest that regulated infrastructure assets typically operate with moderate leverage ratios of 2.0x to 4.0x to optimize weighted average cost of capital, making FWAG's debt-averse stance a profound structural outlier
