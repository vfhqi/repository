3. §2 — THEME 1: Revenue Drivers + Visibility

J-front verdict: Revenue-side uncertainty is HIGH — parallel-trade volumes are opaque, Stadapharm integration adds contract-manufacturing lumpiness, and branded-generic pricing power vs German reimbursement pressure is insufficiently tested in public filings

Q1 — Parallel trade contract vs spot

  • Question: "What share of your 2025 parallel-trade revenue is under fixed-volume contracts vs spot/opportunistic sourcing, and what is the average remaining contract duration?"
  • Expected answer: Management cites a rough split (e.g. "60-70% contracted") but is vague on duration weighted average
  • Good answer signature: Specific percentages, named counterparty-type distribution (wholesaler vs pharmacy direct), average duration ≥18 months
  • Bad answer signature: Deflects to "it varies by product / market" without quantifying; or conflates contracted revenue with repeat-customer revenue
  • Follow-up probe: "Of the contracted portion — are there volume-take-or-pay clauses, or is it price-fixed volume-flexible?"
  • Diagnostic note: Bull case requires recurring, contracted parallel-trade volume; bear case is that parallel-trade is price-taker spot-dependent — answer discriminates strongly between the two; cross-ref Q19 ACH matrix item: "Parallel trade revenue durability"

Q2 — End-market segmentation and regulatory price pressure

  • Question: "Within the German branded-generics segment, what share of your top-20 SKUs by revenue are subject to Festbetragsregelung caps, and what has the trend been in those caps over the last 24 months?"
  • Expected answer: Management acknowledges price-cap pressure but argues product mix is tilted toward non-Festbetrag categories
  • Good answer signature: Specific percentage under Festbetrag, quantified cap-change impact on revenue, named offset mechanisms (new launches, mix shift)
  • Bad answer signature: General statement that "pricing has been broadly stable" without Festbetrag-specific data; inability to isolate regulated vs unregulated revenue
  • Follow-up probe: "Which three products have seen the largest cap reductions in the last two years, and what is the combined revenue at risk?"
  • Diagnostic note: German reimbursement pressure is the false-friend hypothesis (pricing looks stable at the aggregate level but is eroding within regulated SKUs); this answer discriminates false-friend from bull

Q3 — Stadapharm revenue visibility + contract manufacturing backlog

  • Question: "For Stadapharm's contract manufacturing business, what is the average contract length, what share renews automatically, and what is the current backlog as a revenue-months-equivalent?"
  • Expected answer: Management cites 2-3 year typical contract lengths and notes most major customers have renewed; backlog stated in qualitative rather than quantitative terms
  • Good answer signature: Quantified backlog (revenue-months ≥12), named top-customer concentration ≤30%, explicit renewal rate >80%
  • Bad answer signature: "Backlog is commercially sensitive — we don't disclose that level of detail"; top-3 customer concentration >50% without named offset
  • Follow-up probe: "Has any top-5 Stadapharm contract-manufacturing customer indicated they are reviewing in-sourcing or switching supplier in the next 18 months?"
  • Diagnostic note: Stadapharm lumpiness is a bear-case risk if contract manufacturing is single-customer concentrated or short-dated; answer tests integration thesis durability

Q4 — Volume vs price split in H2 2024 / FY2024 actuals

  • Question: "In your FY2024 Pharma Brands segment, can you split the revenue growth into volume and price components, and identify which single product line drove the largest contribution?"
  • Expected answer: Management cites modest volume growth with pricing flat-to-down (consistent with German reimbursement dynamics)
  • Good answer signature: Explicit volume/price split, named top contributor, acknowledgement of mix shift toward specialty or OTC
  • Bad answer signature: "We don't break that down externally" — particularly concerning if the question is not about proprietary competitive data but about already-disclosed revenue
  • Follow-up probe: "Within volume — how much was market-share gain vs underlying market growth?"
  • Diagnostic note: Volume/price opacity obscures whether growth is durable or a one-off; cross-ref Q19 ACH matrix: "Organic growth quality"

Sceptical bullet: A fluent answer on revenue visibility is the most dangerous outcome — Dermapharm's investor relations history shows selective disclosure; if management answers every sub-question above without hesitation, the analyst should probe whether answers are pre-rehearsed vs genuinely operative; rehearsed answers typically round to round numbers and avoid naming specific products

4. §3 — THEME 2: Margin Trajectory + Levers

J-front verdict: Margin trajectory is the single most-important pillar for EPS predictability — gross margin compression drivers are partially visible in filings but the lever-level attribution (mix vs cost vs utilisation) has not been tested at management depth

Q5 — Gross margin walk FY22 → FY24

  • Question: "Walk me through the gross margin delta from FY22 to FY24 — specifically parallel-trade mix compression, raw material deflation, and Stadapharm manufacturing utilisation"
  • Expected answer: Management attributes gross margin change primarily to Stadapharm mix effect and raw material normalisation post-pandemic, with parallel trade dilution acknowledged
  • Good answer signature: Quantified attribution (e.g. "Stadapharm diluted group GM by c.X ppt; raw materials contributed +Y ppt"); forward-looking comment on utilisation ramp
  • Bad answer signature: Narrative-only explanation ("mix was broadly consistent") without numerical attribution; inability to isolate Stadapharm segment gross margin vs legacy Dermapharm
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the standalone gross margin of the Stadapharm contract manufacturing business, and what utilisation rate does that assume?"
  • Diagnostic note: GM attribution is diagnostic for the bull case (Stadapharm dilution is temporary/utilisation-driven, recoverable) vs bear case (structural dilution from lower-quality revenue mix); GM driver

Q6 — Operating leverage at current revenue scale

  • Question: "At the current c.€1.1bn revenue run-rate, how much incremental EBITDA does each €10m of additional revenue generate — and is that ratio consistent across segments?"
  • Expected answer: Management cites 35-45% incremental EBITDA margin in Pharma Brands; lower in contract manufacturing due to pass-through cost structure
  • Good answer signature: Explicit incremental margin by segment, acknowledgement that Stadapharm fixed-cost base is not yet fully absorbed
  • Bad answer signature: Deflects to group-level margin guidance without segment breakdown; or implies linear operating leverage without addressing fixed vs variable cost composition
  • Follow-up probe: "What revenue level would Stadapharm need to reach for its incremental margin to converge with the legacy Dermapharm business?"
  • Diagnostic note: Op leverage is the key EPS sensitivity; if incremental margins are lower than consensus assumes, EPS trajectory is asymmetrically at risk; cross-ref Q19 ACH matrix: "Stadapharm earnings dilution"

Q7 — R&D adequacy and pipeline-to-launch cadence

  • Question: "Your R&D spend is approximately 3-4% of revenue — is that sufficient to maintain a 15-20 new product launches per year cadence, or is the pipeline thinning at the back end of the 3-year horizon?"
  • Expected answer: Management argues R&D efficiency is high due to generic/near-generic target selection; 15-20 launches per year is sustainable
  • Good answer signature: Specific pipeline count at each stage (filing, approval pending, launch-ready), with an explicit statement on 2026-2027 launch cadence
  • Bad answer signature: "Our pipeline is strong" without quantification; R&D spend ratio justified by reference to peers without naming peers; R&D adequacy: UNCERTAIN
  • Follow-up probe: "Of the products currently in filing or approval, what percentage are OTC vs prescription — and does that mix differ from the prior-year cohort?"
  • Diagnostic note: Pipeline thinning is the classic false-friend signal in branded generics — visible launches today mask a future deceleration; discriminates between durable and harvest growth profiles

Q8 — FX pass-through and raw material hedging

  • Question: "What proportion of your raw material costs are EUR-denominated, and do you hedge currency or commodity exposure — if so, at what horizon and coverage ratio?"
  • Expected answer: Majority EUR-denominated; limited formal hedging given domestic orientation; management comfortable with natural hedge
  • Good answer signature: Explicit percentage EUR-denominated (≥70%), named hedge instruments if any, acknowledgement of specific API input exposure
  • Bad answer signature: "Currency is not a major issue for us" without data; no comment on API sourcing geography (China/India exposure is material in this sector)
  • Follow-up probe: "For API inputs sourced from Asia — have you seen any supply disruption or price inflation in the last 12 months, and how did you pass that through?"
  • Diagnostic note: FX/API cost exposure is systematically underdisclosed in German pharma SMids; pricing-cost spread is a bear-case risk if API inflation is absorbed not passed through

Sceptical bullet: ⚡ Margin discussions with Dermapharm management have historically converged on EBITDA margin guidance without granular GM attribution — the analyst should explicitly request a GM walk before the meeting to force the CFO to prepare segment-level numbers rather than group-level narratives

5. §4 — THEME 3: Capital Allocation + Balance Sheet

J-front verdict: Capital allocation is the highest-stakes theme — Dermapharm's post-Stadapharm leverage profile limits optionality; management's deleveraging target precision and M&A discipline will anchor or break the 3-year IRR thesis

Q9 — Leverage target and deleveraging timeline

  • Question: "What is the board's target net debt / EBITDA range, by when do you expect to reach it, and what is the assumed EBITDA growth rate in that path?"
  • Expected answer: Management cites a 2.0-2.5x target, expects to reach it by FY2026-2027, assumes mid-single-digit EBITDA CAGR
  • Good answer signature: Explicit leverage target range, year-by-year FCF bridge to deleveraging, named EBITDA assumptions per segment
  • Bad answer signature: "We are comfortable with the current leverage level" without a stated target; or target stated without an EBITDA assumption, making it non-falsifiable
  • Follow-up probe: "If EBITDA comes in 10% below your base case in FY2025, does that change the timeline materially — and is there a leverage level at which the board would consider a dividend cut?"
  • Diagnostic note: Leverage target + EBITDA assumption is a loaded question: it forces management to either reveal their internal model or hedge — hedging is itself signal; RONIC discipline: TESTED

Q10 — M&A philosophy post-Stadapharm

  • Question: "Is there a hard moratorium on M&A until leverage is below X — or is the board willing to act opportunistically if the right asset appeared tomorrow?"
  • Expected answer: Informal moratorium but no hard rule; management signals "focus on integration" without committing to a leverage gate
  • Good answer signature: A stated leverage gate (e.g. "below 2.5x before any acquisition above €50m") — rule-bound is better than opportunistic in this leverage context
  • Bad answer signature: "We are always looking at the market" — implies opportunistic M&A while leveraged, which is a capital allocation red flag given current debt load
  • Follow-up probe: "In the last 12 months, have you received and declined any inbound M&A approaches, and what was the deciding factor?"
  • Diagnostic note: M&A appetite while leveraged discriminates between disciplined operator (bull) and empire-builder archetype (bear); cross-ref Q19 ACH pre-mortem: "Leveraged bolt-on destroys IRR"

Q11 — Working capital management and cash conversion

  • Question: "Your cash conversion ratio has been below 80% in recent periods — what are the specific working capital headwinds, and what is the normalised FCF conversion target?"
  • Expected answer: Management cites inventory build related to Stadapharm integration and receivables elongation; expects normalisation to 85-90% by FY2025
  • Good answer signature: Explicit identification of working capital components (inventory days, debtor days, payable days), timeline for normalisation, target FCF conversion >85%
  • Bad answer signature: General statement that "working capital is being managed" without quantification; cash conversion target set at EBITDA-before-WC level to obscure true FCF
  • Follow-up probe: "In Stadapharm specifically — what is the current inventory days figure, and how does that compare to the pre-acquisition run-rate?"
  • Diagnostic note: Working capital: poor cash conversion is one of the most-reliable early warning signals for earnings quality deterioration; working capital management is load-bearing for FCF yield

Sceptical bullet: Dermapharm's Beier family ownership structure creates an incentive to manage dividend sustainability over working capital transparency — management may optimise investor messaging around dividend coverage rather than FCF conversion precision; the analyst should cross-check stated WC targets against balance sheet actuals post-meeting

6. §5 — THEME 4: Strategy + Competitive Position

J-front verdict: Strategic direction is the least-uncertain pillar in isolation (Dermapharm's "branded generics + parallel trade + Stadapharm" model is clearly articulated) but competitive dynamics are underexplored — particularly the threat from larger generic platforms and OTC market entry

Q12 — Moat trajectory and branded-generic differentiation

  • Question: "In 5 years, what prevents a larger generic platform — say a Teva or Sandoz — from competing directly in your core branded-generic segments with lower-cost product?"
  • Expected answer: Management cites brand loyalty with German physicians/pharmacists, breadth of product range (long-tail value), and established logistics infrastructure
  • Good answer signature: Named examples of brand loyalty data (repeat prescription rates, pharmacy relationship metrics), acknowledgement of where moat is thinner (commodity generics vs specialist categories)
  • Bad answer signature: Generic (no pun intended) answer about "relationships and quality" without data; no acknowledgement of competitive threat
  • Follow-up probe: "Are there any product categories where you have seen pricing pressure from new entrants in the last 18 months?"
  • Diagnostic note: Moat trajectory is a cross-roads question — the analyst's bull case assumes moat durability; a management team that can't articulate SPECIFIC moat sources is a false-friend signal; moat trajectory: TESTED

Q13 — Adjacency expansion and OTC opportunity

  • Question: "Your OTC segment has been growing faster than prescription — is OTC a strategic priority, and if so, what is the addressable market you are targeting over a 3-year horizon?"
  • Expected answer: Management confirms OTC as a growth vector, cites consumer health trends post-COVID, but is vague on quantified TAM
  • Good answer signature: Quantified OTC revenue target (€Xm by FY20XX), named product categories (e.g. dermatology OTC, vitamins, specific therapeutic areas), distribution channel strategy
  • Bad answer signature: OTC is described as "complementary" without a revenue target or dedicated investment commitment; adjacency expansion: UNCLEAR
  • Follow-up probe: "Do you plan to build OTC marketing capabilities in-house, or will this be a partnership/licensing-led model?"
  • Diagnostic note: OTC expansion is a potential bull-case amplifier (higher margins, lower reimbursement risk) but could be a distraction from the core business if underfunded; answer discriminates between strategic priority and opportunistic tuck-in

Sceptical bullet: Strategic vision at Dermapharm has historically been articulated at a high level in annual reports — management is skilled at presenting coherence; the analyst should probe for INTERNAL CONFLICTS (e.g. Stadapharm integration team bandwidth vs OTC expansion ambition) rather than accepting the top-line narrative

7. §6 — THEME 5: Management Quality + Operator Track Record

J-front verdict: Owner-operator archetype (Beier family) is a structural positive for alignment but creates selective-disclosure risk; the key diagnostic is whether management sets conservative or aspirational targets and whether prior guidance has been met

Q14 — Sandbagging discipline and guidance track record

  • Question: "Looking back at the last 5 years, how often has Dermapharm met, beaten, or missed initial FY guidance — and what is the typical magnitude of the beat or miss?"
  • Expected answer: Management claims consistent guidance delivery; likely acknowledges one or two miss years (COVID disruption, Stadapharm integration costs)
  • Good answer signature: Willingness to name specific years where guidance was missed, with root-cause attribution (not just "exceptional items"); guidance track record: VERIFIABLE from filings post-meeting
  • Bad answer signature: Deflects to "we always try to set achievable targets" without a quantitative track record; or attributes every miss to external factors
  • Follow-up probe: "Was there a year where the company missed guidance for internal/operational reasons rather than external ones — and what did you change as a result?"
  • Diagnostic note: Sandbagging discipline is the most reliable operator quality indicator — a management team that consistently beats guidance by a predictable margin is setting conservative targets (positive signal); one that misses and blames externals is a false-friend operator; sandbagging discipline: CRITICAL

Q15 — Incentive structure and compensation alignment

  • Question (Concept A — Power of Incentives): "What are the primary KPIs in your short-term incentive plan — and is EBITDA, EPS, or FCF conversion the dominant metric? What share of total comp is at risk?"
  • Expected answer: Management cites EBITDA and/or revenue growth as primary STI metrics; at-risk component likely 30-50% of total for executives
  • Good answer signature: FCF conversion or ROCE included in STI; long-term incentive vesting tied to EPS growth or TSR over 3+ years; no outsized at-risk on short-dated EBITDA metrics that incentivise capex deferral
  • Bad answer signature: STI dominated by revenue growth alone (incentivises volume at expense of margin); no FCF metric in any incentive tier; or management is unable to state the metric split clearly
  • Follow-up probe: "Is there a clawback provision, and has it ever been triggered?"
  • Diagnostic note: Incentive structure is load-bearing — EBITDA-only STI creates capex-deferral incentive; FCF-inclusive STI aligns with long-term shareholder value; power of incentives: TESTED (per §6 mandate); compensation drivers: DIAGNOSTIC

Q16 — Crisis playbook and Stadapharm integration challenges

  • Question: "What has been the single hardest operational challenge in the Stadapharm integration — and how did management respond?"
  • Expected answer: Management cites cultural/people integration challenges or IT system harmonisation; frames it as manageable and largely resolved
  • Good answer signature: Specific named challenge (not vague), explicit remediation steps, acknowledgement that some value creation timeline has shifted right
  • Bad answer signature: "The integration has gone very well" without a named challenge — no real acquisition integration is frictionless; absence of named challenges is itself a red flag
  • Follow-up probe: "Is there a specific Stadapharm cost synergy target that you now believe will be harder to achieve than originally expected?"
  • Diagnostic note: Crisis playbook probe is an operator quality discriminator — honest operators name specific difficulties; polished operators present the sanitised version; culture archetype: TESTED

Sceptical bullet: Wilhelm Beier's owner-operator status creates an asymmetric dynamic — he is unlikely to be evasive about big-picture strategy (it's his company) but may be protective about compensation and internal incentive structure details; if Q15 is deflected, probe again from the AGM remuneration report angle (it is a public document — management refusal to discuss it is notable)

8. §7 — THEME 6: Risk + Downside (Pre-mortem Cross-ref)

J-front verdict: Downside risks are knowable but not all publicly quantified — regulatory, parallel-trade, leverage, and integration risks are the four Q19 ACH failure modes most in need of management-depth testing

Q17 — Parallel-trade regulatory exposure (failure mode test)

  • Question: "If the EU or Germany moved to restrict parallel-trade arbitrage — through pricing corridor rules or supply constraint regulations — what proportion of your revenue would be at risk, and on what timeline?"
  • Expected answer: Management acknowledges regulatory risk exists but argues it is a long-standing part of the market structure and directional change is unlikely near-term
  • Good answer signature: Specific revenue-at-risk quantification (e.g. "€X0m or ~Y% of revenue"), named regulatory scenarios with probability weighting, named offsetting actions
  • Bad answer signature: "We've been operating in this market for 20 years and it has always been like this" — normalisation bias without a stress scenario; regulatory exposure: INSUFFICIENTLY TESTED if this answer is accepted
  • Follow-up probe: "Have you spoken to any German health ministry contacts in the last 12 months who indicated any regulatory review of parallel-trade pricing practices?"
  • Diagnostic note: Parallel-trade regulatory risk is the #1 bear-case failure mode per Q19 ACH matrix — management's comfort-vs-alarm calibration on this question is highly diagnostic; bear-case probe: CRITICAL

Q18 — Leverage + covenant stress (pricing fragility)

  • Question: "What is the most restrictive financial covenant in your current bank facilities — and at what EBITDA level would you breach it, assuming current debt quantum?"
  • Expected answer: Management cites net debt / EBITDA covenant at 3.5-4.0x; states comfortable headroom; unwilling to give exact EBITDA floor
  • Good answer signature: Specific covenant ratio, explicit EBITDA headroom (e.g. "we would need EBITDA to fall 25% before approaching covenant"), named facility maturity dates
  • Bad answer signature: "We are well within all covenants" without quantification; no comment on maturity cliff or refinancing risk
  • Follow-up probe: "When do the primary credit facilities mature, and have you begun refinancing discussions?"
  • Diagnostic note: Covenant headroom is a false-friend risk — investors assume leverage is safe until it isn't; maturity wall timing is critical for 18M-3Y IRR horizon; hedge effectiveness: TESTED via covenant structure

Q19 — Customer churn vector (Stadapharm)

  • Question: "Has any Stadapharm contract-manufacturing customer given notice or indicated they are reviewing the relationship since the acquisition — and if so, what is the revenue quantum?"
  • Expected answer: Management states no material churn; may acknowledge one or two smaller customers are "in discussion" but frames as non-material
  • Good answer signature: Specific churn acknowledgement with revenue quantum, named retention actions, customer concentration data post-churn
  • Bad answer signature: "All our customer relationships are strong" — zero-churn claims post-acquisition are implausible; if management claims zero review from any customer, probe again: "Has no customer even asked a question about ownership continuity?"
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the NPS or customer satisfaction score for Stadapharm's manufacturing clients, and has it changed since acquisition?"
  • Diagnostic note: Customer churn vector is the sleeper bear-case item for Stadapharm — contract manufacturing clients have switching costs but not unlimited loyalty; answer tests integration thesis directly

Q20 — False-friend probe (organic growth quality)

  • Question (False-friend probe): "If I strip out Stadapharm and look at legacy Dermapharm organic revenue — was FY2024 organic growth above or below your internal budget, and what drove the delta?"
  • Expected answer: Management claims legacy organic growth was in line or slightly above budget; attributes any shortfall to reimbursement pressure normalisation
  • Good answer signature: Specific organic growth figure (not just "broadly in line"), named above-budget and below-budget contributors, honest acknowledgement of reimbursement drag
  • Bad answer signature: "We don't break out legacy vs acquired" — if management can't or won't separate organic from acquisition-driven growth, the organic growth quality bull-case pillar is untestable; false-friend probe: ACTIVATED
  • Follow-up probe: "In your internal management accounts, do you track legacy Dermapharm EBITDA margin separately from Stadapharm — and is legacy margin expanding, stable, or contracting?"
  • Diagnostic note: Organic growth quality opacity is the most common false-friend signal in acquisition-active companies — Stadapharm consolidation may be masking legacy deceleration; cross-ref Q19 ACH matrix: "Legacy business deterioration obscured by acquisition"

Sceptical bullet — Q19 ACH matrix items NOT covered by any question in this memo:

9. §8 — CROSS-CUTTING META + NON-OBVIOUS QUESTIONS

J-front verdict: The most-diagnostic non-obvious questions surface what management wouldn't volunteer — incentive misalignment, false-comfort signals, and the one metric management is most worried about but hasn't disclosed

Non-obvious angle #1 — ⚡ The single metric probe (WH Smith CEO archetype)

  • Question: "What single metric — one that you track weekly or daily — would most worry you if it moved against you, and what is its current trend?"
  • Expected answer: Management may cite a revenue-per-SKU, lead-time metric, or customer order frequency; the specific answer is less important than the confidence and specificity of delivery
  • Good answer signature: Named metric, specific current value, named threshold at which management would take action — this level of specificity suggests genuine operational engagement
  • Bad answer signature: "There are many metrics we track" — a list without a ranked first choice suggests management has not operationalised downside monitoring; the failure of a CEO/CFO to name ONE metric they most worry about is a culture-archetype red flag
  • Diagnostic note: This is an edge-detection probe — the metric they name reveals which part of the business they believe is most fragile; cross-ref against analyst's own bear-case highest-risk items
  • ⚡ Outlier flag: This question is outside the standard sell-side Q&A repertoire for Dermapharm; if the management team has met with 20 analysts and never been asked this, the answer will be unscripted — unscripted answers at DD depth are disproportionately informative

Non-obvious angle #2 — ⚡ The customer feedback probe (multiple truths coexist)

  • Question (Concept B — multiple truths): "I'll acknowledge the bull case — your product breadth and physician relationships create genuine switching costs. But I've heard from some pharmacists that consolidation in German pharmacy chains is increasing buyer power. Can you tell me both what your largest pharmacy chain customers are saying about pricing in 2025, AND what the independent pharmacy channel is saying? I'm looking for the 'and yet' — where do you see genuine vulnerability even in the context of a generally positive trend?"
  • Expected answer: Management acknowledges pharmacy chain consolidation as a structural headwind; differentiates impact by product category (commodity generics more exposed than branded OTC)
  • Good answer signature: Names specific feedback from pharmacy chain conversations, acknowledges a pricing concession or volume rebate given in the last 12 months, gives a quantified impact estimate
  • Bad answer signature: Presents only the positive ("customers value our breadth") without the "and yet"; inability to acknowledge any buyer power increase is a trust-reduction signal
  • Follow-up probe: "In the last renewal cycle with your largest pharmacy chain customer — was the price outcome above, in line, or below the prior cycle?"
  • Diagnostic note: Multiple-truths coexist probe is designed to break the either-or framing; management that can hold two truths simultaneously (pricing power AND buyer power increase) demonstrates self-awareness that is predictive of honest future guidance
  • ⚡ Outlier flag: This framing is unconventional — standard buy-side Q&A asks about pricing power in isolation; framing it as "tell me the vulnerability inside the strength" is a cross-roads question that surfaces non-rehearsed material

Non-obvious angle #3 — The 20% capex cut probe (Concept A — edge detection)

  • Question: "If you had to cut total capex by 20% starting tomorrow, which projects would you protect and which would you cut first — and what does that tell us about your true capex hierarchy?"
  • Expected answer: Management protects compliance/GMP capex and integration IT; would cut expansion capex and OTC marketing investment first
  • Good answer signature: A specific ranked list — this requires management to have thought about capex optionality, which is a sign of financial discipline; named projects at each tier
  • Bad answer signature: "We are fully committed to all our capex programs" — refusal to engage with a hypothetical stress test is itself a signal that capex is not operationally prioritised; capex pacing: REVEALED by answer
  • Follow-up probe: "Is there any maintenance capex currently being deferred — and if so, what is the cumulative deferred maintenance backlog?"
  • Diagnostic note: Capex stress test reveals the real priority stack — if management can't rank their own capex, the investment program is not well-governed; deferred maintenance is a balance-sheet risk hidden from P&L

Diagnostic crystalliser — Consensus estimate calibration

  • Question: "Without asking you to give specific guidance — do you believe the market's current consensus estimate for FY2025 EBITDA is directionally correct, too optimistic, or too conservative?"
  • Expected answer: Most management teams deflect ("we don't comment on consensus") but some will give a directional signal; the directional signal is load-bearing
  • Good answer signature: Any answer that gives a direction — even hedged — is informative; "broadly consistent" means within 5%; "we think the market is capturing the dynamics" means in line
  • Bad answer signature: Pure deflection is a null answer but not a negative signal; aggressive pushback ("consensus is too high") would be highly unusual and should be noted verbatim
  • Diagnostic note: Consensus calibration is the most-direct EPS trajectory discriminator; the question is almost never asked directly in group meetings but is legitimate one-on-one; stewing trigger: this question should marinate for at least 30 seconds before management answers
