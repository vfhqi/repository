§2 — Quality of Earnings

Quality verdict: High quality — cash conversion consistently excellent, adjustments are large but structurally stable (intangibles amortisation) rather than accumulating or shapeshifting; no evidence of earnings manipulation or aggressive accounting [#J]

One-off impact: Halma's primary adjustment is amortisation of acquired intangibles — £99m (FY2022), £118m (FY2023), £127m (FY2024), ~£135m (FY2025E) [#A]

  • Sub-note: this is large and growing — from 6.4% of revenue (FY2022) to ~6.4% of revenue (FY2025); stable as % of revenue but growing in absolute terms as the acquisition programme accelerates
  • Sub-note: acquisition transaction costs (£15-22m pa) are also excluded from adjusted figures; these recur every year and could reasonably be treated as an operating cost of the acquisition-led model

Adjustments trend: Adjustments growing in absolute terms (£114m total in FY2022 → ~£153m in FY2025) but broadly stable as a % of revenue; the non-GAAP gap is not widening in proportional terms [#A]

Recurring vs one-off: ❌ Acquisition transaction costs and integration costs are treated as one-off but recur every year without exception across the L3Y window — a legitimate bear flag on adjusted EPS quality [#J]

Non-GAAP gap: Adjusted EPS (60.7p, FY2022) vs reported EPS (34.9p, FY2022) implies a 42% gap; this gap has been broadly stable (FY2025E: ~84p adjusted vs ~45p reported, ~46% gap); large but disclosed and consistently applied [#A]

NWC tailwind/headwind: Working capital has been broadly neutral to mildly positive across L3Y — Halma's decentralised model keeps individual subsidiary working capital tight; no evidence of WC-inflated operating cash flows [#J]

Receivables turn: Days sales outstanding approximately 45-55 days, stable across L3Y periods; no lengthening receivables suggesting channel stuffing or credit-quality deterioration [#A]

Cash conversion: Operating cash flow / adjusted EBIT consistently 95-105% across L3Y — top-quartile vs UK industrial peer set (typical UK industrial mid-cap range: 80-90%); strong indicator that adjusted earnings are backed by real cash [#J]

  • Sub-note: FY2023 cash conversion approximately 98% (OCF £365m vs adj EBIT £370m); FY2024 approximately 102%; no deterioration trend
  • ⚡ RARE: Cash conversion: Halma has maintained 95%+ OCF/adj EBIT for at least 5 consecutive years — this level of consistency across a portfolio of 45+ subsidiaries is genuinely unusual for an acquisitive industrial

D&A trend: Total D&A growing as acquisition programme accelerates — depreciation of owned assets stable at ~£50m pa; amortisation of acquired intangibles growing (£99m → £135m); this reflects M&A accounting, not aggressive capitalisation of operating costs [#A]

Aggressive accounting: No evidence — Halma does not capitalise development costs aggressively, does not use "adjusted EBITDA" to eliminate D&A on owned assets, and does not exclude share-based compensation from adjusted figures in a systematically flattering way [#J]

Conservative accounting: Pension scheme (closed defined benefit) is well-funded; no hidden pension liabilities that could emerge as earnings drag [#A]

OCF vs NI: OCF consistently and materially exceeds reported net income (FY2024: OCF ~£415m vs net income ~£185m) — primarily because reported EPS is depressed by intangibles amortisation (non-cash); this actually confirms cash quality is excellent [#J]

Sceptical bullet: What's the bearish read on earnings quality? Through what mechanism might apparent margin growth be flattered by recurring items dressed up as one-off? The strongest bear case is that acquisition transaction costs (~£18-22m pa) are permanently recurring and should be treated as an ongoing cost of the business model — adding these back to the "adjusted" line flatters adjusted EPS by ~2.5-3p pa; over 5 years this compounds into a material EPS overstatement if acquisitions are essential to achieving growth targets

§3 — Revenue vs Profit Growth (Margin Sustainability)

Margin verdict: Structural expansion — driven by pricing power in niche markets, favourable mix shift toward higher-margin Safety and Life Sciences, and modest operating leverage; not primarily a cost-cut story; sustainable but with limited remaining runway at current pace [#J]

Revenue CAGR: +10.4% L3Y (FY2022-FY2025E); organic approximately +7-8% [#A]

Profit CAGR: Adjusted EBIT +12.5% L3Y — 200bps faster than revenue, confirming positive operating leverage [#A]

EPS CAGR: Adjusted EPS +11.3% L3Y — slightly below EBIT CAGR due to rising interest costs (net debt has grown with M&A) and modest share count increase from employee share schemes [#A]

Operating leverage: Halma's model generates operating leverage through (a) fixed cost absorption at group centre (small relative to subsidiary revenues), (b) pricing power in niche markets (inflation pass-through historically 100%+), and (c) mix shift [#J]

Gross margin: Stable 54-55% — exceptional for a diversified industrial (UK industrial mid-cap median typically 35-45% gross margin); reflects the service/IP/regulatory-driven nature of Halma's end markets rather than commodity manufacturing [#A]

EBITDA margin: Expanded from ~24% (FY2022) to ~24.7% (FY2024) — modest but consistent; note adjusted EBITDA excludes intangibles amortisation which inflates this metric vs reported [#A]

Adj EBIT margin: Expanded from 19.6% (FY2022) to 20.7% (H1 FY2025) — approximately 110bps expansion over the L3Y window; roughly 35bps pa [#A]

Margin expansion:
- Mix shift driver: Safety and Environmental divisions (higher-margin) grew faster than Infrastructure division; growing weight of recurring software/service revenue in the mix [#A]
- Pricing driver: Halma's businesses operate in regulated/safety-critical niches with low price sensitivity — inflation pass-through was effective in FY2023 and sustained in FY2024 [#J]
- Scale driver: Group centre costs (small) spreading over a larger revenue base; limited relative to the mix/pricing effect [#A]

Cost cut driver: No material evidence of margin expansion driven by headcount reduction, factory closures, or one-off cost programmes — this is consistent with the decentralised model where subsidiaries operate independently [#J]

Sustainability: Expansion pace likely to moderate — 35bps pa is not a fast-expansion story; the question is whether 20%+ adj EBIT margin is sustainable rather than whether expansion accelerates; current margin reflects the steady-state for this model rather than a cost-cycle peak [#A]

Margin runway: Limited upside beyond ~22-23% adj EBIT margin without material business-model change; bears argue most of the easy mix/pricing gain has already been harvested in FY2022-FY2024 [#J]

Sceptical bullet: What's the bearish read on margin sustainability? Through what mechanism might margin expansion reverse? If organic revenue growth decelerates below 5%, the fixed cost absorption that drives incremental margin improvement stalls — Halma has a relatively high fixed-cost infrastructure at group level (100+ person group centre, M&A integration overhead) that would de-leverage if top-line slows meaningfully; also, if the regulatory/safety tailwind (which allows above-inflation pricing) eases in the US (largest market), pricing power could moderate

§4 — Seasonal Patterns

Seasonality verdict: Modest, consistent H2 weighting — Halma's full-year results are H2-weighted (roughly 52-55% H2 vs 45-48% H1 by adjusted EBIT) in a stable pattern; no Q-level quarterly data available given semi-annual reporting [#J]

H1 pattern: H1 (April-September) consistently delivers approximately 45-48% of full-year adjusted EBIT; this is structurally lower and is well understood by the market [#A]

H2 pattern: H2 (October-March) is the stronger half — approximately 52-55% of FY adjusted EBIT; driven by US fiscal year-end (September) customer buying patterns in Safety sector and UK/European regulatory spending in H2 [#A]

Seasonality direction: Stable and predictable — the H2 weighting has been consistent across the L3Y window and for several years prior; no evidence of pattern change [#J]

H1 FY2023 check: H1 FY2023 adjusted EPS 32.0p → FY2023 adjusted EPS 72.7p; H1 = 44% of FY — consistent with historical H1 weighting [#A]

H1 FY2024 check: H1 FY2024 adjusted EPS 35.8p → FY2024 adjusted EPS 79.8p; H1 = 44.9% of FY — extremely stable ratio [#A]

  • Sub-note: H1 being ~44-45% of FY is a useful anchoring heuristic — when H1 delivers above this ratio, it is a mild positive signal for FY; below, mild negative

Cause — buying pattern: US safety-critical equipment purchasing is concentrated in Q3 calendar (July-September US fiscal year end spending), which falls in Halma's H1; however, UK/European government and regulated-sector customers concentrate orders in Q4 calendar (October-December), supporting Halma's H2 [#A]

Seasonality changing: No evidence of meaningful change in the H1/H2 split across L3Y; the mix shift toward more software/recurring revenue (which is naturally smoother) could very gradually flatten seasonality over a multi-year horizon [#J]

Cause — M&A timing: Acquisition-driven revenue tends to be more H2-weighted because new acquisitions are frequently consolidated mid-year and generate partial-year revenue predominantly in H2 of their first reporting year; this is a mechanical artifact that reinforces but does not fully explain the H2 bias [#A]

Sceptical bullet: What's the bearish read on seasonal patterns? Through what mechanism might apparent seasonality be a weakening cyclical pattern misread as structural? If Halma's US safety sector clients defer capital spending due to end-market softness (e.g., industrial capex recession), the H1 spend concentration weakens in a way that could look like a recurring seasonal miss but actually signals cyclical deterioration; the market's habit of discounting soft H1s as "seasonal" could mask an early-cycle warning

Honest tracker: Halma is among the most conservative guidors in the UK large-cap industrials universe — the absence of quantitative guidance is itself a form of sandbagging that prevents precision miss risk [#A]

Sell-side revision pattern: Consensus estimates are revised upward post each FY release — typically by 2-4% for next-FY estimates; H1 interim releases also prompt modest upward revisions; no pattern of post-release downward revisions across the L3Y window [#A]

New CEO transition: Marc Ronchetti succeeded Andrew Williams as CEO in November 2024; first full reporting period under Ronchetti is H1 FY2025 (announced Nov-24); beat/miss pattern unchanged, suggesting continuity in guidance conservatism [#J]
