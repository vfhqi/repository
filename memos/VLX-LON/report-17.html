This is distinct from translation (which is a reporting-currency artefact) - **EUR transaction exposure:** Volex invoices European customers in EUR (for the EUR revenue segment, ~USD 180m/year). Manufacturing cost for goods sold into Europe is predominantly USD-denominated (Asian factories). This creates a net long-EUR position of approximately USD 100-130m annually — the EUR inflow exceeds the EUR cost outflow, leaving a net EUR receivable that must be converted to USD (or USD-equivalent) to fund the cost base - EUR/USD move of ±10% on this net long-EUR position = ±USD 10-13m revenue/margin impact annually (pre-hedge) - This is the largest single transaction-exposure line - **GBP transaction exposure:** Volex pays GBP-denominated overhead from its USD revenue base. Annual GBP overhead estimated at GBP 30-45m (approximately USD 38-57m at 1.27). This is a net short-GBP transaction exposure — Volex must sell USD to buy GBP to pay its overhead - GBP/USD move of ±10% = ±USD 4-6m annual overhead cost change - This is a recurring, structural transaction exposure with limited natural hedging capability (cannot move UK HQ costs to USD easily) - **CNY transaction exposure:** Vietnamese and Chinese labour in local currency creates a small but persistent CNY/VND transaction exposure. Given the PBoC's managed float (CNY/USD range-bound) and VND depreciation trend, this has been a net positive for L5Y but is directionally a risk - **RMB-specific note:** Repatriation risk from Chinese subsidiary (cash trapped in RMB) could become a transaction exposure in a severe CNY depreciation scenario or capital control tightening. Not material at current scale but worth flagging as a tail - **Pricing pass-through on transaction exposure:** Volex's ability to pass through EUR/USD FX moves to European industrial customers varies by contract type: - Long-term automotive OEM supply agreements (typically 1-3 year pricing) have limited FX pass-through clauses — FX moves inside the contract period are absorbed - Shorter-term industrial/Medical contracts have indexed pricing (annual reviews) — partial pass-through - Consumer electronics contracts are spot-priced, so FX pass-through is more immediate - RESEARCHER estimate: ~40-50% of EUR transaction exposure is subject to annual repricing (partial pass-through); ~50-60% is in fixed-price contracts with limited pass-through over the contract period - **Transaction vs translation magnitude comparison:** Transaction exposure (EUR receivable + GBP overhead = USD ~140-190m notional) is larger than the translation exposure on a net annual P&L-impact basis — consistent with the sector norm for manufacturers where functional currency is matched to reporting but geographic revenue mismatches persist **Sceptical bullet:** The bearish read on transaction exposure is that Volex's automotive supply agreements (growing share of EUR revenue post-2021 acquisitions in Germany and Turkey) are price-fixed for 1-3 year periods with minimal FX indexation — a scenario where EUR weakens 15% against USD mid-contract would compress Volex's contribution margin on those contracts without repricing relief; given automotive OEM pricing power dynamics (Volex as tier-2 supplier cannot break contracts), this is a real economic risk that is systematically understated because pass-through "policy" applies only to shorter-duration contracts --- ## §5 — Hedging Policy Assessment **J-front verdict:** Hedging policy is disclosed in principle (forwards, short-horizon) but lacks quantitative transparency on ratio, duration, and realised effectiveness — below-average disclosure quality for European industrials - **Policy disclosure summary:** Volex discloses in its annual report (FY2024 Notes to Financial Statements, Financial Risk Management section) that the group uses forward foreign exchange contracts to hedge transactional exposures. The disclosure states: "The Group hedges its significant foreign currency transaction exposures using forward contracts entered into for periods matching the expected timing of the underlying transaction" - Critically: no hedge ratio disclosed. No hedge horizon quantified ("significant" and "expected timing" are not quantitative). No instrument breakdown beyond "forward contracts" - **Translation hedging: not performed** — Volex explicitly does not hedge translation exposure, consistent with most industrial manufacturers of its scale (translation hedging is complex and costly relative to the accounting benefit) - **Transaction hedging: yes, forward contracts only** — Volex uses forward FX contracts for near-term transactional exposures. The FY2024 annual report fair value disclosure shows open forward contracts, typically maturing within 12 months. No collars, options, or longer-dated structured products mentioned - **Hedge horizon estimate:** Based on the fair value disclosure of outstanding forward contracts, the maturity profile is predominantly 0-6M with some 6-12M positions. RESEARCHER estimates the effective hedge horizon is approximately 3-9M rolling, which is at the shorter end of industrial hedging practice - A 3-9M horizon means Volex's EUR and GBP transaction exposures beyond 9 months are largely unhedged — in a sustained GBP strengthening scenario (GBP/USD moving from 1.28 to 1.38 over 18 months), only the first 3-9M of GBP overhead cost is hedged - **Hedge ratio: not disclosed** — This is a meaningful gap. RESEARCHER estimates from the notional value of outstanding derivative instruments disclosed in the balance sheet that the hedge ratio on near-term (0-6M) EUR transaction exposure is approximately 60-80% covered, with 0% cover beyond 12M. GBP overhead hedging appears partial (estimated 40-60% of rolling 6M GBP overhead) - These are triangulated estimates; management should confirm at analyst call - **Instruments:** Forward contracts only, entered into with "major UK and international banks" (per disclosure). Single-instrument approach (forwards only, no optionality/collars) is adequate but not best practice. Concentration in forwards without collar protection means Volex gives up upside in favourable FX moves (locked rate) - **Counterparty risk:** Volex uses multiple banks for forward contracts (consistent with a EUR 1bn revenue company — appropriate counterparty diversification). No disclosed concentration with a single counterparty - **Realised hedge effectiveness L3Y:** IFRS 9 requires disclosure of hedge effectiveness testing results for designated cash-flow hedges. Volex's FY2024 notes include a cash-flow hedge reserve of approximately USD 1-3m — a small positive/negative number consistent with a modestly effective but low-coverage hedging programme - The small size of the cash-flow hedge reserve signals that either (a) the programme is small relative to total FX exposure (low coverage), or (b) the programme is well-matched (high effectiveness). RESEARCHER's view: the former — the hedge reserve being small reflects low nominal coverage, not high precision - **Hedge cost:** Forward contracts for USD/EUR and USD/GBP at current rate differentials carry a modest cost (USD currently at a premium vs EUR on rate differentials; GBP at a modest premium vs USD). Hedging cost is not separately disclosed but at modest hedge ratios and short horizons, the cost is unlikely to exceed USD 1-2m annually (not earnings-material) - **No board-level hedge mandate disclosed:** Volex does not publish a treasury policy document or board-level hedging mandate with explicit ratio/duration targets. This is a governance-quality gap vs best-practice peers that publish treasury policy summaries **Sceptical bullet:** The bearish read on hedging discipline is that Volex's short-horizon (3-9M), undisclosed-ratio hedging programme provides minimal protection against the primary FX risks — a sustained GBP appreciation or EUR depreciation cycle lasting 12-24 months would hit Volex's earnings materially once the rolling hedge window expires; the absence of any disclosed hedge ratio means investors cannot independently verify whether the programme is meaningful or largely cosmetic; "we hedge significant exposures with forwards" covers everything from 10% hedge ratio to 90%, and the small cash-flow hedge reserve balance suggests the former --- ## §6 — Quantified Sensitivity Analysis **J-front verdict:** EPS sensitivity to ±10% USD/GBP or EUR/USD moves is estimated at ±3-6% EPS — significant but not thesis-breaking; EUR/USD is the largest single sensitivity; GBP/USD is the most persistent structural exposure ### Static Sensitivity Table (Mandatory Q17-ST) **Table 1: Static FX Sensitivity — Revenue, EBITDA, EPS Impact** | Currency Pair | Move | Translation Revenue Impact | Translation EBITDA Impact | Transaction Revenue Impact | Transaction EBITDA Impact | Total Est.
