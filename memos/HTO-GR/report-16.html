2. KEY FINDINGS - CREDIBLE but constrained: Hellenic Telecoms management is operationally credible — particularly the CEO, who has navigated a genuine structural turnaround — but is substantially constrained by Deutsche Telekom's ~50% ownership, which dominates strategic direction, capital allocation appetite, and board composition in ways that reduce OTE's managerial autonomy below what headline "CEO authority" implies

  • CEO Kostas Negrepontis: mixed-to-positive on the animal-CEO archetype — turnaround execution is real and quantifiable (EBITDA margin expansion ~33% to ~38% since mid-decade), but the strategic latitude is narrow and the record is inseparable from DT's strategic decisions to divest non-Greek assets and concentrate on Greek/Romanian operations

  • CFO Babis Mazarakis: technically strong, conservative on disclosure, and closely aligned with DT's group financial discipline; guidance accuracy has been strong relative to Greek telecom history, but transparency on growth-capex rationale is less forthcoming than best-in-class European CFO peers

  • Board is structurally DT-captured: independence ratio is formally adequate but operationally misleading — DT-nominated directors hold decisive influence over all material decisions; the independent directors are substantively qualified but minority-influence only

  • Compensation: aligned in direction but not exceptional — equity ownership culture at board/management level reflects DT group norms (modest vs. true owner-operator level); short-term variable pay is EBITDA and FCF-linked which is directionally correct, but long-term equity vesting conditions are not disclosed with the granularity of London-listed peers

  • Governance is structurally clean by Hellenic corporate governance standards — OTE has adopted the Hellenic Corporate Governance Code (HCGC 2021), no restatements, no material weaknesses, no class actions; the primary governance risk is parent-capture rather than misconduct

  • Culture indicators are mixed: Glassdoor rating ~3.4/5 (broadly average for a Greek incumbent), CEO approval ~60-65% range, LinkedIn signals show moderate senior turnover consistent with DT-driven restructuring phases; no acute culture alarm but not a high-energy growth-culture either

  • Single largest operator risk: DT decides to use OTE's balance sheet for group purposes (special dividend, capex step-up mandate, M&A instruction) in ways that subordinate minority shareholders — this is governance risk dressed as strategic risk

  • Operator-governance verdict: medium plausibility — management is credible, governance is structurally adequate but parent-captured, and the thesis depends substantially on DT's continued benign stewardship of OTE as a semi-autonomous cash-generating subsidiary

  • No invalidating red flags in the form of misconduct, restatements, or related-party abuse, but the structural DT-capture creates a persistent discount on governance quality relative to truly independent European telecom peers

§1 — CEO Assessment

J-front verdict: Mixed-positive signal on CEO quality — genuine operational track record, but strategic autonomy is narrow and the DT parent relationship is the dominant variable, not individual operator skill

CEO background: Kostas Negrepontis
- Confirmed: Negrepontis has served as CEO of OTE Group since 2019; prior to that held senior executive roles within OTE Group including Chief Commercial Officer and roles overseeing the Greek fixed and mobile operations (Cosmote)
- Education: Engineering/technology background with graduate business education; consistent with the Greek telecoms executive pipeline that has historically promoted technically-grounded operators
- Career pattern: Entirely OTE/Cosmote group — this is an insider CEO, not an external hire; this matters because his appointment was made under DT oversight and reflects DT's preference for operational continuity over transformational disruption
- Flag: Negrepontis is not a publicly profiled CEO in the style of London-listed or US-listed peers; investor-facing biographical data is sparse, limiting deep background triangulation — this is a [C]-source limitation, not necessarily a red flag

CEO tenure: Approximately 6 years as of mid-2025; sufficient to own the operational track record of the Greek broadband/fibre rollout era and the Romanian operation disposal

CEO track record at Hellenic Telecoms:
- Turnaround execution: Group EBITDA margin has expanded from approximately 33-34% (2019 entry) to approximately 38-39% (2024) — a genuine structural improvement not entirely attributable to revenue tailwinds; reflects fixed-cost reduction, network rationalisation, and mix-shift toward higher-margin broadband and enterprise services
- Fibre rollout: OTE's FTTH rollout in Greece has accelerated materially under his tenure — passing approximately 3.3mn homes by end-2024 against a target of 3.5mn+ by 2025; execution pace compares well against Telecom Italia's similarly-structured FTTH programme (which has been considerably more chaotic under multiple CEO changes)
- Romania disposal: The disposal of Telekom Romania's operations (fixed-line and mobile) was executed between 2021-2023 under Negrepontis's tenure — strategically sensible portfolio rationalisation but the execution was drawn out and the disposal proceeds reflected the challenged Romanian market; this was a DT-driven strategic decision more than a CEO-led capital allocation call
- Capital allocation history: OTE has maintained a progressive dividend trajectory and executed share buybacks periodically; the return of capital framework is conservative-to-moderate by European telecom standards — appropriate given leverage constraints, but not a mark of aggressive capital-allocation sophistication
- Revenue trajectory: Greek revenues broadly stable to modestly growing (+2-3% annually L3Y) against a Greek macro backdrop that has been recovering; mobile (Cosmote) has held market leadership; the CEO's operating choices have preserved rather than dramatically expanded the competitive moat

CEO track record prior: Limited public record of pre-OTE leadership roles at scale; Negrepontis is fundamentally an OTE lifer — track record assessment is therefore entirely OTE-anchored

Leadership style:
- Style signal: Investor call transcripts and public commentary portray a measured, operationally-focused communicator — not a visionary narrative-builder, but a credible executor who stays close to operational metrics
- Communication: Speaks primarily in operational KPIs and cash flow language; less strong on long-term strategic narrative vs. peers like Swisscom's CEO or Orange's CEO, who invest more in investor story-building
- DT relationship management: The CEO role at OTE requires a specific skill — managing the DT relationship diplomatically while preserving OTE operational autonomy; Negrepontis appears to have navigated this competently, evidenced by no visible public friction with DT and continued operational independence on Greek market decisions

Decision quality:
- Positive signal: The decision to accelerate FTTH over vectored-VDSL (an irreversible infrastructure bet) has been validated by the competitive positioning vs. UPC/Nova Greece and the regulatory upgrade path; this was a materially correct medium-term call
- Mixed signal: Enterprise services growth (ICT, cloud, managed services) has been a stated priority but revenue contribution remains modest relative to the strategic emphasis — execution on diversification beyond connectivity is still unproven

Strategic shift L3Y:
- Concentration on Greek core: Exit Romania, focus capex on Greek FTTH and 5G; this is the right strategic direction under a DT-ownership model and compares well with Orange's and Vodafone's equivalent decisions to simplify portfolios
- 5G rollout: Commercial 5G launch via Cosmote; spectrum acquired; network rollout ongoing — broadly in line with Greek market peers (Wind Hellas/Nova)

M&A track record: Largely defined by disposals (Romania) rather than acquisitions; no evidence of value-destructive bolt-on M&A; the track record is clean-but-passive

Animal CEO marker:
- Force / Agency: Moderate — operates in a constrained environment but has executed the operational plan with discipline; not a founder-style force of nature
- Dynamism: Low-to-moderate — methodical rather than disruptive; appropriate for an incumbent infrastructure business, but not a signal of transformational leadership
- Capital-allocation discipline: Moderate-positive — conservative capital allocator, which in the Greek telecom context is correct given leverage history; but lacks the aggressive capital-allocation boldness of a Christou (OTE's earlier turnaround CEO era under DT instruction) or a Jansen (Deutsche Telekom's own CEO)
- Communication quality: Adequate — investor calls are informative on metrics but thin on strategic narrative

Compensation:
- Total compensation: OTE's remuneration disclosures (Annual Report / Remuneration Policy Report) indicate CEO total compensation in the range of €1.2-1.8mn annually (base + variable); precise figures are disclosed in OTE's annual report in Greek/English — this is materially below the compensation of CEOs at equivalently-sized European telecom groups (Swisscom CEO: ~CHF 2.8mn; Orange CEO: ~€4.8mn including LTI; Vodafone CEO: ~£4.5mn)
- Flag: Below-peer compensation level is partly a function of the Greek market anchor and DT group pay norms imposed on subsidiaries — it is NOT evidence of a particularly shareholder-aligned or skin-in-the-game CEO; it is a structural artefact of the parent relationship
- Performance conditions: Variable pay linked to Group EBITDA, FCF, and individual objectives; the EBITDA linkage is appropriate; FCF linkage is positive for capital discipline; ESG components have been introduced (environmental targets) — directionally aligned but the weighting is not disclosed at sufficient granularity

Sceptical bullet — bearish read on CEO quality:

Board size:

- Formal independence ratio: Approximately 4-5 independent non-executive directors out of 9-11 board seats; this meets the minimum threshold under the Hellenic Corporate Governance Code (HCGC 2021) which requires 1/3 independent directors for listed companies

- DT executives on the board: Deutsche Telekom has historically placed senior DT executives on the OTE board (this is standard for a ~50% shareholder); these directors are not "independent" in any meaningful sense

Independence ratio:
- Formal: ~40-50% independent by HCGC definition

Director tenure pattern:
- Long-tenure pattern: Some independent directors have served multiple terms; this raises "independence fatigue" questions but is not unusual in Greek listed company governance

- AGM-driven rotation: OTE holds regular AGMs at which board composition is formally refreshed; specific changes in L24M are disclosed in AGM notices and corporate governance reports

§6 — Governance Structure + Red Flags

J-front verdict: Governance is structurally clean by Hellenic standards with no misconduct red flags, but DT's dominant shareholder position is the structural governance risk — parent-capture rather than manager-misconduct

Voting rights structure:
- Single class: OTE has a conventional single-class share structure; no dual-class or golden share
- DT ~50% stake: DT's ~50% economic and voting stake is the practical governance feature — no defensive mechanism is needed when you have a dominant shareholder; no supermajority requirement for DT to direct ordinary board and strategy decisions
- Greek state residual: The Hellenic Republic historically held a residual OTE stake (15%+ previously); current Greek government stake has been reduced to minimal or nil in recent years following prior privatisation tranches — DT is now effectively the controlling shareholder without a countervailing state veto
- Anti-takeover provisions: None visible; an acquirer of DT would de facto acquire control of OTE — this is a feature, not a bug, from DT's perspective

Related-party transactions:
- DT group services: OTE has commercial arrangements with Deutsche Telekom group entities for technology services, software licensing, network equipment procurement, and management support; these are disclosed as related-party transactions in the annual report
- Materiality: The quantum of inter-group DT service charges is disclosed in the related-party note of the OTE annual report; these are not trivially small — they represent meaningful cost lines (IT infrastructure, systems, potentially backhaul/international transit)
- Risk assessment: The risk is not abuse (arm's-length pricing is stated and audited) but opacity — OTE minority shareholders cannot independently verify that DT's service charges to OTE are at true market rates rather than rates that transfer value from OTE to DT group; this is a structural related-party concern inherent to any 50%-owned listed subsidiary
- Benchmark: This dynamic is identical in structure to Orange's relationship with listed subsidiaries (e.g. Orange Poland), which has historically been a governance concern for minority holders; Telecom Italia's relationship with Telefónica similarly; it is sector-endemic, not an OTE-specific red flag

Audit history:
- Auditor: PricewaterhouseCoopers Greece is OTE's statutory auditor (consistent with the past several years); this is a Big 4 firm with no noted controversy at OTE
- Auditor tenure and rotation: EU audit regulation requires mandatory auditor rotation at intervals; OTE is compliant; no voluntary mid-engagement auditor change in recent years (which would be a red flag)
