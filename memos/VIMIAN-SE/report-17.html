The policy as disclosed: "The Group is exposed to foreign exchange risk primarily through its international operations. The Group does not currently use derivative financial instruments to hedge its foreign currency exposure in a systematic way." This is an explicit policy statement confirming minimal hedging discipline. **Translation hedging:** Explicitly absent. Vimian does not hedge net investment in foreign subsidiaries (the standard mechanism for translation hedging — cross-currency swaps or foreign-currency borrowings designated as net-investment hedges). This is partially explained by the practical difficulty and cost of hedging SEK vs USD/EUR at Vimian's subsidiary scale, but it also means 100% of translation risk falls through to the P&L and OCI. **Transaction hedging:** Limited and episodic. The annual report discloses that some operating subsidiaries may use forward contracts for specific near-term transaction exposures (e.g. contract currency mismatches at point of procurement), but this is: - Not a group-level programme - Not subject to a stated hedge ratio - Not disclosed in terms of quantum hedged, duration, or instrument type - Not subject to effectiveness testing disclosure **Hedge horizon:** Not disclosed for any systematic programme. Implicitly: any subsidiary-level hedging is likely 1–3 months in duration (short-term transactional coverage) — the minimum and least protective form. **Hedge ratio:** Not disclosed. Best inference from filing language: near-zero for translation; low-to-moderate (estimated 20–40% of specific transaction exposures) at subsidiary level. **Hedge instruments:** Forward contracts implied from IFRS 7 note disclosures; no options, collars, or structured FX products disclosed. Simple forwards only, where used. **Hedge effectiveness L3Y:** Not formally disclosed (no hedge accounting applied, so IFRS 9 effectiveness testing is not required). This is both a transparency gap and a disclosure gap — Richard cannot assess whether episodic subsidiary hedging has been value-additive or cost-neutral. **Hedge cost / hedge income recognition:** Because Vimian does not apply hedge accounting, any derivative gains/losses flow through P&L as financial items (not through OCI). The IFRS 7 sensitivity disclosures show small net FX gain/loss positions (typically SEK ±20–80m annually in recent filings — a rounding item relative to total revenue). This confirms transaction hedging is immaterial in quantum. **Net investment hedges:** None disclosed. Some international compounders use EUR- or USD-denominated debt to create a natural translation hedge (debt currency matches subsidiary functional currency, so asset/liability translation effects partially offset). Vimian's debt is SEK and EUR-denominated (senior secured facilities in EUR primarily); the EUR-debt provides a partial natural translation hedge vs EUR-revenue subsidiaries, but the detail is not disclosed explicitly in the hedging policy section. This is worth flagging as a positive nuance: EUR-denominated debt effectively auto-hedges the balance-sheet translation exposure of EUR-functional subsidiaries to some degree. **EUR-debt as partial natural hedge — quantification:** Vimian's senior debt is partially EUR-denominated (the leveraged buyout structure retained EUR facilities). If EUR debt outstanding is approximately EUR 300–500m (estimated from leverage ratios and disclosed facility sizes), then EUR/SEK balance-sheet translation of the debt partially offsets EUR/SEK translation of EUR-revenue subsidiaries. This is an imperfect and indirect hedge, but it is an operational hedge not reflected in the "we don't hedge" disclosure language. **Sceptical bullet:** The bearish read on hedging discipline is that the combination of (a) no translation hedging, (b) no systematic transaction hedging programme, (c) no disclosed hedge ratio, (d) no disclosed hedge duration, and (e) no hedge-effectiveness reporting makes Vimian effectively a leveraged, unhedged SEK-vs-USD/EUR vehicle for any investor focused on reported SEK earnings. The partial natural hedge from EUR-denominated debt is real but imprecise — it matches the balance-sheet translation of debt, not the P&L translation of earnings. An investor who buys Vimian for its specialty animal health earnings story is also implicitly buying a short-SEK / long-USD-EUR FX position, and this position is neither disclosed in those terms nor actively managed. In a multi-year SEK-strengthening environment (non-trivial probability given Riksbank normalisation), the FX-orphaned earnings could compress by 10–15% vs the constant-currency EPS trajectory, with no hedging buffer to absorb the adjustment. --- ### §6 — Quantified Sensitivity Analysis **J-front verdict:** A ±10% SEK/USD move produces approximately ±6–8% revenue impact and ±8–12% EBITDA impact at group level — making FX the single largest source of EPS forecast uncertainty after organic growth; the sensitivity is asymmetric, with the downside case more concerning given SEK trajectory and US macro outlook **Building the sensitivity:** **Key parameters for sensitivity build:** - Revenue ~SEK 4.5–5.0bn (FY2024 estimated; use SEK 4.7bn mid-case) - EBITDA ~SEK 900m–1.1bn (FY2024 estimated; use SEK 1.0bn mid-case, ~21% margin) - Revenue by currency: USD ~42%, EUR ~28%, GBP ~7%, SEK ~7%, Other ~16% - EBITDA margin by currency: USD segment ~22–24%, EUR segment ~20–22%, SEK corporate (net drag, loss-making due to HQ costs) - No systematic translation hedging; transaction hedging immaterial - Translation = nearly pure pass-through for USD/EUR revenues vs SEK **USD ±10% impact (translation effect, revenue):** USD revenue ~SEK 1.97bn × 10% = SEK ~197m revenue impact.
