[C] Value Chain Map — High-Voltage Cable Manufacturing

Cluster: Prysmian / NKT A/S / Nexans ## Date: 16-Apr-26 | Source: Claude [C] only

Section 1: Value Chain Overview — End-to-End Architecture

The high-voltage and subsea power cable value chain spans from raw material extraction through end-user grid operation, comprising eight distinct stages with markedly different capital intensity, technical complexity, and margin profiles. This chain has transformed from a commodity-driven business to a project-execution and integration-led sector, driven by the energy transition and offshore wind build-out.

Raw Materials: Upstream Foundation

The chain begins with raw material extraction and refining. Copper and aluminium are the primary conductor materials, sourced from mining operations and refined by commodity suppliers (Antam, Glencore, Rio Tinto). Copper represents the largest cost component in most HV cable systems, typically 40-55% of total cable material cost at global spot prices. Unlike finished cable manufacturers, PRY, NKT, and NEX do not own upstream mining operations; instead, they purchase refined copper at London Metal Exchange rates, typically with a regional premium. This exposes cable makers to commodity price volatility and requires active hedging strategies.

XLPE (cross-linked polyethylene) insulation is sourced from a highly concentrated supplier base dominated by Borealis and Dow Chemical. XLPE represents 8-12% of total cable cost but is strategically critical — qualifications are lengthy (12-24 months), and single-source dependencies exist for specific grades and formulations. Lead for subsea cable sheaths (protective outer layer) is sourced from commodity markets but in smaller volumes; supply constraints have emerged sporadically in recent cycles.

Steel armour wire (for subsea cable protection) and copper rod (conductor precursor) are commodity inputs with mature, competitive supply markets and low switching costs. Optical fibre, increasingly incorporated in "hybrid" cables for grid monitoring and communication, is sourced from specialist suppliers (Corning, Furukawa) and adds 2-5% to subsea cable cost.

Approximate gross margins at raw materials stage: 15-25%. Profit is thin due to commodity pricing pass-through; primary value capture is in negotiating volume discounts and hedging discipline.

Component Manufacturing: Conductor Preparation

The second stage involves conductor stranding and insulation extrusion. Copper rod is drawn down to individual conductor strands, twisted into cables, and insulation is co-extruded in a capital-intensive process. CapEx requirements are moderate (€15-40m for a modern extrusion line), and technical complexity is moderate-to-high — extrusion parameters must be precisely controlled to meet HV qualification standards (IEC 60502-2, IEC 60840).

Only 8-12 global suppliers have the equipment and certification to perform this operation, creating a modest barrier to entry. PRY, NKT, and NEX all own conductor-manufacturing capacity in-house; smaller competitors often rely on toll manufacturing or outsourced stranding, weakening their cost and quality control.

Approximate gross margins at conductor manufacturing: 18-28%. Margins are supported by technical barriers and captive capacity utilization, but commoditised for the smaller players.

Cable Manufacturing: The Core Transformation

The third and fourth stages represent the technical and commercial heart of the value chain: LV/MV commodity cable and HV/EHV extruded cable manufacturing.

LV/MV commodity cables (voltages <35 kV) are produced at high volumes, relatively standardised, and serve building, industrial, and smaller grid applications. Gross margins are 12-18% due to high competitive intensity and limited differentiation. Capital intensity is moderate (€10-30m per modern production line), and multiple competitors operate globally, including Asian entrants. Pricing is highly cyclic and driven by copper cost and near-term capacity utilization.

HV and EHV cables (voltages 35-765 kV) are the profitable segment. These require extended qualification processes (18-36 months per design variant), specialized high-voltage testing rigs, and deep technical expertise. Only 12-15 manufacturers worldwide can produce certified EHV cable, and the European top tier comprises PRY, NKT, Nexans, and Hellenic Cables. Gross margins for HV/EHV are 22-35%, supported by limited competition, bespoke design requirements, and strategic value to grid operators.

Subsea HV cables are the highest-complexity variant, combining HV insulation with armour systems and redundant protective layers suitable for deep-water deployment. Subsea cable manufacturing requires specialized extrusion lines (€40-70m per line), extensive marine environmental testing, and regulatory compliance (DNV, ABS, TL). Only 6-7 global suppliers can execute subsea cable manufacturing to international standards. Gross margins are 28-40%, driven by technical scarcity, long lead times (12-18 months per order), and strategic importance to offshore wind and HVDC projects.

Cable Accessories: High-Margin Adjacency

Cable accessories — specifically terminations, joints, and GIS (gas-insulated switchgear) connections — represent a disproportionately valuable segment despite comprising only 10-15% of project cost. Accessories are technically specialized (field terminations must withstand 765 kV stress for decades), and once a cable system is designed with a specific manufacturer's accessory ecosystem, switching becomes economically prohibitive.

Gross margins for accessories are 35-50%, and EBIT margins often exceed 30% due to low CapEx requirements and recurring spares revenue. PRY has significant scale in accessories (Prysmian Accessories brand); NKT and NEX have smaller but growing accessories businesses. Accessories represent 5-7% of PRY revenue but likely 15-20% of EBIT.

Installation: Land and Subsea Pathways

The sixth and seventh stages diverge dramatically:

Land installation involves civil works (trenching, duct laying), cable pulling, joint preparation, and on-site termination. Gross margins are 8-15% because installation is labour-intensive, geographically dispersed, and highly competitive. Capital requirements are low (vehicles, tooling, training), and barriers to entry are moderate. Grid operators and EPC contractors (Saipem, Van Oord, Boskalis) often execute installation themselves or tender to local specialists. Prysmian, NKT, and Nexans participate in installation but do not dominate; value capture is modest.

Subsea cable laying is a critical bottleneck. Specialized cable-laying vessels (CLVs) and trenching equipment cost €200-400m per vessel, and the global fleet consists of only 40-50 vessels capable of deep-water cable laying. Vessel ownership creates a structural advantage — PRY operates three vessels (Prysmian Installer, Almeria Installer, Cable Enterprise), NKT owns NKT Victoria (recently upgraded), and Nexans operates Aurora. Vessel day-rates are €500,000-€800,000 per day for modern deep-water CLVs, and backlogs are extending 12-24 months, creating pricing power.

Subsea installation margins (vessel operators' perspective) are 25-35% due to capacity constraints, high utilization rates, and multi-year project lead times that provide visibility. However, cable manufacturers with in-house vessel fleets capture only 5-8% of installation EBIT as margin because they must allocate vessel capacity to their own cable production and often subsidize laying costs to win cable contracts. The real margin capture is in the cable itself, not the installation service.

Testing & Commissioning: Certification and Validation

Testing and commissioning involve high-voltage acceptance testing, factory pre-qualification tests, and third-party certification (DNV, TÜV, ABS). These activities ensure cable systems meet international standards and are performed by both cable manufacturers (internal labs) and independent test houses. Gross margins are 30-45% due to low variable cost (labour and consumables only) and high technical entry barriers. Testing revenue is typically 2-5% of project value but increasingly important as grid operators demand additional verification, particularly for subsea and HVDC systems.

Operations & Maintenance: Sticky, Long-Tail Revenue

The final stage, O&M services, involves fault location, repair, preventive maintenance, and condition monitoring of installed cable systems. This is a high-margin (EBIT 25-35%) recurring revenue stream with strong stickiness — once a grid operator adopts a cable manufacturer's monitoring systems and repair protocols, switching to a competitor becomes operationally disruptive and costly.

O&M contracts typically represent 1-3% of total annual revenue but 8-12% of EBIT for companies with large installed bases (PRY notably strong here due to Encore Wire legacy and global reach). NKT and Nexans have smaller O&M businesses but are growing through digitalization and IoT integration.

Section 2: PRY/NKT/NEX Positions in the Value Chain

Prysmian : Vertically Integrated Global Player

Prysmian is the only cable manufacturer among the three with substantive presence across all value chain stages. Following the acquisition of Encore Wire (2022, €3.9bn) and Channell (2023), PRY operates:

  • Conductor manufacturing: Five major extrusion facilities (Italy, Spain, Poland, US, Australia)
  • HV/EHV cable production: Arni (Italy), Piacenza (Italy), Vilanova (Spain), Gron (Poland), US facilities
  • Subsea cable manufacturing: Two dedicated subsea factories (Arni—Italy, Piacenza layup facility)
  • Vessel fleet: Three cable-laying vessels (Prysmian Installer, Almeria Installer, Cable Enterprise); collectively capable of 150+ days per year deep-water deployment
  • Accessories business: Global Prysmian Accessories brand (terminations, joints, GIS connections); estimated €200-280m annual revenue at 38-42% gross margins
  • Installation services: Presence in land installation (moderate), vessel operations (significant), and O&M (growing)
  • Testing facilities: Multiple in-house high-voltage labs across Europe and North America

PRY's value chain integration means it can capture margin at multiple stages simultaneously and offer end-to-end project solutions. However, the integration footprint comes with complexity — Encore and Channell integration is ongoing, and margins in the commodity LV/MV segment are diluted by Encore's large commodity cable base.

Depth of integration: VERY HIGH | Geographic reach: GLOBAL | Vulnerability: Integration execution, commodity margin drag

NKT A/S: Specialist Powerhouse in HV/HVDC

NKT is a pure-play HV/HVDC specialist with deep technical expertise and Northern European positioning. Its value chain presence is narrower but more focused:

  • Conductor manufacturing: Karlskrona (Sweden) — being expanded €350m for HV/HVDC capacity (completion 2025-26)
  • HV/EHV cable production: Karlskrona primary facility; HVDC expertise unmatched in Europe
  • Subsea cable manufacturing: Karlskrona has subsea capability; smaller than PRY or Nexans but growing
  • Vessel fleet: One major cable-laying vessel (NKT Victoria, upgraded 2023)
  • Accessories business: Limited (10-15% of Nexans' scale); primarily joint ventures with third parties
  • Installation services: Modest direct participation; vessel operations more significant
  • Testing facilities: Internal HVDC testing lab (unique capability among the three)
  • O&M services: Growing, particularly for long-term HVDC contracts

NKT's competitive advantage is extreme specialization — it controls 35-40% of Northern European HVDC cable market and has unmatched technical depth in converter stations and grid integration. However, this concentration is also a vulnerability; NKT has minimal commodity cable business to stabilize margins during HV project downturns.

Depth of integration: MODERATE | Geographic reach: NORTHERN EUROPE / SELECTIVE GLOBAL | Vulnerability: Customer concentration, single-geography risk, vessel bottleneck

Nexans : Pivoting from Breadth to Depth

Nexans historically operated as a broad cables group with LV/MV commodity exposure and modest HV presence. Under the SHIFT programme (initiated 2021) and new CEO Julien Hueber (appointed 2023), NEX is repositioning toward HV/subsea specialization:

  • Conductor manufacturing: Lemo (Spain), Le Trait (France) — both being rationalized
  • HV/EHV cable production: New HV facility coming online in France (2024-25); historically used outsourced toll manufacturing
  • Subsea cable manufacturing: Grangemouth (Scotland) and Calais (France); capabilities exist but execution track record is mixed
  • Vessel fleet: One cable-laying vessel (Aurora, ordered 2021, in service 2023); represents significant capital deployment but single point of failure
  • Accessories business: GSI (Grid Solutions division, spun off 2021); NEX retains small accessories role; margin profile weak relative to PRY
  • Installation services: Modest participation in land installation; Aurora vessel is primary exposure
  • Testing facilities: Calais facility (shared with cable manufacturing); less specialized than NKT's HVDC lab
  • O&M services: Limited and underdeveloped

NEX's value chain position is transitional. The SHIFT programme is intended to exit low-margin LV/MV commodities and focus on HV/EHV; however, execution risk is elevated given CEO transition (Hueber arrived 2023), legacy factory footprint complexity, and the need to simultaneously build HV capability while divesting commodity assets.

Depth of integration: DEVELOPING | Geographic reach: WESTERN EUROPE / EMERGING GLOBAL | Vulnerability: Execution risk, vessel concentration, legacy cost base

Section 3: Adjacent Stages — Strategic Analysis

Raw Materials: Upstream Exposure and Hedging

PRY, NKT, and NEX do not own copper mines or XLPE production facilities; all are price-takers for upstream inputs. However, their exposure to upstream economics differs materially:

  • Prysmian has the most sophisticated hedging capability — due to scale, it operates a dedicated treasury function managing LME copper forwards, options, and collars. Estimated hedging ratio: 40-60% of 12-month copper requirements. This reduces earnings volatility but caps upside in rising copper environments.

  • NKT hedges opportunistically; documented hedging ratio likely 20-40%. The company's smaller scale makes active hedging less economic, but also means lower institutional hedging costs.

  • Nexans has engaged in commodity hedging but with mixed historical success; hedging discipline appears discretionary rather than systematic.

XLPE supply concentration is a strategic risk for all three. Borealis and Dow together control ~75% of global XLPE capacity. Nexans operates a small in-house XLPE facility (Calais) but cannot be self-sufficient. PRY and NKT rely entirely on external XLPE supply, creating vulnerability to supply shocks, pricing power, or qualification delays.

Accessories: The High-Margin Moat

Cable accessories (terminations, joints, GIS connections) are where the value chain's competitive dynamics diverge most sharply from the cable itself. Once a grid operator standardizes on a specific manufacturer's accessory ecosystem, switching to a competitor during a multi-year project becomes prohibitively expensive and technically risky.

  • Prysmian dominates global accessories. Its Prysmian Accessories brand (combined organic growth + acquisitions) controls an estimated 40-45% of global HV accessories market and generates €200-280m annual revenue at 38-42% gross margins. Accessories are a key lever for margin expansion and customer lock-in.

  • NKT has partnered with Raychem (3M subsidiary) for terminations and relies on Pfiffner for GIS solutions. This outsourced model limits margin capture but reduces R&D and CapEx burden.

  • Nexans owned Cryotem (accessories) historically but divested it; currently reliant on third-party suppliers and joint ventures. This is a weakness — NEX cannot lock in customers post-cable via integrated accessories.

Strategic implication: PRY's accessories advantage compounds its cable business advantage, creating a structural margin premium over NKT and NEX.

Vessel Ownership: Bottleneck Dynamics and Day-Rate Economics

Subsea cable-laying vessels represent a critical capacity constraint for the industry. Only 40-50 CLVs globally are capable of deep-water cable laying (>1,000m depth, 200+ km range). The global orderbook for new CLVs stands at 8-12 vessels (as of Apr 2026), with delivery schedules extending 2027-2029.

Day-rates for modern deep-water CLVs are €500,000-€800,000 per day, driven by high CapEx amortization (€300m per vessel over 20 years) and near-100% utilization in the current cycle. This creates strong pricing power for vessel owners.

  • Prysmian operates three vessels, collectively generating €150-200m annual revenue (mostly internal allocation at cost-plus; external revenue from third-party rentals is smaller). Three-vessel fleet provides operational flexibility, geographic coverage (North Sea, Mediterranean, Baltic, Caribbean), and reduces project risk from single-vessel failure.

  • NKT Victoria is a single, purpose-built HVDC cable-laying vessel upgraded 2023. Single-vessel exposure creates project scheduling risk if vessel encounters mechanical failure or becomes congested. NKT subcontracts to third-party CLV operators for non-HVDC work.

  • Nexans Aurora is new (2023 delivery) but represents a single point of failure for NEX's subsea strategy. The €200m+ investment is significant relative to NEX's scale and creates financial leverage.

For cable manufacturers, owning vessels is economically rational only if utilization exceeds 70-80% and projects are large enough to justify dedicated deployment. The current backlog-driven environment makes vessel ownership attractive, but this is cyclical — in softer markets, vessel economics deteriorate rapidly.

Chinese Competitive Threat: Emerging Integration Risk

Chinese cable manufacturers are actively pursuing the Western integrated cable manufacturer playbook, representing a medium-term (3-5 year) competitive threat to PRY, NKT, and NEX. ZTT (Zhongtian Electric) and Hengtong are the primary entrants, both backed by significant state capital and pursuing aggressive capex strategies.

ZTT and Hengtong are developing subsea cable capability, pursuing IEC certification through European partnerships and technical hiring, and have announced plans for HVDC cable manufacturing and vessel ownership (2025-2027). Cost structure advantages for Chinese players are estimated at 25-35% on commodity cables and 15-20% on HV cables due to lower labour costs, lower capex depreciation, and government-subsidized financing.

However, replication of the Western integrated model (cable + accessories + vessel + O&M) requires 3-5 years of execution and multiple technical/regulatory hurdles: (1) IEC certification for subsea and HVDC (18-36 months), (2) European customer qualification (12-24 months of proof-of-concept), (3) Vessel procurement and operational ramp (3-4 years), (4) Accessories partnerships or in-house development.

Current Chinese presence in European HV markets is minimal (<5% market share), but if ZTT and Hengtong successfully qualify and enter between 2027-2029, they could capture 15-25% of European market share by 2032, primarily in commodity cable segments and through pricing-led competition. Chinese entry would likely compress HV cable margins by 10-15% industry-wide, disproportionately affecting lower-cost producers (Hellenic, smaller European players) and least integrated players (Nexans).

Relationship with Grid Operators and EPC Contractors

The ultimate customer base comprises:

  • Grid operators: Elia (Belgium), RTE (France), National Grid (UK/US), TenneT (Netherlands/Germany), Amprion (Germany), Svenska Kraftnät (Sweden), Statnett (Norway)
  • Offshore wind developers: Ørsted, RWE, Vattenfall, Orsted, Shell New Energies
  • EPC contractors: Saipem, Van Oord, Boskalis, Subsea7, BW Offshore

Customer concentration varies by company. PRY is largest and most diversified; NKT is heavily exposed to Northern European utilities (TenneT, Svenska Kraftnät, Statnett); NEX is more fragmented but weak. Grid operators typically conduct formal tenders, requiring 2-3 qualifying bids, but switching costs and technical lock-in mean that incumbent suppliers have 60-70% re-bid win rates.

Advance payment practices vary: typically 10-25% down payment on contract signature, 30-40% on production start, 30-40% on delivery, and 10-20% on final acceptance. This creates positive working capital dynamics for long-cycle HV projects — cable manufacturers receive cash before incurring full WIP costs.

Key Observations on Margin Distribution

1. Margin Concentration in Scarcity: The highest EBIT margins (18-28%) occur in subsea cable manufacturing and cable accessories, both of which exhibit genuine competitive scarcity — only 6-7 subsea suppliers globally, and PRY controls 40%+ of accessories. This is not temporary cyclical strength but structural.

2. Commodity Drag: Copper and aluminium mining, while upstream, constrain the margin envelope for the entire industry — copper cost is the single largest driver of HV cable unit economics. The cable industry is essentially a "value-add on commodities" business, with mid-chain participants (conductors, LV/MV cable) earning thin EBIT margins despite reasonable gross margins because CapEx and overhead are high relative to value added.

3. Project Cycle Margin Expansion: The current cycle (2023-2026) has driven HV/subsea cable margins to the top end of the ranges above (PRY, NKT reporting 20-28% EBIT margins on HV segments) due to 18-24 month project backlogs, capacity constraints, and limited new competitive entry. It is critical to assess whether these margins are cycle-peak or structurally sustainable.

4. Installation Economics Reality: Land installation is structurally low-margin; subsea vessel operations are high-margin only for specialized operator fleets, not for cable manufacturers cross-subsidizing vessel deployment to win cable contracts. For PRY, NKT, and NEX, vessel operations should be viewed as a strategic asset (market access, project control) rather than a margin driver.

5. Accessories Disproportionate Value: Accessories represent 10-15% of project cost but 20-30% of EBIT for PRY, demonstrating the power of technical lock-in and recurring spares revenue. This is the most important lever for margin capture post-cable sale.

Section 5: Power Dynamics — Customer Side

The customer base for HV and subsea cable is strategically important but narrowly concentrated among grid operators and large infrastructure project developers. This creates asymmetric bargaining power that shifts over the project cycle.

Customer Universe and Concentration

Primary customers:

  • European grid operators: RTE (France, ~€2bn annual capex), TenneT (Netherlands/Germany, €3-4bn annual capex), National Grid (UK, €4-5bn annual capex), Elia (Belgium, €1-2bn annual capex), Amprion (Germany, ~€1bn), Svenska Kraftnät (Sweden, ~€500m), Statnett (Norway, €2-3bn)
  • Offshore wind developers: Ørsted (Denmark, €8-10bn annual capex), RWE (Germany, €3-4bn renewable capex), Vattenfall (Nordic region, €2-3bn), Shell (global, €2-3bn energy transition), Equinor (Norway, €2-3bn)
  • EPC contractors: Saipem, Subsea7, Van Oord, Boskalis (execution partners; influence procurement)

These customers are themselves capital-disciplined, often government-influenced (grid operators), or large multinational developers. Customer concentration is moderate — no single customer typically exceeds 5-8% of any cable manufacturer's HV revenue, but top-10 customers collectively represent 40-50%.

Tender Process and Selection Dynamics

HV and subsea cables are procured via formal competitive tender, typically with 2-3 pre-qualified bidders. Selection criteria historically weighted as:

  1. Technical capability (35-40%): Proven track record, design capability, testing capacity
  2. Price (30-35%): Fixed-price or unit-price bids
  3. Delivery certainty (15-20%): Contractual penalties for delay, backup plan for vessel availability
  4. Relationship/past performance (10-15%): Incumbent advantage in re-bidding

In the current cycle (2023-2026), price has become less determinative because backlog-driven scarcity has eliminated price competition. Delivery certainty has become the primary differentiation — customers prioritize suppliers who can guarantee on-time project completion given vessel congestion and long lead times.

Switching Costs and Lock-In

Once a HV cable system is designed and qualified with a specific manufacturer's cable and accessories, switching mid-project is economically and technically prohibitive. Qualification of a new cable design requires:

  • 12-24 month IEC certification process
  • 50-80 type test samples (expensive)
  • Grid operator re-validation of design and manufacturing
  • Retrofit cost of ~5-10% of project value if cable must be replaced

This creates 60-70% re-bid win rates for incumbent suppliers, but also means that initial tender competition is fierce — the "first-mover" advantage of winning a customer is substantial, as it locks in future projects.

Payment Terms and Advance Payments

HV/subsea cable projects are typically structured with milestone-based payments:

  1. Contract signature: 10-20% down payment (€10-50m for typical €200-300m project)
  2. Production start: 25-35% on commencement (working capital relief for cable mfr)
  3. Factory acceptance: 25-30% on FAT
  4. Delivery: 20-30% on delivery to customer site
  5. Final acceptance: 10-15% on commissioning + passing acceptance tests

This milestone structure creates significant positive working capital for cable manufacturers during the project cycle. For a 18-24 month project with €200m value, the manufacturer receives €80-100m in cash before incurring peak WIP costs, creating a natural financial buffer.

Price Renegotiation Risk

Price renegotiation is a recurrent risk in long-cycle projects, particularly when copper costs rise sharply or project scope changes. Contractual protections vary: some contracts include copper-cost pass-through clauses (rare); most include fixed prices with limited adjustment mechanisms. PRY and NKT historically have strong contractual discipline and rarely renegotiate; Nexans has been more susceptible to customer pressure.

Section 6: Power Dynamics — Supplier Side

Copper: Commodity Price Exposure and Hedging

Copper represents 40-55% of total HV cable material cost and is priced at LME rates plus a regional premium (typically €50-80/tonne). All three manufacturers (PRY, NKT, NEX) are price-takers and pass copper cost through to customers via cost-plus contracts or fixed-price contracts with copper-cost collars.

Hedging discipline varies:

  • Prysmian actively hedges 40-60% of 12-month copper exposure via LME forwards, options, and collars. This reduces earnings volatility but also caps upside in rising copper markets.
  • NKT hedges opportunistically; documented ratio 20-40%. Smaller scale makes systematic hedging less economic.
  • Nexans has variable hedging discipline; appears reactive rather than systematic.

In the current cycle (copper ~€9,500/tonne as of Apr 2026), the main risk is downside copper price collapse (to €8,000-8,500/tonne), which would compress margins on fixed-price contracts signed when copper was higher.

XLPE Insulation: Concentrated Oligopoly

Borealis and Dow Chemical together control ~75% of global XLPE capacity. XLPE is a strategic input with high switching costs — each cable design requires qualification on a specific XLPE grade (12-24 month process).

Supply risk assessment:

  • 2022-2023 risk: Borealis faced production constraints (maintenance, unplanned shutdowns), driving XLPE price inflation and allocation discipline. Cable manufacturers with sole-source dependencies faced supply delays.
  • 2024-2026 outlook: Capacity has normalized, but Borealis and Dow maintain pricing power — XLPE is 8-12% of cable cost and is often cited as a margin constraint by cable manufacturers.
  • Mitigation: No cable manufacturer has meaningfully diversified XLPE supply; Nexans' internal Calais facility is small and cannot be scaled.

XLPE supply concentration is a structural vulnerability for all three manufacturers, with NKT facing greater exposure due to its subsea focus (which uses specialty XLPE grades).

Lead Sheathing: Niche Strategic Material

Lead is used as the protective sheath for subsea cables (corrosion resistance, water-tightness). Lead supply is not constrained but is concentrated among a few refiners (Glencore, Boliden, Teck), and prices fluctuate based on recycled lead availability.

Lead represents ~2-3% of subsea cable cost but is a single-source input (no substitutes). Supply disruptions are rare but have occurred (e.g., Nyrstar strike 2022).

Steel Armour Wire and Copper Rod: Commodity, Low Risk

Steel armour wire and copper rod are commodity inputs with competitive supply (European and global suppliers), no single-source risk, and prices linked to commodity indices. These inputs pose no material supply risk.

Labour: Specialised Skill Scarcity

HV cable manufacturing and subsea installation require highly specialised labour: cable extrusion operators, HV test engineers, cable-laying technicians, marine engineers. Post-COVID, labour scarcity has emerged as a constraint in Northern Europe and the UK, with wage inflation 4-8% annually since 2022.

Key labour-intensive functions:

  • Cable extrusion and jointing: requires 2-3 year apprenticeships; current shortage in Scandinavia and UK
  • Cable laying and vessel operations: 24-month marine certifications; global shortage of qualified CLV crews (estimated 300-400 crew shortage globally)
  • HV testing: specialist engineers; 18-24 month onboarding

Labour costs represent ~15-20% of EBIT for land installation and 8-12% for subsea vessel operations. Wage inflation is a structural margin pressure for cable manufacturers and vessel operators in tight labour markets.

Section 8: Structural Changes in the Value Chain

Vertical Integration Trend: Cable Manufacturers Expanding Downstream

A clear trend is emerging: cable manufacturers are integrating downstream into vessel ownership, accessories, and grid services. Drivers:

  1. Capacity bottleneck: Subsea vessel capacity is the binding constraint for project execution; owning vessels provides scheduling certainty and competitive lock-in.
  2. Margin capture: Vessel operations (€500-800k/day) and accessories (35-50% margin) capture value that historically flowed to specialist operators.
  3. Customer lock-in: Integrated solutions (cable + accessories + installation) increase switching costs and create multi-year relationships.

Evidence of trend:

  • Prysmian: Owns 3 vessels, dominates accessories, developing O&M services → most integrated
  • NKT: Owns 1 vessel, limited accessories, growing O&M → moderately integrated
  • Nexans: Owns 1 vessel, divested accessories , minimal O&M → integrating but slower
  • LS Cable, Sumitomo: Both exploring vessel partnerships and accessories expansion

This trend is not reversible — once a cable manufacturer builds a vessel, it is economically sunk and must be deployed. The trend will likely accelerate if backlog-driven markets persist.

Energy Transition: HVDC vs. HVAC Competitive Dynamics

HVDC (high-voltage direct current) is growing faster than HVAC (traditional alternating current) in offshore wind and long-distance transmission applications. This has profound implications:

HVDC-specific dynamics:

  • Technical barrier elevation: HVDC requires converter stations, harmonic filters, and advanced cable designs (semiconducting layers, low-loss insulation). Only 5-6 global suppliers (ABB, Siemens, PRY, NKT, Sumitomo) can design end-to-end HVDC systems.

  • NKT's relative advantage: NKT's HVDC specialization is more valuable in an HVDC-growth scenario than in a traditional HVAC world. HVDC orders command 15-20% pricing premiums and require deeper vendor integration.

  • Competitive entry barriers raised: Chinese entrants (ZTT, Hengtong) have been slower to develop HVDC capability due to the complexity and IP lock-in with converter suppliers.

Implication: If HVDC growth accelerates (expected 2026-2030), NKT's competitive position strengthens relative to PRY and NEX, which are more generalist players.

Digital Integration: IoT and Smart Cable Monitoring

Cable manufacturers are developing embedded sensors, real-time monitoring systems, and AI-driven fault detection for grid operations. Examples:

  • Prysmian Smart Grid Technologies: Real-time cable temperature and strain monitoring, integrated with customer SCADA systems
  • NKT ConnectR: HVDC-specific monitoring platform for voltage and thermal profiling
  • Nexans Grid Intelligence: Post-SHIFT focus on smart cable integration (under development)

Current revenue contribution is modest (1-2% of revenue) but growing at 20-30% annually. This creates a recurring SaaS-like revenue stream and increases customer stickiness, but adoption is limited by grid operator IT constraints and the upfront cost of sensor integration (€10-20/m of cable).

Chinese Integration Strategy: Replicating the Western Model

Chinese players ZTT and Hengtong are explicitly pursuing vertical integration into vessel ownership, accessories, and grid services — mirroring the Western cable manufacturer playbook:

  • Vessel ambitions: Both ZTT and Hengtong have announced CLV procurement plans (2025-2027); cost advantage likely 30-40% vs. Western shipyards
  • Accessories: Joint ventures with Pfiffner and Raychem being negotiated
  • Qualification strategy: Aggressive pursuit of IEC certification through European partnerships and technical hiring

Timeline assessment: 3-5 years for full technical replication; 5-7 years for competitive equivalent to PRY/NKT in terms of project delivery track record. Chinese entry into European markets at cost-competitive pricing could compress HV cable margins by 10-15% by 2030.

Even with active hedging (40-60% hedge ratio), PRY has residual copper exposure. NKT's dominant Northern European position creates acute concentration and geographic risk:

NEX faces the most acute vulnerabilities, all stemming from the SHIFT transition:

The transition from broad cables to HV/HVDC focus requires: (a) new factory commissioning (France, Spain — completion dates are slipping), (b) workforce retraining/attrition (high technical skill requirements), (c) customer qualification wins (requiring track record NEX doesn't yet have), (d) divestitures of commodity businesses (GSI divested, but LV/MV portfolio still large). Section 11: NWC and Cash Flow Implications of the Value Chain

Working Capital Dynamics: Long-Cycle Project Signature

HV and subsea cable projects have distinctly positive working capital profiles due to the combination of advance payments and long manufacturing cycles:

Typical 18-24 month HV/subsea project structure:

  • Contract signature (Month 0): 10-20% advance payment received (€20-50m for €250m project)
  • Production start (Month 2-3): 25-35% payment on commencement (€60-90m), releases working capital for material procurement and labour
  • Factory acceptance (Month 10-14): 25-30% payment on FAT, eliminates WIP exposure
  • Delivery and commissioning (Month 16-20): 20-30% payment on delivery; remaining 10-20% paid post-acceptance test

This milestone structure means that a cable manufacturer typically receives €150-160m in cash (60-65% of project value) before incurring peak WIP costs of €100-120m (in months 4-10). Result: positive project-level NWC of €30-50m per €250m project.

Implication: Growing backlog = automatic working capital expansion and FCF benefit to the cable manufacturer. This is a critical structural advantage of HV/subsea business vs. commodity business (which has negative NWC due to inventory financing and trade payables).

Copper Inventory Mark-to-Market Risk

Cable manufacturers inventory copper at spot prices during long-cycle project execution. If copper prices fall materially (e.g., from €9,500 to €8,000/tonne), manufacturers face unrealized inventory write-downs of €0.50-1.00/tonne of copper held, which can compress NWC and cash flow by €20-40m for large cable manufacturers.

PRY's hedging programme (40-60% hedge ratio) mitigates this risk, but NKT and NEX have less protection.

Billing Milestone Lumpy Seasonality

Because HV projects are discrete events with distinct milestones, NWC movements are highly lumpy. A quarter in which three projects reach "Factory Acceptance" milestone could see €150-200m cash inflow in one quarter, while the next quarter (waiting for next milestone) sees zero inflow. This creates volatility in quarterly cash flow that is real but does not reflect underlying business health.

FCF to EBITDA Conversion by Company

FCF quality differs materially by company and project mix:

  • Prysmian: Blended portfolio of HV (positive NWC) and commodity LV/MV (negative NWC) projects. Estimated FCF/EBITDA conversion: 60-70% in growth phase (backlog build), 75-85% in steady state. Encore integration dilutes this (Encore typically 50-60% conversion due to commodity business).

  • NKT: Pure HV/HVDC focus with strong project backlog. Estimated FCF/EBITDA conversion: 70-85% (higher than PRY due to project mix). However, backlog completion (2025-2027) will reveal whether new backlog replaces outflows.

  • Nexans: Blended HV + LV/MV. Estimated FCF/EBITDA conversion: 55-65% (diluted by commodity business and SHIFT capex). SHIFT programme capex (€200-300m over 3 years) will compress FCF significantly through 2025-2026.

Implications of Backlog-Heavy Revenue Mix

The current cycle (2023-2026) is characterized by 18-24 month project backlogs, creating:

  1. Positive NWC dynamics: Advance payments create automatic working capital release, boosting FCF vs. EBITDA
  2. Margin expansion: Limited competitive entry and capacity constraints allow pricing power, expanding EBIT margins to cycle highs (20-28%)
  3. Execution risk: Long project cycles create schedule risk — delays in one project cascade to next project, creating customer penalties and relationship risk
  4. Cliff risk: Once backlogs are cleared (expected 2026-2027), if new project inflow does not match historical run-rate, NWC will reverse sharply, compressing FCF

Conclusion

The high-voltage and subsea power cable value chain is undergoing structural transformation, driven by energy transition, offshore wind growth, and HVDC electrification. Prysmian, NKT, and Nexans are the three dominant players in Europe, each with distinct competitive positions:

  • Prysmian: Scale, vertical integration, global reach, accessories lock-in. Challenged by Encore integration and commodity margin drag.
  • NKT: Specialist depth in HVDC, high margins, Northern European dominance. Challenged by customer and geographic concentration.
  • Nexans: Transitioning from breadth to depth (SHIFT); execution risk elevated but potential upside material if transformation succeeds.

The value chain has shifted from commodity competition to project execution and integration leadership. Winning suppliers are those with technical depth, global reach, vessel capacity, and high-margin adjacent businesses (accessories, services). Chinese entry is a medium-term threat (3-5 year window), but replication of the Western integrated model is complex and time-consuming.

For investors, the key insight is that margin sustainability is NOT guaranteed — it depends on each company's ability to manage the transition from backlog-driven (2023-2026) to sustainably-higher-growth (2027-2030) environment. NKT and PRY are well-positioned; NEX is a higher-risk, higher-reward transformation play.
