§2 — METHODOLOGICAL LANDSCAPE: WHAT SUBSTITUTES FOR PEER DATA?

  • J-front verdict: Three substitution strategies are established in investment-research methodology — intra-company time-series, cross-sector analogue, and macro/industry base rates — each valid but carrying distinct bias profiles
  • Intra-company time-series: The company's own prior cycles (revenue growth rates, margin expansion/contraction, ROIC evolution, FCF conversion across phases) provide a self-referential benchmark that is unaffected by peer staleness; it is the first-best substitute when peers are absent
  • Limitation: Survivorship bias — the company exists because it survived; its historical distribution excludes failure modes that peers would expose
  • Practical anchor: For a 5-10 year time-series, analyse at minimum: revenue CAGR by phase, EBIT margin by cycle, FCF conversion rate by capex cycle, ROIC vs WACC spread evolution
  • Cross-sector analogue: Identifies structurally similar businesses in different sectors or geographies — e.g., a niche B2B software company with no listed peers may have analogues in a different vertical with similar switching-cost economics
  • Validity condition: The analogue must share the causal mechanism (switching costs, network effects, regulatory moat, asset intensity) not just surface-level financial ratios
  • Risk: False analogy — drawing on an analogue that shares financials but not the underlying driver creates spurious confidence
  • Macro/industry base rates: Sector-level data from industry associations, regulatory filings, national statistics agencies, and sell-side thematic/industry reports provides a distributional anchor for market growth rates, pricing dynamics, and margin structures
  • Sell-side application: Industry reports (not stock-specific notes) are the primary SS source in peer-starved runs; they satisfy the 5L breadth gate when pulled from ≥5 distinct brokers across thematic/sector coverage
  • Primary source forensics: When peers are absent, primary source depth on the single stock increases in importance — every management commentary, every investor day transcript, every regulatory filing becomes load-bearing because there is no cross-validation from peer disclosures
  • Academic and practitioner base rates: Published meta-analyses of corporate financial performance (McKinsey's Valuation base rates, HOLT database distributions, Credit Suisse HOLT median CFROI data historically) provide distributional benchmarks for FCF conversion, ROIC, and growth persistence that are peer-independent
  • Concept A — edges: The non-consensus read is that peer-starvation can improve research quality in one narrow respect: the analyst is forced to understand the company's internal mechanics more deeply than when relative comparisons are available as shortcuts; some of the highest-conviction single-stock theses have come from researchers who had no peers and therefore built causal models rather than comparative ones
  • Concept A — edges (cont.): The outlier case is a company so unique that no valid substitution exists — niche technology platform, first-mover with no precedent, post-restructuring entity with discontinuous history; in this case, the honest research output is a scenario-based framework rather than a point estimate
  • Confidence calibration under peer starvation: The standard rule-of-thumb is: any claim that would normally be graded relative to peers (e.g., "above-average margin quality") should have its confidence reduced by one tier when peers are absent; this is not a failure disclosure but a calibration discipline
  • Source conflict: Some practitioners argue time-series substitution is better than peer comparison for mature businesses because peer sets are often heterogeneous and misleading (the "apples to oranges" problem in peer selection); others argue peer cross-section is irreplaceable because it controls for cycle effects the time-series cannot; the composite truth is that both are needed for robust triangulation, and either alone is a partial view
  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on substitution strategies is that they are all self-referential to varying degrees — intra-company data reflects management choices, cross-sector analogues reflect the analyst's subjective selection, and macro base rates reflect aggregate distributions that may not apply to the specific business; the analyst must resist the temptation to mistake the comfort of having a framework for the validity of the underlying claims

Confidence: Medium — anchor: substitution strategies are well-established in practitioner methodology but their effectiveness varies significantly by business type; no single substitution is universally valid

§3 — SELL-SIDE BREADTH LANDSCAPE UNDER PEER STARVATION

  • J-front verdict: SS breadth gate (5L, ≥40%) is achievable under peer starvation but requires a deliberate pivot from stock-specific to thematic/industry SS sourcing — the mechanics change, the gate does not
  • Stock-specific SS coverage: In a peer-starved run, stock-specific broker notes remain the primary SS source for company-specific claims (earnings revisions, target prices, capital allocation commentary); these should still be sourced from ≥3 distinct brokers if coverage exists
  • Thematic/industry SS coverage: For relative and industry-level claims, the researcher pivots to sector-wide broker publications — strategy notes, sector initiations, thematic deep dives — which provide distributional anchors that substitute for peer-specific data
  • Breadth mechanics: 5L gate requires ≥40% of substantive claims to be SS-anchored; under peer starvation, "substantive claims" that cannot be SS-anchored (because no broker covers the relative dimension without peer data) should be explicitly flagged as [C]-only and counted toward the breadth denominator
  • Practical SS sources in peer-starved runs: (a) Sector-wide industry surveys (e.g., broker sector outlook reports); (b) Global strategy notes that cite the sector; (c) Primary research reports that cover the industry dynamics; (d) Initiation-of-coverage reports which typically contain the most detailed industry-structure sections; (e) Conference/investor day notes that aggregate industry commentary
  • Expert call breadth (5M gate, Warn-only): Expert call sourcing is more important, not less, under peer starvation — an expert who has worked across the sector can provide the distributional context that peers would otherwise supply; the researcher should flag if no expert calls are available in a peer-starved run
  • Concept A — edges: The non-consensus read is that some of the most rigorous research is written without peer data precisely because the analyst must build from first principles using industry experts rather than anchoring to sell-side consensus peer tables — expert calls become the primary peer-substitution mechanism
  • Source conflict: Some sell-side methodology guidelines (e.g., CFA Institute Research Challenge norms) treat peer comparison as mandatory for relative valuation and would require explicit disclosure if omitted; others treat it as best-practice but not required for a valid research output; the composite truth is that disclosure is always required, but the research can be valid without peers if the substitution methodology is explicit
  • Historical note: Pre-Bloomberg Terminal era research (pre-1990) was routinely peer-starved by modern standards and relied primarily on industry association data, time-series, and expert interviews — the research quality was not necessarily inferior, but it was differently calibrated
  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on SS breadth in peer-starved runs is that industry-level SS notes may have been written with the assumption that peer context exists — a sector initiation that says "margins are below the peer median" is useless without knowing where the subject company sits; the researcher must screen out comparative SS claims that implicitly require peer data and replace them with absolute-level industry data

Confidence: Medium — anchor: SS breadth mechanics are well-understood; the peer-starvation pivot is practitioner-standard but requires deliberate execution discipline

§5 — DATA GAPS

  • J-front verdict: Under peer starvation, the most material data gaps fall into three categories — missing comparative distributions, missing industry structure data, and missing expert triangulation — all resolvable via named next steps
  • Critical data gap: Peer financial distributions (median ROIC, margin percentiles, EV/EBITDA ranges, FCF conversion norms for the sector) — without these, all relative valuation and competitive quality claims are unanchored; resolvable via: rapid peer data commission, HOLT database pull, or thematic SS sector note
  • Critical data gap: Industry structure data (market share distributions, pricing dynamics, customer concentration norms) — without this, competitive position claims are opinion not evidence; resolvable via: industry association reports, regulatory filings, broker sector initiations
  • Material gap: Expert triangulation — expert calls that could provide on-the-ground distributional context for the sector; resolvable via: AlphaSense expert call library, Tegus, GLG/Gerson Lehrman; flagged as Warn-only per 5M gate but material under peer starvation
  • Material gap: Cross-sector analogue validation — the researcher's selection of cross-sector analogues requires at minimum one expert or SS note confirming the structural similarity; without this, analogue selection is subjective; resolvable via: expert call with former executive from analogue sector
  • Minor gap: Historical precedent for the specific business model under the specific macro conditions — time-series data is available but interpretation requires context; resolvable via: academic literature search, McKinsey/BCG archival research
  • Unresolvable from desk: The causal reason for peer absence (is it genuine niche scarcity or a staleness/database problem?) — this requires a direct check of the AS database coverage and the D-BATCH-25 batch history; the researcher cannot determine this from [C] alone
  • Wait-and-monitor: If peer absence reflects a company that is genuinely pre-peer (recent IPO, spin-off, restructuring), the gap resolves naturally as the company builds track record and attracts coverage — the correct response is to flag and re-run in 2-3 quarters
  • Sceptical bullet: The data gaps may not reflect genuinely thin evidence — they may reflect the analyst not looking in the right places; per Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 1, the most-different source in a peer-starved run is not another broker note but a primary customer/supplier interview or a regulatory filing; the researcher should check whether primary sources have been exhausted before flagging a gap as unresolvable

§6 — SECOND + THIRD-ORDER IMPLICATIONS (CONCEPT A EDGE-DETECTION)

  • J-front verdict: The most important second-order implication of peer starvation is that APM confidence bands must widen proportionally, which has direct consequences for position sizing, IRR hurdle application, and hold-period conviction
  • Second-order: If peers are genuinely absent (not just stale), the stock may be a monopoly/oligopoly operator, a niche platform, or a structurally differentiated business — all of which have above-average long-run ROIC persistence in the academic literature (Mauboussin/CSFB base rate work); peer absence could be a quality signal, not just a data gap
  • Second-order: If peers are absent because the stock is in a sector with poor analyst coverage (e.g., micro-cap, regional operator, dual-listed in a secondary market), the information asymmetry is the edge — the concentrated long-only investor who does the forensic work gets the valuation advantage that peer-covered stocks do not offer
  • Third-order: Widened confidence bands reduce the APM's willingness to apply full position sizing — a stock that would be a 10% position with full peer triangulation may be capped at 5-6% under peer starvation; this has direct portfolio construction implications
  • Third-order: If multiple stocks in the portfolio are peer-starved simultaneously (a systematic peer-data staleness problem rather than a stock-specific issue), the aggregate confidence of the portfolio degrades in a correlated way — this is a process risk, not a single-stock risk
  • M/M/O test #1 — Management: Does management have means, motive, and opportunity to benefit from peer data absence? Means: yes, management controls investor relations and can selectively frame comparisons. Motive: yes, if peers show unflattering comparisons (lower margins, lower ROIC), management benefits from their absence in research. Opportunity: yes, if the company occupies a genuine niche and there are no forced peer disclosures. Implication: researcher should be alert to management framing that exploits peer absence — e.g., "we are unique and incomparable" narratives that may be obscuring unflattering peer dynamics
  • M/M/O test #2 — Sell-side: Does sell-side have means, motive, and opportunity to exploit peer data absence? Opportunity: yes, in peer-starved runs where the researcher has limited cross-check capability. Implication: researcher should cross-check any SS peer selection against an independent peer identification exercise before accepting the SS peer set
  • M/M/O test #3 — Short sellers: Does the short-selling community have means, motive, and opportunity to exploit peer starvation in the long-only investor's research? Means: yes, short sellers often have more complete peer context from their own research. Motive: yes, if a short thesis depends on unflattering peer comparisons that the long-only investor cannot see. Opportunity: yes, precisely in peer-starved research runs. Implication: the researcher should explicitly search for short-seller reports, activist research, and forensic accounting analyses as a counter-hypothesis check in peer-starved runs
  • Cross-pillar: Peer starvation does not affect all pillars equally — the Business Quality pillar (which relies most on relative comparisons) is most impaired; the Balance Sheet / Financial Strength pillar (which can be assessed on absolute solvency metrics) is least impaired; researcher should sequence the research to lead with the least-impaired pillars
  • Cross-thematic: In a market regime where the peer-data infrastructure is systematically stale (e.g., immediately post a sector restructuring, post-COVID dislocation of peer financials), peer-starvation is a market-wide condition — the edge is the researcher who corrects for it fastest
  • Inversion: If peer data were fully available and showed the stock was average on every dimension, would the investment case survive?
