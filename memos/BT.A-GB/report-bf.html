Section 1 – Business Model + Organisation 28 findings

1.
BT Group operates as the United Kingdom's incumbent telecommunications provider, managing a vertically integrated but highly regulated model that pairs a monopoly-legacy wholesale infrastructure network with a highly competitive retail services footprint. Net signal – mixed transition: BT balances immense infrastructure scale against retail saturation, enduring high structural cash requirements today for long-term operating leverage tomorrow
2.
Description – core business: The group designs, builds, and operates national fixed and mobile networks, selling wholesale access and retail connectivity solutions
3.
Wholesale physical infrastructure is managed exclusively by Openreach, providing regulated access to over 700 alternative communications providers. Retail services are distributed through three distinct units targeting consumers, domestic enterprises, and global multinational corporations. Organisational structure – recent carve-out: The group restructured its commercial footprint in July 2025, splitting the former enterprise division into distinct UK and International units
4.
The UK Business unit solely targets domestic corporate, public sector, and wholesale clients to sharpen local execution. The standalone International unit manages multinational corporations, absorbing 8,000 personnel to focus entirely on borderless connectivity. - Earnings Calls Revenue mix – Consumer dominant: The Consumer division drives the largest share of group revenue, generating £9.5bn or roughly 48% of gross revenue in FY26
5.
Openreach contributes £6.2bn, Business adds £5.3bn, and the International segment provides £2.1bn, prior to intra-group eliminations. Geography – UK concentrated: Following a sustained strategic withdrawal from global markets, BT's revenue is heavily concentrated domestically, generating £15.4bn in UK service revenues
6.
Management deliberately scaled back international operations to mitigate the structural drag of low- margin legacy IT contracts. Five targeted non-core overseas divestments, including BT Radianz and operations in Italy and Ireland, completed by early 2026. Revenue model – subscription recurring: Consumer revenues rely on monthly subscriptions across 13.4m UK households, capturing 8.2m broadband and 15.6m mobile connections
7.
This model generates an average broadband ARPU of £41.70, which declined 1% year-over-year in FY26 amid intense competitive discounting. Revenue model – B2B contractual: The Business and International units rely on multi-year managed solution contracts, ranging typically from one to five-plus years
8.
Growth in cybersecurity and cloud connectivity is required to offset a structural £150-200m annual headwind from legacy voice product attrition. Revenue model – wholesale concession: Openreach generates predictable revenue by charging regulated rental and connection fees to third-party ISPs for network access

Section 3 – Industry Structure + Concentration + Value Chain Economics 24 findings

1.
Industry structure – fragmented transition: The UK telecom market remains fiercely competitive and highly fragmented, though nascent consolidation and easing regulations offer pathways to market repair
2.
Growth trajectory – below GDP: European telecommunications revenue growth has historically lagged broader nominal GDP growth by approximately five percentage points annually over the past 15 years
3.
Recent growth – price-driven: Despite historic lags, recent broad-based price increases have elevated European mobile service revenue growth to approximately 2% year-over-year
4.
Structural driver – digitalisation: Accelerating data consumption, driven by AI adoption and fixed-to-mobile substitution, continues to drive underlying demand with data traffic growing 30% annually
5.
Fragmentation – extreme levels: Europe contains approximately 95 to 100 mobile network operators serving 515 million people, averaging just 5 million POPs per operator
6.
Peer comparison – fragmentation: This European fragmentation starkly contrasts with the US and China, which average 107 million and 467 million POPs per operator respectively
7.
Consolidation – accelerating pace: In-market consolidation is accelerating across Europe to repair margins, evidenced by the Vodafone/Three merger in the UK and MasOrange in Spain
8.
Regulatory shift – easing oversight: The European Commission is revising Horizontal Merger Guidelines to better support scale, network resilience, and the emergence of European industry champions
9.
Regulatory shift – investment focus: The impending Digital Networks Act aims to reduce market fragmentation and incentivize heavy infrastructure investments required for 5G and full-fibre rollouts
10.
Value chain economics – infrastructure premium: Profitability is increasingly concentrating at the infrastructure layer, prompting European operators to separate and monetize passive assets through NetCo and FiberCo structures
11.
Margin pool – upstream concentration: Scale-advantaged infrastructure owners command stronger margins than retail service providers, who suffer from high churn and intense price competition
12.
Pricing leverage – wholesale strength: Infrastructure monopolies historically commanded strong pricing leverage, though this is currently being tested by the rapid deployment of alternative fibre networks
13.
Competitor attitudes – aggressive discounting: Challenger brands and alt-nets deploy highly aggressive pricing strategies, such as 1Gbps symmetric broadband for £23, to rapidly capture market share
14.
Competitor attitudes – MVNO disruption: The UK market faces continued disruption from new MVNO entrants, such as Monzo Mobile, which utilize incumbent networks to offer heavily discounted tariffs
15.
New entrant share – alt-net threat: UK alt-nets are gaining critical mass, with operators like Community Fibre achieving 33% penetration within their specific network footprints
16.
Industry rank – BT dominant: BT maintains its position as the UK's dominant fixed-line incumbent, though it faces an eroding market share as alternative infrastructure providers expand
17.
Distribution curve – steep structure: The UK broadband market exhibits a steep distribution curve, heavily dominated by BT, Virgin Media O2, and Sky, with a long tail of sub-scale alt-nets
18.
Share stability – structural bleed: BT Openreach is experiencing a structural bleed of wholesale lines, losing 825k broadband connections in FY26 to rival infrastructure providers
19.
Shark attack risk – severe: Virgin Media O2's strategy to wholesale its combined fixed network presents a direct shark-attack threat to Openreach's historical wholesale monopoly
20.
MOL stability – alt-net consolidation: High interest rates are pressuring the viability of sub-scale UK alt-nets, potentially triggering a wave of defensive consolidation to achieve sustainable scale
21.
Macro shift – unequal burden: High UK inflation and business rates place a disproportionate financial burden on asset-heavy incumbents like BT compared to their European peers
22.
Competitiveness trend – convergence premium: Operators offering converged fixed-mobile bundles experience significantly lower churn rates, driving a market-wide strategic shift toward multi-play service offerings
23.
Value chain stage – retail commoditization: The retail layer of the European telecommunications market is heavily commoditized, characterized by deflationary pricing pressures and low brand loyalty
24.
Other side of the story: – broken economics: What is the bearish counter-read on industry structure? Through what mechanism might alt-net consolidation create a scaled wholesale rival that permanently breaks Openreach's pricing power and volume assumptions?
