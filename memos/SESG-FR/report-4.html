§7 — Sector & Value Chain Context

Net signal: SES's outperformance relative to GEO peers is partly company-specific (MEO constellation quality) and partly sector-driven (government demand tailwind); the GEO attrition headwind is entirely sector-wide; disaggregating the two is essential for thesis durability assessment [#J]

  • Sector revenue: Sector-wide GEO satellite revenue has been declining at 5-8% per annum for 3+ consecutive years; this is structural, not cyclical, driven by fibre/terrestrial displacement of video distribution and Starlink commercial broadband competition [#A]
  • The decline is consistent across Eutelsat, SES, and the SIA (Satellite Industry Association) annual report data for the global GEO market

  • No forecaster is modelling a return to GEO revenue growth — the debate is pace of decline, not direction

  • Sector margin: Sector EBITDA margins are being defended by cost-cutting rather than revenue growth; this is a "harvesting" dynamic for GEO, which is inherently margin-dilutive over time as fixed costs cannot be permanently reduced [#A]

  • Operators cutting ground infrastructure, headcount, and maintenance capex to defend near-term margins at the cost of long-term operational flexibility

  • Supplier signals: Launch cost environment is the most relevant supplier signal; SpaceX Falcon 9 has structurally reduced launch costs by ~50-70% vs historical Arianespace benchmarks — this benefits SES's future replenishment satellite economics [#A]

  • Arianespace's competitive position has weakened; SES has used SpaceX for recent launches

  • Satellite bus/spacecraft manufacturer lead times remain extended (2-3 year backlogs at prime contractors like Airbus Defence & Space, Thales Alenia)

  • Customer signals: Government/defence customers (US DoD, NATO, EU sovereign users) are increasing satellite connectivity budgets; MEO demand from government is confirmed by multiple SES contract announcements in 2023-24 [#A]

  • Commercial maritime (shipping, energy) also a growth end-market; offshore energy connectivity demand growing with energy transition (offshore wind, LNG monitoring)

  • Aviation IFC market is being disrupted by Starlink airline partnerships — a genuine customer signal negative for SES's legacy aviation GEO contracts

  • Distributor signals: VSAT distributors (resellers of satellite capacity) are shifting purchasing toward MEO/LEO capable systems; traditional GEO resellers are shrinking their GEO capacity commitments [#A]

  • This channel shift is a leading indicator of further GEO revenue attrition — SES's own reseller network is under transition pressure

  • End-market growth: Government connectivity, offshore energy, maritime, and defence are growing end-markets; video broadcasting, consumer broadband via GEO, and aviation GEO are all contracting [#A]

  • SES's revenue mix is shifting toward the growing end-markets — the mix transition is the bull case structural driver

  • Competitive intensity: Competitive intensity is intensifying in every segment — Starlink in commercial broadband, OneWeb in mobility/maritime, Amazon Kuiper entering in 2025-26; MEO's moat is narrowing as LEO constellations mature [#J]

  • SES's MEO technical differentiation (power, coverage, latency for government use cases) is real but not permanent

  • The 18-24 month window before Kuiper scales is strategically significant for SES to lock in government contracts

  • Cyclical vs structural: GEO attrition is structural, not cyclical — there is no macro recovery scenario that reverses terrestrial fibre/Starlink displacement of GEO video and broadband; MEO growth demand is also structural (defence budget trends, offshore energy) but early-stage [#J]

  • The structural tailwind for MEO is less certain in magnitude than the structural headwind for GEO — this asymmetry matters for thesis confidence

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish cross-cutting read is that SES's outperformance is more sector-tailwind-dependent than the thesis implies; if government defence spending is cut (US budget sequestration risk, NATO spending re-prioritisation), the fastest-growing end-market disappears; mechanism — US DoD budget pressure in 2025-26 leading to satellite capacity contract delays or cancellations, directly hitting mPOWER activation timeline [#J]

§8 — Quality of Earnings & Accounting

  • NWC days: Satellite operators are structurally low-NWC businesses (government/enterprise customers on advance payment terms); SES's NWC position is modestly positive (customer prepayments exceed trade payables) — not a quality concern relative to peer norms [#A]
  • Eutelsat similar NWC profile; Viasat more NWC-intensive due to consumer broadband equipment inventory

  • NWC days vs peer median: SES approximately neutral; peer median slightly positive; no material divergence

  • Cash conversion: OCF/adjusted EBITDA conversion approximately 75-80% in FY2024, below the long-run satellite operator norm of ~85-90%; the shortfall reflects higher cash interest expense and timing of advance customer payments [#A]

  • Eutelsat cash conversion similarly below historical norms — sector-wide feature during high-leverage period

  • FY2025-26 cash conversion expected to improve as mPOWER capital spending peaks and interest coverage stabilises

  • OCF vs EPS: Reported EPS is negative (impairments); OCF is positive (~€800-850m in FY2024) — the OCF/EPS divergence is explained by non-cash impairment charges, not genuine earnings manipulation [#A]

  • This is the "honest" version of the non-GAAP gap — impairments are real economic losses but non-cash in year of recognition

  • One-offs: SES's L2Y one-off items include: SES-17 satellite impairment (~€500m+), insurance recoveries (partial offset, ~€100-150m), restructuring charges (~€30-50m per year), and C-band clearing gain recognition (tail payments) [#I]
