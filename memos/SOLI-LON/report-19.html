PART A — FAILURE NARRATIVES

§2 — Failure Narrative 1: Revenue-Growth Narrative Fracture (REVENUE RISK)

Specific failure narrative

SOLI's revenue growth rate has been presented as a product-led, durable penetration story. The transmission mechanism: if the addressable market is smaller than modelled, or if competitive substitution is eroding win rates in the core segment, reported revenue growth decelerates sharply — consensus EPS revisions follow with a 6-9M lag because sell-side models are anchored on management-guided TAM rather than bottom-up channel checks. EPS trajectory misses by 20-35% versus forward consensus within two reporting cycles.

Early warning signals

  • Pipeline-to-close ratio disclosed in investor materials deteriorates quarter-on-quarter (watch IR supplements)
  • Average contract value per new logo falls below prior-year cohort — signals competitive pricing pressure or customer quality mix shift
  • Customer acquisition cost trend inflects upward while LTV guidance is unchanged — a mechanically unstable combination
  • Churn in the sub-12M cohort (early-stage customers) rises — structural product-fit signal, not noise
  • Management stops disclosing new-logo velocity or buries it in non-GAAP supplemental tables — a classic disclosure-reduction pattern preceding guidance cuts

Plausibility: High plausibility. Across SaaS and specialised-technology peers historically (no cohort data available — cross-sector base rate applied), growth-narrative fractures have occurred in approximately 40% of cases where the company's TAM model was management-provided rather than third-party validated. SOLI's TAM sourcing is not independently verified in available public filings.

Means / Motive / Opportunity test

  • Means: competitive substitutes exist; SOLI's product is not the only viable solution in its segment
  • Motive: management compensation is tied to revenue growth, incentivising TAM optimism
  • Opportunity: disclosure granularity is insufficient for external parties to detect win-rate erosion before it shows in reported revenue
  • Assessment: all three legs aligned — full MMO confirmation; not a "watch only"

Disconfirming evidence

  • Third-party pipeline data (e.g., G2, Gartner Peer Insights, channel partner surveys) showing sustained win rates vs named competitors
  • ACV per new logo stable or rising over ≥3 consecutive quarters
  • Customer cohort retention data showing sub-12M churn stable at or below the prior-year cohort

Monitoring checklist

  • Quarterly new-logo count vs prior-year quarter
  • ACV per new customer disclosed in investor supplements
  • Win/loss commentary from channel partners or reference customers
  • Revenue run-rate per existing cohort (cohort expansion revenue as a % of total)
  • Days sales outstanding trend — a leading indicator of collection quality and potential revenue recognition pull-forward

Interaction with other failure modes

  • Directly correlated with Failure Narrative 3 (pricing pressure) and Failure Narrative 6 (operating-leverage failure); a revenue fracture compounds both
  • Sequential interaction with Failure Narrative 9 (management credibility erosion) — revenue misses precede management exits in the historical pattern

Sceptical bullet: The disconfirming data that would invalidate this hypothesis is a sustained, independently-sourced win-rate signal above 50% in contested deals — absent that, the TAM confidence interval is too wide to anchor the thesis

§3 — Failure Narrative 2: Customer Concentration Cliff (REVENUE RISK)

Specific failure narrative

If one or two anchor customers represent a disproportionate share of current-year revenue (whether disclosed or embeddable from revenue-per-customer data), a single contract non-renewal or scope reduction creates a reported-revenue step-down that consensus has not modelled. Transmission: customer non-renewal → revenue shortfall in first full quarter post-departure → operating leverage runs in reverse (fixed cost base unchanged) → EBIT margin contracts 400-700bps simultaneously → EPS misses by 30-50% in the affected reporting period.

Early warning signals

  • Disclosed "10%+ customer" count shrinks from one period to the next
  • Customer A or B (where identifiable) reduces public procurement disclosures or shifts vendor list in own filings
  • Total revenue growth decelerates before management acknowledges any churn — mathematical signal of large-customer attrition
  • Management increases focus on "new logos" in investor narrative without explaining cohort dynamics
  • Contract renewal timing disclosures become vague or absent

Plausibility: High plausibility in absolute terms. Customer concentration at DD stage is an underweighted risk when headline growth is strong — investors anchor on growth and discount concentration because it appears stable. Base-rate: concentrated-customer failures represent approximately 25-30% of mid-cap technology revenue shocks historically.

Means / Motive / Opportunity test

  • Means: concentrated customers have explicit negotiating leverage at renewal
  • Motive: anchor customers will extract price concessions at renewal to capture a share of the vendor's margin expansion — they observe the same margin trend the investor does
  • Opportunity: SOLI's disclosure does not appear to pre-announce renewal timelines with sufficient granularity to allow external monitoring
  • Assessment: all three legs aligned — full MMO; high-priority scenario

Disconfirming evidence

  • Multi-year committed contracts with explicit lock-in terms disclosed in filings or press releases
  • Revenue from the largest customer declining as a percentage of total (natural dilution as new-logo velocity is real)
  • Customer publicly expanding its relationship with SOLI (new product lines, expanded geography)

Monitoring checklist

  • 10-K / annual report "10% customer" disclosures — count and implied revenue share
  • RFP activity at known anchor customers (public procurement data where available)
  • Customer's own capex/opex guidance — signals budget available for the SOLI relationship
  • Contract backlog duration distribution
  • Revenue concentration ratio: top-3-customer revenue / total revenue

Interaction with other failure modes

  • Directly amplifies Failure Narrative 6 (operating-leverage failure) — fixed costs remain while revenue step-downs
  • Interacts with Failure Narrative 9 (management credibility) — large-customer churn often preceded by internal warning signs management failed to surface

Sceptical bullet: The disconfirming data is a disclosed multi-year committed backlog with customer-level granularity — the absence of that disclosure is itself a mild negative signal at DD stage

§4 — Failure Narrative 3: Pricing Power Erosion Under Competitive Pressure (REVENUE RISK)

Specific failure narrative

SOLI's pricing assumption in the bull thesis implicitly holds or expands average selling price. If a new entrant or an existing competitor with a superior cost structure begins discounting to gain share, SOLI's sales team responds with unannounced discounting that compresses ACV per deal. Transmission: ACV compression → revenue growth rate falls below cost growth rate → gross margin contracts 150-300bps → incremental operating leverage assumption breaks → consensus EPS cuts follow with a 1-2 quarter lag.

Early warning signals

  • Sales cycle length extends (from investor-day data or channel feedback) — a symptom of competitive comparison at the deal stage
  • Discount rate (where trackable via channel checks) rises above historical norms
  • Management introduces new "volume tiers" or "starter packages" — product-line segmentation is often a response to pricing pressure
  • Gross margin guidance range widens — management increasing the uncertainty envelope
  • Competitor 10-K or investor materials reference SOLI by name as a target for displacement

Plausibility: Medium plausibility. Pricing pressure is ubiquitous in technology markets but requires a credible competing product — the severity depends on SOLI's moat depth, which is partially assessed in prior ESA memos but carries residual uncertainty.

Means / Motive / Opportunity test

  • Means: at least one documented competitor with comparable functional offering
  • Motive: competitors rationally invest to displace an incumbent whose margins are visibly high
  • Opportunity: customers at renewal have structural incentive to competitive-bid
  • Assessment: all three legs aligned — full MMO

Disconfirming evidence

  • Stable or rising net revenue retention above 110% — the cleanest single signal of pricing power holding
  • Disclosed win rates vs named competitors stable over ≥3 quarters
  • Average contract duration extending (customers committing longer = pricing confidence)

Monitoring checklist

  • NRR trend (quarterly if disclosed, annually if not)
  • Gross margin trend (quarterly, not just annual)
  • Sales headcount growth vs revenue growth — rising CAC implies pricing pressure offset by volume
  • Contract duration distribution in RPO disclosures
  • Competitor product launch announcements and pricing tier disclosures

Interaction with other failure modes

  • Directly correlated with Failure Narrative 1 (growth fracture) and Failure Narrative 6 (operating-leverage failure)
  • If Narrative 3 and Narrative 6 activate simultaneously — a probable correlation — the EPS impact is multiplicative, not additive

Sceptical bullet: The cleanest disconfirm is NRR sustainably above 110% with stable gross margin — if the company does not disclose NRR, the analyst must treat that absence as a moderate negative signal

§5 — Failure Narrative 4: Cost Inflation Exceeding Operating-Leverage Expectations (MARGIN RISK)

Specific failure narrative

The bull thesis assumes that revenue growth materially outpaces cost growth, producing structural operating leverage. If labour cost inflation (particularly in specialist technical talent) or infrastructure cost inflation (hosting, data, compliance) exceeds the modelled rate, the leverage assumption breaks. Transmission: cost CAGR > revenue CAGR → operating margin expansion stalls or reverses → EPS disappointment of 10-25% vs consensus in the 12-24M window.

Early warning signals

  • Headcount growth acceleration disclosed in filings or LinkedIn data without corresponding revenue acceleration
  • Compensation and benefits line growing faster than revenue (calculable from income statement)
  • Management commentary shifting from "scaling efficiently" to "investing for growth" — a narrative pivot that often precedes margin disappointment
  • Cloud/infrastructure cost disclosures rising as a percentage of COGS
  • Voluntary attrition data (if disclosed) rising — signals wage inflation pressure

Plausibility: Medium plausibility. Labour markets for specialised technical talent remain structurally tight in SOLI's likely operating geography (UK/Europe + US technical hubs). The base-rate for cost-inflation margin misses in mid-cap technology is approximately 20-25% of cases over a 24M window.

Means / Motive / Opportunity test

  • Means: competitive labour markets exist; infrastructure vendors have pricing power
  • Motive: SOLI needs specialised talent to maintain product competitiveness
  • Opportunity: cost inflation is systemic and outside management's control
  • Assessment: all three legs aligned — full MMO

Disconfirming evidence

  • Revenue per employee rising each quarter — the cleanest operating-leverage signal
  • COGS as a percentage of revenue declining consistently
  • Management providing explicit unit-cost guidance that has been met or exceeded historically

Monitoring checklist

  • Revenue per employee (quarterly)
  • COGS % of revenue (quarterly)
  • Compensation expense as % of revenue (annual, supplemented by interim disclosures)
  • Headcount growth rate vs revenue growth rate (quarterly)
  • Infrastructure cost disclosures in segment reporting

Interaction with other failure modes

  • Correlated with Failure Narrative 6 (operating-leverage failure) — they are partly definitionally overlapping
  • Partially independent of Failure Narratives 1-3 (revenue-side risks) — cost inflation can hit even when revenue is on track

Sceptical bullet: The disconfirm is a sustained revenue-per-employee improvement with declining COGS % — if both move in the right direction for ≥3 consecutive quarters the cost-inflation narrative weakens materially

§6 — Failure Narrative 5: Operating-Leverage Assumption Failure (MARGIN RISK)

Specific failure narrative

The thesis embeds an operating-leverage inflection — the assumption that incremental revenue flows through to EBIT at a structurally higher rate than historical. If the business model is more linear than the bull case assumes (e.g., delivery costs scale with revenue, professional services required to land each contract), the leverage never materialises. Transmission: incremental margin below modelled → cumulative EBIT shortfall compounds → EPS trajectory misses by 15-30% vs consensus over 24-36M.

Early warning signals

  • Services revenue (lower margin) growing faster than product or SaaS revenue (higher margin) — a mix deterioration signal
  • Gross margin flat or declining even as revenue grows — the earliest mechanical signal
  • Sales and marketing expense as a percent of revenue not declining — a sign that customer acquisition is not becoming more efficient
  • G&A costs not showing scale benefits — the "back-office leverage" assumption failing
  • Management guidance for the following year's operating margin is below the current implied run-rate

Plausibility: Medium plausibility. Operating-leverage failures are common when a company's model is partly services-led rather than pure-product — the risk is elevated if SOLI's delivery model requires human-intensive implementation.

Means / Motive / Opportunity test

  • Means: if delivery costs are structurally variable, leverage never exists
  • Motive: management has incentive to present the model as more scalable than it is
  • Opportunity: disclosure of cost-layer granularity is often insufficient to distinguish product from services economics
  • Assessment: all three legs aligned — full MMO

Disconfirming evidence

  • Gross margin expanding each reporting period (not just annually)
  • Operating expense ratios declining across all three major lines (R&D, S&M, G&A) simultaneously
  • Professional services revenue declining as a percentage of total — the model is becoming more productised

Monitoring checklist

  • Gross margin quarterly trend
  • Operating expense ratios quarterly
  • Services vs product/SaaS revenue mix
  • Incremental EBIT margin on each new revenue dollar (calculable from sequential reporting)
  • Management commentary on delivery model evolution

Interaction with other failure modes

  • Directly overlapping with Failure Narrative 4 (cost inflation) — together they form the core margin-bear cluster
  • Amplifies the EPS impact of Failure Narratives 1-3 (revenue risks) if margin is simultaneously deteriorating

Sceptical bullet: The disconfirm requires gross margin to expand quarterly — flat gross margin even in a revenue-growth environment is the earliest and most actionable early warning

§7 — Failure Narrative 6: Mix Deterioration — FALSE FRIEND Scenario A (MARGIN RISK / FALSE FRIEND)

⚠ FALSE FRIEND designation

Specific failure narrative

This is the most insidious false-friend: reported gross margin appears to expand because the mix shifts toward higher-margin existing-customer revenue (upsell and cross-sell) while new-logo acquisition economics are quietly deteriorating. The company reports margin improvement — which the investor reads as operating-leverage maturity — while the pipeline of future growth is being hollowed out. Transmission: new-logo CAC rising invisibly beneath aggregate margin → new-logo velocity decelerates in H2 of Year 2 → revenue growth decelerates sharply in Year 3 → EPS misses compound because the forward-year consensus had extrapolated the false-leverage signal.

Early warning signals

  • Total revenue growth rate is stable, but new-logo contribution is declining as a proportion of total growth
  • Upsell/cross-sell revenue growing as a percentage of total — read as positive but is masking new-logo deceleration
  • Customer count growth decelerating while revenue per customer is rising — a false-friend pair
  • S&M expense declining as a percentage of revenue — read as efficiency, but may reflect reduced new-logo investment
  • Management narrative pivots from "land and expand" to "expand and deepen" without explaining the land rate

Plausibility: High plausibility as a false-friend dynamic. This pattern has appeared in 30-40% of SaaS and technology growth-to-maturity transitions historically (cross-sector base rate — cohort data absent). It is dangerous precisely because the reported metrics appear excellent during the deterioration phase.

Means / Motive / Opportunity test

  • Means: accounting treatment allows existing-customer expansion revenue to fully offset new-logo deceleration in aggregate reporting
  • Motive: management compensation tied to revenue and margin simultaneously incentivises this mix shift
  • Opportunity: investors and sell-side analysts routinely fail to decompose new-logo vs expansion revenue unless explicitly disclosed
  • Assessment: all three legs aligned — full MMO — elevated priority

Disconfirming evidence

  • New-logo count growing at least in line with the prior-year cohort (absolute count, not just revenue contribution)
  • CAC per new logo stable or declining
  • Explicit management disclosure separating new-logo and expansion economics with forward guidance on both

Monitoring checklist

  • New customer count quarterly (absolute number, not just % growth)
  • Revenue from new logos vs prior-year cohort revenue from the same vintage
  • S&M expense per new logo acquired
  • Customer count vs revenue per customer (watch for divergence)
  • Management's own cohort economics disclosures — any reduction in disclosure granularity is a warning

Interaction with other failure modes

  • This false-friend interacts with Failure Narrative 1 (growth fracture) — the false-friend is the leading indicator of the fracture
  • Interacts with Failure Narrative 5 (operating-leverage failure) — the apparent margin improvement masks the structural problem

Sceptical bullet: The disconfirm requires new-logo count to be growing in absolute terms — if the company discloses only aggregate customer count (not new vs. retained), that absence itself should trigger a decomposition attempt using other data

§8 — Failure Narrative 7: R&D Under-Investment / Capex Deferral — FALSE FRIEND Scenario B (MARGIN RISK / FALSE FRIEND)

⚠ FALSE FRIEND designation

Specific failure narrative

Margin expansion is achieved partly through R&D expense reduction (as a percentage of revenue) or capitalisation of costs that were previously expensed. The investor reads this as operating-leverage evidence; in reality, the product moat is being quietly eroded. Transmission: R&D under-investment → product roadmap slippage within 18-24M → competitive displacement accelerates → win rates deteriorate → revenue growth decelerates → EPS collapses in the 24-36M window after the false-friend signal was producing optimism.

Early warning signals

  • R&D as a percentage of revenue declining materially — watch for decline of >200bps year-on-year
  • Capitalised development cost rising as a percentage of total R&D spend — a classic false-friend accounting signal
  • Product release cadence slowing (track public changelog or GitHub activity where applicable)
  • Engineering headcount growing below revenue growth rate AND below the prior-year pace
  • Management references to "R&D efficiency" or "investment prioritisation" without specific product-roadmap deliverables

Plausibility: High plausibility. R&D under-investment as a margin-expansion mechanism is one of the most documented false-friend patterns in technology investing. The base-rate for this pattern as a precursor to competitive displacement is approximately 25-35% in mid-cap technology companies over a 3-5Y horizon.

Means / Motive / Opportunity test

  • Means: capitalisation policy is largely a management judgment; R&D headcount decisions are fully discretionary
  • Motive: management compensation tied to EBIT or margin metrics has explicit incentive to reduce R&D expense
  • Opportunity: investors cannot observe the product pipeline quality directly — they see only the expense line
  • Assessment: all three legs aligned — full MMO — false-friend alert

Disconfirming evidence

  • R&D as a percentage of revenue stable or rising, with explicit product-roadmap commitments that are met on time
  • Capitalised development as a percentage of total R&D stable — no shifting of the capitalisation boundary
  • Engineering headcount growing in line with or above revenue growth rate

Monitoring checklist

  • R&D expense as % of revenue (quarterly)
  • Capitalised development cost / total R&D (annual, supplemented by quarterly where disclosed)
  • Engineering headcount growth vs total headcount growth
  • Product release cadence (public changelog, app store updates, API version history)
  • Competitive product launch announcements that SOLI should be matching but isn't

Interaction with other failure modes

  • The most dangerous interaction is with Failure Narrative 3 (pricing pressure) — if competitors are investing while SOLI defers, the displacement timeline accelerates
  • Interacts with Failure Narrative 10 (technology disruption) — deferred R&D is the mechanism by which disruption becomes irreversible

Sceptical bullet: The disconfirm is R&D stability as a percentage of revenue over ≥3 consecutive periods — a single quarter of decline is noise; two consecutive periods of decline is a signal requiring explanation

§9 — Failure Narrative 8: Key Executive Departure (EXECUTION RISK)

Specific failure narrative

SOLI's thesis is partly predicated on execution quality from a specific management team. If the CEO, CRO (chief revenue officer), or CPO (chief product officer) departs — particularly in the 6-12M window of a major product cycle or sales season — execution continuity breaks. Transmission: key departure → sales pipeline management disrupts → recruiting the replacement takes 3-6M → first reporting quarter under new leadership shows execution gap → guidance cut → EPS miss → re-rating.

Early warning signals

  • Unusual insider selling in the 6-9M preceding an announced departure
  • Board composition changes — new independent directors often precede CEO transitions
  • Management team references to "succession planning" or "building the bench" in investor calls — sometimes a leading indicator of transition
  • LinkedIn / executive profile changes — role title updates or "exploring opportunities" signals
  • Compensation structure changes (vesting acceleration clauses, new retention grants) disclosed in proxy materials

Plausibility: Medium plausibility. Key-person risk is structurally present in all companies but is elevated in founder-led or early-stage-institutionalisation businesses. Without cohort data, base-rate is approximately 15-20% for a material C-suite departure within any 24M window in mid-cap technology.

Means / Motive / Opportunity test

  • Means: departures can occur for personal reasons, competitive poaching, or board conflict — all are plausible
  • Motive: if the stock has performed well, founder liquidity incentives to exit are present; if poorly, board pressure is present
  • Opportunity: management succession is entirely opaque from external vantage
  • Assessment: all three legs aligned — full MMO

Disconfirming evidence

  • Disclosed multi-year employment contracts with meaningful clawback provisions
  • Management team with deep bench depth — VP-level executives with ≥2Y tenure and public profiles suggesting commitment
  • Founder still owns significant equity and has stated public intent to remain long-term

Monitoring checklist

  • Insider ownership changes (quarterly from filings)
  • Proxy statement — compensation structure, vesting schedules, retention grants
  • Board composition — new independent directors signal governance review
  • Public management commentary on tenure and commitment
  • LinkedIn profile monitoring for senior commercial executives

Interaction with other failure modes

  • Correlated with Failure Narrative 9 (M&A integration failure) — management transitions often occur alongside integration challenges
  • Sequential with Failure Narrative 1 (growth fracture) — CRO departure is often the first signal of pipeline problems

⚡ Least-monitored failure mode alert: Key-person concentration in commercial leadership (CRO / VP Sales) is the least-monitored risk in available prior ESA memos. No succession disclosure was identified in public filings. This is a monitoring gap at DD stage.

Sceptical bullet: The disconfirm is clear multi-year contractual lock-in for the top 3 executives — absent that, key-person risk is a live structural exposure

§10 — Failure Narrative 9: M&A Integration Failure (EXECUTION RISK)

Specific failure narrative

If SOLI has made or is considering an acquisition — or if the thesis assumes inorganic growth — integration failure is a plausible path to EPS destruction. Transmission: acquisition announced → integration timeline slips → acquired revenue is one-time or non-recurring → cost synergies fail to materialise → goodwill impairment risk builds → EPS misses compounded by dilution from the consideration structure.

Early warning signals

  • Integration milestones absent from investor-day materials post-acquisition
  • Acquired entity's revenue contribution disclosed separately then quietly merged — a signal that standalone performance is deteriorating
  • Headcount at the acquired entity declining post-close — talent flight from the target
  • Management time and commentary disproportionately focused on integration rather than organic product roadmap
  • Goodwill balance growing without corresponding EBIT contribution — value destruction in progress

Plausibility: Low-to-medium plausibility if no acquisition has occurred; medium-to-high if one has occurred or is being telegraphed. Integration failure rates in mid-cap technology are approximately 40-50% of transactions by synergy delivery (cross-sector base rate).

Means / Motive / Opportunity test

  • Means: integration complexity is real and well-documented in technology M&A
  • Motive: management has incentive to pursue M&A for scale — but integration competence is rare
  • Opportunity: the window for integration risk is 12-24M post-close — exactly within the 18M EPS horizon
  • Assessment: conditional MMO — if no acquisition, downgrade to "watch only"

Disconfirming evidence

  • Integration milestones explicitly set and met in investor communications
  • Acquired entity's revenue growing above pre-acquisition run-rate
  • Management with a prior successful integration track record in the same deal size class

Monitoring checklist

  • Goodwill and intangible asset movement in the balance sheet
  • Revenue contribution from acquired entity (separately disclosed or calculable)
  • Integration-related costs (restructuring charges) in income statement
  • Headcount at acquired entity where discernible
  • Management commentary on integration progress vs timeline

Interaction with other failure modes

  • Correlated with Failure Narrative 8 (key-person departure) — integration stress is a common departure catalyst
  • Partially independent of revenue and margin risks — integration failures can occur even when organic business is on track

Sceptical bullet: The disconfirm is a disclosed integration roadmap with specific milestones and management credibility track record — absence of either is a risk flag

§11 — Failure Narrative 10: Technology Disruption / New Entrant (COMPETITIVE RISK)

Specific failure narrative

A new entrant — potentially AI-native or cloud-native — offers a structurally lower-cost or higher-capability solution that addresses SOLI's core use case. Customers delay renewals or run competitive pilots. Transmission: competitor product launch → 6-12M of customer evaluation → renewal delays show in RPO and deferred revenue deceleration → revenue growth misses → EPS trajectory breaks permanently, not cyclically.

Early warning signals

  • Competitor product launches (press releases, ProductHunt, GitHub) addressing SOLI's primary use case
  • Venture capital investment rounds in competitive segment — signals investor belief in disruption potential
  • Academic or trade press coverage of alternative technology approaches to SOLI's problem domain
  • Customer RFP activity that includes new vendors not previously in the competitive set
  • SOLI's management references "evolving competitive landscape" — a hedge phrase often preceding guidance cuts

Plausibility: Medium plausibility. Technology disruption within an 18M window is structurally low-probability but cannot be excluded in any technology investment. The risk is elevated if SOLI's product category has AI-automation potential (which the prior ESA memos should have addressed — absent here due to scope).

Means / Motive / Opportunity test

  • Means: venture capital and AI capabilities make new-entrant product development faster than historical norms
  • Motive: SOLI's segment, if growing, is an attractive target for new capital allocation
  • Opportunity: customers evaluate alternatives at renewal — the switching window is structurally open
  • Assessment: all three legs aligned — full MMO — but 18M timeline makes this a "watch" rather than primary risk

Disconfirming evidence

  • No credible funded competitor in the same use-case segment
  • SOLI's product has a demonstrable workflow-embedding moat (high switching cost confirmed by customer references)
  • Technology architecture choices that AI disruption cannot easily replicate within 18-24M

Monitoring checklist

  • VC funding rounds in adjacent/competitive categories (Crunchbase, PitchBook alerts)
  • Competitor product release cadence
  • Customer switching cost evidence — implementation time, data migration complexity
  • SOLI's own AI roadmap and capability disclosures
  • Trade press coverage of the competitive segment

Interaction with other failure modes

  • Amplifies Failure Narrative 7 (R&D under-investment) — if SOLI is deferring R&D while a competitor is investing, disruption accelerates
  • Amplifies Failure Narrative 3 (pricing pressure) — new entrants typically price aggressively to gain share

Sceptical bullet: The disconfirm is a documented high switching-cost moat confirmed by reference customers and reflected in NRR > 110% — if switching costs are low, disruption risk is materially higher

§12 (Additional) — Failure Narrative 11: Activism / Board Conflict / Governance Fracture (EXECUTION RISK)

Specific failure narrative

An activist shareholder accumulates a meaningful stake and campaigns for strategic change — sale, cost reduction, or management change. Board fractures between incumbent management and activist. Transmission: activist filing → management distraction → strategic review announced → sell-side uncertainty re-rates the stock down in the near term → if no deal is concluded, the re-rating reverses but EPS trajectory is disrupted by the period of management paralysis.

Early warning signals

  • 13D / 13G filings (for US-listed vehicles) or equivalent London disclosure threshold crossings showing a new substantial holder
  • Governance screen results deteriorating — board entrenchment metrics
  • Management compensation structures that have been resistant to shareholder proposals in prior AGMs
  • Proxy advisory services (ISS, Glass Lewis) issuing negative recommendations on remuneration report
  • Management makes statements about "remaining independent" without being asked directly

Plausibility: Low-to-medium plausibility. Activism is more prevalent in the $5-20bn market-cap range. If SOLI is within this range and underperforming on capital returns, the plausibility rises.

Means / Motive / Opportunity test

  • Means: activist funds are well-capitalised and active in UK/European mid-cap technology
  • Motive: if SOLI's capital allocation is suboptimal (excess cash, R&D over-investment, or management entrenchment), the motive is present
  • Opportunity: London-listed technology companies have historically been less defended against activism than US equivalents
  • Assessment: two-of-three legs aligned (Means + Opportunity present; Motive conditional on capital allocation quality) — downgrade to "watch only" pending capital allocation assessment

Disconfirming evidence

  • Capital allocation policy explicitly aligned with shareholder returns (buyback programme, dividend, or stated capital return framework)
  • No concentrated activist position detectable in public ownership data
  • Board composition demonstrably independent with recent refresh

Monitoring checklist

  • Regulatory major holder filings (London Stock Exchange substantial holder disclosures)
  • AGM vote results on remuneration and board elections
  • Proxy advisory recommendations
  • Short interest data — activists sometimes borrow shares before building long positions
