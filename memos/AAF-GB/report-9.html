KEY FINDINGS - Trajectory verdict: Materially deteriorating — AAF-GB delivered strong revenue and EBITDA growth through FY22–FY23 but suffered a pronounced earnings-quality collapse in FY24 and FY25 H1, driven by FX headwinds, Nigerian naira devaluation, and rising financing costs [#J]

  • EPS trajectory: Negative CAGR — reported EPS declined from ~21.5¢ (FY22) to a loss in FY24 at the headline level; adjusted EPS held better but still fell ~40% peak-to-trough; L3Y adjusted EPS CAGR approximately -12% to -15% depending on the FX-adjusted basis used [#J]
  • Beat/miss pattern: Consistent operational beats on constant-currency EBITDA — management has beaten CC guidance repeatedly; however reported USD EPS has missed consensus materially in FY24 and FY25 H1 as FX translation effects overwhelmed operational delivery [#J]
  • Earnings quality: Mixed — CC EBITDA growth is real and cash-generative; however OCF/NI ratios are flattered by D&A step-up post-IFRS 16; reported net income is depressed by large non-cash FX losses on USD-denominated intercompany balances and hyperinflation accounting (IAS 29) [#J]
  • Management credibility: High on operational execution vs low on macro/FX prediction — management consistently delivered CC EBITDA guidance; their repeated characterisation of naira devaluation as "transitory" proved incorrect through three successive devaluation events [#A]
  • Seasonality: Modest but consistent — H2 (Oct–Mar) typically stronger than H1 across most metrics; no material shift in this pattern observed L3Y [#J]
  • Forward plausibility: Conditional — the bull case rests on naira stabilisation and USD EPS recovery; L3Y track record provides strong support for CC EBITDA delivery but zero precedent for navigating sustained EM FX headwinds at this scale [#J]
  • Single largest risk: Nigeria (≈35–38% of group revenue) is both the highest-growth and highest-risk market; a structurally weaker naira permanently impairs the USD EPS base from which any recovery is measured [#J]
  • Sceptical flag: Adjusted EPS strips recurring FX losses — if naira weakness is structural not transitory, "adjusted" earnings are a misleading anchor for forward valuation [#A]
  • Confidence in track record as forward predictor: MEDIUM (conditional on FX regime normalisation) [#J]

§1 Analytical Commentary

  • L3Y revenue CAGR: Negative at USD level — $4,711m FY22 to $3,955m FY24 implies approximately -8.5% two-year CAGR in reported USD; CC CAGR approximate +18–20% — the gap is almost entirely naira translation [#J]
  • L3Y EPS CAGR: Reported EPS swung from +21.5¢ (FY22) to -1.4¢ (FY24); adjusted EPS fell from 21.5¢ to 10.5¢ — approximately -27% two-year adjusted CAGR, significantly worse than consensus entering FY24 [#J]
  • Trajectory: Bifurcated — FY22–FY23 showed genuine acceleration (revenue +19.5%, then CC +20%+); FY24 was an abrupt deceleration in USD terms driven entirely by the June 2023 naira float decision [#J]
  • Sub: CC EBITDA growth remained positive (+20–22% CC in FY24) — operationally the business did not deteriorate
  • Sub: USD EBITDA fell 22% in FY24 — for a USD-reporting company, the CC story is real but the USD story is what moves the stock
  • Inflection point: June 2023 CBN naira float is the single most material inflection in the L3Y record — triggered a structural step-down in USD reported financials from which the company has not recovered as of FY25 H1 [#J]
  • Operating leverage: Gross margin broadly stable (~58–59% FY22–FY24 CC basis), suggesting pricing power preserved; EBITDA margin also broadly stable in CC terms (~47–48%) — cost leverage is real at the CC level [#A]
  • Sub: USD-reported EBITDA margin contracted slightly (47.0% FY22 → 47.3% FY24) only because FX hit revenue and EBITDA proportionally — more a translation artefact than structural margin pressure
  • FY23 peak: FY23 was the high-water mark — $5,023m revenue, $2,399m EBITDA, 24.3¢ EPS; all three metrics have retreated significantly in USD terms since [#J]
  • Acceleration pre-devaluation: FY22 H1 to FY23 H1 showed genuine acceleration (+28.2% revenue growth) as subscriber base expanded rapidly and mobile money monetised; this acceleration was cut short by FX [#A]
  • Sceptical bullet: The trajectory read could be flattered by the pre-devaluation FY23 base — if naira weakness persists, the "CC growth story" is permanently stranded in a lower USD envelope; CC CAGR becomes meaningless if USD conversion never recovers [#J]

§2 — Quality of Earnings

Quality verdict: Mixed — CC EBITDA is high-quality and cash-generative; reported net income is heavily distorted by non-cash FX losses, IAS 29 hyperinflation adjustments, and rising financing costs that are structurally real even if non-cash at the income statement line [#J]

  • One-off impact: FX losses are large and recurring — FY24 reported a $436m net FX loss vs $78m in FY23 and $31m in FY22; the company classifies these below EBITDA but they flow through to reported EPS [#J]
  • Sub: IAS 29 hyperinflation adjustments for Ethiopia (FY23+) and Nigeria (FY24+) add another layer of non-cash distortion to reported EBITDA
  • Sub: IAS 29 Nigeria adoption in FY24 inflated Nigerian EBITDA in local currency while reducing it in USD — creates a "moving target" for margin analysis
  • Adjustments trend: Adjustments between reported and adjusted EPS widening materially — FY22 gap was ~0; FY24 gap was ~12¢ (reported -1.4¢ vs adjusted +10.5¢); this trend is directionally concerning [#J]
  • Sub: The company adjusts for FX losses, exceptional items, and IAS 29 effects; all individually defensible but cumulatively the adjusted number diverges significantly from cash reality
  • Recurring vs one-off: FX losses are dressed as one-off but have occurred in three successive periods — FY22 ($31m), FY23 ($78m), FY24 ($436m), FY25 H1 continuing; pattern strongly suggests these are structural not exceptional [#A]
  • Non-GAAP gap: The spread between reported and adjusted EPS reached ~12¢ in FY24 vs near-zero in FY22; a widening non-GAAP gap is a standard red flag in earnings quality frameworks regardless of sector [#A]
  • NWC tailwind: Working capital management appears disciplined — AAF-GB is a prepaid-dominated business (subscribers pay upfront); structural negative NWC position means the business does not accumulate receivables risk [#J]
  • Sub: Receivables are primarily interconnect and wholesale balances — historically well-managed
  • Sub: FY24 showed slight NWC deterioration as government receivables in Nigeria built up (spectrum fees owed back to AAF)
  • Cash conversion: OCF/EBITDA broadly healthy at ~55–60% L3Y (after lease payments under IFRS 16 treatment); however OCF/net income is elevated and volatile due to net income distortion — not a useful signal in this case [#J]
  • Sub: Free cash flow (post-capex) has been positive in FY22 and FY23; FY24 free cash flow was under pressure from elevated capex in Nigeria and FX translation of capex costs
  • D&A trend: D&A rising in absolute terms — driven by network expansion capex in Nigeria and East Africa, plus IFRS 16 right-of-use asset depreciation; not aggressive accounting but a real cost of growth [#A]
  • Aggressive accounting: ❌ The IAS 29 Nigeria adoption in FY24 creates a one-period EBITDA inflation in local currency terms that partially reverses the USD FX impact at the EBITDA line — presentation is technically compliant but analytically confusing [#A]
  • Conservative accounting: No evidence of channel stuffing or revenue pull-forward — prepaid model makes this structurally difficult; subscriber KPIs corroborate revenue trends [#J]
  • OCF vs NI: OCF significantly exceeds reported NI in FY24 — confirming the net income distortion is non-cash (FX losses); however, financing cash flows are increasingly negative as USD debt service rises [#A]
  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read is that "adjusted" EPS strips recurring structural losses (FX, IAS 29) and presents a permanently flattering picture; if the naira never recovers to pre-2023 levels, the accumulated FX losses represent a permanent destruction of USD value that adjusted EPS papers over indefinitely [#J]

§3 — Revenue vs Profit Growth (Margin Sustainability)

Margin verdict: Structurally sound in CC terms — EBITDA margin stability at ~47–48% CC over L3Y reflects genuine scale advantages and pricing power; however USD-reported margin is subject to ongoing FX translation noise [#J]

  • Revenue CAGR: USD basis: ~-8.5% two-year CAGR FY22→FY24; CC basis: ~+18–20% — one of the most extreme CC/USD divergences visible in African telecom [#J]
  • Profit CAGR: EBITDA USD basis: ~-8.1% two-year CAGR FY22→FY24 ($2,216m → $1,870m); EBITDA CC basis: approximately +18–20% — closely tracking revenue CC growth [#J]
  • Sub: Operating leverage at the CC level is minimal (revenue CC CAGR ≈ EBITDA CC CAGR) — cost base is growing proportionally, suggesting scale benefits are being reinvested rather than flowing to margin
  • Operating leverage: Modest positive CC operating leverage exists but is being consumed by network expansion costs and rising power costs across African markets [#A]
  • Gross margin: Broadly stable ~58–59% L3Y in CC terms; slight compression in FY25 H1 reported (~56.5%) reflects USD cost translation asymmetry (some costs are USD-denominated, revenue is local currency) [#J]
  • EBITDA margin: Stable to mildly contracting — 48.0% FY22 → 47.8% FY23 → 47.3% FY24; within normal range for African telco; no structural compression signal [#J]
  • Sub: FY25 H1 at 46.9% represents slight further pressure — worth monitoring over next 2 periods
  • EBIT margin: Declining more than EBITDA margin — 27.8% FY22 H1 → 23.1% FY25 H1; driven by rising D&A on expanding asset base plus IAS 29 adjustments [#A]
  • Cost cut driver: No evidence of material cost-cut-driven margin expansion — margins are held by volume growth and pricing; this is healthier than cost-cut margin but means there is less runway if growth slows [#J]
  • Mix shift: Positive mix shift visible — mobile money (Airtel Money) growing faster than voice and contributing higher margins; data growing faster than voice; both trends support gradual margin accretion over time [#A]
  • Pricing driver: Price increases implemented post-naira devaluation in Nigeria — ~40% CC revenue growth in Nigeria in Q4 FY24/Q1 FY25 partly reflects tariff increases, not just volume; pricing power is real [#A]
  • Sustainability: Structural in CC terms; vulnerable in USD terms until FX stabilises — the ~47–48% EBITDA margin is achievable and defensible; the USD revenue base needed to support USD EBITDA recovery depends on naira/other currency trajectories [#J]
  • Margin runway: Limited additional margin expansion likely in CC terms given cost pressures (power, spectrum fees, inflation); mix shift toward mobile money offers 100–200bp potential over 3Y [#A]
  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on margin sustainability is that CC margin stability masks rising USD-denominated costs (USD debt service, USD equipment imports, USD spectrum fees in some markets) that will compress margins if local currencies don't recover; margin is structurally sound only if the FX regime normalises [#J]

§4 — Seasonal Patterns

Seasonality verdict: Modest and consistent — H2 (Oct–Mar) is typically the stronger period; pattern has been stable L3Y and reflects consumer/agricultural seasonality in sub-Saharan Africa [#J]

  • H1 pattern: H1 (Apr–Sep) is the seasonally weaker half — coincides with start of academic year (higher discretionary pressure on consumers), dry-season agricultural cycles in some markets [#J]
  • Sub: H1 FY23 represented 47.0% of FY23 full-year revenue; H1 FY22 was 39.2% — the latter unusually low due to post-COVID base effects in H1 FY22
  • H2 pattern: H2 (Oct–Mar) benefits from holiday season mobile data/money usage, agricultural harvest seasons (mobile money disbursements), and South Africa/East Africa peak economic activity [#J]
  • Sub: Pattern most pronounced in East Africa and Francophone regions; Nigeria is more even due to large prepaid base
  • Strongest quarter: Q3 (Oct–Dec) consistently the strongest quarter in operational KPI terms — mobile money volumes peak; corroborated across FY22, FY23, FY24 quarterly operational updates [#A]
  • Weakest quarter: Q1 (Apr–Jun) is consistently softest — consistent with the H1 pattern; post-tax-season consumer pressure in Nigeria compounds this [#J]
  • Seasonality direction: Slight moderation in seasonality amplitude observed over L3Y as mobile money creates more even monthly usage; voice seasonality dampening as data usage is less discretionary [#A]
  • Cause — mobile money: Mobile money (Airtel Money) has a strong Q3/Q4 seasonality tied to harvest disbursements and remittances; this contributes ~25–30% of group EBITDA and is growing [#A]
  • FX complication: Seasonal patterns in USD-reported figures are distorted by quarterly FX movements — particularly in FY24 where the June 2023 naira devaluation fell in Q1, creating an artificial H1/H2 split [#J]
  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read is that the H2 seasonal strength may partially reflect lumpy spectrum renewals, government contract payments, or one-off mobile money promotions rather than durable consumer behaviour; these could shift quarters without changing the annual total [#J]

§5 — Beat/Miss Pattern + Management Credibility

Credibility verdict: Consistently credible on CC operational delivery; management's USD forecasting track record is poor — they have repeatedly characterised naira devaluation as transitory, and it has not been [#J]

  • Beat/miss frequency: CC EBITDA beat in 5 of 6 reporting periods L3Y (H1 and FY for FY22, FY23, FY24 + FY25 H1 = 7 periods; 6 beats on CC EBITDA); USD revenue missed in 2 of the last 3 full-year periods [#J]
  • Sub: The one CC EBITDA miss (FY24 H1 on USD basis by 13.9%) was entirely attributable to the June 2023 naira devaluation, not operational deterioration
  • Beat magnitude: Average CC EBITDA beat vs consensus: approximately +2–3% in FY22 and FY23; the concept of "beat" became complicated post-FY24 H1 as consensus split into CC and USD estimates with wide dispersion [#A]
  • USD miss magnitude: FY24 H1 revenue missed consensus by -$317m (-13.8%); FY24 FY missed by -$245m (-5.8%); FY25 H1 missed by -$134m (-7.2%) — pattern of persistent USD misses vs pre-period consensus [#J]
  • Direction of surprise: Consistently positive on CC operational metrics (subscriber adds, ARPU growth, mobile money revenue); consistently negative on USD translation metrics (revenue, EBITDA, EPS in USD) [#J]
  • Sandbagging signal: Modest sandbagging evident on CC operational guidance — management has guided for "mid-teens to 20% CC revenue growth" and delivered 20%+ in each of the last 3 FYs at the CC level; slight conservative bias at the CC level [#A]
  • Overpromise signal: Management's language around naira recovery has been consistently too optimistic — "exceptional FX headwinds" characterisation used from FY23 H1 through FY25 H1 across five consecutive reporting periods without resolution [#J]
  • Honest tracker: Management is an honest tracker on CC operational delivery; on macro/FX they have a credibility gap that has persisted for 2+ years [#A]
  • Guidance accuracy: CC guidance accuracy: HIGH; USD guidance accuracy: LOW (FX forecasting effectively impossible given CBN policy opacity) [#J]
  • Trend in accuracy: No improvement in USD accuracy — FY25 H1 miss was smaller in absolute terms (-$134m vs -$317m in FY24 H1) but the "naira will stabilise" narrative remains unresolved [#A]
  • Sell-side revision pattern: Sell-side has progressively reset USD estimates downward — [AS-Citi] cut FY24 EPS estimates three times through the year; [AS-JPMorgan] noted "visibility on USD earnings recovery is very low" in post-FY24 note [#A]
  • Sub: [AS-Berenberg] maintained a more optimistic CC-anchored view, arguing reported USD EPS is not the right lens; [AS-Deutsche Bank] explicitly flagged the credibility gap on naira recovery narrative
  • Analyst dispersion: Unusually wide estimate dispersion on FY25 EPS — range of approximately 5¢ to 15¢ adjusted EPS for FY25E across the broker pool, reflecting genuine disagreement about naira trajectory [#A]
  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on management credibility is that the consistent CC beats are simply the result of setting easy CC targets while hiding behind FX; a management team that cannot forecast its own FX exposure for even one period ahead — despite having Nigeria as 35%+ of revenue since listing — may be demonstrating structural overconfidence in their FX hedging ability or macro read [#J]

§6 — Per-Period Narrative

Per-period verdict: The dominant narrative thread is strong operational execution overwhelmed by FX events; the key shifts are (1) FY22→FY23 genuine acceleration, (2) FY24 H1 naira devaluation inflection, (3) FY24 full year multiple-devaluation compounding, (4) FY25 H1 stabilisation-but-not-recovery [#J]

FY22 H1 (Apr–Sep 21) — Positive delivery, post-COVID recovery

  • Period: FY22 H1 — first post-COVID normalisation period; strong subscriber recovery across all three regions [#A]
  • Positive surprise: Mobile money subscriber adds +14% ahead of consensus — monetisation velocity surprised positively; Airtel Money EBITDA beat by ~10% [#A]
  • Confirmed expectation: Revenue growth of +17.9% landed slightly above guidance; EBITDA margin of 48.0% confirmed management's 47–49% guided range [#J]
  • Mgmt commentary shift: Tone notably more confident than FY21 — "multi-year growth runway" language introduced; mobile money IPO optionality signalled for the first time [#A]

FY22 full year (Mar 22) — Strong execution, full-year delivery

  • Period: FY22 FY — delivered $4,711m revenue (+19.5%), $2,216m EBITDA; first full-year post-COVID demonstration of the business model [#A]
  • Positive surprise: EPS of 21.5¢ exceeded 20.5¢ consensus by ~5% — driven by lower-than-expected net financing costs and tax efficiency [#A]
  • Confirmed expectation: EBITDA margin held above 47%; CC growth in all three regions; mobile money growth +30%+ CC [#J]
  • Strategic pivot: First formal articulation of "progressive dividend with earnings growth" policy; buyback programme initiated ($100m tranche) [#A]
  • Mgmt commentary shift: Confident, accelerating tone — "business performing at the top of expectations" [#A]

FY23 H1 (Apr–Sep 22) — Peak operational momentum

  • Period: FY23 H1 — best single period of the L3Y on USD metrics; $2,362m revenue (+28.2%), $1,127m EBITDA (+27.3%) [#J]
  • Positive surprise: Revenue beat consensus by +2.7%; mobile money penetration ahead of schedule; East Africa data revenue growth accelerated [#A]
  • Confirmed expectation: EBITDA margin 47.7% — in line with guidance; all three regions growing double-digit CC [#J]
  • One-off item: IAS 29 Ethiopia adoption introduced ~$8m charge — minor; management correctly flagged as non-recurring in Ethiopia-specific context [#A]
  • Mgmt commentary shift: Peak confidence tone — Olusegun Ogunsanya introduced accelerated mobile money timeline; mentioned potential Africa Money IPO preparation [#A]

FY23 full year (Mar 23) — Moderate deceleration in H2, but solid annual delivery

  • Period: FY23 FY — $5,023m revenue (+6.6% on FY22), highest-ever USD revenue; EPS 24.3¢ highest-ever [#J]
  • Negative surprise (relative): H2 FY23 disappointed vs H1 momentum — H2 revenue implied ~$2,661m vs ~$2,700m expected; macro headwinds in Nigeria (Tinubu election uncertainty) and Ethiopia [#A]
  • Sub: Full-year delivery was solid but H2 deceleration was first signal of Nigeria political/macro risk
  • Confirmed expectation: EBITDA margin held at 47.8%; mobile money EBITDA +28% CC for full year [#J]
  • Strategic pivot: Buyback programme completed; special dividend paid; management flagged Airtel Money IPO as "subject to market conditions" — subsequently deferred [#A]
  • Mgmt commentary shift: First mention of "macro headwinds" in Nigeria in closing remarks; tone less uniformly positive than prior periods [#A]

FY24 H1 (Apr–Sep 23) — Naira devaluation inflection point

  • Period: FY24 H1 — structural break; June 2023 CBN decision to float naira caused immediate ~60% devaluation [#J]
  • Negative surprise: Revenue missed consensus by -$317m (-13.8%); EPS reported 3.8¢ vs 11.5¢ expected — largest single-period miss in AAF-GB's listed history [#J]
  • ❌ Inflection point: Naira went from ~460/USD pre-June 2023 to ~770/USD by September 2023; Nigeria was ~38% of group revenue — translation impact was immediate and severe [#J]
  • IAS 29 Nigeria: Nigeria adopted as hyperinflationary economy for accounting purposes — added further P&L noise [#A]
  • Mgmt commentary shift: "Exceptional and unprecedented FX headwind" — first of what would become repeated characterisations over the next 5+ periods; management emphasised CC strength repeatedly [#A]
  • Sub: [AS-Deutsche Bank] noted "management's emphasis on CC metrics at the expense of USD discussion is a credibility concern" in post-results note

FY24 full year (Mar 24) — Compounding devaluation, reported EPS turns negative

  • Period: FY24 FY — $3,955m revenue (-21.3%); reported EPS -1.4¢; adjusted EPS 10.5¢ [#J]
  • Negative surprise: Second tranche of naira devaluation in Jan–Feb 2024 (naira to ~1,500/USD) drove further ~$700m revenue translation hit beyond what consensus had modelled [#J]
  • Confirmed expectation: CC growth remained strong — Nigeria CC revenue +38%, East Africa CC +14%, Francophone CC +18% — operational delivery in-line at CC level [#J]
  • One-off item: $436m net FX loss on USD-denominated intercompany balances — largest ever; drove headline P&L to reported loss [#J]
  • Strategic pivot: Airtel Money IPO formally deferred; special dividend suspended; buyback paused — capital allocation shifted to debt management [#A]
  • Sub: Shift in capital return narrative is a material negative signal relative to prior guidance
  • Mgmt commentary shift: Tone more defensive — "balance sheet resilience" language introduced; forward guidance shifted to CC metrics only [#A]
  • Sub: [AS-Berenberg] "we believe the CC story remains intact and the stock is pricing in permanent impairment" in post-FY24 note; [AS-Barclays] cut target price citing "USD earnings visibility zero for at least 12 months"

FY25 H1 (Apr–Sep 24) — Partial stabilisation, not recovery

  • Period: FY25 H1 — $1,716m revenue (-13.0% vs FY24 H1); adjusted EPS 6.8¢; reported EPS -2.1¢ [#J]
  • Negative surprise: Revenue missed consensus by -$134m (-7.2%); smaller miss than FY24 H1 but miss pattern continues [#A]
  • Positive (relative): EBITDA margin of 46.9% held above investor fears; mobile money EBITDA grew CC +28%; pricing power in Nigeria demonstrated (CC +40%) [#J]
  • Mgmt commentary shift: "Naira stabilisation visible" narrative introduced — management pointed to naira trading range of 1,550–1,650/USD vs 1,500/USD prior; [AS-Investec] described this as "premature and unconvincing" [#A]
  • Sub: [AS-RBC] noted this is the 5th consecutive period management has signalled naira stabilisation is "imminent"
  • Per-period synthesis: The dominant bearish thread across all six periods is that management's credibility on FX/macro has been progressively eroded while operational delivery has been consistent; the risk is that investors price a "boy who cried wolf" discount on the eventual naira recovery story [#J]

§7 — Sell-Side Estimate Revisions

Revision verdict: Sell-side has been systematically reactive, not pre-emptive — revisions to USD EPS have followed devaluation events rather than anticipating them; CC estimate revisions have been more stable and modestly upward [#J]

  • Pre-release revision (FY24 H1): Consensus did not pre-empt June 2023 naira devaluation — estimates were cut only after the CBN announcement; [AS-Citi] and [AS-JPMorgan] both lowered FY24 USD revenue estimates by ~20% within a week of the devaluation but before the H1 results [#A]
