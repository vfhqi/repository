- The most bearish scenario is a PureGym price war or aggressive co-location strategy targeting GYM catchments. This is not happening currently but is the downside scenario that would most damage GYM's economics.

1. PUBLISHED SHORT-SELLER REPORTS

No formal short-seller reports have been published on The Gym Group by any named activist short sellers including ShadowFall, Muddy Waters, Citron, Hindenburg, Gotham City, or Viceroy. GYM does not present the characteristics typically targeted by activist short sellers: the business is straightforward, governance is clean, accounting is simple, and there are no off-balance-sheet structures or aggressive related-party transactions.

GYM Group has been the subject of negative commentary by retail investors and financial bloggers in prior periods, primarily centred on the underperformance during the 2018-2020 period under the prior CEO regime. The company's share price significantly underperformed between its IPO in 2015 and the COVID period, creating a legacy of scepticism among some investors.

The current bear case is purely fundamental and valuation-driven rather than fraud or governance based.

2. SHORT INTEREST DATA

The Gym Group is a mid-cap UK listed company (AIM/Main Market). Short interest disclosure under UK FCA rules requires reporting above 0.5% of outstanding shares. No significant disclosed short positions have been identified as of Q1-26.

The stock has benefitted from the trading momentum and guidance upgrades under Orr, which has progressively squeezed out short sellers who were positioned for ongoing underperformance. The January 2026 £10m buyback announcement — a management signal of undervaluation — has likely further discouraged short selling. Short interest is estimated to be low (below 2% of shares outstanding) based on available commentary.

Days to cover would be short given GYM's decent daily liquidity relative to its market cap.

3. BEARISH SELL-SIDE VIEWS

No sell-side Underperform or Sell ratings have been identified in recent coverage. The analyst community is broadly constructive following the FY25 results beat and guidance upgrade.

Bears within the sell-side community have raised the following themes without formal negative ratings:

ARPMM deceleration: Growth fell from approximately 7% in FY24 to 4% in FY25 (ARPMM £20.44 H1-24 to £21.16 H1-25, then £21.60 at FY25). The Standard membership price increased to £25.10 in June 2025. Bears ask: is the gap between headline price and average ARPMM (£25.10 headline vs £21.60 average) widening due to discount/promotional activity, or is it purely a mix effect from monthly vs annual contracts? If promotional discounting is required to maintain membership volumes, underlying pricing power is weaker than reported.

Mature site ROIC stagnation: ROIC reached 27% (FY25), up from 25% (FY24). Excluding 13 problematic "workforce-dependent" sites, ROIC is 30%. Bears note: the 13 excluded sites are real sites with real capital invested and real underperformance. The gap between reported and adjusted ROIC is a watch item. Management's target is to return to 30% on the full estate; it is not there yet.

Expansion pace risk: Accelerating from 16 new sites (FY25) to 20-22 (FY26) and 75 over 3 years implies capex of £60-65m in FY26 and ~£50m/year thereafter. Bears question whether the next cohort of sites delivers comparable ROIC to 2018/19-vintage clubs that are now fully mature. Location scarcity (best sites already taken) could mean newer sites are in second-choice locations with softer membership ramp.

4. ACCOUNTING AND GOVERNANCE RED FLAGS

GYM Group's accounting is simple and transparent. There are no complex non-GAAP adjustments beyond the standard IFRS 16 treatment (which the company discloses both ways). "Adjusted EBITDA less normalised rent" is the primary operating metric and is directly comparable to pre-IFRS 16 economics. The business does not rely on aggressive non-cash adjustments to tell its story.

The primary accounting risk is IFRS 16 lease liabilities. GYM's clubs are all leased. A 15-year lease on a club generating £800k EBITDA/year at maturity is a valuable asset; the same lease on a club generating £200k is a liability. Under IFRS 16, the right-of-use asset and lease liability both sit on the balance sheet. If GYM is forced to close an underperforming site, it must either negotiate a lease exit (expensive) or write off the remaining right-of-use asset. The risk is concentrated in the newest cohorts, opened 2024-26, which are not yet mature and whose performance has not been tested through a full economic cycle.

GYM Group's non-property net debt of £59.3m (1.0x leverage) is its most significant balance sheet strength. This is low leverage for an expansion-stage infrastructure business. The business is FCF-positive (£38.3m FY25) and expanding. There is no near-term refinancing risk, no convert put option, and no acquisition integration debt.

Corporate governance: Will Orr has rebuilt management credibility after the Treharne era. Non-executive board composition is appropriate for a £400m+ market cap company. No related-party concerns have been identified. The £10m buyback is modest but signals a capital allocation framework that is growing in sophistication.

5. ONLINE BEARISH COMMENTARY

Online bearish commentary on GYM is relatively muted compared to BFIT. The UK investment community has largely shifted from sceptical to constructive following the FY25 results.

Residual bear themes from prior periods:

The prior CEO era (Treharne) oversaw a prolonged period of share price underperformance. Under Treharne, GYM expanded aggressively to ~230 sites by 2020 but failed to convert membership growth into EBITDA leverage. Energy cost spikes in 2022 hit the business hard. The stock traded below IPO price for extended periods. This history creates a "show me" investor cohort who have been sceptical of multiple previous guidance upgrades.

The 13 "workforce-dependent" sites excluded from the 30% ROIC calculation are a consistent point of criticism. These sites (acquired from YMCAs or similar community operators) have embedded staff cost structures that are incompatible with GYM's model. Management has been managing these down; bears note that true estate-wide ROIC remains below target.

Some retail investor commentary queries whether ARPMM can grow meaningfully from £21.60, given that PureGym offers comparable access at similar price points and GYM's lowest-cost positioning is its primary competitive advantage. If GYM raises prices materially, it risks ceding its competitive positioning.

6. COUNTER-ARGUMENTS TO BULL CASE

Bull: ARPMM can grow 5%+/year. Bear: The deceleration from 7% to 4% suggests the easy pricing gains have been taken. The headline Standard price of £25.10 is already at the upper end of what a budget gym brand can credibly charge without becoming a mid-market proposition. The ARPMM-to-headline price gap (£21.60 vs £25.10) implies that a significant proportion of the member base is on legacy pricing tiers, introductory offers, or discounted annual memberships. Growing ARPMM requires either converting these members to higher price points (churn risk) or relying purely on new member mix at current prices.

Bull: 30% ROIC on the full estate is achievable. Bear: ROIC on the full estate is currently 27%. Thirteen sites are explicitly excluded from the 30% calculation. These sites require restructuring (lease exits, refits, renegotiation) before the estate average can reach 30%. Restructuring costs are real. Meanwhile, the 2026 cohort of new sites (20-22 sites, £60-65m capex) will temporarily dilute estate ROIC as these sites ramp toward maturity.

Bull: UK has room for 300+ more budget gym sites, giving GYM a long runway. Bear: The national-level headroom does not mean GYM captures it. PureGym is targeting 300+ additional UK sites. JD Gyms is growing. Anytime Fitness is expanding via franchising. The available catchment areas are being claimed simultaneously by multiple well-capitalised operators. GYM's site selection discipline will be tested as the best locations are increasingly contested. Co-location (a budget gym opening within 1km of an existing GYM site) is the single most damaging competitive development; evidence from 2023-24 shows it does suppress membership ramp rates at affected sites.

Bull: FCF is self-funding the expansion. Bear: FCF of £38.3m funds approximately 25-30 new sites at £1.3m average capex per site. This is adequate for the current 20-22 site target. However, the 75-site-over-3-years target requires approximately £97m of capex over 3 years (£32m/year), meaning FCF barely covers expansion capex without any returning to shareholders. The buyback (£10m) is partially in tension with the expansion capex ambition unless FCF continues to grow. If new site returns disappoint (lower ROIC, slower ramp), the decision to prioritise growth capex over balance sheet strength becomes a source of regret.

Bull: GYM is the lowest-cost national chain — structural competitive advantage. Bear: This position is vulnerable to PureGym, which has access to much more capital (private equity backed) and is actively targeting the same sites. PureGym's membership price is broadly comparable to GYM's. PureGym has 600+ clubs vs GYM's 245. Scale advantages (brand awareness, procurement, marketing) favour PureGym. GYM's edge is app and technology; but if PureGym closes that gap, GYM's differentiation narrows to location proximity and brand NPS — neither of which is strongly proprietary.

7. HISTORICAL PATTERN

The Gym Group has a track record of underdelivering against ambitious growth targets under the prior management regime. Between 2015 and 2022, the company opened sites aggressively but failed to convert membership growth into EBITDA leverage. The COVID period was particularly damaging: forced closures, rent negotiations, and member churn required significant financial management.

Under Will Orr (CEO from late 2022), the narrative changed. EBITDA margins began expanding. ROIC started recovering. FCF turned meaningfully positive. The buyback is the clearest signal that the capital discipline era has arrived.

Bears note that the prior regime also made confident growth projections that subsequently required revision. The "show me" investors demand multiple quarters of evidence before fully endorsing the expansion acceleration.

8. QUALITY OF BEAR EVIDENCE

High-quality concern: ARPMM deceleration (7% → 4%) is observable fact. Whether this continues or reverses is uncertain. The structural ceiling argument has merit: at £25/month, GYM is approaching the upper bound of what the budget brand supports without a full service upgrade.

Medium-quality concern: UK market saturation. The evidence for this is PureGym's aggressive 300+ UK site target and JD Gyms' growth. However, UK penetration at ~15% implies 85% non-members. The addressable market is not exhausted. The concern is real at the level of prime catchment site scarcity, not total market demand saturation.

Low-quality concern: Balance sheet risk. GYM's 1.0x non-property leverage and £38.3m FCF mean there is no near-term financial risk. Lease liabilities are a theoretical downside risk only in a deep recession.

Low-quality concern: Management track record. Will Orr has now delivered 3 years of improving results. The prior-regime concern is diminishing with each positive quarter.

9. RISK ASSESSMENT

Downside scenario A: ARPMM growth stalls at 0-1% in FY26. LFL guidance is approximately 3% ARPMM growth. If this fails (membership grows but discounting required to retain members due to PureGym competition), revenue growth slows from 8-9% to 4-5%. EBITDA margin expansion stops. Target £65m run rate EBITDA is not achieved. Price impact: 15-25% de-rating from missed guidance.

Downside scenario B: New site cohort ROIC disappoints. 20-22 FY26 sites open at below-target membership ramp (due to contested catchments or economic weakness). Capital invested (£60-65m capex) returns less than 30% ROIC. Management guided ROIC to stay above 30% hurdle rate for new sites. If this proves optimistic, the medium-term target of 75 sites in 3 years is revisited downward. Price impact: 20-30% de-rating.

Downside scenario C: PureGym co-location strategy intensifies. PureGym opens 60+ UK sites in 2026, with disproportionate clustering near GYM sites in contested catchments. Membership ramp at affected GYM sites slows materially. Some sites fail to reach maturity economics within 3 years. Price impact: 20-30% de-rating plus sentiment damage to the rollout thesis.

Downside scenario D: Recession forces member churn. UK consumer recession in 2026-27 (not the base case but possible in an Iran/oil shock scenario). Budget gym members at £25/month are not immune to income shocks. If 5-10% of the 923k member base churns and is not replaced, revenue and EBITDA decline simultaneously while lease costs are fixed. Price impact: 30-40% de-rating.

10. SCEPTICAL REBUTTAL

GYM Group's balance sheet quality is the most compelling rebuttal to every bear thesis. 1.0x non-property leverage, £38.3m FCF, £10m buyback: these are the characteristics of a business in a strong position, not a stressed one.

ARPMM deceleration from 7% to 4% still represents material pricing power. Most consumer businesses would be delighted with 4% organic revenue per unit growth in a UK consumer environment that has faced 3-4 years of inflation pressure.

The 13 workforce-dependent excluded sites are a known issue and are being actively managed. When resolved (whether through refits, lease exits, or operational restructuring), estate ROIC improves toward 30% without any new site contribution.

PureGym competes in the same budget segment but its private equity ownership (Leonard Green & Partners) creates pressure for an eventual exit — likely IPO. This may constrain PureGym's ability to invest in prolonged price competition that destroys returns for both operators.

Will Orr's track record over 3 years is real. The FY25 results — EBITDA +19%, FCF +10%, statutory profit tripling — are not the product of accounting manipulation. They reflect operational improvement.
