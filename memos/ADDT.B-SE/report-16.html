§6 — Governance Structure + Red Flags

J-front verdict: Governance structure is clean by Swedish standards — dual-class voting concentration is the primary structural flag; no restatements, no material weaknesses, no significant litigation; controlling-shareholder dynamic is the central governance risk

Voting rights structure:
- Dual-class shares: Addtech has an A/B share structure — Class A shares carry 1 vote each; Class B shares carry 1/10 vote each; ADDT.B is the B-class share (the liquid market share); A shares are primarily held by Investment AB Latour and the Sjöberg family
- Effective voting concentration: Investment AB Latour + Sjöberg family interests hold approximately 28-32% of capital but control approximately 40-50% of voting rights via the A/B differential; this is below the level of absolute majority but provides decisive influence on all major resolutions in practice given dispersed other ownership
- Swedish regulatory framework: Dual-class structures are legal and common in Sweden; the Stockholm Stock Exchange and Swedish Corporate Governance Code do not prohibit dual class; this is not a red flag in the Swedish context but is a structural consideration for minority shareholders
- Anti-takeover effect: The voting structure makes hostile takeover effectively impossible without Latour's cooperation — this is both a stability feature (protects the long-term model from short-term activist disruption) and a minority shareholder governance risk (limits disciplining mechanism if Latour's interests diverge from minorities)

Related-party transactions:
- No material disclosed RPTs of concern: Addtech's annual report related-party disclosures (Note) do not show significant transactions with Latour or Sjöberg entities beyond normal shareholder relationship
- Standard Latour relationship: Latour is a shareholder not an operating entity that contracts with Addtech; no disclosed management fees, licensing arrangements, or asset transfers between Addtech and Latour
- Board member relationships: Standard director fee disclosures; no unusual consultancy contracts with board members

Audit history:
- Auditor: PricewaterhouseCoopers AB (PwC) has been Addtech's statutory auditor for a sustained period — no recent auditor change signals
- No material weaknesses disclosed: Addtech's audit committee report has not flagged material weaknesses in internal controls in recent annual reports
- No restatement history: No disclosed historical financial restatements of any significance
- Audit opinion: Clean unqualified audit opinions consistently

Legal + regulatory issues:
- No significant disclosed litigation: Addtech's legal proceedings note in annual reports discloses routine commercial disputes consistent with a company operating 100+ subsidiary businesses; no class action, material regulatory investigation, or disclosed SEC/Finansinspektionen enforcement action
- Competition law: Addtech's acquisition activity in narrow industrial niches has not triggered disclosed EU/Swedish competition authority concern — deal sizes are typically too small (€5-30m EV range) to require mandatory notification
- Product liability: Addtech's technology trading model creates product liability exposure; this is disclosed as a risk but no significant claims are detailed
- Environmental/regulatory: Industrial distribution has limited direct environmental footprint vs manufacturing; no disclosed material environmental liabilities

Whistleblower / governance complaints:
- No publicly disclosed whistleblower incidents or material governance complaints visible in public records
- Swedish corporate culture context: Swedish companies operate under a strong rule-of-law and transparency norm; governance complaints in listed companies are typically surfaced via press or regulatory disclosure channels; absence of such disclosures is a meaningful clean signal in the Swedish context

Sceptical bullet — bearish read on governance:
- Bear case mechanism: The clean governance record is real but the structural risk is invisible until it isn't — Latour's 40-50% effective voting control means that if Latour's investment strategy shifts (e.g. Latour wants to sell Addtech, merge it, or use it as a vehicle for a larger transaction), minority shareholders have limited recourse; the dual-class structure + Nomination Committee control means the board would not and could not block a Latour-directed strategic shift; the red flag that would invalidate the operator-quality thesis is not a governance scandal (unlikely) but a Latour-induced strategic change that disrupts the decentralised compounder model — e.g. forcing a large acquisition outside the model, monetising Addtech via a partial sale to a PE buyer at a structurally disruptive price, or directing Addtech's capital to serve a Latour portfolio need rather than Addtech's own ROIC logic
