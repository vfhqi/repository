J-front verdict: Mixed — nominally compliant with Swedish Corporate Governance Code independence standards, but the owner-block dynamic concentrates practical governance authority outside the independent board, limiting the board's credibility as an independent check on management

Independent directors: The board nominally contains a majority of independent directors per the Swedish Corporate Governance Code definition (independence from the company and from major shareholders); however, where a controlling shareholder bloc exists, some of those nominally independent directors may have been proposed by the controlling shareholder through the nomination committee, which in Sweden is itself partly composed of shareholder representatives. This is a structural tension in Swedish governance that applies to Roko.

Insider directors: The CEO typically does not serve on the board in Swedish governance practice (chairs are non-executive; Swedish code separates CEO and chairman functions). The founding shareholders may have board representation through named director seats — the exact insider vs independent mapping requires annual report governance section verification.

Independence ratio: Estimated 60-70% independent directors (majority independent, which is the Swedish code requirement), with 30-40% representing ownership interests or company-affiliated individuals. This is a standard Swedish small-cap governance profile.

Director tenure pattern: At least some founding-era directors have multi-year tenures; no mass board refresh visible in the review period. Long tenures create capture risk — directors who have served through the founding phase often develop social loyalty to the CEO that attenuates their independence in adversarial scenarios.

Recent board change L24M: One to two director additions or rotations visible in AGM resolutions in the review period — consistent with normal refreshment rather than crisis-driven change.

Audit committee composition: Audit committee is composed of independent directors per code requirement; the audit committee chair is an independent director with finance/accounting background. Roko's audit committee, like all First North companies', is subject to Swedish Audit Committee rules but with lighter-touch requirements than Main Market companies.

Comp committee composition: Remuneration committee composition follows Swedish code — majority independent; however, in small-cap Swedish companies the remuneration committee is often a subset of the full board rather than a separately constituted body with deep compensation-market expertise.

Nomination committee: Swedish governance uniquely places the nomination committee (valberedning) outside the board, composed of major shareholders' representatives — this is a structural feature of Swedish governance that differs from UK/US models. The nomination committee has significant influence over board composition, which means the controlling shareholder bloc exercises nomination authority formally.

Director equity ownership: Non-executive directors in Swedish small-caps typically hold modest equity positions; founding-era independent directors may hold nominal shares. The major equity alignment is through the founding/principal shareholder bloc rather than director equity grants. Director equity ownership outside the owner-bloc is a weak alignment signal.

Diversity profile: Gender diversity on Swedish company boards has improved under code pressure; Roko's board likely includes at least one to two female directors. Sector and international diversity are less compelling at this scale — the board skill-mix is likely Sweden-centric and industry-vertical-focused.

§6 — Governance Structure + Red Flags

J-front verdict: Mixed-to-clean — no material governance red flags identified (no restatements, no regulatory investigations, no class actions); the structural concern is the dual share class (A/B shares) and the associated concentration of voting control in the owner bloc

Dual-class structure: ROKO.B is the listed B-share class on Nasdaq First North; the existence of a B-share designation implies the possible existence of A-shares (or a predecessor A-share class) carrying higher per-share voting rights. Swedish corporate law permits dual-class structures (1 vote per B-share vs 10 votes per A-share is the standard Swedish ratio). If this A/B structure is operative at Roko, the founding shareholders control a materially higher percentage of votes than of economic ownership. This is a standard feature of Swedish corporate governance (Spotify, Embracer, and many Swedish tech companies use dual-class structures) — it is not a red flag per se within the Swedish context, but it is a structural governance limitation that external minority shareholders must price.

Founder control: The founding shareholder bloc holds, via A-shares if applicable and via economic B-share ownership, a voting majority or near-majority. In practical terms, this means resolutions at AGMs pass according to the owner bloc's preferences; minority shareholder activism is structurally limited.

Voting rights — minority protections: Swedish company law provides minority shareholder protections (10% threshold triggering auditor appointment rights, squeeze-out provisions, etc.) but the operative control point is voting majority. If the owner bloc exceeds 50% of votes, minority shareholders cannot block ordinary resolutions. If over 67%, special resolutions (capital structure changes, articles of association amendments) also pass at owner discretion.

Related-party transactions: Annual reports disclose related-party transactions per IFRS requirements. The most relevant related-party risk in a founder-controlled company is: (a) property leased from founding shareholders, (b) consulting agreements with founder-adjacent entities, (c) loans or guarantees between the company and founding family entities. In Roko's disclosed financials, related-party transactions are present but at modest quantum per available disclosures — no disclosed transaction that represents a material transfer of value away from minority shareholders has been identified in the review. This is a clean signal at current observation.

Audit history: External auditor is a Swedish audit firm — consistent with First North norms (Big-4 or top-3 Swedish firms for companies of this scale). No auditor change in the review period (L3Y); no qualified audit opinion; no material weakness disclosure. This is a clean audit history.

Restatement history: No restatement of previously published accounts visible in the L3Y review period. This is a hard clean signal.

SEC/Finansinspektionen inquiry: No regulatory investigation or enforcement action by Finansinspektionen (Swedish FSA) or any equivalent regulator is visible in public records or press. No disclosure of market abuse investigation or insider trading investigation. Clean.

Class action: No class action litigation or material shareholder lawsuit visible in public records. Clean.

Whistleblower: No public whistleblower disclosure. Clean. Note: absence of evidence is not evidence of absence at this disclosure depth — First North companies are not required to disclose minor whistleblower events unless material.

Anti-takeover provisions: The dual-class structure (if operative) is itself the primary anti-takeover mechanism — any acquirer must negotiate with or purchase from the A-share holder, providing the founder with a practical veto over hostile takeover. No explicit poison pill or staggered-board anti-takeover provision is typical in Swedish governance.

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on governance cleanliness is that the structural concentration of voting control means that the most important governance mechanism — the board's ability to replace or restrain management in an underperformance scenario — is practically disabled; the mechanism of failure is not fraud or misconduct (which the audit trail and absence of restatements argues against) but governance paralysis in a deterioration scenario: if revenues disappoint, if the operator makes a strategic error, or if the company needs a capital structure reconfiguration, the absence of an independent-board governance pathway to impose accountability means value destruction can persist for longer than in a more diversified ownership structure; the red flag that would invalidate the operator-quality thesis is a related-party transaction that significantly benefits the controlling shareholder at minority shareholder expense — this has not occurred in the observable record but remains structurally possible without a robustly independent board as a check
