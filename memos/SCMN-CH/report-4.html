§1 Executive Summary

Swisscom enters the L2Y (2024-2025) review period as a company mid-transformation: a stable Swiss incumbent that has voluntarily increased its complexity, leverage, and execution risk via the EUR 8bn acquisition of Vodafone Italia (completed 31 Dec 2024, consolidated from Jan 2025). The Swiss base remains a cash-generative, high-ARPU, operationally disciplined core — but it is not a growth business. Italy is the swing factor: the bull case is scale + synergy realisation in the EUR 700m integration budget over 2-3 years; the bear case is that Italy dilutes margins, absorbs management bandwidth, and faces secular headwinds from TIM/WindTre that the combined entity cannot outrun.

  • Group scale shift: 2025 reported revenue jumped +36.6% to CHF 15,048m solely due to Italy consolidation — the underlying organic story is flat-to-declining [#I]
  • Swiss service erosion: Telecom service revenue in Switzerland fell -2.1% in 2024 and -0.5% in 2023 — the treadmill is running, not the flywheel [#J]
  • Fastweb pre-acquisition: Fastweb standalone revenue grew +6.7% to EUR 2,809m in 2024 (vs B2B-led Italian peers TIM +low-single-digits), primarily B2B/wholesale/enterprise; margin lagged Swiss core materially [#A]
  • Vodafone Italia (post-merger Italy segment): Pro forma Italy revenue for 2025 fell -1.1% to EUR 7,291m; residential down -2.9%; EBITDAaL down -3.1% on adjusted basis — a declining income stream inherited at scale [#J]
  • Guidance discipline: Met 2022, 2023, and 2024 targets; 2025 group targets met (EBITDAaL CHF 4,984m vs guidance ~CHF 5.0bn) [#A]
  • Net income compression: CHF 1,743m (2023) → CHF 1,542m (2024) → CHF 1,270m (2025) — three consecutive years of net income decline, each driven by Italy-related charges [#J]
  • Dividend trajectory: Board maintained CHF 22/share for 2024, then raised to CHF 26/share for 2025 (+18%) and guided CHF 27/share for 2026 — dividend progression lags earnings but signals management confidence in FCF stability [#A]
  • Sceptical read: The 18% dividend jump while net income fell -17.6% is a red flag on payout sustainability absent Italian synergy delivery; FCF generation (CHF 1,920m in 2025) currently supports dividend but leaves thin margin [#J]

  • Group 2025 revenue: CHF 15,048m — +36.6% YoY; wholly explained by Vodafone Italia consolidation (full year vs zero in 2024) [#I]

  • Fastweb pre-acquisition 2024: Revenue EUR 2,809m, +6.7% YoY (vs TIM Italy consensus low-single-digit); B2B wholesale connections +39.7% to 0.91m — genuine outperformance in a competitive Italian market [#A]
  • Italy segment 2025 (combined): Pro forma revenue EUR 7,291m, -1.1% vs pro forma 2024; residential -2.9%; business/enterprise broadly stable — the combined Fastweb+Vodafone entity is already showing revenue pressure in the first full year of consolidation [#J]

  • Sunrise spin-off impact: Sunrise (Liberty Global spin-off, Nov 2024) enters 2025 with ~30% broadband share and ~28% mobile share as an independent company with clean balance sheet; structural competitive pressure on Swisscom Swiss core is intensifying [#J]
  • Revenue quality: ~90% of Swiss revenue is recurring contract-based; hardware and one-off revenues represent a minor and declining share; this is a high-quality revenue base by EU telco standards [#J]
  • Swiss fixed-line decline: Fixed-line telephony connections continue structural decline; Swisscom managed this via broadband upsell but net fixed-line revenue per RGU is declining [#I]
  • ARPU premium thesis: Switzerland ARPU is structurally the highest in Europe (Mordor Intelligence: Swiss telecoms among highest globally); this is a GDP/purchasing-power effect, not pricing power per se — no evidence of active ARPU growth L2Y [#A]
  • Sceptic: Italian revenue trajectory is unfavourable in the first year of consolidation; if pro forma -1.1% revenue decline is the run rate, the deal arithmetic becomes highly sensitive to synergy delivery timing [#J]

§3 Margin Profile & Evolution

J-front verdict: Switzerland segment EBITDA margin is structurally superior (~40%) vs EU telco median (30-35%); but the Vodafone Italia deal has created a blended group margin step-down to ~33% EBITDAaL in 2025, and Italy's ~25% margin requires significant integration delivery before group margin recovers to pre-deal levels [#J]

  • Swiss EBITDA 2023: CHF ~4,622m at group level on CHF ~11.2bn revenue = ~41% margin — top-decile EU converged telco margin; Deutsche Telekom ex-T-Mobile US runs ~35-37% EBITDAaL; Proximus domestic ~38% [#A]
  • Swiss EBITDA 2024: CHF 4,355m (adjusted, excluding Italy integration costs); Swiss-segment standalone margin maintained ~40%+ — the core franchise is holding margin well [#A]
  • Group EBITDAaL 2025: CHF 4,984m on CHF 15,048m revenue = ~33.1% blended margin — a 7-8 percentage point group-level dilution vs pre-deal; this is entirely Italy-mix; Swiss core unchanged [#J]
  • Italy margin profile: Pro forma Italy EBITDAaL ~EUR 1,805m on EUR 7,291m revenue = ~24.7% margin — materially below EU telco median, reflecting competitive Italian market dynamics and integration-phase costs [#A]
  • Integration cost stripping: EUR 176m integration costs recognised in 2024 Fastweb; management guides CHF ~700m total integration spend over 2-3 years; adjusted margins are materially higher than IFRS-reported margins in 2024-2025 [#I]
  • Capex/revenue ratio: Switzerland maintained ~20% capex/revenue in 2022-2024 (CHF ~2.3bn capex on CHF ~11bn revenue); 2025 group capex guided CHF 3.1-3.2bn on CHF 15bn revenue = ~21% — broadly in line with EU infrastructure build intensity [#A]
  • Operating free cash flow 2024: CHF 1,752m (-CHF 290m YoY) — impact of elevated Italy-related costs; Swiss core FCF stable [#A]
  • Operating FCF 2025: CHF 1,920m (+9.6% YoY) — recovery beginning; guided CHF ~2.0bn for 2026 [#A]
  • Swiss margin flywheel vs Italian treadmill: Switzerland shows economies-of-scale attributes (high fixed-cost absorption, low incremental cost per customer) while Italy runs the opposite dynamic — high competitive intensity, price wars, and integration-phase inefficiency [#J]
  • Net profit margin: Swisscom net profit margin compressed from ~15.5% (2023, group basis) to ~14.0% (2024) to ~8.4% (2025) — but 2025 numerator is CHF 1,270m on a vastly larger CHF 15bn revenue base; net margin not directly comparable across the deal discontinuity [#I]
  • IT services margin drag: Swiss IT services (CHF 1.2bn, growing +3% p.a.) carry lower margin than core telecoms; growth in IT dilutes Swiss segment margin modestly at the margin [#I]
  • Proximus margin comparison: Proximus domestic EBITDA grew +2.8% in 2024 to EUR ~1.68bn; domestic margin ~36-38% — similar to Swisscom Switzerland on an apples-to-apples basis, suggesting Swiss premium is modest [#A]
  • Capex peak year assessment: Swisscom guided CHF 3.0-3.1bn for 2026 (vs CHF 3.1-3.2bn in 2025) — capex appears to be at or near peak; declining trajectory from 2026 provides FCF tailwind if EBITDA stabilises [#J]
  • Sceptic: Group margin dilution from Italy is not temporary; even on synergy delivery, Italian margin reaches ~28-30% by 2027-2028 estimates — the blended group margin structurally lands below pre-deal Switzerland-only levels [#J]

§4 EPS Trajectory

J-front verdict: EPS has been in structural compression for three years; net income fell from CHF 1,743m (2023) to CHF 1,542m (2024) to CHF 1,270m (2025) — a cumulative -27% decline — driven by integration charges, higher D&A from Vodafone Italia asset base, and EUR/CHF headwinds; underlying Swiss-segment EPS power is intact but masked [#J]

  • Net income 2023: CHF 1,743m — peak of the L3Y cycle; strong EBITDA delivery and low exceptional charges [#I]
  • Net income 2024: CHF 1,542m (-11.5% YoY) — EUR 176m Italy integration costs the primary driver; Swiss core net income broadly stable [#A]
  • Net income 2025: CHF 1,270m (-17.6% YoY) — full consolidation of Vodafone Italia D&A + integration charges + EUR/CHF headwind drove the largest absolute net income decline in the L3Y period [#A]
  • Dividend vs EPS gap: Board raised dividend to CHF 26/share for 2025 (from CHF 22/share for 2024) — an +18% increase while net income fell -17.6%; payout ratio has jumped sharply and is sustained only by FCF, not earnings growth [#J]
  • EPS (TTM approximation): ~CHF 24-27 per share (companies market cap data) against a CHF 22 dividend floor for 2024; for 2025, dividend CHF 26/share against net income implying EPS ~CHF 24-25 — dividend payout ratio near or above 100% on reported earnings [#A]
  • D&A uplift from Italy: Vodafone Italia acquisition introduces significant intangible amortisation; this is a below-EBITDA charge that permanently depresses reported EPS relative to EBITDA/FCF metrics [#I]
  • FCF supports payout: Operating FCF CHF 1,920m in 2025 comfortably covers dividend (estimated CHF ~1.3-1.4bn annual payout on ~51.7m shares at CHF 26/share); leverage is the binding constraint, not FCF [#A]
  • Leverage post-deal: Net debt/EBITDA ~2.4x end-2024; 2025 pro forma ~2.5-2.6x with Vodafone Italia consolidation; guided to deleverage toward ~2.0x over medium term [#A]
  • Peer EPS comparison: Proximus EPS was broadly flat-to-down in 2024-2025 as Belgian fibre rollout capex peak suppressed FCF; Deutsche Telekom ex-US delivered modest EPS growth; Swisscom's -27% cumulative EPS compression is an outlier in the peer set [#J]
  • EPS recovery thesis: Assumes (a) Italian synergy delivery on CHF ~700m program, (b) EUR/CHF stabilisation, (c) capex declining from 2026; all three are conditional [#J]
  • Sceptic: Three consecutive years of net income decline with a rising dividend is a yellow flag; the board's confidence may be warranted by FCF dynamics, but the narrative requires scrutiny — rising dividends during earnings compression is the classic "managed decline" playbook [#J]

  • Guidance conservatism pattern: Swisscom's revenue guidance tends to be slightly conservative (CHF strength creates downside optionality); EBITDA guidance is tighter and met consistently [#A]

  • Swiss ARPU premium: Switzerland has the highest mobile ARPU in Europe (~EUR 35-40/month estimated vs EU median ~EUR 22-25/month); this is structurally driven by GDP/PPP not Swisscom's pricing power — Sunrise captures similar ARPU [#A]
  • Swisscom vs Proximus — revenue trajectory: Proximus domestic revenue grew +3.4% in 2024 vs Swisscom Swiss telecom service revenue -2.1% — Proximus is outgrowing Swisscom on volume in domestic operations; different market dynamics (Belgian cable rebuilding, FMC growth) [#A]
  • Swisscom vs Proximus — EBITDA margin: Both run ~38-42% domestic EBITDA margins; Swisscom Switzerland is marginally superior; Proximus carries Global Teleservices (BICS) which dilutes group margin [#A]
  • Swisscom vs Proximus — guidance delivery: Swisscom 3/3 guidance met (2022-2024); Proximus cut 2025 guidance mid-year — Swisscom wins on predictability [#J]
  • Swisscom vs Liberty Global: LBTYK comparison is limited — Liberty is a different structural animal (cable-first, no incumbent fixed-line), operates in Netherlands/Belgium/UK/Ireland; no Swiss/Italian exposure; lower ARPU markets; different capital allocation model (buyback-heavy vs Swisscom dividend-heavy) [#A]
  • Sunrise as direct Swiss peer: Sunrise 2025 guidance "broadly stable revenue + stable to low-single-digit EBITDAaL growth" — similar stagnation to Swisscom Switzerland; mobile ARPU -1.6% in Q2 2025, fixed ARPU -4.5% in Q2 2025 for Sunrise — confirms Swiss market-wide ARPU erosion not Swisscom-specific weakness [#A]
  • Fastweb vs TIM Italy: Fastweb pre-acquisition grew +6.7% in 2024 (revenue) vs TIM Italy consensus low-single-digits; Fastweb was gaining B2B/wholesale share; post-Vodafone merger, combined entity is second-largest Italian telco [#A]
  • Deutsche Telekom comparison: DT EBITDAaL margin ~35-37%; capex/revenue ~18%; ND/EBITDA ~2.5x — broadly comparable leverage but DT benefits from T-Mobile US (high-growth, high-margin) that has no Swisscom equivalent; not a clean peer [#I]
  • EU telco sector median reference: EU converged telcos median EBITDA margin ~30-35%, capex/revenue ~18-22%, ND/EBITDA ~2.5-3.0x; Swisscom Switzerland is above-median on EBITDA, in-line on capex, below-median on leverage [#I]
  • Swisscom leverage post-deal: ND/EBITDA ~2.4x end-2024; marginally above EU telco median but committed to de-levering; Proximus ND/EBITDA ~3.5-4.0x during peak capex — Swisscom balance sheet is cleaner than Proximus despite Italy acquisition [#A]
  • FCF yield comparison: Swisscom operating FCF ~CHF 1,920m on a market cap of ~CHF 25-30bn (estimated) = ~6-7% FCF yield — competitive with EU telco peers; Proximus adjusted FCF CHF 58m in 2024 is negligible (peak capex impact) [#A]
  • Sceptic: Swisscom's Switzerland-versus-peers ARPU and margin advantage is a function of market structure (oligopoly, high-income population) not management excellence; the moment Sunrise inflects Swiss competitive pricing, the premium compression risk is real and relatively rapid [#J]

§7 Sector / Macro Context

J-front verdict: The European telecom sector faces a crossroads — declining legacy voice and copper revenue, rising fibre capex, softening ARPU in competitive markets; Switzerland is structurally better positioned than Belgium/Netherlands/France, but the Sunrise spin-off has changed the competitive clock speed in Swiss markets for the first time in a decade [#J]

  • Swiss market structure: Three MNOs (Swisscom, Sunrise, Salt) + strong cable/fibre players; more oligopolistic than Belgium (3 MNOs + DAZN/cable) or Netherlands (KPN + VodaZiggo + T-Mobile); Swiss regulatory environment is less interventionist than COMCO's recent fibre architecture rulings (though that ruling itself created some uncertainty) [#A]
  • Sunrise spin-off dynamics: Liberty Global spun off Sunrise in Nov 2024 as an independent listed entity; Sunrise has clean balance sheet, ~30% fixed broadband share, ~28% mobile share; first year as independent it has launched 5G SA network (Mar 2025) and new tariff plans — this is structurally more dangerous than when Sunrise was a Liberty subsidiary with capital constraints [#J]
  • Swiss fibre rollout: Swisscom targets 57% FTTH coverage by end-2025 (from ~45% at end-2023); target 75-80% by 2030; copper decommissioning underway; COMCO (Swiss Competition Commission) ruling on P2P vs P2MP architecture created some delay and cost uplift in rural rollout [#I]
  • Capex cycle position: Swisscom Swiss capex has been ~CHF 2.3bn for three consecutive years; guided to ~CHF 3.0-3.1bn for 2026 group level (includes Italy ramp-down); Swiss-specific capex intensity appears near peak; Italian integration capex should normalise post-2026 [#J]
  • EU regulatory tailwinds: European Commission's push for investment in fibre + 5G has reduced regulatory friction for incumbents vs a decade ago; Swisscom benefits as the dominant Swiss infrastructure owner [#I]
  • Italian macro context: Italy GDP growth ~0.7-1.0% (2025 consensus); soft consumer environment weighing on residential ARPU; B2B/enterprise is the growth pocket; Italian government's 5G spectrum ambitions + AI infrastructure drive B2B opportunity (FastwebAI Suite positioning) [#A]
  • EUR/CHF headwind: Swiss franc structural strength (~EUR 0.93-0.95/CHF as of late 2025) creates persistent translation headwind on Italian revenue/EBITDA consolidation; management flagged CHF strength as a CHF ~70-100m annual drag at group EBITDA [#I]
  • Interest rate environment: ECB cutting rates in 2025; CHF rates low; Swisscom benefits from modest refinancing cost reduction on EUR debt, but interest expense uplift from Vodafone Italia acquisition is the dominant near-term driver [#I]
  • Sceptic: The Swiss market's structural ARPU advantage persists but is narrowing — Sunrise's aggressive post-spin pricing means the "floor" on Swiss competitive intensity has shifted upward; any assumption of stable Swiss ARPU must be tested against Sunrise's 2025-2026 tariff moves [#J]

§8 Quality of Earnings

J-front verdict: Swiss-segment earnings are high quality: predominantly recurring subscription revenue, limited one-offs, clean EBITDA delivery history, and low working-capital volatility; the principal earnings quality risk in 2024-2025 is the management of Italy integration adjustments, where the boundary between "integration costs" and ongoing operational inefficiency is hard to police from the outside [#J]

  • Revenue recurring profile: ~90%+ of Swiss revenue is subscription-based (mobile, broadband, TV contracts); hardware revenues are minor and declining; this matches or exceeds EU telco sector norms on recurring revenue quality [#A]
  • Swiss EBITDA reliability: Swiss EBITDA has been within CHF 100m of guidance for three consecutive years (2022-2024) — a very tight delivery corridor; Proximus allowed itself >5% variance in 2025 [#A]
  • Adjustments discipline: Management distinguishes "reported" vs "adjusted" EBITDA for Italy integration costs; the adjustments are specific, time-bounded (2-3 year integration), and pre-announced; there is no pattern of ongoing "restructuring" adjustments to Swiss-segment EBITDA [#I]
  • IT services growth quality: IT services revenue growing +3-5% p.a. but IT margin structure is lower and revenue recognition more complex (project-based); not a quality concern but a mix headwind [#I]
  • Italy integration cost risk: EUR 176m recognised in 2024; total integration budget ~EUR 700m over 2-3 years; annual run rate ~EUR 200-230m in 2025-2026; if synergy delivery slips, the integration cost line extends and what was presented as one-off becomes recurring [#J]
  • Lease accounting: EBITDAaL (post-lease) is the primary disclosure metric; lease commitments for network infrastructure are material but stable and well-disclosed; no concern about lease structuring to flatter EBITDA [#I]
  • Capex vs maintenance capex split: Not explicitly disclosed; Swiss fibre rollout is growth/maintenance blend; Italian network harmonisation is predominantly integration/growth; maintenance capex lower than reported gross capex — FCF is a cleaner measure [#I]
  • Working capital: Telco working capital is structurally negative (subscribers pay in advance); no red flags in the disclosed working capital evolution L2Y [#I]
  • State ownership as quality signal: 51% Swiss federal government ownership creates implicit support for dividend policy and limits financial risk-taking; also creates mild governance inertia [#A]
  • Fastweb track record: Pre-acquisition Fastweb EBITDA grew +1.5% in 2024 (EUR) — below revenue growth (+6.7%), suggesting margin compression from investment or competition; not a quality red flag but a pace-of-investment indicator [#A]
  • Sceptic: Integration cost classification (one-off vs recurring) is the single largest earnings quality risk in 2025-2026; investors relying on adjusted metrics must stress the scenario where Italian synergy delivery falls 30-50% short of EUR 700m target [#J]

§9 Sceptical Synthesis

J-front verdict (bear case): Swisscom is a cash-generative, well-managed Swiss incumbent that has voluntarily increased its risk profile by paying EUR ~8bn for Vodafone Italia — a declining residential revenue asset in Europe's most competitive mass-market — at the top of the Italian telco valuation cycle; the Swiss core bleeds slowly; the combination may be value-neutral to -destructive over a 3-5Y horizon if Italian synergies disappoint or Swiss competitive dynamics worsen

  • Bear case thesis: The Vodafone Italia deal prioritised strategic scale over financial returns; management paid 4-5x EV/EBITDA for a business showing -2.9% residential revenue decline in its first year of consolidated reporting [#J]
  • Swiss service revenue structural decline: Three consecutive years of telecom service revenue erosion (-0.5%, -2.1%, and likely continued pressure in 2025-2026) is not cyclical — it reflects structural substitution (cord-cutting, VoIP, over-the-top) and Sunrise competitive pressure [#J]
  • ARPU erosion is market-wide: Sunrise data (mobile ARPU -1.6%, fixed ARPU -4.5% in Q2 2025) confirms Swiss ARPU headwinds are a market structural feature, not company-specific; Swisscom faces same dynamics [#A]
  • Dividend sustainability question: Paying CHF 26/share dividend on CHF 1,270m net income implies near-100% earnings payout ratio; FCF is the only real support; if capex stays elevated or Italian FCF disappoints, dividend coverage narrows [#J]
  • Currency structural headwind: EUR/CHF translation is a permanent headwind with Vodafone Italia; CHF structural strength (safe-haven status) is more likely to persist than to reverse; management cannot hedge this at scale [#J]
  • Integration complexity: Merging Fastweb (B2B/enterprise DNA) with Vodafone Italia (consumer mass-market) creates brand, IT, and network complexity that has no easy precedent in Swiss M&A history; management bandwidth risk is real [#J]
  • Italian EBITDAaL trend: Pro forma EBITDAaL in Italy fell -3.1% adjusted in 2025; if this trajectory continues, the synergy delivery must both arrest the organic decline AND deliver positive absolute growth — a double lift that few European telco mergers have achieved [#J]
  • Flywheel vs treadmill verdict: Switzerland is running a treadmill (price erosion, volume loss in fixed, flat ARPU) with IT services as a modest offset; Italy is a treadmill with integration complexity overlaid; there is no flywheel in evidence [#J]
  • Regulatory risk — Switzerland: COMCO's fibre architecture ruling (P2P mandate) has already delayed Swiss rural rollout and increased unit costs; further intervention in wholesale access pricing is a non-trivial risk [#J]
  • State ownership duality: 51% state ownership means management incentives may not be fully profit-maximising; the 18% dividend jump during earnings compression looks more like a political dividend than a financial one [#J]
  • Wisdom Library check — predictability obsession (Gold): Swisscom scores well on the guidance delivery dimension; but predictable earnings in a declining earnings trend is a predictably bad outcome for long-term investors [#J]
