§1 — Executive Summary

Swisscom enters 2026 as the strongest-balance-sheet, highest-market-share incumbent in the EU telecom cohort — but the investment case has structurally shifted from a Swiss yield compounder to a dual-engine story where Italian integration execution is the pivotal variable. The dividend is growing, not static, and FCF coverage is improving. The key question for GARP: does Fastweb+Vodafone Italy reach its EUR 600m synergy target on schedule, and does Swiss FTTH monetisation generate the operating leverage to fund ongoing growth capex without compressing Swiss returns?

  • Driver set verdict: Six of eight candidate drivers are active and material at Triaging stage; Italian regulatory risk and CHF cost protection are real but secondary in magnitude relative to the integration and fibre themes [#J]
  • Highest-impact driver: Fastweb+Vodafone Italy integration synergies — EUR 600m annual target, 15.8% realised in first year, multi-year ramp still ahead [#A]
  • Second-highest impact: Swiss FTTH operating leverage — copper switch-off path enables structural OpEx improvement over 2025-2030 horizon [#A]
  • Most uncertain driver: Italian market execution — B2B ICT mix shift from telco to cloud/IoT not yet proven at scale; WindTre and TIM competition is fierce [#A]
  • Most misunderstood driver: Dividend sustainability — consensus treats CHF 22 as the base; actual base is now CHF 26 (raised 18% in Mar-26) with CHF 27 signposted; this changes the yield story materially [#J]
  • Sceptical anchor: Swiss ARPU is structurally capped by Sunrise and Salt competition — mobile ARPU has been declining modestly, fixed ARPU held only by FMC and security upsell; headline Swiss revenue growth is unlikely to exceed low single digits [#J]

§2 — Key Driver #1: Swiss Domestic ARPU Defence

Definition: Swisscom's ability to sustain or grow ARPU in the Swiss mobile and fixed broadband market against Sunrise and Salt through pricing discipline, upsell, and brand premium.

  • Verdict: Swiss ARPU is in a managed decline on mobile, stabilised on fixed; the defence mechanism is FMC upsell, security bundles, and loyalty programmes — not headline price increases [#J]
  • Market share anchor: Swisscom holds ~54% mobile and ~45% broadband market share in Switzerland (end-2024) vs EU telco incumbents typically 40-50% mobile; position is premium [#I]
  • Mobile ARPU trajectory: Second/third brand customer growth is the primary driver of blended ARPU dilution (-CHF 1 per sub in recent periods); first-brand Swisscom ARPU is holding but mix shift is the structural drag [#A]
  • Fixed ARPU levers: Wireline ARPU roughly stable; upsell into security offerings and higher-value TV products contributing marginal uplift; higher FTTH-tier upsell is the medium-term lever [#A]
  • Pricing discipline vs Sunrise/Salt: Sunrise and Salt maintain promotional intensity that prevents Swisscom from pushing through broad price increases for existing customers; price competition is structural, not transient [#J]
  • NPS advantage: NPS of 17 well above competitors; brand premium is real and supports lower churn even without pricing power [#I]
  • Sceptic: Swiss mobile market is a three-player oligopoly with Sunrise now independently funded — logic for price discipline from all three is sound, but execution in practice shows persistent promotional warfare at the margin [#J]
  • Peer anchor: EU telco incumbents on average see mobile ARPU in low-to-flat growth; Swisscom's CHF-denominated ARPU (~CHF 50 mobile) is among the highest in Europe — base is premium, upside is limited [#I]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — Swiss ARPU will be defended via upsell and FMC; outright growth is unlikely without structural change in competitive intensity

§3 — Key Driver #2: Fastweb Italy B2B/Enterprise Growth

Definition: Fastweb+Vodafone Italy's ability to grow revenues and margin in the Italian B2B/enterprise segment through cloud, IoT, AI, cybersecurity, and managed services as telco connectivity revenues decline.

  • Verdict: B2B/enterprise ICT growth is real and accelerating at +9.2% YoY, but the base (EUR 202m value-added services within a EUR 800m enterprise unit) is modest relative to the transformation ambition [#J]
  • Enterprise unit scale: Enterprise Business Unit revenues EUR 800m in Q1 2025 (+2.7% YoY); value-added services (Cloud, IoT, cybersecurity, MPN) EUR 202m within that (+9.2% YoY) — growth segment is about 25% of enterprise, growing fast [#A]
  • AI/cloud positioning: FastwebAI Suite launched 2025; AI supercomputer third-largest in Italy by compute capacity; AWS partnership for B2B market; positioning ahead of peers on Italian enterprise AI stack [#I]
  • Network infrastructure: Fastweb+Vodafone FTTH coverage 53% of Italian households/businesses at end-June 2025 (+14% YoY); 5G at 87% population (+14% YoY) — infrastructure base is genuinely competitive [#A]
  • Structural headwind: Italian B2B total revenues stable at EUR 3,237m (-0.5%) for FY2025; telecom services declining offsetting ICT growth — net effect is near-zero top-line growth while ICT mix shifts higher [#A]
  • Competition: TIM Enterprise (stronger brand in PA), Aruba (cloud infra), Noovle (TIM cloud JV), and increasingly hyperscalers competing directly for Italian enterprise ICT spend [#I]
  • Public sector exposure: Italy's PNRR cloud migration programme is a potential positive catalyst for Italian enterprise ICT spend; Fastweb has PA relationships from legacy telco contracts [#I]
  • Sceptic: EUR 202m ICT revenue within a EUR 800m enterprise unit growing at 9% solves a modest arithmetic problem — it would take years of sustained growth to offset structural telco service revenue decline at scale [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — B2B ICT growth is genuine but the pace of mix shift is gradual; net Italian B2B revenue growth is low single digits at best over N1-3Y

§4 — Key Driver #3: Swiss FTTH Completion and Operating Leverage

Definition: Swisscom's rollout of fibre-to-the-home across Switzerland, enabling copper switch-off, OpEx reduction from legacy network decommission, and ARPU uplift from fibre-tier upsell.

  • Verdict: Swiss FTTH is the clearest structural value-creation driver — the path to 60% by 2026 and 90% by 2035 is credibly funded and engineered; operating leverage from copper decommission is the under-appreciated element [#J]
  • Coverage trajectory: 56% FTTH coverage at end-2025; target 60% by end-2026; 75-80% by ~2030; 90%+ by 2035; partnership with four construction firms and local electricity utilities accelerating rollout pace [#I]
  • Copper switch-off path: Swisscom has formally announced copper network switch-off; digitec/Swisscom materials confirm decommission timeline is active; OpEx on legacy copper per-home is materially higher than FTTH [#I]
  • Capital intensity: Annual investment ~CHF 1.7bn per year in network (Switzerland); within group capex of ~CHF 2.5bn; capex intensity is high but stable, not escalating [#A]
  • OpEx leverage mechanism: As copper homes migrate to FTTH and copper decommissions, Swisscom removes dual-network maintenance cost; EU comparables (BT Openreach, Deutsche Telekom) show 10-15% network OpEx reduction on full copper switch-off [#A]
  • ARPU upsell: Fibre customers can access higher-bandwidth tiers (10Gbps offered in Switzerland); upsell premium vs VDSL/copper broadband is CHF 10-15/month in peer markets [#I]
  • Peak capex question: Swisscom has not yet signalled peak capex year — with 40% of Swiss households still to connect, build spend likely sustained through 2028 [#A]
  • Sceptic: OpEx leverage from copper switch-off is real but slow — decommission follows coverage by 2-3 years minimum; free cash flow benefit is back-weighted towards 2028-2030 [#J]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — FTTH operating leverage is structurally sound; timeline is back-weighted but the engineering and financial logic is clear and committed

  • Verdict: FMC is one of Swisscom's genuine structural advantages — empirically high penetration, measurable churn reduction on both wireline and mobile, and NPS premium vs Sunrise/Salt are confirmed by management in 2025 commentary [#J]
  • NPS anchor: NPS of 17 is well above Swiss competitors; Swisscom management attributes recent NPS improvement to Swisscom Benefits loyalty programme and FMC stickiness [#I]
  • Churn impact: Lower churn confirmed on both wireline and mobile in 2025; FMC churn reduction is the primary mechanism for maintaining customer base in a mature, saturated market [#A]
  • ARPU dynamics: Industry meta-analysis (Simon-Kucher/Tefficient) shows FMC bundles generate 25-51% higher blended ARPU vs standalone services across operators; Swisscom's own ARPU uplift is partially offset by second/third brand dilution [#A]
  • FMC penetration ceiling: Switzerland is a small, dense market — FMC penetration likely already high among Swisscom's first-brand base; incremental gains are at the margin of the remaining unconverged segment [#A]
  • Lock-in debate: Tefficient research notes FMC lock-in is weakening for ~60% of EU operators — Swisscom's NPS data suggests it is in the 40% where lock-in is still working, but this cannot be assumed permanent [#A]
  • Sceptic: FMC has stabilised the Swiss subscriber base but is unlikely to drive material revenue acceleration; it is a defensive rather than offensive driver at this stage of market maturity [#J]
  • Product examples: VR Family, BlueKids bundles cited as high-value segment anchors; multi-play bundles extending into streaming/entertainment add-on layers [#I]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility as a churn-defence mechanism — ARPU uplift is real but incremental; FMC is a moat, not a growth engine

§6 — Key Driver #5: Sunrise Competitive Intensity Post Spin-Off

Definition: Whether Sunrise's independence as a public company since November 2024 changes competitive behaviour in the Swiss market — specifically capex posture, promotional intensity, and spectrum strategy.

  • Verdict: Sunrise's post-spin-off financials argue for capital discipline, not aggression — capex fell 6.1% in 2025 to CHF 478.7m (16% of revenue); FCF is being distributed as dividends; no evidence of a share-grab strategy [#J]
  • Capex trajectory: Sunrise guiding capex at ~15% of revenue going forward; 2025 capex reduction driven by 5G SA completion, 3G switch-off savings, and end of integration costs — further decline expected through 2027 [#A]
  • Competitive quality: Sunrise achieved 975 points in the 2025 connect annual test — one of five best networks globally; Switzerland has three networks in the global top 10 — network quality is not a competitive differentiator between Swisscom and Sunrise [#I]
  • FCF distribution mandate: Sunrise committed to distributing a substantial portion of FCF as dividends from 2025; adjusted FCF CHF 379.7m in 2025 (+4.7% YoY); this creates a shareholder-return orientation that limits the scope for value-destructive share-grab spending [#A]
  • Spectrum auction risk: Frequency auction renewal not due until 2028 — this is the next material capex event for Sunrise; until then, investment pressure is modest [#I]
  • 5G SA launch: Sunrise launched 5G Standalone in March 2025 — enables network slicing for industrial IoT; both Swisscom and Sunrise are now 5G SA capable, maintaining competitive parity on next-generation services [#I]
  • Sceptic: Public company status gives Sunrise management independent incentives to chase market share in ways that were constrained under Liberty Global's portfolio logic; the first full year of independence warrants monitoring before declaring competitive stability [#J]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility that Sunrise maintains capital discipline — dividend mandate and capex decline are structurally observable; aggressive competitive posture is not the base case
