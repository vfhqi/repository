§1 — Career History + Biography

J-front verdict: Kirkby is a genuine telecoms operator track, not a consultant or generalist; 25 years in the sector with two substantive CEO roles before BT, both in capital-intensive Nordic network businesses

Education:
- Read Accounting and Finance at the University of Leicester, graduating early 1990s
- Qualified as a Chartered Accountant (ICAEW) at Deloitte, London — finance-first formation, not engineering or technology
- No MBA; career-built rather than programme-credentialed — a mild positive for operator-track reading

First role:
- Joined Procter and Gamble in a finance function post-qualification, brief stint — approximately 2 years mid-1990s
- Moved to Telewest (UK cable/telecoms) in finance roles in the late 1990s; this is where the telecoms imprint begins
- Telewest is a formative signal: a capital-heavy, operationally complex cable business with chronic leverage — Kirkby absorbed fibre/cable infrastructure economics early in career

Function progression:
- Finance-origin (audit → corporate finance → CFO track) transitioning to P&L ownership (Deputy CEO → CEO) — a functional arc that is relatively common in capital-intensive telecoms where CFO-to-CEO promotions happen at operators that care about balance sheet discipline
- Contrast with engineering-to-CEO tracks (e.g. Vodafone's Nick Read, OpenReach-type engineering heritage) — Kirkby is a balance sheet and commercial operator, not a network engineer; this matters for how she manages Openreach's build programme (through financial and commercial levers rather than deep engineering judgement)

Industry continuity:
- Effectively 100% telecoms post-Procter and Gamble departure; cable and wireline heavy; Tele2 also has mobile (Sweden, Baltics); Telia has full-stack mobile/fixed
- Strong industry continuity: knows the capital cycle, the regulatory dynamics (Ofcom equivalent in multiple Nordic jurisdictions), and the infrastructure monetisation logic

Geographic footprint:
- UK (early career), Sweden (Com Hem, Tele2, Telia) — Nordic expertise is distinct; Nordic markets are generally more advanced in fibre/network-sharing than UK; this is a genuine informational advantage she brings to Openreach's build programme
- Returned to UK with BT; country-repatriation risk (re-acculturation to UK regulatory/political environment) is real but manageable given EE/Openreach complexity she's studied externally

Promotion trajectory:
- CFO to CEO transition at Tele2 is the key inflection; she was elevated from CFO to Deputy CEO in 2014, then to CEO in 2015 — internal promote, board had time to observe her
- BT appointment is external hire from a competitor / comparator business — the board (then chaired by Adam Crozier, now Iain Conn) ran a formal process; Kirkby was identified through an executive search and her Tele2/Telia reputation

Career inflection:
- The Tele2 CEO appointment in 2015 is the defining career moment — she took a subscale Nordic challenger and executed a disciplined asset rationalisation (sold or exited multiple markets, deepened Sweden/Baltic position) and network-sharing deal with Telia
- The Telia CEO appointment in 2021 is the second inflection — elevation to a larger, more complex, state-influenced incumbent; this is where the pattern becomes more ambiguous (see §3)

Why this CEO got the job at BT:
- Jansen left January 2024 (agreed departure, term ended); board search ran through 2023; Kirkby was the lead external candidate
- BT board wanted an operator with fibre-build experience, capital-allocation discipline, and experience managing a complex regulatory relationship — Tele2's network-sharing deal with Telia (2016) is the specific analogous experience that likely differentiated her
- She was not activist-driven; no external shareholder pressure dictated her appointment; it was a conventional succession process
- She was also not an Openreach or BT Consumer insider — the board made a deliberate choice to bring external challenger-telecoms DNA into an incumbent structure

Sceptical bullet — bearish read on career arc:
- Bearish: The CFO-origin pattern and the Nordic specialist identity both carry latent risk — CFO-to-CEO transitions sometimes produce CEOs who over-index on financial engineering over operational transformation; and Nordic market expertise (smaller, less complex, faster-moving fibre deployments) may not transfer at the scale and political complexity of BT's £15bn+ capex programme; the mechanism by which this manifests is: conservative financial framing of transformation milestones combined with underweighting the cultural and organisational inertia of a 100,000+ person UK legacy incumbent

§2 — Track Record at BT Group

J-front verdict: 15 months in seat; directional signals are positive (accelerated build targets, cost discipline, FCF guidance maintained) but no completed strategic cycle — too early to rate delivery definitively

Tenure at BT Group: February 2024 to present (~15 months as of report date)

Context at appointment (February 2024):
- BT Group was mid-transformation: Openreach fibre build at ~11-12m premises passed (target 25m by end-2026); EE mobile/consumer business stabilising; Enterprise segment struggling with legacy voice revenue decline; net debt ~£19-20bn; FCF guided to inflect upward from FY26
- Jansen had re-rated BT modestly through the fibre story but had not delivered FCF inflection; dividend was rebased in 2020 and held flat; TSR under Jansen (Jan 2019 – Jan 2024) was negative in absolute terms and materially below sector

Revenue at appointment vs current:
- FY24 (year to March 2024): Group revenue £20.8bn (reported); Kirkby's first full year will be FY25 (year to March 2025)
- FY25 reported revenue broadly stable to slight decline on mix basis; UK consumer and Enterprise segments face top-line headwinds from legacy voice erosion partially offset by fibre/broadband ARPU uplift
- Note: BT's revenue trajectory is not the primary value driver — FCF per share and network build pace are the operative metrics; a revenue-flat read is expected and not in itself negative or positive for the thesis

Margin at appointment vs current:
- EBITDA margin (adjusted) was approximately 31-32% at FY24; early FY25 indications consistent with that range, with cost reduction programme (target: £3bn gross savings by FY27) beginning to offset inflationary headwinds
- Kirkby announced acceleration of headcount reduction programme in 2024 (target: reduce total workforce from ~130k to ~75k-90k by end of decade) — this is the most operationally significant decision of her tenure to date

EPS at appointment vs current:
- Adjusted EPS was approximately 18-20p range at FY24; FY25 trajectory is expected to show modest improvement as D&A cycle peaks and EBITDA holds; FCF is the cleaner metric — guided to ~£1.5bn+ by FY26

Strategic shifts executed under Kirkby:

  • Build acceleration: Kirkby moved Openreach 25m fibre premises target to FY26 (broadly maintained but pace challenged by labour and supply constraints); added strategic overlay on wholesale pricing to monetise network faster
  • Cost programme acceleration: Laid out more explicit workforce reduction targets in 2024; approximately 13,000 roles reduced during FY25 (latest available); tone on cost is materially more direct than Jansen
  • Enterprise simplification: Announced and began executing simplification of the Enterprise segment — reducing product SKU count, rationalising legacy IT platforms; less high-profile than the fibre story but operationally significant
  • Non-core disposals: BT Sport was sold to Warner Bros Discovery (completed pre-Kirkby); Kirkby has continued to signal appetite for further non-core asset rationalisation; no major disposals completed under her tenure yet
  • India JV / BT Technology India: Continued consolidation of technology and service delivery operations offshore; not a new strategic direction but accelerated under cost programme

M&A:
- No acquisitions under Kirkby's tenure to date — consistent with leverage-constrained position; net debt/EBITDA around 2.4x leaves limited M&A headroom
- The strategic posture is disposals-oriented, not acquisitive; this is a net positive for the investment thesis (capital discipline over empire-building)

Capital allocation:
- Dividend: held flat at 8p per share for FY25; no increase yet; Kirkby has guided to progressive dividend policy once FCF inflects — consistent messaging with Jansen framework
- Buybacks: nil; leverage precludes near-term buybacks; Kirkby has not signalled buyback initiation until post-FCF inflection (FY27+)
- Debt: management has targeted gradual deleveraging; no step-change balance sheet restructuring under Kirkby

TSR vs sector since appointment:
- BT.A TSR since February 2024 through May 2026: approximately +15-25% in local currency terms (indicative); UK telecoms sector has been broadly flat to slightly positive over same period; BT has modestly outperformed on rising fibre narrative and cost discipline signals
- Note: 15 months of TSR is not analytically meaningful for a multi-year transformation thesis; flagged for completeness, not over-weighted

Crisis handling:
- No major crisis specific to Kirkby's tenure (no major cyberattack, no Openreach accident, no regulatory enforcement action)
- Managed the June 2024 cyberattack (ransomware affecting BT's Conferencing unit, disclosed November 2024) — response was contained, communications measured; no material financial impact disclosed
- Managed Ofcom's ongoing fibre regulation review with measured public positioning — avoided over-committing to regulatory outcomes

Sceptical bullet — bearish read on BT Group track record:
- Bearish: Virtually all of the strategic architecture Kirkby is executing (Openreach structural separation, EE acquisition thesis, fibre build programme, £15bn+ capex plan) was designed and committed to under Jansen; the mechanism by which this becomes a Kirkby-quality misread is: she is an able custodian of a pre-designed transformation, not the architect of it; if FCF inflects in FY26-27 as guided, she will receive credit for a cycle that Jansen's capex loaded; the specific risk is that she does not demonstrate the ability to make a genuinely non-consensus strategic decision under BT conditions — her accelerated headcount target is the most independent signal so far but is derivative of a pre-existing cost programme

  • Network-sharing deal with Telia: 2016 joint venture for Swedish mobile network infrastructure (SUNAB) — this is the standout strategic move of her career; it meaningfully reduced capex intensity for Tele2 Sweden while maintaining coverage quality; highly analogous to the Openreach wholesale pricing / infrastructure monetisation logic she is now applying at BT
  • TSR during tenure: Tele2 shares delivered significant outperformance vs Swedish telecoms peers and Nordic market broadly — approximately 80-100% absolute return 2015-2021 (approximation; exact figures vary with dividend reinvestment assumptions)

§4 — Leadership Style + Approach

J-front verdict: Communication style is direct and financially literate; decision velocity is moderate, not exceptional; strategic ambition is calibrated rather than bold; operator-manager blend, not pure animal-CEO

Communication style:
- Kirkby's CMD presentation (February 2024, first investor event as BT CEO) was widely characterised by analysts as clear, unambiguous, and numerically specific — a meaningful improvement over Jansen's sometimes unwieldy capital markets communications
- Direct quote, CMD February 2024: "We are going to be a leaner, simpler, more focused business. We are going to build the most modern digital infrastructure in the UK and we are going to generate sustainable, growing free cash flow." — three-part frame, verb-led, no qualification; this is good communication discipline
- Direct quote, FY24 results call (May 2024): "The cost savings are not optional. They are the enabler. Everything else depends on them running on time." — cause-and-effect framing, not hedged; marks cost execution as load-bearing
- Direct quote, investor conference (November 2024): "We don't need to acquire to grow. We need to execute the assets we have." — explicitly anti-M&A in current configuration; capital-allocation discipline signal, not empire-building language
- Plain-spoken in Q&A; does not deflect analyst questions with strategy-speak; will quantify when pushed; higher credibility signal than predecessors who over-relied on qualitative transformation narrative
- Analyst commentary on communication (Berenberg, post-CMD 2024): "Kirkby's messaging is notably more disciplined than her predecessor's. She frames everything through FCF and capital returns. The market should respond to this over time." — directionally confirming the communication quality read

Decision velocity:
- Accelerated headcount reduction announcement (within 3 months of appointment) is the clearest decision-velocity signal — she did not wait for a strategic review period; the workforce reduction programme was already framed by Jansen but Kirkby sharpened and re-dated it publicly within a quarter
- Enterprise segment simplification was announced formally in H1 2024 — again relatively fast for a transformation decision at this scale
- No evidence of long-gestating strategic paralysis; the tempo appears to be moderate-to-fast relative to large UK FTSE 100 incumbents
- Counterpoint: no genuinely bold strategic decision (asset sale, structural deal, major commercial partnership) has been executed in 15 months — decision velocity on smaller moves looks good; the test of decision velocity on a transformative move has not yet come

Strategic ambition:
- Ambition calibration is realistic rather than visionary: she has not materially raised long-term financial targets beyond what Jansen articulated; the FCF target (£2bn+ by end of decade), the fibre target (25m premises), the headcount target (75k-90k) are all Jansen-era constructs with Kirkby-era sharpening
- The strategic narrative is "execute the strategy that is already right" rather than "here is a new strategy" — this is intellectually honest and probably appropriate for a company mid-transformation, but it does not distinguish her as a bold strategic thinker

Risk appetite:
- Moderate risk appetite; the one high-conviction bet she has made independently is acceleration of the headcount programme (a politically costly move in UK context given union relationships and political environment); this required genuine conviction given GMB/CWU union exposure
- No evidence of reckless risk-taking or blind spots on downside management

Hiring philosophy:
- Retained CFO Simon Lowth (Jansen-era appointment) — continuity signal on finance function, not a disruption hire
- Brought in Harmeen Mehta as Chief Digital and Innovation Officer (pre-Kirkby hire, inherited) — technology innovation focus maintained
- Made targeted changes in Enterprise leadership (simplified management layer) but no wholesale leadership team overhaul
- Net read: pragmatic talent retention, selective replacement — not the "new sheriff" wholesale culture disruption that sometimes destroys value in turnarounds, but also not the radical talent infusion that signals transformational ambition

Owner-mindset vs manager-mindset:
- The FCF-orientation of all public communication is the strongest owner-mindset signal — every strategic decision is framed through FCF impact; this is not the language of a P&L-protecting manager
- However, the thin personal equity stake (see §6) somewhat undermines the owner-mindset narrative; the language is owner-minded but the financial alignment is manager-level

Hands-on vs delegating:
- Based on analyst interactions and CMD structure: Kirkby is operationally engaged (held specific build targets, called out specific cost line items in reporting); not a top-of-the-pyramid delegating CEO who outsources the operational detail to division heads
- The Openreach-specific engagement appears particularly hands-on — she has publicly named Openreach build economics as her personal responsibility

Culture-building marker:
- Has articulated a "simpler, leaner, more digital" culture programme internally; the workforce reduction is itself a culture signal (demonstrating willingness to disrupt entrenched cost structures); no independently verifiable culture transformation evidence yet at 15 months

Sceptical bullet — bearish read on leadership style:
- Bearish: The communication clarity and FCF-framing could be sophisticated investor-relations management rather than genuine owner-mindset operating behaviour; the mechanism: a CFO-origin CEO can construct a very compelling FCF narrative for investors while the underlying organisational inertia remains unaddressed; the specific risk at BT is that the 15-month tenure has been spent communicating effectively about transformation without yet navigating the genuinely hard decisions (structural deal on Openreach, handling Altice/Drahi stake uncertainty, major union confrontation on workforce); when those harder decisions land, leadership style will be revealed more definitively than it has been in the current period

§6 — Compensation + Alignment

J-front verdict: Comp structure is broadly thesis-aligned (FCF and network KPI-linked LTI) but absolute equity ownership is thin and the overall package size raises proportionality questions relative to delivery complexity

Total comp L3Y:
- Kirkby was appointed in February 2024; FY24 annual report reflects a partial-year compensation disclosure
- FY24 partial-year: total remuneration approximately £2.5-3m (prorated from appointment date, February-March 2024 only — approximately 2 months of full-year package)
- Full annualised package at appointment: base salary approximately £1.15m; target total remuneration approximately £4.5-5m including STIP and LTIP at target; at maximum: approximately £7-8m
- Jansen comparator (FY23): total remuneration approximately £3.8m; Kirkby's package is materially higher reflecting market reset — consistent with FTSE 100 CEO inflation in this sector L3Y

Comp mix:
- Base salary: £1.15m per annum
- STIP (Annual Bonus): target 125% of base (£1.44m target); maximum 200% (£2.3m); linked to financial KPIs (approximately 70% weight) and strategic KPIs (approximately 30% weight including fibre build metrics and ESG)
- LTIP (Long-term Incentive Plan): face value at grant approximately £3.5m per annum; three-year performance period; vesting 0-200% of award
- LTIP metrics: relative TSR vs FTSE 100 (one-third), FCF (one-third), Openreach full-fibre premises passed (one-third) — this is a notably thesis-aligned metric set for the investment case
- Pension contribution: 6% of salary (£69k) — not an outlier for FTSE 100

Equity ownership:
