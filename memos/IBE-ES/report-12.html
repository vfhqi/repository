KEY FINDINGS - Net verdict: Iberdrola's value-chain position is structurally favourable across most dimensions — regulated revenue dominates, customers are largely captive via regulatory frameworks, and supplier diversification has improved materially since 2021 [#J]

  • Customer power: Iberdrola's regulated/quasi-regulated revenue base (~75% of EBITDA) structurally neutralises customer concentration risk; end-retail customers have near-zero switching leverage over the regulated network; the residual risk sits in the ~25% competitive/liberalised segments [#J]
  • Supplier dynamics: Wind turbine and solar panel supply chains have transitioned from acute scarcity (2021-23) toward balance (2024-25), with Vestas, Siemens Gamesa, and GE Vernova all flagging easing lead times — a genuine input-cost tailwind for Iberdrola's capex pipeline [#A]
  • Competitor intensity: Competitive pressure is rising in offshore wind development rights and green hydrogen tenders, with Ørsted, RWE, and EDF competing aggressively for the same Atlantic/North Sea lease slots — share-of-pipeline risk is real even if grid revenue is insulated [#A]
  • Pricing leverage: Regulated asset base return formulas and long-term PPA structures (10-15yr) lock in the majority of earnings; near-term rate cases in Spain and the US are the key swing variables on pricing [#J]
  • NWC signal: ❌ Spanish regulatory receivable lag and UK/US contract payment cycles have kept DSO elevated vs European utility median; not a crisis but a structural drag on cash conversion worth monitoring in rate-case years [#A]
  • Cross-chain divergence: Iberdrola management commentary on cost inflation relief is broadly consistent with what turbine OEM suppliers are reporting, but grid connection equipment (transformers, cables) remains tight — a partial divergence worth flagging [#A]
  • Single largest risk: ❌ Spanish regulatory reset (CNMC rate review cycles) can reprice ~30% of EBITDA retroactively; this is not a supplier/customer/competitor risk but a structural state-as-regulator risk that sits above the value-chain frame [#J]
  • Outlook for 18M-3Y EPS: Value-chain dynamics are net supportive — input cost deflation on solar/wind equipment, long-dated PPAs locking in revenue, grid capex earning RAB returns; primary risk is regulatory reset in Spain + US rate case timing [#J]

§1 — Customer Analysis (concentration + relationships)

Iberdrola's customer structure is unusual vs typical industrial or technology companies: the largest single "customer" relationship is with regulatory regimes (RAB-based returns paid via tariffs), not discrete corporate buyers. This fundamentally changes the concentration/churn/satisfaction analytical frame — the sections below distinguish regulated customers (tariff-payers, state-backed offtakers) from competitive-market customers (PPA counterparties, liberalised retail/B2B).

  • Net verdict: Customer concentration risk is structurally low in regulated segments and moderate in the competitive/PPA segment; churn is near-zero in regulated business; PPA counterparty quality is high but increasingly competitive to sign [#J]

  • Customer profile — regulated: The regulated customer is effectively the state or state-delegated regulator in Spain , UK (Ofgem), and US (FERC/state PUCs) — not a churn-able relationship; these "customers" pay a formula-determined return on the RAB, insulating ~55-60% of group EBITDA from commercial customer risk [#A]

  • Sub: Spain networks (Avangrid's NYSEG/RG&E in the US, Red Eléctrica equivalent functions via Iberdrola's distribution assets) operate under multi-year regulatory determinations — Spain's current network tariff framework runs to 2025 with a pending reset

  • Sub: UK (Avangrid's Scottish Power networks, now largely divested to OHL/Macquarie) — UK network divestiture in 2011 reduced Iberdrola's direct regulatory customer exposure in the GB market

  • Customer profile — generation/wholesale: Iberdrola sells power into wholesale markets (Spain, UK, Nordics, Brazil, Mexico) and via bilateral PPAs — these are the "commercial customer" relationships [#A]

  • Sub: PPA counterparties include large industrial corporates (data centres, steel, chemicals), utilities buying green certificates, and sovereign/quasi-sovereign offtakers in LatAm

  • Sub: US offshore wind PPAs (e.g. Vineyard Wind, though troubled) and onshore PPAs with hyperscalers represent the highest-growth PPA segment — counterparty credit quality is investment-grade

  • Top-1 concentration: No single commercial customer represents a disclosed material % of group revenues — regulatory revenue is diffuse by nature; largest identifiable PPA counterparty blocs are US hyperscalers and Spanish industrial bilaterals [#A]

  • Sub: Iberdrola does not disclose a "top customer = X% of revenue" figure in its annual reports, consistent with the regulated-utility model where no individual customer is material

  • Top-5 concentration: Estimated top-5 commercial PPA counterparties represent under 10% of group revenue — very low vs industrials sector median of ~25-35%; regulated tariff diffusion means true concentration is near zero at the end-customer level [#A]

  • Sub: Peer base-rate for European utilities with heavy regulated exposure: customer concentration is structurally lower than for competitive IPPs or energy retailers — Iberdrola's profile is towards the low-concentration end

  • Contract length — PPA: Iberdrola's standard PPA length is 10-15 years for new renewable projects; existing contracted book has weighted average remaining life of ~12-13 years per Iberdrola Investor Day disclosures (2023) [#A]

  • Sub: Long-dated PPAs with IG corporates are near-equivalent in risk profile to regulated revenue — the "effective regulated %" is arguably higher than the reported ~55-60%

  • Multi-year vs spot: Iberdrola explicitly targets a high "contracted/regulated" share — management guided 85%+ of EBITDA from regulated or contracted sources at the 2023 Capital Markets Day; spot/merchant exposure is intentionally minimised [#A]

  • Sub: Merchant tail (sub-15% of EBITDA) is exposed to Iberian and UK/Nordic power price cycles — the 2021-22 European power price spike inflated this segment; normalisation reduces it

  • Customer churn — regulated: Effectively zero in regulated networks; RAB-based returns are contractual/regulatory entitlements, not commercially won or lost [#A]

  • Customer churn — PPA/competitive: No publicly disclosed PPA terminations or counterparty defaults at material scale in Iberdrola's portfolio; the one exception is the troubled Vineyard Wind project (US offshore, involving Avangrid) where the PPA/regulatory framework stalled, not a customer credit event [#A]

  • Satisfaction signal — PPA counterparties: Data centre and hyperscaler customers (Amazon, Google, Microsoft all known PPA market participants with Iberdrola or its affiliates) remain active buyers of European renewable capacity — demand signals from those corporates' sustainability reporting are positive [#A]

  • Sub: No public commentary from major PPA counterparties expressing dissatisfaction with Iberdrola specifically — though contract renegotiation requests are a known sector dynamic in markets where power prices have fallen below PPA strike prices

  • Satisfaction signal — retail/B2B (competitive): Spain and UK liberalised retail and B2B segments show elevated price sensitivity post-2022-23 price spike — customer complaints to CNMC and Ofgem spiked in that period, though directed at the energy market broadly rather than Iberdrola specifically [#A]

  • ⚠️ Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on customer relationships centres on PPA repricing risk — if wholesale power prices in Spain/UK stay structurally below the strike prices locked into long-dated PPAs, counterparties face incentive to renegotiate or invoke force-majeure/change-in-law clauses; apparent retention could be late-cycle (locked in during high-price environment) rather than structural; the 10-15yr PPA book masks optionality the corporate buyer embedded at origination that may not show up in headline contracted % figures [#J]

§2 — Switching Costs + Pricing Leverage

  • Net verdict: Iberdrola's pricing leverage is high in regulated segments (formula-determined) and moderate in PPAs (long-dated lock-in); switching costs in retail/B2B are low but that segment is not EBITDA-critical [#J]

  • Switching cost — regulated network: Effectively infinite — regulated networks are natural monopolies; no customer can switch away from the physical distribution/transmission infrastructure; this is structural not commercial [#A]

  • Switching cost — PPA (project-specific): High during term — PPA counterparties who signed 12-15yr contracts face early termination penalties (typically 5-15% of remaining contract value per industry standard); mid-contract exit is costly enough to make switching economically unattractive except in extreme price divergence scenarios [#A]

  • Sub: New PPA origination (counterparty switching to a competitor for the next contract at renewal) is the real switching risk — and that is a competitive bidding dynamic, not a within-contract dynamic

  • Switching cost — retail/B2B competitive: Low — Spanish and UK liberalised retail markets have designed-in switching; Ofgem and CNMC both mandate easy switching; this is the structurally weak pricing segment [#A]

  • Sub: However, retail/B2B competitive is sub-5% of group EBITDA in Iberdrola's current portfolio mix — the switching risk is real but not financially material at group level

  • Pricing pass-through — regulated: RAB return formulas automatically reset for inflation (CPI/RPI-linked in UK, regulatory review periods in Spain/US) — inflation pass-through is embedded by design; Iberdrola does not need to negotiate pass-through with customers [#A]

  • Sub: Spain's regulatory framework uses an allowed ROE on RAB; the key risk is the regulator reducing the allowed ROE at each review cycle rather than customer resistance to pricing

  • Pricing pass-through — PPA: Fixed-price PPAs do NOT pass through input cost inflation directly — the PPA strike price is set at origination; if construction/financing costs rise post-contract, Iberdrola absorbs the squeeze; this is the inverse of a typical pass-through situation [#A]

  • Sub: Iberdrola's mitigation: contracts post-2022 include higher strike prices (reflecting elevated capex/capex-inflation environment) — the inflation risk is therefore more acute for the legacy book than the new-vintage book

  • Renewal pricing: PPA renewal dynamics are a genuine unknown for contracts originating in the 2010-2015 period — those were signed at lower strike prices in a lower-rate environment; renewal will occur in a higher-rate environment which should support renewal pricing [#A]

  • Sub: Competitor capacity additions (Ørsted, RWE, EDF all expanding) will increase supply of renewables at renewal — pricing pressure at renewal is a 5-10yr risk, not an immediate one

  • Competitive bidding — new capacity: ❌ New PPA origination is increasingly competitive; Iberdrola competes with European IPPs, oil-and-gas majors with renewables arms (Shell, BP, TotalEnergies), and infrastructure funds for the same corporate offtaker demand — pricing discipline across the industry is under pressure [#A]

  • Sub: Spanish RECORE and UK CfD auction mechanisms cap the upside on new renewable PPAs by introducing competitive bidding discipline — Iberdrola cannot command a premium vs peers in these processes

  • Customer pushback — regulated: Spanish consumer groups and large industrial users have lobbied CNMC for lower network tariffs — the regulator is the intermediary, insulating Iberdrola from direct customer pushback; risk is regulatory concession, not customer departure [#A]

  • Concession signal: No publicly disclosed material PPA concessions or contract renegotiations in Iberdrola's portfolio through mid-2025 — management has not flagged any forced pricing concessions; IG counterparty base suggests low near-term default risk [#A]

  • RFP pressure: Corporate PPA RFP processes are becoming more sophisticated — hyperscalers now run competitive auctions for green power procurement, moving from bilateral negotiations (which favoured established developers like Iberdrola) toward auction dynamics [#A]

  • ⚠️ Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on switching costs and pricing leverage turns on the distinction between in-contract stickiness (high) and at-renewal competitiveness (deteriorating) — management's contracted % metric flatters current positioning but does not reflect the competitive environment at the margin where new volume is won; if new PPA strike prices are set in a hyper-competitive environment, the high-quality legacy book rolls off into a lower-margin replacement book over a 10-15yr horizon [#J]

§3 — Supplier Analysis (concentration + input costs)

  • Net verdict: Iberdrola's supplier landscape has shifted from acute shortage/leverage in 2021-23 toward balance/moderate tailwind in 2024-25; wind turbine and solar module supply is easing; grid equipment (transformers, HV cables) remains tight — the one genuine residual supplier risk [#J]

  • Critical input — wind turbines: Iberdrola's primary turbine suppliers are Vestas (Denmark), Siemens Gamesa (Spain/Germany, now fully owned by Siemens Energy), and GE Vernova ; these three cover the vast majority of Iberdrola's onshore and offshore wind procurement [#A]

  • Sub: Siemens Gamesa has been the highest-risk turbine supplier link — Siemens Energy had to receive a €15bn German state-backed guarantee in late 2023 following Gamesa's blade quality failures and financial distress; supply continuity risk has since stabilised

  • Supplier concentration — turbines: High concentration in wind turbines — effectively a three-supplier global market for utility-scale turbines; Iberdrola cannot fully de-risk by spreading across a larger supplier pool [#A]

  • Sub: This is sector-wide — all large European offshore wind developers face the same three-supplier dynamic; Iberdrola is not uniquely exposed but cannot escape it

  • Input cost trend — wind turbines: Vestas and GE Vernova have both reported easing supply chain pressure and returning to positive order book margin guidance in 2024-25 earnings calls — turbine pricing peaked in 2022-23 and is now plateauing/declining slightly for new contracts [#A]

  • Sub: Vestas Q4 2024 earnings: margins recovering to 4-5% EBIT range vs negative in 2022; lead times improving from 24+ months toward 18 months — positive read-through for Iberdrola's capex cost per MW

  • Input cost trend — solar modules: Strong deflation tailwind; Chinese manufacturing overcapacity has driven utility-scale solar module prices down >50% from 2022 peaks to ~$0.10-0.12/W in 2024-25; this is a meaningful capex cost reduction for Iberdrola's solar pipeline [#A]

  • Sub: US tariff risk (IRA, Section 201/301 tariffs) partially offsets this for Avangrid/US-based projects; in Spain and Brazil, Chinese module pricing benefits flow through more directly

  • Supplier concentration — solar modules: Lower concentration risk than wind; global solar module supply is highly competitive with multiple Chinese and non-Chinese manufacturers; Iberdrola is a price-taker but benefits from the competitive dynamics [#A]

  • Critical input — grid equipment: ❌ Transformers and high-voltage cables remain supply-constrained globally; lead times for large power transformers extended to 2-3 years in 2024 vs 12-18 months pre-pandemic; this is a genuine bottleneck for Iberdrola's network capex and renewable connection programmes [#A]

  • Sub: Key suppliers: ABB, Siemens Energy (grid division), Hitachi Energy, Nexans, Prysmian — all have flagged order backlogs and extended delivery timelines in their 2024-25 earnings commentary

  • Sub: This creates a pacing constraint on Iberdrola's grid investment deployment — not a margin squeeze per se (regulated capex earns RAB return regardless of when it's deployed) but a revenue/earnings timing risk

  • Geopolitical risk — solar: EU anti-dumping investigations into Chinese solar modules (active in 2024-25) create policy uncertainty around the cost structure for Iberdrola's European solar pipeline; if tariffs are imposed at scale, capex cost per MW rises [#A]

  • Sub: Iberdrola's geographic diversification (Spain, Brazil, US, UK, Germany) provides partial natural hedge against any single jurisdiction's supply policy

  • Geopolitical risk — rare earths/magnets: Offshore wind turbines (particularly direct-drive designs) use rare earth permanent magnets; Chinese export restrictions on rare earths (2023-25 escalation) create a longer-term supply risk for turbine manufacturers which would feed through to turbine pricing/availability [#A]

  • Sub: Near-term impact is indirect (through turbine OEM cost structures) rather than direct Iberdrola procurement risk; flag for DD

  • Long-term partnership: Iberdrola has disclosed preferred-supplier framework agreements with Vestas and Siemens Gamesa for multi-year turbine procurement — this provides some volume security in exchange for reduced price optionality [#A]

  • Sub: Preferred supplier arrangements can become a double-edged sword if a preferred OEM suffers quality or financial distress (Siemens Gamesa 2022-23 was the live example)

  • Supplier bargaining power — current: Declining vs 2022 peak; turbine OEMs competed aggressively for large pipeline commitments from developers like Iberdrola when their own margins recovered — supply/demand balance has normalised; grid equipment OEMs retain higher bargaining power due to persistent bottlenecks [#A]

  • ⚠️ Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on supplier dynamics is that the apparent easing in turbine supply is real but could reverse quickly if the European offshore wind development pipeline accelerates (new EU offshore auctions, UK Round 4/5 buildout) — re-tightening in 2026-27 is plausible; grid equipment constraints are structural (manufacturing capacity investment cycles are 3-5yr) and may not ease within the 18M-3Y investment horizon; a scenario where capex costs re-inflate while PPA strike prices are set competitively would squeeze project IRRs without an easy pass-through mechanism [#J]

§4 — Competitor Analysis (market share + responses)

  • Net verdict: Iberdrola holds a top-3 global renewables developer position but faces intensifying competition for development pipeline from both European utility peers and new entrants; grid/network moats remain insulated from competitive challenge [#J]

  • L3Y share trend — renewables development: Broadly stable at top-3 globally by installed renewables capacity; Iberdrola reported ~62 GW installed renewables at end-2024 vs ~43 GW in 2021 — absolute growth strong, but competitors (Enel, NextEra, Ørsted, RWE) have also grown their portfolios [#A]

  • Sub: Absent injected cohort data, base-rate estimate: Iberdrola holds ~5-6% of European utility-owned renewables installed base vs Enel (~6-7%) and NextEra (~7-8% globally if included)

  • Geographic share — Spain: Dominant position in Spanish electricity networks (RAB-based) — ~25% of Spanish distribution network assets; this share is not competitively contestable [#A]

  • Geographic share — US (Avangrid): Avangrid is a mid-scale US utility (ranked ~12-15 by regulated asset base among US IOUs); US offshore wind competition is intense — Vineyard Wind setbacks have ceded ground to competitors [#A]

  • Sub: Vineyard Wind (Avangrid-Iberdrola JV) has been one of the most publicly troubled US offshore wind projects — blade failure (Siemens Gamesa blades), federal review uncertainty — competitors (BP/Equinor, Ørsted, Dominion) have used this to differentiate their US offshore programmes

  • Geographic share — Brazil: Neoenergia (Iberdrola ~53% stake) is a top-3 Brazilian electricity utility by distribution footprint — regulated concession model similar to Spain; competitive challenge is limited in core distribution [#A]

  • Segment share — offshore wind development: ❌ Offshore wind lease competition has intensified sharply since 2022; Iberdrola/Avangrid lost the New England offshore lease auctions to Vineyard Wind, and the subsequent project difficulties have impaired their US offshore credibility vs Ørsted and BP/Equinor [#A]

  • Sub: European offshore: Iberdrola competes with Ørsted, RWE, Vattenfall, and now Shell/BP in North Sea lease auctions — no single developer dominates; lease prices have risen materially since 2021

  • Competitive response — capacity: RWE announced a €50bn investment programme through 2030 in renewables/networks; Enel and NextEra are similarly expanding; the competitive capacity additions are real and will increase supply of renewable power in all key Iberdrola markets [#A]

  • Competitive response — M&A: Sector consolidation is active; Brookfield/EDF transactions, Macquarie infrastructure acquisitions, and private capital (KKR, Blackstone) entering utility-scale renewables — Iberdrola faces competition not just from listed utilities but from well-capitalised private capital [#A]

  • Competitive response — price: Pricing rationality is deteriorating at the margin in competitive PPA markets; some competitors (particularly private-capital-backed developers with lower cost of capital targets) have signed PPAs at prices that imply thin margins — this has pressured market PPA clearing prices [#A]

  • Innovation rate: Iberdrola is not a technology innovator (it is a project developer/operator); R&D intensity is low vs industrial companies — appropriate for a regulated utility; the technology innovation risk comes from disruption of the energy system (storage, distributed generation, demand response) rather than product R&D [#A]

  • Sub: Iberdrola's innovation exposure is through project risk (new offshore turbine technology, floating offshore, green hydrogen) — these are pilot/early-stage and represent a small fraction of capex

  • New entrant threat — grid: Near-zero competitive threat to regulated network monopolies; no new entrant can build a competing distribution network in Spain or Brazil — regulatory moats are structural [#A]

  • Disruption signal — distributed generation: Rooftop solar + battery storage growth is a long-term structural headwind to regulated distribution network throughput volumes; Spain's self-consumption regulatory framework (post-2019 "sun tax" removal) has accelerated distributed generation — this reduces kWh throughput on Iberdrola's networks over time [#A]

  • Pricing discipline — sector-wide: Mixed; regulated segment is disciplined (formula-driven); competitive renewable development is showing signs of margin pressure as more capital chases fewer high-quality sites [#A]

  • ⚠️ Sceptical bullet: The bearish competitive read is that Iberdrola's pipeline growth story increasingly depends on winning competitive auctions (CfD, RECORE, US state processes) at prices that are being driven down by rational-to-aggressive competitor bidding; the "top-3 global renewables developer" framing flatters the position — scale in installed base does not automatically translate to scale in new wins; competitors with lower cost of capital (infrastructure funds, sovereign wealth vehicles) can outbid on price while still meeting their return thresholds; over a 5-7yr horizon this could compress Iberdrola's returns on new capital deployed [#J]

§5 — Distributor + Channel Analysis

  • Net verdict: Iberdrola's distribution model is predominantly direct-to-end-market via regulated networks and bilateral PPA structures; intermediary/channel power is minimal; the relevant "channel" risk is regulatory/government rather than commercial distributor leverage [#J]

  • Direct-to-customer: Dominant model for regulated network and generation segments; electricity distribution is inherently direct (physical grid to end consumer); no distributors in the traditional industrial-company sense [#A]

  • Sub: Iberian and LatAm regulated distribution: Iberdrola/Neoenergia owns the physical network and delivers directly to ~35m end-point customers across its concession areas

  • Indirect — energy supply (competitive retail): In competitive retail markets (Spain, UK historically), Iberdrola's supply arm acts as an intermediary between wholesale generation and end consumers; this creates a thin-margin channel step with limited pricing leverage [#A]

  • Sub: Iberdrola's competitive retail exposure has been deliberately reduced over time — UK supply exit (2011 network sale) and focus on regulated/contracted revenue is the strategic response to channel margin compression in retail

  • Platform — energy aggregators/brokers: B2B energy procurement is increasingly mediated by energy management consultants and aggregators (Schneider Electric Energy Management, Engie Energy Management, etc.) who run RFP processes on behalf of large industrial buyers — this intermediary layer reduces Iberdrola's direct pricing leverage in the B2B segment [#A]

  • Sub: Iberdrola's competitive PPA business interfaces with these aggregators in Spain and Germany — the aggregator layer is not dominant but is growing

  • Channel concentration: Not applicable in the traditional sense for regulated networks — the "channel" is the regulatory determination; for PPA business, bilateral relationships with named IG corporates are the primary route, not a concentrated intermediary [#A]

  • Channel evolution — direct PPA structuring: Trend toward direct bilateral PPAs (rather than through brokers) with hyperscalers and large industrials is positive for Iberdrola; deepening relationships with data centre developers (Amazon Web Services, Google, Microsoft Azure) create semi-permanent preferred-supplier status [#A]

  • Sub: Data centre demand growth is a structural demand tailwind — not a channel risk but a channel opportunity; Iberdrola has explicitly targeted this segment in its 2025-2030 strategic plan

  • Disintermediation risk: Some large industrials are moving toward self-generation (own rooftop solar, own wind behind-the-meter) — this is a form of disintermediation of the regulated network supply relationship; long-term volume risk for distribution networks, though short/medium term is not material [#A]

  • Channel margin: Regulated network return is formula-determined (RAB × allowed WACC) — not a margin subject to channel pressure; generation margin depends on PPA strike price vs LCOE — not intermediated [#A]

  • DTC shift: The sector-wide trend is toward longer-dated, direct bilateral relationships between renewable developers and large offtakers — a positive for Iberdrola's direct model [#A]

  • ⚠️ Sceptical bullet: The bearish channel read is that the energy aggregator/intermediary layer in B2B procurement is growing faster than management commentary suggests — as large industrials increasingly outsource energy procurement decisions to specialist advisors, Iberdrola loses the relationship advantage it has vs pure-commodity competitors; simultaneously, the self-generation disintermediation trend for industrial users could reduce long-run demand for Iberdrola's distribution network throughput in Spain and the US, compressing RAB-return revenue in ways that are hard to recover in rate cases [#J]

§6 — Cross-Value-Chain Signals (LOAD-BEARING for forward credibility)

  • Net verdict: Cross-chain signals are broadly consistent with Iberdrola management commentary on capex cost trajectory and demand pipeline; the one notable divergence is grid equipment availability (OEM calls flagging tighter delivery than Iberdrola's capex deployment timeline implies) [#J]

  • Supplier — Vestas earnings signal: Vestas Q3/Q4 2024 earnings commentary: order intake recovering strongly (+40% YoY in H2 2024), margins improving, lead times normalising — positive read-through for Iberdrola's wind turbine cost and delivery reliability [#A]

  • Sub: Vestas flagged that large-frame offshore turbine (15MW+) supply is tighter than onshore — directly relevant to Iberdrola's offshore pipeline

  • Supplier — Siemens Energy/Gamesa signal: Siemens Energy Q1/Q2 2025 calls: Gamesa restructuring substantially complete, blade quality programme on track, offshore deliveries resuming; however, management flagged residual warranty provision risk on legacy blade portfolio — Iberdrola has exposure to this legacy fleet [#A]

  • Sub: Siemens Energy stock price recovery (+100%+ from 2023 lows) and restored order book suggest the financial distress risk has passed — supply continuity risk is substantially lower than in 2022-23

  • Supplier — GE Vernova signal: GE Vernova 2024 IPO and subsequent earnings: Onshore wind profitable, offshore wind on a defined path to profitability (flagging 2025 as the inflection year); grid division growing strongly with strong order book — both signals are positive for Iberdrola's US-facing capex programme [#A]

  • Supplier — grid equipment (Hitachi Energy, ABB, Nexans, Prysmian): ❌ All four named grid equipment OEMs have reported order backlogs of 2-3 years in their 2024-25 earnings commentary; Hitachi Energy specifically cited transformer delivery times of 24-36 months; this diverges from Iberdrola's capex deployment guidance which implies smoother near-term grid investment pacing [#A]

  • Sub: Prysmian and Nexans both flagged cable manufacturing capacity at full utilisation through 2026-27; offshore wind cable and HVDC cable are the most constrained sub-segments — relevant for Iberdrola's UK/Northern European offshore programme

  • Customer — hyperscaler demand signal: Amazon, Google, and Microsoft have all disclosed material renewable energy procurement targets in their 2024-25 sustainability reports; AWS specifically flagged 100% renewable matching target driving multi-GW PPA procurement programmes in Europe — strong structural demand signal for Iberdrola's European PPA pipeline [#A]

  • Sub: Microsoft's 2030 carbon-negative pledge and Google's 24/7 carbon-free energy target are creating demand for hourly-matched renewables — Iberdrola's diversified portfolio (solar + wind + hydro) positions it well for hourly-matching PPAs vs pure-solar competitors

  • Customer — industrial energy demand (Spain/Germany): European industrial energy demand recovery has been sluggish post-2022 price shock; Spanish industrial production data (2024) shows modest recovery but below 2019 levels in energy-intensive sectors; this softens the near-term demand tailwind for Iberdrola's Spanish merchant generation [#A]

  • Customer — Neoenergia/Brazil signal: Brazilian electricity demand growing at 3-4% per annum (2023-24 data), driven by industrial recovery and data centre buildout; Neoenergia's regulated concessions benefit directly — Brazil is the most positive demand-side signal in Iberdrola's portfolio [#A]

  • Competitor — Ørsted earnings signal: Ørsted's 2023-24 impairments on US offshore wind (DKK 28.4bn write-down) signal sector-wide cost/return pressure in US offshore; this is a negative read-through for Iberdrola's Avangrid offshore programme — if Ørsted with superior offshore expertise cannot make US offshore work at current strike prices, Iberdrola faces the same dynamic [#A]

  • Competitor — RWE signal: RWE's 2024-25 capex acceleration commentary (€50bn through 2030) confirms aggressive competitive posture in onshore wind/solar in Germany and US — RWE is a more direct competitive threat in continental European PPA markets than Ørsted [#A]

  • Competitor — Enel signal: Enel's 2024 strategic plan (retreating from some international markets, refocusing on Italy, Spain, LatAm) creates some competitive breathing room for Iberdrola in Spain — Enel is reducing capex ambition, not expanding, in Iberdrola's core markets [#A]

  • Sub: Enel's partial retreat is a mild positive for competitive dynamics in Spain and Brazil specifically

  • Industry expert — green hydrogen signal: Expert commentary (from sector consultants, not AS network — no live access) on green hydrogen is consistently sceptical; cost of green hydrogen remains 3-5x unsubsidised parity with grey hydrogen; Iberdrola's green hydrogen ambitions (Puertollano, Palos de la Frontera) face the same structural economics challenge [#I]

  • Sub: Iberdrola has ~200MW of electrolysis capacity planned/in-construction; this is small relative to total capex and therefore not a make-or-break risk — more of a capital allocation optionality question
