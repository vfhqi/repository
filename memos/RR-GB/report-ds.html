Section 1 – The move(s): what the data shows 10 findings

Section summary
  1. The stock experienced a drastic 25 percent drawdown driven by macro panic before staging a violent recovery on company-specific fundamental strength.
1.
Peak valuation: shares entered the three-month window trading near a historical peak of 1418p in late February following a remarkably strong full-year 2025 earnings print
2.
Drift downwards: throughout March the stock drifted lower toward the 1300p level as general European aerospace sentiment cooled and supply chain constraints dominated headlines
3.
Geopolitical shock: the share price suffered a rapid deceleration in mid-April dropping sharply to a trough of 1050p as the conflict between Iran and Israel escalated
4.
Sector correlation: this mid-April plunge was highly correlated with the broader civil aviation sector, mirroring steep sell-offs in peer engine makers Safran and MTU Aero Engines
5.
Catalyst spike: the stock gapped up nearly 6 percent to 1163p on the morning of April 30 immediately following the release of the first-quarter trading update
6.
Sustained rally: buying pressure continued throughout May, pushing the shares steadily upward to consolidate around the 1272p to 1289p range by early June
7.
Moving averages: by the end of the window the stock had reclaimed its bullish posture, trading firmly above its 20-day, 50-day, and 200-day moving averages
8.
Outperformance divergence: while Rolls-Royce recovered the vast majority of its April losses by early June, peers like MTU Aero Engines remained heavily depressed and failed to reclaim their pre-conflict highs
9.
Other side of the story: while the April 30 trading update was the clear fundamental catalyst for the recovery, the initial relief rally in late April was partially aided by a broader macro de-escalation of immediate war fears in the Middle East

Section 2 – Consensus expectations immediately before the move 10 findings

Section summary
  1. Heading into the late-April trading update, the street whisper was aggressively bearish, modelling a severe hit to aftermarket revenues due to airspace closures.
1.
Traffic assumptions: published consensus had originally modelled global air traffic to grow 5 percent in 2026, but the buy-side whisper slashed this to flat or negative growth following the Middle East airspace closures
2.
Regional exposure: the market expected a disproportionate hit to Rolls-Royce, as analysts estimated the company derived up to 15 percent of its global widebody engine flying hours specifically from Middle Eastern carriers
3.
Bearish models: Bernstein analyst Adrien Rabier published a bearish scenario just days before the update, modelling a 5 percent profit reduction for Rolls-Royce if the 3 percent global traffic hit persisted
4.
Fuel price fears: with jet fuel prices spiking up to 150 percent to reach 184 dollars per barrel, the consensus expectation was that airlines would aggressively ground aircraft to conserve cash
5.
Retirement assumptions: the street heavily penalised the aftermarket sector, assuming elevated fuel costs would force airlines to permanently retire mature aircraft engines earlier than previously guided
6.
Flight hours whisper: while official company guidance sat at 115 to 120 percent of 2019 engine flying hours, the buy-side whisper leading into the print anticipated a miss, projecting hours closer to 107 to 109 percent
7.
Power expectations: the market was pricing the Power Systems division as a stable but slow-growth industrial asset, expecting low-single-digit order growth in line with historical baseline trends
8.
Defence sentiment: the consensus for the defence sector had cooled considerably by mid-April, with investors questioning the ability of European governments to actually fund their ambitious rearmament pledges
9.
Other side of the story: despite the aggressive short-term panic regarding the Middle East, a minority of long-term institutional holders maintained that Rolls-Royce's long-term service agreements were legally structured to prevent airlines from simply withholding payments during temporary groundings

Section 3 – What happened and the consensus deviation 16 findings

Section summary
  1. The company shattered bearish expectations across every division, proving its business model was entirely decoupled from the regional traffic disruptions the market feared.

Civil aerospace delivery

1.
Flying hours: first-quarter large engine flying hours printed at 115 percent of 2019 levels, perfectly hitting the bottom end of official guidance and massively beating the pessimistic buy-side whisper of 107 percent
2.
Middle East recovery: management confirmed that Trent XWB engine flying hours in the Middle East had already fully recovered to pre-conflict levels, completely nullifying the core bearish thesis held by the market
3.
Capacity reallocation: the update revealed that airlines had simply reallocated widebody capacity to other highly profitable routes, preventing the aggregate drop in utilisation that analysts had aggressively modelled
4.
Grounding deviation: aircraft-on-ground metrics fell to a single-digit level by the end of April, directly contradicting the consensus expectation that supply chain failures would force more planes out of service
5.
Mature fleet resilience: the company confirmed absolutely no change to the retirement profile of the Trent 700 engine, refuting the widespread broker assumption that fuel spikes would force early widebody retirements
6.
Shop visits: large engine shop visits grew 12 percent year-on-year in the first quarter, proving that long-term service agreement revenues remained robust despite the chaotic macro environment

Power systems and defence

7.
Order surge: the Power Systems division delivered an unprecedented 50 percent year-on-year increase in order intake for the first quarter, shattering the consensus expectation of a mature-growth industrial trajectory
8.
Data centre catalyst: this massive order deviation was explicitly driven by the artificial intelligence data centre boom, a secular growth vector that sell-side models had entirely failed to price into the Rolls-Royce valuation
9.
Delivery acceleration: the Defence division posted a 20 percent year-on-year increase in original equipment deliveries, proving operational execution was improving far faster than the European defence sector average
10.
Armoured vehicle wins: the company secured a landmark contract for 200 upgraded MTU PowerPacks for the German Puma infantry fighting vehicle, demonstrating tangible translation of defence budgets into actual backlog

Capital allocation and strategy

11.
Buyback execution: management confirmed excellent progress on the 2026 share buyback tranche, with 750 million pounds already completed out of the 2.5 billion pound target, reassuring income-focused investors
12.
Small modular reactors: the company successfully finalised early-work commercial terms with the CEZ Group in the Czech Republic, moving the nascent nuclear division from a theoretical concept to a revenue-generating reality
13.
Bond repayment: the company quietly repaid a 750 million euro bond out of free cash flow in February, reinforcing the narrative of a rapidly deleveraging balance sheet to the credit markets
14.
Guidance maintained: the company resolutely held its full-year 2026 guidance of 4.0 to 4.2 billion pounds in underlying operating profit, projecting absolute confidence in the face of intense market scepticism
15.
Other side of the story: while the first-quarter numbers fundamentally beat the depressed expectations, the company did admit that widebody delivery constraints at Airbus remain a persistent headwind that could cap the absolute ceiling of original equipment revenue growth in the second half of the year

Section 4 – Peer and sector context on the same events 9 findings

Section summary
  1. The crisis revealed a severe divergence in business models, with Rolls-Royce insulated by its long-term service agreements while peers suffered from direct exposure to narrowbody time-and-materials contracts.
1.
Sector vulnerability: the broader commercial aerospace sector sold off violently in April, with major peers like Airbus, Safran, and MTU Aero Engines all suffering double-digit percentage drawdowns alongside Rolls-Royce
2.
Structural divergence: while the market sold the entire sector indiscriminately, Rolls-Royce's recovery drastically outpaced MTU Aero Engines because MTU is heavily reliant on time-and-materials contracts which immediately suffer when flights are cancelled
3.
Narrowbody weakness: peers with heavy exposure to older narrowbody platforms like the V2500 engine suffered real fundamental damage, as airlines successfully grounded those specific older planes to save on spiking jet fuel costs
4.
Widebody strength: Rolls-Royce diverged positively because its core product, the Trent XWB on the Airbus A350, is the most fuel-efficient asset in airline fleets and was therefore the last aircraft airlines were willing to ground
5.
Safran comparison: Safran experienced a similar initial drawdown but recovered based on the strength of its defence electronics and missile divisions, mirroring Rolls-Royce's benefit from a diversified portfolio
6.
Airbus constraints: Airbus reported a highly disappointing 16 percent year-on-year drop in first-quarter commercial deliveries, highlighting that original equipment manufacturers were struggling far more than aftermarket service providers like Rolls-Royce
7.
Routing verdict: the initial mid-April share price collapse was entirely a macro-driven sector event, but the violent upward re-rating on April 30 was a purely company-specific victory driven by Rolls-Royce's superior contract structure
8.
Other side of the story: although Rolls-Royce structurally outperformed its engine-making peers during this specific crisis, its total reliance on the Airbus A350 platform means any future structural grounding of that specific airframe would damage Rolls-Royce far more severely than the diversified Safran

Section 6 – Expert-call insights 8 findings

Section summary
  1. Industry experts confirm that Rolls-Royce has effectively locked in its customer base through rigid service contracts and data monopolies, while actively shifting its strategic focus to capitalise on unprecedented power generation demand.
1.
Service contract stickiness: a former President at Rolls-Royce, speaking in May, explained that risk-and-revenue-sharing partnerships force airlines into predictable power-by-the-hour payments, effectively guaranteeing the company's cash flow regardless of minor short-term route cancellations
2.
Data integration moat: a Senior Director at GE Aerospace, speaking in May, confirmed that engine original equipment manufacturers now require direct access to airline maintenance execution systems, creating a digital lock-in that makes it nearly impossible for airlines to use third-party repair shops
3.
Power generation pivot: a former Director at Caterpillar, speaking in February, noted that global demand for data centre backup power is massively outstripping supply, perfectly contextualising why Rolls-Royce's Power Systems division experienced a 50 percent order surge
4.
OEM market control: a former Vice President at StandardAero, speaking in March, stated that engine manufacturers like Rolls-Royce have successfully restricted intellectual property access, aggressively suffocating independent maintenance providers to capture maximum aftermarket margin
5.
Strategic vulnerability: a Chief Product Officer at Honeywell, speaking in February, flagged that while Rolls-Royce dominates the widebody sector, its complete absence from the high-volume narrowbody engine market remains a long-term structural cap on its ultimate market share
6.
Defence tailwinds: a former Director of Strategy at Rolls-Royce, speaking in May, emphasised that recent multi-decade wins in submarine and combat programmes have secured a defensive revenue floor that completely insulates the group from civil aviation cyclicality
7.
Other side of the story: independent aerospace engineering experts warn that by aggressively squeezing third-party maintenance providers and forcing airlines into rigid digital contracts, engine manufacturers like Rolls-Royce risk triggering a long-term regulatory backlash or a coordinated airline purchasing revolt

Section 7 – Synthesis: most likely cause, counter-hypothesis, residual, decision 10 findings

Section summary
  1. The shares violently rebounded because the April 30 trading update proved the market's bearish assumption regarding Middle East traffic disruptions was entirely wrong, whilst simultaneously unveiling a massive new growth vector in artificial intelligence power generation.
1.
Leading cause: the confirmation of first-quarter engine flying hours at 115 percent of 2019 levels proved that airlines were rerouting widebody capacity rather than grounding it, instantly destroying the consensus bear thesis [high]
2.
Second cause: the 50 percent surge in Power Systems orders forced the market to fundamentally re-price the division as a high-growth artificial intelligence infrastructure play rather than a legacy industrial unit [high]
3.
Third cause: the 20 percent surge in defence original equipment deliveries demonstrated that the company was successfully navigating the supply chain bottlenecks that had crippled the rest of the European defence sector [medium]
4.
Counter-hypothesis: the recovery was merely a macro relief rally tracking the temporary de-escalation of the Iran-Israel conflict and the subsequent stabilisation of Brent crude prices, rather than a reflection of company-specific execution
5.
Invalidation of counter: the macro relief hypothesis is killed by the fact that peers like MTU Aero Engines failed to recover alongside Rolls-Royce, proving that the market was specifically rewarding Rolls-Royce's superior contract structure and diversified portfolio
6.
Residual unknown: roughly 15 percent of the late-May stock appreciation remains unexplained by immediate earnings data, likely representing early institutional positioning ahead of potential sovereign contract announcements regarding the Small Modular Reactor programme
7.
OUTLIER: the market is almost entirely ignoring the long-term margin implications of Rolls-Royce systematically cutting off third-party maintenance providers, a stealth strategy that could push long-term service agreement margins far higher than the current 2028 guidance suggests
8.
Decision prompt: investors should immediately verify the forward production rates of the Airbus A350 programme, as any supply chain failure at the airframer level is now the single largest remaining threat to Rolls-Royce's engine delivery guidance
9.
Other side of the story: if global inflation forces central banks to hold interest rates higher for longer, the resulting corporate distress could finally break the pricing power of the airlines, forcing the mass widebody groundings that the market prematurely priced in during April
