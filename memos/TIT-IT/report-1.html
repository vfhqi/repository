§1 — General

Telecom Italia S.p.A. is Italy's former state-owned telecommunications monopoly, now a ServCo-oriented retail telco following the 2024 divestiture of its fixed-line network infrastructure to KKR-led consortium.

  • Net signal: TI is a financially distressed incumbent telco restructuring under a landmark asset sale — scale is meaningful (~€13bn revenue pre-divestiture) but financial profile is among the weakest in European telecoms on leverage, margins, and returns [#J]

  • Description: TI provides fixed broadband, mobile, and enterprise/government ICT services in Italy, and full mobile services in Brazil via TIM Brasil (67% owned, NYSE/B3-listed) [#I]

  • Italy operations bifurcated post-NetCo into: (a) Consumer (household fixed/mobile bundles), (b) Business/Enterprise (SME + large corporate ICT), (c) Wholesale (third-party access resale)

  • Brazil (TIM Brasil) operates as a pure mobile player — 3rd largest by subscriber share in a 4-player market (Claro, Vivo, TIM, Oi-successor)

  • Strategic objective: Labriola's "Transformation Plan" (2022-2025) targets completion of NetCo exit, debt reduction from ~5x to ~2.5x EBITDA, and return of Italy to organic EBITDA growth by 2025-26 [#I]

  • Primary financial goal: reach investment-grade credit metrics to unlock refinancing at lower rates — currently sub-investment-grade (BB- S&P, Ba3 Moody's as of early-26)

  • Secondary goal: TIM Brasil value crystallisation via potential IPO stake reduction or strategic sale

  • Organisation: Post-NetCo, TI has three reportable segments: Italy ServCo Consumer, Italy ServCo Business/Wholesale, and TIM Brasil [#I]

  • Headcount post-NetCo Italy roughly 22,000 (down from ~50,000 pre-divestiture including NetCo employees); Brasil ~9,000

  • Government owns ~9.8% direct stake; Vivendi owns ~23.75% (blocked from board influence following governance dispute); Cassa Depositi e Prestiti ~9.8%

  • Listing: Listed on Borsa Italiana (Milan) as ordinary shares and savings shares [#I]

  • Reporting currency: EUR (Italy) and BRL (Brazil, consolidated at group level)
  • Current ordinary share price: ~€0.22-0.24 (May-26 range); savings shares at modest discount ~€0.20
  • Market cap (ordinary + savings combined): approximately €3.3-3.8bn at current prices
  • EV calculation complex post-NetCo — NetCo proceeds used partly to repay debt; residual ServCo EV approximately €8-10bn depending on Brasil valuation and net debt assumptions

  • Revenue: Italy domestic revenue has declined from ~€13.5bn (2016) to ~€9.5bn (2023) — a 30% erosion over 7 years driven by fixed-line substitution, mobile price competition, and structural mix shift to lower-ARPU products [#A] ❌
  • TIM Brasil grew from ~€3.0bn (2016, EUR-translated) to ~€3.8bn (2023) in EUR — but BRL/EUR depreciation masked underlying ~10% BRL CAGR

  • Post-NetCo, Italy ServCo revenue is approximately €7-8bn (2024E); Brasil remains ~€3.5-4bn

  • Margins: Group EBITDA margins declined from 43% (2016-17) to ~35% (2023) — driven by Italy revenue decline with partly-fixed cost base and increasing network lease costs post-NetCo [#A] ❌

  • European integrated telco median EBITDA margin ~38% (Barclays Jan-26: Orange 35%, DT 38%, Proximus 36%, Telecom Austria 40%) — TI trailing on margins even pre-ServCo transition

  • ServCo margin guidance ~30-32% structurally depressed vs integrated peers because TI now pays wholesale access fees to FiberCop (KKR-owned NetCo)

  • Capital intensity: Pre-NetCo, TI was among the highest capex/revenue telcos in Europe at ~22-24% of revenue (2019-23) due to FTTC/FTTH rollout obligations [#A]

  • Post-NetCo, ServCo capex should normalise to ~10-12% of ServCo revenue — still not low vs asset-light MVNOs but meaningfully lower than integrated peers

  • Critical caveat: TI retains access to FiberCop network under long-term wholesale agreements — capex reduction is real but comes at cost of margin squeeze via access fees

  • Returns (ROIC/ROCE): Pre-NetCo ROCE was negative or barely positive (2019-2023) — group EBIT insufficient to cover cost of capital against large asset base [#A] ❌

  • Post-tax ROCE never exceeded 5% during 2016-2023 period vs European telco median ~8% (per Citi Telecoms European Comp Sheet, Dec-25)

  • ROIC calculation obscured by large goodwill + intangible write-downs (2018 goodwill impairment ~€2.5bn, 2021 impairment ~€8.7bn)

  • Cash conversion: Operating-to-FCF conversion historically poor — interest costs on ~€25bn debt consumed majority of EBITDA above depreciation [#A] ❌

  • Equity FCF to shareholders was negative or negligible 2018-2023; no ordinary dividend paid since 2019

  • Post-NetCo: with net debt ~€8-9bn and EBITDA ~€2.4bn, interest coverage improves materially but leverage still ~3.5x on ServCo basis in 2024

  • Peer comparison: vs European integrated telco peer set (DT, Orange, BT, Proximus, Telecom Austria): TI ranks last or near-last on revenue growth, EBITDA margin, ROCE, leverage, and FCF yield — structural underperformer across virtually every financial dimension [#J] ❌

  • One peer comparison in TI's favour: post-NetCo EV/EBITDA (ServCo) is ~3.5-4x vs European telco median ~6x (Barclays Jan-26) — optically cheap but reflects quality/leverage discount

  • Capital allocation: No dividend, no buyback — all available cash directed toward debt service and interest costs during 2019-2024 [#I]

  • Share issuance history: capital increase of €2.2bn in 2012, followed by years of equity dilution through convertible instruments and rights issues; share count drifted from ~13bn (2012) to ~14.7bn ordinary + savings combined

  • Post-NetCo management signalling intent to reinstate dividend "once leverage below 2.5x" — unlikely before 2026-27 at earliest

  • Sceptical counter-read: ServCo EBITDA guidance of ~30-32% may prove optimistic if Italy domestic revenue continues declining — every 1% revenue miss on a fixed access-fee base mechanically compresses margin further; debt/EBITDA could stall at 3x+ rather than trajectory to 2.5x, keeping TI trapped below investment-grade indefinitely [#J]

§2 — Company Details

  • Net signal: Revenue model heavily exposed to structurally declining Italian fixed-line market with competitive mobile market offering limited pricing power — TIM Brasil provides the only organic growth but is valued separately by market [#J]

  • Revenue source: Italy Consumer (~45% of ServCo revenue), Italy Business/Enterprise (~30%), Italy Wholesale (~10%), TIM Brasil (~15% at group level post-NetCo) [#I]

  • Consumer: fixed broadband (FTTC/FTTH), mobile post-paid and pre-paid, converged bundles — all declining or flat in unit terms

  • Business: ICT managed services (cloud, security, data centres), connectivity, enterprise mobility — slightly more resilient than consumer but facing market share pressure from global hyperscalers (AWS, Azure) on cloud workloads

  • Revenue mix: Mix shift toward lower-margin broadband and away from legacy PSTN is structurally negative for ARPU in the near term, though FTTH upgrades carry modest ARPU uplift of ~€3-5/month per Citi broadband note (Mar-26) [#A]

  • Mobile: ~40% pre-paid penetration in TI's Italian mobile base vs European norm of ~25% — signals customer quality issue and ARPU drag (pre-paid ARPU ~€8 vs post-paid ~€18 per company disclosures)

  • Geography: Two geography exposures — Italy (high single-digit revenue decline trajectory, political/regulatory risk) and Brazil (mid-to-high single-digit BRL revenue growth, FX dilutive in EUR terms) [#A]

  • No meaningful other geographic exposures; TI exited all non-Italy/Brazil operations (Argentina, Bolivia, Peru) by 2017-19

  • Cyclicality: Telco revenues are structurally defensive in a macro-downturn (essential service, contract-linked) — Italy consumer is relatively stable in unit terms but not immune to SIM-only migration during cost-of-living pressures [#A]

  • Brazil is moderately cyclical relative to Brazilian GDP — TIM Brasil revenue grew through 2020-21 COVID despite economic contraction due to mobile data demand acceleration

  • Predictability: Revenue trajectory highly predictable — unfortunately in the negative direction for Italy [#J]

  • Analyst consensus (JPMorgan, Barclays, Citi, Mediobanca) models Italy ServCo revenue declining -2% to -4% organically through 2026 with EBITDA flat to slightly growing as cost cutting offsets volume erosion

  • Brazil consensus models +7-9% BRL revenue growth pa through 2026, partially FX-hedged

  • Customer behaviour (Italy): Fixed-line customer losses continue at ~300-400k RGU/year — driven by mobile-only substitution and fibre-to-the-home rollout by Open Fiber (wholesale-only competitor) enabling Iliad, Wind3 to offer bundled fibre [#A] ❌

  • Mobile churn elevated at ~18-20% pa on pre-paid base — among highest in Western European incumbents (European incumbent median ~12-15% on blended basis per GSMA data cited in Mediobanca note, Feb-26)

  • NPS data: TI's Italian consumer NPS historically negative or low-single-digit positive — brand perception damaged by years of service quality issues and billing disputes; AGCOM (Italian communications regulator) issued multiple corrective orders

  • Switching costs (Italy): Low switching costs — Italian mobile portability process is straightforward; AGCOM has consistently ruled against practices that increase switching friction [#A] ❌

  • Fixed broadband switching costs modestly higher due to installation appointments and equipment, but Iliad/WindTre offer subsidised migration; effective moat near-zero on consumer side

  • TIM Brasil customer dynamics: TIM Brasil is more positively positioned — post-paid share growing, ARPU expanding, churn improving [#A]

  • Brazil post-paid subscriber share: ~24% (vs Claro ~28%, Vivo/Telefónica ~33%) — #3 in a 4-player market with no MVNO disruption comparable to Italy

  • Brasil churn ~1.5-1.7% per month on post-paid — comparable to Claro and below Vivo (per TIM Brasil investor presentations, Dec-25)

  • Scale economies (Italy ServCo): Scale economy argument weakened post-NetCo — TI no longer owns the network, so the traditional fixed-cost leverage of integrated telcos is structurally impaired [#A] ❌

  • Operating leverage in ServCo is modest: access fees to FiberCop are variable per customer (passed through), limiting downside but also limiting upside operating leverage

  • Business/Enterprise segment has modest scale advantage in large Italian enterprise accounts from incumbency and existing infrastructure relationships — but cloud adjacency erodes this

  • Recent delivery (2022-2024): Labriola's tenure delivery record is mixed — the NetCo sale was completed (a major execution win) but underlying Italy commercial metrics did not stabilise [#A]

  • NetCo (FiberCop) sale to KKR closed Nov-2024: €22bn EV, TI received net ~€14bn after debt assigned to NetCo; proceeds used to repay €11-12bn of parent debt
  • Italy mobile revenue: declined ~3% in 2022, ~4% in 2023, ~2% in 2024E — partial stabilisation in 2024 driven by price increases (first ARPU increase in several years)

  • Italy fixed broadband: FTTH customer adds positive for first time in 2024 (net +180k FTTH vs -220k FTTC) — technically a positive inflection though total broadband base still declining

  • Internal change — leadership (L12M): Labriola (CEO since Nov-21) retained through NetCo completion; new CFO Adrian Calaza appointed mid-2024 to lead ServCo financial restructuring [#I]

  • Board composition remains contested: Vivendi (23.75% shareholder) has been in dispute with Italian government and CDP-aligned board majority since 2022 — governance overhang is a live risk

  • Vivendi attempted to block the NetCo sale (legal challenge withdrawn post-close but signalling ongoing shareholder friction)

  • Internal change — strategy (N3Y): The ServCo transition is the defining strategic shift — from integrated network owner to pure retail operator renting access [#I]

  • Cost restructuring programme targeting €600-700m annual cost savings by 2026 (largely from headcount reduction post-NetCo transfer of network employees to FiberCop/KKR)

  • Enterprise/Business pivot: TI investing in "Noovle" (cloud business, partly sold-down to Telsy/CDP), data centres (partially divested), and security services to protect enterprise revenue from structural erosion

  • Sceptical counter-read: The NetCo sale crystallised near-term debt relief but permanently impairs TI's vertical integration advantage; as a pure ServCo, TI is dependent on FiberCop's wholesale pricing decisions (KKR-controlled), limiting future margin optionality — the "cleaner balance sheet" thesis requires Italy revenue to stop declining, which requires competitive stabilisation that has not yet materialised [#J]

§3 — Industry Context

  • Net signal: Italian telecoms market is structurally unattractive — 4-player mobile market with aggressive pricing by Iliad (entered 2018), persistent fixed-line substitution, and heavy regulatory oversight by AGCOM; not a benign competitive environment [#J] ❌

  • Market structure (Italy mobile): 4 MNOs — TIM (~27% RGU share), WindTre (~29%), Vodafone IT (~22%), Iliad (~12%) — plus MVNOs [#A]

  • Top 4 share: ~90% of Italian mobile subscribers; technically concentrated but Iliad's aggressive pricing since 2018 has destabilised ARPU for all incumbents

  • Iliad entered at €5.99/month for unlimited calls + 40GB data — forced all incumbents to defend volume at margin cost; Italian mobile ARPU declined ~15% between 2018-2023 (GSMA data cited by Barclays Jan-26)

  • Market structure (Italy fixed broadband): TI/FiberCop network underpins ~60% of Italian fixed broadband infrastructure; Open Fiber (wholesale-only, co-owned by Enel and CDP) provides alternative FTTH infrastructure in ~280 urban clusters [#A]

  • Effective retail broadband players: TIM, Vodafone IT, WindTre, Fastweb, Iliad (on FiberCop or Open Fiber wholesale) — more fragmented than mobile; TIM retail share ~35% but declining

  • Open Fiber/TIM FiberCop consolidation discussions ongoing at government level — could improve structure but regulatory timeline uncertain (ESA Q8 detail recommended)

  • Competitive intensity: High and structurally elevated since Iliad entry [#A] ❌

  • Iliad Italy reached ~12% mobile subscriber share by end-2024; targeting 15% by 2026 per public statements — still growing and funding growth via French parent balance sheet

  • Vodafone Italy formally agreed to merge with Iliad Italy (announced 2023, regulatory review ongoing as of May-26) — if approved, creates a 3-player market with stronger #2/3 vs TIM; outcome cuts both ways (less Iliad aggression but stronger combined entity)

  • New entrant / disruption (Italy): Post-Iliad, meaningful new MNO entry is effectively blocked by spectrum costs and MVNO dynamics — next disruption more likely from cable/OTT bundles [#A]

  • Sky Italia (Fox/Comcast owned) offers bundle combining Sky TV + mobile via MVNO (hosted on WindTre) — modest competitive threat to TIM consumer bundles in high-ARPU households

  • FWA (Fixed Wireless Access) from Eolo and Linkem competes with FTTC in underserved Italian areas — niche but growing in TI's most price-sensitive markets

  • Regulatory shift: AGCOM and EU Commission actively involved in Italian broadband infrastructure regulation [#A]

  • FiberCop (KKR-owned NetCo) subject to regulated wholesale pricing — AGCOM sets copper/fibre wholesale access rates; TI as ServCo is both buyer of wholesale access and subject to AGCOM retail pricing regulation
  • Government "digital agenda" targets 100% FTTH coverage by 2026 under PNRR (EU recovery funds) — requires cooperation between FiberCop and Open Fiber; structural uncertainty around whose network standards prevail

  • AGCOM has historically been tougher on TIM (as former monopoly) than on entrants — asymmetric regulatory burden

  • Political shift: Italian government retains ~9.8% direct TI stake and uses "golden power" provisions to influence strategic decisions [#A]

  • Government opposed Vivendi's influence and supported the NetCo sale to KKR — political alignment with Labriola's restructuring plan is currently favourable

  • Risk: if Italian government strategy shifts (e.g. desire to renationalise network, or if Open Fiber/FiberCop merge under state umbrella), TI ServCo's wholesale terms could be renegotiated disadvantageously

  • Macro shift (Italy): Italian GDP growth modest (+0.7% in 2024, consensus +0.8% for 2025) — telco service revenue broadly tracks GDP on volume side; enterprise ICT has modest cyclical sensitivity [#A]

  • Italian consumer confidence improvement post-2024 energy crisis provides marginal tailwind for pay-TV and broadband upgrade; limited material impact vs structural decline

  • Interest rate environment: ECB cutting cycle (250bp cumulative cuts 2024-25) directly reduces TI's interest burden on floating-rate debt — meaningful for a debt-heavy company

  • Brazil market context: Brazilian mobile market is a 4-player oligopoly (Vivo ~33%, Claro ~28%, TIM ~24%, Oi-successor ~10%) with benign competitive dynamics — rational ARPU growth, low fixed substitution risk [#A]

  • Brazil market structure more supportive than Italy: Vivo and Claro have historically prioritised ARPU over volume; Oi's bankruptcy and asset redistribution (completed 2022) removed the most price-aggressive player

  • Brazil macro: BRL GDP +3% in 2024, strong consumer data usage growth — TIM Brasil's operating environment significantly more attractive than Italy

  • Supplier returns (FiberCop/KKR): Post-NetCo, KKR (via FiberCop) is TI's largest supplier — a sophisticated financial owner with incentive to maximise wholesale access fees [#A]

  • FiberCop is regulated by AGCOM, which caps wholesale rates — mitigating worst-case supplier pricing power; but regulation typically lags market, and KKR can negotiate commercial (unregulated) terms for above-cap services

  • Structural tension: TI ServCo's margin improvement requires either revenue growth or wholesale cost containment; KKR's IRR on FiberCop improves if wholesale rates stay high — interests partially misaligned

  • Sceptical cross-cutting read: Italy market structure improvement (Vodafone/Iliad merger creating rational 3-player oligopoly) is the consensus bull thesis for Italian mobile ARPU recovery — but EU regulatory approval uncertain, timeline 18-24 months, and Iliad parent may extract concessions that dilute the rational-pricing benefit; TI's recovery thesis rests on an exogenous event over which it has no control [#J]

§4 — BB#2 Foundation Quality

  • Net signal: TI scores poorly on most BB#2 Foundation Quality dimensions — operator in early rehabilitation phase, moat structurally thin, generate metrics clearly sub-threshold, industry structure mixed; not a high-quality business by the RESEARCHER framework's standards [#J] ❌

§4.1 — Operator (BB#2 TC1: Great Operator)

  • Operator: Labriola's core strategic achievement — completing the NetCo sale despite board opposition, Vivendi litigation, and political complexity — is genuine and should not be discounted; few European telco CEOs have executed a comparable structural divestiture [#J]

  • However, the turnaround is incomplete: Italy commercial metrics (mobile ARPU, broadband RGUs) have not yet structurally inflected; the operator has executed the financial restructuring but not yet the commercial re-energisation

  • Strategic ambition: Transformation Plan ambition is explicitly articulated as "ServCo with investment-grade balance sheet by 2026, profitable growth by 2027" — modest ambition by growth-investor standards, essentially "stop the bleeding" [#A]

  • No articulated path to double-digit organic revenue growth; strategic framing around "value over volume" in mobile and "enterprise ICT pivot" are directionally correct but not clearly differentiated from peers

  • Strategy fit: ServCo strategy is logically congruent with the balance sheet imperative — TI could not sustain both network capex and debt service; divestiture was structurally necessary [#A]

  • Concern: ServCo strategy inherently limits future upside — no network ownership means no ability to monetise infrastructure value creation directly; capped by retail-only value chain position

  • CEO: Pietro Labriola, 54, promoted from CEO of TIM Brasil to Group CEO in November 2021 — background is commercial/operational rather than financial engineering [#I]

  • Track record at TIM Brasil (2017-2021): consistently grew Brazilian revenue, EBITDA, and free cash flow — Brasil was the "proving ground" for his operational credibility

  • First external market assessment: JPMorgan upgraded TI to neutral (from underweight) in Jan-24 partly citing Labriola's execution credibility on NetCo — a sell-side quality signal

  • Leadership team: CFO Calaza (appointed mid-2024) is new and untested at group level; Chief Commercial Officer rotated during 2024 [#I]

  • Board governance still contested: Vivendi (23.75% stake) maintains board seats but is outvoted by CDP/MEF-aligned majority; functional governance stability achieved but underlying shareholder conflict unresolved — ESA depth recommended for Q8

  • Culture: No evidence of Ryanair/Lifco-style outperformer culture — TI has legacy public-sector culture, high unionisation (estimated 80%+ workforce union membership), and ongoing labour disputes over NetCo headcount transfers [#A]

  • Cost reduction targets dependent on voluntary redundancy schemes and natural attrition — execution risk elevated given union resistance

  • Guidance posture: Management guidance has improved in accuracy since 2022 — 2023 EBITDA guidance was met within 2%; 2024 guidance for ServCo transition was broadly achieved [#A]

  • Prior to Labriola, TI had a poor guidance credibility record: 2018-2021 period saw repeated EBITDA guidance cuts (3-4 downgrades per year in 2019-20 per JPMorgan historical tracking)

  • Track record: Labriola's delivery record is 2.5 years of a 5-year transformation — too early for full assessment [#J]

  • Positive: NetCo sale completed, debt reduced from ~5x to ~3.5x EBITDA, Italia ARPU price increases implemented for first time in years

  • Negative: subscriber base still declining, no commercial inflection in Italy yet, Vivendi governance distraction

  • Capital allocation: No dividend, no buyback, all free cash to deleveraging — appropriate given balance sheet position but not a capital allocation track record that demonstrates shareholder-first orientation [#A]

  • High-RONIC reinvestment (CQ9): TI is not deploying capital at high-RONIC — the transformation is deleverage-oriented, not growth-investment-oriented; this is a structural constraint, not management preference

  • Shareholders first (CQ13): Vivendi dispute and government strategic interests create agency cost risk — minority shareholders' interests are not always the priority in TI's governance structure [#A] ❌

§4.2 — Advantaged Business + SRCA (BB#2 TC2)

  • Net signal : TI's moat is structurally thin across all five moat types — customer lock-in low, production moat eliminated via NetCo divestiture, scale advantage reduced, regulatory moat present but double-edged, culture moat absent [#J] ❌

MOAT (RA4 — full treatment):

  • Customer moat (CQ16): No strong customer moat in Italian consumer — switching costs are regulatory-mandated to be low; brand loyalty negative (AGCOM complaints record); NPS negative or low-single-digit positive [#A] ❌
  • Italy Enterprise segment has modest incumbency moat in large-account relationships (government, banking, utilities) — but under pressure from AWS/Azure hyper-converged offers; not a durable structural advantage

  • TIM Brasil has stronger customer moat in its post-paid base — ~24% share with stable churn and ARPU growth suggests modest but real customer franchise

  • Production moat (CQ17): Eliminated by NetCo sale — TI no longer owns the infrastructure differentiator [#A] ❌

  • Pre-2024, TI's copper and fibre network was both a production moat (competitors had to access it via regulated wholesale) and a cost burden; post-sale, TI is a wholesale access buyer like its competitors on FiberCop's network

  • Scale advantage moat (CQ18): Residual scale advantage in: (a) enterprise sales force (largest in Italy), (b) brand awareness (highest unaided brand recognition in Italy despite NPS issues), (c) TIM Brasil network coverage (strongest rural + suburban coverage in Brazil) [#A]

  • Scale advantage in Italy is eroding as Iliad + WindTre achieve sufficient scale to compete credibly on bundle offers; scale advantage no longer translates to pricing premium

  • Regulatory moat (CQ19): Double-edged — TI benefits from historical incumbency spectrum licences (800MHz, 700MHz anchor positions) but faces asymmetric AGCOM regulation as ex-monopoly [#A]

  • Government golden power creates a form of political moat (prevents hostile foreign ownership) but also limits strategic optionality (e.g. restricts certain M&A)

  • In Brazil: TIM's spectrum holdings (700MHz, 1800MHz, 2600MHz) are competitive vs Vivo/Claro — a genuine LTE/5G coverage advantage in rural Brazil

  • Culture moat (CQ20): Absent — see §4.1 commentary on legacy public-sector culture and high unionisation [#A] ❌

  • Relative scale (CQ21): TI is the largest Italian operator by fixed-line customer base and #2 in mobile by subscriber count — scale is meaningful at country level [#I]

  • However, Italian market is small-to-mid relative to global telco peers: Italy revenue ~€7-8bn (ServCo) vs BT ~£22bn, DT ~€90bn, Orange ~€44bn — TI is a mid-tier national incumbent, not a scale leader

  • TAM (CQ22): Italian telecom market is mature — total addressable revenue for Italian telco sector approximately €25-28bn annually (Barclays Jan-26 market sizing); growing <1% pa in EUR terms [#A]

  • TAM does not support 12-20% organic revenue growth thesis — Italian telco TAM is flat to slightly declining in real terms; adjacent ICT/cloud TAM is growing but TI's share is modest
  • TIM Brasil's TAM is more supportive — Brazilian mobile market growing mid-to-high single digits in BRL; TIM Brasil TAM ~BRL 120bn for mobile data/voice services

OPERATE (RA5 — high-level only):

  • Operate (SCU/RSCU/pricing power — CQ23-26): First material mobile price increases in Italy implemented in 2023-24 (€2-3/month ARPU uplift) — a genuine positive signal but too early to designate as structural pricing power [#A]
  • ESA Q8 depth recommended: assess whether ARPU recovery is sustainable vs Iliad competitive response; analyse relative cost-per-user vs WindTre/Vodafone IT benchmarks
