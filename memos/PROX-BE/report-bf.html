EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (BLUF)

Proximus operates as a dominant Belgian telecom incumbent controlling high-margin domestic infrastructure while simultaneously managing a complex turnaround of its troubled international CPaaS and digital identity operations, navigating an industry structure marked by high concentration but rising threats from low-cost disruptors and structural technology migration away from legacy SMS messaging. Core Business and Segmental Structure Proximus divides operations into two primary segments, anchoring core profitability in the Domestic division which spans Belgium, the Netherlands, and Luxembourg, while the newly unified Proximus Global segment consolidates international assets BICS, Telesign, and Route Mobile spanning the full digital communications value chain. The Domestic segment generated approximately €1.7 billion in FY25 EBITDA compared to €0.17 billion for Global, illustrating the extreme profit concentration in domestic operations. Consumer revenue remains highly resilient, supported by steady expansion of the convergent footprint with convergent customer accounts growing 4.0% year-on-year in 2025 to reach 1,221,000 customers generating €1,300 million in FY25 revenue. The B2B division is consolidated under the Proximus NXT brand, delivering converged telco-IT solutions with specialized IT affiliates like Codit, Davinsi Labs, and ClearMedia operating across the Benelux. 2 3 4

Proximus Global faces severe pricing pressure in legacy SMS messaging markets, with global revenue falling 14.8% year-on-year on a pro forma basis in FY25 to €1,548 million and global direct margin declining 11.1%, reflecting a massive slowdown in CPaaS SMS that necessitated a €275 million non-cash goodwill impairment of the Global segment in early 2026. Competitive Positioning and Market Structure The Belgian telecom sector represents a mature and highly stable revenue market where the top three operators control over 95.00% of the national mobile market, with Proximus maintaining the leading national position holding a 41.00% market share in mobile and 47.00% overall market share in fixed broadband. The entrance of low-cost disrupter DIGI Belgium introduces heightened structural competitive risk in mobile, launching a highly disruptive €9.00 monthly subscription offering 60GB of data and unlimited calls that is at least twice as cheap as incumbent sub-brands including Proximus's Scarlet. Despite aggressive marketing, DIGI's initial subscriber acquisition remains temporarily muted and contained within mobile, with incumbents perceiving the threat from DIGI as highly limited for fixed broadband products.

Proximus and Orange Belgium established Mwingz as a 50/50 owned joint operation to plan, build, and operate a shared mobile access network, with equipment sharing targeting a 40.00% reduction in total mobile sites by 2026 and improving coverage at active shared sites by an estimated 20.00% footprint expansion. The group signed long-form binding agreements with Wyre to deploy Flanders' high-speed gigabit networks targeting 2.7 million homes, where Wyre builds 60.00% of medium-dense fiber while Proximus's Fiberklaar builds the remaining 40.00% allocation with reciprocal wholesale access to both infrastructures.

Operator Quality and Strategic Execution The group launched new Amplify domestic and Elevate global strategic plans in early 2026, with domestic execution pivoting to premium fiber rollout and symmetrical high-speed tiering monetization, as Chief Executive Officer Stijn Bijnens joined on September 1, 2025 and Seckin Arikan was appointed CEO of Proximus Global on November 1, 2025. Management halved the dividend over 2026 results to 30 cents to align with FCF, while consolidating Unifiber raises FY26 capital expenditure expectations up to €1.3 billion compressing organic cash flows for 2026 to around €50 million post Unifiber capex consolidation.

Fixed-mobile convergence reaches 65% of residential services revenue driving extremely low churn, with fiber footprint expanding to cover over 42% of Belgian households in early 2026, while tower sharing via the Mwingz JV with Orange Belgium reduces duplicate mobile opex and capex. The international CPaaS messaging business exhibits virtually no switching costs or unique IP, with global customers easily disintermediating standard SMS forcing intense price and volume erosion. Unlike automated compounders, Proximus relies on intense managerial intervention and headcount cuts, targeting €180 million in gross savings relying on attrition and AI adoption, while mandatory automatic wage indexation in Belgium creates a recurrent mechanical drag on opex.

Long-Term Disruption and Growth Outlook Traditional P2P voice and SMS messaging face accelerating volume and revenue declines as enterprises are actively migrating from legacy SMS authentication to alternative cheaper OTP methods, with the P2P voice and messaging market projected to decline by 5% to 10% annually through 2029. Proximus Global shifts messaging toward high-margin omnichannel services like WhatsApp and RCS, with the omnichannel messaging market estimated to grow 20% to 30% annually through 2029, while Route Mobile leverages BICS to accelerate global RCS rollout and telco API integration.
