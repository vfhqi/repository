2. KEY FINDINGS — §1

J-front verdict: Three questions are load-bearing for the investment decision; all three probe areas where public filings are opaque and management answers are diagnostic of bull vs. false-friend.

  • Most diagnostic Q1 — MVNO/wholesale margin haircut: How much of OBEL's Belgian retail ARPU premium is already being competed away by its own MVNO partners on the Orange network, and what is the contractual floor on wholesale pricing? Answer discriminates bull (pricing discipline intact) from false-friend (volume propped by margin-dilutive MVNO traffic)
  • Most diagnostic Q2 — RAL/convergence revenue recognition: What share of the reported residential ARPU uplift post-VOO integration is genuinely incremental ARPU vs. accounting re-classification of bundled discounts against the subscriber line? Answer discriminates bull (real bundle premium) from bear (accounting flattery)
  • Most diagnostic Q3 — CEO incentive alignment on FCF vs. EBITDA: Are STI/LTI metrics weighted to EBITDA-AL or to levered FCF-per-share, and has the weighting changed since the VOO deal closed? Answer discriminates operator archetype (value-compounder vs. empire-builder)
  • BLUF signal: Revenue visibility is higher than peers because Belgian market is three-player post-consolidation, BUT convergence integration risk, €1bn+ net debt load post-VOO, and uncertain regulatory stance on broadband access pricing are the three live uncertainty pillars; questions weight accordingly
  • False-friend flag: OBEL's 2023-2024 print shows improving EBITDA trend; the risk is that margin improvement is partly capex deferral and partly MVNO mix flattery rather than structural cost-out — management fluency on this topic is itself a diagnostic signal
  • What questions does the analyst NOT want to ask, and why? The uncomfortable question is: "Has integration of VOO already surfaced negative surprises that have not been disclosed?" Analysts avoid this because it implies distrust; per Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 1, this is exactly the question that must be asked — see §8

3. THEME §1 — Revenue Drivers + Visibility

J-front verdict: Revenue-side uncertainty centres on post-VOO bundle ARPU durability and B2B ICT growth credibility; churn and MVNO mix are the two embedded traps.

Q1.1 — Convergence ARPU: real vs. reclassified

  • Question: "Post-VOO integration, what percentage of the residential ARPU uplift reflects genuinely new spend by the customer versus internal re-bundling of previously separate mobile and fixed lines — and how do you measure that distinction?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite blended ARPU trajectory (likely €42-47 range) and reference customer-level revenue; unlikely to give a clean incremental-only number
  • Good answer signature: Provides customer-cohort revenue data, distinguishes new-product attach from re-bundling, offers a gross-adds vs. upgrade split
  • Bad answer signature: Deflects to aggregate ARPU; references only headline convergence penetration rate without cohort-level revenue delta
  • Follow-up probe: "If we strip out customers who simply migrated from separate mobile and fixed contracts to a bundle with a net discount — what is the residual ARPU uplift?"
  • Diagnostic note: Bull hypothesis requires genuine ARPU uplift from convergence; false-friend hypothesis has ARPU flattery from re-bundling. Fluent but aggregate answer = yellow flag

Q1.2 — MVNO wholesale pricing floor

  • Question: "What contractual protection do you have against MVNO partners undercutting your own retail mobile pricing, and at what wholesale margin do those MVNO contracts price today versus two years ago?"
  • Expected answer: Management will reference MVNO contracts as commercially confidential; likely to say pricing is cost-based with a floor; unlikely to give precise wholesale margin
  • Good answer signature: Distinguishes traffic volume MVNO (margin-accretive to network utilisation) from brand-competing MVNO (strategic cannibalism risk); gives directional margin delta
  • Bad answer signature: Claims MVNOs are not a pricing threat without explaining the mechanism; refuses to discuss wholesale margin trajectory
  • Follow-up probe: "Is the MVNO book growing faster or slower than your own-brand subscriber base, and what does that imply for network revenue mix at full MVNO loading?"
  • Diagnostic note: If MVNO traffic is growing faster than own-brand, network economics improve but retail revenue mix degrades — bear hypothesis on ARPU per retail sub

Q1.3 — B2B ICT revenue: organic vs. acquired

  • Question: "In your B2B ICT revenue line, what proportion is organic new contract wins versus revenue from acquired entities or transferred government contracts — and what is the renewal rate on the top-20 B2B contracts?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite B2B as a growth driver; likely to reference enterprise convergence; unlikely to give contract-level renewal data
  • Good answer signature: Clear organic/acquired split; renewal rate above 85%; references pipeline conversion metric
  • Bad answer signature: Uses "B2B momentum" language without revenue-type decomposition; declines to give renewal rates citing commercial sensitivity
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the average remaining contract life on your top-20 enterprise accounts, and when do the largest renewals fall?"
  • Diagnostic note: Long contract tails = EPS visibility; short tails or silence = revenue-cliff risk not visible in reported figures

Q1.4 — Churn vectors post-convergence

  • Question: "Since launching your converged bundles, has involuntary churn (non-payment) increased or decreased as a share of total churn — and what is the current monthly churn rate on converged versus standalone mobile subscribers?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite convergence as a churn-reduction tool; likely headline churn rates around 1.5-2% monthly for standalone mobile
  • Good answer signature: Converged subscriber churn materially below standalone; involuntary churn stable or declining; references credit-quality metrics on new subscriber intake
  • Bad answer signature: Only cites headline blended churn; refuses to disaggregate converged vs. standalone; deflects to "churn is in line with market"
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the credit-quality profile of new converged bundle subscribers acquired post-VOO — are they additive to your existing customer quality distribution?"
  • Diagnostic note: Churn disaggregation tests whether the convergence premium is real or whether bundle uptake is subsidised by lower-quality customers

Sceptical bullet: A management team that answers §1 questions fluently and with cohort-level comparisons is more reassuring — but a perfectly rehearsed revenue narrative without admitting any adverse churn or ARPU trend is itself a red flag; probe whether the "good" numbers are recent or trailing-12M averages that mask a late-2024 deterioration

4. THEME §2 — Margin Trajectory + Levers

J-front verdict: EBITDA-AL margin improvement narrative is the core bull thesis; the key diagnostic question is how much of reported improvement is structural cost-out versus capex pacing versus mix flattery.

Q2.1 — VOO cost synergy realisation: phasing and confidence

  • Question: "Of the announced VOO integration synergies, what percentage has been realised to date, which workstreams are running ahead vs. behind schedule, and where is the highest execution risk in the remaining synergy book?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite synergy tracking on plan; likely reference IT/network overlap and headcount; will probably underplay execution risk
  • Good answer signature: Specific realisation percentage by workstream; honest identification of one behind-schedule area; clear timeline for remaining realisation; one-off costs clearly quantified
  • Bad answer signature: "On track" without workstream decomposition; one-off integration costs described as "substantially complete" without a number
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the net present value of synergies already banked versus what was in the original deal model — and has the discount rate you apply to remaining synergies changed?"
  • Diagnostic note: Any revision to synergy quantum or timeline since deal close is a leading indicator of deal quality; silence on execution risk = bear flag

Q2.2 — Capex pacing and whether margin improvement is real

  • Question: "Your capex-to-revenue ratio has moved materially over the last three years — how much of the current EBITDA-AL improvement is structural versus capex timing, and what is the normalised maintenance capex run-rate post-5G and post-broadband infrastructure build?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite declining capex intensity as network build matures; will likely give a 2025-2026 capex guidance range; may be reluctant to give a maintenance-vs-growth split
  • Good answer signature: Clear maintenance/growth capex split; explicit year-by-year capex glide path; willing to state a normalised capex percentage of revenue at steady state
  • Bad answer signature: Capex guidance only in absolute euros without maintenance/growth decomposition; deflects to "we'll update at capital markets day"
  • Follow-up probe: "If 5G densification requirements turn out to be higher than your current model, what is the capex step-up risk — and at what spectrum cost do you revise your capex envelope?"
  • Diagnostic note: Capex deferral can flatter EBITDA-AL for 2-3 years before network quality degradation surfaces; management's willingness to give a maintenance baseline is a quality indicator

Q2.3 — Energy cost pass-through and hedging

  • Question: "Belgian telecom energy costs rose sharply in 2022-2023 — what percentage of your energy cost base is currently hedged, at what prices, and when do those hedges roll off?"
  • Expected answer: Partial hedging disclosed; management likely cites procurement improvements and network efficiency; unlikely to give precise hedge roll-off schedule
  • Good answer signature: Clear hedge book quantification; roll-off timeline; sensitivity analysis (e.g. €X EBITDA impact per €10/MWh move); proactive network efficiency programme quantified
  • Bad answer signature: "We manage energy costs actively" without specifics; no sensitivity framework offered
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the unhedged energy cost exposure as a percentage of EBITDA-AL, and what is the 2025 hedge position versus 2024?"
  • Diagnostic note: Energy cost is a known margin headwind; opaque hedging = margin volatility risk not in consensus models

Q2.4 — Staff cost trajectory and headcount post-VOO

  • Question: "Post-VOO, what is the combined group headcount trajectory — are you still in headcount reduction mode, and what is the staff cost as a percentage of revenue target at full integration?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite staff integration as ongoing; likely reference a headcount target; severance costs may still be running
  • Good answer signature: Clear end-state headcount target with timeline; severance costs quantified and ring-fenced; staff cost as % revenue target stated
  • Bad answer signature: "We are managing the workforce carefully" without a number; staff cost trajectory conflated with synergy narrative without decomposition
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the remaining severance provision on the balance sheet, and is it sufficient or will there be further charges?"
  • Diagnostic note: Residual severance risk is often underestimated; insufficient provision = one-off charges in 2025-2026 that damage FCF

Sceptical bullet: Margin improvement guided through 2026 could be validated by capex phasing alone without any structural cost improvement; ask management to show the margin bridge with capex held constant — if the bridge collapses without the capex tailwind, the earnings quality question is open

5. THEME §3 — Capital Allocation + Balance Sheet

J-front verdict: Post-VOO leverage is the balance sheet constraint; dividend policy and deleveraging timeline are the two areas where management's stated priorities and actual behaviour are most likely to diverge.

Q3.1 — Deleveraging timeline and dividend vs. deleverage trade-off

  • Question: "Your net debt/EBITDA-AL is currently above your stated comfort range — at what leverage level do you resume dividend growth or buybacks, and is the current dividend level sacrosanct even if FCF is weaker than plan in 2025?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite 2.0-2.5x as target; likely to defend the dividend as a signal; unlikely to admit dividend is at risk
  • Good answer signature: Clear leverage target with year-by-year glide path; explicit FCF-to-dividend coverage ratio cited; scenario where dividend is rebased if integration slows
  • Bad answer signature: "The dividend is well supported" without a FCF coverage ratio; leverage target stated without timeline or FCF sensitivity
  • Follow-up probe: "If FCF-per-share comes in 20% below plan in 2025, which adjusts first — the dividend, the capex, or the deleveraging pace?"
  • Diagnostic note: Management unwilling to discuss dividend rebasing risk = the dividend is more likely at risk than the market prices; this is a bear-hypothesis discriminator

Q3.2 — M&A philosophy post-VOO

  • Question: "Now that VOO is integrated, what is your stated M&A strategy for the next three years — bolt-on only, no M&A, or would you consider a transformational deal if the opportunity arose?"
  • Expected answer: "Bolt-on only, we are focused on integration and deleveraging" — this is the expected answer; the interesting signal is any deviation from it
  • Good answer signature: Clear no-transformational-M&A commitment with leverage condition attached; any bolt-on parameters defined (size, geography, capability type)
  • Bad answer signature: Non-committal on transformational M&A; references "strategic optionality"; mentions infrastructure separation or tower sale as "under review" without specifics
  • Follow-up probe: "Would you consider selling the tower portfolio or a network-sharing arrangement with Proximus or Telenet — and has that been evaluated recently?"
  • Diagnostic note: Tower monetisation is a live debate in European telco; management silence or deflection signals either active process or board disagreement

Q3.3 — Working capital management post-integration

  • Question: "VOO's legacy working capital and billing systems are being integrated — what is the net working capital impact of the systems cutover, and have you had any billing or collection disruption that affected receivables?"
  • Expected answer: Management will say integration is on track; unlikely to disclose systems disruptions voluntarily
  • Good answer signature: Proactively mentions working capital normalisation timeline; quantifies any billing disruption; days-sales-outstanding trend discussed
  • Bad answer signature: "Working capital is well managed" without specifics; no mention of any integration disruption on receivables; only references days payable
  • Follow-up probe: "Has the DSO on residential or enterprise accounts moved since the VOO billing system migration — and if so, by how much?"
  • Diagnostic note: Billing disruption is a common post-integration trap; unrecognised bad debt is a FCF risk not in sell-side models

Sceptical bullet: A management team that presents a clean capital allocation story immediately post a €1bn+ acquisition is likely presenting the best-case scenario; probe for any covenant headroom and the nearest covenant test date — this is the load-bearing question if the integration runs slow

6. THEME §4 — Strategy + Competitive Position

J-front verdict: Three-player Belgian market structure is strategically favourable but Proximus's fibre rollout and Telenet's cable superiority create asymmetric threats that management may underweight in public communications.

Q4.1 — Fibre competitive response

  • Question: "Proximus is rolling out fibre aggressively in Flanders — where Orange Belgium does not have a broadband network. Does the VOO footprint (Wallonia/Brussels) give you sufficient geographic buffer, or is there a credible path by which Proximus fibre reaches VOO subscribers before your integration is complete?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite VOO's cable network as competitive; likely reference upgrade to DOCSIS 3.1/4.0; may downplay fibre overbuild risk
  • Good answer signature: Honest acknowledgement of fibre overbuild risk in specific VOO geography; timeline for DOCSIS upgrade vs. Proximus fibre rollout schedule; win/loss data in fibre-competed postcodes
  • Bad answer signature: Dismisses Proximus fibre as "not a near-term threat to our footprint"; no win/loss data; relies on price rather than network quality as the defence
  • Follow-up probe: "In the postcodes where Proximus fibre is already live, what has happened to your broadband net adds and churn — and is that a leading indicator for the broader rollout?"
  • Diagnostic note: If fibre-competed postcodes show adverse broadband dynamics, the VOO asset quality thesis is weaker than the acquisition price assumed

Q4.2 — Regulatory positioning on broadband access

  • Question: "The Belgian Institute for Postal Services and Telecommunications has ongoing proceedings on broadband access pricing — what is the worst-case regulatory outcome for Orange Belgium's wholesale access costs, and what is the timeline for the next key decision?"
  • Expected answer: Management will downplay regulatory risk; cite BIPT history as reasonable; unlikely to quantify the downside
  • Good answer signature: Quantifies the EBITDA impact of the adverse regulatory scenario; references BIPT decision timeline; has a contingency plan for the adverse case
  • Bad answer signature: "Regulation is in line with European norms" without scenario analysis; no quantification of adverse case
  • Follow-up probe: "If BIPT imposes a 10% reduction in wholesale broadband access pricing, what is the direct EBITDA impact — and does it affect your synergy model?"
  • Diagnostic note: Regulatory risk is systematically underpriced in European telco; opaque management answer = the analyst should use the disclosed regulatory risk as a floor, not a ceiling

Q4.3 — Win condition in the three-player market

  • Question: "In a three-player Belgian market, what is Orange Belgium's sustainable competitive differentiation versus Proximus (incumbent) and Telenet (cable quality leader) — and where in the last 12 months have you won subscribers you wouldn't have won three years ago?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite convergence bundle, price-value positioning, and customer service improvements; likely to reference NPS scores
  • Good answer signature: Specific subscriber-type or geography where share gain is documented; honest about where Proximus or Telenet is winning; NPS data with trend
  • Bad answer signature: Generic "customer-centric" language; no specific competitive win story; NPS cited without peer comparison or trend
  • Follow-up probe: "What percentage of your new residential convergence subscribers were previously Proximus or Telenet customers — and what was the primary switch driver?"
  • Diagnostic note: Share gain from specific competitors is the only durable evidence of competitive position improvement; if management can't cite a specific win story, the differentiation is aspirational

Sceptical bullet: The three-player structure creates a comfortable oligopoly narrative; the bear case is not price war but gradual network quality gap widening if Proximus's fibre investment materially outspends Orange Belgium's cable upgrade — ask for the relative capex-per-home-passed comparison

7. THEME §5 — Management Quality + Operator Track Record

J-front verdict: CEO/CFO credibility on VOO execution is the load-bearing management quality question; incentive structure alignment with FCF vs. EBITDA is the single most diagnostic management-quality probe.

Q5.1 — Incentive structure: FCF vs. EBITDA weighting (Power of incentives — Concept A)

  • Question: "Can you walk us through the exact weighting of your short-term and long-term incentive plans — specifically what percentage of your variable compensation is tied to FCF per share versus EBITDA-AL, and has that weighting changed since the VOO acquisition closed?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite EBITDA and/or EPS as the primary STI metric; LTI likely tied to TSR with EBITDA underpinning; unlikely to have changed post-VOO
  • Good answer signature: Meaningful FCF-per-share weighting in LTI (>25%); explicit net debt target in bonus scorecard; answers without hesitation and can cite the actual percentage
  • Bad answer signature: "Compensation is aligned with shareholder value" without specifics; cannot cite actual weightings; or EBITDA weighting dominates with no FCF component
  • Follow-up probe: "If FCF per share in 2025 comes in below your internal plan, does any of your personal compensation adjust — and is that metric visible in the remuneration report?"
  • Diagnostic note: Pure EBITDA weighting = incentive to grow revenue and EBITDA at expense of capex discipline; FCF weighting = operator archetype aligned with shareholder value; this is a binary discriminator between value-compounder and empire-builder

Q5.2 — Sandbagging discipline and guidance track record

  • Question: "Looking at your last four annual guidance sets — how many times did you end above the midpoint, and is there a systematic pattern of conservative guidance on synergies, capex, or EBITDA?"
  • Expected answer: Management will claim guidance has been met or exceeded; unlikely to volunteer any miss
  • Good answer signature: Acknowledges specific guidance misses honestly; explains the cause (external vs. internal); cites a guidance philosophy (e.g. "we guide to what we can see with high confidence")
  • Bad answer signature: "We have a track record of delivering on commitments" without specifics; no acknowledgement of any miss
  • Follow-up probe: "In 2022-2023 when energy costs spiked, did your EBITDA guidance incorporate or lag the energy cost signal — and what was the miss magnitude if any?"
  • Diagnostic note: Sandbagging is a positive signal for investor confidence; systematically meeting guidance within a narrow band suggests either very good forecasting discipline or very conservative guidance — both are positive; a pattern of guidance misses is a structural red flag

Q5.3 — Crisis playbook: what went wrong in VOO integration

  • Question: "Every large acquisition surfaces at least one unexpected negative — what is the single biggest negative surprise from the VOO integration to date, and how did you respond?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely cite a minor operational issue (IT migration timing, customer overlap complexity) and frame it as resolved; the revealing answer is the specificity and honesty
  • Good answer signature: Names a specific, material surprise; explains the response mechanism; gives a current status that is verifiable; does not minimise the surprise
  • Bad answer signature: "The integration has gone better than expected" — this is a red-flag non-answer; or cites only a technical / minor issue while deflecting from financial or cultural surprises
  • Follow-up probe: "Has the surprise changed any of your assumptions in the synergy model or the long-range plan — and if so, by how much?"
  • Diagnostic note: Quality of candour on this question is a meta-signal; management that cannot name a specific surprise either hasn't been paying attention or is withholding — both are negative

Q5.4 — Hiring posture and succession bench

  • Question: "How many senior hires (VP and above) have you made externally versus promoted internally in the last 18 months — and is the leadership bench strong enough to execute the convergence strategy without the current CEO/CFO in seat?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite a mix of internal promotions and targeted external hires; unlikely to address succession directly
  • Good answer signature: Has a clear succession bench and can name specific leaders developed for the next level; external hires are targeted at specific capability gaps (e.g. digital, enterprise ICT)
  • Bad answer signature: Deflects succession question entirely; or cites only one or two key people below themselves
  • Follow-up probe: "If you were to leave Orange Belgium in the next 12 months, who is the internal candidate most likely to step up — and what is their track record?"
  • Diagnostic note: Key-man risk is systematically underpriced; a company reliant on one or two leaders for an integration-phase is vulnerable to a management departure discount

Sceptical bullet: Management teams are trained to present a composed crisis narrative; the tell is not the content of the answer but the latency — a CEO who answers "biggest negative surprise" instantly with a specific, non-trivial example is more credible than one who pauses and then produces a minor operational footnote

8. THEME §6 — Risk + Downside (Pre-Mortem Cross-Ref)

J-front verdict: Three pre-mortem failure modes dominate: (a) VOO integration runs 18 months longer than plan with €150-200m additional one-off cost, (b) BIPT adverse broadband access ruling, (c) leverage covenant breach if FCF disappoints — questions in this theme test each failure mode directly.

Q6.1 — Bear-case probe: integration delay scenario

  • Question: "If VOO integration runs 18 months behind the current plan — what is the additional one-off cost, the EBITDA-AL impact, and the leverage implication — and have you stress-tested this with your banking syndicate?"
  • Expected answer: Management will say the integration is on track and stress-testing is ongoing; unlikely to volunteer a specific delay-cost number
  • Good answer signature: Has a quantified delay scenario in the CFO's model; can cite the leverage impact; confirms the banking syndicate has the same information
  • Bad answer signature: "We don't plan for failure" or "The integration is on track" without quantification of the downside scenario
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the covenant headroom on your RCF at current leverage, and at what EBITDA level does the nearest covenant become binding?"
  • Diagnostic note: A CFO who cannot quantify the delay scenario is either not running the model or unwilling to share it — both are negative signals; a quantified delay scenario with covenant headroom confirmed is a positive

Q6.2 — Pricing fragility: what breaks the ARPU model

  • Question: "If Proximus or Telenet launches a sustained price promotion on converged bundles in 2025, what is Orange Belgium's response playbook — and at what ARPU level does your convergence margin story break?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite brand positioning and loyalty as defences; likely reference "value-based pricing discipline"
  • Good answer signature: Has a specific margin-floor analysis; knows the ARPU level at which convergence investment return breaks; has modelled a price-war scenario with a specific EBITDA impact
  • Bad answer signature: "We compete on value, not price" — this is a deflection; or "we have not seen pricing aggression from competitors" without a scenario for what they would do if they did
  • Follow-up probe: "In the last 12 months, have you had to match any promotional price from Proximus or Telenet — and what was the revenue impact?"
  • Diagnostic note: Evidence of matching competitor promotions = implicit price-war already underway; denial = either genuinely stable pricing environment or management not tracking competitive response carefully enough

Q6.3 — Regulatory exposure: BIPT and EU policy

  • Question: "Beyond the current BIPT broadband access proceeding, what is the next regulatory decision that could materially affect Orange Belgium's P&L — and what is the range of outcomes and your probability-weighted view?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite BIPT as manageable; may reference EU Gigabit Infrastructure Act implications; unlikely to give a probability-weighted scenario
  • Good answer signature: Names the specific next regulatory decision with a date; gives a range of EBITDA outcomes; has a lobbying/engagement strategy that is specific
  • Bad answer signature: Generic "we engage constructively with regulators" without a named decision or timeline
  • Follow-up probe: "Has Orange Group (parent) been actively involved in the BIPT dialogue — and does the parent's broader EU regulatory experience inform your local strategy?"
  • Diagnostic note: Parent Orange SA's regulatory experience in France is relevant precedent; if OBEL is managing BIPT independently without parent support, regulatory risk is higher

Q6.4 — False-friend probe: what consensus is wrong about

  • Question: "What is the single most widely held investor view about Orange Belgium that you think is wrong — and what evidence would change your mind about your own bullish view?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely cite market underestimation of synergy potential or convergence premium; interesting if they cite something unexpected
  • Good answer signature: Gives a specific investor misconception with evidence; and genuinely names one piece of evidence that would change their own view (intellectual honesty signal)
  • Bad answer signature: Says investors underestimate the business and provides only positives; cannot name any evidence that would change their own view
  • Follow-up probe: "Has there been a data point or trend in the last six months that has caused you to revise downward any internal assumption?"
  • Diagnostic note: A CEO who can name their own potential blind spot is a high-quality operator; one who cannot is either overconfident or trained to never show doubt — both are negative in a high-leverage integration

Sceptical bullet: Q19 ACH matrix items NOT covered by any question in this memo — (a) Orange Group (parent) strategic review risk: if parent decides to divest OBEL, the strategic context changes completely — this is NOT directly tested in §1-§6 (see §8 for this probe); (b) 5G spectrum re-farming cost in 2026-2027 is not explicitly covered — add to the follow-up question bank; (c) Enterprise ICT margin profile versus retail convergence is tested in Q1.3 but not in detail on gross margin decomposition — should be added if time permits in the meeting

9. CROSS-CUTTING META + NON-OBVIOUS QUESTIONS — §8

J-front verdict: The questions management would most prefer not to be asked are the Orange Group parent relationship, the personal exit plan of the CEO, and the candid post-mortem on VOO deal economics at original deal price.

Non-obvious angle (⚡): Parent relationship — the question consensus never asks

  • Question: "Has Orange SA (parent) ever communicated internally that Orange Belgium's strategic role within the group has changed since the VOO acquisition — and is OBEL a permanent core asset or a monetisation option?"
  • Expected answer: Management will assert OBEL is a core asset; will cite the parent's support for the VOO acquisition
  • Good answer signature: Specific reference to group capital allocation framework showing OBEL's standing; cites explicit parent commitment horizon
  • Bad answer signature: Pivots to "we focus on what we can control"; declines to characterise parent's view; "that's a question for the group"
  • Diagnostic note: ⚡ This is the question that surfaces whether the CEO is building a business or managing a disposal — the answer is diagnostic of the 3-year holding thesis; if management is in "managing for exit" mode, capex discipline and synergy capture will both be sub-optimal

WH Smith probe — the unconventional revenue question

  • Question: "What is the most surprising piece of customer feedback you have received in the last six months — and did it change anything you are doing operationally?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite a service-quality or digital-experience theme; the interesting signal is whether the feedback was genuinely operationally disruptive
  • Good answer signature: Names a specific, non-obvious customer insight (e.g. VOO customers responded adversely to rebranding; enterprise customers flagging support quality post-integration); and shows a specific operational response
  • Bad answer signature: Generic NPS improvement narrative; "customers tell us they value our service" — this answer has zero diagnostic value
  • Diagnostic note: The quality of management's market listening is a predictor of competitive positioning durability; management that cannot recall a specific surprising customer insight is either not gathering it or not processing it

Edge-detection probe — the single most-worrying metric

  • Question (⚡ — unconventional crystalliser): "If you had to identify the single internal metric that, if it moved against you by 10%, would most worry you about the 2025-2026 plan — what is that metric, and what is the current trend?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely cite a network quality KPI or a broadband net-add rate; the interesting answer is if they cite a financial metric they don't normally disclose
  • Good answer signature: Names a specific, non-obvious metric (e.g. VOO broadband churn in Proximus-fibre-competed postcodes; average revenue per converged household at month 12 of tenure; enterprise RFP win rate) and gives the current direction
  • Bad answer signature: Names a generic market metric (e.g. "GDP growth") or a metric that is already publicly disclosed — this suggests either no genuine internal worry metric or a refusal to engage honestly
  • Follow-up probe: "Is that metric currently moving in the right direction — and what's the trend over the last two quarters?"
  • Diagnostic note: The specific metric named (and whether management volunteers the current trend without being pushed) is a high-resolution signal of operational candour; this is the single most information-dense question in the meeting if answered honestly

Multiple-truths probe (Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 3)

  • Question: "I accept the bull case — VOO synergies, convergence ARPU uplift, three-player stability — AND I also see a scenario where Belgian broadband pricing softens, fibre overbuild accelerates, and leverage limits your response flexibility. Can you tell me which part of that bear case you take most seriously, and what you are doing about it?"
  • Expected answer: Management will acknowledge the fibre risk and reference network upgrade plans; the "and yet" question prevents a pure bull deflection
  • Good answer signature: Acknowledges all three bear-case components with honest assessment of probability and timeline; identifies a specific mitigation that is not already in the public disclosure
  • Bad answer signature: Dismisses any component of the bear case; reverts to bull-case narrative without acknowledging the co-existence of both truths
  • Diagnostic note: Management that can hold both truths simultaneously is a high-quality operator; management that reverts to bull-only narrative when explicitly invited to engage with the bear is a red flag for group-think or over-optimism

Incentive probe — the alignment crystalliser

  • Question: "If you personally held 100% of Orange Belgium shares with no liquidity for five years, what would you do differently in the next 18 months — and is there anything you're currently doing that you wouldn't do if you had skin in the game at that level?"
