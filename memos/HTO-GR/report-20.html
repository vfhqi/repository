2. KEY FINDINGS - VERDICT — Top-3 most-diagnostic questions identified: (1) fibre monetisation per-line economics vs build cost; (2) DT relationship governance post-2025 strategic review; (3) management's own bear-case trigger on Greek mobile ARPU

  • DIAGNOSTIC PRIMACY: Q on fibre ARPU uplift per activated line is the single highest-discrimination question — bull thesis lives or dies on whether consumer FTTH yields >€35/month blended ARPU at scale
  • CAPITAL ALLOCATION ambiguity: OTE's dividend + buyback cadence is partially constrained by DT's stake mechanics and Greek state minority — management's stated vs revealed capital-allocation preference is the second-order diagnostic
  • MOBILE MARGIN RISK: Greek mobile market is three-player (OTE/Cosmote, Vodafone GR, Nova/United) — any management commentary suggesting pricing discipline is durable should be interrogated hard; consensus assumes it, bear case breaks it
  • ROMANIA DRAG: Romania segment has historically compressed group margins; exit/restructure path is under-interrogated in public filings — this is a clean DD gap
  • COST TRAJECTORY: OTE has guided OpEx reduction tied to network modernisation; whether "digital transformation" savings are structural or one-time is the margin-thesis load-bearing question
  • INCENTIVE STRUCTURE GAP: CEO Eustathios Kyriakos-Sarantis's compensation structure (DT-aligned vs local equity incentive) creates a potential misalignment with minority shareholder value maximisation — one of the ≤3 ⚡ probes targets this
  • SCEPTICAL OPENING: Management at DT-controlled subsidiaries systematically over-represent strategic alignment with parent while under-disclosing parent service-fee drag — rehearsed fluency on DT "partnership" is a red flag, not comfort

3. §1 — EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (Top-3 Most-Diagnostic Questions)

  • VERDICT — most-diagnostic triad: fibre monetisation yield, DT governance leverage, and Greek mobile ARPU durability are the three questions that maximally discriminate bull from bear
  • Top diagnostic Q1: "What is your realised ARPU uplift on FTTH-activated lines vs legacy copper, and how does that split between price, speed-tier mix, and bundle attach rate?" — answer is quantifiable and directly tests the revenue-side of the fibre bull thesis
  • Top diagnostic Q2: "What services does Deutsche Telekom charge OTE for, at what aggregate annual cost, and how is that pricing governed?" — DT parent-fee opacity is the most likely source of hidden value leakage; management deflection here is itself signal
  • Top diagnostic Q3: "If Greek mobile ARPU declines 5% from today's level, walk me through the EBITDA impact and which cost lines flex to partially offset it" — forces a P&L walk; vague answer = insufficient internal modelling or unwillingness to disclose
  • SCEPTICAL BULLET: the analyst should resist NOT asking the DT governance question because it feels impolite toward a controlling shareholder — Concept B CA1: that discomfort is exactly why it is the most valuable question; go to it

4. §2 — THEME 1: Revenue Drivers + Visibility

  • VERDICT — revenue-side uncertainty is moderate-high: fibre rollout progress is disclosed, but monetisation yield per activated line is not; mobile ARPU durability is an unanchored consensus assumption

Q1 — Fibre ARPU uplift

  • Question: "What is the realised average monthly ARPU on FTTH-activated residential lines, and how does it compare to legacy copper lines you migrated from?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite a blended consumer broadband ARPU in the €25-35 range and claim uplift is "meaningful" without giving a precise delta
  • Good answer signature: Cites specific per-line uplift (e.g. +€8-12/month), explains speed-tier attach, confirms churn reduction vs copper
  • Bad answer signature: Deflects to aggregate broadband revenue growth; refuses to isolate FTTH-specific economics; conflates broadband ARPU with bundle ARPU
  • Follow-up probe: "What % of your FTTH-activated base has taken a speed tier above your base 100Mbps tier, and what is the ARPU on that cohort?"
  • Diagnostic note: Fluent, quantified answer = bull case (fibre monetisation is real and scalable); vague/deflected answer = bear-case flag (rollout is capex-heavy but yield is uncertain); false-friend watch — high aggregate broadband ARPU growth can mask mix-unfavourable fibre attach if legacy copper is repriced upward simultaneously

Q2 — Mobile ARPU and competitive discipline

  • Question: "Greek mobile ARPU has been broadly stable for 3 years. What is sustaining that, and where is the next source of pricing risk?"
  • Expected answer: Management will credit rational three-player market structure and shift to value-added services / convergence bundling as ARPU stabilisers
  • Good answer signature: Names specific competitive dynamics (e.g. Nova's financial constraints limiting aggression), quantifies the convergence-bundle ARPU premium vs standalone mobile
  • Bad answer signature: Generic "disciplined market" answer with no competitor-specific content; inability to quantify convergence uplift; avoids mentioning Nova/Vodafone by name
  • Follow-up probe: "If Vodafone GR or Nova were to launch a SIM-only price campaign at €5-8 below your current entry tier, what is your playbook?"
  • Diagnostic note: Bear case for HTO-GR mobile is a re-run of the 2012-2015 Greek market destruction; question tests whether management has war-gamed the downside; ACH cross-ref — the "mobile repricing" scenario is the #1 plausibility item in any pre-mortem

Q3 — Romania revenue trajectory

  • Question: "Romania revenue has been declining/stagnant for several years. Is the path forward organic turnaround, restructure, or exit — and what is the timeline?"
  • Expected answer: Management will express commitment to Romania with vague "restructuring underway" language; explicit exit discussion is unlikely but their degree of hedging is signal
  • Good answer signature: Honest assessment of competitive position in Romania; explicit EBITDA margin target and timeline; willingness to say "exit is an option if X threshold not met"
  • Bad answer signature: Strategic boilerplate about "growth opportunities in Romania"; no margin target; no timeline; no exit optionality acknowledged
  • Follow-up probe: "What is Romania's EBITDA margin today vs three years ago, and what is the terminal margin assumption in your internal plan?"
  • Diagnostic note: Romania is a chronic drag; bear-case scenario is that OTE continues subsidising a sub-scale operation; bull case is clean exit or inflection — management credibility on this question is a proxy for operator honesty across the board

Q4 — Enterprise/B2B revenue quality

  • Question: "What share of enterprise revenue is contracted on multi-year terms, and what is the average remaining contract life?"
  • Expected answer: Management will claim strong backlog visibility; likely cite 60-70% contracted but may not disclose remaining contract life
  • Good answer signature: Specific contracted-revenue % + average contract duration (>24 months) + explicit comment on auto-renewal clauses
  • Bad answer signature: "Strong backlog" language without quantification; inability to distinguish B2B contracted vs transactional
  • Follow-up probe: "What % of enterprise revenue renews in the next 12 months, and what is the expected retention rate?"

  • Diagnostic note: Enterprise revenue is the highest-quality OTE revenue stream; if management can't quantify contract coverage, the quality of their own planning process is questionable

  • SCEPTICAL BULLET: fluent answers on revenue visibility are the most-rehearsed in telecom management conversations; a CFO who gives precise ARPU-uplift numbers is either highly instrumented (bull signal) or has been coached to deflect with false precision — cross-check the number against disclosed segment revenue and subscriber count arithmetic in the filing

5. §3 — THEME 2: Margin Trajectory + Levers

  • VERDICT — margin trajectory is the central financial uncertainty: OTE's EBITDA margin has been one of the highest in Southern European telco, but the combination of fibre capex absorption, energy cost exposure, and labour restructuring creates a wide range of plausible outcomes

Q5 — OpEx structural reduction

  • Question: "Your digital transformation programme targets OpEx reduction. How much of that saving is structural vs one-time, and what is the annual run-rate benefit by 2026?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite a cumulative savings number (likely €50-100m over 3 years) and claim mostly structural; one-time vs recurring split will be vague
  • Good answer signature: Provides split between network-related savings (structural) vs headcount-related (one-time restructuring with tail), quantifies by cost line
  • Bad answer signature: Aggregate savings number only; no split; conflates transformation investment with gross savings; cannot quantify net benefit
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the gross savings before reinvestment into growth capex, and what is the reinvestment rate against those savings?"
  • Diagnostic note: Bull case depends on OpEx leverage at scale; bear case is that savings are reinvested into fibre/5G at parity, leaving margin flat; the "gross vs net savings" distinction is the load-bearing discriminant

Q6 — Energy cost and hedging

  • Question: "Energy is a material OpEx line for network operators. What is OTE's current energy cost as % of OpEx, and what portion is hedged over the next 12-24 months?"
  • Expected answer: Management will acknowledge energy exposure; hedging posture likely partial (30-60% over 12 months); Greek electricity price dynamics are a real variable
  • Good answer signature: Specific % of OpEx, hedge ratio, hedge instrument (fixed contract vs spot index), and sensitivity to unhedged spot price
  • Bad answer signature: "We manage energy actively" without a number; no hedge ratio; no sensitivity disclosure
  • Follow-up probe: "If spot electricity prices move +20% from today, what is the unhedged EBITDA impact in absolute euros?"
  • Diagnostic note: Energy cost is an underappreciated margin risk in Southern European telco; failure to quantify = either material exposure or under-instrumented management

Q7 — Labour / headcount trajectory

  • Question: "OTE has reduced headcount materially over the past decade. What is the remaining headcount reduction runway, and what is the cost per departure under your current programmes?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite ongoing voluntary redundancy; residual pool of legacy workforce still eligible; cost per departure likely €50-80k range
  • Good answer signature: Remaining eligible headcount, cost per departure, net present value of future savings, explicit statement on whether programmes are funded from OpEx or treated as exceptional
  • Bad answer signature: "We continue to rightsize" without numbers; inability to quantify remaining savings potential; avoidance of cost-per-departure figure
  • Follow-up probe: "At what point does headcount reduction become a service-quality risk — are you below the efficient frontier on customer-facing staff?"
  • Diagnostic note: OTE's legacy labour structure is a historical drag; bull case includes continued structural release; bear case is that remaining workforce is protected (union agreements) and further cuts trigger industrial action

Q8 — Fibre capex trajectory and peak capex timing

  • Question: "When does OTE reach peak annual fibre capex, what is that peak number, and what is the expected annual capex run-rate 3 years post-peak?"
  • Expected answer: Management likely guided peak capex in the 2024-2026 window; run-rate normalisation to <€600m/year post-peak is the consensus expectation
  • Good answer signature: Specific peak year + peak capex figure + normalised run-rate + FCF implication of the normalisation (e.g. +€150m annual FCF uplift)
  • Bad answer signature: "Capex remains elevated through the plan period" without a peak year or normalised level; no FCF bridge
  • Follow-up probe: "What % of your total fibre rollout target remains to be built, and what is the build cost per passing for the remaining tranche?"

  • Diagnostic note: FCF inflection is the bull-case trigger for share price re-rating; if management cannot provide a peak-capex date with confidence, the FCF inflection thesis is a longer-dated option than the market prices

  • SCEPTICAL BULLET: margin targets in DT-controlled subsidiary presentations have a documented pattern of being aspirational rather than commitment-grade — ask whether EBITDA margin guidance is a "target", a "floor", or an "internal budget number"; the precise word used is diagnostic

6. §4 — THEME 3: Capital Allocation + Balance Sheet

  • VERDICT — capital allocation is partially constrained by DT governance and Greek state optics: OTE's dividend policy and buyback cadence are not fully management-discretionary — understanding who actually controls the capital return decision is more valuable than the stated policy

Q9 — Dividend decision-making process

  • Question: "OTE's dividend policy is set annually. Who has effective decision rights — OTE board, Deutsche Telekom, or Greek state through its minority stake?"
  • Expected answer: Management will emphasise OTE board independence; DT's influence will be acknowledged obliquely; Greek state veto rights (if any) will be minimised
  • Good answer signature: Transparent description of DT's practical influence over payout; explicit statement on whether DT has a stated preference on payout ratio; acknowledgment of any Greek state objection history
  • Bad answer signature: "The OTE board decides independently" with no further detail; refusal to discuss DT's input; no mention of the Greek state's posture
  • Follow-up probe: "Has Deutsche Telekom ever indicated a preferred dividend yield or payout ratio in board discussions?"
  • Diagnostic note: DT uses OTE as a cash-generative subsidiary; if DT's preference is for high payout, that is bullish for dividend sustainability but potentially at the expense of reinvestment; if DT's preference is for retained cash for intragroup purposes, that is a value leakage risk

Q10 — Share buyback programme discipline

  • Question: "OTE has been running buyback programmes. What is the price-sensitivity of your buyback — do you buy aggressively at current valuation or does internal value-vs-price discipline vary the cadence?"
  • Expected answer: Management will claim valuation-driven buyback; in practice, DT-controlled subsidiaries tend to run buybacks mechanically
  • Good answer signature: Explicit IRR-hurdle or price-to-intrinsic-value discipline; willingness to say "we would stop buyback and accelerate fibre investment if capex returns exceed buyback returns"
  • Bad answer signature: "We buy back shares as part of our capital return policy" with no price-sensitivity; no mention of opportunity cost vs capex
  • Follow-up probe: "At what EV/EBITDA multiple would you consider acquisitions more attractive than buybacks?"
  • Diagnostic note: Mechanical buybacks regardless of valuation indicate DT mandate rather than independent capital allocation; this matters for the investor who is buying OTE at current multiples — if management is indifferent to price, so is the most natural incremental buyer

Q11 — Leverage target and headroom

  • Question: "What is OTE's net leverage target, and at what leverage level would you consider a special distribution?"
  • Expected answer: OTE has historically operated with low leverage (~1x net debt/EBITDA); management will likely target 0.8-1.2x; special distribution probably requires <0.5x
  • Good answer signature: Specific leverage target range + trigger conditions for special distribution + FCF waterfall priority (capex > ordinary dividend > buyback > special distribution)
  • Bad answer signature: "Conservative leverage approach" without a number; no trigger for special distribution; no FCF waterfall clarity
  • Follow-up probe: "Does Deutsche Telekom have any preference on OTE's leverage that influences your target range?"

  • Diagnostic note: Leverage headroom is the latent capital return optionality; understanding whether DT constrains or facilitates special distributions determines how much of that optionality is real vs illusory

  • SCEPTICAL BULLET: OTE's RONIC on fibre investment is the most critical return-on-capital question and is conspicuously absent from investor presentations — the absence is not accidental; any management team that can't articulate RONIC on their largest capital commitment either hasn't modelled it or is unwilling to defend it

7. §5 — THEME 4: Strategy + Competitive Position

  • VERDICT — strategic positioning has surface clarity but depth uncertainty: OTE's fixed-mobile convergence narrative is well-articulated; what is under-probed is the moat trajectory as fibre overbuild risk emerges and 5G capex compounds

Q12 — Fibre overbuild risk

  • Question: "What % of OTE's FTTH build footprint is currently overbuilt by an alternative network operator, and what is your modelled market share assumption in overbuild areas?"
  • Expected answer: Management will acknowledge some overbuild (likely 10-20% of build area) but claim market share defence is strong due to brand and service bundling
  • Good answer signature: Specific overbuild % + observed market share in overbuild areas vs non-overbuild + ARPU comparison; honest assessment of where overbuild is accelerating
  • Bad answer signature: "Competition exists but we are confident in our market position" with no data; refusal to quantify overbuild penetration by competitor
  • Follow-up probe: "Which geographic markets have the highest overbuild density, and what is your churn rate in those specific areas vs national average?"
  • Diagnostic note: Overbuild is the structural bear-case accelerant for European fibre investment stories; management's willingness to quantify overbuild exposure is a proxy for operator credibility; false-friend watch — high aggregate market share can mask deteriorating share in overbuilt areas

Q13 — 5G strategy and return expectations

  • Question: "What is OTE's 5G capex commitment over the next 3 years, and what specific revenue use cases underpin the return expectation?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite a capex envelope and reference enterprise IoT / B2B 5G use cases as revenue drivers; consumer 5G premium pricing will be vague
  • Good answer signature: Specific 5G capex number + B2B vs consumer revenue split + return timeline + honest acknowledgment that 5G consumer premium is limited in Greece currently
  • Bad answer signature: "5G is strategically important" without revenue model; 5G capex conflated into total network investment without isolation; no RONIC framework for 5G
  • Follow-up probe: "Which B2B 5G verticals have you closed commercial contracts in, and what is the aggregate contracted value?"

  • Diagnostic note: 5G returns remain unproven across European telcos; if management cannot articulate a specific revenue model with contracted evidence, 5G capex is a near-term drag with optionality value only — which is fine, but should be modelled as such

  • SCEPTICAL BULLET: Greek competitive dynamics are structurally different from Northern European markets (lower income per capita, higher customer price sensitivity) — management's tendency to benchmark OTE against DT Germany or Orange France on convergence ARPU opportunity is a systematic upward bias; ask explicitly whether the Greek consumer convergence ARPU opportunity is comparable to Spain or Portugal rather than Germany

8. §6 — THEME 5: Management Quality + Operator Track Record

  • VERDICT — management quality signals are mixed: OTE's operational execution on fibre rollout has been creditable; but DT-subsidiary CEO incentive alignment with minority shareholders is structurally uncertain

Q14 — Compensation structure and incentive alignment

(Power of incentives — Concept A: mandatory probe)

  • Question: "What % of your total compensation is tied to OTE standalone metrics vs Deutsche Telekom group metrics, and what are the 2-3 specific KPIs in your LTI plan?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely deflect or give a high-level answer; DT-group metrics are probably dominant in LTI; standalone OTE TSR may be a component
  • Good answer signature: Transparent disclosure of LTI structure; explicit statement that OTE TSR or OTE EBITDA/FCF is the primary LTI driver; acknowledgment that DT-group metrics are secondary
  • Bad answer signature: "Compensation is market-competitive and performance-linked" without specifics; refusal to characterise the KPI split; immediate pivot to OTE's performance record
  • Follow-up probe: "If OTE's share price underperforms over your vesting period but DT group metrics are met, does your LTI still pay out?"
  • Diagnostic note: DT-controlled CEO compensation aligned predominantly to group metrics creates a principal-agent problem for OTE minority investors; this is the most structurally important management quality question; Concept A: incentives predict behaviour more reliably than stated strategy

Q15 — Sandbagging discipline and guidance track record

  • Question: "Looking back at your last 3 annual guidance sets, how did actual EBITDA and FCF compare to initial guidance — and what drove the variance?"
  • Expected answer: Management will claim consistent guidance delivery; actual OTE track record is strong guidance delivery with modest upward bias (typical DT-subsidiary conservative guidance culture)
  • Good answer signature: Proactively discloses the guidance-vs-actual table; explains variance drivers (upside from cost savings, downside from energy prices); honest about where they were wrong
  • Bad answer signature: "We have a strong track record" without the actual data; attributes all upside to management skill, all downside to external factors
  • Follow-up probe: "Has OTE ever missed EBITDA guidance in a year where you hit all your operational KPIs? What does that tell you about your guidance construction process?"
  • Diagnostic note: Sandbagging discipline is a critical DD attribute; consistent guide-and-beat is a mechanical signal for the analyst; DT-subsidiary guidance culture tends toward conservatism, which is bullish for EPS trajectory reliability

Q16 — Crisis playbook

  • Question: "In the 2020-2021 Greek economic shock and COVID period, what was the first cost line you cut, and what was the last one you would have cut?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite specific cost actions (likely marketing, T&E, discretionary capex deferral) as first cuts; customer-facing service investment as the protected line
  • Good answer signature: Specific, ranked crisis-response actions; explicit statement on which capex is deferrable vs non-deferrable; evidence that the crisis playbook has been stress-tested recently
  • Bad answer signature: Generic "we prioritise resilience" answer; inability to name specific cost lines; no ranked priority
  • Follow-up probe: "If EBITDA declined 15% from today's level, what would the FCF impact be after your crisis-response actions?"

  • Diagnostic note: Crisis playbook credibility is a proxy for management's depth of operational knowledge vs presentation-layer management; the analyst should watch whether management answers from operational memory or from IR script

  • SCEPTICAL BULLET: DT-subsidiary CEOs are typically selected for operational execution and DT-system compatibility, not for independent strategic vision or minority-shareholder advocacy — asking about a time management DISAGREED with DT's strategic direction is the highest-signal management quality probe; management that has never pushed back is not independent; management that can describe a specific pushback with outcome is genuinely operator-grade

9. §7 — THEME 6: Risk + Downside (Pre-Mortem Cross-Ref)

  • VERDICT — bear-case probes are the most under-articulated in consensus research: the pre-mortem scenarios most worth testing are mobile ARPU break, DT service-fee escalation, and fibre overbuild acceleration

Q17 — Mobile ARPU durability stress test

(Bear-case probe / ACH matrix cross-ref: mobile repricing scenario)

  • Question: "What would it take — competitor action, regulatory intervention, or demand shift — to put Greek mobile ARPU into structural decline, and how confident are you it won't happen in the next 3 years?"
  • Expected answer: Management will say competitive dynamics are stable; they will acknowledge MVNO risk and potential SIM-only pressure but downplay probability
  • Good answer signature: Identifies specific triggers (Nova financial distress leading to distressed pricing, MVNO entry, EU roaming regulation extension) with explicit probability assessment; honest acknowledgment of the last ARPU cycle
  • Bad answer signature: "The market has matured into a rational structure" without any scenario-specific risk identification; no acknowledgment of historical ARPU cycles in Greece
  • Follow-up probe: "In your internal stress scenario, what is the EBITDA impact of a 10% ARPU decline in Cosmote GR mobile over 24 months?"
  • Diagnostic note: Mobile ARPU is the single largest earnings sensitivity for OTE; management's willingness to engage with the quantitative downside is the most direct test of whether they have stress-tested their own plan

Q18 — DT service-fee and related-party transaction exposure

(False-friend probe — DT relationship)

  • Question: "What is the total annual cost OTE incurs for services provided by Deutsche Telekom group companies — IT, network, procurement, management fees — and what is the governance process for pricing those services?"
  • Expected answer: Management will acknowledge related-party transactions exist; will likely cite competitive benchmarking as the governance mechanism; absolute cost will be disclosed only if directly asked
  • Good answer signature: Provides aggregate annual related-party service cost, benchmarking methodology, and board approval process; acknowledges the inherent conflict of interest in self-dealing
  • Bad answer signature: "Transactions are arm's-length and approved by the board" with no figures; redirect to related-party disclosures in the annual report without elaboration
  • Follow-up probe: "Has the independent audit committee ever rejected or renegotiated a proposed DT service contract on the grounds that pricing was above market?"
  • Diagnostic note: Related-party transactions are the primary mechanism for value extraction from DT-controlled subsidiaries; this is the false-friend probe — the stock can look attractive on reported EBITDA while systematically underearning on a normalised basis if parent fees are excessive

Q19 — Regulatory exposure

  • Question: "What is OTE's exposure to EECC / NGA regulatory decisions on wholesale fibre access pricing, and what is the sensitivity of your investment case to an adverse ruling?"
  • Expected answer: Management will frame regulation as manageable; Greece's regulatory regime has been relatively supportive of OTE's fibre investment; management will likely cite the NRA's investment-incentive framework
  • Good answer signature: Specific pending regulatory decisions, timeline, worst-case wholesale access price, EBITDA sensitivity to adverse decision; acknowledgment of lobbying posture
  • Bad answer signature: "We engage constructively with regulators" without quantifying the exposure; no sensitivity analysis; no pending decision timeline
  • Follow-up probe: "If the NRA mandated open access to your FTTH network at cost-plus pricing, what would that do to your expected IRR on the fibre build?"
  • Diagnostic note: European fibre investment cases are universally exposed to wholesale access regulation; OTE's IRR on fibre is partly predicated on maintaining retail pricing power over wholesale entrants; the degree to which management has quantified this risk discriminates disciplined capital allocators from asset-builders

Q20 — Competitive response to cable/alternative broadband

  • Question: "Where does OTE face its most intense fixed broadband competition today, and what has your share trend been in those specific markets over the last 24 months?"
  • Expected answer: Management will point to Nova cable network as primary competitive threat in Athens/Thessaloniki; will claim overall share is stable or growing
  • Good answer signature: City-level or region-level share data; explicit acknowledgment of where share is under pressure; honest assessment of Nova's network upgrade trajectory
  • Bad answer signature: National aggregate share data only; no acknowledgment of geographic heterogeneity; Nova's competitive capability underestimated
  • Follow-up probe: "What is OTE's market share in Nova cable's core footprint areas, and how has it trended since Nova's DOCSIS 3.1 / fibre upgrade?"

  • Diagnostic note: Geographic concentration of competitive pressure matters; if OTE is losing share in high-density urban areas (highest ARPU), the national-level stability of share metrics is misleading

  • SCEPTICAL BULLET on Q19 ACH-matrix gaps: the pre-mortem failure mode NOT covered by any question above is the macro/sovereign scenario — a Greek fiscal crisis redux (2010-2015-style) would compress consumer ARPU, enterprise IT spend, and OTE's own cost of borrowing simultaneously; this scenario is low-probability but should be explicitly acknowledged; the analyst has chosen not to include a direct macro/sovereign stress question because management is unlikely to engage meaningfully with it — this is a legitimate omission, but it should be flagged as a model risk, not a management-testable item

10. §8 — Cross-Cutting Meta + Non-Obvious Questions

  • VERDICT — the questions management WOULDN'T volunteer answers to are the load-bearing diagnostic items: DT relationship governance, incentive misalignment, and overbuild quantification are the three topics where management deflection is most informative

Non-obvious angle — the "capex regret" question:

  • Parent: Ask management to identify the capex decision from the past 3 years they would reverse if they could
  • Sub-bullet: Most managements will dodge; the minority who engage are demonstrating genuine capital allocation self-awareness
  • Sub-bullet: Expected deflection: "All our investments have delivered according to plan" — this is itself a red flag; no large network operator bats 1.000 on capital allocation
  • Follow-up probe: "Which legacy IT system investment, in retrospect, could have been replaced by a cloud-native solution at lower cost and faster speed?"

WH Smith probe — the metric management tracks that investors don't:

  • Parent: "What is the single internal operating metric you track most closely that is NOT in your investor presentations, and why isn't it disclosed?"
  • Sub-bullet: This is a classic unconventional question — the answer reveals management's own view of what is genuinely predictive of business performance
  • Sub-bullet: Good answer: specific non-disclosed KPI with explanation (e.g. "FTTH activation speed per technician team" or "enterprise churn within 90 days of contract start")
  • Sub-bullet: Bad answer: "All our key metrics are disclosed" — signals either PR-scripted management or genuinely poor internal instrumentation

⚡ OUTLIER PROBE — the DT strategic optionality question:

  • Parent: "If Deutsche Telekom decided to divest its OTE stake in the next 3 years, what do you think the realistic acquirer universe is, and would you support a full private-equity buyout of OTE?"
  • Sub-bullet: Management will almost certainly not answer this directly — but HOW they deflect is diagnostic
  • Sub-bullet: A CEO who deflects nervously suggests DT has communicated something about strategic optionality; a CEO who deflects smoothly with "DT is committed to OTE" has rehearsed the answer, which is itself a signal
  • Sub-bullet: ⚡ This question has a low probability of a direct answer but the highest potential information content of any question in the session — management body language and deflection style is load-bearing

Cross-roads question — the convergence-ARPU ceiling:

  • Parent: "Bull case says convergence ARPU in Greece can reach €60-65/month. Bear case says Greek income per capita limits convergence ARPU to €45-50. Where do you think the realistic ceiling is, and why?"
  • Sub-bullet: This is the Concept B CA3 "multiple truths" question — explicitly frames both hypotheses and invites management to navigate between them rather than retreat to the bull case alone
  • Sub-bullet: Good answer: management engages with the bear-case ceiling, cites specific Greek affordability data, positions OTE's strategy relative to both scenarios
  • Sub-bullet: Bad answer: management defaults to bull case only, refuses to engage with the ceiling concept, says "we see significant headroom"

Edge-detection probe — Romania exit:

  • Parent: "What would have to be true about Romania's competitive position for you to recommend exit to the DT board?"
  • Sub-bullet: This forces management to state their own exit criteria, which they almost never do voluntarily
  • Sub-bullet: An operator who says "Romania needs to reach X% EBITDA margin by Y date or we exit" is showing genuine strategic discipline
  • Sub-bullet: An operator who says "Romania is a long-term strategic market" with no exit criteria is showing an inability or unwillingness to apply capital allocation discipline

Incentive probe — post-vesting behaviour:

  • Parent: "What do you plan to do with OTE shares that vest under your LTI programme — hold or sell?"
  • Sub-bullet: Share retention post-vesting is the most direct observable signal of management confidence in forward earnings
  • Sub-bullet: Management who sell immediately post-vesting at current valuation are expressing a view; management who hold are expressing a different view
  • Sub-bullet: This question is considered impolite in investor meetings, which is precisely why it should be asked

Diagnostic crystalliser — the "what would make you wrong" question:

  • Parent: "If the OTE investment case looks wrong 3 years from now, what will have been the cause — and what early indicators would have predicted it?"
  • Sub-bullet: Concept B CA3: this is the quintessential multiple-truths question that invites the "and yet" answer
  • Sub-bullet: Good answer: management names 2-3 specific leading indicators with current trends (e.g. "FTTH churn rate in activated cohort 12+ months" or "mobile ARPU in price-sensitive urban segments")
  • Sub-bullet: Bad answer: "We don't see material downside risk" — this is not an answer; it is a signal that management either hasn't done the pre-mortem or won't share it

⚡ OUTLIER PROBE — the "Greek state call option" question:

  • Parent: "The Greek state retains a minority stake in OTE. Is there any governance scenario under which the Greek state could use its position to block a transaction, impose a strategic direction, or extract a concession that would reduce returns for ordinary shareholders?"
