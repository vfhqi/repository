KEY FINDINGS - Structural verdict: The satellite communications value chain is a hub-and-spoke structure with SES occupying the infrastructure hub — the capacity layer between satellite manufacturers/launchers and broadcast/data distributors — a position with structurally above-average margin capture historically [#J]

  • Margin pool location: Satellite capacity (SES's core stage) and ground infrastructure collectively capture the largest EBIT margin pool in the chain, structurally superior to device manufacturing (commoditised), terminal retail (thin), and many content aggregation segments (fragmented competitive) [#J]

  • Pricing power signal: Mixed — SES has pricing power in video broadcasting (orbital slots + neighbourhood effects create lock-in) but faces structural pricing erosion in broadband/data capacity as HTS (High Throughput Satellites) and LEO competition increase supply [#J]

  • Supplier stage: Concentrated but not captive — Boeing, Airbus Defence, Thales Alenia Space, Maxar, and SpaceX dominate satellite manufacture and launch; SES has multi-vendor relationships but faces supplier power given long lead times and limited manufacturer count [#A]

  • Customer stage: Moderately concentrated — major customers are broadcasters (Sky, MBC, Canal+), telcos/ISPs, and government/institutional clients; video broadcast revenue is structurally declining as OTT erodes linear TV, shifting revenue mix toward data/government [#J]

  • Vertical integration threat: Credible from below (SpaceX Starlink integrating from launch into broadband service delivery) and from above (major telcos exploring own satellite capacity); SES's moat depends on orbital slots, spectrum licenses, and ground infrastructure that are non-replicable short-term [#J]

  • Change thesis anchor: SES's 2024 acquisition of Intelsat (completed, subject to regulatory conditions) is the primary value-chain event — consolidating two major GEO operators creates scale at the capacity stage, but also concentrates the structural risk of the GEO-vs-LEO technology transition [#J]

  • Structural evolution risk: LEO constellation deployment (Starlink, Amazon Kuiper, OneWeb/Eutelsat) is compressing GEO broadband pricing and redirecting new demand away from GEO; SES's MEO fleet (O3b mPOWER) partially addresses this but the transition is capital-intensive and incomplete [#J]

  • Single largest positional risk: GEO capacity commoditisation — if LEO/MEO supply continues to expand, SES's GEO broadband pricing power collapses, the video segment keeps shrinking, and the combined SES+Intelsat entity faces overcapacity with high fixed-cost leverage [#J]

  • IRR read: Structural position supports the thesis conditionally — the O3b mPOWER MEO build + Intelsat scale consolidation could reset SES's position in a data-centric chain; execution and timing of GEO-to-MEO revenue migration are the key uncertainties for 18M-3Y EPS trajectory [#I]

§1 — Value Chain Map + Stage Table

The satellite communications value chain is a hub-and-spoke structure centred on the satellite capacity operator, not a simple linear chain. The capacity layer sits between a concentrated upstream (manufacturers, launchers) and a fragmented-to-moderately-concentrated downstream (service providers, end users), creating a structural bottleneck that has historically generated above-industry-average margins.

  • Chain shape: Hub-and-spoke with the **satellite capacity layer as the hub; upstream arms are manufacturers + launchers; downstream arms are broadcasters, telcos, government, and enterprise — multiple customer verticals share the same infrastructure layer [#J]

  • Linear vs platform: Not purely linear — the capacity operator effectively acts as a two-sided infrastructure platform aggregating transponder supply (upstream) and distributing to multiple heterogeneous demand verticals (downstream); this platform-like quality differentiates it from a simple industrial chain [#A]

  • Stage count: Seven identifiable stages from raw component manufacturing through to end-user; SES occupies Stages 3-4 (satellite manufacturing customer + capacity operator + ground network operator); not a pure single-stage player [#J]

  • Geographic footprint: Genuinely global at the capacity stage — GEO satellites serve continental footprints (ARC positions); ground networks are geographically distributed; uniquely global relative to terrestrial telecoms infrastructure operators [#I]

  • Capital intensity gradient: Highest at Stages 2-4 (satellite manufacture, launch, and capacity operation); falls sharply at Stages 5-6 (service provision, content distribution); end-user retail is lowest-capex stage [#J]

§2 — SES's Position Within the Chain

  • Verdict: SES is a multi-stage integrated operator concentrated in Stage 4 (capacity) with partial downstream extension; not purely a single-stage specialist but the capacity layer dominates economics [#J]

  • Stage occupied: Primarily Stage 4 (GEO + MEO satellite capacity leasing), with secondary presence in Stage 5 (teleport/ground ops) and Stage 6 (managed data/government services) [#J]

  • Upstream integration: None — SES does not manufacture satellites or operate launch vehicles; it is a pure customer of Stages 1-3; this is standard for GEO operators and contrasts with SpaceX's vertical model [#A]

  • Downstream integration: Partial — SES operates ground infrastructure (teleports, gateways) and offers managed services to government/enterprise; but it does not own distribution to end consumers (no DTC retail play like Viasat's residential broadband) [#J]

  • O3b mPOWER significance: The MEO O3b mPOWER fleet represents SES's vertical repositioning within Stage 4 — shifting from GEO high-latency capacity (commoditising) toward MEO low-latency high-throughput capacity (differentiated and growing), partially insulating from LEO pressure [#I]

  • Intelsat merger: Post-merger SES+Intelsat creates the world's largest GEO satellite operator by fleet size and orbital slot portfolio; scale consolidation at Stage 4 increases negotiating leverage with Stage 3 suppliers and Stage 5-6 service providers [#J]

  • Peer integration vs comparables: Eutelsat/OneWeb integrates Stage 4 with Stage 4-alt (LEO via OneWeb) — a horizontal diversification rather than vertical; Viasat integrates Stage 4 (ViaSat-3) with Stage 6-7 (retail broadband, in-flight connectivity) — more downstream-integrated than SES; Telesat focuses purely on Stage 4 with Lightspeed LEO build [#A]

  • Sector peer context gap: No D-BATCH-25 cohort/sector peer research injected; peer comparisons above sourced from public filings and company presentations; flagged in QC [#I]

  • Revenue mix significance: SES's revenue is split approximately ~55% video / ~45% data+government (2023 standalone); video is Stage 4 capacity leased to Stage 6-7 broadcasters; data/government is Stage 4-5-6 integrated services; the mix is shifting structurally toward data [#J]

  • Positional asymmetry: SES's orbital arc positions in prime video neighbourhoods (Astra 19.2°E serving ~118m European homes) are non-replicable assets — even a fully-funded competitor cannot acquire these positions through market purchase; ITU priority rights are assigned historically [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: What is the bearish read on SES's positional choice? The multi-stage span (capacity + ground + managed services) may prevent SES from achieving the pure-play infrastructure multiples of a spectrum/slot landlord; partial downstream integration adds operating complexity without equivalent margin uplift; a purer capacity-only model (rent the slots, outsource the ground ops) might command higher multiples with lower capex intensity

§4 — Margin Distribution Across the Chain

  • Verdict: Stage 4 (satellite capacity operation) is the clear margin pool winner — EBITDA margins 50-70%, EBIT margins 15-35%, structurally superior to all other stages; SES occupies this stage but with declining margin trajectory in GEO video [#J]

  • Most-profitable stage: Stage 4 capacity operation — high barriers to entry (orbital slots, spectrum, capex), recurring contracted revenue, operating leverage on fixed satellite cost base; SES historical EBITDA margins ~65-70% (2019-2021 peak) now compressing toward 55-60% as video declines [#J]

  • Margin driver — regulatory: ITU orbital slot licensing creates a de-facto regulatory barrier at Stage 4 that no other stage possesses; this is the single most important structural margin driver for GEO operators including SES [#J]

  • Margin driver — switching cost: Neighbourhood effect at Astra 19.2°E — broadcasters cannot move to alternative positions without losing millions of consumer satellite dishes already aligned; creates high switching cost for video customers [#J]

  • Margin driver — scale: O3b mPOWER illustrates how scale translates to per-Gbps cost reduction; the MEO constellation's throughput economics are structurally superior to GEO on a per-bit basis for connectivity services, creating a scale-driven margin advantage vs smaller MEO competitors [#I]

  • Margin-thin stage: Stage 6 (service providers/systems integrators) — competitive fragmentation, customer price sensitivity, reseller margin compression; Speedcast ~10-15% EBITDA; managed service providers typically earn 15-25% EBITDA vs SES's Stage 4 core [#A]

  • Margin-thin stage: Stage 7 (end-consumer retail) — Starlink's retail model is demonstrably thin at current pricing; Sky satellite retail operates on narrow distribution margins; SES deliberately does not own retail exposure [#J]

  • Commoditised stage: Stage 2 (satellite manufacturing) — intensifying competition from Maxar, Airbus, Boeing, plus new entrants from small-sat ecosystem; manufacturing margins compressed vs 2000s peak; Long-term contracts limit pricing flexibility [#A]

  • Margin trend: GEO capacity margins contracting — SES's standalone EBITDA margin declined from ~70% (2019) to ~58-62% (2023) as video revenue declined faster than costs could be reduced; MEO/data partially offsets but not yet at scale to replace [#J]

SpaceX's marginal cost of capacity is falling faster than SES's can, given SpaceX's reusable launch and vertically-integrated manufacturing; if Starlink targets SES's enterprise/government data customers (beyond consumer), Stage 4 EBIT margins could compress to Stage 6 equivalents over a 5-7 year horizon — a scenario not priced in current consensus

§5 — Power Dynamics + Pricing Leverage

  • Verdict: SES has strong pricing power in video (neighbourhood lock-in) but structurally declining pricing power in data capacity (LEO/HTS supply expansion); supplier leverage is moderate given manufacturer concentration [#J]

  • Pricing power — video: Strongest in the portfolio — Astra 19.2°E neighbourhood effect means major European broadcasters (Sky Deutschland, ARD/ZDF, Canal+ Digital, Al Jazeera) cannot practically migrate; switching cost is dish re-alignment for millions of consumers — a de-facto captive position [#J]

  • Pricing power — video (constraint): But video volume is declining — pricing power over existing customers is high, but the addressable base is shrinking; SES cannot use pricing power to grow video revenue, only to defend per-transponder yields on a shrinking base [#J]

  • Pricing power — data/broadband: Weak and deteriorating — HTS GEO capacity (e.g., Intelsat IS-39, SES-17) competes directly with Hughes, Viasat, and increasingly Starlink; capacity prices per Mbps have fallen 50-70% over 2015-2024 (industry estimate, Euroconsult) — customers have optionality [#A]

  • Pricing power — government/defence: Strongest in data segment — O3b mPOWER's low latency and high throughput for government/military creates differentiation vs GEO alternatives; classified connectivity requirements and security-of-supply concerns reduce customer switching propensity [#J]

  • Switching cost — by segment: Video customers: very high (dish ecosystem); government: high (procurement cycles, security clearances, dedicated capacity agreements); enterprise data: moderate (multi-year contracts but alternatives growing); consumer broadband: low to moderate (Starlink portability increases switching) [#J]

  • Substitutes — video: OTT streaming is the structural substitute for satellite-delivered linear TV — not a direct substitute for SES's capacity (SES doesn't own content or platforms) but a substitute for the end service, reducing broadcaster demand for transponders [#J]

  • Substitutes — data: LEO constellations (Starlink, Kuiper, OneWeb) are direct substitutes for GEO broadband capacity with better latency economics for most use cases; MEO O3b competes more directly with LEO on latency but less on cost per bit [#J]

  • Supplier leverage — satellite manufacturers: Moderate-to-high supplier power — Boeing, Airbus Defence, Thales Alenia Space form a concentrated triad for large GEO satellites; SES has multi-vendor strategy (SES-14 Airbus, SES-15 Boeing, SES-17 Thales) to avoid single-vendor lock-in but cannot easily play them against each other below cost [#A]

  • Supplier leverage — launch: Improving for SES as buyer (SpaceX competition reduces prices) but SpaceX is the dominant reliable launcher for large GEO payloads, creating a new form of supplier concentration risk if SpaceX policies change [#J]

  • Customer leverage — broadcasters: Moderate — while neighbourhood lock-in constrains migration, broadcasters know their demand is structurally declining and may seek capacity price reductions at renewal; SES's negotiating position weakens as video TAM shrinks [#J]

  • Customer leverage — government: Government customers (US DoD, NATO, ESA, national agencies) have low price sensitivity and high continuity requirements; multi-year capacity agreements with government clients are SES's most stable revenue stream [#J]

  • Segment-specific power — maritime/aviation: Mixed — connectivity services for maritime/aviation are high-growth but competitive; Inmarsat (Viasat), Telesat, and Starlink all targeting; SES's MEO advantage on throughput partially differentiates but price competition is increasing [#I]

  • Dictated terms — GEO broadband: Telco and ISP customers increasingly have the ability to dictate pricing terms in GEO broadband capacity contracts — HTS supply glut means sellers compete on price; SES's integrated managed service (Stage 6) offer partially re-bundles to prevent pure commodity pricing [#J]

  • Regulatory barrier: ITU filing priority is an irreplaceable pricing support — no new entrant can legally occupy SES's GEO orbital positions; the regulatory moat is the deepest structural advantage in the chain [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: What is the bearish read on power dynamics? The neighbourhood effect moat in video is finite in time — as linear TV households fall below a critical mass (estimated 50% penetration by late 2020s in Western Europe), the neighbourhood effect itself weakens; at some threshold, enough consumers have migrated to streaming that dish re-alignment becomes viable, collapsing the switching-cost advantage entirely

§6 — Competitive Map at SES's Stage

  • Verdict: Stage 4 (GEO satellite capacity) is an oligopoly of 4-6 global players with high concentration; MEO is a duopoly; LEO is bifurcating between Starlink dominant and a fragmented rest-of-field [#J]

  • Competitor — leader (GEO, post-merger): SES + Intelsat (combined) — world's largest GEO operator by fleet (~100+ satellites combined), broadest orbital arc portfolio (~50+ GEO positions), strongest enterprise/government relationships; scale creates per-satellite cost advantages [#J]

  • Competitor — challenger : Eutelsat (with OneWeb) — European peer, ~€1.4bn revenue, ~36 GEO satellites plus OneWeb LEO constellation (~648 LEO satellites); vertical integration of GEO+LEO is strategic differentiator but integration execution has been complex; financially weaker than SES [#A]

  • Competitor — specialist (HTS GEO + consumer): Viasat (post-Inmarsat merger) — ~$4bn combined revenue; integrates GEO capacity (ViaSat-3 ultra-HTS) with managed services and retail; stronger in aviation/maritime connectivity; competes directly with SES on data capacity and government services [#A]

  • Competitor — regional (Asia-Pacific): SkyPerfect JSAT, Measat, Thaicom, AsiaSat — regional operators with limited overlap to SES's European/global arcs; not direct competitive threats at SES's scale but constrain SES's expansion into Asia-Pacific [#J]

  • Competitor — MEO (duopoly): SES O3b mPOWER vs Telesat Lightspeed — effectively a two-horse race in MEO; Telesat's Lightspeed is still under construction; SES O3b mPOWER is already partially operational (2023), giving SES a material first-mover advantage in MEO capacity deployment [#J]

  • Competitor — LEO (dominant): SpaceX Starlink — ~5,500 satellites in orbit (May 2026 est.), >3m subscribers globally, >$6bn ARR (est.); vertically integrated from manufacture to launch to service; cost structure structurally below legacy GEO/MEO operators; primary long-term competitive threat [#J]

  • Competitor — LEO (challenger): Amazon Kuiper — first commercial launch 2024, targeting enterprise and government; Amazon's balance sheet can fund full constellation; not yet operational at scale but represents credible medium-term threat [#J]

  • Competitor — LEO (restructured): OneWeb (now under Eutelsat) — ~648 LEO satellites, government/enterprise focus; weaker commercially than Starlink; integration with Eutelsat GEO is strategic but execution uncertain [#A]

  • Stage concentration — GEO: Very high post-Intelsat merger — SES+Intelsat account for an estimated 40-50% of global commercial GEO capacity revenue; top 3 players (SES+Intelsat, Eutelsat, Viasat) account for ~70-75%; a tight oligopoly [#J]

  • Stage concentration — LEO: Starlink-dominated but structurally growing more competitive as Kuiper deploys; Starlink's share of commercial LEO capacity is ~80%+ currently; this concentration creates a dominant competitor without traditional oligopoly pricing discipline [#J]

  • Scale-cost dimension: SES+Intelsat combination improves scale position in GEO but does not close the structural cost gap vs Starlink's vertically integrated model; per-transponder economics are not directly comparable to per-Gbps LEO economics [#I]

  • Differentiation dimension: SES differentiates on orbital asset base (slot portfolio, arc coverage), MEO low-latency for enterprise, long-standing government relationships, managed-service bundling; pure GEO bandwidth is commoditising [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: What is the bearish read on competitive position at SES's stage? The oligopoly rationale assumes pricing discipline between GEO peers; but Eutelsat's weaker financial position and Viasat's consumer-broadband expansion both create incentives to undercut on capacity pricing — a destabilising dynamic that could pressure the entire GEO tier's margin structure

§7 — Competitive Maps at Adjacent Stages + Vertical Integration Threats

  • Verdict: Vertical integration threats are asymmetric and credible — SpaceX integrating from launch (Stage 3) through capacity to retail (Stage 7) is the defining threat; customer integration is limited by capital requirements but not zero [#J]

  • Supplier — leader (manufacturing): Airbus Defence & Space and Thales Alenia Space dominate European GEO satellite manufacturing (relevant given SES's European heritage); Boeing Space dominates US; together these 3-4 firms command ~70-80% of GEO satellite manufacturing revenue globally [#A]

  • Supplier consolidation: The satellite manufacturing stage is modestly consolidating — Maxar acquired by MDA (Canada); Orbital Sciences absorbed into Northrop Grumman; supplier base is not expanding; this is mild adverse pressure on SES's procurement leverage [#J]

  • Supplier — launch (dominant): SpaceX's Falcon 9/Heavy now handles ~60-70% of commercial GEO launches globally; Arianespace/Ariane 6 and ULA are secondary; launch market has become more concentrated on SpaceX since Ariane 5 retirement [#J]

  • Supplier vertical integration — critical: ⚡ SpaceX has vertically integrated from Stage 3 (launch) through Stage 4 (Starlink capacity) to Stage 7 (retail service delivery); it now manufactures its satellites (Starlink) internally, launches them at near-marginal cost, and sells to end customers directly — the most disruptive value-chain repositioning in satellite history [#J]

  • Supplier integration threat — Starlink enterprise: SpaceX is actively pursuing enterprise and government contracts that previously went to SES/Intelsat managed services (Stage 6); US DoD Starshield contract, maritime broadband deployments, and aviation connectivity are all moving toward Starlink — each removes revenue from SES's downstream Stage 6 exposure [#J]

  • Customer — leader (video broadcasters): Sky Group (Comcast), ARD/ZDF (Germany), Canal+ Group (Vivendi) are leading SES video customers; these are large organisations with significant negotiating leverage at contract renewal; Sky in particular has scale to drive hard bargaining [#A]

  • Customer consolidation — video: Broadcasting customers are consolidating and shrinking their satellite capacity requirements — Sky's in-house platform migration, streaming shifts, and DTH household decline all reduce the number of transponders needed; customer consolidation at Stage 6-7 is adverse for SES's video capacity pricing [#J]

  • Customer — leader (data/enterprise): Telcos, ISPs, and government agencies are SES's growth customer tier; these are generally financially strong counterparties; enterprise data customers buying managed O3b mPOWER capacity tend to sign multi-year contracts reducing churn risk [#J]

  • Customer vertical integration: Limited credible risk in video (Sky/Canal+ not in satellite capacity manufacturing) but moderate risk in government — US DoD, UK MoD, and European defence agencies have all explored sovereign satellite capacity; national sovereign satellite programmes (France, Germany, UK) create a partial bypass of SES [#I]

  • Customer vertical integration — Amazon Kuiper: Amazon is building Kuiper as both a capacity asset (Stage 4) and a retail service (Stage 7), bypassing SES's managed service layer entirely for Amazon's own cloud/logistics connectivity needs; this is customer-to-competitor vertical integration at scale [#J]

  • Adjacent threat — teleports: Stage 5 teleport operators (Satcoms UK, Arqiva, Encompass) are partially competing with SES's own ground infrastructure but at too small a scale to threaten SES's integrated position [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: What is the bearish read on adjacent-stage threats? The most credible vertical integration vector is Amazon Kuiper — Amazon has the capital (>$100bn annual FCF), the technology ecosystem , and the strategic motivation to build an integrated Stage 4-7 competitor that would hollow out SES's enterprise data customer base; a 7-10 year risk but not a 20-year one

§8 — Structural Changes + Evolution

  • Verdict: The satellite capacity value chain is in the most rapid structural transformation in its 60-year history — GEO consolidation is a defensive response to LEO disruption, not a growth-driven consolidation; the chain is bifurcating between legacy GEO (declining, consolidating) and new-orbit infrastructure (growing, capital-intensive) [#J]

  • Stage consolidation — GEO: Clear consolidation in progress — SES+Intelsat is the most significant merger; Eutelsat+OneWeb (2023); Viasat+Inmarsat (2023); this consolidation is defensive — scaling to manage GEO revenue decline rather than scaling to capture growing demand [#J]

  • Stage fragmentation — LEO: Simultaneous fragmentation at the new-orbit layer — Starlink, Kuiper, OneWeb/Eutelsat, Telesat Lightspeed, and a long tail of small-constellation operators are all building capacity; this fragmentation creates supply surplus at the broadband/data layer that will structurally compress data capacity pricing [#J]

  • Vertical integration trend: The dominant trend is vertical integration by tech-platform-backed entrants (SpaceX, Amazon) across the full Stage 3-7 stack; legacy operators like SES are responding by extending downstream (managed services, government) rather than upstream (manufacturing) — a different integration vector but not fully defensive [#J]

  • Disintermediation — capacity layer: Partial disintermediation underway — Starlink sells directly to enterprise and maritime customers that previously would have contracted through SES/Intelsat managed services; the traditional wholesaler/capacity-operator intermediary is being bypassed in broadband segments [#J]

  • DTC trend: SES has historically been wholesale-only (no DTC exposure); Viasat's DTC broadband model shows downstream integration is viable but capital-intensive; SES has not pursued DTC, consistent with its infrastructure-landlord positioning [#J]

  • New entrant — tech-platform backed: Amazon Kuiper represents a new entrant of a qualitatively different type — not a telecom company, not a legacy satellite operator, but a cloud/logistics platform using satellite capacity as infrastructure for its own ecosystem; this is not a traditional competitor and will not behave like one on pricing [#J]

  • Technology disruption — LEO physics: MEO (SES O3b) occupies a structural middle ground between GEO (high latency, global coverage, high power) and LEO (low latency, lower power per satellite, large constellations); MEO's 8,000km orbit delivers ~150ms latency vs GEO's ~600ms and LEO's ~25-40ms; for many enterprise applications, 150ms is acceptable — this is SES's technological hedge [#I]

  • Technology disruption — software-defined satellites: Software-defined payloads (Airbus Spacebus Neo, Boeing 702X) allow in-orbit reconfiguration of capacity; SES's newer satellites incorporate this; reduces the risk of deploying capacity into the wrong frequency band or geographic market [#I]

  • Pricing power shift — upstream: SpaceX's reusable launch has shifted pricing power upstream (toward SpaceX) in the launch market; simultaneously, SpaceX's vertical integration into Starlink reduces it as an arms-length launch supplier for SES — SES is funding a competitor every time it uses Falcon 9 [#J]

  • Pricing power shift — downstream: Pricing power in the data/broadband capacity layer is shifting downstream to customers as HTS and LEO supply increases; broadcasters are already exercising renewal leverage; data customers will increasingly do the same [#J]

  • Business model innovation — capacity-as-a-service: SES and Intelsat both offer managed capacity / FlexIntelsat / SES Flex services that unbundle transponders into flexible bandwidth; this is a partial transition from pure capacity landlord to managed infrastructure provider — higher customer stickiness but lower unit margins [#I]

  • C-band spectrum windfall : ⚡ SES received ~$3.97bn in FCC C-band accelerated relocation payments (2021-2023), representing a non-recurring but extraordinary regulatory windfall that funded fleet investment; this was unique to the US C-band transition and is now largely completed; no structural recurring analog exists [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: What is the bearish read on structural evolution? The GEO consolidation thesis assumes that scale + managed services will defend EBITDA as video declines; but if Starlink's enterprise penetration accelerates and Amazon Kuiper deploys at scale by 2027-2028, SES+Intelsat may have built the world's largest legacy asset base at precisely the moment legacy economics terminally deteriorate

§9 — Structural Advantages + Strategic Implications + Sceptical Synthesis
