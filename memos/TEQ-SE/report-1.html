§1 — GENERAL

Net signal: Teqnion is a structurally sound Swedish industrial compounder — sub-$500M market cap, SEK ~1.8bn revenue, EBITA margins 13-15%, compounding via disciplined M&A at 4-6x EBITA; too small for Richard's stated mandate but high-quality archetype [#J]

Teqnion is a Stockholm-listed holding company that acquires, owns, and develops profitable niche industrial businesses in Sweden and the Nordic region. The business is built explicitly on the Lifco/Lagercrantz model of permanent decentralised ownership combined with disciplined bolt-on M&A.

Description

  • Description: Teqnion acquires profitable Nordic SME industrial businesses — typically SEK 20-100M revenue, 10%+ EBITA margins — retaining management, granting full operational autonomy [#I]
  • Subsidiary CEO P&L responsibility is structural, not aspirational; central functions are deliberately lean (c.20 group FTE vs 700+ subsidiary FTE)
  • Acquisition thesis: buy at 4-6x EBITA, improve to 8-10x market multiple via compounding, generate 15-25% IRR per deal
  • Strategic objective: Build the "Swedish Danaher" — acquire niche industrial leaders across defence/security, infrastructure, life sciences, and process industries [#I]
  • Explicitly targets GDP+ organic growth in acquired niches plus ~5-8 acquisitions pa; no stated revenue ceiling
  • Capital return is secondary to reinvestment — dividend yield near-zero; all surplus capital directed to M&A
  • Organisation: Three business segments — Industry (process/manufacturing components), Growth (higher-growth niche tech), and People (specialist staffing) [#I]
  • Industry segment: ~55% of revenue; stable, capital-light, 12-14% EBITA margins
  • Growth segment: ~30% of revenue; higher-growth, 14-18% EBITA margins; defence and life-science biased
  • People segment: ~15% of revenue; staffing model, lower margins (~5-7% EBITA), cyclically exposed

Listing

  • Share price range L24M approximately SEK 220-340 (mid-2024 c.SEK 280-310 range, per available data)

§2 — COMPANY DETAILS

Net signal: Revenue model is highly M&A-dependent with solid but not exceptional organic underpinning; business model has genuine decentralised-ownership moat; recent delivery track record strong on deal count and EBITA consistency but organic acceleration unproven [#J]

Revenue Model

  • Revenue source: ~85% from industrial products/services (Industry + Growth segments); ~15% from staffing (People) [#I]
  • Geographic split: ~90% Sweden/Nordics, ~10% other European markets — highly domestic, limited FX exposure but also limited geographic optionality near-term
  • End-market split: process industries (~25%), defence/security (~20%), life sciences (~15%), construction/infrastructure (~15%), staffing (~15%), other (~10%)
  • Revenue mix: No single subsidiary >10% of group revenue — portfolio diversification across ~35 subsidiaries (FY2023) is a structural risk dampener [#A]
  • Lifco comparable: ~170 subsidiaries; Indutrade ~200 — Teqnion significantly earlier in portfolio building; concentration risk is higher per-subsidiary
  • Revenue per subsidiary ~SEK 50M average — typical for early-stage Lifco archetype (Lifco average ~SEK 80M)
  • Cyclicality: Industry segment moderately cyclical (correlated to Swedish manufacturing PMI); Growth segment less cyclical (defence, life sciences); People segment most cyclical (staffing) [#A]
  • FY2020 COVID test: revenue grew (M&A-driven); organic sales modest dip then recovery — limited stress-test data given company's early stage
  • Predictability: M&A-driven revenue growth is partially predictable — deal pipeline visibility 6-12M; organic growth 5-10% is relatively stable [#A]
  • Risk: acquisition financing conditions (interest rates) are a key variable; SEK lending rates rose significantly 2022-2024, squeezing acquisition economics for Swedish compounders broadly [AS-Carnegie]

Business Model

  • Competitive advantage: Permanent ownership structure — subsidiaries never sold — creates trust with founding/owner-manager sellers who fear private equity exit [#J]
  • This is the core sourcing moat: deal flow from SME succession pool (Sweden has ~60,000 SMEs with founder succession challenges per Statistics Sweden) that is underexploited by PE due to deal size
  • Decentralisation preserves subsidiary culture/autonomy — lowers post-acquisition attrition risk vs integration-heavy acquirers
  • Strategic objective: Acquire 5-8 businesses pa at 4-6x EBITA; grow organic per subsidiary at GDP+ pace; maintain EBITA margins 13-15%+ [#I]
  • Secular trend: SME founder succession wave in Sweden/Nordics — demographic bulge of 1970s-founded businesses entering ownership transition 2020-2035 [#A]
  • Carnegie (2023) estimates 15,000-20,000 Swedish SMEs with revenue >SEK 10M will change hands 2020-2030 — structural M&A tailwind for Teqnion's acquisition model
  • Lifco/Lagercrantz/Indutrade already well-positioned in this pool; Teqnion's competitive advantage is the smaller deal size tier (<SEK 100M revenue) that larger peers have outgrown

Customer Side

  • Customer behaviour: Teqnion's subsidiaries typically serve as single-source or dual-source suppliers of critical components/services — high switching cost per subsidiary [#A]
  • Customer moat is at subsidiary level, not group level; group brand has no customer-facing meaning
  • Lock-in mechanisms vary: some subsidiaries have OEM relationships (proprietary components), others have framework agreements with industrial customers
  • Churn: No group-level churn data published; at subsidiary level, customer retention reportedly high (management commentary per Redeye 2023) — consistent with niche industrial supplier archetype [#I]
  • ESA depth needed on subsidiary-level NPS/retention data — flagged for Q8
  • Switching cost: Varies by subsidiary — highest in proprietary components/life sciences; lowest in staffing [#A]
  • Staffing (People segment) is lowest-moat — essentially commodity; intentionally small segment but watch for size creep ❌

Economies of Scale

  • Scale economy: Group-level scale advantages are currently modest — Teqnion too small to extract meaningful procurement, financing, or distribution scale [#A]
  • Lifco comp: Lifco's scale (SEK 20bn+ revenue) enables group-level procurement + insurance + treasury advantages that Teqnion cannot yet replicate
  • Financing scale: Teqnion's credit facility terms likely improve as revenue scales through SEK 2-5bn range — lower cost of debt is a mechanical margin benefit
  • Operating leverage: At subsidiary level, fixed-cost leverage is structurally embedded — as subsidiaries grow organically, EBITA margins expand toward 15-18% [#A]
  • Group overhead is deliberately thin: 20 central FTE managing 700+ subsidiary FTE — group SG&A as % of revenue <2% (Lifco comparable ~1.5-2%)

Recent Delivery — L3Y

  • FY2023 revenue grew to SEK 1.8bn: further 7 acquisitions; EBITA margin held at ~14%; demonstrating model scalability
  • Organic delivery: Organic growth estimated 5-8% FY2022, 6-9% FY2023 — in line with Swedish industrial GDP+ growth; not a standalone alpha driver [#A]
  • Handelsbanken (2023) flagged that organic growth trajectory in the Growth segment (defence/life sciences) was running ahead of the consolidated average at ~8-12%

Internal Change — L12M + N3Y

  • Leadership change: Johan Steene remains CEO — no leadership disruption; management team is small (5-6 group executives) and stable [#I]
  • Daniel Zhang joined 2021 — brought more structured reporting as company scaled through First North listing phase
  • Board composition professionalised 2022-2023: two independent industrial directors added — governance scaffolding consistent with continued scaling ambition
  • Internal change: No strategic pivots; model discipline maintained — no diversification outside original M&A framework [#I]
  • Notable: Teqnion explicitly declined to chase larger deals (>SEK 150M revenue targets) in 2022-2023 despite balance sheet capacity — capital discipline signal

Competitive Intensity

  • Competitive intensity: Low for deal sourcing in the sub-SEK 100M revenue SME tier; higher for talent (subsidiary CEOs) and financing [#A]

  • Financing competition: post-2022 interest rate normalisation increased cost of debt materially; Handelsbanken noted Teqnion's revolving credit facility re-priced in 2023, adding ~100-150bps to blended financing cost [AS-Handelsbanken]
  • New entrant: Family office capital increasingly targeting Swedish SME M&A — structural threat to deal sourcing over N36M [#A]
  • Innovative new entrant risk: "roll-up as a service" models (e.g. EQT-backed platforms) targeting exactly Teqnion's deal size from above — ESA depth needed Q8
  • Disruption: Minimal disruption risk to Teqnion's subsidiaries individually; staffing (People segment) faces digital marketplace disruption (LinkedIn, AMS platforms) [#A]
  • Core Industry/Growth subsidiaries: niche industrial components and services — low disruption risk given criticality and low digital substitutability

Macro Shifts

  • Macro shift: Swedish manufacturing PMI contraction through 2023-2024 created organic revenue headwinds for Industry segment subsidiaries [#A]
  • Redeye (2024): Sweden manufacturing PMI averaged ~47-49 through H1 2024 vs 50-52 in FY2022 — organic growth moderated to ~5% from ~8-9% peak
  • Interest rate normalisation (Riksbank hiking 2022-2024) increased acquisition financing costs and reduced SME seller valuations, creating a timing mismatch for Teqnion's pipeline
  • Macro tailwind: Defence spending uplift (Sweden NATO accession February 2024) — Growth segment's defence/security subsidiaries are direct beneficiaries [#A]
  • Sweden committed to 2% GDP defence spend target; Teqnion's defence-exposed subsidiaries (estimated ~15-20% of Group revenue) are in a structurally growing end-market

Non-Macro Shifts

  • Regulatory shift: No material regulatory changes affecting core acquisition strategy; Sweden corporate tax at 20.6% (competitive vs European average ~23-25%) [#I]
  • Political shift: Swedish coalition government (post-2022) broadly supportive of SME sector; no adverse changes to M&A tax treatment [#I]
  • Thematic trend: ESG pressure on industrial SMEs — Teqnion's portfolio companies face increasing ESG reporting requirements as group scales toward regulated disclosure thresholds; compliance cost headwind [#A]
  • ESA depth Q8: whether Teqnion's portfolio companies have structural exposure to energy transition tailwinds (e.g. electrification components)

Sceptical Bullet

  • Sceptic: The Swedish serial-acquirer archetype is now extremely well-flagged by institutional capital — valuation premium in the sector (Lifco at 30-35x EV/EBITA) may compress as interest rates normalise and capital rotates back to other compounders globally, dragging Teqnion's multiple with it regardless of fundamental delivery [#J]
  • If Swedish manufacturing PMI remains below 50 for a sustained period, organic growth in the Industry segment could turn negative, exposing the M&A flattery in headline revenue numbers
