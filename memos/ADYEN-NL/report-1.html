§1 — Business Model and Value Proposition

The Single-Stack Payments Platform

Adyen simplifies global commerce by replacing fragmented, multi-party payment setups with a unified technology platform built entirely in-house. The core of the company's business model relies on integrating the full payments stack — encompassing the payment gateway, risk management, processing, acquiring, and settlement — within a common back-end infrastructure. In traditional merchant services, transactions must pass through multiple disjointed systems, including third-party gateways, separate risk engines, processing networks, and acquiring banks. Adyen collapses this complex value chain by acting simultaneously as the gateway, the acquiring processor, and the merchant acquirer.

When a transaction is initiated, Adyen routes the data through its own infrastructure to obtain network authorization, conducts real-time fraud assessment, and manages the clearing and settlement of funds directly into the merchant's account. To facilitate this seamless flow, Adyen maintains its own banking licenses across the United States, the United Kingdom, and Europe. Operating these licenses internally allows the company to connect directly to major payment rails and card schemes like Visa and Mastercard without relying on third-party partner banks, thereby improving reliability, reducing intermediary fees, and accelerating settlement speeds. [ARS, 4 Mar; Deutsche Bank Research, 27 Nov 18]

Unified Commerce Strategy

A foundational element of Adyen's market approach is its Unified Commerce offering, which connects online and in-person payments for global retailers, hospitality brands, and food and beverage groups. The company strategically treats the physical point of sale as a critical source of intelligence, unifying physical and digital environments to capture a comprehensive view of the customer journey. By processing all omnichannel transactions through a single database, Adyen eliminates data gaps and operational fragmentation that merchants typically face when expanding globally. [ARS, 4 Mar; INTRM, 12 Feb; Bernstein Research, 6 May]

Value Proposition and Expanding Capabilities

Adyen's value proposition extends beyond basic authorization, focusing on improving the overall economics of merchant transactions. The company has aggressively expanded its capabilities into embedded financial services, offering issuing, capital, and business accounts to support the complex operational workflows of platform customers. Furthermore, the introduction of Intelligent Money Movement unifies pay-ins, liquidity management, and payouts into a single operating environment, providing enterprise treasurers with real-time cash visibility. Adyen's strategic acquisition of Talon.One also builds upon this unified strategy by allowing merchants to link customer identity directly to SKU-level promotions and pricing dynamically within the payment flow. [ARS, 4 Mar; Press Release, 9 Apr; Press Release, 23 Apr]

Jefferies: Adyen's single, unified platform sets it apart from traditional acquirers that frequently rely on a patchwork of disjointed legacy systems. This architecture reduces technical complexity for merchants while offering high flexibility, scalability, and system reliability as transaction volumes grow. [Jefferies Research, 23 Oct 25]

Bernstein: Adyen's true omnichannel offering delivers structural advantages over legacy peers, including higher authorization rates, lower total processing costs, and a much wider array of local payment methods. The simplicity of Adyen's single-stack model fundamentally eliminates the need for enterprise merchants to manage different vendors for gateways, risk management, and acquiring. [Bernstein Research, 10 Apr; Bernstein Research, 11 Jun 25]

Deutsche Bank: Adyen's vertically integrated infrastructure allows the company to redesign core payment primitives rather than merely layering new features on top of existing legacy rails. This foundational control positions Adyen as a natural partner for emerging workflows such as agentic commerce, enabling the platform to act as a universal translator that preserves merchant ownership of data and payment logic. [Deutsche Bank Research, 19 Nov 25]

§2 — Revenue Model and Financial Profile (FY2021–FY2025)

Revenue Generation and Unit Economics

Adyen operates a transparent pricing model separating gross revenue into distinct performance obligations: settlement fees (% of transaction value for acquiring, marked up over interchange), processing fees (fixed fee per transaction for gateway use), sales of POS terminals and other services (FX, third-party commissions, issuing). Management monitors "net revenue" which strips out pass-through costs from financial institutions and costs of goods sold. Net revenue also includes net interest income from embedded financial products and merchant-related funds held in business accounts. [ARS, 4 Mar]

Take Rate Dynamics and TPV Monetization

FY2025 blended take rate: 17.0 bps (up from 15.5 bps FY2024), largely driven by shifts in overall merchant mix. Because Adyen employs a tiered pricing model offering volume discounts to its largest enterprise clients, individual large-volume customers can have an outsized impact on TPV without proportionately driving up net revenue.

Deutsche Bank: take rate fluctuations are primarily mix-driven, reflecting successful expansion with larger merchants rather than underlying pricing pressure. [Deutsche Bank Research, 6 May]

Bernstein (Q1 2026 estimates): Unified Commerce commanded the highest net yield at 18 bps; Digital at 17 bps; Platforms at 12 bps. [Bernstein Research, 6 May]

The H1 2023 Earnings Miss and Subsequent Recovery

H1 2023: net revenue +21% YoY to €739.1m; North American revenue growth decelerated sharply to 23% (from 52% prior year); EBITDA margin compressed to 43% (from 59% H1 2022). Margin contraction intentionally driven by counter-cyclical investment — Adyen added 551 new FTEs (mostly tech/engineering) as peers cut headcount. [INTRM, 17 Aug 23; ARS, 29 Feb 24]

H2 2023 recovery: net revenue +23% YoY to €887.0m; North America rebounded as fastest-growing region at +27%; EBITDA margins recovered to 48% as hiring phase completed. Deutsche Bank: strong H2 2023 finish eased fears of sustained share losses and margin compression. [INTRM, 8 Feb 24; Deutsche Bank Research, 9 Feb 24]

§3 — Competitive Positioning and Geographic Mix

Single-Platform Architecture vs. Legacy PSPs

Adyen's competitive advantage lies in its proprietary single-stack technology platform built entirely in-house, bypassing acquisitions and third-party dependencies common among legacy providers. Unified architecture reduces operational friction, accelerates settlement speeds, and improves authorization quality globally. The platform acts as unified gateway, risk management system, and acquirer, allowing merchants to operate seamlessly without fragmentation that limits scale.

Rothschild & Co Redburn: specialist, integrated payment platforms have structurally outperformed generalist legacy providers such as Worldpay, Fiserv, and Global Payments. The legacy strategy of pursuing large-scale payments M&A has frequently failed to deliver sustainable returns, leaving incumbents with operationally complex and highly fragmented back-end architectures. [Rothschild & Co Redburn, 12 May]

Jefferies: rising competitive pressure from modern platforms like Adyen has successfully squeezed traditional players at the enterprise level, leading to stagnant or pressured market share for legacy acquirers across Europe. [Jefferies Research, 13 May]

Unified Commerce and the Enterprise Focus

Adyen specifically targets large, global enterprises through its Unified Commerce strategy. While direct SMB acquisition has not been Adyen's primary focus, the company captures the SMB market indirectly through "Adyen for Platforms" — enabling software providers and marketplaces to embed payments and financial services natively, reaching the long-tail SMB merchant base without heavy direct sales costs. Bernstein: enterprise moat continually fortified by differentiated omnichannel capabilities, including Dynamic Identification tools and AI-powered Adyen Uplift optimization suite. [Bernstein Research, 10 Apr]

Peer Evaluation

Stripe: Most formidable modern competitor; dominates SMB, developer, and vertical SaaS (especially US). Excels through rapid feature development, seamless platform onboarding, developer-friendly API. Bernstein: Stripe increasingly differentiating through faster pace of product innovation and deep integration with emerging AI-native customers, capturing exponential volume growth in the AI economy. [Expert Call, 27 Mar; Bernstein Research, 10 Apr]

PayPal/Braintree: Bernstein views PayPal as lagging behind on omnichannel capabilities and geographic reach. Aggressive commercial pricing wins unbranded processing contracts, but struggles with operational fragility and outages; branded PayPal button retains powerful consumer trust moat. [Bernstein Research, 10 Apr; Bernstein Research, 26 May]

Worldline/Nexi: Face significant structural challenges competing with Adyen's organic platform. Nexi grew market share through regional acquisitions (Nets, Ratepay, Concardis) but cannot consolidate into single cohesive platform — cannot offer enterprise merchants the single point of contact and unified global contract. Deutsche Bank: prefers Adyen over cautious outlooks for European legacy peers Worldline and Nexi. [Expert Call, 19 May; Deutsche Bank Research, 27 Apr]
