2. KEY FINDINGS — BLUF

  • MOST DIAGNOSTIC Q1: Ask management to decompose mobile money (Airtel Money) ARPU trajectory ex-FX, by market, for the last 8 quarters — the answer discriminates bull (structural fintech monetisation) from false-friend (volume without yield)
  • MOST DIAGNOSTIC Q2: Ask CFO specifically what the dollar-denominated debt-service profile looks like against functional-currency cash generation in Nigeria and Ethiopia — the answer discriminates solvency-risk bear from manageable-leverage bull
  • MOST DIAGNOSTIC Q3: Ask CEO what the single largest barrier to converting mobile-data subscribers into Airtel Money monthly-active-users is, market by market — the answer reveals whether the fintech flywheel thesis is internally believed or externally marketed
  • FX/macro compression is the dominant near-term P&L risk; management's hedging candour is the highest-information-yield topic in any meeting

  • Capex efficiency (tower-sharing, low-band spectrum deployment, network-as-a-service structures) is a credible margin-expansion path but requires management to quantify tower monetisation economics specifically
  • SCEPTICAL FRAMING: Management at pan-African telcos historically presents local-currency growth to sidestep the USD reporting pain; the analyst's job is to anchor every revenue question back to USD free cash flow per share

3. §1 — EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (TOP-3 MOST-DIAGNOSTIC QUESTIONS)

  • VERDICT: Three questions are load-bearing — Airtel Money yield per MAU, USD debt service vs functional-currency FCF, and the management-stated barrier to fintech conversion; all others are supporting
  • Discriminatory power: Each of the top-3 directly maps to a bull/bear fork that cannot be resolved from public filings alone
  • Bull hypothesis: Local-currency compounding in data + mobile money creates durable EPS growth that re-rates the stock once FX stabilises; management buy-in to this narrative is genuine and internally stress-tested
  • Bear hypothesis: FX attrition on USD revenue permanently impairs FCF/share; leverage is denominated in the wrong currency; Airtel Money is a compliance-heavy regulatory option, not a monetised flywheel
  • False-friend hypothesis: Revenue grows in reported USD as NGN/ETB stabilise but margin does not expand because network investment is underspent in the downturn, destroying competitive position vs MTN and Safaricom; EPS trajectory looks fine for 12 months then reverses
  • Incentive observation (Concept A): CEO and CFO compensation is USD-denominated; they feel the FX pain personally; but short-term incentive structures (revenue growth, EBITDA margin) may create pressure to present the local-currency story selectively
  • SCEPTICAL BULLET: "If management answers the top-3 questions fluently and confidently, the analyst should ask a fourth: which market is performing worst on the metric just described, and why? Fluency on the aggregate often conceals a single market dragging the portfolio"

4. §2 — THEME 1: REVENUE DRIVERS + VISIBILITY

  • VERDICT: Revenue-side uncertainty is highest on Airtel Money yield per MAU and on data ARPU sustainability — local-currency volume trends are visible but USD dollar conversion is opaque quarter-to-quarter

Q1 — Airtel Money ARPU per MAU, ex-FX, by market

  • Question: "Can you walk us through Airtel Money revenue per monthly-active-user, in local currency, for each of your top-5 Airtel Money markets over the last 8 quarters?"
  • Expected answer: Management will provide a consolidated ARPU figure and gesture to Nigeria's outsized weight; granular market-level data will require follow-up
  • Good answer signature: Specific per-market local-currency ARPU trend with named drivers (merchant payments, lending, B2B) showing positive trajectory in ≥3 markets
  • Bad answer signature: Deflects to total-transaction-value growth without per-user yield; inability to separate float income from transaction fee income
  • Follow-up probe: "Of that ARPU growth, how much is from float income at current interest rates vs fee-based transaction revenue — and what happens to ARPU if central bank rates in Nigeria or Kenya fall 200bps?"
  • Diagnostic note: Bull answer (ARPU growing on fee income, not rate-dependent float) validates fintech-flywheel thesis; bear answer (ARPU propped by float income at elevated rates) means Airtel Money's profitability is a macro-rate derivative, not a structural asset

Q2 — Data ARPU: volume vs price decomposition

  • Question: "On mobile data, how much of ARPU growth in the last 12 months is volume (more GBs used per subscriber) vs effective price per GB — in local currency terms?"
  • Expected answer: Volume-led growth as data consumption rises; price-per-GB likely declining under competitive pressure from MTN and local operators
  • Good answer signature: Volume growth sufficiently rapid that ARPU grows even as price-per-GB falls; clear visibility on next-generation bundle tiering
  • Bad answer signature: Price-per-GB stable only because competition hasn't yet arrived in rural corridors; no pricing-power thesis beyond geographic expansion
  • Follow-up probe: "In markets where MTN has deployed 5G or mid-band spectrum ahead of you, what has happened to your effective price-per-GB relative to theirs?"
  • Diagnostic note: Distinguishes durable data-ARPU-growth bull from geographic-footprint-expansion false-friend (growth from expanding into lower-ARPU markets, not deepening monetisation in existing ones)

Q3 — Customer concentration / enterprise revenue

  • Question: "What share of total group revenue comes from enterprise and B2B contracts, and what is the renewal cycle and churn rate on those contracts?"
  • Expected answer: Enterprise is a minority of revenue (<20%) but growing; churn low due to switching costs; renewal cycles 12-24 months
  • Good answer signature: Named enterprise verticals (financial services, government, FMCG) with multi-year contracts providing forward visibility on ≥12 months of revenue
  • Bad answer signature: Enterprise revenue characterised as "growing" without disclosed retention metrics; no visibility on contract expiry calendar
  • Follow-up probe: "Can you share the top-3 enterprise client segments by revenue and whether any single client represents >5% of segment revenue?"
  • Diagnostic note: High enterprise churn or concentration into a single sovereign/government client base materially changes the revenue-visibility profile; cross-refs Q19 regulatory-dependence bear case

Q4 — Forward subscriber trajectory: active-subscriber definition

  • Question: "How do you define an 'active' subscriber for both voice and data, and has that definition changed in the last 3 years? What's the 90-day churn rate?"
  • Expected answer: 30-day activity threshold for voice; 30-day data use for data subscribers; management will likely confirm definition unchanged
  • Good answer signature: Stable definition with declining churn rate citing improving network quality and loyalty programme penetration
  • Bad answer signature: Definition change in the last 2 years that inflated active-subscriber counts; churn stable only because of multi-SIM market dynamics, not genuine retention
  • Follow-up probe: "In Nigeria specifically, post the naira devaluation, did you observe a step-up in churn or a shift from postpaid to lower-value prepaid?"
  • Diagnostic note: Subscriber-definition games are a classic false-friend signal; NGN devaluation likely pressured Nigerian prepaid affordability, making this market the most important stress test

SCEPTICAL BULLET: "If management answers revenue questions fluently — particularly on Airtel Money — the more dangerous risk is that the fluent answer is the investor-relations-polished version; ask the follow-up probe on which market is underperforming the group average, and watch whether the answer requires a pause to retrieve or is instantly available — retrieval speed is diagnostic of operational intimacy"

5. §3 — THEME 2: MARGIN TRAJECTORY + LEVERS

  • VERDICT: Margin uncertainty is medium-to-high — EBITDA margin resilience in local currency has been credible, but USD-reported margin is FX-compressed and operating-leverage benefits from data growth are partially obscured by tower cost inflation and network-roll-out capex

Q5 — Tower cost and tower-sharing economics

  • Question: "What percentage of your towers are co-located with at least one other tenant, and what is the EBITDA contribution per tower-sharing agreement in USD terms per annum?"
  • Expected answer: Tower-sharing rates likely 30-50% in more mature markets (Kenya, Tanzania); Nigeria lower; management will cite improving trend
  • Good answer signature: Tower-sharing EBITDA contribution growing as a share of total EBITDA; explicit IRR per new tower tenancy disclosed or computable
  • Bad answer signature: Tower-sharing economics described as "positive" without unit economics disclosed; no visibility on IHS Towers or American Tower contract renegotiation timeline
  • Follow-up probe: "Your tower lease agreements with IHS and others — what proportion are USD-denominated on the cost side, and do those agreements have CPI or FX escalators?"
  • Diagnostic note: USD-denominated tower lease costs against local-currency revenue is a structural margin squeeze at the unit level; this is the most under-discussed P&L compression vector in public filings — cross-refs false-friend hypothesis

Q6 — Operating leverage: incremental EBITDA margin on data revenue

  • Question: "For every incremental $100 of data revenue added in FY25, what is the incremental EBITDA contribution — i.e. what is your marginal EBITDA margin on data?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite group EBITDA margin (historically ~47-49%) as a proxy; unlikely to have pre-prepared the incremental-margin calculation
  • Good answer signature: Marginal EBITDA margin on data >55% cited with reference to low incremental network opex (spectrum already deployed, towers already in place)
  • Bad answer signature: Redirects to group EBITDA margin without decomposing incremental vs average; or admits marginal margin is lower than average due to rural network roll-out costs
  • Follow-up probe: "In your most mature data market — Kenya or Tanzania — what has the EBITDA margin trajectory been as data penetration crossed 50% of revenue?"
  • Diagnostic note: If incremental margin on data is below group average, the operating-leverage thesis for data-mix-shift-driven margin expansion is structurally weaker than the bull case assumes

Q7 — FX pass-through: pricing power in local currency

  • Question: "In markets where the local currency has devalued materially — Nigeria particularly — have you been able to raise local-currency tariffs to offset USD cost inflation? What's the lag, and what's the regulator's posture on tariff increases?"
  • Expected answer: Partial pass-through achieved in Nigeria post-NCC tariff liberalisation in early 2025; management will cite successful tariff increases; exact quantum variable
  • Good answer signature: Documented tariff increase of 20%+ in Nigeria in FY25, with evidence that churn was <3% in the 90 days post-increase; regulator confirmed further increases permissible
  • Bad answer signature: Tariff increases applied once but further increases politically sensitive; regulator has signalled cap on future increases; churn elevated post-increase
  • Follow-up probe: "Following the Nigerian tariff increase, what was your observed 90-day churn versus the pre-increase run rate, and did churn differ materially by ARPU tier?"
  • Diagnostic note: Nigeria is >30% of group revenue; if tariff pass-through is structurally capped by regulatory or competitive pressure, the USD-margin recovery thesis is impaired; Nigeria NCC did permit a tariff increase in January 2025 — management should be able to quantify the ARPU uplift already in the numbers

Q8 — Capex pacing: what gets deferred if macro worsens?

  • Question: "If group FCF fell 20% from plan due to additional currency pressure, which capex line items would you defer, and what is the revenue impact of that deferral over 12-18 months?"
  • Expected answer: Management will distinguish maintenance capex (non-deferrable) from growth capex (roll-out of new sites, fibre backhaul); will assert only growth capex would be deferred
  • Good answer signature: Explicit capex triage framework — can articulate which projects are paused without competitive-position impairment; maintenance capex <35% of total capex
  • Bad answer signature: "We wouldn't defer capex" (non-answer) or inability to separate maintenance from growth capex in the stated budget
  • Follow-up probe: "In FY23 or FY24, did you in fact defer any planned capex as a result of FX pressure — and if so, what was the network quality impact?"
  • Diagnostic note: Historical capex deferral = revealed preference over stated policy; if they deferred in FY23-24, the false-friend scenario (underspent network, competitive position erosion) becomes more plausible

SCEPTICAL BULLET: "A fluent answer on EBITDA margin resilience should trigger the question: is the margin stable because costs are genuinely well-managed, or because maintenance capex is being underspent and booked as growth deferral? Network quality metrics (NPS trend, data throughput vs peer) are the external validators; ask management which external benchmarks they track for network quality and who publishes them"

6. §4 — THEME 3: CAPITAL ALLOCATION + BALANCE SHEET

  • VERDICT: Capital allocation is the highest-risk theme — USD-denominated debt against multi-currency functional cash flows, elevated net leverage post-FX attrition, and dividend-vs-debt-service tension require direct interrogation

Q9 — Dollar debt service vs functional-currency FCF

  • Question: "What is the scheduled USD and hard-currency debt service — principal plus interest — over the next 24 months, and how does that compare to expected USD cash upstream from operating companies?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite group leverage metrics (net debt/EBITDA ~1.5-2.0x on reported numbers); will reference repatriation track record
  • Good answer signature: Explicit debt maturity calendar with matched USD FCF upstreaming; demonstrates liquidity runway ≥24 months without refinancing
  • Bad answer signature: Deflects to group leverage ratio without providing maturity profile; or acknowledges repatriation restrictions in Nigeria/Ethiopia without quantifying the shortfall
  • Follow-up probe: "In Nigeria specifically, what was the aggregate dividend and intercompany loan repayment you were able to upstream to HoldCo in FY24 vs what was blocked by CBN restrictions — and what's the current unblocked balance?"
  • Diagnostic note: Nigeria-trapped cash is the most acute balance-sheet risk; if management cannot quantify the trapped-cash balance and the timeline for release, the bear-case solvency scenario becomes live

Q10 — Dividend policy: sustainability vs leverage

  • Question: "The group has maintained a progressive dividend policy — at what net debt/EBITDA level, and/or at what level of currency-blocked cash, would you consider pausing or cutting the dividend?"
  • Expected answer: Management will assert dividend is well-covered; will cite FY25 dividend as covered by HoldCo-level FCF; may acknowledge the question but resist giving an explicit trigger level
  • Good answer signature: Explicit trigger stated (e.g. "if net debt/EBITDA exceeds 2.5x on a sustained basis or if >30% of EBITDA becomes blocked, we would review the dividend"); demonstrates credible financial discipline
  • Bad answer signature: "The dividend is safe" without trigger levels; or assertion that dividend is funded by RCF drawdowns
  • Follow-up probe: "Is any part of the FY25 dividend being funded from the revolving credit facility or from asset disposal proceeds rather than operating FCF?"
  • Diagnostic note: Dividend funded by debt or disposals is a structural-quality red flag; cross-refs ACH bear case: capital structure impairment leading to forced equity raise

Q11 — M&A and spectrum: capital discipline

  • Question: "You operate in 14 markets — are there any markets where you would consider disposal or partnership if you couldn't achieve adequate returns on capital, and what return hurdle governs that decision?"
  • Expected answer: Management will point to the Ethiopia partnership/IPO path and to historical exit from less profitable markets; will resist naming specific disposal candidates
  • Good answer signature: Explicit ROIC hurdle rate stated (e.g. "we target 15%+ ROIC on incremental capital; markets below that sustainably are candidates for partnership or disposal"); cites Airtel Africa's Africa-specific precedent
  • Bad answer signature: "We're committed to all 14 markets" without an economic framework for market retention; strategic language without financial discipline framing
  • Follow-up probe: "In your bottom-quartile markets by ROIC — can you name the cohort and what the improvement pathway is?"
  • Diagnostic note: Willingness to exit sub-threshold markets is a positive management-quality signal; unwillingness to engage with the question suggests flag-planting over capital discipline

SCEPTICAL BULLET: "Management is incentivised to project balance-sheet resilience; the most revealing capital-allocation question is whether the dividend policy has ever been discussed at Board level in the context of a downside scenario — ask whether the Board stress-tested dividend sustainability at NGN 2,000/USD and what the conclusion was"

7. §5 — THEME 4: STRATEGY + COMPETITIVE POSITION

  • VERDICT: Strategic direction is clearest on mobile money but least clear on the long-term moat thesis — network quality vs MTN and the Ethiopia market-structure evolution are the two highest-uncertainty strategic items

Q12 — Competitive response: MTN and Safaricom

  • Question: "In markets where you compete directly with MTN or Safaricom — Kenya most acutely — what is your competitive-differentiation strategy, and has your market share trajectory in those markets been positive or negative over the last 12 months?"
  • Expected answer: In Kenya, Airtel competes on price vs Safaricom; management will cite share gains in data and Airtel Money but may be cagey on voice share trends
  • Good answer signature: Explicit market-share data cited (e.g. "we grew data RMS from 22% to 25% in Kenya in FY25"); differentiated product (specific Airtel Money use case Safaricom M-Pesa doesn't offer)
  • Bad answer signature: "We focus on our own growth" without market-share data; or admits share is static/declining in Kenya and pivots to other markets
  • Follow-up probe: "In Kenya, is your data pricing strategy sustainable at current levels, or are you investing market share gains at a margin cost?"
  • Diagnostic note: Pricing-led share gains in Kenya are not durable; if data ARPU is below Safaricom and shrinking, AAF's Kenya business is a declining-margin growth story — discriminates bull (genuine competitive gain) from false-friend (subsidised share)

Q13 — Ethiopia: market-structure evolution and JV pathway

  • Question: "Ethiopia is one of your largest addressable markets but also one of your most capital-intensive and structurally uncertain — what is the timeline and structure of any partnership, listing, or capital-recycling event you're targeting in Ethiopia?"
  • Expected answer: Management has guided toward an Ethiopia IPO or strategic partner; timeline has slipped previously; will likely say "we're evaluating options" without a hard date
  • Good answer signature: Concrete timeline (H2 FY26 or similar), named partnership structure, clear articulation of what capital recycling from Ethiopia enables (debt reduction or reinvestment)
  • Bad answer signature: "Evaluating options" without timeline; no named partner types; Ethiopia treated as a strategic optionality call without a stated decision framework
  • Follow-up probe: "If the Ethiopia partnership process takes another 24 months, what is the total cumulative capex commitment you're on the hook for in that market, and how does that affect group leverage?"
  • Diagnostic note: Ethiopia capital drag without a monetisation path is the most under-discussed bear-case pillar; management evasiveness on timeline is itself a negative diagnostic signal

Q14 — Airtel Money: super-app or pipe?

  • Question: "Is Airtel Money's long-term strategic positioning as a financial infrastructure pipe — earning transaction fees and float — or as a consumer financial-services product with lending, insurance, and savings? Which generates the better unit economics?"
  • Expected answer: Management will assert "both" — but pressed, the lending and insurance adjacencies are nascent; core model remains transaction-fee and float
  • Good answer signature: Clear articulation of the economics of each layer, with lending and insurance contribution explicitly sized as a share of Airtel Money EBITDA; honest about where the unit economics are better
  • Bad answer signature: Strategic language about "financial inclusion ecosystem" without unit-economic decomposition; lending described as an opportunity without NPL rates or book size disclosed
  • Follow-up probe: "What are the current NPL rates on Airtel Money lending products in your top-3 lending markets, and what is the blended cost of credit risk vs the lending yield?"
  • Diagnostic note: Airtel Money lending NPLs are an undisclosed risk; if lending is growing rapidly and NPL rates are elevated, the Airtel Money EBITDA is a future impairment risk — not a fintech-premium asset

SCEPTICAL BULLET: "Strategic framing from pan-African telco management teams historically over-indexes on TAM and adjacency language; the most useful strategic probe is to ask what they STOPPED doing in the last 12 months — strategic discipline on exits and de-prioritisation reveals more about management quality than entry announcements"

8. §6 — THEME 5: MANAGEMENT QUALITY + OPERATOR TRACK RECORD

  • VERDICT: Management quality is medium-high on operational execution but insufficiently tested on capital-allocation discipline and crisis communication — the FY24 FX crisis was the first major stress test of this team at group level

Q15 — Sandbagging discipline + guidance track record

  • Question: "Over the last 3 years, how has actual EBITDA or FCF compared to your original annual guidance — and in years where you missed, what was the single largest source of variance?"
  • Expected answer: Management will acknowledge FX as the primary variance driver; will likely avoid quantifying miss magnitude vs initial guidance
  • Good answer signature: Honest quantification of guidance misses (FX-adjusted and reported); demonstrates that operational delivery was on-plan even when FX impaired reported results; names specific market misses
  • Bad answer signature: "We don't provide explicit guidance" or re-frames actual vs consensus rather than vs internal plan; inability to name the single largest source of operational (non-FX) variance
  • Follow-up probe: "Setting aside FX, were there any markets in FY23 or FY24 where subscriber growth or ARPU missed your internal plan — and what was the cause?"
  • Diagnostic note: Distinguishing FX miss from operational miss is critical; a management team that blames all variance on FX while operational metrics also disappoint is the false-friend archetype

Q16 — Incentive structure: compensation alignment

(Concept A power-of-incentives — MANDATORY per §6)

  • Question: "Can you describe the CEO and CFO's short-term and long-term incentive structure — specifically what financial metrics drive annual bonus, and over what period do long-term equity grants vest?"
  • Expected answer: Short-term bonus likely tied to EBITDA margin, revenue growth, and possibly FCF; long-term equity vesting 3-5 years, possibly with a TSR comparator
  • Good answer signature: LTIP explicitly tied to USD FCF per share or ROIC; vesting dependent on relative TSR vs comparator group that includes relevant EM telco peers; no acceleration provisions for corporate events
  • Bad answer signature: LTIP tied to local-currency EBITDA (decoupled from the USD shareholder's return experience); or short-term bonus dominated by revenue growth without margin discipline; significant proportion of compensation in cash with short deferral
  • Follow-up probe: "Is there any component of management compensation that is explicitly linked to Airtel Money valuation — either standalone valuation or a revenue/EBITDA contribution threshold?"
  • Diagnostic note: If CEO is compensated on revenue growth, local-currency framing of the bull case is partly self-interested; if LTIP is TSR-linked in USD, alignment with shareholders is genuine; Concept A: compensation structure predicts which KPIs management will be most attentive to in the next 12-18 months

Q17 — Crisis playbook: FX shock response

  • Question: "When the NGN devalued 70% in FY24, what were the first 3 decisions you made in the first 30 days, and which of those decisions do you now think was wrong or too slow?"
  • Expected answer: Management will describe cost-cutting, capex deferral, and regulatory engagement; asking for a mistake is non-standard — most management teams will deflect
  • Good answer signature: Willingness to name one specific decision that in hindsight was wrong (e.g. "we were too slow to raise tariffs" or "we hedged too little of our cost base"); demonstrates intellectual honesty and operational learning
  • Bad answer signature: "We responded effectively and the business has stabilised" — no mistake acknowledged; deflects to FX recovery narrative
  • Follow-up probe: "What permanent changes to your operating model — not just crisis management — did you make as a result of the NGN devaluation?"
  • Diagnostic note: Management teams that learn from crises and institutionalise the learning are higher quality operators; inability to name a mistake is a culture-of-accountability flag; cross-refs Q19 ACH: management quality as a mitigant of bear-case scenarios

SCEPTICAL BULLET: "The most revealing management quality question is not on strategy or finance — it's on talent and hiring; ask the CEO who the three most important hires they made in the last 18 months were, and why those people were the highest-leverage additions; the answer reveals whether the CEO is a talent-obsessive or a process-manager"

9. §7 — THEME 6: RISK + DOWNSIDE (PRE-MORTEM CROSS-REF)

  • VERDICT: Downside risk is concentrated in three clusters — FX/repatriation, regulatory fragmentation in Airtel Money, and competitive position erosion in Nigeria; questions below test whether management's stated mitigants are substantive or performative

Q18 — FX repatriation: Nigeria worst-case

  • Question: "Bear-case probe: if Nigeria's CBN reimposed de facto repatriation restrictions — as occurred in 2022-23 — what is the maximum duration your HoldCo could service debt and pay dividends from non-Nigeria cash flows alone?"
  • Expected answer: Management will assert repatriation has normalised; will be reluctant to engage with the worst-case framing; may cite diversification across 14 markets
  • Good answer signature: Explicit stress-test answer — "ex-Nigeria, we generate approximately $Xm of HoldCo-available FCF, which covers debt service for Y months without refinancing"; demonstrates management has run this scenario
  • Bad answer signature: "We don't expect repatriation restrictions to return" — refuses the framing; or inability to quantify ex-Nigeria FCF
  • Follow-up probe: "Has your lender group or rating agency explicitly stress-tested this scenario with you — and what was their conclusion?"
  • Diagnostic note: Lender/rating-agency stress-test engagement is a proxy for how seriously the financial community views this risk; if Moody's or S&P has run this scenario with management, their conclusion is a high-quality data point

Q19 — Airtel Money regulatory risk: licence fragmentation

  • Question: "Regulatory positioning: in how many of your 14 markets does Airtel Money operate under a licence that could be revoked or materially restricted in the next 24 months — and which market represents the highest regulatory risk?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite strong regulatory relationships; may acknowledge Ethiopia and one other market as higher-risk; unlikely to name a specific licence-at-risk market
  • Good answer signature: Honest identification of 1-2 high-risk markets with specific regulatory risk factor named; evidence of proactive regulatory engagement (e.g. formal MOU with central bank, technical assistance programme)
  • Bad answer signature: "Our regulatory relationships are strong in all markets" — refuses to engage with the differentiated risk question; no specific markets named
  • Follow-up probe: "In the last 12 months, have you received any formal regulatory correspondence — warning letters, show-cause notices, or compliance requirements — relating to Airtel Money operations in any market?"
  • Diagnostic note: Airtel Money regulatory risk is the most material unquantified downside in the investment case; formal regulatory correspondence is a leading indicator of licence risk that is not disclosed in standard filings until material — this question is high-information-yield

Q20 — Competitive position erosion in Nigeria: MTN + infrastructure

  • Question: "Failure mode test: in Nigeria, MTN has materially more spectrum and tower density — what is your honest assessment of your network quality gap vs MTN in Lagos and Abuja, and what is the capex required to close it?"
  • Expected answer: Management will assert network quality is competitive; will cite recent capex investment; unlikely to volunteer a quantified gap
  • Good answer signature: Acknowledges a specific spectrum or tower-density gap (e.g. "MTN has approximately 2x our 4G tower density in Lagos"); provides a 3-year capex roadmap to close a stated portion of the gap
  • Bad answer signature: "We have a competitive network" without data; or deflects to rural markets where competitive dynamics are different
  • Follow-up probe: "What is your 4G population coverage as a percentage of Nigeria's urban population vs MTN's — and what does a third-party benchmarking report (e.g. Opensignal, GSMA) show on download speed?"
  • Diagnostic note: Network quality gap vs MTN in Nigeria is the most structurally threatening competitive risk; if AAF is losing urban high-ARPU subscribers to MTN due to quality, the Nigeria revenue recovery thesis depends on capex that isn't currently in the plan

SCEPTICAL BULLET: "Which Q19 ACH-matrix diagnostic items are NOT covered by any question in this memo? The primary uncovered item is the political risk vector — government ownership of spectrum, potential forced roaming obligations, or national-security-motivated network sharing requirements in Ethiopia and Nigeria; none of the above questions directly probe political-interference risk; this is a deliberate omission only if the analyst has already assessed political risk as below-threshold in prior DD memos — if not, it needs a dedicated question"

10. §8 — CROSS-CUTTING META + NON-OBVIOUS QUESTIONS

  • VERDICT: The most diagnostic questions are the ones management hasn't been asked — standard sell-side Q&A covers FX impact and Airtel Money MAU growth but systematically avoids unit economics, failure-mode engagement, and management self-assessment

Non-obvious angle Q1 (⚡): "What is the single metric — not one you disclose publicly — that you watch internally as the earliest warning that the Nigeria business is deteriorating? What's the current trend on that metric?"

  • Why non-obvious: Sell-side Q&A focuses on disclosed KPIs; the internal leading indicator is higher-information-yield; management's willingness to share it signals operational transparency
  • Expected answer: Management may cite data-active-subscriber growth, churn cohort analysis, or ARPU by prepaid tier; if they name a metric that isn't disclosed, the follow-up is to ask why it isn't disclosed
  • Good answer: Specific, named, non-public KPI with a current trend that is either reassuring or honestly concerning
  • Bad answer: "All our metrics are disclosed" or names a disclosed metric

Non-obvious angle Q2 (⚡): "If you had to reduce group capex by 25% for 24 months due to a refinancing constraint, which single market would you prioritise for capital protection — and which would you de-prioritise first?"

  • Why non-obvious: Standard Q&A asks about capex guidance; the forced-triage question reveals the implicit capital-allocation hierarchy; it is the Concept A incentive probe applied to capital, not management compensation
  • Expected answer: Management will resist the framing but should be able to articulate that Nigeria, Kenya, and Tanzania are the core; Ethiopia and smaller Francophone markets are the margin
  • Good answer: Honest hierarchy stated with economic rationale (ROIC ranking, subscriber density, competitive moat)
  • Bad answer: "We wouldn't make that choice" or "all markets are equally important"

Cross-roads Q3 (⚡): "The Airtel Money fintech valuation thesis assumes eventual standalone listing or strategic sale at a fintech multiple — if you had to choose today between (a) accelerating Airtel Money growth by reinvesting all its FCF for 5 years at the cost of group dividends, or (b) distributing Airtel Money FCF as dividends and growing it more slowly, which would the Board choose — and what does your current capital allocation actually imply?"
