§3 — Industry Context (Peer Comparison, Structure, Macro and Non-Macro Change)

- Swiss distribution is geographically ring-fenced by canton; no head-to-head competition between REHN and BKW for grid customers
- Effective regional monopoly structure — not a competitive market in traditional sense

Concentration: Swiss electricity distribution: top-5 cantonal utilities account for ~85–90% of end-customers; effectively a series of regional monopolies with no realistic consolidation path (cantonal sovereignty over energy infrastructure) [#A]
- REHN = #1 in Canton Vaud by definition; no ranking competition within its franchise area
- Supranational (pan-Swiss) ranking: REHN is mid-tier by customer count vs Axpo/Alpiq by assets, BKW by profitability [#I]

Competitive intensity: Negligible in distribution; moderate in electricity supply for liberalised large customers; low in household supply (captive until Swiss full liberalisation law implemented) [#A]
- Swiss electricity market liberalisation: full liberalisation of household market was delayed repeatedly; current Swiss legislation targets full liberalisation post-2025/2026 — key risk if implemented as it would expose captive supply revenues to competition [#A]

New entrant risk: De minimis in distribution (legal monopoly); moderate in supply post-liberalisation; low in renewables production (Swiss permitting is restrictive, capital intensity high) [#A]

Disruption force: Grid-scale battery storage and distributed solar could reduce demand for central grid over very long term (10–20 years); near-term (3–5 years) grid investment requirement is accelerating, not declining — electrification of EVs and heat pumps requires grid upgrades [#A]

- Swiss Energy Strategy 2050 (federal): mandates 50%+ renewable electricity by 2035; supportive of REHN's hydro + solar investment programme
- Full market liberalisation legislation risk: if Swiss parliament passes full liberalisation for households, REHN's captive supply base (~150,000 households) becomes contestable — material earnings risk [#A] ❌

Political shift: Canton Vaud (54% shareholder) politically committed to energy security and local control — reduces risk of privatisation or transformational M&A; also limits management independence and capital allocation optionality [#A]

Macro shift — L12M: European and Swiss wholesale electricity prices normalised significantly from 2022 crisis peak (Cal-25 Swiss baseload ~CHF 80–90/MWh vs 2022 peaks of CHF 300–400/MWh); REHN's production segment earnings supported by higher-than-normal electricity prices even at normalised levels vs 2019 [#A]
- Swiss franc strength: minimal FX impact (revenues 100% CHF; no material foreign currency exposure) [#I]
- Interest rates: Swiss rates rose 2022–2023 (SNB policy rate peak ~1.75%); REHN's regulated WACC benefits from higher rate environment in tariff calculations; SNB pivot to rate cuts (2024) could compress future WACC allowances [#A]

Peer divergence — BKW comparison: BKW is the most relevant listed Swiss peer; BKW's higher EBITDA margin (~32–35% vs REHN ~26–28%) reflects BKW's stronger renewables production mix and higher-margin engineering/services segment — a structural mix difference, not an operational gap REHN can close easily [#A]

Thematic trend: Energy transition capex super-cycle benefits regulated grid operators — ElCom allows higher capex recovery through RAB (regulatory asset base) growth; REHN's grid RAB expected to grow ~3–5%/year through 2030 driven by EV infrastructure and grid modernisation [#A]

Sceptical bullet — §3: Bearish cross-cutting read: the Swiss energy transition benefits grid RAB growth on paper, but ElCom's tariff-setting discipline means actual return-on-incremental-RAB is capped at regulated WACC (~5–7% real, WACC-era dependent); if Swiss market liberalisation proceeds fully for households, REHN faces margin compression in its most predictable supply segment; BKW's structural superior margin position is not a temporary gap but reflects genuinely better business mix — REHN's catch-up path is limited without Alpiq stake disposal and capital redeployment [#J]
