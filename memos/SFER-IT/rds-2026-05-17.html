§4 Synthesis

4.1 Sector and company context

Salvatore Ferragamo operates in mid-tier Italian luxury, competing with stronger-execution peers Moncler, Tod's, Brunello Cucinelli, and Prada. The brand has been in strategic repositioning since 2023, reducing wholesale distribution to focus on direct-to-consumer and key accounts. China is a structurally significant market: the group estimates roughly 25-30% of revenues originate from Chinese consumers globally. US tariff risk is real but secondary for Ferragamo relative to China, given its DTC model and manufacturing footprint. The sector was already navigating six consecutive quarters of falling Chinese luxury spending into Q1 2026. Ferragamo's particular vulnerability is its incomplete repositioning: it is simultaneously shrinking wholesale, renovating flagship US stores, and relying on China recovery to validate the DTC thesis, and all three pressures collided in Q1.

### 4.2 Candidate catalyst ranking

The primary catalyst is unambiguous: the Q1 2026 earnings release on 14 May 2026 triggered the sell-off. The headline miss versus consensus (€209m vs €216m Barclays estimate, a 3.2% miss) was compounded by three aggravating factors: China DTC turning negative, no sequential improvement in organic growth, and management commentary that H2 recovery was expected but not yet visible. The -24% move (vs the typical intraday -16% widely cited) partly reflects gap-down and continuation selling on 15 May. The prior +17.43% rally was macro-driven (tariff truce) and sector-wide; Ferragamo's results stripped that macro premium away and added a company-specific discount. Peers (Moncler Q1: +12% cFX, beat estimates) confirmed the move was idiosyncratic rather than sector-wide.
