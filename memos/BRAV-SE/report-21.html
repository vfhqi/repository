§2 — Market Structure Dimension (Framework Map)

J-front verdict: Market structure is consistently the highest-confidence dimension in DD-stage KQ research when public filings and industry data are available — but requires a named sector to anchor

  • Market structure: the first landscape dimension to map in any DD KQ; establishes whether the stock operates in a concentrated oligopoly, a fragmented market, or a platform-mediated network — each has different EPS predictability profiles
  • Oligopoly dynamics: markets with 3–5 dominant players and high switching costs tend to show EPS predictability at the 70th percentile or above relative to fragmented markets (base rate from cross-sector public filings analysis)
  • Concentration metrics: Herfindahl-Hirschman Index > 2,500 is the standard "highly concentrated" threshold; stocks in highly concentrated markets command structural pricing power that supports margin predictability
  • Platform vs. linear: platform-mediated markets have non-linear scaling economics; EPS trajectories in platforms can be predictable at low penetration (network effects dominant) but become noisy at saturation; the analyst must identify where on the S-curve the stock sits
  • Market size anchoring: TAM claims in management commentary should be cross-checked against bottom-up unit-economics models; top-down TAM estimates from sell-side typically overstate serviceable addressable market by 30–60% in tech-adjacent sectors
  • Structural tailwinds vs. cyclical uplift: the most common analytical error at DD stage is misclassifying cyclical volume uplift as structural market expansion; requires at least one full cycle of data to distinguish
  • Geographic concentration: single-geography exposure concentrates both upside and regulatory risk; multi-geography operators show lower EPS volatility but also lower peak-cycle margins
  • Entry barrier audit: the analyst should map: (a) capital intensity of entry, (b) regulatory licensing barriers, (c) brand/switching cost moats, (d) network effect barriers; each contributes differently to EPS durability
  • Disruption vector: every market structure analysis must explicitly map the most plausible disruption vector — typically technology substitution, platform disintermediation, or regulatory unbundling
  • Historical precedent: markets that appeared structurally oligopolistic but were disrupted (e.g. newspaper classified advertising, retail banking in Nordic markets post-open banking) show the risk of over-weighting static market structure analysis
  • Non-consensus read (Concept A): the market structure the stock participates in may be less relevant than the micro-market structure of its specific customer segment; a company can be a price-taker in aggregate but a price-setter in its niche
  • Edge signal: regulatory-mandated market restructuring (e.g. energy market liberalisation, telecom unbundling) can dissolve structural moats with 24–36 months of lead time; the analyst should check for pending regulatory reviews in the named sector
  • Synthesis (Concept B): market structure analysis and management narrative often contradict — management emphasises structural moat; sell-side emphasises competitive intensity; the composite truth is usually "moat exists but is narrowing"; the analyst should quantify the rate of narrowing, not just its existence

Confidence: Medium — anchor: framework is well-established; stock-specific application requires sector + ticker data unavailable in this run

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on market structure is that the analyst is likely to anchor on the most recent competitive equilibrium — static snapshots systematically underestimate disruption velocity; the evidence the analyst is NOT seeing is the disruptor who has not yet filed public financials

§3 — Customer Dynamics Dimension (Framework Map)

J-front verdict: Customer dynamics is the most load-bearing DD dimension for EPS predictability — churn, concentration, and switching cost data are the proximate drivers of revenue visibility

  • Revenue concentration risk: the 80/20 rule applies broadly — in B2B markets, the top 3 customers typically represent 30–60% of revenue; a KQ about EPS predictability cannot be answered without knowing customer HHI
  • Churn rate benchmarking: annual gross churn in SaaS markets averages 8–15% for SMB-focused products, 3–7% for enterprise; professional services markets show lower nominal churn but higher project-revenue lumpiness
  • Net Revenue Retention : NRR > 120% is the threshold for "land and expand" dynamics that can sustain EPS growth even with flat logo count; NRR < 100% means the company is shrinking on existing cohorts regardless of new logo performance
  • Switching cost quantification: switching costs should be mapped in money + time + risk dimensions; contracts with multi-year lock-in, deep system integration, or regulatory certification requirements have the highest switching cost moats
  • Customer concentration vs. customer quality: a single customer representing 40% of revenue is a risk flag, but only if that customer itself is fragile; a 40% customer who is an investment-grade sovereign entity is a different risk profile than a 40% customer who is a venture-funded startup
  • Volume vs. price growth decomposition: EPS growth driven by price/mix improvement is structurally superior to volume growth because it does not require incremental capex or working capital; the analyst must decompose revenue growth into volume, price, and mix
  • Cohort analysis: the most informative customer dynamic data is cohort-level retention and expansion — what do customers acquired 3 years ago look like relative to customers acquired 1 year ago? Cohort degradation is an early warning sign
  • Contract structure: recurring revenue (subscription, retainer, service contract) is categorically different from transactional revenue for EPS predictability; the analyst should map the recurring/transactional split and its trend
  • Customer acquisition cost and payback: CAC payback > 24 months at scale typically signals either market saturation or pricing pressure; the trend in CAC is more informative than the level
  • Cross-sell and upsell dynamics: in platform businesses, cross-sell revenue per customer is a leading indicator of moat deepening; flat cross-sell per customer indicates the platform is not expanding its share of wallet
  • Non-consensus read (Concept A): the consensus analysis focuses on existing customer retention; the non-consensus signal is often in prospective customer dynamics — the pipeline of customers who evaluated and did NOT convert, and why
  • Edge signal: customer advisory board composition and public case studies are under-utilised signal sources; if the company's reference customers are disproportionately early adopters or non-representative use cases, the mainstream customer dynamics may be materially weaker

Confidence: Medium — anchor: framework well-established; stock-specific churn and NRR data require ticker-level filings or expert calls unavailable here

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read is that customer dynamics data in public filings is selectively disclosed — companies report NRR when it is above 100% and go quiet when it degrades; the analyst is systematically NOT seeing the cohort data for periods of stress; expert calls are the only resolution path

§4 — Competitive Landscape Dimension (Framework Map)

J-front verdict: Competitive landscape is medium-confidence by default at DD stage — it is the dimension most susceptible to management narrative bias and requires the most explicit triangulation

  • Competitor mapping: a rigorous competitive landscape maps: (a) direct competitors on the same product/service axis, (b) indirect competitors via substitutes, (c) potential entrants (incumbents from adjacent markets), (d) disruptors (technology substitutes)
  • Moat classification: the Buffett-Munger moat taxonomy (cost advantage, switching costs, network effects, intangibles, efficient scale) remains the most useful first-pass framework; each moat type has different durability profiles and different valuation multiples
  • Competitive intensity indicators: price discounting behaviour, sales cycle lengthening, and win/loss ratio trends are the three most informative leading indicators of competitive intensity; all require either expert calls or channel checks to observe in real-time
  • Market share trend: a company can have a structurally strong position but be losing share slowly — the "boiling frog" dynamic; the analyst must compute revenue CAGR relative to named market CAGR over at least three years
  • Competitor financial health: the competitive threat from a well-capitalised competitor (large balance sheet, patient strategic capital) is categorically different from a venture-funded challenger on a 12-month runway; the analyst should map competitor financial health explicitly
  • Geographic competitive asymmetry: competitive dynamics often differ materially by geography; a company may be the dominant player in its home market and a distant follower in international markets; blended share statistics obscure this
  • R&D intensity comparison: R&D/revenue ratios compared across the peer set reveal investment-in-moat — companies systematically underinvesting relative to peers are trading future competitive position for near-term margin
  • Pricing behaviour observation: the most informative competitive signal is pricing behaviour in contract renewals — are discounts increasing, flat, or decreasing? This requires expert calls or channel checks and is rarely visible in public filings
  • Porter's Five Forces — static vs. dynamic: Five Forces is useful for the current snapshot but fails to capture force-trajectory; the analyst should run Five Forces for "today" and "3 years forward" under a bear-case competitive scenario
  • Non-consensus read (Concept A): consensus competitive analysis focuses on named peers with public financials; the non-consensus risk is typically from a private company, a large incumbent entering from an adjacent market, or a geographic player internationalising — none of which appear in standard peer screens
  • ⚡ Outlier signal: the most dangerous competitive threat in B2B markets historically has been the customer who decides to build rather than buy — vertical integration by large customers can collapse revenue for suppliers with 12–18 months of visible warning; this is systematically underweighted in sell-side competitive analysis

Confidence: Medium — anchor: framework is standard; live competitive intensity data requires expert calls and channel checks unavailable in this run

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on competitive landscape is that the analyst is working from the incumbent's map — companies define their competitive set to include manageable peers and exclude existential threats; the evidence NOT in the data room is the threat the management team is not discussing

§5 — Regulatory Environment Dimension (Framework Map)

J-front verdict: Regulatory dimension is high-variance — low-probability, high-impact; systematic underweighting is the most common DD error

  • Regulatory risk taxonomy: regulatory risks fall into three categories: (a) existing regulation compliance risk, (b) pending regulatory change, (c) novel regulatory application (regulator applying existing rules to new business models)
  • Sector-specific baseline: financial services, healthcare, energy, and telecoms carry structurally higher regulatory risk than industrial, consumer, and tech sectors; the analyst must know the sector's regulatory baseline before evaluating stock-specific risk
  • Regulatory review pipeline: the most material near-term regulatory risk is typically a pending review (competition authority, sector regulator, or legislature) rather than theoretical future regulation; the analyst should check for open regulatory consultations explicitly
  • Regulatory moat vs. regulatory risk: regulation can be both a moat (licensing barriers) and a risk (enforcement action, unbundling order, price cap); the same regulatory environment can be net-positive or net-negative depending on the company's positioning
  • Cross-border regulatory arbitrage: companies operating across multiple regulatory jurisdictions face both higher compliance cost and the opportunity to structure operations to exploit regulatory arbitrage; the analyst must map which jurisdictions drive economics and which drive regulatory exposure
  • ESG/sustainability regulation: the trajectory of climate-related regulation, supply-chain due diligence requirements, and data privacy enforcement has materially increased regulatory cost for most sectors since 2020; this should be modelled as an ongoing cost headwind, not a one-time adjustment
  • Litigation tail risk: regulatory investigations frequently precede civil litigation; the analyst should map open regulatory proceedings and estimate civil litigation optionality (negative optionality in the base case)
  • Management regulatory track record: how has management handled prior regulatory interactions? Companies with a history of regulatory cooperation tend to face lower enforcement risk than adversarial counterparts
  • Non-consensus read (Concept A): the consensus treats regulatory risk as binary (fine/no fine); the non-consensus risk is behavioural change forced by regulatory scrutiny even without formal enforcement — management bandwidth consumed, product roadmap constrained, customer perception affected
  • ⚡ Outlier signal: regulatory capture risk cuts both ways — incumbents who have built deep regulatory relationships can be destabilised when a new political regime or regulatory leadership reverses prior interpretations; this is observable ex-ante from election cycles and regulatory appointment timelines

Confidence: Low-to-Medium — anchor: framework is established; stock-specific regulatory exposure requires sector + jurisdiction identification unavailable here

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read is that the analyst is systematically too late on regulatory risk — by the time regulatory risk is consensus, it is priced in and the damage is done; the evidence NOT seen is the informal signal from regulatory staff-level consultation that precedes public announcement by 12–24 months

§6 — Technology / Disruption Dimension (Framework Map)

J-front verdict: Technology disruption is the hardest DD dimension to time — directionally correct analysis is common; timing the disruption curve is where most DD work fails

  • Disruption taxonomy: Christensen's disruption framework distinguishes sustaining innovation (better product for existing customers) from disruptive innovation (cheaper/simpler product starting at the low end); the disruption risk profile differs materially
  • Technology S-curve positioning: every technology has an adoption S-curve; the analyst must identify where both the incumbent technology and the disruptive technology sit on their respective S-curves; disruption risk is highest when the disruptor is at 10–30% penetration of the market
  • AI/ML disruption vector: since 2023, the most pervasive cross-sector disruption vector is AI-enabled automation of knowledge-work tasks; the analyst must explicitly assess whether the stock's core value-add is in a task category that AI is automating or augmenting
  • Build vs. buy dynamics (technology): the competitive risk from customers choosing to build internally using AI tools has materially increased since GPT-4 deployment; this is a specific variant of the vertical-integration threat that is sector-agnostic
  • Technology capex cycle: technology-intensive businesses face capex cycles driven by technology refresh; the analyst must identify where the stock is in its technology capex cycle and whether the next cycle creates an inflection in margin or a period of margin compression
  • Open-source competitive risk: in software-adjacent markets, open-source alternatives increasingly compete with proprietary solutions; the competitive moat of proprietary software requires explicit comparison against the best open-source alternative
  • Data moat assessment: in data-driven businesses, the proprietary data asset is the moat; the analyst should assess: (a) data exclusivity, (b) data quality vs. alternatives, (c) regulatory risk to data exclusivity (GDPR, open banking, data portability requirements)
  • Platform network effect durability: network effects are often cited as durable moats; in practice, multi-homing (users using multiple platforms simultaneously) significantly weakens network effect moats; the analyst should test whether the network effect is exclusive or multi-homed
  • Non-consensus read (Concept A): the consensus technology analysis focuses on whether the company is deploying technology offensively; the non-consensus risk is often in whether the company's technology is being used as a channel by a more powerful platform player who could disintermediate at will
  • Historical base rate: technology disruption timelines are systematically underestimated for early-stage disruption and overestimated for late-stage; the analyst should apply a 1.5–2x multiplier to management timelines for technology transitions that require behaviour change

Confidence: Low — anchor: technology disruption timing is inherently uncertain; framework provides directional signal only; stock-specific assessment requires sector identification

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read is that the analyst is evaluating the disruptive threat using the incumbent's definition of the product category; the most dangerous disruptors re-define the category and therefore don't appear in any competitive screen the analyst runs using the incumbent's own taxonomy

§7 — Historical Precedent and Cross-Sector Analogues (Framework Map)

J-front verdict: Historical precedent is the most under-utilised dimension in DD — cross-sector analogues routinely resolve KQs that appear novel when sector-specific evidence is thin

  • Base rate discipline: Kahneman's inside-view/outside-view framework is directly applicable; when the analyst has thin stock-specific data, the outside view (base rate across comparable situations) should be the prior, updated by stock-specific evidence
  • Analogue selection discipline: a good analogue shares at least three of: (a) market structure, (b) business model, (c) competitive dynamics, (d) regulatory environment, (e) technology maturity; single-dimension analogues (e.g. "it's like Uber but for X") are almost always misleading
  • EPS trajectory precedent: for the target investor (25%+ IRR, 12–24 month hold), the relevant historical precedent is: what is the base rate of 25%+ IRR outcomes from DD-stage entry in similar situations? This requires defining the peer set precisely
  • Re-rating precedent: multiple expansion from re-rating events (market recognising undervaluation) has a different precedent base rate than EPS growth-driven return; the analyst must be explicit about which IRR driver is load-bearing
  • Margin expansion analogues: if the thesis depends on margin expansion, the most informative analogues are companies in the same sector that successfully expanded margins from a similar starting point — what drove it, how long did it take, what was the reversion rate?
  • Competitive moat durability base rates: moats erode at sector-specific rates; consumer brand moats have historically lasted 20–30 years; technology product moats have lasted 5–10 years; switching-cost moats in B2B enterprise software have lasted 10–15 years
  • Value-trap analogues: the most dangerous DD error is the value-trap — a stock that appears cheap on a static valuation basis but has a structurally deteriorating earnings trajectory; the analyst should explicitly identify the value-trap base rate for the sector
  • Cross-sector disruption analogues: some disruption patterns repeat across sectors with a lag; print-to-digital (newspapers → 2000s), linear-to-streaming (TV → 2010s), physical-to-digital (retail → 2010s) provide calibration for the pace and shape of disruption in analogous sectors
  • Management track record as historical precedent: the single most informative stock-specific historical precedent is the management team's prior capital allocation decisions and their outcomes; this is observable from filings and deserves dedicated research time
  • Non-consensus read (Concept A): the consensus historical precedent analysis focuses on successful analogues; the analyst should explicitly weight the failure rate — for every analogue where the thesis played out, how many did not, and what distinguished the successes from the failures?

Confidence: Medium — anchor: analogue selection methodology is well-established; stock-specific analogue identification requires ticker + sector data unavailable here

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on historical analogues is that the analyst selects analogues that confirm the prior thesis — confirmation bias in analogue selection is endemic; the discipline is to nominate the most challenging disconfirming analogue first and explain why the stock escapes it

§N+3 — Second + Third-Order Implications (Concept A Edge-Detection)

J-front verdict: The most under-weighted edge implication in any DD-stage research is the recursive effect of the analyst's own framing on what evidence is collected — the KQ itself is a prior

  • Second-order: a KQ framed from the bull case will generate evidence that confirms the bull case; the Q21 template addresses this through the sceptical-bullet discipline, but the framing of the KQ itself is the upstream prior that needs to be interrogated
  • Second-order: the absence of peer data (D-BATCH-25 gap) has a second-order effect on the investment case beyond the obvious comparative anchoring loss — without a peer set, the analyst cannot detect whether the stock's apparent strengths are sector-wide or stock-specific; a rising tide inflates all readings
  • Third-order: if the DD KQ cannot be resolved because data is genuinely thin (not just missing from this run), that itself is signal — in efficient markets, information that is genuinely hard to obtain often carries higher-than-average alpha potential because other market participants face the same gap
  • Cross-pillar implication: the regulatory dimension (§5) is often treated as a standalone pillar, but regulatory risk has cross-pillar effects on competitive dynamics, customer behaviour, and technology investment; a pending regulatory review suppresses competitor investment and can inadvertently protect the incumbent's moat temporarily
  • Cross-stock implication: in a concentrated 5–15 position book, a DD-stage KQ about one stock often has cross-stock implications — if the KQ is about, for example, AI disruption of a B2B knowledge-services business, the same disruption vector applies to other positions in the book
  • M/M/O test #1 — Management/motive/opportunity: management teams have means (information asymmetry), motive (comp tied to stock price), and opportunity (earnings call narrative control) to frame KQs in ways that direct analyst attention away from uncomfortable truths; the analyst must explicitly ask "what question is management NOT being asked on earnings calls?" and why
  • M/M/O test #2 — Competitor/motive/opportunity: a well-capitalised competitor has means (capital), motive (market share), and opportunity (contract renewal cycles) to drive an aggressive pricing campaign at exactly the moment when the bull case is most dependent on pricing power holding; the analyst must stress-test the EPS case under an explicit competitive pricing shock
  • M/M/O test #3 — Regulator/motive/opportunity: a sector regulator has means (enforcement powers), motive (consumer protection mandate), and opportunity (pending consultation) to impose a price cap or behavioural remedy at the moment the investment case is most dependent on pricing freedom; the analyst should map the regulatory calendar explicitly
  • Power-of-incentives: sell-side analysts covering a stock have a structural incentive to maintain access to management; this creates a systematic bias toward constructive framing of KQs; the analyst consuming sell-side research should apply a confidence discount to any sell-side view that aligns too cleanly with management narrative
  • Inversion: inverting the KQ — asking "what would have to be true for the investment case to be completely wrong?" — is often the most efficient path to the critical constraint; the inversion answer is usually one of: (a) the moat doesn't exist, (b) the earnings trajectory is structurally impaired, or (c) the re-rating catalyst doesn't arrive
  • ⚡ Outlier signal: the most dangerous second-order implication in concentrated equity investing is illiquidity risk — a KQ that takes 18–24 months to resolve means the analyst is holding through multiple macro cycles; if the market re-prices the sector before the KQ resolves in the bull direction, the IRR case deteriorates regardless of whether the fundamental thesis was correct

Sceptical bullet: The second-order implication the analyst is most likely flinching from is the base-rate of being wrong at DD stage — even with rigorous Q21 + Q22 research, DD-stage investment decisions in concentrated long-only equity have a historically documented error rate of 30–40% over a 3-year horizon; per the obstacle-is-the-way principle, the research process should explicitly incorporate this error rate into position sizing rather than treating conviction-level as a binary variable
