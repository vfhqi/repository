Section 2 – The 80/20 (bottom line up front) 8 findings

Section summary
  1. Hill & Smith offers a highly profitable, US-driven infrastructure growth story with predictable mid-single-digit organic growth, exceptional cash generation, and a strong balance sheet supporting consistent earnings compounding.
1.
Business operations: The company manufactures and supplies highly engineered products and coatings designed to improve infrastructure safety and resilience. It makes money through direct product sales, project-based contracts, asset rentals, and specialized industrial services. The portfolio spans three segments, US Engineered Solutions, UK & India Engineered Solutions, and Galvanizing Services
2.
Investment case: The investment thesis centers on the structural onshoring and infrastructure boom in the US. The group is poised to capture multi-year government funding via the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA) and grid modernization projects. These high-margin US divisions will drive group returns and margins upward as their share of the business expands
3.
Earnings swing factor: The single most important swing factor for earnings is the pace of US infrastructure and grid capacity expansion capex. If US utility and transport spending accelerates, the company's highest-margin divisions will grow disproportionately. Conversely, any delay in project funding or raw material spikes would severely restrict near-term volume growth
4.
Recent performance: The group delivered record FY25 results with revenue reaching £868.8m, up from £855.1m in FY24. Underlying operating profit increased to £151.3m from £143.5m in the prior year, representing a strong margin expansion to 17.40%. Operating cash conversion remained excellent at 91.00% in FY25, down slightly from 99.00% in FY24
5.
Multi-year compounding: Over the long term, the company has operated as a consistent compounder, growing EPS at a 10.00% compound annual growth rate (CAGR) over the past decade. This track record of consistent growth has been driven by a combination of mid-single-digit organic growth and disciplined bolt-on acquisitions. This strategy has successfully pushed operating margins from 12.40% in 2019 to 16.20% in FY24
6.
Core bear risk: The primary threat to the investment case is the ongoing weakness and structural drag in the non-Galvanizing UK businesses. The UK Engineered Solutions division has suffered from lower activity levels in road infrastructure and commercial construction, which fell from £267.0m of revenue in FY24 to £239.4m in FY25. These localized headwinds have required active management intervention and portfolio rationalization to protect group margins
7.
First-look verdict: A closer look is strongly warranted given the structural US growth, high cash conversion, and a massive balance sheet capacity with leverage at just 0.10x net debt to EBITDA. The combination of a £100.00m share buyback and active M&A provides highly predictable earnings growth over the next 18 to 36 months

Section 3 – What the company does 12 findings

Section summary
  1. Hill & Smith operates a highly profitable decentralized model focused on critical infrastructure products and steel protective coatings, with dominant market positions and a massive exposure to US secular growth.

Divisional structure and offerings

1.
Segment reorganization: Effective January 1, 2025, the company reorganized its segments to better align with geographic end markets and internal management reporting. This decision was made under IFRS 8 to reflect the way the Group is now managed and to enable closer focus on geographic growth opportunities. It dissolved the previous Engineered Solutions, Galvanizing Services, and Roads & Security structure which had been in place for several years. The new structure consists of US Engineered Solutions, UK & India Engineered Solutions, and Galvanizing Services
2.
US Engineered Solutions: This division manufactures and supplies a diverse range of composite and steel solutions for infrastructure construction. Key products include energy transmission and distribution poles, composite bridge decks, water and wastewater management products, and off-grid solar lighting. In FY25, this segment generated £416.6m in revenue, up from £390.3m in FY24, and underlying operating profit of £75.0m, up from £69.4m
3.
UK & India Engineered Solutions: This segment supplies products to transport infrastructure, residential construction, data centres, and industrial end markets. Key offerings include hostile vehicle mitigation barriers, bridge parapets, and engineered pipe supports for liquid natural gas markets. Performance has been challenged, with FY25 revenue falling to £239.4m from £267.0m in FY24, and underlying operating profit dropping to £20.9m from £23.8m
4.
Galvanizing Services: This segment provides hot-dip zinc coating services to protect steel products from corrosion. It serves structural steelwork, bridges, agricultural, and lighting markets in both the US and UK. This is the group's highest-margin segment, generating £55.4m in underlying operating profit on £212.8m of revenue in FY25, representing a superb operating margin of 26.03%
5.
Revenue generation model: Revenue is primarily transactional and project-based, generated from the manufacture and supply of physical products. Product and services transferred at a point in time represented £568.9m of total FY25 revenue, while long-term contracts accounted for £299.9m. Asset rentals, principally temporary road safety barriers, provided a highly stable £18.6m of high-margin rental income in FY25, down from £23.6m in FY24

Customer dynamics and advantage

6.
Customer profile and concentration: Customers include transport departments, public utilities, commercial contractors, and industrial players. While the customer base is generally diversified, localized customer concentration is a challenge in specific business units. For instance, the US off-grid solar division, National Signal, has a high concentration with Sunbelt Rentals, which has historically caused earnings volatility due to lumpy order patterns
7.
Competitive advantages and local monopolies: The company possesses substantial competitive advantages rooted in high switching costs and transport-related local monopolies. In Galvanizing Services, the cost of transporting heavy steel limits the geographic reach of any single plant to a 100-150 mile radius. This creates highly profitable, localized market structures where the major players do not compete on price and capacity expansion is highly disciplined
8.
Composite substitution barrier: The Creative Composites Group enjoys high barriers to entry from proprietary technology and structural certification standards. Fiber-reinforced polymers (FRP) are increasingly substituted for traditional wood, steel, and concrete because of their lower embedded energy and maintenance-free profiles. Hill & Smith's scale and engineering expertise in FRP make it the largest integrated structural composites manufacturer in the US
9.
Industry consolidation trends: The underlying infrastructure markets are highly fragmented, allowing Hill & Smith to act as a disciplined consolidator. The company targets 3 to 4 bolt-on acquisitions per year, spending £50m to £70m annually. This active pipeline allows them to acquire small, entrepreneurial competitors, integrate them into the global platform, and extract commercial synergies
10.
Value chain positioning: The company occupies a highly favored position in the mid-to-downstream value chain. By providing essential safety and protective components, its products represent a tiny fraction of total project costs but are critical to regulatory compliance. This provides the group with strong pricing power and the ability to fully pass through volatile raw material costs like steel and zinc
11.
Other side of the story: The group's high exposure to public infrastructure spending exposes it directly to political and budgetary cycles. A significant portion of its revenues depend on government highway funding and utility regulatory approvals in the US and UK. If a major government enters a period of severe fiscal consolidation or delays capital allocations, project backlogs will freeze, causing immediate volume contraction

Margin expansion: Operating profit margins have improved substantially, rising from 14.80% in FY23 to 16.80% in FY24. The group's operating margin reached a record 17.40% in FY25, driven by a positive mix shift toward the higher-margin US businesses. This represents a 260 basis point expansion over just two years
3.
EPS compounding: Earnings per share have followed a predictable, upward trajectory over the medium term. Adjusted EPS rose from 88.94p in FY22 to 104.10p in FY23. 5.
High cash generation: Hill & Smith possesses an exceptionally high-quality earnings profile, as evidenced by its cash conversion history. Free cash flow generation: The group's strong cash conversion translates into material free cash flow. Covenant leverage, measured as net debt to EBITDA, fell from 0.40x in FY23 and 0.30x in FY24 to just 0.10x in FY25. Guidance track record: Management has a proven history of highly conservative guidance, consistently under-promising and over-delivering. For example, in May 2026, early double-digit US growth prompted management to raise full-year guidance just four months into the fiscal year. The willingness to increase EBITA guidance so early in the year is a rare and positive sign from a naturally cautious company

Other side of the story: Despite group-level margin expansion, the severe profitability decay in the UK Engineered Solutions segment represents a structural weakness. Profitability at the UK Roads business is currently estimated to be roughly half of its 2019 level. If management's planned restructuring measures to improve the UK business fail, this division will continue to act as a significant margin and capital drain on the group

Section 5 – The possible investment case 9 findings

Section summary
  1. The investment case for Hill & Smith is highly compelling and strengthens due to structural US infrastructure tailwinds, grid capacity expansion, and high-margin capacity deployment.

US structural growth drivers

1.
US IIJA and infrastructure funding: The largest driver is the massive multi-year tailwind from the US Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA). This program provides $550bn in additional funding, including $110bn for roads and bridges and $73bn for the electrical grid. As this funding flows to projects, it directly translates into increased volumes for Hill & Smith's US safety and electrical segments
2.
Grid modernization and electrification: The group's electrical grid infrastructure platform accounts for approximately 33.00% of US sales as of FY25. Investment in the US electrical transmission and distribution grid is experiencing a multi-year structural expansion, driven by renewable integration and onshoring. This high-margin segment grew at a double-digit rate in the first four months of FY26, supporting group-level EPS expansion
3.
US highway volume acceleration: Leading indicators for US highway construction contracts put in place continue to point to steady volume expansion. For example, Jefferies noted highway construction contracts surged 21.00% year-on-year in early macro data, filtering directly into the company's US order books. This volume growth is highly margin-accretive because the US businesses operate at superior margins compared to the UK

Capacity expansion and rationalization

4.
Capacity expansion investments: To capture growing US demand, management announced a £35.00m capacity expansion plan in March 2026. This capex will be deployed over two years, with £25.00m allocated to expanding US transmission & distribution and galvanizing capabilities. The capacity expansion will begin delivering high-returning volumes starting in FY27, supporting the group's medium-term EBITA margin target of 18.00% plus
5.
Portfolio rationalization: Management is actively pruning underperforming assets to enhance group-level margins. In early FY26, the company sold its VRS permanent steel road barrier business for £5.25m. Although VRS generated £25.00m in revenue in FY25, it operated at breakeven, meaning its immediate removal automatically enhances the group's EBITA margin by 30 basis points
6.
Re-acceleration of bolt-on M&A: After an 18-month hiatus, the group returned to the M&A trail in early 2026. It acquired Freeberg, an engineered metal products manufacturer, and Hentech, a data centre steel solution supplier, for €7.3m. These acquisitions strengthen Hill & Smith's presence in high-growth niche markets like US electrical transmission and European data centres
7.
Bull case criteria and independence: For the bull case to succeed and deliver a 25.00% plus IRR, these drivers do not all need to fire simultaneously because they are largely independent. If the US grid expansion slows, the £100.00m share buyback and margin enhancement from UK restructuring can still sustain double-digit EPS growth. The primary prerequisite for the bull case is simply the avoidance of a severe US macroeconomic recession
8.
Other side of the story: The risk to the strategic capital deployment driver is M&A integration failure or overpayment in a competitive US market. If Hill & Smith deploys £50m to £70m annually on low-return acquisitions, or if newly acquired businesses face sudden customer concentration issues, return on invested capital will deteriorate. A key example is National Signal, which suffered from high customer concentration and experienced severe revenue headwinds post-acquisition

Section 7 – Key risks and the other side 8 findings

Section summary
  1. While macroeconomic risks exist, the overall risk picture remains highly manageable given the unleveraged balance sheet and robust pricing power protecting against commodity volatility.

Operational headwinds and macro exposure

1.
Raw material volatility: The group is highly exposed to volatile input costs, specifically steel and zinc. A sudden spike in global zinc prices directly increases the operating costs of the Galvanizing Services segment. While the company has historically demonstrated strong pricing power to pass these through, rapid hyper-inflation would squeeze margins in fixed-price contracts
2.
UK highway spending delays: Profitability in the UK Roads segment has been severely impacted by delays and structural changes in the UK Road Investment Scheme (RIS3). While RIS3 has finally been released, its focus is heavily weighted toward road maintenance rather than high-margin, large-scale barrier projects. If spending flows remain slow through 2026, UK Roads margins will remain depressed at nearly half of their 2019 levels
3.
US solar lighting customer drag: The off-grid solar division, National Signal, remains a localized headwind. Its key customer, Sunbelt Rentals, guided to a massive 40.00% year-on-year cut in US capital expenditure, suppressing demand for temporary solar lighting. This soft commercial demand acts as a persistent drag on US Roads & Security volumes
4.
Short interest and short-seller reports: A comprehensive review reveals no active short-seller reports or unusually elevated short interest targeting Hill & Smith. The highly predictable earnings trajectory, exceptional cash conversion, and net cash-like position make it an unattractive target for short sellers
5.
The "false friend" warning: Investors must be cautious of strong headline revenue growth driven purely by acquisitions. Acquisitive growth can mask an underlying organic volume decline in legacy segments. It is critical to monitor organic constant currency (OCC) growth rates separately from total growth to verify legacy health
6.
Early-warning signal: The single most critical early-warning metric to monitor is the direction of US highway and utility capital expenditure budgets. A deceleration in state-level highway construction awards or utility grid capex would serve as a leading indicator of an upcoming EPS peak
7.
Other side of the story: The bear case regarding cyclical vulnerability is materially overstated. The group's products are critical for regulatory compliance and safety, making them non-discretionary even during recessions. Furthermore, the multi-year funding cycle of the US IIJA ensures that project allocations are locked in, guaranteeing stable demand regardless of short-term economic fluctuations

Insider shareholdings and transactions: Executive directors are required to build up a shareholding of 200% of their base salary.
