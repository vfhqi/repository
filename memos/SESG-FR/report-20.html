3. THEME §1 — Revenue Drivers + Visibility

J-front verdict: Revenue-side uncertainty is HIGH; backlog quantum is published but backlog quality (duration, take-or-pay fraction, MEO vs GEO split) is not transparent — these questions are load-bearing

Q1 — Backlog composition by technology and contract type

Question: "Of the current backlog, what percentage is MEO versus GEO, and within each, what share is take-or-pay versus usage-based?"

Expected answer: MEO backlog likely <50% of total given GEO's longer legacy; take-or-pay fraction probably 60-70% management will claim

Good answer signature: MEO share growing, >70% take-or-pay across both — confirms revenue predictability and demand pull for new capacity

Bad answer signature: MEO backlog thin, high usage-variable fraction — suggests capacity built ahead of contracted demand, monetisation lag risk

Follow-up probe: "What is the average remaining duration of the MEO take-or-pay contracts you have today?"

Diagnostic note: Bull case requires MEO backlog to be building faster than GEO erosion; if MEO backlog is thin with short duration, the "capacity ramp meets demand" hypothesis is challenged — cross-ref Q19 ACH failure mode F2 (MEO fill rate below plan)

Q2 — Video revenue churn trajectory

Backlog visibility / Churn-retention: "What is the annual churn rate on GEO video contracts today, and has the rate accelerated in the last 4 quarters?"

Expected answer: Management likely quotes ~5-8% annual churn on video; likely to say rate is stable or slightly improving with selective renewals

Good answer signature: Churn rate stable or below 5%, long tail of contracts with 3+ year remaining life — buys time for data revenue to fill the gap

Bad answer signature: Churn accelerating above 8-10%, short remaining life on large accounts — implies video cliff is sooner than consensus models

Follow-up probe: "What fraction of your top-20 video customers have contracts expiring within 24 months, and what is your renewal rate assumption?"

Diagnostic note: False-friend profile — management fluency on "video is managed decline" does not confirm pace of decline; bad answer here is the most underpriced risk in consensus models

Q3 — Data/mobility revenue unit economics vs GEO equivalents

Volume vs price split / End-market segmentation: "On a per-Gbps basis, how does MEO pricing compare to your GEO per-MHz equivalent, and is that spread widening or compressing as MEO supply grows?"

Expected answer: Management will likely claim MEO is fundamentally superior economics per bit — lower latency commands premium; will resist giving a specific number

Good answer signature: Clear Gbps pricing disclosure, premium to GEO on a capacity-adjusted basis, no evidence of price dilution as constellation scales

Bad answer signature: Evasive on specifics, anecdotal customer wins cited instead of pricing data — suggests pricing pressure from LEO competitors (Starlink, OneWeb) is real and not yet fully disclosed

Follow-up probe: "Is the pricing per Gbps in your most recent MEO deals above or below the pricing in deals signed 18 months ago?"

Diagnostic note: This is the single most important unit-economics question — discriminates between "SES is winning on value" vs "SES is discounting to fill capacity" — cross-ref Q19 ACH F4 (LEO substitution accelerates)

Q4 — Government revenue contract pipeline

Forward order book / Customer concentration: "What is the government segment revenue pipeline for 2026-2028, and how much is awarded vs in active procurement?"

Expected answer: Government is growing, management will cite NATO/US DoD relationships; awarded pipeline likely €400-600m range on own estimate

Good answer signature: Material awarded backlog, multi-year contract terms, named programmes (PACE, US government mobility) with clear revenue recognition timeline

Bad answer signature: Heavy reliance on "pipeline" language without awarded contracts — government pipeline is notoriously slow to convert; if awarded backlog is thin, government revenue growth is a forecast not a contract

Follow-up probe: "What is your historical win rate on competed government tenders, and how long from award to first revenue recognition?"

Diagnostic note: Government is the most credible near-term MEO demand vector; thin awarded backlog = bear case gains plausibility

Sceptical bullet: If management answers Q1-Q4 fluently and comprehensively, ask: is this confidence earned from contracted revenue, or is it rehearsed investor-relations narrative? The test is whether they provide numbers (contract coverage rates, churn rates by segment, pricing per Gbps) rather than themes ("strong demand", "unique positioning") — fluency without quantification is a bad-answer signature regardless of tone

4. THEME §2 — Margin Trajectory + Levers

J-front verdict: Margin trajectory is the most consequential near-term modelling variable; SES's high fixed-cost base creates both meaningful operating leverage and significant downside torque — questions should force quantification of the flex vs fixed cost split

Q5 — EBITDA bridge from 2024 to 2026 — what are the dominant levers?

GM driver / Op leverage: "Can you walk me through the 2025-2026 EBITDA bridge — what are the three largest positive and negative levers, and what are the assumptions behind each?"

Expected answer: Positive: MEO revenue ramp, Intelsat synergies, cost reduction; Negative: video decline, integration costs, depreciation step-up from new constellation

Good answer signature: Specific quantification of each lever, synergy phasing tied to integration milestones, clear sense of the EBITDA floor if revenue misses plan by 10%

Bad answer signature: Vague reference to "growth trajectory" without quantifying the video headwind — if management cannot size the video drag precisely, the EBITDA bridge is not well-managed

Follow-up probe: "What is the single largest EBITDA risk you see for 2026 that the market is not pricing correctly?"

Diagnostic note: The answer to the follow-up is the most diagnostic of the entire meeting — management who answer genuinely (vs. deflect) are demonstrating operator credibility; cross-ref BB#2 TC1 RA2

Q6 — Intelsat synergy quantum and phasing

Mix shift / Productivity: "What is the current synergy run-rate from the Intelsat combination, and what phasing do you expect for the remaining €100m+ target — by year, by category (cost vs revenue)?"

Expected answer: Integration is 12-18 months old; early cost synergies (headcount, procurement) likely partially realised; revenue synergies (cross-selling, combined fleet) probably 12-24 months lagged

Good answer signature: Specific realised-to-date number, clear category breakdown (cost vs revenue synergies), realistic phasing with named milestones

Bad answer signature: Aggregate target only, no phasing, "on track" without evidence — the classic pre-disappointment language; if synergies are delayed, margin guidance becomes unreliable

Follow-up probe: "What is the integration cost still to be incurred, and is it above or below your original estimate?"

Diagnostic note: Post-merger integration execution is a high-uncertainty pillar — synergy overstatement and cost understatement are the two most common failure modes in satellite consolidation deals

Q7 — Fixed vs variable cost structure and flex capacity

Capex pacing / Op leverage: "What percentage of your cost base is truly variable with revenue, and what is the minimum revenue level at which EBITDA margin remains above 40%?"

Expected answer: Satellite operators are predominantly fixed-cost; variable fraction likely 15-25%; management may resist giving a breakeven-style floor

Good answer signature: Clear fixed/variable split, willingness to state the revenue floor for target EBITDA margin, evidence of cost flex mechanisms (orbital slot monetisation, frequency leasing)

Bad answer signature: Refusal to frame a downside case — "we don't manage to a floor" is an evasion, not an answer; operators with high fixed costs must manage downside scenarios

Follow-up probe: "In 2020-2021 when revenue was under pressure, what did the variable cost response look like?"

Diagnostic note: Historical behaviour under pressure is the most reliable predictor of future cost discipline — cross-ref operator track record RA3

Q8 — Depreciation trajectory and D&A drag on reported earnings

Capex pacing / R&D adequacy: "O3b mPOWER capital investment is substantial — what is the depreciation schedule for the new constellation, and at what revenue level does the D&A drag invert to margin accretive?"

Expected answer: Constellation capex of ~$3bn implies significant D&A ramp; management likely cites a 15-year asset life; EBITDA-to-EPS bridge is key

Good answer signature: Precise depreciation schedule, clear articulation of EBITDA-to-net-income conversion timeline, no attempt to manage the conversation to EBITDA-only

Bad answer signature: Exclusive focus on EBITDA — SES's history of depreciation-heavy guidance means EBITDA can grow while EPS stagnates; avoiding the D&A conversation is a flag

Follow-up probe: "What is your 2027 net income estimate, and what assumptions drive the difference between EBITDA growth and net income growth?"

Diagnostic note: For EPS-trajectory-focused investors, EBITDA is insufficient; the D&A bridge to EPS is the most commonly elided part of SES investor communications

Sceptical bullet: Fluent margin narrative from management is expected (SES has a sophisticated IR function); the bad-answer signal is precision-avoidance — any answer that stays at the EBITDA level without bridging to free cash flow and EPS should trigger a follow-up on the D&A schedule and interest cost trajectory

5. THEME §3 — Capital Allocation + Balance Sheet

J-front verdict: Post-Intelsat, SES carries materially higher leverage; capital allocation discipline is the highest-uncertainty pillar for the 18-36M investment thesis — questions should test the leverage ceiling, the dividend commitment, and the RONIC hurdle on incremental investment

Q9 — Leverage target and deleveraging path

Leverage target / Investment runway: "What is your target net debt/EBITDA range, and what is the explicit timeline to reach it — what is the single largest lever pulling you there?"

Expected answer: Management has guided to leverage reduction post-Intelsat; target likely 3.0-3.5x; FCF generation and synergy realisation are the stated levers

Good answer signature: Specific target range with year attached, clear FCF-to-debt-repayment schedule, no reliance on asset sales as primary deleveraging mechanism (which would signal insufficient organic FCF)

Bad answer signature: Target range only without timeline, reliance on "portfolio optimisation" language — if organic FCF is insufficient to hit the leverage target, asset sales become a necessity not a choice

Follow-up probe: "What is the 2026 free cash flow estimate after all capex and interest, and how much of that is committed to debt reduction versus other capital returns?"

Diagnostic note: The leverage timeline discriminates between "balance sheet normalises and opens capital return optionality" (bull) vs "leverage constrains dividends and creates covenants risk" (false-friend / bear)

Q10 — Dividend policy under leverage constraint

Dividend policy / SBB cadence: "Given the current leverage level, how durable is the dividend — under what revenue/EBITDA scenario would the board consider a cut or rebasing?"

Expected answer: Management will defend the dividend robustly; may cite progressive dividend commitment; unlikely to volunteer a cut scenario

Good answer signature: Explicit coverage ratio at which dividend remains comfortable, acknowledgement that leverage constrains buyback capacity, honest framing of the trade-off

Bad answer signature: Unconditional dividend commitment without quantification of the coverage floor — an unconditional commitment is a credibility risk, not a comfort

Follow-up probe: "What is the dividend coverage ratio at your 2026 consensus EBITDA, and what does it look like in a 10% revenue miss scenario?"

Diagnostic note: Dividend security is a key factor for the reader's total-return model — an uncovered or thinly-covered dividend at current consensus would make the bull-case IRR fragile

Q11 — M&A appetite post-Intelsat

M&A philosophy / RONIC discipline: "Is the Intelsat integration complete enough to consider further M&A, and what would the ideal next acquisition look like — spectrum asset, vertical customer, technology capability?"

Expected answer: Management will likely position as "integration focus for now" but leave optionality open; spectrum assets (especially C-band monetisation in new markets) are the most plausible target

Good answer signature: Clear statement that integration must demonstrate results before further M&A, explicit RONIC hurdle, resistance to transformative deals at current leverage

Bad answer signature: Appetite for further consolidation before Intelsat synergies are proven — serial acquisitions before integration completion are a known value-destruction pattern in satellite sector

Follow-up probe: "What is the minimum time from Intelsat close to when the board would consider another material acquisition?"

Diagnostic note: M&A discipline in a capital-intensive sector with high integration risk is a critical operator-quality test — cross-ref BB#2 TC1 RA3 RONIC discipline

Sceptical bullet: Capital allocation conversations with SES management have historically been EBITDA-dominated and leverage-tolerant — the sceptical prior is that "balance sheet normalisation" language masks a slower deleveraging pace than stated; ask for the FCF waterfall explicitly

6. THEME §4 — Strategy + Competitive Position

J-front verdict: SES's strategic narrative (MEO as the premium layer, GEO as the cash engine, government as the growth vector) is coherent but increasingly contested by LEO proliferation — questions should probe where SES is actually winning rather than where it claims to compete

Q12 — LEO competitive response and displacement risk

Competitive response / Disruption risk: "In the mobility and enterprise segments, are you seeing head-to-head displacement by Starlink or OneWeb on live deals — and where are you winning, where are you losing?"

Expected answer: Management will acknowledge LEO competition but frame it as serving different needs (reliability, SLA, latency for MEO vs low-cost point solution for LEO); likely to deny significant displacement

Good answer signature: Honest acknowledgement of LEO wins in specific sub-segments, clear articulation of where MEO/GEO is genuinely superior (maritime safety, government COMSEC, broadcast), win/loss data by segment

Bad answer signature: "LEO serves a different market" without any win/loss evidence — if management cannot cite specific instances where SES won against Starlink on a competed bid, the competitive moat is weakening

Follow-up probe: "In the last 12 months, how many maritime or enterprise bids have you lost to a LEO provider, and what was the stated reason?"

Diagnostic note: The LEO displacement question is the most important strategic discriminator — cross-ref Q19 ACH F4; fluent but unquantified answers on this topic are a false-friend signal

Q13 — O3b mPOWER utilisation trajectory and break-even timing

Win condition / Moat trajectory: "What is the current utilisation rate on the mPOWER constellation, and at what utilisation level does it reach its targeted EBITDA contribution — what is the timeline?"

Expected answer: Early-stage utilisation; management likely guides to progressive ramp through 2025-2026; full-constellation economics probably require 50-60% utilisation

Good answer signature: Specific current utilisation figure, clear ramp milestones, quantified EBITDA contribution at target utilisation, acknowledgement of the ramp period costs

Bad answer signature: "Strong demand" without utilisation data — capacity built ahead of demand is the satellite sector's most common value-destruction pattern; if management resists quantifying utilisation, the ramp is behind plan

Follow-up probe: "What was the utilisation rate 6 months ago versus today — is the ramp linear or accelerating?"

Diagnostic note: Utilisation trajectory is the single most important leading indicator for the MEO revenue thesis — cross-ref Q19 ACH bull-case anchor B1

Q14 — C-band spectrum value realisation

Regulatory positioning / Adjacency expansion: "How much C-band spectrum value remains unrealised on SES's balance sheet, and are there further monetisation opportunities in non-US markets?"

Expected answer: US C-band repacking is largely complete and proceeds recognised; some residual value in non-US markets; management may not be forthcoming on specific valuations

Good answer signature: Clear accounting of recognised vs unrealised C-band value, specific non-US markets where regulatory proceedings are active, timeline to potential additional receipts

Bad answer signature: "US process is done" with no view on non-US — if management hasn't mapped non-US C-band optionality, it suggests they are not maximising spectrum asset value

Follow-up probe: "In Latin America and Southeast Asia, what is the regulatory posture on C-band repurposing, and is SES actively engaged in those proceedings?"

Diagnostic note: C-band optionality is an underexplored value layer in most broker models; a credible answer here could be a bull-case upside surprise

Sceptical bullet: Strategic fluency from satellite operator management is high — they have sophisticated investor-day presentations; the sceptical read on a fluent §4 answer is that SES management knows how to frame competitive positioning better than they know how to win competitive deals; ask for the win/loss data not the framework

7. THEME §5 — Management Quality + Operator Track Record

J-front verdict: CEO Steve Collar (2018-present) has guided SES through the MEO investment cycle and the Intelsat consolidation — the quality question is whether he is a capital-allocator or an operator-at-scale, and whether the incentive structure rewards the right behaviour

Q15 — Compensation structure and alignment with EPS trajectory

Power of incentives / Operator track record: "What is the composition of your long-term incentive plan — what metrics drive vesting, and are those metrics EBITDA-weighted or include EPS and free cash flow?"

Expected answer: LTI likely includes TSR, EBITDA growth, possibly leverage/FCF metrics; EBITDA weighting is common in capital-intensive operators and can create misalignment with EPS trajectory

Good answer signature: FCF and/or EPS included in LTI metrics, relative TSR component against a named peer group, vesting cliff aligned with the MEO ramp timeline (3-5 year horizon)

Bad answer signature: EBITDA-only or EBITDA-heavy LTI with no FCF/EPS component — this is the strongest structural misalignment signal; management optimising for EBITDA while depreciation dilutes EPS is a known pattern

Follow-up probe: "If EBITDA grows as planned but net income growth is muted by D&A, does the LTI still vest fully?"

Diagnostic note (Power of incentives, Concept A): This is the most direct test of whether management incentives are aligned with the concentrated-long investor's EPS-trajectory mandate; an EBITDA-only LTI is a structural flag — cross-ref BB#2 TC1 RA3

Q16 — Sandbagging discipline and guidance history

Sandbagging discipline / Communication clarity: "Looking back at your guidance track record over 2020-2024, how often have you met, exceeded, or missed EBITDA guidance — and what caused the misses?"

Expected answer: Management will likely highlight met/beat; may acknowledge 2020 COVID disruption; probably won't volunteer the Intelsat integration cost overruns

Good answer signature: Honest acknowledgement of misses, specific causal explanation, evidence that guidance methodology has been tightened post-Intelsat

Bad answer signature: "We have a strong track record" without specifics, or deflection to exceptional items — management who cannot discuss their misses honestly are higher-risk communicators

Follow-up probe: "On the Intelsat acquisition, was the integration cost budget higher or lower than the original estimate, and by how much?"

Diagnostic note: Guidance track record is the most objective management-quality metric available — cross-ref operator RA2; a pattern of meeting or beating guidance is the strongest predictor of EPS trajectory reliability

Q17 — CEO's personal investment thesis and what would change it

Animal-CEO probe / Crisis playbook: "As CEO, if you were an outside investor with a 3-year horizon, what would be the two or three things you'd most want to see happen for this to be a great investment — and what would be the bear case you'd worry most about?"

Expected answer: CEO will likely articulate the MEO monetisation story and cost synergies; the bear case answer will be the most diagnostic

Good answer signature: CEO articulates a genuine bear case (e.g. MEO fill slower than expected, LEO pricing pressure accelerating, leverage remains elevated) — this signals strategic honesty and self-awareness

Bad answer signature: Only upside articulated, bear case deflected to "macro risk" or "competitor irrationality" — a CEO who cannot articulate his own bear case is either overconfident or incentivised not to disclose it

Follow-up probe: "What is the single most important internal KPI you watch weekly that would be the earliest warning of the bear case materialising?"

Diagnostic note: The answer to the follow-up is the highest-diagnostic single data point in the meeting — management's actual internal leading indicator is almost never disclosed in public filings; cross-ref Q19 ACH diagnostic items

Sceptical bullet: Management quality is easy to mistake for polish — SES's management team is experienced and media-trained; the sceptical test is whether answers to Q15-Q17 reveal thinking or presentation, i.e. whether management can be surprised into an honest answer by a non-standard question

8. THEME §6 — Risk + Downside (Pre-mortem Cross-ref)

J-front verdict: The Q19 pre-mortem failure modes (MEO fill rate, LEO displacement, leverage trap, video cliff acceleration) map directly to the questions below — these are the highest-plausibility downside paths and must be explicitly tested

Q18 — MEO fill rate and contractual protection against slow ramp

Bear-case probe / Failure mode test: "If MEO utilisation ramps 30% slower than your base case, what is the EBITDA and FCF impact in 2026, and do any customer contracts have minimum guarantees that protect SES?"

Expected answer: Management will likely resist the 30%-miss scenario as too conservative; minimum guarantee coverage is likely partial

Good answer signature: Willingness to quantify the downside scenario, honest assessment of minimum-guarantee coverage, articulation of cost-flex mechanisms that cushion FCF in a miss scenario

Bad answer signature: "We don't manage to downside scenarios" or deflection to pipeline strength — an operator who cannot quantify the impact of their largest investment missing plan has insufficient financial controls

Follow-up probe: "What is the minimum contracted MEO revenue for 2026 versus what you need to hit your EBITDA guidance?"

Diagnostic note: Cross-ref Q19 ACH failure mode F2 (MEO fill rate) — this is the highest-plausibility bull-case invalidator; management reluctance to answer = bad signal regardless of tone

Q19 — Covenant and refinancing risk

Regulatory exposure / Pricing fragility: "At what EBITDA level do any bond covenants or credit facility covenants come under stress, and when is the next major refinancing event?"

Expected answer: Management likely comfortable with current headroom; next refinancing probably 2026-2028 depending on bond maturity profile

Good answer signature: Specific covenant headroom (e.g. 2.0x EBITDA cushion to nearest covenant), clear maturity wall map, proactive refinancing strategy

Bad answer signature: Vague "comfortable headroom" without quantification, or evasion on the maturity wall — if the refinancing window coincides with a period of elevated leverage and/or rate uncertainty, the FCF outlook is compromised

Follow-up probe: "How much of your gross debt is fixed-rate vs floating, and what is the average cost of debt today versus 12 months ago?"

Diagnostic note: Leverage + rate sensitivity is the most direct balance-sheet downside risk — cross-ref Q19 ACH failure mode F3 (leverage trap); the CFO's answer here is more diagnostic than the CEO's

Q20 — Regulatory and orbital/spectrum risk

Regulatory exposure / False-friend probe: "Are there any pending regulatory proceedings — orbital slot challenges, frequency coordination disputes, or government access restrictions — that could materially impact revenue in the next 24 months?"

Expected answer: Management will likely say no material proceedings; may reference ongoing ITU coordination but downplay risk

Good answer signature: Transparent inventory of active proceedings, acknowledgement of any disputed orbital slots or frequency coordination timelines, clear legal/regulatory contingency plan

Bad answer signature: "No material risks" without specificity — orbital/spectrum risk is the single most asymmetric downside in the satellite sector; a single disputed slot at a critical orbital position can strand capacity; blanket dismissal is under-thinking

Follow-up probe: "Specifically on GEO orbital slots serving European and Asian markets — are there any ITU filing deadlines in the next 36 months that require capital expenditure to protect?"

Diagnostic note: Regulatory risk in satellite is systematically undermodelled by consensus; this question surfaces information that almost never appears in sell-side models

Sceptical bullet (Q19 ACH coverage audit): Q19 ACH failure modes NOT covered by questions in this memo include: (1) Key-person risk on CEO tenure — if Collar departs mid-integration, what is the succession plan?; (2) Launch failure risk for residual O3b mPOWER satellites — what insurance coverage exists?; (3) Currency mismatch risk — SES reports in euros but earns significant USD revenue; Q20-Q3 partially touches pricing but not the FX hedge book; these should be added as supplementary questions if meeting time permits

9. CROSS-CUTTING META + NON-OBVIOUS QUESTIONS

J-front verdict: The questions management will NOT volunteer answers to are more diagnostic than the ones they will — the unconventional angles below are specifically designed to surface what rehearsed investor-relations presentations conceal

Non-obvious angle #1 — The inverse question: what would make SES a bad investment?

WH Smith probe: "I've heard the bull case many times — tell me the three things that would make SES a genuinely bad investment from here, in order of probability"

Expected answer: Management will likely give the rehearsed bear-case framing (macro, LEO disruption) — the diagnostic is whether they go beyond the consensus risk factors

Good answer signature: A genuinely surprising bear-case item — e.g. "mPOWER latency is not as differentiated as we claimed against next-gen LEO" or "the government procurement cycle is 18 months longer than we modelled" — demonstrates strategic honesty

Bad answer signature: Entirely macro/exogenous risks, no endogenous risk acknowledgement — means either management has blind spots or is incentivised not to discuss them

Follow-up probe: "Which of those three is the one you lose sleep over?"

Diagnostic note: The answer to "which one you lose sleep over" is the highest single-data-point in the meeting — cross-ref Concept A (power of incentives: management will resist disclosing the risks that are closest to becoming real)

Non-obvious angle #2 — The customer perspective question

⚡ Outlier angle: "What is the most surprising negative piece of feedback you have received from a major customer in the last 6 months — something that made you update your own view of the product?"

Expected answer: Management is extremely unlikely to have been asked this before; will likely deflect to positive feedback or generic "always listening to customers"

Good answer signature: A specific and honest piece of negative feedback (e.g. "pricing per Gbps is still 2x LEO in certain use cases and customers are telling us that gap needs to close faster") — demonstrates customer-centric self-awareness

Bad answer signature: Redirection to Net Promoter Score or general customer satisfaction language — this deflection is itself diagnostic of a management team that has over-insulated itself from critical feedback

Follow-up probe: "Did that feedback change anything in your commercial strategy?"

Diagnostic note: ⚡ The unconventional customer-feedback question surfaces the gap between investor-day narrative and commercial reality — the consensus interview would never ask this; cross-ref WH Smith probe archetype

Cross-roads question — Multiple truths probe (Concept B CA3)

Multiple-truths probe: "I accept the bull case on MEO demand and the GEO harvest story simultaneously — and I also accept that LEO pricing is compressing your addressable market faster than consensus models — given both are true, what is your honest assessment of the 3-year revenue trajectory?"

Expected answer: Management will find this framing uncomfortable because it denies them the either-or deflection; they must engage with the simultaneous-truth framing

Good answer signature: Genuine engagement with the "and yet" — e.g. "you're right that both are true, and the question is whether government and safety-of-life segments can grow fast enough to offset the commercial pressure — here's what we think the numbers look like"

Bad answer signature: Rejection of the framing ("I don't accept that LEO is compressing our addressable market") — a refusal to sit with the multiple-truth framing suggests strategic rigidity

Follow-up probe: "If LEO pricing in maritime falls another 30% over the next 18 months, what does that do to your maritime revenue line?"

Diagnostic note (Concept B CA3): The multiple-truths probe is the most intellectually honest question in the memo — it does not allow bull-framing or bear-framing; only operators with genuine strategic clarity can answer it well

Edge-detection probe — The capex optionality question

Edge-detection probe: "If you had to cut 20% of capex tomorrow, which programmes would you protect and which would you cut — and what does that tell us about your true capital allocation priorities?"

Expected answer: Management will resist the premise ("we don't manage to arbitrary cuts"); if forced to answer, mPOWER Phase 2 and government capability programmes are probably protected

Good answer signature: Specific and honest answer — reveals true priority ranking of capital programmes, confirms that video infrastructure capex has the highest optionality (can be reduced without near-term revenue impact)

Bad answer signature: "We have a well-optimised capex plan" without engaging with the hypothetical — this answer tells the investor that management has not stress-tested its capital allocation

Follow-up probe: "What is the minimum sustaining capex to maintain current revenue, and how far below your current capex budget is that number?"

Diagnostic note: The minimum-sustaining-capex question is the most precise way to size the FCF upside optionality — almost never answered in public; if answered, it is a materially valuation-relevant data point

Diagnostic crystalliser — The number management watches most

Diagnostic crystalliser: "What is the single most important number you look at each month that is not in any of your public filings — and why does that number matter?"

Expected answer: Management may deflect to published KPIs; if they engage, likely to mention utilisation rate, pipeline conversion, or churn by segment

Good answer signature: A specific internal metric disclosed (e.g. "90-day pipeline conversion rate on MEO government bids" or "average contract size trend on new MEO wins") — this is the most direct window into management's information advantage over the market

Bad answer signature: Deflection to public KPIs — means management either doesn't have a sharper internal indicator or is incentivised not to share it; both are information

Follow-up probe: "Is that metric currently above or below where you expected it to be 6 months ago?"

Diagnostic note: The answer to the follow-up ("above or below expectation") is more valuable than the metric itself — it reveals whether the investment thesis is ahead of or behind plan in real time
