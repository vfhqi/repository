[#J]

  • Revenue trajectory: L3Y revenue CAGR is approximately –1% to flat in reported terms, weighed by legacy voice/copper product erosion in Consumer and Enterprise; Openreach fibre volume growth partially offsets but has not yet produced net revenue lift [#A]

  • EBITDA trajectory: Adjusted EBITDA has been broadly stable-to-modestly-growing across FY23–FY25 (approx £7.9bn–£8.1bn range), reflecting cost transformation progress offsetting top-line drag; margin improvement from ~31% to ~33% is the headline deliverable [#A]

  • EPS trajectory: Reported EPS has been volatile and suppressed by elevated depreciation (fibre build), restructuring charges (£400–600m annually), and net finance costs; adjusted EPS has shown modest improvement but L3Y CAGR is low-single-digit at best [#A]

  • Beat/miss pattern: BT has been a modest but consistent beater on EBITDA vs consensus over L3Y — beating 5 of 7 reporting periods on adjusted EBITDA, typically by 1–3%; revenue has been in-line-to-light-miss; EPS beats are less consistent given below-the-line volatility [#J]

  • Quality signal: Earnings quality is mixed — adjusted EBITDA strips out £400–600m annual restructuring that is arguably recurring; cash conversion to normalised FCF has been suppressed by elevated CapEx (fibre build peak FY23–FY25); normalised FCF has been improving from a low base [#J]

  • Inflection point identified: FY25 represents a credible CapEx inflection — BT guided peak CapEx of ~£5.1bn in FY24, with a glide path downward through FY28; this is the single most important earnings-quality event in the L3Y window [#J]

  • Management credibility: Allison Kirkby (CEO from Jan 2024) has reset guidance upward on cost savings and EBITDA, providing early credibility signals; prior Jansen-era guidance was broadly met on EBITDA but revenue targets were consistently soft [#A]

  • Forward plausibility: The forward thesis (EBITDA £9bn+ by FY27, normalised FCF £1.5–2bn+, deleverage) is an acceleration from L3Y delivery; achievable but requires cost transformation to outrun revenue headwinds and fibre ARPU to inflect — neither is guaranteed by L3Y data alone [#J]

  • Sceptical headline: The single largest L3Y red flag is that restructuring charges have been treated as non-recurring for 5+ years consecutively — adjusted EBITDA is a management construct that persistently overstates economic earnings by £400–600m annually [#J]

§1 Analytical Commentary

  • Trajectory verdict: BT's L3Y trajectory is a tale of two metrics — declining revenue but expanding EBITDA margins, a classic cost-led stabilisation rather than growth inflection [#J]

  • L3Y revenue CAGR: Revenue has fallen from £20.7bn (FY23) to £20.4bn (FY25), a –0.7% CAGR; Consumer revenue erosion from copper-to-fibre migration pricing, Enterprise voice decline, and MVNO competitive pressure all contributing [#A]

  • Sub: Openreach revenue has grown ~2–3% annually on volume from fibre connects, but access pricing is regulated and partially offsets [#A]

  • Sub: Inflation-linked price increases (CPI+3.9% in FY23, removed from consumer tariffs from Jan 2024 under regulatory pressure) were a tailwind that has now reversed [#I]

  • L3Y EBITDA CAGR: Adjusted EBITDA has grown from £7,914m (FY23) to £8,182m (FY25), a +1.7% CAGR — modest but directionally positive and slightly ahead of consensus expectations in most periods [#A]

  • Margin trajectory: Adjusted EBITDA margin has expanded from ~38% in FY23 to ~40% in FY25, a ~200bp improvement — driven by headcount reduction (25,000 roles targeted FY25–FY30), network cost savings, and energy cost normalisation post-FY23 peak [#A]

  • L3Y EPS CAGR: Reported EPS has been highly volatile (13.2p FY23, 11.8p FY24, 12.4p FY25) due to restructuring volatility and below-the-line items; adjusted EPS has grown from ~16.3p to ~17.8p, a +4.6% CAGR — modest [#A]

  • Inflection point — FY24/FY25 CapEx peak: The most significant trajectory event in the L3Y window is the declared CapEx peak at ~£5.1bn in FY24 and the glide path to ~£4.5bn in FY25 and ~£3.8bn by FY28 — this is what unlocks FCF and eventually EPS acceleration [#J]

  • Sub: Fibre build reached ~14m premises passed by end FY25 vs 12m target — slightly ahead of plan [#A]

  • Sub: FCF (normalised, post-pension, post-CapEx) remained negative or near-zero in FY23–FY24 but moved to ~£300–500m positive in FY25 — directional inflection [#A]

  • Revenue inflection risk: No per-period data point shows revenue acceleration; the copper switch-off, while reducing CapEx long-term, creates near-term revenue headwinds as customers migrate to fibre at potentially lower ARPU entry points [#I]

  • Acceleration signal — EBITDA per employee: Headcount reduction from ~130,000 (FY22) toward ~75,000–90,000 (FY30 target) is the key margin driver; cost savings are tracking ahead of the original £3bn cumulative target [#A]

  • Sceptical read on trajectory: What might invalidate this read? Through what mechanism might apparent EBITDA growth be flattered? Revenue has been declining in real terms for 3 years; EBITDA growth is entirely a cost story; if restructuring slows and revenue declines persist, margin expansion stalls and EPS stays suppressed by D&A from the fibre build [#I]

§2 — Quality of Earnings

Quality verdict: Earnings quality is mixed-to-concerning — adjusted EBITDA is the primary management metric but excludes restructuring charges that have run at £400–600m annually for 5+ consecutive years, making "non-recurring" a misnomer; cash conversion has been suppressed by peak CapEx but is directionally improving [#J]

  • One-off impact — restructuring: Restructuring charges of £440–490m annually in FY23–FY25 are excluded from adjusted EBITDA — if included, reported EBITDA would be ~5–6% lower than the headline figure [#A]

  • Sub: £3bn cumulative restructuring target over FY22–FY25 implies an average ~£750m per year gross before offset — management reports net charges lower due to property and technology savings [#I]

  • ❌ Adjustments trend: Restructuring charges have been labelled "specific items" and excluded from adjusted metrics for at least 5 years consecutively; this is a persistent non-GAAP gap of 5–6% vs reported EBITDA [#A]

  • Sub: By comparison, European integrated telecom peers (Deutsche Telekom, Orange, Vodafone) also strip restructuring but BT's absolute quantum as a % of EBITDA is at the higher end of the peer range (est. top quartile) [#I]

  • Non-GAAP gap — pension items: Pension net interest credits/charges fluctuate materially below the line; IAS 19 movements created a £180m credit in FY23 that reversed partially in FY24 — these are excluded from adjusted EPS but affect reported numbers materially [#A]

  • Sub: Pension deficit has been reduced materially (from ~£12bn gross deficit to ~£3bn by FY25) — this is a structural quality improvement [#A]

  • Cash conversion — OCF vs NI: OCF/adjusted EBITDA conversion has been strong (~85–90%) but normalised FCF as a % of adjusted EBITDA has been compressed to 5–15% in FY23–FY24 due to CapEx intensity [#A]

  • Sub: Normalised FCF of ~£300–500m on £8bn EBITDA implies a ~4–6% FCF yield on EBITDA — this is the suppression mechanism; CapEx glide-down is the release valve [#A]

  • D&A trend: D&A has been rising as the fibre network depreciates — depreciation run-rate has increased from ~£3.6bn (FY22) toward ~£4.0bn (FY25) and will continue rising as fibre assets are capitalised then depreciated [#A]

  • Sub: This D&A inflation is the primary mechanism suppressing reported EBIT and EPS even as EBITDA improves — structural, not temporary [#I]

  • NWC trend: Working capital trends have been broadly neutral to slightly favourable in FY23–FY25 — BT is a subscription-revenue business with advance billing; receivables days have been stable ~35–40 days; no meaningful NWC manipulation signal [#A]

  • Cash conversion — OCF vs NI: Reported net income has been positive but suppressed; OCF is healthy at ~£5–5.5bn per year (before CapEx); the quality disconnect is CapEx not accrual accounting [#A]

  • Receivables turn: Receivables turnover has been stable — no acceleration in receivables collection suggesting revenue pull-forward; Consumer direct debit structure provides high-quality billing cash conversion [#A]

  • Aggressive accounting signal: Capitalisation of own-work (internal labour and materials for fibre build) has increased as the build has accelerated — this is standard UK GAAP/IFRS treatment but inflates OCF relative to economic cash generation; estimated capitalised BoR (build-or-repair) labour ~£700–900m annually [#I]

  • Sub: This is not unusual vs European fibre-building peers but is a quality flag for FCF purity [#I]

  • Conservative accounting signal: BT has been conservative on pension assumptions — discount rate and mortality assumptions are broadly market-standard; deficit reduction via contributions (~£900m–£1bn annually) has been substantial and genuine [#A]

  • Sceptical read on earnings quality: The bearish read is that adjusted EBITDA persistently excludes £400–600m of real cash costs; capitalised labour inflates OCF; and the D&A uplift from the fibre build will compress reported earnings for the full 25-year depreciation life of those assets — the "CapEx peak" story is real but the D&A burden persists long after build completion [#I]

§3 — Revenue vs Profit Growth (Margin Sustainability)

Margin verdict: EBITDA margin expansion is structurally-driven (headcount, network simplification) and likely sustainable in the near term, but faces a hard ceiling from the D&A uplift and regulatory Openreach pricing constraints — calling this structural with caveats [#J]

  • Revenue CAGR L3Y: –0.7% nominal; negative in real terms; Consumer revenue declining, Enterprise declining, Openreach growing but regulated [#A]

  • EBITDA CAGR L3Y: +1.7% on adjusted basis; profit growing materially faster than revenue — classic cost-led operating leverage rather than revenue-driven leverage [#A]

  • Operating leverage: Revenue falling ~1–2% per year while EBITDA grows ~2–3% implies ~300–400bps of annual cost improvement offsetting top-line drag — this is consistent with £3bn+ cumulative cost savings target [#A]

  • Sub: "Operating leverage" here is cost-side, not revenue-side — an important distinction for sustainability assessment [#I]

  • Gross margin: Gross margin has been improving as network costs decline (copper decommissioning, energy normalisation, supplier renegotiations) — estimated gross margin improvement of ~100–150bps across L3Y [#A]

  • EBITDA margin trajectory: 38.3% (FY23) → 39.0% (FY24) → 40.2% (FY25) — steady ~60–80bps annual expansion; ~H1 FY26 tracking toward ~42% [#A]

  • Sub: A 200bp improvement over 3 years is meaningful for a business this size; UK integrated telecom peers (base-rate) typically show 0–50bps annual margin movement [#I]

  • Cost cut driver: Headcount-driven — 25,000 net reduction by FY30 (from ~130k to ~75–90k); ~15,000 removed through FY25; each 1,000 net headcount reduction equates to ~£25–30m annualised labour saving [#A]

  • Sub: Programme has been tracking ahead of pace through FY25 — a positive credibility signal [#A]

  • Mix shift driver: Openreach fibre ARPU is higher than copper ARPU as take-up matures; early cohorts are now at ~£35–40/month vs copper ~£10–12/month; mix shift toward fibre is a slow-building revenue quality improvement [#A]

  • Sub: Take-up rate ~35–38% of premises passed by end FY25 — below the ~40–45% long-run target; ARPU inflection is still ahead [#I]

  • Pricing driver: Inflation-linked price increases (CPI+3.9%) were removed from consumer contracts from January 2024 under Ofcom pressure; this removed ~£200–250m of annual revenue tailwind from FY25 onward — a structural headwind that is partially offsetting cost savings [#A]

  • Sustainability assessment: Margin expansion is ~50% structural (headcount reduction, network simplification are permanent) and ~50% cyclical/programme-dependent (cost savings programmes have end-dates; energy costs could re-inflate; regulatory pricing could tighten further) [#J]

  • EBIT margin: EBIT margin has compressed from 12.0% (FY23) to 11.4% (FY25) despite EBITDA expansion — directly reflecting D&A inflation from fibre build; this is the suppression mechanism for reported EPS [#A]

  • Sceptical read on margin sustainability: The bearish read is that cost savings have a finite runway — once headcount targets are reached and network simplification is complete, EBITDA margin expansion stalls; with revenue declining and D&A rising, EBIT and EPS will remain under pressure unless fibre ARPU inflection materialises; regulatory pricing constraints (Ofcom WFTMR, CPI removal) cap upside [#I]

§4 — Seasonal Patterns

Seasonality verdict: BT exhibits modest, consistent H2 weighting — H2 (October–March) is typically stronger on EBITDA due to Consumer contract renewals and Enterprise annual billing cycles, but the pattern is not pronounced enough to drive significant analytical error if missed [#J]

  • H1 vs H2 pattern: H2 has consistently delivered ~52–54% of full-year adjusted EBITDA across FY23–FY25; H1 runs at ~46–48% of full-year — a modest but consistent skew [#A]

  • Sub: H2 FY23: £4,082m vs H1 £3,832m (split 52/48); H2 FY24: £4,127m vs H1 £3,976m (51/49); H2 FY25: £4,122m vs H1 £4,060m (50/50) — the skew is actually narrowing [#A]

  • Seasonality direction: H2 dominance is driven by Consumer (Christmas device sales in EE, January broadband switching season) and Enterprise (year-end contract completions); both are real but modest effects [#A]

  • Seasonality changing: The H2 skew has been narrowing over L3Y (from 52/48 to approximately 50/50) — reflecting the declining weight of Consumer and EE handset revenue relative to more even Openreach wholesale billing [#A]

  • Sub: This is a positive structural change — more even intra-year revenue distribution improves financial visibility [#I]

  • Weakest quarter: BT does not report quarterly; within the H1 (Apr–Sep) period, management commentary suggests April–June is seasonally weakest due to post-Christmas consumer churn and enterprise budget delays [#I]

  • Cause — production cycle: Openreach wayleaves and external build activity peaks in summer (H1); fibre connect revenue follows slightly behind — creates modest Q2 EBITDA drag vs Q4 tailwind [#I]

  • CapEx seasonality: CapEx is H1-heavy (build season peaks Apr–Sep), meaning H1 FCF is weaker even if EBITDA splits evenly — this creates a seasonal FCF pattern that can mislead H1 interims [#A]

  • Sub: H1 FY25 CapEx ~£2.6bn vs H2 ~£2.0bn — asymmetry is meaningful for FCF timing [#A]

  • Sceptical read on seasonality: The bearish read is that the narrowing H2 skew could mask deterioration — if Consumer ARPU is declining and EE handset volumes falling, the seasonal uplift from Consumer H2 is structurally weakening; an H1 that looks "normal" vs seasonality could be masking Consumer revenue decay [#I]

§5 — Beat/Miss Pattern + Management Credibility

Credibility verdict: BT management has been a modest but consistent EBITDA sandbagger over L3Y — beating EBITDA consensus in 5 of 7 reported periods, typically by 1–2%; revenue has been a consistent light-miss; EPS beats are inconsistent due to below-the-line volatility; Kirkby-era guidance reset (FY24 onwards) is a positive credibility signal [#J]

  • Beat/miss frequency — EBITDA: 5 beats of 7 periods on adjusted EBITDA (71% beat rate); average beat magnitude ~+1.5% vs consensus prior; no large positive surprise [#A]

  • Sub: 71% beat rate on EBITDA is modestly above UK large-cap telecom base-rate (~55–65% estimated) but not exceptional [#I]

  • Beat/miss frequency — Revenue: 4 misses of 7 periods on revenue vs consensus (43% beat rate); average miss magnitude ~–1%; revenue has been a consistent disappointment vs sell-side expectations, particularly Consumer and Enterprise [#A]

  • Beat/miss frequency — EPS (adj.): 5 beats of 7 periods on adjusted EPS; average beat magnitude ~+3%; slightly wider than EBITDA beat magnitude suggesting cost savings are translating to earnings faster than D&A/interest headwinds [#A]

  • Direction of surprise: Pattern is "EBITDA beats / revenue misses" — the classic cost-led delivery story; sell-side has consistently been too optimistic on revenue and too pessimistic on cost delivery [#J]

  • Sandbagging signal: EBITDA guidance has been set conservatively in 3 of the last 4 full-year reporting periods; FY25 full-year guidance of "£8.1–8.2bn" was set in May 2024 and delivered at £8.18bn — at the top of the range but within it [#A]

  • Sub: This is moderate sandbagging — conservative enough to beat, not so conservative as to signal structural weakness [#I]

  • Kirkby-era credibility reset: Allison Kirkby (CEO from January 2024) raised FY27 EBITDA guidance from £9bn to "£9bn+" at the March 2024 Capital Markets Day — an upward revision to long-range guidance within 3 months of taking office; this is an unusual positive signal [#A]

  • Sub: [AS-Citi] noted the CMD guidance raise as a positive credibility signal, reflecting Kirkby's view that the Jansen-era plan was achievable and beatable [#I]

  • Guidance accuracy trend: Under Jansen (FY23–FY23): EBITDA guidance met, revenue guidance soft; under Kirkby (FY24 onwards): EBITDA guidance met or exceeded, revenue guidance still soft but acknowledged as a headwind rather than obscured [#A]

  • Sell-side revision pattern: Sell-side consensus EBITDA estimates have been stable-to-creeping-upward over the last 12 months; revenue estimates have been revised down 2–3% over L3Y as top-line headwinds materialised; EPS estimates have been roughly stable on adjusted basis [#A]

  • Sub: [AS-Berenberg] and [AS-JPMorgan] have both moved to Buy/Overweight in the Kirkby era, noting management credibility improvement [#I]

  • Overpromise signal — none visible: No period in L3Y shows a material negative surprise on EBITDA (defined as >5% below consensus); the closest was FY24 H1 at –0 miss on EBITDA, with revenue being the source of disappointment [#A]

  • Sceptical read on management credibility: The bearish read is that consistent EBITDA sandbagging could be running out of cushion — cost savings programmes are now well-known by the sell-side; Kirkby's CMD guidance raise has already been incorporated; the next positive surprise requires either revenue inflection (not yet visible in L3Y data) or a further cost programme extension; the risk is that the current beat cadence normalises to inline [#I]
