3. §1 — EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (TOP-3 MOST-DIAGNOSTIC QUESTIONS)

  • Diagnostic verdict: Three questions discriminate bull/bear/false-friend more sharply than any others — organic volume/price decomposition, EV mix gross margin ceiling, and M&A hurdle-rate discipline
  • Q-1: "Walk me through organic revenue growth in the last two reported periods — what percentage was volume-driven vs price/mix-driven, and how does that split look in the order book for the next two quarters?"
  • Why most-diagnostic: if volume is the dominant driver, the structural growth thesis is real; if price/mix is carrying growth, the bear case (commoditisation snapping back) is live
  • Q-2: "As EV/electrification moves from X% to a higher share of revenue — what happens to blended gross margin: does mix help, or does the competitive intensity in EV cable manufacturing compress your advantage?"
  • Why most-diagnostic: the bull case requires both volume AND margin to compound together; if management hedges on margin, the EPS multiple compresses
  • Q-3: "Name the last two acquisitions you walked away from and explain why — what price/multiple/cultural issue broke the deal?"
  • Why most-diagnostic: discipline is revealed by what you don't do, not what you do; if management cannot name walk-aways fluently, M&A discipline is narrative not practice
  • Top-3 clustered signal: If management answers all three confidently and specifically, the bull case is meaningfully de-risked; if any two are hedged or deflected, the false-friend profile rises sharply
  • Sceptical bullet: What questions does the analyst NOT want to ask? The hardest one — "Is the EV tailwind genuinely differentiated for Volex, or are you a commodity cable maker with a premium narrative?" — is the one most worth asking; the obstacle is the way

4. §2 — THEME 1: REVENUE DRIVERS + VISIBILITY

  • Net signal verdict: Revenue-side uncertainty is HIGH — organic vs M&A decomposition, end-market mix, and order book duration are all insufficiently visible from public filings alone; these questions are load-bearing

Q2.1 — Backlog visibility / Volume vs price split:
- Question: "In your two largest end-markets by revenue, what percentage of next-12-months revenue is contracted or under framework agreement vs spot — and what is the volume vs price decomposition of reported organic growth?"
- Expected answer: Mid-50s% contracted/framework, with volume the majority driver of organic growth in structural markets
- Good answer: Specific percentages, framework contract durations cited, volume clearly dominant with price a modest tailwind
- Bad answer: Vague ("most is under long-term relationships"), inability to split volume from price, deflection to total-company figures
- Follow-up probe: "In the EV segment specifically — are framework agreements volume-committed or just pricing-committed, and what happens if an OEM misses volume call-offs?"
- Diagnostic note: Volume-driven growth + contracted backlog = bull case confirmed; price-driven growth + spot exposure = false-friend (sell-side consensus may be modelling wrong driver)

Q2.2 — Customer concentration:
- Question: "What percentage of revenue does your top-3 and top-10 customers represent — and in the last 12 months, has concentration increased or decreased?"
- Expected answer: Top-10 probably 40-55% of revenue; management likely to say concentration is managed/diversifying
- Good answer: Specific data, trend toward diversification, no single customer above 10-12%, proactive commentary on new-customer pipeline
- Bad answer: Refusal to give numbers, deflection ("we're comfortable with our mix"), or implicit admission that concentration has increased
- Follow-up probe: "Which single customer, if lost, would most change your EBIT profile — and what is the contract renewal timeline for that relationship?"
- Diagnostic note: High and rising concentration + upcoming renewal = bear-case trigger; declining concentration = structural improvement in revenue quality

Q2.3 — End-market segmentation / Share gain or loss:
- Question: "In each of your three largest end-markets, are you gaining or losing share — and what is the evidence: win rates on new programmes, RFQ conversion, or customer feedback?"
- Expected answer: Share gain claim in EV/electrification; more nuanced or defensive answer in legacy industrial/datacom
- Good answer: Specific win-rate data, named programme wins, RFQ pipeline quantified
- Bad answer: Generic "we're well-positioned" language, inability to cite specific programme wins, deflection to market growth as proxy for share
- Follow-up probe: "What is your strike rate on new programme RFQs in EV in the last six months — and how does that compare to 18 months ago?"
- Diagnostic note: Declining RFQ conversion in EV = early signal of competitive pressure commoditising the product set; rising = moat strengthening

Q2.4 — Forward order book / Revenue guidance confidence:
- Question: "How much of your FY+1 consensus revenue estimate is already in the order book today — and at what gross margin does that backlog convert?"
- Expected answer: Partial coverage (perhaps 40-60%), with management confident about the remainder from pipeline visibility
- Good answer: Precise coverage percentage, margin visibility on backlog, candid about the portion still to be won
- Bad answer: "We don't give that level of detail" — acceptable for some companies but at DD stage this is a yellow flag; or implausibly high coverage with no margin detail
- Follow-up probe: "What is the historical conversion rate from RFQ pipeline to order book — and is that rate stable or compressing?"
- Diagnostic note: Low backlog coverage + high consensus revenue estimate = execution risk; high coverage at in-line margins = bull case has runway

  • Sceptical bullet: Fluent answers on visibility should be probed harder, not accepted — management who can quote exact backlog percentages without notes may have been asked this question many times; the rehearsed answer does not equal comfort; ask the follow-up probe regardless

5. §3 — THEME 2: MARGIN TRAJECTORY + LEVERS

  • Net signal verdict: Margin trajectory is the single most contested element of the bull case — gross margin improvement claims must be decomposed into mix, pricing power, and operational leverage to be credible

Q3.1 — GM driver / Mix shift:
- Question: "Walk me from your current blended gross margin to your medium-term target — what percentage of the bridge is mix, what is pricing-cost spread, and what is pure operational efficiency?"
- Expected answer: Mix the largest driver (higher-value EV/medical cable), with modest price/cost and scale benefits — probably a 2-4pp gross margin expansion narrative
- Good answer: Specific bridge with percentages per driver, timeline, named product families driving mix improvement
- Bad answer: "Mix will be a tailwind" with no quantification, or inability to separate mix from pricing in the bridge
- Follow-up probe: "What is the gross margin differential between your highest-margin product family and your median — and is the highest-margin family growing faster than the median?"
- Diagnostic note: If mix is the dominant bridge driver but the high-margin product families are also attracting competition, the margin expansion story collapses; this question surfaces whether management has stress-tested the claim

Q3.2 — Pricing-cost spread / FX pass-through:
- Question: "In the last 12 months, how much raw material cost inflation did you absorb vs pass through — and on which products do you have pricing power to pass through and on which do you not?"
- Expected answer: Partial pass-through (50-70%), with better pass-through in custom/specialist products and compression in commodity-grade cables
- Good answer: Specific pass-through percentages by product segment, acknowledgement of where pricing power is limited, mechanism for pass-through (contractual escalators vs renegotiation)
- Bad answer: "We pass through most costs" without differentiation by product — overly bullish and not credible; or admission of significant margin compression with no recovery plan
- Follow-up probe: "Do your framework contracts with OEM customers contain automatic raw material escalators — and if not, what is the renegotiation cycle length?"
- Diagnostic note: Long renegotiation cycles with no automatic escalators = bear-case trigger in an inflationary environment; automatic escalators = meaningful moat element worth upgrading in the investment case

Q3.3 — Operating leverage / Productivity:
- Question: "At what revenue growth rate does operating leverage meaningfully kick in — and what is the incremental EBIT margin on the next £50m of organic revenue?"
- Expected answer: Incremental EBIT margins probably 18-25% if management is credible on fixed cost base claims
- Good answer: Specific incremental margin estimate with reasoning (fixed cost base, capacity headroom, labour leverage); consistent with disclosed cost structure
- Bad answer: Inability to quantify incremental margins, or implausibly high claims (>30% incremental EBIT margin) that aren't consistent with disclosed capex/headcount trajectory
- Follow-up probe: "How much of your current manufacturing capacity is utilised — and at what utilisation rate do you need to invest in a new facility, which would reset the operating leverage profile?"
- Diagnostic note: Low utilisation headroom + high consensus growth = capex acceleration coming, which is a near-term free cash flow headwind the market may not be pricing

Q3.4 — R&D adequacy / Capex pacing:
- Question: "Is your current R&D spend and engineering headcount sufficient to win the next generation of EV/electrification programmes — or are you investing below the level required to stay on the bid list in 3 years?"
- Expected answer: Management likely to defend current spend; the diagnostic is whether they acknowledge the question's premise or dismiss it
- Good answer: Acknowledgement that investment is increasing, specific headcount additions in engineering, named capability build-out (e.g. thermal management, higher-voltage charging cables)
- Bad answer: Defensive deflection ("our customers come to us"), no acknowledgement of capability investment requirement, or conflation of maintenance capex with capability investment
- Follow-up probe: "Which OEM customers have told you your current product capability is insufficient for a next-generation programme — and what did you do with that feedback?"
- Diagnostic note: If no OEM has ever raised a capability gap, management is either genuinely best-in-class or not getting candid feedback; distinguish between these via follow-up

6. §4 — THEME 3: CAPITAL ALLOCATION + BALANCE SHEET

  • Net signal verdict: Capital allocation is the highest-uncertainty pillar — Volex's string-of-pearls M&A model requires consistent RONIC discipline; this is where management credibility is most testable

Q4.1 — RONIC discipline / Returns hurdle:
- Question: "What is your explicit RONIC hurdle for acquisitions — and on your last three completed deals, what is the actual post-integration RONIC vs the acquisition-case projection?"
- Expected answer: Stated hurdle probably WACC+4-6%; management likely to claim deals are on track but may hedge on the earliest acquisitions
- Good answer: Specific RONIC figures per deal, honest acknowledgement of cases where early returns were below hurdle with explanation of recovery, credible revision to acquisition underwriting if track record reveals optimism bias
- Bad answer: Vague "we target double-digit returns" without actual post-integration data; inability to compare projected vs actual RONIC; defensive "it's too early to tell" on deals closed 2+ years ago
- Follow-up probe: "On the acquisition that has most disappointed vs its business case — what went wrong operationally and what did you change in your M&A process as a result?"
- Diagnostic note: Inability to identify a disappointing deal = management credibility red flag; specific post-mortem with process change = operator archetype confirmed

Q4.2 — M&A philosophy / Walk-away discipline:
- Question: "Name two acquisitions you were close to completing in the last 18 months but walked away from — what specifically broke the deal?"
- Expected answer: Management who have genuine pipeline discipline will name deals fluently; management without it will struggle to be specific
- Good answer: Specific deals named (or described without naming), clear articulation of the value/price/culture issue, no regret that the deal didn't close
- Bad answer: Vague "the market was too expensive," inability to describe specific situations, or implicit admission that price was the only filter
- Follow-up probe: "What multiple would have made those walk-aways attractive — and is there a price at which quality doesn't matter?"
- Diagnostic note: ⚡ This is the most unconventional question in §4 — the answer to walk-aways reveals the actual M&A discipline filter better than any stated hurdle rate

Q4.3 — Leverage target / Working capital management:
- Question: "At what net debt/EBITDA level does your acquisition programme pause — and what is your working capital investment per £1 of revenue growth in a typical organic-only year?"
- Expected answer: Leverage ceiling probably stated at 2.0-2.5x; working capital intensity likely to be a modest headwind given Volex's asset-light model in some segments
- Good answer: Clear leverage ceiling with explanation of the credit-facility headroom, specific working capital ratio with trend data, proactive commentary on leverage reduction timeline post-acquisition
- Bad answer: "We're comfortable with the balance sheet" without specifics, inability to quantify working capital investment per revenue pound, leverage ceiling that conveniently exceeds the current level
- Follow-up probe: "In a recession scenario where revenue declines 15% — what happens to your covenants, and what is the first lever you pull?"
- Diagnostic note: Covenant headroom in a stress scenario is a binary risk factor for a leveraged acquirer; management who can answer this fluently have stress-tested it; those who can't may not have

Q4.4 — Dividend policy / Investment runway:
- Question: "Walk me through the capital allocation priority stack — organic investment, M&A, deleveraging, and dividend; how does the priority order change if EBITDA disappoints 10% vs budget?"
- Expected answer: M&A and organic investment likely top of stack; dividend modest; deleveraging a secondary priority
- Good answer: Explicit priority stack with triggers for reordering, candid about dividend as lowest priority, clear on M&A pause trigger
- Bad answer: Diplomatic non-answer ("we balance all stakeholders"), no trigger articulation, dividend characterised as a commitment rather than a residual
- Follow-up probe: "Has the board ever formally discussed suspending the dividend to accelerate M&A — and what was the conclusion?"
- Diagnostic note: If dividend has never been formally subordinated to M&A in a board discussion, capital allocation governance may be less disciplined than stated

7. §5 — THEME 4: STRATEGY + COMPETITIVE POSITION

  • Net signal verdict: Strategy narrative is well-rehearsed — the diagnostic questions here are the ones that probe for the gaps in that narrative rather than confirming it

Q5.1 — Moat trajectory / Competitive response:
- Question: "In EV cable assemblies — what specifically prevents a tier-1 automotive supplier from vertically integrating the cable assembly step and cutting you out of the supply chain?"
- Expected answer: Engineering complexity, certification requirement, speed-to-market benefit of outsourcing — these are the stated moats; management should be able to quantify them
- Good answer: Specific certification barriers named (UL, ISO, OEM-specific qualification), named instances where OEMs considered and rejected in-sourcing, quantified cost-of-switching-away-from-Volex
- Bad answer: Generic "we're the specialist, OEMs want to focus on their core," no quantification of switching cost, no acknowledgement that large OEMs have the capability to insource
- Follow-up probe: "Which of your top-5 OEM customers has the internal capability to replicate your cable assembly at scale — and are any of them investing in that capability?"
- Diagnostic note: If a top-3 customer has invested in insourcing capability, the moat narrative requires immediate re-evaluation

Q5.2 — Adjacency expansion / Win condition:
- Question: "Where is the next £100m of incremental revenue coming from in 5 years — name three specific product/market adjacencies and the reason Volex wins in each one?"
- Expected answer: Higher-voltage charging cables, medical-grade power systems, datacentre power infrastructure — management should be specific
- Good answer: Three named adjacencies with current beachhead revenue, identified route-to-market, and honest assessment of competitive position in each
- Bad answer: Generic "electrification is a megatrend," inability to name specific adjacencies beyond current segments, or three adjacencies that all require major capability investment with no current presence
- Follow-up probe: "In the adjacency where you have the strongest position today — what is your current revenue and what is the annual growth rate?"
- Diagnostic note: Inability to quantify current beachhead position in stated adjacencies = strategy is aspiration not execution; specific beachhead data = credible expansion platform

Q5.3 — Disruption risk / Defensive posture:
- Question: "What is the most credible scenario in which Volex's EV cable business is worth materially less in 7 years than it is today — and what are you doing about it now?"
- Expected answer: Commoditisation of EV cable assembly as the technology matures and specifications standardise; management should acknowledge this risk
- Good answer: Explicit acknowledgement of commoditisation risk, stated migration toward higher-complexity products (thermal management, integrated battery connections), investment in capability to move up the value chain before commoditisation hits
- Bad answer: Dismissal ("EV is increasingly complex"), no acknowledgement of the technology-maturation/commoditisation dynamic, or defensive pivot to other end-markets without addressing the core question
- Follow-up probe: "At what percentage of EV cable revenue do you see specs sufficiently standardised that price becomes the primary competitive variable — and what is your margin assumption at that point?"
- Diagnostic note: If management cannot quantify the commoditisation horizon, the long-term margin trajectory is structurally uncertain

8. §6 — THEME 5: MANAGEMENT QUALITY + OPERATOR TRACK RECORD

  • Net signal verdict: Management credibility is supported by the track record but the incentive structure details — LTIP design, performance metrics, EPS vs revenue weighting — are load-bearing and underexplored

Q6.1 — Sandbagging discipline / Communication clarity:
- Question: "Look back at your last 5 annual guidance statements — how many times did you beat, miss, or meet consensus at year-end — and what was the average beat/miss magnitude?"
- Expected answer: Modest beat pattern if management sandbaggs; or inline track record; management will likely claim beats were driven by execution rather than conservative guidance
- Good answer: Specific beat/miss data cited or acknowledged, honest characterisation of the guidance philosophy (conservative, realistic, ambitious), candid about years where performance disappointed
- Bad answer: "We don't give specific guidance" deflection if in fact they do; or inability to recall the beat/miss track record (their IR team knows it cold)
- Follow-up probe: "In the year where you most missed expectations — what specifically went wrong operationally and what changed as a result?"
- Diagnostic note: Management who can't describe a miss candidly without blame-shifting are a culture red flag; management who describe it with specificity and process-change are operator-archetype positive

Q6.2 — Power of incentives / Compensation drivers:
- Question (Concept A — MANDATORY): "Walk me through your LTIP structure — what are the performance metrics, the weighting between EPS and revenue targets, and the total payout as a percentage of base at on-target performance?"
- Expected answer: EPS and ROCE probably the dominant metrics; revenue may feature; total on-target LTIP payout probably 100-150% of base
- Good answer: Specific metric weighting, clear articulation of the EPS/ROCE targets and the trajectory they require, acknowledgement of whether the targets are stretch or achievable
- Bad answer: "Talk to the remuneration committee" deflection; or vague description of the structure without the metric specifics; management should know their own LTIP cold
- Follow-up probe: "At current consensus estimates — are you on track to hit full LTIP payout, partial payout, or at risk of zero vesting — and what does the EPS trajectory need to look like to hit full?"
- Diagnostic note (Concept A): LTIP metric weighting determines management behaviour — heavy EPS weighting incentivises margin management and possibly revenue trade-offs; heavy revenue weighting incentivises top-line growth with less margin discipline; the actual payout trajectory predicts near-term behaviour more reliably than strategy presentations

Q6.3 — Crisis playbook / Culture archetype:
- Question: "Describe a moment in the last three years when the business faced a serious operational setback — what was your personal role in the response and what did you learn about the organisation's resilience?"
- Expected answer: COVID supply chain disruption, a specific customer programme delay, or a post-acquisition integration problem — management should have a real answer
- Good answer: Specific, personal, un-PR'd narrative with candid acknowledgement of what went wrong and genuine learning, credible account of how the organisation responded under pressure
- Bad answer: Generic "we've built a resilient business" without a specific incident; an incident described entirely in terms of management triumph rather than genuine challenge; blame directed entirely externally
- Follow-up probe: "In that situation — what decision do you wish you had made faster, and what would you do differently now?"
- Diagnostic note: The decision they wish they'd made faster is often the decision they are currently making slowly on a different problem — the answer is diagnostic of current organisational speed

Q6.4 — Operator track record / Hiring posture:
- Question: "In the last 18 months, which single hire has had the most positive impact on the business — and is that person incentivised to stay?"
- Expected answer: A specific name/role in a growth-critical area (EV business development, supply chain, M&A integration)
- Good answer: Specific person/role described, clear articulation of impact on business performance, credible retention plan
- Bad answer: "We have a great team" without specifics; inability to identify a single impactful hire; or a hire that turns out to be in a non-growth-critical function
- Follow-up probe: "What role, if it became vacant tomorrow, would most worry you — and do you have a succession plan?"
- Diagnostic note: The role they'd most worry about losing is the single-point-of-failure in the operating model; if no succession plan exists, key-person risk is underpriced

9. §7 — THEME 6: RISK + DOWNSIDE (PRE-MORTEM CROSS-REF)

  • Net signal verdict: Three failure modes dominate — margin compression from EV commoditisation, customer concentration unwinding, and leverage overhang in credit stress; each has a diagnostic question

Q7.1 — Bear-case probe / Pricing fragility:
- Question: "In your EV cable business — what would a 20% price reduction from a large Chinese tier-1 competitor do to your volume and margin, and has this happened in any product category already?"
- Expected answer: Management will likely acknowledge Chinese competition but frame Volex as differentiated on quality/certification/speed; the diagnostic is whether they can name a product already under Chinese price pressure
- Good answer: Acknowledgement of specific product categories where Chinese pricing has entered the market, honest assessment of where Volex can defend (high-complexity/certified) vs where it cannot (commodity specifications), proactive migration plan
- Bad answer: Dismissal of Chinese competition risk, inability to name any product currently under price pressure, or deflection to quality/certification without quantifying the price premium customers will actually pay
- Follow-up probe: "What is the current price premium Volex commands over a comparable Chinese supplier in your highest-volume EV cable product — and has that premium widened or narrowed in the last 12 months?"
- Diagnostic note (pre-mortem cross-ref): If the price premium is narrowing in the highest-volume product, the margin expansion thesis is at risk before mix improvement can offset it

Q7.2 — Customer churn vector / False-friend probe:
- Question: "Which customer relationship, if it deteriorated, would most surprise the market — and what is the current health of that relationship?"
- Expected answer: Management will be reluctant to name the most-at-risk relationship; the diagnostic is whether they engage seriously with the question
- Good answer: Engagement with the question's premise, credible characterisation of the top-3 relationship health, proactive commentary on renewal timelines and any current friction
- Bad answer: Deflection ("all our customer relationships are strong"), inability to acknowledge that any relationship has friction, formulaic "we're a strategic partner" language
- Follow-up probe: "When is the next major contract renewal with a top-5 customer — and at what revenue and margin terms is the renewal likely to clear?"
- Diagnostic note: Upcoming renewal with uncertain terms + concentration = near-term revenue risk the market may not be pricing; proactive renewal language with specific terms = de-risking signal

Q7.3 — Regulatory exposure / Inventory at risk:
- Question: "If tariff regimes on Chinese-manufactured electrical components tighten further — what percentage of your COGS is exposed, and what is the relocation/re-sourcing timeline?"
- Expected answer: Volex has manufacturing across multiple geographies, so management likely to characterise exposure as manageable; the diagnostic is the specific COGS percentage and the timeline to de-risk
- Good answer: Specific COGS exposure percentage, named alternative sourcing already in progress, honest timeline for mitigation (12-18 months minimum is credible)
- Bad answer: "We're well-diversified" without specifics, inability to quantify COGS exposure by geography, or implausibly short mitigation timeline
- Follow-up probe: "In a scenario where tariffs on Chinese components rise 25% in 90 days — what is the gross margin impact before mitigation, and what is your mitigation playbook?"
- Diagnostic note: Tariff stress test is binary-risk relevant given current trade environment; management who have not stress-tested this in the last 6 months are running operational risk unacknowledged

Q7.4 — Failure mode test / Hedge effectiveness:
- Question: "What single macroeconomic event — not company-specific, purely macro — would most damage Volex's earnings in the next 18 months, and are you hedged against it?"
- Expected answer: Probably a sharp EV demand slowdown (OEM capex pause), a sharp industrial production decline, or a currency shock; management should have a primary answer
- Good answer: Specific macro scenario named, honest characterisation of hedge effectiveness (or lack thereof), acknowledgement of which end-markets have the least cyclical buffer
- Bad answer: "We're diversified across end-markets" without naming a specific worst-case macro scenario; inability to identify the most cyclically exposed segment
- Follow-up probe: "If EV adoption growth rates halve from current consensus in the next 18 months — what is the revenue and EBIT impact, and what is the management response?"
- Diagnostic note (pre-mortem cross-ref): EV adoption slowdown is the highest-plausibility single-event failure mode for the investment case; management response quality to this question is the most direct test of scenario planning discipline

  • Sceptical bullet: Q19 ACH-matrix items NOT covered by this question set — supply chain disruption from a specific geography (Taiwan/ASEAN) concentration in manufacturing, key-person departure at divisional operating level (below CEO/CFO), and reputational risk from a quality recall in a safety-critical cable segment; these are flagged as gaps; consider adding a specific Q on quality-recall exposure given medical and EV safety criticality

10. §8 — CROSS-CUTTING META + NON-OBVIOUS QUESTIONS

  • Net signal verdict: Management will answer the consensus questions fluently — the load-bearing information is in the edges, the walk-aways, the surprises, and the things they stopped measuring

Non-obvious angle 1 — WH Smith probe / Edge-detection:
- Question: "What is the most surprising piece of feedback you received from a customer in the last 6 months — something that genuinely changed how you think about the business?"
- Why non-obvious: Consensus interviews ask about strategy and markets; this asks management to surface information they weren't expecting — their response quality (specific vs generic) is directly diagnostic of how close they are to the customer
- Expected answer: A specific operational piece of feedback (delivery speed, specification flexibility, engineering support quality) that prompted an internal change
- Good answer: Specific, named (without customer identity) feedback with a described internal response; evidence that the feedback loop is short and acted upon
- Bad answer: Generic "customers tell us they value our quality and flexibility" — this is the worst possible answer because it confirms the feedback loop is filtered before it reaches the CEO
- Diagnostic crystalliser: If the CEO cannot recall a specific piece of surprising customer feedback in the last 6 months, the customer-proximity culture claim is narrative not practice

Non-obvious angle 2 — Capex triage / Investment runway:
- Question (Concept A — what management wouldn't volunteer): "If you had to cut capex 20% tomorrow with no ability to recover it, which three projects would you protect and which would you abandon — and what does that tell us about your actual investment priorities?"
- Why non-obvious: Forces management to reveal the true priority ranking under constraint, which is more informative than stated priorities in a resource-abundant environment
- Expected answer: EV-related capacity and engineering capability likely protected; legacy industrial capex likely cut; the diagnostic is whether EV investment would survive the cut or be partially sacrificed
- Good answer: Clear, specific triage with reasoning; willingness to name what would be cut without defensiveness; consistency between stated priorities and triage choices
- Bad answer: "We wouldn't be in that situation" deflection; vague "we'd review everything"; or triage that reveals legacy business investment is actually higher priority than stated EV focus
- Diagnostic crystalliser: If legacy-business capex would be protected over EV capability investment in a 20% cut, the EV growth narrative is structurally weaker than presented

Non-obvious angle 3 (Concept B — multiple truths / "and yet" question):
- Question: "The bull case on Volex is clear — structural electrification tailwind, capable management team, disciplined M&A, margin expansion runway; and yet — what is the argument you would make for selling the stock today, if you had to make it honestly?"
- Why this is Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 3: Invites management to occupy the bear-case framing simultaneously with the bull case; fluent bears-case articulation from management is actually a positive signal (intellectually honest); inability to articulate it is a cultural warning
- Expected answer: Valuation, execution risk in integration, macro sensitivity of EV demand — management who have genuinely stress-tested the investment case can name these
- Good answer: Specific, un-PR'd bear-case articulation with at least one non-obvious risk (e.g. "the quality of our M&A pipeline has declined as multiples rose and we're not immune to vintage risk")
- Bad answer: "I can't make that case" or pivot to why the bears are wrong — this is the worst answer because it confirms the feedback loop to the CEO is filtered; or a bear-case that is entirely external/macro (competitor risk, rate environment) with no internal acknowledgement

Stewing trigger — incentive misalignment probe:
- Question: "If EPS growth disappoints 15% vs budget in FY+1 — what happens to the executive team's total comp, and does that create any incentive to manage short-term earnings vs invest for long-term value?"
- Expected answer: Management will likely characterise the LTIP as long-term focused; the diagnostic is whether they acknowledge the tension
- Good answer: Honest acknowledgement of the short-term/long-term tension, specific explanation of how the LTIP design mitigates it (3-year vesting, no annual cash bonus that creates perverse incentive), credible account of a prior decision to sacrifice short-term EPS for long-term investment
- Bad answer: "Our incentives are aligned with shareholders" without acknowledging the tension; inability to name a specific investment decision that hurt short-term EPS but was the right long-term call
