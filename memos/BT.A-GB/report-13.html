§1 — Current Management Guidance

BT's current formal guidance was last substantively updated at the FY25 results (May 2025) and carries forward targets established at the November 2023 Capital Markets Day . The CMD represents the primary medium-term guidance anchor.

  • Net direction: Guidance is directionally credible but quantitatively stretched — management has held medium-term targets unchanged through two years of macro headwinds, which is either confidence or rigidity [#J]

  • FY current — EBITDA: Adjusted EBITDA guided to £8.2–8.3bn for FY26 (April 2025–March 2026), the midpoint representing ~2% growth versus FY25's £8.0bn actual [#A]

  • FY current — revenue: Group revenue guided broadly flat to low single-digit decline in FY26 in absolute terms; management frames this as "normalisation" of Consumer and stabilisation in Enterprise — organic growth narrative depends heavily on mix shift toward higher-margin FTTP and 5G [#A]

  • FY current — FCF: Normalised FCF guided to "at least £1.5bn" by FY27 — management's signature commitment; FY26 interim target implied ~£1.1–1.2bn, consistent with the capex-taper trajectory [#A]

  • FY current — CapEx: Capital expenditure guided to £4.9–5.1bn for FY26, with the network build peak provisionally passed in FY24/FY25; CMD targets a secular capex decline toward £4.5bn by FY27 [#A]

  • Medium-term — N2Y: By FY27, management targets adjusted EBITDA >£9.0bn, FCF ≥£1.5bn, and cumulative capex reduction implying meaningful FCF inflection — this is the "harvest phase" narrative following peak network investment [#J]

  • Medium-term targets: CMD (Nov 2023) also targets 40% FTTP penetration by FY27/FY28, >£3bn in annualised cost savings by FY29, and customer ARPU growth driven by broadband speed upsell — these are the assumption layers underneath the headline financials [#A]

  • Source — investor day: CMD November 2023 is the primary anchor; FY25 results (May 2025) confirmed no change to medium-term targets despite flat revenue in FY25 [#A]

  • Source — earnings call: FY25 call noted "continued confidence in the FY27 FCF milestone" and referenced Openreach build momentum and BT Business simplification as execution progress [#A]

  • Update timing: Annual results (May) and H1 results (November) are the two formal update windows; CMD-level resets occur every 2–3 years; next CMD not yet scheduled [#A]

  • Caveat: All guidance is given on an "adjusted" basis excluding specific items — pension charges, restructuring, EE-legacy integration, and Openreach regulatory settlements are routinely classified as specific items yet consume real cash [#J]

  • Conditional assumption: FTTP build milestones assume no material regulatory intervention on Openreach access pricing; Ofcom's WFTMR review creates a latent asymmetric risk not explicitly modelled in guidance ranges [#A]

  • Sceptical read: The "adjusted" framing systematically flatters the guidance picture — pension charges have appeared in 4 of 4 L4 reporting periods as "specific items", averaging ~£200m per year; adding these back collapses the apparent FCF trajectory materially [#J]

§3 — Guidance Pattern (Sandbagging vs Over-Promising)

The pattern is metric-dependent, not company-level — BT sandbangs EBITDA and manages capex tightly while structurally over-promising FCF.

  • Net posture: Split posture — conservative on operational earnings, optimistic on cash conversion; this split is the most analytically important characterisation of BT's guidance behaviour [#J]

  • Sandbagger signal — EBITDA: 4/4 beats, average +0.95% above mid; management consistently guides to achievable floor, likely reflecting cost-certainty in the Openreach-regulated cost base and BT Business restructuring program [#A]

  • Sandbagger signal — CapEx: 3/4 underspends; the capex number appears to function as a ceiling guidance (maximum spend) rather than a midpoint, making "beating" the guide structurally predictable [#A]

  • Over-promiser signal — FCF: 3/4 misses, average -11% versus midpoint guidance; the FCF miss pattern is structural, not event-driven: pension payments, working capital, and one-off remediation costs recur across periods [#J]

  • Over-promiser signal — EPS: 4/4 misses on implicit pre-release consensus; the EPS story is flattered by the adjusted/reported gap; investors relying on adjusted EPS for valuation are exposed to a systematic presentation risk [#J]

  • Mixed pattern — revenue: Revenue guidance is set with sufficiently wide ranges to almost always be achieved; provides little discriminating information about management's true guidance behaviour [#A]

  • Beat consistency — EBITDA: The EBITDA beat is consistent but narrow (0.6–1.3%) — not the large sandbagging cushion seen in, for example, European infrastructure peers where beats of 3–5% are common; more "precision guidance" than genuine conservatism [#A]

  • Pattern shift: CEO Kirkby introduced tighter H1 guidance language in FY25 — explicit FCF guidance for the interim period rather than year-end only — which represents a structural improvement in transparency, though only 2 periods exist to judge [#J]

  • Magnitude — miss: The worst FCF miss (FY23, -15.8%) is severe by UK telecom standards — Vodafone and Virgin Media O2 peers have historically exhibited FCF guidance variance of ±8% at worst; BT's FY23 miss sits in the bottom quartile of peer experience [#A]

  • Sceptical read: Sandbagging on EBITDA may be approaching exhaustion — with the cost program entering Year 3 and the easy restructuring savings taken, the "guide low, beat high" EBITDA dynamic may narrow as organic revenue headwinds in Consumer and Enterprise intensify; the cushion is thinner than the L4P pattern implies [#J]

§4 — Trend in Guidance Accuracy

The trend shows tentative improvement in FY25, concentrated in FCF, but it is too early to declare a structural shift.

  • Net trend: Marginally improving in FY25 but recovery is fragile — the first near-miss on FCF guidance in four years is encouraging, but the improvement coincides with a lower FCF guidance base and capex underspend, not a step-change in underlying cash generation [#J]

  • Recent accuracy — FCF: FY25 FCF ~in line versus prior-year misses of -5% to -16%; however, the FY25 guide itself was calibrated lower at the CMD, so the "hit" partially reflects downward guidance revision rather than execution improvement [#J]

  • Historical accuracy — EPS: EPS accuracy has deteriorated mildly: FY22 miss -10%, FY23 miss -12%, FY24 miss -9%, FY25 miss ~-4%; the narrowing in FY25 is a positive signal but remains inside a persistent miss range [#A]

  • Accuracy improving — EBITDA: EBITDA accuracy has been stable and consistent across all L4 periods — no deterioration trend; the metric most amenable to management control (cost base) is the metric managed most reliably [#A]

  • Accuracy deteriorating — revenue: Revenue guidance accuracy has subtly deteriorated as Consumer ARPU normalisation has proved harder to model; FY23 and FY24 both saw slight revenue underperformance versus initial guidance [#A]

  • Volatility driver — macro: Inflation in energy, labour, and supply chain inputs (particularly for Openreach civils work) was the primary FCF accuracy driver in FY22/FY23; by FY25 those pressures moderated, which partly explains the FCF improvement — not pure management execution [#A]

  • Volatility driver — execution: BT Business simplification (legacy product wind-down) proved harder and slower than guided in FY23/FY24; execution miss on enterprise drove revenue and margin variance that fed into FCF underperformance [#J]

  • Metric — best accuracy: CapEx guidance (≤3% deviation L4P) remains the most accurate; the capex-as-ceiling framing makes this structurally easier to "hit" [#A]

  • Metric — worst accuracy: FCF guidance, particularly during FY22–FY24, where the gap between EBITDA guidance reliability and FCF guidance delivery was uniquely wide [#J]

  • Sceptical read: FY25 FCF near-hit may be a cyclical benefit rather than structural improvement — the pension discount rate movements that inflated FY22/FY23 deficit repair payments have partly reversed; if interest rates decline materially, pension deficit repair payments re-escalate and the FY25 apparent FCF credibility improvement evaporates [#J]

§6 — Key Assumptions Behind Guidance

The guidance stack rests on four simultaneously-required positive outcomes; historical evidence supports each individually but not collectively.

  • Net assumption credibility: Plausible individually, fragile in combination — BT has never delivered EBITDA growth, capex taper, FCF ramp, and working capital improvement in the same fiscal year; the CMD targets require all four simultaneously [#J]

  • Revenue assumption — customer: FTTP penetration guidance assumes connections grow from ~4.3m (FY25) to ~7m+ by FY27, requiring Openreach to sustain >1.5m net connections per year — achievable given infrastructure lead, but dependent on consumer switching rates and ISP partner economics [#A]

  • Revenue assumption — pricing: Consumer ARPU growth guided at ~low single digits annually, anchored on BT/EE brand premium and FTTP speed upsell; this assumption is vulnerable to competitor re-pricing in a market where Sky and Virgin also hold premium brand positions [#J]

  • Revenue assumption — market: Enterprise/BT Business revenue guided to stabilise by FY27 after legacy product wind-down; this requires the SME/corporate migration to cloud-based products to accelerate — a historically difficult execution challenge for BT Business [#J]

  • Margin assumption — cost: The £3bn+ annualised cost savings target by FY29 assumes successful AI/digital integration, headcount reduction, and property rationalisation — ambitious by UK telecom peer standards where similar programmes have delivered 60–70% of targeted savings [#A]

  • Margin assumption — leverage: Operating leverage on Openreach's fixed-cost infrastructure assumes FTTP penetration sustains above ~35%; below that threshold, incremental FTTP revenue does not cover incremental network maintenance and upgrade costs [#A]

  • EPS assumption — tax: Adjusted EPS guidance implicitly assumes an effective tax rate of ~20–22%; changes to UK corporation tax (now 25%) degrade EPS faster than EBITDA guidance would suggest, and the "adjusted" frame often strips tax effects inconsistently [#A]

  • EPS assumption — capital structure: No material share buyback assumed in guidance; pension liabilities crowd out capital returns, keeping share count roughly stable and limiting EPS leverage from buybacks that would otherwise flatter EPS trajectory in a peer context [#A]

  • Macro assumption — pension: The single most fragile macro assumption is the pension deficit trajectory — BT's IAS 19 deficit is currently ~£4–6bn gross; each 25bps move in discount rates changes deficit by ~£600–800m, and higher deficit directly increases repair payment obligations [#J]

  • Macro assumption — interest rates: BT carries ~£18bn gross debt; a sustained higher-for-longer interest rate environment increases refinancing costs and reduces FCF, compounding pension deficit uncertainty in the same direction [#A]

  • Sensitivity — 1pp revenue: A 1% shortfall in group revenue growth (all else equal) reduces EBITDA by ~£100–120m given BT's ~50% EBITDA-to-revenue ratio, and reduces normalised FCF by a similar amount — sufficient to widen the already-discounted FCF consensus gap [#J]

§7 — Credibility Assessment

  • Net credibility: MEDIUM — conditional on FCF delivery; the operational/EBITDA story carries HIGH credibility, the FCF and EPS stories carry LOW-to-MEDIUM credibility; the investment thesis hinges on the weaker half [#J]

  • Credibility — verbal verdict: MEDIUM overall — management has earned operational trust through consistent EBITDA delivery but has not yet demonstrated the ability to convert operational performance into sustained free cash generation at the guided level [#J]

  • Green flag — recent beat: EBITDA beat in 4/4 L4 periods, with no deterioration in beat frequency as restructuring enters maturation — the cost discipline is credible and the cost program is a genuine structural achievement [#A]

  • Green flag — conservative assumption: CapEx guidance as a ceiling (not midpoint) is a conservative framing that systematically reduces the probability of capex-driven FCF miss [#A]

  • Green flag — execution track record: Openreach FTTP build has met or slightly exceeded quarterly connection targets in FY24/FY25 — the physical infrastructure execution is tracking, which is necessary (but not sufficient) for the revenue and EBITDA targets [#A]

  • Green flag — leadership: CEO Kirkby's operational telecoms background (ex-Proximus) and tighter FY25 guidance language represent incremental credibility improvement versus prior leadership's looser medium-term framing [#J]

  • Red flag — recent miss: FCF guidance missed 3 of 4 L4 periods, with the cumulative miss exceeding £500m against initial guidance midpoints — this is the dominant credibility failure [#J]

  • Red flag — aggressive assumption: The FY27 FCF target of ≥£1.5bn requires approximately £450m improvement from FY25 actual in approximately 2 years — a 43% FCF growth rate that is high by European integrated telco standards [#A]

  • Red flag — pension risk: Recurring pension charges classified as "specific items" are a structural red flag — the pension deficit is a real liability consuming real cash; management's guidance "adjusts away" a recurring £150–250m annual cash cost [#J]

  • Red flag — macro risk: Interest rate and inflation sensitivity remain elevated relative to what guidance assumes; BT's capital structure leaves limited room to absorb adverse rate moves without FCF guidance missing again [#J]

  • Margin of safety — low: At current guidance, a 10% shortfall in FCF (approximately £150m) would put FY27 FCF materially below the CMD target and below SS consensus; given the 75% historical FCF miss rate, this scenario cannot be treated as a tail risk [#J]

  • Margin of safety — EBITDA: A 10% EBITDA shortfall (approximately £820m) is implausible given the L4P track record; the EBITDA guidance carries genuine margin of safety [#A]

  • Thesis sensitivity: The investment thesis is highly sensitive to FCF delivery in FY26 and FY27 — if FCF misses again, the re-rating thesis (FCF yield expansion, dividend sustainability, balance sheet repair) is materially impaired regardless of EBITDA performance [#J]

  • Sceptical read: Credibility could prove brittle if FY26 FCF prints below £1.0bn — at that level, FY27 ≥£1.5bn becomes mathematically implausible without financial engineering; a single FCF miss in FY26 could trigger a cascade of guidance downgrades and re-rating collapse [#J]

§9 — Forward Tracking Metrics + Sceptical Synthesis

The forward monitoring framework concentrates on FCF delivery building blocks — the metrics that will determine whether the CMD targets are achievable or need downward revision.

  • Net cross-cutting bear synthesis: The guidance thesis is hostage to FCF delivery, which requires four simultaneous positive outcomes that have never co-occurred in BT's L4P track record — the monitoring framework must focus on early detection of any one of those four failing [#J]
