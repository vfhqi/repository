§1 — Industry Overview + Value Chain Economics

Romande Energie operates within the Swiss electricity sector — a tightly regulated, geographically segmented industry shaped by cantonal ownership, federal energy law, and a distinctive hydro + nuclear generation mix.

  • Industry size: Swiss electricity market total revenues approximately CHF 10-12bn pa (ElCom 2023 data); Romande Energie's share approximately CHF 700-800m, implying roughly 7% of total Swiss sector revenues — a mid-scale cantonal player, smaller than Axpo (~CHF 7bn revenues) and BKW (~CHF 3.5bn) [#A]
  • Growth trajectory: Swiss electricity consumption broadly flat 2010-2022 at ~55-58 TWh pa; structural inflection expected 2024-2035 from EV adoption and heat-pump penetration — Swiss federal modelling (SFOE 2023) projects 10-15% demand growth to 2035 [#I]
  • Sub: Electrification CAGR estimated 1.0-1.8% pa for Swiss distribution; below EU peer average (~2.0-2.5% pa) given already high efficiency baseline
  • Sub: Network revenue growth structurally linked to regulated asset base growth — capex drives revenue, not volume alone
  • Structural driver: Energiestrategie 2050 (ES2050) legally mandates nuclear phase-down + renewable ramp; revised 2023 Energy Act targets 45 TWh domestic renewable generation by 2050 vs ~36 TWh today [#I]
  • Sub: Grid upgrade investment required for distributed generation integration; ElCom estimates CHF 20-25bn national grid investment needed by 2040
  • Sub: Mandated green tariff structures create regulated revenue streams for renewable operators
  • Geographic split: Romande Energie serves primarily Vaud canton (population ~900k); subsidiary Romande Energie Commerce active broader French-speaking Switzerland; limited beyond-Switzerland exposure [#I]
  • Fragmentation: Swiss distribution is structurally fragmented at the municipal level (over 600 local distribution operators historically), but consolidating upward toward ~80 significant operators post-2014 grid code reforms; Romande Energie is among the top 5 distribution utilities [#A]
  • Consolidation: Cantonal utilities are consolidating slowly; mergers rare due to political sensitivity of cantonal ownership; Romande Energie acquired several municipal networks in Vaud but pace is modest vs European DSO peers (e.g. Iberdrola's >5% annual DSO footprint growth) [#A]
  • Value chain stage: Romande Energie operates across three value chain stages: generation (hydro/nuclear stakes), transmission-connected distribution (HV/MV), and retail supply; full vertical integration within its cantonal footprint [#I]

§3 — Barriers to Entry, Exit, and Disruption Forces

  • J-front verdict: Barriers to distribution entry are among the highest of any industry — regulatory, capital, and geographic; disruption risk is real but slow-moving, concentrated in generation economics and retail liberalisation [#J]
  • Capital requirement: Replacing the Vaud distribution network would require estimated CHF 2-3bn+ capital investment (based on ~10,000 km network × CHF 200-300k/km replacement cost benchmark); prohibitive for any new entrant [#A]
  • Regulatory barrier: Distribution network operation requires ElCom concession + cantonal authorisation; no new cantonal distribution concessions have been granted in modern Swiss history; regulatory barrier is effectively absolute for core distribution [#I]
  • Scale economy: Network density economics strongly favour the incumbent — Romande Energie's cost per customer kilometre improves with density; no new entrant could replicate at sub-scale [#A]
  • Switching cost: Residential customers cannot switch distribution operator (physical network is shared); commercial customers above 100 MWh can switch retail supplier but remain on Romande Energie's physical grid (paying regulated tariffs regardless) [#I]
  • Brand: Brand differentiation is modest in Swiss regulated utilities — not a primary barrier; reliability and service quality matter more in B2B energy services [#A]
  • Structural exit: No meaningful exit barriers for retail supply competitors — they can exit quickly; this limits the depth of retail margins as competitors cycle in/out chasing arbitrage [#A]
  • Lock-in mechanism: Regulated network access agreements create 20-30 year concession lock-in for DSOs; hydro concession renewals (Romande Energie's key hydro assets) extend to 2040-2060+ under Swiss water law [#I]
  • Disruption force — generation: Solar PV cost deflation is the primary disruption force — residential rooftop solar + battery (e.g. SunStore, which Romande Energie has invested in) allows partial customer self-supply; this erodes distribution throughput volume without reducing fixed network costs [#A]
  • Sub: Swiss residential solar penetration ~10% in 2023; forecast 20-25% by 2030 ; potential 3-5% distribution revenue erosion
  • Sub: Distributed generation also requires grid investment (smart meters, grid flexibility), partially offsetting volume erosion with capex-driven RAB growth
  • Disruption force — retail: Full retail liberalisation below 100 MWh would allow pan-European suppliers (Axpo, Alpiq, international aggregators) to compete for Romande Energie residential accounts; currently blocked politically but structural pressure persisting [#A]
  • Disruption force — technology: Smart grid + AI-driven demand response platforms (startups like Tiko, owned by Romande Energie competitor EKZ) could disintermediate the energy services layer; Romande Energie's energy services subsidiary faces this competition [#A]
  • Adjacency entry: BKW (energy services), ENGIE, Schneider Electric, and Bouygues Energies are actively entering Swiss energy services from adjacent positions; Romande Energie Solutions is a direct competitor to BKW Building Solutions at smaller scale [#A]
  • Vanilla entrant: Zero plausible vanilla entrants in distribution; low-probability vanilla entry exists in retail supply (energy traders, pan-European retailers) [#A]
  • Innovative entrant: Aggregators and virtual power plant operators (Next Kraftwerke, Flexitricity model) could disintermediate Romande Energie's flexibility/balancing services role; P2P energy trading platforms (LO3 Energy model) represent a technology disruption threat at the margin [#A]
  • Substitute: No substitute for grid electricity delivery in the medium term; long-term, green hydrogen + on-site fuel cells represent a partial substitute for heat but not power; no credible near-term substitute threat to distribution [#A]
  • Disintermediation: Customer self-generation + battery storage is the primary disintermediation threat — bypasses retail supply layer; Romande Energie partially hedges this by owning SunStore (solar PV installer) [#I]

Sceptical bullet: The deepest bear case on barriers is that solar + battery cost deflation accelerates beyond the SFOE base case, driving residential self-supply penetration to 30%+ by 2030 (vs 25% forecast); the resulting volume loss reduces distribution throughput but regulatory cost recovery mechanisms are imperfect — ElCom's tariff reviews lag volumes by 3 years, creating a structural earnings gap; Romande Energie's relatively smaller scale vs BKW means it has less buffer to absorb this via energy services cross-subsidisation
