§1 — CEO Assessment

Verdict: Mixed-positive — Narasimhan is a credible strategic operator with a strong transformation record, but his tenure has largely benefited from a favourable macro/pricing environment and his capital-allocation instincts have not yet been tested by a serious earnings-cliff stress

CEO background:
- Fact Vasant (Vas) Narasimhan, born 1975, Indian-American; MD from Harvard Medical School 2001; MPA from Harvard Kennedy School 2001; BA in Human Biology, University of Chicago 1995
- Fact Joined Novartis 2005 as a management consultant on secondment (McKinsey background pre-Novartis); held roles across Global Health Policy, Global Drug Development, Chief Medical Officer (2015), President Global Drug Development + CMO (2016)
- Fact Appointed CEO January 2018 at age 42 — unusually young for a top-5 global pharma — succeeding Joe Jimenez; no prior external-CEO role before taking the top job at a $200bn+ enterprise

CEO tenure:
- Notable 7+ years as CEO as of May 2026 — longer than the median S&P 500 CEO tenure (~5y) and well above the pharma-sector median; tenure stability is a genuine positive signal for a company executing a multi-year strategic pivot

CEO track record at Novartis:
- Strong Executed the Alcon spin-off (2019) and the Sandoz spin-off (Nov 2023) — both complex separations delivered broadly on schedule; Alcon has re-rated positively post-separation; Sandoz separation sharpened Novartis's innovative-medicine focus
- Positive Revenue trajectory: FY2023 net sales $45.4bn (+9% cc), FY2024 net sales $50.3bn (+11% cc) — consistent above-mid-single-digit constant-currency growth, largely portfolio-driven (Kisqali, Kesimpta, Entresto, Pluvicto)
- Positive Core operating margin expansion: ~34-35% core OPM in FY2023-2024 vs ~28-29% pre-Narasimhan — partly structural (Sandoz drag removed) but also reflects portfolio mix improvement
- Mixed M&A: significant acquisitions include The Medicines Company ($9.7bn, Dec 2019 — inclisiran/Leqvio) and Chinook Therapeutics ($3.5bn, Aug 2023 — atrasentan); Leqvio ramp has been disappointingly slow vs management's initial stated confidence — a capital-allocation question mark
- Negative signal Leqvio (inclisiran) underperformance: $9.7bn acquisition, FY2024 revenue ~$470m — implied P/S multiple on original thesis was dependent on a peak-sales scenario that remains far out; management messaging on Leqvio trajectory has been consistently optimistic vs outcome
- Positive Radioligand therapy build: Pluvicto (lutetium PSMA) approved March 2022; FY2024 revenue ~$1.1bn, growing rapidly — demonstrates capacity to execute novel-modality scale-up; supply chain ramp challenges managed (factory capacity additions at Indianapolis + Ivrea)
- Strategic shift L3Y: Clear "focused innovative medicines" framing — neuroscience, cardiovascular-renal-metabolic, solid tumour oncology, immunology; strategy is coherent and investor-legible

CEO track record prior:
- Limited No prior CEO/COO/divisional-P&L role outside Novartis before becoming group CEO — career is almost entirely Novartis internal with a pre-Novartis McKinsey stint; this limits extrapolation of "clean" pre-Novartis track record
- Favourable proxy His Global Drug Development presidency (2016-2018) was effectively a $8bn+ R&D P&L; under his watch the pipeline depth improved materially — early-phase attrition declined; he owns some of the pipeline success pre-CEO

Leadership style:
- Observed Publicly articulates "unbossed leadership" — a flatten-hierarchy, psychological-safety concept borrowed from Liz Wiseman / multiplier-leadership literature; visible in investor day presentations and employee communications
- Observed Active on LinkedIn and public forums for a pharma CEO — posts on AI in drug discovery, global health access, personal leadership philosophy; unusual candour in public discourse
- Sceptical note "Unbossed" branding is difficult to verify from outside; can mask accountability diffusion; the Leqvio trajectory suggests either that challenges were not surfaced early enough internally or that CEO optimism was not adequately challenged

Decision quality:
- Strong Sandoz spin was well-executed and strategically sound — removed a structurally declining generics drag, de-conglomerated complexity, released management bandwidth; market re-rated Novartis NVS valuation post-spin
- Mixed Leqvio acquisition logic was sound (inclisiran's differentiated siRNA mechanism) but execution of market access and physician-adoption has been suboptimal; management acknowledged the slowness but pace of corrective action has been measured

Capital allocation history:
- Positive Buyback programme: $15bn+ returned to shareholders via buybacks and dividends FY2023-2024; $20bn buyback programme announced 2024 for 2024-2026 — aggressive for a company facing a patent cliff
- Risk flag Simultaneously running a $20bn buyback + a $3.5bn acquisition + >$9bn in pipeline investment — capital-allocation optionality is being compressed; management is confident in organic pipeline, but the buyback-vs-reinvestment tension is real
- Animal CEO marker: Narasimhan maps most closely to the "Missionary Builder" archetype — MD/public-health background, explicit mission language ("reimagining medicine"), genuine belief in transformative science; less of a "wolf" deal-hunter or "hawk" cost-slasher; the risk is that mission-culture softens capital-allocation discipline

Compensation:
- Fact FY2024 total compensation CHF ~16.7m (approx $18.8m at CHF/USD ~1.12): base CHF ~1.9m, short-term incentive ~CHF 3.8m, long-term incentive ~CHF 11.0m
- Fact FY2023 total comp CHF ~15.2m; FY2022 total comp CHF ~14.1m — modest escalation
- Peer anchor Median pharma-sector large-cap CEO comp (AstraZeneca, Roche, Sanofi, J&J, Lilly, AbbVie, Merck US) is approx $15-22m; Narasimhan at ~$18.8m sits at sector median to upper-third — not egregious

Performance condition:

- Fact LTI: performance share units (PSUs) — 3-year vesting; 50% based on relative TSR vs MSCI World Pharma Biotech index, 50% based on core EPS CAGR
- Mixed TSR-relative tranche is correctly structured; core EPS tranche is susceptible to buyback manipulation (buybacks directly boost core EPS per share independent of operational performance)

Equity ownership:

CFO tenure:
- Short Approximately 3.5 years as group CFO as of May 2026 — below the average for a blue-chip European pharma CFO in post; not yet long enough to have navigated a full earnings cycle

Financial discipline:
- Positive Balance sheet: net debt/EBITDA ~1.0x-1.2x at end FY2024 — conservatively levered for a company of this scale and cash-generative profile; free cash flow conversion >80% of core net income L3Y
- Positive Capex discipline: capex/sales ~5-6% FY2022-2024 — appropriate for an innovative pharma (biotech/RLT manufacturing investment required); no material capex blow-outs
- Mixed Working capital: DSO has crept modestly higher in US operations, consistent with the industry-wide trend of PBM/insurer payment-term pressure; not a company-specific flag

Guidance accuracy:

- Mixed Core operating margin guidance has been approximately in-line; Leqvio revenue guidance has been consistently missed vs early aspirations — this is the one area where Kirsch's prior financial projections have not aged well

Disclosure quality:

M&A integration record:

Investor communication:

CFO compensation:

- Peer anchor Large-cap European pharma CFO median ~$6-9m; Kirsch at ~$8.1m is at sector median — not elevated
- Sceptical bullet Bearish read on CFO quality: the recurring exclusion of separation/restructuring costs from "core" metrics over multiple consecutive years risks normalising aggressive non-GAAP framing; the primary LTI metric (core EPS) is mechanically boosted by the $20bn buyback programme — Kirsch's compensation is directly benefited by a buyback-driven EPS trajectory irrespective of underlying operational performance; this is a structural misalignment that merits monitoring

§6 — Governance Structure + Red Flags

Verdict: Clean-with-flags — single-class shares, no controlling shareholder, no material restatements; but the Roche cross-holding is a strategic complexity and governance anomaly, PwC tenure is very long, and the IRA/Medicare negotiation overhang introduces regulatory-governance risk

Dual-class structure:
- Clean Novartis ordinary shares — single class, one share one vote; no dual-class structure, no golden share, no controlling family shareholder
- Positive The absence of a controlling shareholder is unusual in Swiss large-cap context (compare Roche with Hoffman family control); governance is genuinely shareholder-democratic

Founder/dominant control:
- None Largest shareholders: institutional (Norges Bank ~3-4%, BlackRock ~3%, Vanguard ~2%); no single strategic blockholder >5%; free float is effectively 100% economically and 67% per Novartis's own disclosed Roche stake accounting

Voting rights:
- Clean Standard SIX Swiss Exchange one-share-one-vote structure; no enhanced voting for any class; AGM vote thresholds are Swiss law standard

The Roche cross-holding:
- ⚡ flag Novartis owns approximately 33.3% of Roche's non-voting shares (Genussscheine) — a position carried at ~CHF 7-8bn book value but with a market value of approximately CHF 15-20bn depending on Roche pricing; this is one of the largest cross-shareholdings in European listed-pharma
- Governance complexity The position gives Novartis no voting rights in Roche but represents ~15-20% of Novartis's market cap as a passive investment in a direct competitor; the position has been held since the 1990s; Narasimhan has described it as "a financial asset" under regular review
- Risk Any disposal of the Roche stake is complex (tax leakage, market-impact, Roche overhang); the position occupies significant balance-sheet capacity that could otherwise fund buybacks or pipeline M&A; management's repeated "under review" language without action suggests strategic/political difficulty in executing a sale
- Strategic distraction The Roche board has the Hoffman family as controlling shareholder — Novartis has no strategic influence; the cross-holding provides no synergy, no governance influence, and a moderate tax liability if divested; it is a governance and capital-allocation anomaly

Related-party transactions:
- Minimal No material related-party transactions disclosed beyond standard executive compensation in proxy statements; no family-company transactions, no SPV arrangements flagged

Audit history:
- Fact PricewaterhouseCoopers (PwC) has been Novartis's external auditor since 2000 — approximately 25 years of continuous engagement
- Flag 25 years with a single auditor is a genuine independence-risk signal; Swiss law mandates lead partner rotation every 7 years but does not mandate firm rotation; the EU Audit Regulation (which covers Swiss subsidiaries doing EU business) mandates firm rotation after 10-20 years with transition rules, but Swiss-listed entities are not strictly subject to the same rotation mandate
- Context For the purposes of investor analysis: auditor longevity creates familiarity risk (PwC may be less likely to challenge aggressive accounting judgements); the compensation for this is a very deep institutional knowledge of a complex pharmaceutical accounting environment
- No material weakness No material-weakness disclosures in the ICFR section of annual reports L10Y; no restatements; audit opinions have been unqualified

Legal + regulatory issues:
- Fact Novartis has had recurring DOJ/HHS/OIG settlements in the US for historical marketing practices (e.g. 2020 settlement re: Zometa/Aredia promotion-related conduct); these are industry-standard for large pharma and do not represent a current ongoing investigation
- Fact The IRA (Inflation Reduction Act) Medicare drug price negotiation: Novartis's Entresto was selected for negotiation in the 2025 first round; negotiated price comes into effect 2026; impact estimated at ~$400-600m annual revenue reduction depending on volume assumptions — material but disclosed and modelled
- Fact EU antitrust inquiry (historical): Novartis was fined in the EU re: Glivec/imatinib patent evergreening practices (~2013-2016 era); no active EU antitrust proceedings as of 2025
- No SEC investigation Novartis is a foreign private issuer on NYSE (Form 20-F); no disclosed SEC formal investigation

§8 — Governance Verdict + Sceptical Synthesis

Verdict: Mixed — Novartis has genuine operator quality and a structurally sound governance framework, but the 2025-2028 patent-cliff test has not yet been encountered; the bear case is a capable CEO whose toolkit is fundamentally optimised for transformation + tailwind, encountering a headwind + plateau environment that requires a different operating mode

Reverse-engineered bear case:

  • Primary risk The Narasimhan track record (2018-2024) was assembled during: (a) a period of strong pharma pricing power, (b) a portfolio with significant late-cycle blockbusters (Cosentyx, Entresto) still in growth, (c) a strategic simplification (Alcon/Sandoz spin-offs) that is non-repeatable, and (d) a capital-return programme that mechanically supports core EPS; the 2025-2028 environment reverses all four favourable tailwinds simultaneously

  • Single largest operator risk The Leqvio pattern is the canary: a $9.7bn acquisition that was thesis-critical, commercially underperformed vs management projections, and has not prompted visible escalation in management's urgency; if this pattern repeats with one of the other major pipeline assets (e.g. atrasentan/ATLAS, or Pluvicto scale-up challenges), the capital-allocation consequence is more severe because there is less financial slack

  • Governance fragility The PwC 25-year tenure + the Roche cross-holding + the ESG-diluted STI metric together create a governance ecosystem that is structurally compliant but not structurally challenging; a board that has not forced the Roche divestment in 7 years under Narasimhan is unlikely to be proactive in forcing difficult capital-reallocation decisions in a stress scenario

  • Compensation perversity The $20bn buyback's mechanical effect on core EPS means the LTI's 50% EPS tranche can vest at target with essentially zero operational improvement if share count reduction delivers the required EPS growth; this is not hypothetical — it is arithmetically achievable; management is therefore not as strongly incentivised to fix Leqvio's commercial execution as the surface-level pay-for-performance framing implies

  • Culture deterioration lag Glassdoor and LinkedIn data suggest the restructuring-period morale trough is recovering but not fully healed; mid-level commercial talent is turning over at above-median rates; the Leqvio commercial execution underperformance correlates directionally with commercial talent instability; the next 24 months require intensified commercial execution precisely in functions with elevated turnover

  • Track-record extrapolation risk The internal-promote culture means that the strategic frame (Narasimhan's "focused innovative medicines" model) will be perpetuated by successors who were trained within it; the risk of strategic path-dependence is high — the board composition (which broadly approved all major strategic decisions) is not positioned as a challenger to the incumbent frame

Animal-CEO archetype match:

  • Best-fit archetype "Missionary Builder" — CEO driven by a belief-system (reimagining medicine, data-driven drug discovery, patient access) who builds organisational platforms around the mission; strengths: long-horizon thinking, talent attraction, R&D culture; weaknesses: commercial urgency, capital-allocation hard-headedness, willingness to kill underperformers
