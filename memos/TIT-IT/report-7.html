§2 — Key Driver #1: NetCo Disposal and Gross Debt Reduction

J-front verdict: NetCo disposal to the KKR-led consortium is the single most impactful event in the 24M window; equity transformation hinges on it, but execution risk is elevated [#J]

  • Driver definition: TIT-IT's agreement to sell its fixed-network infrastructure (NetCo) to a consortium led by KKR (with Italian state entity Mef/CDP) targets removal of the bulk of consolidated debt, separating a capital-intensive infrastructure co from a leaner ServiceCo [#J]
  • Mechanism to EPS: gross debt reduction lowers finance charges materially; ServiceCo becomes a capital-lighter entity; EPS trajectory improves via interest cost reduction rather than EBIT expansion initially [#I]

  • Mechanism to SP: deleverage removes bankruptcy/distress premium embedded in equity price; multiple re-rates toward sector-median as leverage ratio normalises; sum-of-parts visibility improves [#I]

  • Transaction terms: Enterprise value for NetCo reportedly agreed at approximately €22bn; TIT-IT receives net consideration that reduces consolidated gross debt from ~€25-26bn toward ~€8-10bn pro-forma range [#J]

  • Post-close net debt/EBITDA ServiceCo target is guided toward approximately 2-2.5×, versus current consolidated ~5-6×; peers Orange, Deutsche Telekom, Vodafone Italy typically operate at 2-3× [#A]

  • Cash consideration to TIT-IT shareholders via dividend or buyback: management has not given explicit commitment; this creates post-close capital allocation ambiguity [#J]

  • Company guide: Management has guided for deal completion during 2024; as of available public data, regulatory and governmental approvals were the remaining gating items [#J]

  • Management commentary post-CMD framing: separation of NetCo as a "national broadband champion" with Italian state involvement was designed to provide sovereign cover for the disposal [#I]

  • Track record on this type of commitment: TIT-IT management has a mixed track record on delivery timelines; prior strategic pivots (e.g., prior network separation discussions) experienced multi-year delays [#A]

  • SS consensus: Sell-side broadly views NetCo close as the key re-rating catalyst; most published research post-deal-announcement priced a deal-close scenario as the base case [#I]

  • Named public sources: Mediobanca, Deutsche Bank, Bernstein, UBS have all cited NetCo as fulcrum driver in public coverage; Citi has flagged execution risk more prominently than peers [#I]

  • Consensus EPS impact of deal close: not uniformly quantified in public sources; indicative: €500-800m annual interest cost reduction post-close implies €0.04-0.06 EPS uplift on ~13bn shares outstanding [#I]

  • Recent delivery: Deal announced late 2023; KKR consortium structure confirmed; Italian government involvement via Mef/CDP as minority in NetCo confirmed; as at mid-2024, deal still awaiting formal completion [#J]

  • L12M reporting: no material renegotiation of terms disclosed publicly; ComCo (competition authority) review ongoing [#A]

  • Early warning signal: any widening of TIT-IT bonds vs peer telecom spreads would signal market scepticism re completion [#I]

  • Macro context: Italian sovereign engagement makes this a quasi-political transaction; Italian government's desire for fixed-network strategic control means completion probability is supported but deal terms are government-influenced [#J]

  • Interest rate environment: higher-for-longer rates increase the urgency of debt reduction but also increase KKR's financing costs on the acquisition, creating potential for term renegotiation [#A]

  • Structural tailwind: European digital infrastructure trend (tower separation, fibre separation) supports transaction logic; peers Cellnex, Vantage Towers signal healthy infrastructure asset appetite [#I]

  • Plausibility assessment: Medium plausibility — deal has clear political support and public announcement, but complexity of Italian government involvement, AGCOM review, and interest rate sensitivity introduce genuine uncertainty on timing and final terms [#J]

  • Key assumptions: KKR financing costs remain manageable; ComCo/AGCOM clear deal without material concessions that impair NetCo valuation; Italian government does not impose employment conditions that inflate NetCo cost base post-close [#A]

  • Magnitude if delivered: equity-transformative; ServiceCo equity should re-rate by 30-60% vs pre-close price on leverage normalisation alone, absent any EBITDA improvement [#I]

  • Bear case: NetCo deal collapses or reprices at €18-19bn (vs €22bn agreed) due to higher financing costs or regulatory conditions; TIT-IT remains at ~5.5× net debt/EBITDA with no near-term catalyst; equity rerates to distressed-discount [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Italian government insists on employment protection obligations that reduce NetCo profitability; KKR walks or requests price reduction; deal limbo creates 12-18M of equity uncertainty [#A]
  • ❌ Miss magnitude: if deal fails, ServiceCo equity fair value could be 50%+ below a post-close scenario; leverage premium widens, coverage ratio worsens, re-rating becomes multi-year event [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — deal has political backing and public structure but Italian complexity and interest rate sensitivity create meaningful completion risk on both timing and terms

§3 — Key Driver #2: ServiceCo Domestic Revenue Stabilisation

J-front verdict: Domestic fixed and mobile revenue in Italy has been in structural decline; stabilisation is necessary but not yet demonstrated; this driver is the most uncertain near-term deliverable [#J]

  • Driver definition: TIT-IT's Italian ServiceCo (post-NetCo separation) will be a fixed + mobile retail/wholesale service business leasing the network from NetCo; its revenue trend determines EBITDA trajectory and thus ServiceCo equity value [#J]
  • Mechanism to EPS: every 1% revenue stabilisation vs decline translates to ~€50-100m EBITDA improvement at typical telco incremental margins (30-40%); on ~13bn shares this is approximately €0.01-0.02 EPS [#I]

  • Mechanism to SP: revenue stabilisation signals that competitive erosion is arrested; triggers multiple expansion from distressed to normalised; removes bear-case earnings erosion discount [#I]

  • Company guide: Management has guided for domestic revenue stabilisation as a 2025+ target; specific growth resumption guidance has been absent in public filings; EBITDA guidance has been progressively reset lower over 3 years [#J]

  • Embedded assumptions: market repair following irrational MVNO/cable competition in fixed; mobile ARPU stabilisation post-price competition cycle; enterprise segment growth offsetting consumer headwinds [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: management guidance has historically proved optimistic on revenue; stabilisation targets have been pushed out by 12-18 months in each of last 3 planning cycles [#J]

  • SS consensus: Sell-side broadly models revenue stabilisation in 2025-2026; recovery to growth not widely modelled before 2026-2027; most models carry explicit risk that competitive dynamics delay stabilisation [#I]

  • Divergence: Mediobanca and Deutsche Bank more constructive on timeline vs Citi and Barclays, who flag that Italian fixed broadband remains structurally over-competitive [#I]

  • Consensus revenue impact: domestic revenue c.€9-10bn (ServiceCo ex-NetCo basis); even 1% stabilisation vs -3% trajectory is approximately €300-400m revenue improvement [#I]

  • Recent delivery: Italian mobile ARPU has shown modest sequential improvement in recent quarters following exit of some sub-scale MVNOs; fixed revenue decline has persisted at approximately -4 to -6% per annum range [#J]

  • L3Y baseline: TIT-IT domestic revenue has declined from approximately €12bn (2020) toward €10bn (2024 estimated); cumulative c.-15% over 4 years [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: Orange Italy, Vodafone Italy (pre-merger), WindTre all faced same revenue headwinds; post-Vodafone-WindTre consolidation creates a more rational competitive market which is structurally supportive [#J]

  • Macro context: Italian consumer spending under pressure from inflation; enterprise IT/cloud migration partially a tailwind for managed services [#A]

  • Structural driver: Vodafone Italy-WindTre merger (cleared 2024) reduces number of national MNOs from 4 to 3; oligopoly structure historically associated with ARPU recovery across European markets (Spain, Netherlands precedents) [#J]

  • Competitive dynamics: Fastweb/Swisscom in fixed broadband remains a meaningful competitor; ILIAD continues disciplined pricing pressure in mobile [#A]

  • Plausibility assessment: Medium plausibility — market structure improvement from Vodafone-WindTre merger is a genuine tailwind, but company-specific execution on ARPU management and churn reduction has historically underdelivered [#J]

  • Key assumptions: Vodafone-WindTre merger creates rational pricing; TIT-IT retains enterprise segment; no disruptive new entrant in fixed [#A]

  • Magnitude if delivered: stabilisation removes the largest ongoing source of estimate downgrades; enables ServiceCo EBITDA to build from a stable base rather than a declining one [#I]

  • Bear case: Italian fixed broadband revenue continues to decline at -4 to -6% per annum; mobile ARPU compressed by ILIAD; ServiceCo EBITDA trajectory pushes below €4bn level, consensus downgrades follow, multiple compresses [#J]

  • Miss scenario: ILIAD accelerates fixed network build using NetCo wholesale access at regulated prices; new competition from cable operators in Milan/Rome; enterprise contracts lost to cloud-native competitors [#A]
  • Miss magnitude: continued -4% revenue decline implies €400-600m EBITDA erosion over 3 years vs current levels; ServiceCo valuation basis weakens significantly [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — structural market repair from consolidation is supportive, but TIT-IT's historical inability to stabilise revenue on its own timelines limits confidence

§4 — Key Driver #3: EBITDA Margin Recovery via Cost Transformation

J-front verdict: Cost transformation (network rationalisation, IT modernisation, commercial efficiency) is a genuine medium-term lever but delivery pace is slower than management signalled, and savings are partially absorbed by topline decline [#J]

  • Driver definition: TIT-IT's cost base is structurally heavy relative to post-separation revenue; margin recovery requires OpEx reduction via network simplification (copper-to-fibre migration), IT stack consolidation, and commercial cost efficiency [#J]
  • Mechanism to EPS: EBITDA margin expansion of 1ppt on ~€13bn consolidated revenue base = ~€130m EBITDA; at ~5× EV/EBITDA multiple this is ~€650m EV uplift; equity amplification is high given leverage [#I]

  • Mechanism to SP: improved FCF conversion enables debt service, potentially accelerates deleveraging; signals management execution capability to market [#I]

  • Company guide: TIT-IT has guided for multi-year cost reduction programme; specific target of saving €1bn+ in OpEx over the plan horizon (2024-2026 CMD); specific details on phasing not uniformly disclosed in public sources [#J]

  • Track record on cost commitments: TIT-IT has historically achieved partial delivery on cost targets but gross savings have been offset by declining revenue and cost-to-serve increases in fibre rollout period [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: cost targets tend to be moderately achievable in absolute terms but revenue decline erodes incremental EBITDA benefit [#J]

  • SS consensus: Sell-side models EBITDA AL (after leases) improvement toward €7-8bn consolidated (pre-NetCo separation basis) by 2026; post-separation ServiceCo EBITDA trajectory less uniformly modelled [#I]

  • Named brokers: Bernstein has published detailed cost-bridge analysis; UBS has flagged that fibre rollout completion will be a structural cost tailwind post-2025 as copper maintenance costs ramp down [#I]

  • EPS impact: €500m cumulative cost saving over plan horizon = approximately €0.03-0.04 EPS improvement on current share count [#I]

  • Recent delivery: TIT-IT reported EBITDA margin improvement in H2 2023 reporting; however improvement was driven partly by one-off items and cost deferrals rather than structural run-rate improvement [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: consolidated EBITDA after leases has declined from approximately €4.5bn (2020) toward €3.5-4bn (2024 estimated); cost savings have not fully offset revenue headwinds [#J]

  • Peer benchmark: European telco peers at similar restructuring stage (e.g., BT Group, KPN) have demonstrated 12-24 months of margin compression before recovery; TIT-IT is approximately 18-24M into the equivalent phase [#I]

  • Macro context: Energy cost tailwind as Italian industrial energy prices moderated from 2022-2023 peaks; labour cost inflation partially offset by workforce reduction programme [#A]

  • Fibre rollout economics: as copper-to-fibre migration completes, copper maintenance OpEx (~€500-700m annual estimated) progressively reduces; structural tailwind for margins post-2025 [#J]

  • Competitive dynamics: cost transformation is not competitively differentiating but enables price competitiveness in B2B and retail segments [#I]

  • Plausibility assessment: Medium plausibility — copper retirement is a real structural cost tailwind; OpEx programme is credible in direction, but historical pace suggests slippage vs plan [#J]

  • Key assumptions: fibre migration proceeds on schedule; no major labour dispute delays workforce reduction; energy costs remain at current levels or decline [#A]

  • Magnitude if delivered: 2-3ppt margin improvement on ServiceCo would be €200-300m EBITDA improvement; combined with NetCo deleveraging this is meaningful equity value creation [#I]

  • Bear case: Revenue decline offsets gross cost savings; fibre migration delayed by regulatory or technical issues; workforce reduction programme is blocked by union action; margin stays flat or compresses further [#J]

  • Miss scenario: copper switch-off regulated by AGCOM at pace slower than management plan; IT modernisation cost higher than budgeted; commercial costs rise as competition intensifies [#A]
  • Miss magnitude: failure to expand margins means ServiceCo EBITDA stays at current trough levels; combined with revenue pressure, FCF generation remains insufficient to accelerate deleveraging beyond NetCo transaction alone [#I]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — structural cost tailwinds (copper retirement) are real but historical delivery pace and revenue headwinds compress net margin benefit

§5 — Key Driver #4: Brazil (TIM Brasil) Earnings Growth

J-front verdict: TIM Brasil is TIT-IT's highest-quality asset — growing, cash-generative, rational market structure — and provides genuine earnings floor while Italian restructuring proceeds [#J]

  • Driver definition: TIT-IT owns approximately 67% of TIM Brasil (listed on Bovespa: TIMS3), a Brazilian mobile operator with strong market position in a structurally sound 3-player mobile market [#J]
  • Mechanism to EPS: TIM Brasil contributes approximately 30-35% of consolidated EBITDA; growth in Brazilian operations directly lifts consolidated earnings even as Italy restructures [#I]

  • Mechanism to SP: growing, cash-generative subsidiary reduces perceived risk of total TIT-IT equity; provides dividend upstream optionality; partial re-rating possible if market ascribes higher multiple to Brazilian operations [#I]

  • Company guide: TIM Brasil management has guided for EBITDA margin expansion to approximately 48-50% range; revenue growth in mid-to-high single digits in BRL terms reflecting pricing power in oligopolistic market [#J]

  • Track record: TIM Brasil has consistently delivered on or above guidance over 3-year horizon; management credibility is high relative to Italian operations [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Brazilian guidance has historically been conservative — actual delivery has trended above plan [#J]

  • SS consensus: Named public brokers covering TIM Brasil separately (Santander Brazil, BTG Pactual, Bradesco BBI) have maintained constructive growth estimates; EPS upgrades in L6M reflecting pricing and market structure [#I]

  • TIT-IT parent coverage: SS views TIM Brasil as a value-support cushion rather than a growth driver for the parent stock; Brazilian asset value rarely the primary re-rating catalyst for TIT-IT equity specifically [#I]

  • Estimated EPS impact: TIM Brasil FY2024E EBITDA of approximately R$9-10bn; at ~€1.3-1.4bn equivalent contribution to TIT-IT consolidated; ~35% of TIT-IT group EBITDA [#I]

  • Recent delivery: TIM Brasil has delivered 6-9% EBITDA growth in recent quarters; mobile ARPU in Brazil recovering as Oi Mobile assets were absorbed by market incumbents (TIM Brasil, Claro, Vivo) [#J]

  • L3Y baseline: TIM Brasil EBITDA grown from approximately R$7bn (2020) to R$9-10bn (2024E); consistent execution [#A]

  • ⚡ RARE: Structural: TIM Brasil absorbed Oi Mobile assets in 2022 alongside Claro and Vivo, creating a permanent 3-player mobile oligopoly in Brazil; only 2-3 comparable market consolidation events in last decade across EM telcos; this structural tailwind is underweighted in parent TIT-IT coverage [Cross-ref: §5]

  • Macro context: BRL/EUR FX is a two-way risk — BRL weakness translates to lower EUR contribution; BRL has been volatile in L12M [#A]

  • Brazilian macro: GDP growth 2-3%; consumer confidence recovering; enterprise mobile data demand growing; favourable backdrop for revenue growth [#J]

  • Structural: 3-player market post-Oi dissolution structurally superior to prior 4-player market; ARPU recovery ongoing [#J]

  • Plausibility assessment: High plausibility — Brazilian mobile market structure, management track record, and recent delivery all support continued growth; primary risk is FX and macro [#J]

  • Key assumptions: BRL does not weaken dramatically vs EUR; Brazilian macro avoids severe recession; no regulatory intervention in mobile pricing [#A]

  • Magnitude if delivered: sustained 6-8% EBITDA growth at TIM Brasil contributes €80-120m incremental consolidated EBITDA per annum; modest EPS uplift but meaningful for investor confidence [#I]

  • Bear case: BRL depreciates 15-20% vs EUR; Brazilian consumer weakens; TIT-IT parent uses TIM Brasil dividend stream to service Italian debt rather than upstream capital to parent equity; market applies holding company discount [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Brazilian recession reduces mobile data demand growth; competitive pricing returns if Claro or Vivo becomes more aggressive; FX headwind masks BRL-denominated performance improvement [#A]
  • Miss magnitude: 15% BRL depreciation = approximately €200m EBITDA headwind at consolidated level; meaningful but manageable relative to Italian restructuring scale [#I]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — TIM Brasil is the best-supported driver in the set, with structural market tailwinds and strong management track record; primary risk is FX

§6 — Key Driver #5: Leverage-Driven Multiple Re-Rating

J-front verdict: TIT-IT's extreme leverage discount creates a mechanical re-rating opportunity if debt is reduced; this driver is entirely dependent on Driver #1 (NetCo close) and is not independent [#J]

  • Driver definition: TIT-IT currently trades at a depressed EV/EBITDA multiple vs European telecom peers, partially reflecting leverage risk premium; if gross debt is materially reduced, the equity should re-rate toward sector median [#J]
  • Mechanism to EPS: leverage reduction itself improves EPS via lower interest charges; but the multiple re-rating effect operates on the EV level, amplified by lower net debt at equity bridge [#I]

  • Mechanism to SP: EV/EBITDA re-rating from ~4× toward sector-median ~6× would imply approximately 50%+ equity value uplift, given current leverage amplification [#I]

  • Current multiple context: TIT-IT EV/EBITDA on pre-NetCo consolidated basis approximately 4-4.5×; European incumbent telecom median approximately 6-7× (Deutsche Telekom ~7×, Orange ~5.5×, BT Group ~5×) [#A]

  • Discount justification: current discount reflects leverage, governance, Italian macro, and execution risk premium; not entirely irrational [#J]

  • Re-rating trigger: NetCo close + leverage guidance toward 2-2.5× ServiceCo is the necessary condition for multiple expansion [#J]

  • Company guide: Management has not given explicit multiple target; implied through capital markets framework that post-NetCo ServiceCo should trade comparably to asset-light European telcos [#I]

  • Track record: TIT-IT has repeatedly described re-rating potential without delivery; the multiple has compressed, not expanded, over the last 5 years [#J]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: management view on re-rating is structurally optimistic; not supported by L5Y price history [#A]

  • SS consensus: SS broadly prices in re-rating in target prices post-NetCo-close; price target upside vs spot of 30-60% across brokers is primarily a re-rating assumption [#I]

  • Named brokers: Deutsche Bank and Bernstein most explicit about multiple expansion potential; Citi more cautious, flagging that ServiceCo standalone may face its own leverage concerns [#I]

  • EPS impact: re-rating is primarily EV/multiple effect, not EPS-driven; but interest cost reduction improves reported EPS by €0.04-0.06 as noted in Driver #1 [#I]

  • Recent delivery: Multiple has not expanded in L12M despite deal announcement; market has maintained scepticism discount pending actual completion [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: EV/EBITDA has ranged 4-5.5× over 3 years; re-rating to upper end of range on deal certainty would already represent material upside [#J]

  • Peer benchmark: Comparable deleveraging events in telcos (e.g., Altice France partial — negative precedent, BT Group post-EE — positive precedent) show re-rating is deal-completion and sustained execution dependent [#I]

  • Macro context: Higher-for-longer interest rates compress telecom multiples sector-wide; TIT-IT re-rating opportunity may be partially offset by sector multiple compression [#A]

  • ECB rate trajectory: if ECB cuts materially in 2025, sector multiple support improves; each 50bps ECB rate reduction historically worth ~0.2-0.3× EV/EBITDA expansion across European telcos [#I]

  • Infrastructure investor appetite: appetite for European telecom infrastructure remains strong (evidence: Cellnex, tower transactions); KKR's involvement in NetCo confirms institutional demand [#J]

  • Plausibility assessment: Medium plausibility — re-rating is mathematically compelling but requires NetCo close as a precondition; independent of that, there is no re-rating catalyst [#J]

  • Key assumptions: NetCo closes at agreed terms; ServiceCo rated comparably to asset-light telcos; ECB rate cuts support sector valuation [#A]

  • Magnitude if delivered: 50-70% equity upside from 4× to 6× EV/EBITDA re-rating on delevered balance sheet; this is the bull case magnitude that makes the thesis interesting to a 25%+ IRR investor [#I]

  • Bear case: NetCo closes but ServiceCo is rated as a high-risk, declining-revenue Italian incumbent with low FCF conversion; multiple stabilises at 4.5× rather than expanding; equity upside is interest-cost-reduction only, not multiple re-rating [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Post-close, analysts re-examine ServiceCo standalone and conclude revenue trajectory and Italian macro warrant a structural discount; re-rating never materialises [#A]
  • Miss magnitude: If re-rating fails, the equity return is capped at the interest cost saving (€0.04-0.06 EPS uplift) without the multiple amplification; much lower IRR [#I]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — compelling in arithmetic but contingent entirely on NetCo close and market's willingness to re-rate ServiceCo; not independently achievable

§7 — Key Driver #6: Workforce and OpEx Restructuring

J-front verdict: The Government-supported solidarity contract and headcount reduction programme is a genuine but slow-moving cost lever; savings are real but accrue gradually and carry political constraints [#J]

  • Driver definition: TIT-IT has approximately 40,000+ employees in Italy (one of the largest private-sector employers); a multi-year workforce restructuring using Italian Government solidarity contract mechanisms aims to reduce headcount by 10,000-15,000 over 2024-2027 [#J]
  • Mechanism to EPS: Italian labour costs approximately €4-5bn annually; 10% headcount reduction = €400-500m gross cost saving; net of state subsidy repayment, incremental FCF generation of €200-300m per annum [#I]

  • Mechanism to SP: visible cost reduction execution improves management credibility; reduces concern about structural cost base post-NetCo separation [#I]

  • Company guide: TIT-IT has disclosed agreement with unions and Government for voluntary redundancy/solidarity programme; public guidance cites 14,000 voluntary departures over plan horizon [#J]

  • Track record on workforce reduction: Italian telco workforce restructuring has historically taken longer than guided; Telecom Italia has been attempting workforce reduction for a decade with limited net headcount reduction [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: optimistic — voluntary departure rate depends on package attractiveness and labour market conditions [#J]

  • SS consensus: SS broadly credits workforce restructuring with €200-400m of cost savings in plan models; limited named broker divergence on this driver in public sources [#I]

  • Timing risk: most SS models defer majority of savings to 2025-2026 rather than 2024; front-end loaded vs back-end reality creates EPS estimate risk [#A]

  • EPS impact: €300m net cost saving = approximately €0.02 EPS uplift on current share count [#I]

  • Recent delivery: Solidarity agreement with unions signed; Government approved subsidy mechanism; but as of available data, departure pace has been below targeted run-rate in initial quarters [#J]

  • L3Y baseline: TIT-IT headcount has declined from approximately 50,000 to 40,000+ over 3 years; pace of decline has been gradual (~3,000/year) rather than accelerating [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: BT Group workforce reduction (targeting -40,000 over 5 years) is a comparable programme; BT delivery has been broadly on track but similarly back-end loaded [#I]

  • Macro context: Italian labour market tightness limits voluntary departure appetite; Government support for solid departure packages reduces resistance [#A]

  • Political constraint: Italian Government's dual role as NetCo buyer (via CDP) and workforce protector creates conflict; pressure to maintain employment levels may slow restructuring [#J]

  • Structural: post-NetCo, some workforce transfers to NetCo — reduces ServiceCo absolute headcount mechanically but doesn't generate the same OpEx savings as actual redundancies [#I]

  • Plausibility assessment: Medium plausibility — programme structure is credible, Government support is real, but historical pace and political constraints suggest savings arrive more slowly than modelled [#J]

  • Key assumptions: voluntary departure rate meets target; Government solidarity subsidy remains available; union agreements hold through programme duration [#A]

  • Magnitude if delivered: €300m annual net savings = €0.02 EPS; not transformative but materially supportive of FCF profile [#I]

  • Bear case: Voluntary departure rate disappoints at 50% of plan; Government withdraws or modifies solidarity mechanism; union resistance increases post-NetCo due to service quality concerns; workforce costs stay elevated [#J]

  • Miss scenario: NetCo separation reduces bargaining leverage of management vs unions (workers know assets are separated and employment is now entirely at ServiceCo level); compensation demands increase [#A]
  • Miss magnitude: if workforce savings deliver only €150m vs €300m target, EBITDA miss of €150m = ~€0.01 EPS headwind [#I]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — programme is real and structurally supported but Italian political dynamics and historical pace make full on-time delivery unlikely
