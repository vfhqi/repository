EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (BLUF) 7 findings

Umicore is a Brussels-based global materials technology and recycling group operating across four core divisions: Catalysis, Recycling, Specialty Materials, and Battery Materials Solutions. The company processes 23 critical metals through a proprietary closed-loop metallurgical system, serving automotive, semiconductor, aerospace, and battery cell manufacturing end markets. The March 2025 CORE strategy represents a disciplined pivot from growth-at-all-costs to value recovery, prioritizing cash extraction from foundation businesses while rightsizing the Battery Materials footprint through the Canadian plant pause and Korean consolidation . This strategic realignment addresses severe underutilization in battery assets while protecting high-returning legacy operations in Catalysis and Recycling that generate exceptional returns on capital.

Value Chain Position and Margin Distribution. Umicore operates as an integrated specialist across chemical refining, cathode manufacturing, and precious metals recycling, enabling a closed-loop metallurgy model that returns manufacturing scrap to battery-grade quality . The Recycling division remains the most profit-favored node, generating an adjusted EBITDA margin of 39.2% in 2025 , benefiting from structurally negative working capital cycles and unique supplier payment structures. In contrast, Battery Materials remains highly dilutive with an adjusted EBITDA loss of €21 million, as skeptical brokers argue the implied technology premium of €17-18 per kWh for NMC cathodes is unsustainable . Upstream miners command immense bargaining power due to concentrated global refining capacities, while downstream automotive OEMs hold substantial purchasing leverage by standardizing platforms to force price concessions . Midstream capital investments are defended by take-or-pay agreements covering 85% of contracted battery volumes, but slower platform ramps expose contract renegotiation risks . The value chain margin pool concentrates at upstream extraction (20-30% EBIT margins) and recycling (15-25% EBIT margins) , while midstream cathode manufacturing suffers severe compression at -2% to 5% EBIT margins

Competitive Landscape and Integration Threats. The global autocatalyst market is dominated by a triopoly of Umicore, Johnson Matthey, and BASF, controlling 65-70% of volumes, with Umicore holding the leading global share in highly profitable light-duty gasoline catalysts . However, competitors have responded divergently to the EV transition, with Johnson Matthey completely divesting battery materials to focus on clean air and precious metals, while BASF reorganized battery activities into a standalone unit earmarked for potential divestment . Integrated South Korean rivals POSCO Future M and EcoPro BM are aggressively building global cathode capacities, with POSCO converting high-nickel lines to LFP and CNGR Advanced Materials capturing 20.3% of the precursor market with over 70% high-nickel shipments . Vertical integration threats intensify rapidly, with upstream mining conglomerate Zhejiang Huayou Cobalt building a fully integrated model from mine resources to cathode manufacturing, shipping over 100,000 tons of ternary cathode materials and securing longterm contracts for 215,800 tons of cathode materials . Downstream OEMs are integrating backward into cell manufacturing, with Volkswagen's PowerCo centralizing production at Salzgitter and Tesla actively ramping Texas cathode capacity targeting 10 GWh and lithium refining at 30 GWh . Umicore's midstream specialization faces existential risk if integrated miners commercialize ex-China cathode plants , bypassing independent refiners entirely
