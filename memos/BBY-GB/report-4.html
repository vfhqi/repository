  • BBY FY2024 group revenue: £10,015m, up from £9,727m in FY2023 (approx +3%); FY2025's acceleration to 8% is a meaningful step-up.
  • [AS·ARS]: Revenue rose from £8.5bn in FY2022 to £10.76bn in FY2025, a three-year CAGR of approximately 8%.
  • US Buildings growth: Revenue up 24% year-over-year in H1 2025, driven by data centre and commercial construction demand.
  • [AS·Deutsche Bank]: US revenue grew 18% at constant exchange rates in 2025; geographic mix shifting decisively toward the United States.
  • US Civils drag: A single Texas highway joint venture (SH-161, commenced 2019) incurred design-related rework cost overruns; US Civils represents only 8% of US Construction revenues.
  • ⚡ Order book conversion: Group order book at record £22.7bn (up 23% on FY2024); order book to annual revenue ratio approximately 2.1x — providing multi-year revenue visibility above sector average.
  • [AS·Deutsche Bank]: Revenue visibility 2.4x vs 2.2x sector average; underlying contract momentum continued into early 2026 with order book maintaining peak ~£23bn level.
  • UK power generation tailwind: Year-end order book surge partly driven by long-term UK power generation projects; structural, not cyclical, demand driver.
  • [AS·Deutsche Bank]: UK Construction order book expanded 44% year-on-year — a RARE sector outlier performance, anchored by Sizewell C and Net Zero Teesside.
  • Peer comparison — organic revenue growth:
  • MGNS FY2025: +10% to £5.0bn — beating BBY's 8%.
  • KIE FY2025: +3% to £4.1bn — meaningfully below BBY.
  • RNWH FY2025: +5.6% to £1.1bn — specialist engineering, more modest scale.
  • GFRD FY2025: +6.3% to £1.8bn — accelerating from lower base.
  • [AS·ARS]: BBY's 8-9% organic revenue growth rate materially outperforms Morgan Sindall's ~4%, Kier's ~3%, and Galliford Try's ~2-3%.
  • ❌ Sceptical note: The 23% order book growth includes large power generation contracts — long-duration, low near-term revenue recognition; headline order book may flatter near-term organic revenue conversion.

  • [AS·Earnings Presentation]: UK margins of 3.5% match Skanska, outperform Galliford Try's 3.2%, trail Kier's 3.9%.
  • UK Construction margin trajectory: Estimated progression approx 1.5% (FY2021) → 2.0% (FY2022) → 2.5% (FY2023) → 2.7% (FY2024) → 3.5% (FY2025); consistent directional improvement.
  • Group blended margin: Underlying PFO from earnings-based businesses £293m on revenue of £10,767m implies approximately 2.7% blended construction margin — lower than UK standalone because US Civils loss drags the average.
  • Insurance recovery caveat: ❌ FY2025 UK Construction margin included an £11m insurance recovery; management guided FY2026 UK margin growth to be assessed excluding this item — flagging that headline 3.5% is partly non-recurring.

  • Material inflation risk mitigated via target-cost escalators vastly superior to fixed-price models.

  • ❌ Sceptical note: IIJA expires September 2026; reauthorisation uncertainty creates a potential cliff for US civil infrastructure spending; BBY's US Civils strategic pivot to Southeast highways could be caught in a federal funding gap.

§8 — Quality of Earnings

Verdict: Earnings quality is broadly satisfactory — cash conversion is strong, the balance sheet is in net cash, and exceptional items are reducing in absolute terms; however, several flattering items warrant careful scrutiny before accepting the 47.6p underlying EPS at face value.

  • Cash conversion: Average net cash increased to £1,212m in FY2025 from £766m in FY2024 — a £446m improvement indicating strong operating cash generation well in excess of reported earnings.
  • [AS·ARS]: Average net cash of £1.21bn driven by £408m working capital inflows; 2026 guidance upgraded to average monthly net cash of £1.55bn.
  • ❌ Working capital FY2024 flattery: Working capital was an inflow of £99m in FY2024, partly driven by mobilisation payments received on new Power projects — timing items that reverse as projects progress.
  • ❌ PPP income decline: Net investment income H1 2025 was £4m vs H1 2024 £11m — a £7m year-on-year decline; as the portfolio shrinks, this income stream diminishes; FY2026 group earnings face a headwind here.
  • ❌ Insurance recovery in underlying: The £11m insurance recovery was included within the underlying UK Construction result — it is not separately excluded from the 47.6p underlying EPS figure.
  • Exceptional item trend: Non-underlying items appear to be net gains in FY2025 (SH-161 release + disposal gains) whereas prior years had net charges; an improving trend but partly coincidental rather than structural.
  • ❌ Sceptical note: The combination of mobilisation payment timing, an £11m insurance recovery within underlying earnings, declining PPP income, and a £50m non-underlying provision release means headline FY2025 results are arguably 10–15% higher quality-adjusted than they first appear.

§9 — Sceptical Synthesis

Verdict: The earnings trend is credible enough to justify further research — the multi-year UK margin improvement is structural, the order book is at record levels, and sector demand is robust; the principal risk is US Civils and the question of whether it is genuinely contained or a recurring liability.

  • Most important risk: US Civils project execution — the Texas SH-161 highway has been a multi-year drag; if further cost-to-complete revisions emerge before H2 2026 completion, a second adverse item would damage management credibility and trigger consensus downgrades.
  • Bull case summary: UK margin continues toward 4% as contract mix improves; US Buildings sustains 20%+ revenue growth on data centre and defence demand; order book conversion provides earnings visibility to FY2028; buybacks mechanically lift EPS.
  • Bear case summary: US Civils produces a third significant project loss; UK margin reverts to 3.0–3.2% as insurance recovery disappears; PPP income continues to decline; EPS grows at 3–5% rather than 9%, triggering re-rating to sector median.
  • Final verdict: The earnings trend is credible and improving; direction is unambiguously right and the company is delivering on guidance; the thesis merits further research — particularly on US Civils resolution and FY2026 margin delivery ex-insurance recovery.
