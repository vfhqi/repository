§8 — Key Driver #7: Structural Compounder Model Durability

J-front verdict: OEM.B-SE's decentralised value-added distribution model is structurally sound and has compounded returns through multiple cycles; this driver is the thesis floor — it prevents permanent capital loss but does not drive IRR alone [#J]

  • Driver definition: The durability of OEM's business model — value-added distribution with technical expertise, supplier relationships, and customer stickiness — against secular threats (digitisation, direct sourcing, e-commerce) [#A]

  • Sub: Mechanism to SP: model durability prevents the bear case of structural revenue erosion; it supports the premium multiple and reduces the probability of the "melting ice cube" secular decline scenario

  • Business model characteristics: OEM operates as a technical distributor with application engineering support; this is structurally more defensible than commodity distribution because switching costs are higher for the customer [#J]

  • Sub: OEM adds value through product selection, specification, and kitting — not just inventory arbitrage; this is a key differentiator vs. pure catalogue distributors

  • Digital disruption risk: Amazon Business, RS Components, and Würth digital have increased competitive intensity in standard commodity components; OEM's moat relies on technical complexity and application support in non-standardised segments [#A]

  • Sub: OEM has partially offset this by focusing on higher-complexity, lower-volume technical components — segments less vulnerable to commoditisation

  • Supplier relationships: OEM holds preferred distributor status with key component suppliers; these relationships are long-dated and provide pricing and availability advantages [#J]

  • Sub: Supplier relationship quality is hard to verify from public sources but management commentary consistently emphasises this as a competitive moat

  • Customer stickiness: Industrial maintenance customers exhibit high switching costs and inertia; the cost of sourcing disruption in a manufacturing environment is high relative to component cost [#A]

  • Sub: Customer churn at OEM has historically been low — this supports the structural durability thesis

  • ⚡ RARE — secular risk flag: The one scenario that breaks this driver is structural demand destruction in traditional industrial components driven by EV transition, automation, and additive manufacturing reducing the frequency and volume of traditional component replacement [#J]

  • Sub: This is a 10-year+ dynamic not a 24M risk, but it is an underweighted consideration in current SS framing

  • Track record: OEM has grown organically and through M&A through the 2015-16 industrial downturn, the 2019-20 pre-pandemic softness, and the 2022-25 destocking cycle; no structural revenue impairment observed in any cycle [#A]

  • Bear case: Digital distribution captures share faster than expected; OEM's revenue mix drifts toward lower-complexity, more commoditised products as new entrants win technical advisory positions; EBITA margins compress structurally toward 10-12% [#J]

  • Sub: This is the slow-motion bear case that is hardest to detect in quarterly reporting

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility on a 24M view — structural model durability is well-evidenced; secular digital risk exists but operates on a longer horizon; this driver is the investment thesis floor
