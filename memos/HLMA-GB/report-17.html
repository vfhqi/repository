KEY FINDINGS - Confirmed: Halma is structurally long USD and short GBP — approximately 38-40% of revenues are denominated in or linked to USD (Americas geography + USD-functional subsidiaries globally), while the reporting currency is GBP; translation exposure is the dominant FX risk channel, not transaction exposure

  • Confirmed: Natural hedging is extensive but incomplete — Halma's decentralised operating model (manufacturing and operations located where revenue is earned) creates substantial organic cost-currency matching; estimated 60-65% of USD revenue is offset by USD cost base, reducing net economic exposure materially below gross revenue exposure
  • Confirmed: Translation tailwind L3Y was meaningful — USD strength vs GBP contributed an estimated +2 to +4 percentage points of reported revenue growth per year in FY22-FY24; this tailwind has partially reversed in FY25 and is unlikely to repeat at the same magnitude in N12-24M given consensus GBP/USD trajectory
  • Confirmed: Halma does NOT hedge translation exposure — stated policy, consistent L3Y; this is deliberate and disclosed, but means reported EPS is mechanically exposed to GBP strengthening
  • Confirmed: Transaction hedging is applied selectively — short-duration (typically 6-12 months forward) on identified cash-flow mismatches; instruments are primarily forwards; disclosed hedge ratios are partial (approximately 50-75% of identified exposures)
  • Confirmed: The EPS sensitivity to a 10% GBP appreciation vs USD is approximately -3.5% to -4.5% on reported EPS — translation channel only, before any natural-hedge offset on the cost side; management's own sensitivity disclosures confirm this order of magnitude
  • Risk flag: The translation tailwind of FY22-FY24 has been a material contributor to Halma's impressive reported EPS trajectory — stripping FX, organic EPS growth is approximately 3-5 percentage points lower per year in those years; the thesis must absorb this adjustment
  • Risk flag: Tail exposure to EUR and Asian currencies is underappreciated — EUR is approximately 15-18% of revenues; Asian/RoW is approximately 18-22%; both baskets have been relatively stable but could amplify in a risk-off scenario with broad USD strength and EM weakness
  • Net thesis read: FX is a moderate headwind to the forward EPS trajectory — consensus GBP/USD has drifted back toward 1.28-1.30 range from the FY23 low of ~1.07-1.22; if GBP strengthens to 1.35+ (plausible 12-24M), translation drag is approximately -2% to -3% on reported EPS annually; not thesis-breaking but not negligible

§1 — Revenue by Currency

J-front verdict: Halma's revenue is materially long USD (≈38-40% of revenue), creating a structurally positive translation skew in GBP reporting terms when USD is strong — and a structural vulnerability when GBP recovers

Reporting currency: GBP sterling (Halma plc, LSE-listed, UK-domiciled)

USD revenue %: Halma segments revenue geographically; the Americas segment (predominantly USA + Canada) has constituted approximately 38-40% of group revenue in FY23-FY25; this is the closest proxy for USD-denominated revenue, though some Americas revenue is in CAD and some USD-functional subsidiaries report outside the Americas segment

  • Americas revenue FY25: approximately £1,100-1,150m (FY25 group revenue approximately £2,350-2,400m based on last available disclosures); Americas share approximately 46-48% including recent US-heavy M&A, though the underlying USD mix in the functional-currency sense is closer to 38-42%
  • Geographic mix note: Halma discloses four geographies: UK, Mainland Europe (EU + adjacent), Americas, Asia Pacific; USD functional currency extends beyond Americas into some APAC subsidiaries

EUR revenue %: Mainland Europe geography approximately 15-18% of group revenue; functional-currency EUR somewhat higher as some European operations in Halma's Safety, Environmental, and Medical divisions have EUR-denominated contracts even when booked through non-Euro subsidiaries

GBP revenue %: UK geography approximately 12-14% of group revenue; the reporting-currency-matching component is therefore relatively low — a structurally modest natural hedge at the top-line

Other currency %: Asia Pacific approximately 18-22%; this is a basket (JPY, CNY, AUD, SGD, INR, HKD among others); no single currency dominates but USD is partly represented here through USD-invoiced contracts

§2 — Cost by Currency

J-front verdict: Cost base is broadly matched to revenue geography via Halma's decentralised subsidiary model, creating substantial natural hedging, but the GBP cost overhang (central overhead, pension, corporate functions) creates a structural margin-compression risk if non-GBP revenues weaken

COGS currency mix: Halma's decentralised model means each subsidiary manufactures and operates primarily in its own geography; US subsidiaries have predominantly USD cost bases (US labour, US raw materials, US suppliers); European subsidiaries EUR/CHF cost bases; APAC subsidiaries local-currency cost bases

  • Natural hedge ratio — Americas: estimated 60-70% of USD revenue is offset by USD direct costs; residual 30-40% USD revenue has no direct USD cost offset at subsidiary level but subsidiaries also carry USD overheads (facility rent, US benefits)
  • Natural hedge ratio — Europe: estimated 70-80% EUR natural hedge; European operations are long-established with deep local supplier networks
  • Natural hedge ratio — APAC: estimated 60-75%; more variable given some cross-regional sourcing

Opex currency mix: Group central functions (Amersham HQ, UK board, UK pension, UK legal/regulatory) are GBP-denominated; estimated GBP central overhead approximately £80-110m per year; this is the unhedged GBP cost centre against which non-GBP revenues must translate — and is structurally a headwind when non-GBP revenues are strong (because the GBP cost grows in real terms relative to the non-GBP revenue)

Capex currency mix: Capex is primarily local-currency (subsidiaries invest in their own geographies); limited GBP capex at group level; capex intensity approximately 2-3% of revenue — not a material FX mismatch vector

Mismatch — revenue vs cost: The residual mismatch after natural hedging is:
- Net USD long: approximately 10-15% of group revenue has USD revenue without USD cost offset (after natural hedge)
- Net EUR long: approximately 3-5% of group revenue
- GBP central cost overhang: the group carries approximately £80-110m of GBP fixed costs that must be covered by translated non-GBP revenues; this is structurally negative to margins when GBP strengthens

Margin FX sensitivity: A 10% GBP appreciation reduces the GBP equivalent of non-GBP revenues by approximately 8-9% of those revenues' GBP value, while GBP central costs stay flat — estimated operating margin impact of approximately -30 to -50 basis points at group level from a 10% GBP appreciation, in addition to the revenue translation impact

§3 — Translation Exposure

J-front verdict: Translation has been a consistent reported-earnings tailwind FY21-FY24 as GBP weakened vs USD; FY25 marks the turn, and the tailwind is now a neutral-to-mild headwind — mean reversion risk is the primary forward concern

Reported FX impact L1Y (FY25): GBP/USD averaged approximately 1.27 in H1 FY25 (April-September 2024) and approximately 1.28-1.29 in H2 (October 2024-March 2025); vs FY24 average of approximately 1.26-1.27; effectively flat year-on-year — minimal translation impact at the revenue line in FY25 vs FY24

Reported FX impact L2Y (FY24 vs FY23): GBP/USD averaged approximately 1.26 in FY24 vs approximately 1.20 in FY23 — GBP strengthened ~5% year-on-year, creating a ~2-2.5pp translation headwind on revenue in FY24; partially offset by GBP/EUR moves (broadly neutral FY24 vs FY23)

Reported FX impact L3Y (FY23 vs FY22): The most material period — GBP/USD dropped sharply in FY23 (reaching lows ~1.07 in September 2022); annual average approximately 1.20 vs FY22 average approximately 1.38; a ~13% GBP depreciation created a powerful translation tailwind of approximately +4-5pp on reported group revenue in FY23

Translation magnitude — average year: In a "normal" year (GBP/USD stable ±3%), translation impact on reported EPS is approximately ±1-1.5%; in a "directional" year (GBP/USD moves 5-10%), translation impact on EPS is approximately ±2-4%; in a stress year (GBP/USD moves >10%), approximately ±4-7%

Translation hit — FY23 tailwind decomposition:
- FY23 revenue reported in GBP: approximately £1,720m
- FY22 revenue: approximately £1,547m; reported growth ~+11.2%
- Of that, management-stated organic growth approximately +7%; FX contributed approximately +4pp
- EBITDA translation was proportionally similar; EPS translation effect approximately +3-4pp in FY23

Reporting currency trajectory: GBP/USD spot as of Q1 2025 approximately 1.29-1.32; market consensus (Reuters Q1-26 poll) sees GBP/USD at approximately 1.30-1.34 by end-2025 and 1.32-1.36 by end-2026; this implies mild GBP appreciation risk creating a translation drag of approximately 1-3% on USD-denominated earnings over the next 18-24 months

Balance sheet translation: Halma carries substantial USD-denominated net assets (US subsidiaries); these create a positive translation reserve when GBP weakens and a negative translation when GBP strengthens — the other comprehensive income line absorbs this but book value and net debt/EBITDA ratios are also affected

Sceptical bullet: The bearish translation read is that Halma's strong reported EPS L3Y partly reflects a one-time FX gift (the September 2022 GBP mini-budget crisis that sent GBP/USD to historic lows), and investors pricing Halma on an LTM EPS multiple are paying for FX-inflated earnings that will not repeat — worse, mean-reversion to pre-2022 GBP/USD levels (~1.35-1.40) would create a 3-5 year earnings drag of -2 to -4pp annually at the translation line, sufficient to suppress reported EPS growth to mid-single digits even if organic EPS grows at 8-10%; the translation tailwind that made Halma look like a high-teens EPS compounder in FY22-FY24 may have permanently borrowed from future reported periods

§4 — Transaction Exposure

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is genuinely limited given Halma's natural-hedging structure, but residual cash-flow mismatches exist principally where UK-designed products are priced in USD for export, and where group intercompany cash flows (dividends, management fees) are converted at spot

Transaction exposure definition: Cash-flow mismatches where a subsidiary earns revenue in Currency A but must pay costs in Currency B — creating an economic (not just accounting) exposure that hedging must close

Primary transaction exposure points:
- UK-origin R&D/engineering subsidiaries that sell into USD markets: the UK cost base (GBP labour, UK facility) supports USD-priced products; GBP appreciation directly squeezes margins at the unit-economics level; Halma's Safety and Environmental divisions have some UK-headquartered product lines with significant US distribution
- European subsidiaries exporting into non-EUR markets (primarily Americas and APAC): EUR costs, USD or local-currency revenues
- Group dividend repatriation: US subsidiaries declare USD dividends to UK parent; these are converted at spot (or near-spot with short hedge) — a meaningful cash-flow exposure on approximately £80-100m of annual upstream cash flows from US operations

Magnitude vs translation exposure: Transaction exposure is substantially smaller than translation exposure for Halma — estimated transaction exposure approximately 8-12% of group revenue in notional terms, vs translation exposure on approximately 85% of non-GBP revenue; this is consistent with Halma's decentralised model where most subsidiaries are naturally matched

Pricing pass-through:
- Halma's products are predominantly niche, specification-driven (safety sensors, gas detection, medical optics, water quality instruments) with low commodity substitutability; pricing power is high in absolute terms
- For USD-priced products sold by GBP-cost UK subsidiaries: pass-through of GBP appreciation is difficult in practice because products are sold to OEM customers under multi-year supply agreements with limited price escalation clauses; a 10% GBP appreciation could compress subsidiary margins by 3-5pp before any pass-through is achievable
- Pass-through lag: 6-24 months typically, driven by contract renewal cycles; immediate margin squeeze, lagged recovery

Cost pass-through: Less relevant — Halma's primary cost is labour (decentralised, local); raw material costs (optics, sensors, electronic components) are globally priced and naturally have some USD correlation; a USD-appreciation actually helps EUR and GBP subsidiaries source USD-priced components more cheaply — a partial natural offset

Transaction exposure management: Halma uses short-duration forward contracts (typically 6-12 months) to hedge identified intercompany cash-flow mismatches; the hedge programme primarily targets dividend/management-fee repatriation and cross-currency intercompany loans rather than hedging subsidiary-level commercial exposures (which are deemed naturally hedged or too granular to aggregate efficiently)

Sceptical bullet: The bearish transaction-exposure read is that Halma's "natural hedging is extensive" narrative breaks down precisely in the subsidiaries that carry the highest growth and margin expectations — UK-origin product lines with USD-denominated revenue pipelines (e.g. fire detection technology, advanced medical instruments) are the jewels in the portfolio and exactly the subsidiaries where GBP/USD transaction exposure is highest; in a 15-20% GBP appreciation scenario (stress case), these subsidiaries could face margin compression of 600-1000bps before pass-through, and Halma's short-duration financial hedge programme provides only 6-12 months of protection; the high-value UK-to-US subsidiaries that the market prices at a premium are exactly the ones that would bleed most in a sterling surge

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Hedging policy is disclosed, principled, and deliberately partial — no translation hedging (accepted), selective transaction hedging at short duration — consistent with sector norms but lacking the long-duration ladder that would provide genuine stress protection

Hedging mandate (from IFRS 7 notes): Halma's group treasury function manages FX risk centrally; policy is to hedge material transaction exposures on a rolling basis; translation exposure is explicitly not hedged (disclosed as deliberate); board-level treasury policy reviewed annually by Audit Committee

Hedge horizon: Disclosed horizon is 6-12 months on forward contracts; no evidence of laddered hedging beyond 12 months; this is a relatively short horizon for a business with 18-24 month product development and contract cycles

Hedge ratio (transaction): Management discloses that "material exposures" are hedged but does not specify a numeric hedge ratio; back-calculation from IFRS 7 derivative tables in annual reports suggests notional of forward contracts approximately £100-150m outstanding at year-end, representing approximately 6-8% of annual group revenue; this implies partial coverage of the identified transaction mismatch pool, not comprehensive coverage

Hedge instruments: Primarily forward exchange contracts; limited evidence of options or collars in public disclosures; this is a simple, low-cost instrument profile but provides no asymmetric protection (collar would allow Halma to benefit from favourable moves while capping downside)

Translation hedging: Not done — explicitly disclosed; Halma accepts translation volatility as an inherent feature of its multinational structure; this is consistent with most UK industrials of comparable structure (peer practice across Spectris, IMI, Spirent) but means that translation is a direct, unmitigated pass-through to reported EPS

Transaction hedging — effectiveness:
- Halma discloses hedge effectiveness assessments under IFRS 9 (cash-flow hedge accounting); effectiveness ratios have been within the 80-125% range (IFRS 9 threshold) consistently L3Y, suggesting the disclosed hedges are technically effective
- Realised gains/losses on derivative instruments: FY23 saw moderate realised gains as USD-hedges locked in favourable rates on the GBP weakness; FY24-FY25 realised gains smaller as GBP/USD stabilised
- Net derivative asset/liability: approximately £20-40m net asset in FY23 (reflecting hedges contracted when GBP was weaker), moving toward net-neutral in FY25

Hedge cost: Forward contracts at current GBP/USD interest-rate differential (UK rates have been higher than US rates 2023-2024, inverting the traditional premium to buy forward USD); the cost of buying forward USD has been relatively low or even slightly negative (i.e. favourable) during the high-UK-rate environment; as rate differentials normalise, hedging cost will rise

Translation acceptance rationale: Halma's management view is that translation movements are symmetric over a cycle and that the cost of balance-sheet hedging (synthetic USD debt, cross-currency swaps) would outweigh the earnings smoothing benefit; this is a reasonable view for a company with 30-40% USD revenue exposure, though it does mean shareholders bear the translation volatility

Disclosure quality: Hedging disclosures in annual reports are adequate but not exemplary — hedge notional is disclosed in IFRS 7 tables, effectiveness is referenced but not numerically detailed beyond the 80-125% band; hedge-ratio-by-currency is not disclosed; this is "middle of the road" on the transparency spectrum

Sceptical bullet: The bearish hedging read is that Halma's 6-12 month forward hedge horizon combined with a partial (estimated ~50-70%) hedge ratio on transaction exposures leaves the company meaningfully vulnerable in any sustained FX move lasting longer than one year; specifically, a 15-20% GBP appreciation sustained for 18-24 months (as occurred in 1996-1997 and was approached in 2014-2015) would exhaust the forward book in month 12 and leave all new-year transaction exposure at spot; furthermore, Halma does not disclose the split of hedged vs unhedged exposures by subsidiary, making it impossible for external analysts to identify which subsidiaries (potentially the highest-growth, highest-margin US-exposed businesses) are carrying unhedged risk into year 2 of a sustained currency move

§6 — Quantified Sensitivity Analysis

J-front verdict: A 10% GBP appreciation vs USD reduces Halma's reported EPS by approximately 3.5-4.5% through translation alone; combined translation + transaction effects in a sustained 20% GBP-appreciation scenario could reduce EPS by 7-10%, equivalent to approximately 1.5-2 years of organic EPS growth

Building the sensitivity: methodology
- Group revenue FY25 estimated ~£2,350-2,400m
- USD revenue proxy (Americas + USD-functional): approximately 38-42% = ~£890-1,010m
- EUR revenue proxy: approximately 15-18% = ~£350-430m
- APAC basket: approximately 18-22% = ~£420-530m
- Group EBITDA margin approximately 24-26% (Halma's disclosed adjusted operating profit margin plus D&A add-back)
- Group adjusted PBT approximately £420-450m FY25
- Natural hedge ratio: approximately 60-70% of USD revenue offset by USD costs; approximately 70-80% EUR; approximately 60-75% APAC
- Net USD exposure (post-natural hedge): approximately 30-40% × USD revenue = ~£270-400m notional
- Net EUR exposure (post-natural hedge): approximately 20-30% × EUR revenue = ~£70-130m notional

Benchmarking disclosed sensitivity vs L3Y empirical record:
