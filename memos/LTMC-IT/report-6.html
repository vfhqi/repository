Research Plan

Lottomatica Group SpA currently enjoys a highly favorable standing among sell-side analysts, who view the company as a "national champion" uniquely positioned to benefit from the structural shift toward online gambling in Italy. The consensus narrative is defined by robust confidence in the management's ability to execute accretive M&A, navigate a stable regulatory environment, and return significant capital to shareholders through an upsized €700 million buyback program. While analysts acknowledge the risks associated with single-country concentration, the prevailing sentiment is that LTM's valuation remains "too cheap" given its double-digit growth profile and superior cash generation relative to global peers like Flutter and Entain.

Sources: Rothschild & Co Redburn (8 Apr), Jefferies Research (3 Mar), Morgan Stanley (6 May)

§2 The Bull Case: Key Arguments for Outperformance

Buy-rated analysts generally anchor their optimism on three primary pillars: market leadership in a high-value regulated market, the structural "mix shift" to high-margin online verticals, and disciplined capital allocation.

Source: Jefferies Research (22 Apr)

2.1 Dominant "National Champion" Status

LTM is viewed as the undisputed leader in Italy, the largest gambling market in Europe. Morgan Stanley highlights that LTM's omnichannel strategy — leveraging a massive retail network to acquire customers and migrate them online — is a structural advantage that smaller "online-only" competitors cannot easily replicate.

Source: Morgan Stanley (12 Dec 25)

Rothschild & Co Redburn notes that LTM's online market share continues to hit new highs, reaching approximately 32.2% in Q1 2026, further consolidating its lead over its nearest competitor, Flutter (Sisal).

Source: Rothschild & Co Redburn (17 Apr)

2.2 High-Margin Online Growth and Synergy Realisation

A core part of the bull thesis is the earnings tailwind from online penetration, which offers profitability more than twice that of retail operations. Jefferies emphasises that as LTM skews more toward online, group EBITDA margins will naturally expand. Analysts also point to the successful integration of SKS365 (Planetwin Online), which is expected to deliver significant cost synergies and market share recovery.

Sources: Rothschild & Co Redburn (17 Apr), Jefferies Research (6 May, 13 Jan)

2.3 Attractive Valuation and Capital Returns

Deutsche Bank argues that LTM is attractively valued, trading at approximately 9.4x FY27E EV/EBITDA despite a stable regulatory backdrop and strong organic growth. Analysts are particularly focused on the €700m share buyback authorisation for FY26/27, which, combined with dividends, could result in €1 billion in total shareholder returns over two years.

Source: Jefferies Research (6 May)

[AS·Rothschild & Co Redburn]: Rothschild & Co Redburn characterises LTM as a "compounder" delivering a 22% adjusted EPS CAGR from 2025 to 2029, making current multiples look "just too cheap." — Rothschild & Co Redburn, 14 May

§3 The Bear Case: Risks and Sceptical Viewpoints

While there are currently no active "Sell" ratings from the major brokers covered, analysts do identify specific structural risks that could cap valuation or lead to downside.

Source: Deutsche Bank Research (6 May)

3.1 Geographic and Regulatory Concentration

The most cited risk is LTM's 100% revenue exposure to Italy. Deutsche Bank notes that this lack of geographic diversification leaves the company vulnerable to localised regulatory shifts, tax hikes, or macroeconomic downturns in the Italian economy. Morgan Stanley lists "single country risk" as a primary downside factor, noting that any unexpected changes in the Italian concession framework would have an outsized impact on the group.

Sources: Deutsche Bank Research (6 May), Morgan Stanley (3 Mar, 6 May)

3.2 Execution and Integration Risks

Jefferies mentions that a combination of falling market share and slowing market growth could lead to a "downside scenario" price target of €18.00. There is also a persistent concern regarding the potential for higher-than-expected concession renewal costs, which must be factored into long-term cash flow models.

Sources: Morgan Stanley (3 Mar), Jefferies Research (8 Apr)

3.3 Market Disruption: Prediction Markets

A recent "bear scenario" explored by Morgan Stanley involves the rise of Prediction Markets (PMs) like Polymarket. While currently illegal in Italy, analysts suggest that if PMs were to achieve the breadth of established sports betting and target the market, they could offer a theoretical "channelisation risk" similar to offshore offerings.

Source: Morgan Stanley (21 Apr)

§4 Key Debates and Disagreements Among Analysts

Divergence in the sell-side community primarily centres on the "right" valuation multiple for a single-country operator and the expected pace of online market consolidation.

4.1 The "Single Country Discount"

A major point of contention is whether LTM deserves a discount relative to multi-jurisdictional peers like Flutter. Rothschild & Co Redburn explicitly argues that this discount is "unjustified" because Italy's new online concession model offers superior regulatory certainty compared to the "headwinds" facing operators in other European and unregulated markets. Conversely, Morgan Stanley applies a "single country discount" in its relative valuation methodology, even while maintaining an Overweight rating.

Sources: Rothschild & Co Redburn (6 Jan), Morgan Stanley (6 May)

4.2 Consensus Underappreciation

Rothschild & Co Redburn frequently asserts that the broader consensus has not fully factored in LTM's growth potential from higher online penetration. They argue that many analysts have not adequately accounted for the incremental revenue upside from market share gains as smaller "tail" operators are squeezed out by the new, more expensive licensing regime.

Sources: Rothschild & Co Redburn (8 Apr, 2 Jan)
