§1 Business Overview

Verdict: Diageo is the world's largest premium spirits company by revenue, with a genuinely diversified brand portfolio spanning whisky, tequila, beer, vodka, rum, and gin — but the breadth that historically justified premium valuation now creates as much complexity as it resolves. [#J]

  • Scale: Net sales $20.2bn (FY25); 200+ brands sold in 180+ countries; ~27,000 employees pre-Accelerate restructuring. Peer context: Pernod Ricard c.€9bn revenue; Remy Cointreau c.€1.1bn — Diageo is 2-3x the size of the next largest listed peer by revenue. [#I]
  • Category mix (approx. FY24-25):
  • Scotch whisky: ~25-28% of net sales (Johnnie Walker, J&B, Bell's, Buchanan's, Singleton)
  • Beer: ~15-17% (Guinness, Harp) — unusually large beer exposure for a spirits peer
  • Tequila: ~10-12% (Don Julio, Casamigos) — fastest-growing category pre-FY26 reversal
  • Vodka: ~8-9% (Smirnoff, Cîroc, Ketel One)
  • Rum: ~5-6% (Captain Morgan)
  • Gin: ~4-5% (Tanqueray, Gordon's)

  • Liqueurs/other: ~7-9% (Baileys, Baileys Almande) [#I]

  • Geographic mix (FY25 approximate net sales %):

  • North America: ~38% (largest single region; primarily US spirits + Guinness)
  • Europe: ~22% (Guinness dominant; Johnnie Walker; Baileys)
  • Asia Pacific: ~16% (India, China via Shui Jing Fang/baijiu, SE Asia)
  • Latin America & Caribbean (LAC): ~9% (Mexico, Brazil, Colombia; heavily tequila/scotch)
  • Africa: ~9% (transitioning to asset-light after EABL/Guinness Nigeria disposals)

  • Global Travel Retail (GTR): ~6% [#I]

  • Channel mix: On-trade (bars/restaurants) ~35-40%; off-trade (retail) ~55-60%; e-commerce and DTC a small but growing proportion (~3-4%). Guinness is structurally on-trade dependent; Smirnoff off-trade dominant. [#I]

  • Guinness Microdraught device expanding on-trade into venues without traditional draught lines — material distribution unlock. [#I]

  • Route to market: Primarily third-party distribution in the US (Southern Glazer's, Breakthru Beverage as major accounts); own sales force in UK, Ireland, India; JV/partner models in many Asian markets. [#I]

  • Non-alcoholic pivot: Non-alcoholic portfolio grew ~40% organically in FY25; Ritual Zero Proof acquired (top-selling non-alcoholic spirits brand in the US, acquired Sep-24). Diageo claims the world's largest non-alcoholic spirits brand position. [#I]

  • Sceptical check: The breadth of brand categories creates cross-cutting resource allocation conflicts — A&P budget spread across 200+ brands means even strong brands may be underfunded. Dave Lewis's H1-FY26 admission that customer service to distributors was "poor" and demand for Guinness went unfulfilled suggests operational focus deficit inherent to the current portfolio scale. [#J]

§2 Financial Profile

Verdict: Diageo's financial track record from FY21-FY23 (post-COVID boom) looked like the compounder thesis in full flower; FY24-FY26 reveals that much of that growth was a demand pull-forward, leaving the business now running below its pre-pandemic trend with a balance sheet that is stretched by historical standards. [#J]

  • Revenue trajectory (net sales, USD):
  • FY23: $20.3bn — organic growth +6.0% (price/mix +6.6%, volume -0.6%)
  • FY24: $20.3bn — organic growth -0.6% (price/mix +2.9%, volume -3.5%); ❌ first organic decline in a decade; LAC -21.1% the key driver
  • FY25: $20.2bn — organic growth +1.7% (price/mix +0.8%, volume +0.9%); reported flat due to FX

  • FY26E guidance: organic net sales -2% to -3%; H1-FY26 reported: -4% / organic -2.8% [#I]

  • Organic growth benchmarking vs. cohort:

  • Remy Cointreau FY24-25: -18% organic — significantly worse than Diageo, reflecting 70%+ cognac exposure and deep China/US destocking ❌
  • Pernod Ricard: broadly similar trajectory to Diageo — mid-single-digit declines in key periods

  • Fevertree (spirits mixer, not spirits): category growth complicates direct comparison but serves as demand-environment cross-check [#I]

  • EBITDA/margin profile:

  • Reported operating margin FY25: 21.4% (FY24: ~29.6%) — the 819bps collapse is primarily due to ~$1.4bn exceptional charges (impairments, restructuring)
  • Organic operating margin FY25: contracted 68bps — modest but ongoing compression

  • H1-FY26: organic operating profit -2.8% vs. organic net sales -2.8% — margin broadly held at operating level despite tariff headwinds and volume deleverage; Accelerate savings beginning to offset [#A]

  • EPS trajectory:

  • FY23 EPS: ~190 cents (approximate, per consensus)
  • FY24 EPS: 173.2 cents
  • FY25 EPS: 105.9 cents — ❌ -38.8% YoY; dominated by exceptional charges; adjusted EPS likely c.135-140 cents

  • FY26E analyst consensus: ~$1.67 (DEO equivalent) — implies further pressure [#I]

  • Free cash flow: FY25: $2.75bn — robust cash generation remains a key structural positive; FCF yield is more defensive than EPS given impairment-heavy P&L. FY26E: guided c.$3bn (before one-off SAP S/4HANA inventory build). [#A]

  • Leverage: Net debt $21.7bn; ND/EBITDA 3.4x (FY25, above 2.5-3.0x target); FY24 was 3.0x. EABL disposal ($2.3bn proceeds) expected to reduce leverage ~0.25x; target return to 2.5-3.0x band. ❌ versus spirits peers who typically operate 2.0-2.5x. [#A]

  • Sceptical check: The "strong FCF" narrative requires scrutiny — capex remains elevated (Guinness capacity expansion; new distilleries); working capital in spirits is structurally high (multi-year maturing stocks); and Accelerate exceptional costs consume reported FCF. The FCF quality improvement thesis depends on Accelerate delivery, not organic business recovery. [#J]

  • FY25: US spirits +1.6% organic (Don Julio driving); early signs of recovery post-LAC drag
  • H1-FY26: NAM organic sales declined — consumer wallets under pressure; spirits category experiencing volume decline at standard and premium tiers (Beam Suntory and Brown-Forman reported similar dynamics)
  • Q3-FY26: US spirits -15.4% — ❌ accelerating deterioration, not a gentle softness
  • Tariffs compounding weakness: Scotch (UK-origin) faces 10% tariff; European gins/liqueurs 15%; tequila/mezcal under USMCA but retaliatory risk exists

  • Guinness US continues to outperform; now #1 draft beer in key metropolitan markets [#A]

  • Europe (~22% of sales):

  • Relative outperformer; Guinness dominant; UK draught channel structurally Guinness-positive
  • Scotch declining in the UK retail channel (price elasticity at standard tier); Baileys and Tanqueray broadly stable

  • Asia Pacific (~16% of sales):

  • India: Diageo holds ~33% of United Spirits (USL); India is a long-term structural growth story; population demographics, rising middle class, premiumisation of local whisky. USL results not always consolidated into group at line-item level in press releases [#I]
  • Japan, SE Asia: small but growing markets; Johnnie Walker and premium Scotch relevant; muted near-term given broader APAC macro [#I]

  • FY25: LAC returned to organic growth as destocking neared completion

  • Africa (~9% of sales):

  • Strategic pivot to asset-light model: disposals of EABL (Kenya, sold to Asahi $2.3bn Dec-25), Guinness Nigeria, Guinness Ghana, Seychelles Breweries
  • Loss on Guinness Nigeria sale: $125m recorded FY25
  • Despite disposals, spirits (international premium brands) performing well; Guinness lager and spirits brands maintain strong positions

  • Asset-light reduces capital intensity but also reduces upside capture in a structurally high-growth region [#A]

  • Sceptical check: Post-EABL disposal, Africa contribution to group net sales will shrink. The simultaneous headwinds in Diageo's two largest regions (NAM, APAC) with limited offset from Europe are not a temporary misalignment — they reflect underlying category and macro pressures that may persist 18-36M. §5 LatAm Crisis Deep-Dive

Verdict: The LatAm crisis was self-inflicted — over-ordering into a post-COVID demand surge, compounded by Mexico-specific category dynamics (tequila and scotch demand collapsed simultaneously). The crisis is largely resolved at the channel inventory level; the residual risk is that consumer demand does not recover to pre-crisis baseline, particularly for Scotch in Mexico. [#J]

  • What happened:
  • 2021-22: COVID-driven spirits boom; LAC consumers traded up aggressively; Diageo and distributors built significant inventory to meet apparent demand
  • Mid-2023 onwards: demand normalised; distributors sat on excess stock; new orders stopped; Diageo's reported sell-in collapsed regardless of consumer demand levels [#I]
  • Mexico: -30% net sales decline in H1-FY24 (Jul-Dec 2023); Don Julio -strong double-digit; Casamigos -14%; Scotch also declining

  • LAC total: -23% H1-FY24; -21.1% full-year FY24 organic — ❌ devastating to group organic; without LAC, group FY24 organic was +1.8% [#A]

  • Geographic concentration of crisis:

  • Mexico: Diageo's second largest LAC market; tequila + scotch = dual category implosion
  • Brazil: secondary impact; consumer sentiment affected by macro uncertainty

  • Colombia: premium spirits demand proved more resilient; less concentrated [#I]

  • How bad were the numbers:

  • H1-FY24 LAC: -23% organic; group total: -1.4% reported
  • Management confirmed "inventory levels have dramatically reduced to appropriate levels in Mexico" by FY25

  • $167m unfavourable FX impact in H1-FY24 compounded the reported pain [#I]

  • Impact on group margin:

  • LAC carries above-average margins (premium tequila and Scotch, export pricing)
  • Loss of LAC revenue at 30-40% gross margin product mix was meaningful EBITDA deleverage

  • No isolated LAC EBITDA data disclosed publicly; estimated gross margin impact in FY24 of 30-60bps at group level from LAC volume loss [#A]

  • Recovery status (as of May-26):

  • FY25 (Jul-24 to Jun-25): LAC returning to organic growth — destocking "near completion"
  • H1-FY26: LAC cited as outperformer alongside Europe and Africa (no specific percentage disclosed in early releases)
  • Q3-FY26 (Oct-Dec 25 + Jan-Mar 26): LAC growth continuing — management characterised as genuine recovery not base effect

  • Residual risk: tequila category globally under down-trading pressure (H1-FY26 global tequila -17% for Diageo); a fully normalised channel does not guarantee demand recovery [#A]

  • New technology and data actions (Jan-24 management response):

  • Diageo announced investment in improved demand-sensing data and route-to-market analytics for LAC
  • Objective: detect inventory imbalances earlier; avoid repeat

  • Implementation timeline: 12-24 months; effectiveness unproven [#I]

  • Sceptical check: The "destocking is over" narrative was used by multiple companies in multiple cycles — the channel normalisation is genuine but the question of whether sell-through (consumer demand) has recovered to trend is separate and unanswered for Mexico tequila specifically, which is now facing global category softness on top of the local destocking. [#J]

§6 Pricing Power & Destocking

Verdict: Diageo's pricing power from 2021-23 was demonstrably strong — price/mix contributed 6-7% in FY23 — but the volume response was worse than any other major spirits company suggests that Diageo either over-priced at some tier levels or that its brand premium was not sufficient to absorb 25-30% cumulative price increases at the value-standard tier. [#J]

  • Price/mix history (3Y):
  • H1-FY23 (Jul-Dec 22): price/mix +7.6%; volume +1.8% — pricing at peak; volume still positive
  • FY23 full year: organic +6.0%; price/mix +6.6%; volume -0.8% — first volume softness signal
  • FY24: price/mix +2.9%; volume -3.5% — pricing continues but volume collapse, especially LAC (-21% volume)
  • FY25: price/mix +0.8%; volume +0.9% — normalisation; pricing power faded as company needed to restore volume; small volume recovery

  • H1-FY26: price/mix -1.9%; volume -0.9% — ❌ price/mix now NEGATIVE; indicates pricing reversal (promotional intensity, pack-size discounting, or channel mix shift to lower-priced formats) [#A]

  • Pricing power (Wisdom Library model applied — Pricing Power Gold):

  • Global scale + JW/Guinness/Don Julio brands: theoretical pricing power is high (no close substitutes at equivalent brand equity)
  • Actual outcome: Price increases 2021-24 were absorbed by premium segment (Don Julio Reposado, JW Blue) but drove volume defection at standard tier (JW Red, Smirnoff, Gordon's)
  • Conclusion: Diageo has strong pricing power at the premium end; weak-to-nil pricing power in the value tier. Portfolio mix complicates the aggregate read. [#A]

  • Peer contrast: Remy Cointreau — more concentrated in premium/super-premium cognac; took larger price increases with less volume loss at brand level (but overall revenue fell more due to China); Diageo's volume sensitivity is higher due to broader standard-tier exposure [#A]

  • Volume response to price increases:

  • FY23-FY24 cumulative: ~4.3% volume decline (organic) against ~9.5% cumulative price/mix
  • Elasticity implied: approximately -0.5 (inelastic at aggregate; but masking highly elastic standard tier)

  • Excluding LAC: FY24 ex-LAC volume -2.1% vs. price/mix +3.9% — better but still meaningful volume attrition [#I]

  • Inventory/destocking channel status (as of May-26):

  • US on-trade: broadly normalised; distributor inventories returned to pre-COVID levels in most spirit categories by H1-FY25
  • US off-trade: normalised; some category-level overhang in vodka and gin persists
  • Mexico: destocked by end of FY25 per management; but tequila consumer demand softening
  • China: ❌ baijiu channel inventory NOT normalised; Shui Jing Fang -56% H1-FY26 suggests ongoing distributor channel work

  • Europe: no significant destocking issues; organic demand broadly stable [#A]

  • Dave Lewis's pivot on pricing strategy:

  • H1-FY26 strategy presentation: Lewis explicitly cited opportunity in "small packs" and mass market accessibility — a shift away from pure premiumisation orthodoxy

  • Small-pack entry-price products could drive volume recovery but compress price/mix further [#A]

  • Sceptical check: The price/mix turning negative in H1-FY26 is the most concerning data point in recent history. If Diageo has to buy back volume with price concessions, the margin recovery implied by Accelerate savings becomes a treadmill — cost savings funding price investment rather than flowing to the P&L. Spirits operating leverage works both ways. [#J]

§7 CEO Transition

Verdict: The transition from Debra Crew to Sir Dave Lewis is a fundamental strategic reset, not a refinement — Lewis's background (consumer staples turnaround at Tesco, 28 years at Unilever) and stated priorities are materially different from Crew's premiumisation-first approach. The strategic review due Aug-26 is the most significant near-term catalyst for thesis formation. [#J]

  • Debra Crew tenure (Jun-23 to Jul-25):
  • Inherited a business in the early stages of a post-COVID normalisation with elevated inventory
  • Struggled to win investor confidence; shares lost ~44% of their value during her tenure
  • Stepped down "by mutual agreement" on 16-Jul-25; immediate departure, no transition period

  • Crew was the first outsider in a decade to lead Diageo (previously: Ivan Menezes, promoted from within); her departure after just 25 months signals board dissatisfaction with strategy execution [#A]

  • Nik Jhangiani interim period (Jul-25 to Dec-25):

  • CFO since Sep-24 (joined from Coca-Cola HBC); stepped up as interim CEO
  • Launched Accelerate cost programme ($500m → $625m target); maintained dividend at 103.48c

  • Positioned as caretaker; no major strategic announcements during interim period [#I]

  • Sir Dave Lewis appointment (effective Jan-1-26):

  • Led Tesco turnaround 2014-2020 (restored profitability, reduced debt, rebuilt supplier relationships after accounting scandal)
  • 27 years at Unilever in various senior roles before Tesco
  • No direct spirits or beverage industry experience — parachute appointment
  • Critical statement (H1-FY26 presentation): customer service to distributors was "poor"; Diageo failed to capitalise on Guinness demand — an extraordinary self-criticism at a public results presentation

  • Dividend cut: interim dividend to 20c (vs. 40.5c prior year) — c.51% cut; target payout ratio reset to 30-50%; floor dividend 50c annually. This is a significant signal — frees ~$500-600m annual cash for reinvestment/deleveraging [#A]

  • Three stated priorities (H1-FY26 presentation):
    1. Build competitive category strategies — winning with relevant brands, including "small packs" and mass-market accessibility (notable de-premiumisation signal)
    2. Customer focus — rebuild distributor/retailer relationships and service standards
    3. Redesign operating framework — remove management layers; increase agility; reduce complexity (confirmed job cuts May-26) [#A]

  • Transition risks:

  • No spirits-specific category knowledge from Lewis; learning curve on brand-building and trade marketing
  • Cultural disruption: Diageo is historically a brand-led organisation; Tesco-style operational discipline is a different management culture

  • Strategic vacuum: Aug-26 strategy day means 8 months of "watch and wait" during which investor uncertainty persists [#J]

  • Transition opportunity:

  • External perspective may identify portfolio optimisation (disposals of sub-scale brands) that insiders resisted
  • Tesco turnaround involved balance sheet repair + supplier relationship reset + operational simplification — all relevant to Diageo's situation

  • Lewis's Unilever background provides consumer insight for brand portfolio management [#A]

  • Sceptical check: A turnaround CEO from grocery retail taking charge of a premium spirits company is a category mismatch that markets may not quickly forgive. The pivot toward mass-market accessibility risks muddying Diageo's premium positioning at exactly the point when the premium long-term story was what justified the stock's historical premium multiple. [#J]

§8 Capital Allocation & Shareholder Returns

Verdict: Capital allocation has shifted decisively from return-of-capital mode (FY23-24 buybacks) to balance sheet repair and reinvestment mode — the dividend cut, disposal of EABL, and suspension of buybacks confirm that leverage reduction takes priority. This is the correct triage decision but materially reduces near-term income appeal. [#J]

  • Dividends:
  • FY24 full-year: 103.48 cents per share (+5% YoY) — last "normal" dividend
  • FY25 full-year: 103.48 cents per share (held flat despite declining earnings; leverage rising)
  • H1-FY26 interim: 20 cents vs. 40.5 cents prior — c.51% cut
  • Target payout ratio: 30-50% of earnings (vs. historical ~50-55%)
  • Floor dividend: 50 cents per annum — Lewis committed to not cutting below this floor

  • Context: ❌ First dividend cut in Diageo's history as an independent company; an ESG and income-fund forced-seller event [#A]

  • Buybacks:

  • FY24: completed $1.0bn buyback programme (announced Aug-23)
  • FY25: no new buyback programme announced (Accelerate launch and leverage concerns meant capital preserved)

  • FY26: buybacks suspended; no expectation of resumption until leverage within target range [#I]

  • Disposals:

  • EABL (Kenya): stake sold to Asahi Group for ~$2.3bn (Dec-25 agreement); 17x EBITDA multiple; closes H2-FY26; ~0.25x leverage reduction; loss of East Africa's strategic growth exposure
  • Guinness Nigeria PLC: disposal in progress; $125m loss recorded FY25
  • Guinness Ghana: disposal announced; de-risking African consolidated brewery exposure
  • Seychelles Breweries: sold
  • Sheridan's liqueur: sold to Casa Redondo
  • Royal Challengers Sports: stake divested

  • Pattern: systemic de-risking of non-core, particularly African breweries; focus capital on international premium spirits [#A]

  • Balance sheet:

  • Net debt: $21.7bn (FY25); $21.9bn including EABL (pre-disposal)
  • ND/EBITDA: 3.4x (FY25) vs. 3.0x target floor; ❌ outside stated 2.5-3.0x range
  • EABL proceeds reduce to approximately 3.1-3.2x post-close (H2-FY26)
  • Maturity profile: Diageo has well-staggered debt; no near-term refinancing cliff risk in current information

  • FCF $2.75bn FY25; $3bn guided FY26 (before one-offs) — sufficient to service debt comfortably; leverage issue is balance sheet stock not cash flow coverage [#A]

  • Capex:

  • Elevated due to Guinness capacity expansion (St. James's Gate, Dublin; US expansion); new distilleries; SAP S/4HANA ERP implementation
