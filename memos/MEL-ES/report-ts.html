Section 3 – What the company does 14 findings

Section summary
  1. Melia's strategic pivot toward high-margin fee contracts is improving structural capital efficiency while maintaining its dominant competitive moat in prime resort destinations.

Portfolio Segmentation and Core Revenue Streams

1.
Luxury repositioning: Melia operates 383 hotels across brands ranging from Sol to Gran Melia, with premium and luxury segments comprising 63% of the existing portfolio and 78% of the forward pipeline
2.
High-value contribution: Premium and luxury properties represent only 20% of room inventory but contribute nearly 40% of hotel operating revenues, highlighting the success of the company's upscaling strategy
3.
Luxury revenue contribution: Luxury brands, including Gran Melia and ME by Meliá, represented 15% of operating hotels but contributed 25% of total hotel revenues in 2023
4.
Mixed operating models: Melia utilizes an "asset-right" model where 89% of rooms in its 16,352-room pipeline are structured under asset-light management or franchise systems, leaving only 14% of existing rooms under direct ownership
5.
Geographic concentration: Spain is the company's core market, accounting for 38% of rooms, followed by EMEA at 22%, the Americas at 12%, Cuba at 15%, and Asia-Pacific at 13%
6.
Leisure dominance: Unlike major peers that focus heavily on corporate travel, Melia has 60% of its operating portfolio in resorts and 40% in city locations, creating a pure-play leisure exposure
7.
Sales channels: Melia has established a robust digital footprint, routing over 50% of centralized sales through its proprietary direct channels like melia.com and the MeliaRewards program, which boasts over 17 million members

Value Chain Position and Moat Mechanics

8.
Customer value proposition: Melia holds a highly favored position in the hospitality value chain as the largest pure-play resort company in the world, giving it significant pricing power over online travel agencies
9.
Scale advantages: The primary competitive moat is driven by scale in resort operations, where Melia's brand equity and loyalty platform lower customer acquisition costs compared to independent operators
10.
Real estate value: Melia's competitive position is underpinned by c.EUR 5.3bn in gross asset value for its owned real estate, securing premium beach and urban locations that are impossible for competitors to replicate
11.
Fragmented market opportunity: The European hotel market is highly fragmented with only 30% branded hotels versus over 70% in the United States, providing a substantial secular runway for Melia to consolidate market share
12.
Intense peer competition: In executing its asset-light expansion, Melia faces stiff competition for new leisure management contracts from massive global peers like Marriott, Hilton, and IHG, who possess far larger loyalty schemes
13.
Other side of the story: Melia's hybrid model may dilute focus, as maintaining its massive EUR 5.3bn real estate portfolio ties up capital and results in a structurally lower return on equity relative to pure-play asset-light hotel operators

Section 5 – The possible investment case 12 findings

Section summary
  1. The investment case for Melia strengthens significantly due to structural pricing power, a rapid asset-light pipeline conversion, and geopolitical tailwinds driving tourist flows to safe European resort hubs.

Major Demand and Geopolitical Drivers

1.
Safe haven effect: The ongoing conflict in the Middle East has triggered a major re-routing of European tourism, with bookings accelerating toward Melia's safe Spanish and Caribbean resort destinations
2.
High visibility bookings: Forward booking momentum is exceptionally strong, with bookings for Q2 and Q3 2026 exhibiting double-digit year-on-year growth and the Easter period alone up 9%
3.
Leisure secular tailwind: A sustained increase in consumer propensity for high-end leisure and experiential travel acts as a powerful multi-year tailwind for Melia's upscale resort segment
4.
Spanish demand dynamics: Spain continues to experience favorable tourism demand-supply dynamics, allowing Melia to sustain high double-digit average daily rate increases across resort and urban properties
5.
Feeder market strength: High-income traveler segments from the United States and United Arab Emirates are showing increasing contribution to Melia's hotels, enhancing overall average rate quality and mitigating regional European slowdowns

Structural Upgrades and Pipeline Expansion

6.
Luxury mix expansion: The ongoing shift toward high-margin premium and luxury brands, which currently represent 63% of the portfolio and 78% of the pipeline, will structurally lift average unit profitability
7.
Asset-light growth: Rapid pipeline expansion, with the 16,000-room pipeline representing 15% of current portfolio size, will generate highly predictable, asset-light management fee streams with minimal capital investment
8.
Strategic scale targets: The company's mid-term goal to surpass 100,000 rooms in operation will establish critical mass in key emerging markets like Southeast Asia and Albania
9.
Joint venture partners: To support its expansion without high debt, Melia has doubled its strategic collaboration with top-tier partners since 2023, establishing 13 joint ventures valued at nearly EUR 3bn
10.
Bull case alignment: For the full bull case to work, these drivers do not need to fire simultaneously, as the geopolitical tailwind alone is powerful enough to offset any localized operational disruptions
11.
Other side of the story: If a sudden European economic recession materializes, highly cyclical leisure spending could collapse, leading to a projected 0.9% EBITDA drop for every 1% decline in RevPAR

Section 7 – Key risks and the other side 11 findings

Section summary
  1. Melia's overall risk profile is moderate but highly manageable, as the structural geopolitical upside in Spain successfully offsets localized consumer cyclicality and family-controlled corporate governance bottlenecks.

Geopolitical and Regional Volatilities

1.
Macroeconomic sensitivity: A sharp slowdown in household discretionary spending in core European feeder markets like the UK or Germany represents a direct threat to occupancy levels and mid-scale pricing power
2.
Mexico security concerns: Escalating localized violence in Cancun, such as the major security events recorded in early 2026, represents a persistent regional risk given that Mexico and Punta Cana contribute approximately 40% of group EBIT
3.
Adverse currency impacts: Continued strengthening of the Euro against the US Dollar creates significant translation headwinds for Caribbean revenues, as witnessed by the negative Q1 2026 foreign exchange impacts
4.
Sovereign Cuban bottlenecks: Operations in Cuba, representing 15% of room capacity, remain severely constrained by structural fuel shortages, electrical grid instability, and weak international flight connectivity
5.
Natural disaster exposure: Melia's Caribbean properties face chronic structural exposure to extreme weather events, such as the natural disasters and hurricanes that previously disrupted operations in Mexico and Punta Cana
6.
Refinancing pressure: Although the recent syndicated loan secured comfortable maturities, any unforeseen spike in global interest rates would increase the post-hedging cost of Melia's remaining variable-rate bank loans

Operational Risks and early indicators

7.
Execution in refurbishments: Delays in completing major flagship refurbishments, such as the Paradisus Cancun closure, represent a key risk that directly impacts near-term EBITDA generation
8.
Labor cost inflation: Potential labor cost inflation across Spain and Mexico represents a chronic operational risk that could compress EBITDA margins if room rate growth begins to normalize
9.
False friend indicator: Strong headline booking volume can act as a false friend if it is achieved by discounting room rates, which erodes high-margin luxury positioning and degrades overall earnings quality
10.
Other side of the story: A premature ceasefire in the Middle East could quickly reverse the positive re-routing of tourism flows, sending high-spending travelers back to Eastern Mediterranean competitors and stalling Melia's ADR growth

Section 8 – Management: what is changing 6 findings

Section summary
  1. Senior leadership is a significant source of positive change right now, as the recent passing of the founder has consolidated executive power and introduced active institutional board representation.
1.
Leadership consolidation: CEO Gabriel Escarrer Jaume, who has spearheaded the company's digital upscaling since 2009, assumed undivided executive leadership as Chairman following the passing of his father, founder Gabriel Escarrer Julia, in late 2024
2.
Board of Directors updates: Private equity investor Stoneshield secured an active board seat in May 2026 after rapidly increasing its equity stake to 9.5%, bringing much-needed external capital allocation discipline to a historically family-dominated board
3.
Family voting agreement: To prevent any multi-generational fractional disputes, the founder's six children signed a formal concerted voting action in February 2026, consolidating their combined 54.805% stake under the Chairman's proxy control
4.
Governance modernization: The company has actively modernized its governance structure, appointing independent director Ms. Cristina Henríquez de Luna Basagoiti as Lead Independent Director to ensure strong oversight over the unified Chairman/CEO role
5.
Executive compensation alignment: The annual general meeting approved a new medium-term variable remuneration plan for senior management linked directly to share price performance, closely aligning executive incentives with minority shareholder value
