Section 3 – What the company does 14 findings

Section summary
  1. The group operates two highly autonomous divisions with dominant niche market positions, utilizing a localized business model that shields earnings from tariff and supply chain shocks.

Segment and subsegment details

1.
Dominant spring manufacturing: Beijer Components, formerly Lesjöfors, comprises nearly 50 companies manufacturing customized industrial springs, wire forms, flat strip components, and aftermarket chassis springs
2.
Specialized industrial trading: Beijer Tech operates some 20 companies in the Nordic region, offering specialized manufacturing, value-added sales, and automation in profitable niche technology sectors
3.
Segment sales split: Beijer Components generated 64% of total group revenue in 2025, while Beijer Tech contributed the remaining 36% of sales
4.
Diversified subsegment exposure: Subsegment exposure in 2025 was led by Industrial Springs at 52%, Chassis Springs at 13%, Industrial Products at 14%, Fluid Technology at 11%, and Niche Technologies at 10%
5.
Resilient aftermarket sales: Replacement chassis springs account for approximately 20% of Beijer Components sales, providing a highly profitable aftermarket buffer that is less sensitive to macroeconomic cycles
6.
Broad geographic diversification: Group sales outside of Sweden represent 89% of the total, providing strong risk diversification across European and global industrial markets

Competitive positioning and value chain

7.
Localized manufacturing strategy: Beijer Components operates a local-for-local production model on three continents, which shields the division from rising global tariff risks compared to export-heavy peers
8.
High customer dispersion: Customer concentration is exceptionally low as no single customer represents more than 5% of Beijer Alma's consolidated revenues
9.
Durable pricing power: The customized, low-cost nature of the company's springs relative to the customer's total system cost creates extremely high switching costs and stable pricing
10.
Niche technological trading: Beijer Tech concentrates on specialized B2B industrial niches, such as manufacturing laminated bus windshields and automated battery acid filling machines
11.
Consolidated market leadership: Beijer Components remains one of the largest spring manufacturers globally and is the only player with an active, programmatic consolidation agenda
12.
High-return industrial niches: Beijer Tech targets capital-efficient, repeat-purchase business models with minimal central overhead, allowing acquired companies to maintain local specialist brands

Other side of the story

13.
Other side of the story: Increased focus on the industrial springs segment over chassis springs risks structural margin erosion because industrial components have lower historical profitability and are highly exposed to volatile global factory utilization rates

Section 5 – The possible investment case 10 findings

Section summary
  1. The forward investment case is highly compelling because of a structural re-decentralization that is unlocking hidden margin potential and accelerating high-ROIC Nordic acquisitions.

Forward earnings drivers

1.
Operational margin restoration: The primary driver is a targeted restoration of Beijer Components' EBITA margin back to 20% from the 15.0% recorded in FY2025
2.
Highly plausible target: Restoration plausibility is high as margins reached 19.4% in the division's chassis springs subsegment during Q1 2026, though failure could occur if raw material inflation outpaces price hikes
3.
Accelerated Tech acquisitions: The second driver is compounding EBITA growth by deploying excess capital into Beijer Tech, which has historically generated superior capital efficiency
4.
Proven roll-up execution: Acquisition plausibility is high as Beijer Tech has executed over 25 transactions since 2019, though failure could bite if deal competition drives acquisition multiples past historical levels
5.
Strategic component diversification: The third driver is widening Beijer Components' M&A scope to acquire general industrial component businesses rather than restricting deals solely to spring manufacturers
6.
High-risk geographic expansion: Broadened M&A plausibility is medium as the team targets European markets, though failure would occur if the division struggles to integrate non-spring product lines
7.
Lean overhead model: The transition to a pure decentralized compounder model will permanently lower corporate headquarters costs, boosting net margins

Bull case and compound probability

8.
Independent growth engines: Achieving the bull case of 25%+ IRR does not require all drivers to fire simultaneously because Beijer Tech's serial acquisitions and Beijer Components' margin improvements operate as two independent cash-generative engines

Other side of the story

9.
Other side of the story: Re-decentralization efforts could stall if the board fails to recruit a permanent CEO who genuinely shares Johnny Alvarsson's passion for local subsidiary autonomy, leaving the group with bloated central costs

Section 6 – Valuation 8 findings

Section summary
  1. The shares look highly attractive because the stock trades at an unwarranted 15% discount to its Swedish serial acquirer peer group despite showing superior near-term earnings momentum.
1.
Undemanding absolute multiples: Beijer Alma trades at a 2026e EV/EBITA of roughly 15x, representing a notable discount to its historical five-year average multiple of approximately 18x
2.
Deep discount to peers: The company's 15x EV/EBITA multiple stands roughly 15% below the Swedish compounder peer group average of 18x, which includes proven names like Addtech and Indutrade
3.
Generous dividend yield: The Board proposed a dividend of SEK 4.00 per share for 2025, reflecting a 42% payout ratio and a stable 1.38% dividend yield at current prices
4.
Compelling target price upside: The sell-side consensus target price was raised to SEK 335 in April 2026, implying an attractive 15.91% upside and a target multiple of 17.5x EV/EBITA
5.
Mispriced market consensus: The market is currently pricing in a weak industrial cycle, completely ignoring the margin-accretive structural changes and self-funded growth opportunities within Beijer Alma's decentralized portfolio
6.
Strong institutional support: Major institutional holders, including Lannebo and various Wall family foundations, hold a consolidated 34% of outstanding equity, aligning long-term incentives
7.
Other side of the story: If industrial production in Europe remains depressed for longer than expected, the valuation is highly likely to de-rate toward Swedish small-cap manufacturing peers at roughly 14x EV/EBITA

Section 7 – Key risks and the other side 8 findings

Section summary
  1. The overall risk profile is highly manageable as the company's local-for-local footprint and aftermarket diversification provide robust protection against macroeconomic and tariff-related shocks.

Macroeconomic and M&A risks

1.
High industrial cyclicality: The single largest risk is macroeconomic sensitivity, as approximately 77% of consolidated revenues are directly exposed to general global industrial production trends
2.
Dilutive acquisition track record: Programmatic M&A carries execution risk, illustrated by the historical poor performance of Alcomex in the Netherlands, which dragged down Lesjöfors' overall return on capital
3.
Minimal short-seller interest: No published short-seller reports or unusually high short interest are currently active on the shares, reflecting strong institutional trust in the corporate equity story
4.
Inflationary margin pressure: A rapid rise in steel and energy costs could compress earnings if the decentralized local units fail to pass price increases through to diversified end customers
5.
False friend signal: A sudden surge in Chassis Springs revenue could mask underlying structural decay in the larger Industry segment, as chassis replacement sales are highly dependent on unusually harsh northern European winters

Early warning metrics

6.
Key operational warning sign: Investors should closely monitor consolidated capital efficiency as a critical early-warning metric, as any drop below the 45.9% level would indicate deteriorating working capital management

Other side of the story

7.
Other side of the story: Bear concerns regarding tariff exposure are largely overstated because Beijer Alma's localized, local-for-local manufacturing strategy means 95% of products sold in regions like the US are produced domestically

Section 8 – Management: what is changing 6 findings

Section summary
  1. Leadership is a significant source of positive change right now, with acting CEO Johnny Alvarsson successfully re-decentralizing operations and cutting corporate overhead.
1.
Rapid CEO succession: Following Oscar Fredell's abrupt departure in January 2026 after only two months in office, board member Johnny Alvarsson assumed the role of acting President and CEO
2.
Restored decentralization philosophy: Alvarsson, the former highly successful CEO of Indutrade, is actively re-decentralizing operations and cutting corporate overhead built up by previous management
3.
Strengthened financial leadership: Peter Forslund assumed the CFO role in 2025, partner-steering alongside Alvarsson to implement rigorous capital allocation targets across the entire decentralized portfolio
4.
Under-the-radar governance shift: The board has designated local division managing directors to lead their own regional M&A pipelines, shifting execution ownership closer to the market
5.
Restructured division leadership: Ola Tengroth departed as President of Lesjöfors in 2025, allowing Johnny Alvarsson to step in directly as acting President of Beijer Components to drive immediate operational turnarounds
