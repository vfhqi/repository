KEY FINDINGS

- Core momentum is structural, not cyclical. H1 FY2026 core revenue grew 17.3% to £316.1m even against a base that included early Space Marine 2 royalty tailwind — proving the underlying hobby engine is accelerating, not flattering.
- Licensing is volatile but strategically transformative. FY2025 delivered record £50m licensing (2.5x prior year) driven by Space Marine 2's 7m+ copies; H1 FY2026 licensing dropped 46.8% YoY to £16m against that distorted comp. The Amazon/Cavill TV deal, if it lands, could reset the licensing floor permanently — but the timeline is opaque.
- The tariff hit is real but manageable. US tariffs (primarily on China-sourced terrain/accessories) cost approximately £12m annually. GAW's response — 3.5% price increases effective October 2025 — was swift, decisive, and apparently well-absorbed by customers. Gross margin held at 69.4% in H1 FY2026, up 190bp.
- Factory 4 is a capacity and capability signal. The 63,000 sq ft facility due summer 2026, combined with a new dedicated paint plant at Easter Park and 62 injection moulding machines (up 7 in H1 FY2026 alone), signals a multi-year investment cycle in manufacturing that is demand-led, not speculative.
- 3D printing is a growing structural irritant, not yet an existential threat. A massive STL leak in November 2025 produced 180,000 downloads before takedown. GAW's legal action (160+ enforcement cases in 2024, lawsuit naming 1,000+ models in January 2025) is active but IP enforcement at this scale requires sustained legal resource and is imperfect.
- The independent trade channel is the growth engine. Trade now accounts for 61.2% of net product sales, grew 21.7% in H1 FY2024-25, and expanded from 7,200 to 8,100 retailers in FY2025. This is the least-discussed but most important structural growth lever.
- Geographic diversification is accelerating, with Asia as the frontier. South Korea first-party stores targeted for FY2026; Japan team "adding key jobs"; Asia-Pacific contributing from a small but rapidly growing base. The Gundam Assemble launch in 2026 is the first credible cultural competitor in Japan.
- Kevin Rountree's leadership model is an asset and a concentration risk. Twelve years at the helm with a demonstrably consistent strategy and exceptional financial delivery. Low-profile, no public market-signalling — but succession planning is entirely opaque.
- ROCE of 191% (FY2025) is extraordinary by any measure. The combination of asset-light retail (single-staff stores), in-house manufacturing scale efficiencies, and a recurring hobby spend model creates a capital deployment machine that has compounded at ~36% operating profit CAGR over a decade.
- The base effect in H2 FY2026 is manageable but the licensing comp is not. Full-year FY2026 licensing is unlikely to repeat the £50m FY2025 figure; the company has guided to a lower licensing contribution. Core growth is the story — and the release cadence (Emperor's Children codex, Kill Team drops, The Old World pipeline, AoS 4th Ed battletomes) looks dense enough to sustain momentum.

1. BRIEF COMPANY DESCRIPTION

  • Games Workshop Group plc is the world's largest designer and manufacturer of tabletop miniature wargames, headquartered in Nottingham, UK. Its primary IP properties are Warhammer 40,000 (science-fiction setting) and Warhammer Age of Sigmar (fantasy setting), supplemented by Horus Heresy, Warhammer: The Old World (relaunched January 2024), Necromunda, Blood Bowl, and Kill Team among others.

- The business model is an integrated IP flywheel: GAW designs characters, rules, and lore; manufactures plastic miniatures in its own Nottingham factories using proprietary injection moulding; distributes through its own retail estate and a global network of independent trade retailers; and licenses its IP to video game developers, book publishers, and increasingly television producers. Every link in the chain is majority-controlled.

  • Revenue splits into two segments: core (physical miniatures, paints, books, hobby accessories) and licensing (royalties from video games, novels, other IP exploitation). In FY2025, core revenue was approximately £567m and licensing £50m of a total £617.5m.

- The core revenue model is split across three channels: (1) Trade — selling at wholesale to 8,100+ independent retailers (FLGSs) in 71 countries, accounting for ~61% of net product revenue; (2) Retail — 570 company-owned stores globally, each typically staffed by a single manager, accounting for ~23%; and (3) Internet — direct online sales via the Warhammer webstore, accounting for ~16%.

  • The single-staff store model is a structural choice, not a cost constraint. Each store functions as a demonstration and recruitment hub rather than a high-throughput retail unit, with the manager expected to introduce new players to the hobby and convert them to long-term spending hobbyists. The economic logic is customer lifetime value, not per-transaction margin.

  • Over the last three years (FY2023–FY2025), total revenue grew from £461.6m to £617.5m — a three-year CAGR of approximately 10.3% on total revenue and approximately 13-15% on core revenue. Operating profit grew from approximately £150m to approximately £265m over the same period, demonstrating material operating leverage.

  • Looking to the next three years, the company's stated investment priorities are: manufacturing capacity expansion (Factory 4, paint plant, injection moulding investment), continued store openings (~35/year in North America, Europe, Asia), licensing IP development (Amazon TV deal), and product range deepening across its core game systems. The company does not issue forward guidance beyond qualitative statements.

- Licensing revenue is inherently lumpy and project-dependent. FY2025's £50m was driven almost entirely by Space Marine 2's exceptional launch performance. The structural licensing trajectory depends on (1) video game pipeline depth, (2) progress of the Amazon TV series, and (3) GAW's own willingness to expand its licensing portfolio, on which it remains selectively conservative.

- GAW reports in GBP sterling but earns significantly in USD (North America the largest single market at an estimated 40-45% of revenue), EUR (Continental Europe ~25%), and increasingly in JPY and other Asian currencies. FX is a material swing factor and the company has historically hedged selectively rather than systematically.

Change Rating: GREEN — The business model is stable and well-established. The N3Y trajectory is continuation of established themes with acceleration in licensing optionality. No structural model change underway.

2. KEY COMPONENTS SUMMARY

- (1) Leadership: Kevin Rountree remains CEO (appointed January 2015). No CFO change signalled. Board composition stable. No activist pressure visible. Rountree received a restricted share award under the 2025 plan (14,178 shares, vesting December 2027), aligning his interests with 3-year performance.

  • (2) M&A/Corporate: No acquisitions completed or signalled in L18M. GAW has historically been entirely organic. The Privateer Press Warmachine IP was sold to Steamforged Games in June 2024 — a competitor's distress, not GAW's activity. No divestiture, JV, or partnership of scale announced.

  • (3) Margins: FY2025 gross margin ~70.8%; H1 FY2026 gross margin 69.4% (+190bp YoY on H1 FY2025). Operating margin approximately 42-43% at group level in FY2025. Tariff headwind of ~£12m annual is partially offset by 3.5% price increase. Guidance implies ~2% gross margin compression from tariffs in FY2026 absent further offsets.

  • (4) Shareholder returns: Dividends 225p paid in H1 FY2026 (on track for c.520-550p+ full-year total based on subsequent declarations). Total FY2025 dividend was 520p. No share buyback programme. Policy: dividends from truly surplus cash only. H1 FY2026 declared dividends-to-date reach £4.85/share. Profit share of £20m distributed equally across all employees in FY2025 (up from £18m).

- (5) Demand/revenue growth: Core revenue +17.3% in H1 FY2026; FY2025 core +14.7%. Growth is multi-driver: new product cadence, independent retailer expansion (from 7,200 to 8,100 accounts in one year), new store openings (~30-35/year), and new geography penetration. North American sales +14.6% in H1 FY2025-26 to £51.7m.

  • (6) Company goals/targets: GAW does not issue formal multi-year financial targets. Qualitative goals: (a) grow and recruit new hobbyists worldwide; (b) protect and develop IP; (c) invest in manufacturing to meet demand; (d) continue conservative balance sheet management; (e) return surplus cash via dividends. The company explicitly avoids market cap management or financial engineering language.

- (7) Competitor/political/regulatory disruption: US tariff regime is the most material near-term disruption. 3D printing piracy is a slow-burn structural irritant. Gundam Assemble launch in 2026 is the first culturally credible competitor for GAW's Japanese market. Age of Sigmar 4th Edition required retiring 82 kits — a demand disruption for existing collectors that must be managed carefully.

  • (8) Other macro/industry: UK National Living Wage increase to £12.75/hour adopted (above statutory minimum). Consumer discretionary environment weakening in UK (retail sales -0.7% October 2024) but Warhammer has demonstrated resistance. FX headwinds from £/$ rate are ongoing; USD is the largest single-currency exposure.

Change Rating: YELLOW — Multiple active change vectors (tariffs, licensing lumpiness, factory investment cycle, Amazon deal progress) but none individually threatening. Net read is positive change with manageable headwinds.

3. CHANGE IN LEADERSHIP

  • Kevin Rountree has been CEO since January 2015, making him one of the longest-tenured CEOs in the UK mid-cap universe at over eleven years in role. He joined GAW in March 1998 as an Assistant Group Accountant, worked across multiple management roles including Head of Sales (Other Activities), and knows the business operationally at every level. His tenure predates the dramatic profit re-rating and he has been the consistent strategic voice throughout.

- Rountree's leadership style is deliberately low-profile. He avoids public appearances, social media, and media engagement. GAW holds no analyst calls; instead, it publishes short narrative statements alongside financial results and invites investors to read the annual report. This is a doctrinal choice — the company has repeatedly stated it does not operate to short-term market expectations.

  • The 2025 restricted share award (14,178 shares, vesting December 2027) represents a meaningful but not extravagant long-term incentive aligned to continued compound performance. The structure is notable for its simplicity — no complex TSR or EPS-relative conditions visible from public disclosures.

  • No CFO change has been flagged in public disclosures over the L12M review period. Rachel Tongue, who previously held CFO responsibilities, remains in the investor relations and financial reporting function. GAW's board structure is notably simple: executive directors plus a small number of non-executives, consistent with the company's general aversion to corporate complexity.

- Second-level management changes are not publicly disclosed by GAW in any granular form, which is consistent with the company's general culture of institutional rather than individual identification. The Nottingham manufacturing and design functions appear stable based on operational output continuity.

  • The company's culture is distinctive and deliberately preserved. The profit share of £20m (FY2025, up from £18m), paid equally to every employee regardless of seniority, is both a retention mechanism and a cultural signal. Staff turnover at approximately 17% compares to a UK average of approximately 33%, indicating the culture is economically effective as well as philosophically held.

  • The employee base is growing in absolute terms in line with expansion: Factory 4 is expected to add 37 new manufacturing jobs; the Japanese team is actively hiring; South Korea requires new store management capability. This organic headcount build is consistent with expansion without M&A.

- Succession planning is the most significant governance opacity. Rountree is the sole named executive face of the company. The board has not publicly disclosed a succession plan, and with a founder-era culture and deliberately low external profile, the pipeline of credible internal successors is unknowable from the outside. Any CEO change would likely be a material market event given how much of the investment thesis rests on strategic continuity.

- There is no evidence of activist shareholder pressure, governance challenges, or board composition disputes in the L18M period. The major institutional holders appear content with returns and strategy.

- Warhammer Studio leadership (game design, miniature design, lore development) is not publicly identified but appears stable based on the consistent output quality and accelerating release cadence. The studio's creative output is the commercial engine and its health is signal-worthy.

Change Rating: GREEN — Leadership is stable, incentivised, culturally aligned, and operationally consistent. The only risk flag is succession opacity, which is a structural feature rather than an active change.

4. CHANGE IN STRATEGY/STRUCTURE

  • GAW's strategy has not changed in any fundamental sense under Rountree: make the best miniatures in the world, sell them profitably through controlled channels, protect the IP, invest manufacturing capacity to meet demand, and return surplus cash. The absence of strategic pivots is itself a strategic choice — and one that has compounded extraordinarily.

- The clearest strategic evolution in the L12-18M period is the acceleration of the licensing programme from a secondary revenue stream to an actively managed growth pillar. The Amazon TV deal (finalised creative guidelines December 2024) and the company's public commentary that it is "looking for the next Space Marine 2" signals a deliberate shift from licensing-as-passive-royalty to licensing-as-franchise-builder.

- The geographic expansion into Asia — particularly South Korea (first-party stores planned FY2026), Japan (active recruitment, ongoing store programme), and continued growth through existing offices in Shanghai, Hong Kong, Malaysia, and Singapore — represents an extension of the existing playbook into new addressable markets, not a model change.

- Manufacturing investment is the most significant capital allocation change in the current period. Factory 4 (63,000 sq ft, summer 2026 completion), the new Easter Park paint plant, 62 injection moulding machines (seven added in H1 FY2026 alone), £20.1m invested in the Warhammer Studio, and £7m in tooling in FY2025 alone. The company has described the capex programme as running "for at least five years," which is unusually specific guidance from a company that issues almost none.

  • Product strategy has seen deliberate broadening of the game range. The Old World relaunch (January 2024) — reviving the beloved predecessor to Age of Sigmar — was a risk, successfully executed. Age of Sigmar 4th Edition (July 2024) was a major edition reset requiring retirement of 82 kits, executed with clear player communication. These are large, complex product transitions that require significant operational coordination.

  • Warhammer+ (the subscription streaming and content service at £5.99/month or £49.99/year) remains a small but strategically interesting initiative. It functions as a community retention and depth tool rather than a revenue line at present. New animated content (including the Astartes sequel teased for 2026) could raise its profile.

  • Retail store expansion continues at approximately 30-35 net new stores per year, targeting geographies where trade channel density is insufficient. 570 stores as of FY2025 (up from ~540 the prior year). The single-staff model creates capacity for continued expansion without proportionate overhead growth.

- There is no evidence of any asset sales, non-core disposals, JVs, or M&A pipeline activity. GAW remains entirely organically focused. This is unlikely to change under current leadership.

- The company's stated investor proposition has not changed: disciplined capital allocation, manufacturing reinvestment, organic growth, dividend returns, no financial engineering. This simplicity may look under-ambitious to some investors but it has produced extraordinary returns over a decade.

Change Rating: YELLOW — Strategy is stable but the licensing pillar and manufacturing capex cycle represent meaningful evolution in intensity and scale rather than direction. The N3Y period will test whether licensing can become a durable, growing revenue component or remains event-driven and lumpy.

5. CHANGE IN PRIORITIES/AMBITION

  • CEO commentary in the FY2025 annual report (July 2025) expressed clear ambition for continued global recruitment of new hobbyists: "Our aim is simple — to make the best fantasy miniatures in the world, sell them globally and make money." This has not changed. What has changed is the confidence and scale of the delivery aspiration behind that statement.

- The phrase "clearly we are looking for the next Space Marine 2" (Rountree, post-FY2025 results) marks a visible uplift in licensing ambition. This is the first time GAW has publicly articulated appetite for more video game blockbusters rather than treating each licensing deal as a discrete, conservative transaction.

  • Manufacturing ambition has been concretely scaled up. The description of the capex programme lasting "at least five years" is unusually forward-looking for GAW and implies the board is convinced that demand will run ahead of existing capacity through the late 2020s.

- On geographic ambition, the South Korea initiative (language translation team hired, first-party stores planned) and the continued Japanese expansion (culturally important market with strong existing hobby traditions) signal that the "rest of world" growth vector is being actively resourced rather than passively served via trade accounts.

- Rountree's commentary on new hobbyist recruitment consistently emphasises the video game-to-hobbyist conversion pipeline as a key growth driver. Space Marine 2's 7m+ copies sold created a large new audience exposed to Warhammer 40,000 IP; converting even a fraction of that audience to physical hobby purchases has a multi-year demand tailwind.

- The company's ambition with respect to the Amazon TV series is characterised by caution rather than hype: Rountree noted in the FY2025 report that he would "leave Amazon to manage their own businesses." This is disciplined — GAW is not staking financial projections on an uncertain creative project — but also signals that GAW views the deal as a long-duration option rather than a near-term revenue driver.

- Employee standards and culture standards show continuity in ambition: the profit share distribution (equal, unconditional, £20m in FY2025), the above-minimum-wage floor (£12.75/hour), and the 3% pay uplift for FY2025-26 all reflect a consistent operating philosophy that treats staff as co-owners of outcomes rather than cost centres.

- There is no evidence of cost-cutting, rationalisation, or restructuring ambition. The company's cost base has grown in line with revenue, with the deliberate investment in infrastructure (factories, moulding machines, retail network) indicating a business planning for growth, not consolidation.

- The one area where ambition appears unchanged — perhaps frustratingly for some investors — is M&A and inorganic growth. GAW has consistently declined to pursue acquisitions, even when adjacent IP or distribution assets have come to market. This discipline has been vindicated by organic returns but forecloses certain growth optionality.

Change Rating: YELLOW-GREEN — Ambition is increasing in licensing and manufacturing, consistent in retail, and unchanged in core philosophy. The intensity of the investment cycle and the licensing pivot represent a genuine gear-change, even if the overall strategic framing remains constant.

6. FINANCIAL PROFILE

  • FY2025 (year ended June 1, 2025): Total revenue £617.5m (+18% YoY from £525.7m). PBT £262.8m (+29.5% from £203m). EPS 595p (+29.6% from 459p). Full-year dividend 520p. Gross margin approximately 70.8%. ROCE 191% (up from 176% in FY2024). Best-ever results in company history.

  • H1 FY2026 (six months ended November 30, 2025): Total revenue £332.1m (+10.9% YoY). Core revenue £316.1m (+17.3%). Licensing revenue £16.0m (-46.8%, as Space Marine 2 comps annualised). Gross margin 69.4% (+190bp on H1 FY2025's 67.5%). PBT £140.8m (+11%). EPS 319.9p (+10.7%). H1 dividends declared 225p. Management described this as a "record half-year" despite the steep licensing decline — testament to the core business strength.

- Long-run financial track record: Core revenue 10-year CAGR approximately 17-18%. Operating profit 10-year CAGR approximately 36%. ROCE has ranged from 40% (2015 trough) to 191% (FY2025 peak). The ROCE compounding is extraordinary: a business that earns 191p of profit before tax for every £1 of capital employed is structurally different from any peer in the leisure goods sector.

- Gross margin trajectory: The structural improvement in gross margin from the mid-60% range (FY2020-21 era) to the 69-71% range (FY2024-26) reflects three forces: (1) volume-driven manufacturing efficiencies from higher throughput on fixed-cost injection moulding infrastructure; (2) reduction in air freight usage and normalisation of sea container rates post-COVID; (3) pricing power, with annual price increases of approximately 4-5% absorbed without visible demand destruction.

- Tariff impact quantification: US tariffs (primarily on terrain and accessories manufactured in China) are estimated at approximately £12m annually. Management estimated £8m impacted H1 FY2026 directly (six-month period). The 3.5% price increase effective October 2025 partially offsets this. Management guidance implies approximately 2% gross margin compression from tariffs in FY2026 absent full offset — implying FY2026 gross margin may land nearer 68-69% versus 70-71% underlying.

- Operating cost base: Core operating expenses in H1 FY2024-25 were 32.0% of core revenue (down from 34.1% prior year), demonstrating genuine operating leverage. Key cost components include: staff wages (profit share £20m FY2025), retail store network costs (predominantly single-staff — low per-unit overhead), and logistics/distribution.

  • Capital expenditure cycle: FY2025 capex: £20.1m Warhammer Studio + £7.0m tooling + £1.0m moulding machines = approximately £28m. With Factory 4 construction ongoing (63,000 sq ft, summer 2026), the FY2026 capex is likely to be materially higher — potentially £35-45m estimate — before normalising. The company describes this as a "five-year" investment programme, suggesting elevated capex through FY2030.

  • Licensing revenue volatility: FY2024 licensing £31m → FY2025 licensing £50m (+61%) → H1 FY2026 licensing £16m (annualised run-rate ~£32m). The company has explicitly guided that FY2025's £50m is not expected to be repeated in FY2026. The Amazon TV deal, if it generates revenue, is unlikely to be in FY2026 (no production start date confirmed). The licensing revenue line will likely normalise to £30-40m range absent a new video game blockbuster.

- Cash generation and conversion: GAW is a structurally cash-generative business with modest working capital requirements and limited debt (historically managed on a net-cash or minimal net-debt basis). The dividend policy of paying "truly surplus cash" has resulted in a progressive dividend history: FY2025 total 520p, H1 FY2026 run-rate suggesting £5-6 total for the full year.

  • Shareholder returns concentration: Returns are almost entirely through dividends; no buyback programme disclosed. At current yield (~1.5-2% on share price of approximately £90-100 range), the yield is low relative to absolute payout because the share price has re-rated significantly. The absolute payout is the more informative metric.

Change Rating: YELLOW-GREEN — Financial profile is outstanding and improving. The near-term headwinds (licensing comp, tariff absorption, elevated capex) are manageable and temporary. The structural margin and ROCE trajectory is positive over three years.

7. VALUE CHAIN — DEMAND

  • Customer base: The Warhammer hobbyist is a high-commitment, high-spend individual: average basket sizes are large relative to disposable hobby income, and the hobby generates ongoing, habitual purchases (new armies, paints, supplements, event tickets). The community — organised through clubs, tournaments, and online forums — is self-reinforcing and creates social switching costs.

- New customer acquisition pipeline: Space Marine 2 created a documented funnel of 7m+ video game players newly exposed to Warhammer 40,000 IP. GAW's conversion strategy — in-store painting events, starter kits, YouTube/TikTok tutorial content — is aimed at pulling these players into physical hobby. The conversion rate from video game player to miniature purchaser is unknown but even 2-3% of 7m represents 140,000-210,000 new hobbyists, each with lifetime spend potential in the hundreds of pounds.

- Pricing power: GAW has implemented annual price increases of approximately 4-5% for multiple consecutive years. In October 2025, an approximately 3.5% increase was implemented specifically to offset tariff costs. Demand elasticity appears low — there is no publicly observable revenue decline following price increases, and gross margin expansion over the same period suggests volume has not been sacrificed for price.

- Trade channel dynamics: Independent retailers (8,100 accounts in 71 countries as of FY2025) grew by 900 net accounts in one year. Trade represents 61.2% of product revenue and grew 21.7% in H1 FY2025. The trade channel is controlled: GAW sets terms and conditions, does not discount to trade accounts (unlike most consumer goods businesses), and requires retailers to hold a curated range. This maintains brand equity and price discipline downstream.

- The single-staff store model creates a different demand dynamic to conventional retail. Store managers are hobbyists first, salespeople second. Their role is demonstration, education, and community building — not volume throughput. The economic model is one of converting browsers to committed hobbyists, whose subsequent spending will be spread across the store, trade retailers, and the webstore. This multi-channel conversion approach means the company's retail estate is more a marketing asset than a pure revenue engine.

- Like-for-like store performance: In H1 FY2025-26, 72% of owned stores delivered positive like-for-like growth. This is a healthy figure in a consumer discretionary environment where many UK retailers are experiencing flat to negative LFL. The metric suggests the hobby spending trend is more durable than the broader retail environment.

  • Digital channel: The Warhammer webstore (internet sales, ~16% of product revenue) is growing alongside the other channels. The digital channel is increasingly important as GAW expands into markets where store density is low and trade network is thinner — it serves as the first point of sale access in underpenetrated geographies.

  • Warhammer+ subscription: At £5.99/month, the subscription platform is a community-retention tool. Subscriber numbers are not publicly disclosed, which is itself signal — if the subscriber base were a material revenue contributor, it would feature more prominently in financial reporting. Its value to the business is likely in deepening hobbyist engagement and reducing churn from the physical hobby rather than as a standalone revenue line.

  • Age of Sigmar 4th Edition kit retirement: Retiring 82 kits to launch the new edition (July 2024) created demand volatility — collectors of discontinued armies (Beasts of Chaos, Bonesplitterz, Sacrosanct Stormcast) faced product obsolescence. This is a recurring edition-change risk and was managed with lead-time clearance sales, but it introduces periodic demand disruption for specific customer segments.

  • Release cadence as demand driver: GAW's weekly pre-order cycle (every Saturday, 10am UK) creates a habitual purchasing rhythm among committed hobbyists. The denseness of the FY2026 release calendar — Emperor's Children codex, Kill Team Dead Silence, Kill Team Shadowhunt, Maelstrom narrative waves, ongoing Old World and AoS battletome releases — sustains this habitual engagement through the year.

Change Rating: GREEN — Demand is strong, multi-channel, and structurally supported by new customer funnel (Space Marine 2), trade network expansion, and consistent release cadence. No demand weakness signals. The most notable demand risk is pricing fatigue over a multi-year horizon — not yet visible.

8. VALUE CHAIN — COMPETITION

- Market structure: Games Workshop is categorically dominant in tabletop miniature wargaming. No single competitor approaches its scale, brand recognition, retail presence, product breadth, or manufacturing capability. The closest named competitors — CMON, Mantic Games, Warlord Games, Privateer Press (now in distress after selling Warmachine IP to Steamforged Games in June 2024) — operate at a fraction of GAW's revenue and profit.

  • CMON Limited (Hong Kong): CMON is a crowdfunding-heavy miniature game publisher (Zombicide, Blood Rage, Song of Ice and Fire). Its model relies on Kickstarter to finance development and pre-sell inventory risk. CMON does not operate retail stores and has limited shelf presence outside specialist hobby stores. Its products compete at the margin for hobbyist wallet share but do not directly contest the Warhammer IP loyalty effect.

  • Mantic Games : Mantic explicitly positions as a value alternative to Games Workshop. Its Kings of War system is analogous to Warhammer Fantasy/Old World; Deadzone competes with Kill Team. Mantic's total revenue is estimated at £10-15m — a rounding error relative to GAW. Its value proposition attracts price-sensitive customers that GAW either converts upward or loses to cheaper alternatives, but Mantic does not have the brand depth to compete at the top of the hobby spend curve.

  • Warlord Games : Focus on historical wargaming (Bolt Action, Black Powder, Pike & Shotte). Adjacent to GAW's hobby universe but not a direct IP competitor. Growing through licensed historical and popular culture IPs (Doctor Who, The Walking Dead) but remains a niche operator.

  • Atomic Mass Games / Asmodee: Star Wars: Legion and Marvel Crisis Protocol are scale products with major IP backing. These compete for hobbyist time and wallet share (both require miniature assembly and painting) but operate in very different IP universes. The Asmodee distribution network gives these products strong independent retailer penetration — the same network GAW uses — creating a potential shelf space competition at the retail level.

- Privateer Press (distress signal): The June 2024 sale of the Warmachine IP to Steamforged Games represents the effective exit of one of GAW's most significant historical IP competitors. Warmachine was once cited as a genuine alternative to Warhammer 40,000 in terms of competitive play. Its failure removes a competitor from the top tier and may redirect hobbyist attention back toward GAW's systems.

- Gundam Assemble (Bandai Namco, 2026): The most significant new competitive development in the last 18 months. Bandai is launching an original Gundam miniature wargame (not a model kit — a genuine wargame) in 2026. The Gunpla domestic market in Japan is enormous, and Bandai has the brand recognition, distribution network, and manufacturing scale to compete seriously in Japan specifically. This is the first credible culturally-native competitor for GAW in its fastest-growing Asian market.

  • Barriers to entry: GAW's moat is sustained by IP depth (decades of lore, thousands of miniature designs), manufacturing scale (62 injection moulding machines, in-house tooling), retail network lock-in (single-staff model creates community anchors), and the network effect of an established global tournament and events scene. Replicating any one of these is achievable for a well-capitalised competitor; replicating all simultaneously is not.

- Second-hand/grey market: eBay and specialist secondary market platforms (eBay, Facebook Groups, Vinted) are significant for budget hobbyists who purchase used miniatures. GAW does not earn licensing revenue from secondary market sales and does not appear to view this market as a material threat — it may function as an entry point that eventually converts buyers to new product purchases.

Change Rating: GREEN-YELLOW — Competitive structure is highly favourable. The Gundam Assemble threat in Japan is worth monitoring but is speculative. The Privateer Press exit is a mild positive. Structural barriers remain intact and are being deepened by the manufacturing investment cycle.

9. VALUE CHAIN — DISRUPTION

- 3D printing: the structural threat. Consumer 3D printing technology (resin and FDM) has reached a quality level where motivated hobbyists can produce miniature-quality prints at home. The barrier is no longer technology — entry-level resin printers are available for £200-400 — but model files. GAW's competitive moat against 3D printing rests on (1) IP protection of its STL files and (2) the complexity and detail achievable only through professional injection moulding and multi-part kits.

  • The November 2025 STL leak was a material event. Hundreds of Games Workshop models were uploaded to Cults3D and received approximately 180,000 downloads before GAW enforced takedown. High-value centre-piece models (Imperial Knights, Titans, Horus Heresy Mastodon) were among the leaked files. This was not a small piracy incident — it was a coordinated, large-scale IP breach.

  • GAW's legal enforcement response is active but imperfect. In 2024, GAW pursued over 160 global sellers for IP violations, including many China-based operations. A lawsuit filed January 2025 named over 1,000 models. MyMiniFactory, a platform that had received some enforcement requests, pushed back citing insufficient evidence, before the case was filed formally. The legal strategy is sound but enforcement is whack-a-mole at internet scale.

  • Official 3D printing response: absent. Unlike some competitors (CMON has offered digital bundles), GAW has not offered any official STL or digital miniature download service. The hobbyist community has repeatedly signalled willingness to pay for official GW digital files. The strategic logic of not doing so appears to be: (a) protecting injection moulding margins; (b) avoiding channel cannibalisation of the physical product that drives licensing, community, and store traffic. This is a defensible position but one that is becoming harder to sustain as printer quality improves.

  • The irony: GAW itself uses 3D printing extensively in its design and prototype process — the technology is embedded in the creation workflow. The threat is not 3D printing per se but 3D printing as a distribution vector for pirated IP.

  • Technology disruption in video games: The success of Space Marine 2 (7m+ copies, £50m licensing contribution in FY2025) demonstrates that video game execution can create an IP moment that dramatically expands the commercial surface area of the Warhammer universe. Conversely, a high-profile video game failure (there have been many licensed Warhammer titles of varying quality over the years) creates reputational risk for the IP. GAW's current strategy is to license selectively and maintain creative involvement in major projects.

  • Resin casting (recasting): A parallel disruption to 3D printing is the recasting industry — typically China-based operations that produce near-identical copies of GAW miniatures in lower-quality resin at 30-50% of retail price. This is an established problem that predates 3D printing. GAW's enforcement activity in China appears to be ongoing and is one driver of the 160+ enforcement cases in 2024.

  • Potential future disruption vectors: AI-assisted miniature design could eventually commoditise character and creature sculpting, reducing the creative barrier to entry for competitors. This is a medium-term (5-10 year) risk rather than an imminent one, but the speed of AI capability improvement warrants monitoring.

Change Rating: YELLOW — 3D printing is escalating from nuisance to material structural risk. The November 2025 STL leak and the scale of enforcement activity in 2024-25 indicate the threat is growing. GAW's current defensive posture (legal enforcement, manufacturing quality leadership) is rational but does not resolve the underlying tension. An official digital product strategy is the most likely medium-term adaptation.

10. VALUE CHAIN — POLITICAL AND REGULATORY

- US tariffs: the most immediate and material political risk. The Trump administration's 2025 tariff regime has directly impacted GAW. Most GAW plastic miniature production is in the UK (exempt from the highest tariff tiers), but terrain, accessories, and some kit components are manufactured in China. The estimated annual tariff impact is approximately £12m (£8m in H1 FY2026 alone as costs ran ahead of price increase implementation timing).

  • Price response was decisive. GAW implemented a 3.5% average price increase effective October 2025, weighted toward character kits (~5%) and other kits (~4.6%). This is a larger-than-usual single-year increase (historical average ~4-5% but spread across the full range rather than concentrated). The fact that H1 FY2026 core revenue still grew 17.3% suggests demand elasticity is low and the price increase has not suppressed volumes.

- Supply chain geography creates ongoing tariff optionality. GAW's UK-based injection moulding manufacturing is the core competitive advantage; it is also the tariff shield. If US-China trade tensions persist or escalate (additional tariff rounds), GAW's UK production advantage becomes a structurally more valuable differentiator versus Asian-sourced competitors.

- China operational risk beyond tariffs. GAW has offices in Shanghai and Hong Kong and relies on Chinese logistics partners for part of its Asia distribution. A deterioration in UK-China or US-China trade relations could create secondary risk to this infrastructure — not an immediate concern but a relevant scenario to stress-test.

  • UK regulatory environment: The UK National Living Wage increase to £12.75/hour (adopted by GAW for FY2025-26) is a regulatory cost driver. GAW has proactively moved above the statutory minimum as a culture signal, meaning future statutory increases are unlikely to create step-change cost increases.

  • IP law and enforcement: GAW is an intensive user of trademark and copyright law globally. The January 2025 lawsuit and the 160+ enforcement actions in 2024 represent significant legal expenditure. Cross-border IP enforcement is operationally complex and the legal landscape differs materially across US, EU, UK, and Asian jurisdictions.

  • GDPR and data regulation: GAW operates a growing direct-to-consumer digital channel (webstore, Warhammer+) across multiple jurisdictions. Data privacy compliance costs are growing as digital revenues expand. No specific regulatory action or violation has been reported.

  • Consumer product safety: Miniatures contain small parts and are subject to toy safety regulations in multiple markets. The shift toward increasingly complex, multi-part injection moulded kits increases the surface area of potential regulatory compliance. Age-gating (most products are marketed to adults and older teenagers) provides some protection.

- ESG and supply chain transparency: There is growing investor and regulatory pressure around supply chain transparency, including China-sourced components. GAW's partial China manufacturing exposure means it cannot be fully insulated from ESG-driven investor concerns about supply chain ethics in the N3Y period.

Change Rating: YELLOW — The US tariff regime is an active and material headwind. GAW's UK manufacturing base is a structural mitigant. The broader political environment (US-China tensions, UK trade policy post-Brexit) creates ongoing uncertainty that is not fully controllable by management. Watch H2 FY2026 gross margin for tariff absorption signals.

11. VALUE CHAIN — SUPPLY SIDE

  • Vertical integration is the defining supply-side characteristic. GAW designs in-house (Warhammer Studio), manufactures in-house (Nottingham factories), and sells in-house (retail stores and webstore). This eliminates the principal agent problems and margin leakage that characterise most consumer goods supply chains.

- Injection moulding estate: 62 injection moulding machines as of H1 FY2026 (up 7 in the six-month period, up significantly from prior years). Each machine represents significant capital investment and ongoing tooling cost. The tooling itself — the metal moulds from which plastic sprues are produced — is a capital-intensive proprietary asset. GAW invested £7.0m in moulding tools and £1.0m in tooling/milling machines in FY2025 alone.

  • Factory 4: The 63,000 sq ft facility under construction at Willow Road, Lenton (adjacent to existing campus) is scheduled for summer 2026 completion. The primary stated purpose is to centralise packing operations currently split between two existing factories. Staffing will grow from 13 to approximately 50 (37 new jobs). The facility also expands overall manufacturing throughput capacity.

  • Easter Park paint plant: GAW's dedicated Citadel Paint manufacturing facility has undergone renovation and now has new office space and manufacturing infrastructure installed. The Citadel Paint range — reportedly manufactured in a 3,000-litre tank facility — is a significant product category. Paint is a high-margin, recurring consumable that every hobbyist purchases continuously throughout their hobby lifetime, making it one of the more attractive revenue lines in the product mix.

- Factory 5 — paint dedicated: Spikey Bits has reported GAW is planning a fifth dedicated manufacturing facility specifically for paint production at Easter Park. If confirmed, this would represent GAW effectively splitting its paint manufacturing into a standalone factory operation — a significant scale-up of paint production capacity in line with the growing hobbyist base.

  • Five-year expansion programme: The company has described its manufacturing investment programme as running "for at least five years" — approximately FY2026 to FY2031. This is unusually specific forward guidance from a company that publishes minimal financial forecasts. It signals the board's confidence that demand will sustain above current manufacturing capacity for the medium term.

- China-sourced supply risk: Terrain kits and some accessories are manufactured in China. These are the products most exposed to US tariffs. In the medium term, there may be a commercial case to reshore production to Nottingham if tariffs persist, though the cost-benefit would depend on the relative unit economics of UK injection moulding versus China contract manufacturing for lower-complexity products.

  • Raw materials: The principal raw material for plastic miniatures is polystyrene pellets (for injection moulding) and acrylic compounds (for paints). Both commodities experienced input cost inflation in the post-COVID period but normalised through FY2024-25. The company noted in FY2025 annual report that it secured "reliable prices on transport and materials" — suggesting forward purchasing or supplier arrangements are providing stability.

  • Logistics and distribution: GAW operates intercompany logistics from Nottingham to regional distribution centres. The reduction in air freight usage (replaced by sea freight) has been a meaningful cost driver in gross margin improvement. Sea freight container rates normalised post-COVID and GAW has captured this as margin improvement.

  • Supplier concentration: Given the high degree of vertical integration, GAW's external supplier base is primarily for raw materials and packaging rather than finished goods. This reduces third-party concentration risk significantly compared to an outsourced manufacturing model.

Change Rating: GREEN — Supply side is a source of competitive advantage rather than risk. The manufacturing investment cycle is capacity-adding rather than cost-driven, and the vertical integration model is being deepened, not diluted.

12. REVENUE/DEMAND CYCLE

- Is Warhammer genuinely recession-resistant? The evidence over the last five years strongly suggests yes, with nuance. During COVID (FY2021), GAW benefited from an extraordinary demand spike (hobbyists at home with disposable income and time). Post-COVID normalisation occurred in FY2022-23, but the business did not reverse — it consolidated at a higher base. Through UK consumer discretionary weakness in 2024-25 (retail sales volumes -0.7% October 2024), GAW continued to grow.

  • The hobby engagement model creates demand stickiness. A committed Warhammer hobbyist has sunk costs (armies built and painted, rules learned, community relationships formed) that make switching out of the hobby genuinely costly in social and emotional terms. This sunk cost effect creates demand persistence through economic downturns that discretionary purchases without community dimensions do not enjoy.

- The H1 FY2026 "off-year" framing is important. Management described H1 FY2026 as more challenging in licensing terms due to Space Marine 2 comps, and yet delivered record half-year results on core business. This "off-year" framing sets a conservative reference point for H2 FY2026 comparisons and for FY2027 — where the base will be clean.

  • Release cycle drives revenue periodicity. GAW's revenue is not evenly distributed through the year. Major product launches (new game editions, army codexes, limited-edition sets) create quarterly peaks. The H1/H2 split has historically favoured H2 (containing the Christmas gifting period), but edition launch timing can shift this materially. The Age of Sigmar 4th Edition launch was July 2024, in H1 of that year, inflating the H1 FY2025 core revenue base.

- Warhammer 40,000 11th Edition signals: Community speculation and partial roadmap leaks suggest a potential 10th to 11th Edition transition for Warhammer 40,000 could be approaching within the N3Y period (possibly FY2027-28). Edition changes are revenue-inflecting events: they generate a burst of starter kit and core rulebook purchases, refresh army ranges, and create media attention. The timing of any such transition would materially shape FY2027-28 revenue trajectory.

- Predictability of core revenue: Strip out licensing and the core business has demonstrated remarkable predictability: sequential quarterly growth across multiple years, driven by the combination of pricing, volume from new hobbyists, and channel expansion. The predictability is not perfect — it is subject to release timing and macro — but it is far higher than most consumer discretionary businesses.

- Geographic diversification reduces cyclicality. With revenue now spread across North America (~40-45%), Continental Europe (~25%), UK (~20%), and growing Asia, a regional consumer downturn in any one market (e.g. UK recession) is partially offset by other regions. North America, being USD-denominated, also provides a natural hedge against UK cost inflation.

- The licensing revenue cycle is the opposite of predictable. Licensing is entirely dependent on the success of third-party products over which GAW has limited control post-licensing. FY2025's £50m was exceptional; normalised run-rate appears to be £30-40m absent a new blockbuster. The Amazon TV deal — when it arrives — could create another exceptional licensing year but the timing is completely opaque.

Change Rating: GREEN-YELLOW — Core revenue cyclicality is low and the hobby model has demonstrated genuine recession resistance. Licensing revenue is inherently volatile but is a secondary revenue line, not the primary driver. The main cyclical risk is a prolonged consumer downturn that eventually penetrates even committed hobby spending — not yet visible.

13. VALUE CHAIN — MACROECONOMIC

- UK consumer environment: UK real consumer spending growth has been weak through 2024-25, with headline retail sales volumes declining in late 2024. The cost-of-living pressure on UK households is real. GAW has not shown any visible demand impact from this, consistent with its customer base being relatively affluent, committed hobbyists rather than casual occasional purchasers.

  • US consumer environment: The US is GAW's largest single market (~40-45% of revenue). US consumer confidence has been volatile in 2025-26 against the backdrop of the Trump tariff regime, equity market volatility, and policy uncertainty. US hobbyist spending on Warhammer has remained strong — North American core sales grew 14.6% in H1 FY2025-26 to £51.7m. This is the single most important macro variable to watch.

- FX — GBP/USD: GAW reports in GBP and earns significantly in USD. A stronger pound against the dollar reduces reported sterling revenues without impacting underlying demand. The FX translation effect has been a marginal headwind in some periods and a tailwind in others. With US revenues ~40-45% of total and manufacturing costs overwhelmingly sterling-denominated, the GBP/USD rate is a significant P&L swing factor.

  • FX — GBP/EUR: European revenues (~25% of total) are also subject to GBP/EUR translation. The pound has been relatively stable versus the euro through the review period but remains a variable.

- UK National Living Wage increases: The statutory NLW has increased significantly in successive years. GAW's proactive adoption of a floor above NLW (£12.75/hour as of FY2025-26) means regulatory increases are absorbed in the existing cost base rather than requiring incremental uplift. The 3% general pay award for FY2025-26 is the primary employee cost driver.

  • Input cost inflation: Raw material costs (polystyrene, acrylics) have normalised after the COVID inflation spike. Transport and logistics costs have also normalised — GAW specifically referenced "reliable prices on transport and materials" in FY2025 reporting, suggesting this is no longer a margin headwind.

- Consumer discretionary category risk: Warhammer miniatures are, at some level of abstraction, a discretionary purchase. In a severe recession, even committed hobbyists may reduce their hobby spend. However, the historical record (through the 2008-09 financial crisis, through COVID, through the post-COVID cost-of-living crisis) suggests that the hobby demonstrates unusual resilience versus most consumer discretionary categories.

- Tariff regime — second-order effects: Beyond the direct £12m annual tariff cost, there are second-order macro effects: US tariffs are inflationary for US consumers, reducing real discretionary income and potentially suppressing hobby spend at the margin. If the tariff regime persists or expands, the demand-side effect could eventually exceed the direct cost effect.

- Geopolitical risk: While not an immediate operational concern, GAW's growing presence in China (Shanghai office, Hong Kong distribution) creates geopolitical sensitivity. A US-China military or trade confrontation would have asymmetric supply chain impacts on businesses with China operational presence.

Change Rating: YELLOW — The macroeconomic environment has deteriorated relative to the post-COVID tailwind period but has not materially impacted GAW's performance. The most material single macro variable is US consumer spending trajectory given the scale of North American revenue. FX and UK labour costs are ongoing but manageable variables.
