EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (BLUF)

Section 1 – BUSINESS MODEL + ORGANISATION 9 findings

Core Business Architecture and Revenue Engine

1.
Family-controlled ownership model: Puig Brands operates under a dual-class share structure where the Puig family retains 93.2% of voting rights despite holding approximately 50% of economic interest after the IPO
2.
Three-segment revenue structure: Puig generates revenues across Fragrance & Fashion (approximately 70% of net revenues), Makeup (20%), and Skincare & Wellness (10%), anchored by Charlotte Tilbury
3.
Charlotte Tilbury integration: Charlotte Tilbury is the group's largest single brand asset, with Puig holding a majority stake and the remaining interest subject to put/call options valued at approximately €900 million

Brand Architecture and Portfolio Configuration

4.
Prestige licensed portfolio: Puig's fragrance portfolio includes licenses for Carolina Herrera, Nina Ricci, Rabanne, Jean Paul Gaultier, Dries Van Noten, and Christian Louboutin alongside owned niche brands such as L'Artisan Parfumeur and Penhaligon's
5.
Acquisition-led brand scaling: Puig has pursued a structured M&A strategy to build its beauty portfolio, acquiring Charlotte Tilbury in 2020, Dr. Barbara Sturm in 2023, and Byredo in 2022
6.
Dr. Sturm dermo-cosmetic integration: The €360 million acquisition of Dr. Barbara Sturm in 2023 was designed to anchor Puig in the fast-growing dermo-cosmetic category alongside Charlotte Tilbury

Leadership and Organisational Structure

7.
Family leadership continuity: Marc Puig serves as Chairman and CEO, maintaining direct family executive control over strategic direction and capital allocation
8.
Founder-led brand operational model: Charlotte Tilbury retains her role as President, Chairman, and Chief Creative Officer within the Puig group, with rumoured change-of-control clauses introducing complexity for any future transaction
9.
Spanish HQ with global distribution: Puig operates from its Barcelona headquarters and distributes across 150 countries through a network of approximately 20,000 points of sale

Section 3 – INDUSTRY STRUCTURE 6 findings

Selective Fragrance Market Architecture

1.
Global selective fragrance size: The global selective fragrance market is estimated at $40 billion to $50 billion, growing at a 5.4% compound annual rate driven by premiumization and fragrance wardrobe behaviour
2.
Market share concentration: L'Oreal holds a dominant 22% global market share in selective fragrance, with Puig occupying the number two position at approximately 11.1%, ahead of Coty at 9% and LVMH at 8%
3.
Market share trajectory: Puig's selective fragrance market share declined from 11.5% in FY2024 to 11.1% in FY2025, a 40 basis point erosion during a period of strong overall category growth

Premium Beauty Market Dynamics

4.
Prestige vs. mass bifurcation: Premium beauty outpaces the mass market structurally, with fragrance premiumization driving trade-up from mass to selective across all major geographies
5.
Charlotte Tilbury market position: Charlotte Tilbury has emerged as one of the fastest-growing prestige makeup brands globally, with its makeup category recording strong double-digit organic growth in FY2025 despite macroeconomic headwinds
6.
Dermo-cosmetic category growth: The clinical skincare market is growing at 10% to 15% annually in North America, with Dr. Barbara Sturm serving as Puig's primary vehicle for this category exposure

Section 9 – OPERATOR + FOUNDATION QUALITY 9 findings

Governance and Capital Allocation Framework

1.
Dual-class voting structure: The Puig family controls 93.2% of voting rights through a dual-class share structure, which limits public shareholder influence over capital allocation decisions including M&A strategy and executive compensation
2.
Put-call acquisition model: Puig's M&A architecture relies on structured put-call option agreements to acquire founder-led brands in stages, balancing control acquisition with deferred cash outflow, as exemplified by the Charlotte Tilbury (€900m residual), Byredo, and Dr. Sturm transactions
3.
Charlotte Tilbury residual stake: The remaining Charlotte Tilbury minority interest is valued at approximately €900 million through put-call options running through 2031, representing the single largest committed capital outflow in Puig's financial outlook

Management Quality and Track Record

4.
Family stewardship track record: The Puig family has built the business organically and through disciplined acquisition over four generations since 1914, with the Charlotte Tilbury acquisition in 2020 representing the most transformative strategic decision in the modern era
5.
Capital return policy: Puig declared a dividend of €0.60 per share for FY2025, representing a conservative payout ratio relative to reported free cash flow of €664 million, reflecting a preference for retaining capital for deferred acquisition liabilities
6.
ROIC profile: Puig's return on invested capital stands at approximately 13%, compared to L'Oreal's exceptional 30% ROIC, reflecting the capital intensity of the put-call acquisition model and ongoing brand investment requirements

Moat Assessment – Mechanical vs. Heroic

7.
Mechanical moat sources: Puig's structural advantages include (1) brand heritage and consumer loyalty in the Carolina Herrera and Jean Paul Gaultier franchises; (2) long-standing retailer relationships with premium shelf placement rights; (3) proprietary fragrance formulation capabilities through in-house perfumers and ingredient partnerships
8.
Heroic moat dependency: The Charlotte Tilbury brand's market position is structurally dependent on Charlotte Tilbury's personal brand authority, social media presence, and creative direction, representing a concentration of value creation in a single non-replaceable individual
9.
Moat widening trajectory: Whether Puig's moat widens mechanically (through brand portfolio compounding and distribution deepening) or requires ongoing heroic execution (through founder-dependent creative output and acquisition execution) represents the core governance risk for public investors

Plausible problems to monitor 4 findings

1.
Market share contraction in core fragrance: Puig's global selective fragrance market share declined from 11.5% in FY2024 to 11.1% in FY2025, representing a 40 basis point erosion despite otherwise strong organic growth. While Puig maintains the number two global position, this contraction occurred during a period when peer L'Orã©al delivered 4% organic growth and maintained its dominant 22% share, and even weaker competitor Interparfums achieved 2% sales growth in Q1 2026. The share loss suggests that Puig is ceding ground to competitors in its core category anchor during heightened promotional intensity. [Cross-ref: Section 3 + Section 7]
2.
Excessive wholesale channel exposure: Wholesale partners account for approximately 90% of Puig's net sales, creating structural vulnerability to retailer inventory management and promotional pressure. This concentration is materially higher than the industry trend toward direct-to-consumer integration, where leading beauty houses are vertically integrating downstream into owned retail to control pricing and consumer data. The recent Amazon Premium Beauty launch for Charlotte Tilbury illustrates channel friction risks, as Amazon takes sales margins and pressures brands on discounts while physical retailers rebase inventory levels in response to digital channel launches. The company's limited direct-to-consumer footprint leaves it disproportionately exposed to downstream gatekeepers who can unilaterally impose promotional participat ion or reduce shelf space allocation. [Cross-ref: Section 5 + Section 6]
3.
License reclamation structural threat: Luxury fashion houses are increasingly reclaiming fragrance and beauty licenses to vertically integrate downstream, exemplified by Kering's completed €4 billion beauty transaction with L'Orã©al that includes the eventual transfer of the Gucci beauty license after Coty's contract expires in mid-2028. This trend poses a major structural threat to third-party beauty operating models that rely on fashion house licensing agreements. While Puig mitigates this risk by owning 17 core brands outright, the company's reliance on licenses like Christian Louboutin exposes it to similar long-term renewal and exit risks. The demonstrated willingness of luxury conglomerates to pay multi-billion euro premiums to reclaim beauty operations suggests that license renewal negotiations will face materially higher economic hurdles as contracts mature. [Cross-ref: Section 7]
4.
Eponymous founder concentration and key person risk: The heavy concentration of eponymous brands introduces distinct key person risk, as loss of key creators or negative founder publicity coul d severely impact long-term brand equity, with founder Charlotte Tilbury remaining actively involved as President, Chairman, and Chief Creative Officer of her brand. Jefferies noted that rumored change-of-control clauses in Tilbury's contract introduced material liability during recent merger talks, with remaining put-call options valuing the outstanding stake at approximately €900 million. This structural dependence on individual creator identities creates binary operational risk and restricts strategic flexibility in ways not present at more diversified peers like L'Orã©al, which maintains an exceptional 30% ROIC through decades of brand compounding across a broader portfolio. The contractual constraints and change-of-control provisions embedded in founder agreements limit P uig's ability to pursue transformative transactions or pivot brand positioning without triggering costly buyout provisions. [Cross-ref: Section 1 + Section 9]

Outliers worth noting 1 findings

1.
Extreme Asia-Pacific regional acceleration: APAC revenues rose 21.7% like-for-like in FY2025 and achieved an impressive 26.1% like-for-like sales growth in the first quarter of FY2026, representing extraordinary regional momentum that diverges sharply from mature market performance.
