§4 — Accounting + Governance Red Flags

J-front verdict: SSE's accounting and governance profile is relatively clean for a large-cap UK utility, but non-GAAP adjustments are material and recurring, and capitalisation practices in the Renewables division warrant scrutiny [#J]

  • Non-GAAP gap: SSE reports "adjusted EPS" and "adjusted operating profit" that systematically exclude items characterised as non-recurring — in practice, these excluded items (fair value gains/losses on derivatives, restructuring charges, early bond redemption costs) recur annually [#A]
  • Magnitude: Adjusted EPS is typically 15-25% above IFRS statutory EPS — peer median for European utilities: 10-20% gap; SSE sits within but toward the top of that range [#A]

  • Bear view: The persistence of "non-recurring" items suggests they are operational in nature; investors anchoring on adjusted EPS are potentially overestimating recurring earnings power by 15-20% [#A]

  • Adjustments increasing: The quantum of adjustments has grown in absolute terms as the balance sheet expands — more derivatives hedges, more bond issuances, more project-level restructurings — creating a widening gap between statutory and adjusted metrics [#A]

  • Peer anchor: National Grid shows a similar pattern; Drax's adjustments became controversial when biomass impairments were excluded — SSE not yet in Drax territory but trajectory warrants monitoring [#A]

  • Capitalisation aggressive — Renewables: SSE capitalises development costs and interest during construction for offshore wind assets — standard industry practice, but the scale of construction in progress (multi-billion CIP balance) means any impairment event would flow through materially [#A]

  • Bear view: If an offshore wind project is delayed or cancelled, the capitalised development costs (hundreds of millions per project) would require impairment — not visible in adjusted EPS metrics [#A]

  • Peer anchor: Ørsted took a £2.5bn+ impairment on US offshore wind projects in 2023; SSE's UK/Irish portfolio is more advanced but the structural risk is analogous [#A]

  • Revenue recognition: SSE's energy generation revenue under CfDs (Contracts for Difference) is relatively transparent — strike price vs reference price mechanism is disclosed — minimal revenue recognition ambiguity in the regulated networks businesses [#A]

  • Partial flag: The Wholesale/Trading segment uses mark-to-market fair value accounting on energy derivatives — creates earnings volatility that management adjusts out, reducing transparency [#A]

  • Related-party: No material related-party transaction concerns identified from public disclosures — SSE's major joint ventures (Dogger Bank with Equinor/Vårgrønn, various SSE Renewables JVs) are with major counterparties at arm's length; no insider-enrichment red flags found [#A]

  • Board independence: SSE's board meets UK Corporate Governance Code independence standards — majority independent NEDs, separate Chair/CEO roles, independent Audit Committee — no flagged governance breach [#A]

  • Mild concern: Long board tenures for some NEDs (>9 years) create technical independence questions under UK CG Code — disclosed but not unusual for FTSE 100 [#A]

  • Auditor change: No recent auditor change — KPMG has been SSE's auditor for an extended period; audit tender processes occur but no surprise auditor change flag [#A]

  • Note: Extended auditor relationships in large UK corporates are flagged by governance monitors — SSE disclosure of audit tender timing is publicly available [#I]

  • Material weakness: No material weakness in internal controls disclosed in SSE's annual reports — clean audit opinions throughout the publicly accessible period [#A]

  • Mgmt comp structure: SSE's long-term incentive plan is TSR and EPS-linked — executive remuneration has been periodically flagged by ISS/Glass Lewis as above UK utility peer median, particularly for the CEO [#A]

  • Bear view: Incentive structure rewards EPS growth (achieved partly through non-GAAP adjustments) and absolute TSR — could create pressure to make accretive-looking acquisitions or delay impairments to protect adjusted EPS [#A]

  • NWC liability: Working capital dynamics in SSE's Networks business are relatively stable — regulated businesses have predictable receivables profiles [#A]

  • Renewables WC: Construction phase creates large payables to contractors; any delay in CfD strike price receipts would pressure liquidity, though SSE's revolving credit facility provides buffer [#A]

  • Receivables aging: No disclosed receivable concentration or aging concern in SSE's accounts — Networks revenue is Ofgem-regulated and effectively sovereign in credit quality; Renewables CfD revenue is government-backed [#A]

  • Pending lawsuit: SSE faces routine regulatory and commercial litigation — no disclosed material class-action or enforcement action outstanding as of most recent annual report [#A]

  • Historical note: SSE's 2011-2013 mis-selling of energy contracts (retail business, now divested as OVO) resulted in regulatory fines — retail mis-selling risk is extinguished with the Ovo disposal [#A]

  • Regulator action: Ofgem has engaged SSE on several network performance and investment delivery metrics — not enforcement actions but performance commitments under RIIO framework create financial exposure if targets are missed [#A]

  • Bull rebuttal (sceptical): The non-GAAP gap, while persistent, is standard practice for European energy utilities managing large derivative books and CIP balances; KPMG's clean audit opinions across multiple years provide external validation that capitalisation and recognition practices are within IFRS bounds; the impairment risk is real but flagged in disclosures [#J]

  • Bull thesis #4: SSE's dividend is progressive and well-covered — income reliability is a core pillar [#J]
