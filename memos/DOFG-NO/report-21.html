Dimension 1 — Market structure (canonical):
- Standard: market structure analysis in a Q21 covers industry concentration (HHI or CR4/CR8), pricing power dynamics, capacity utilisation trends, entry/exit barriers, and historical margin dispersion across the cycle
- Peer-anchored: without ticker or sector, no peer HHI or margin dispersion data can be cited; resubmit with sector to enable CR4 anchoring
- Edge signal: market structure analyses frequently miss the second-tier competitive tier — the 5th-through-10th ranked players who are too small to feature in sell-side coverage but whose capacity additions or exits often drive the inflection point the analyst cares about

Dimension 2 — Customer dynamics (canonical):
- Standard: customer concentration (top-10 customer % of revenue), switching cost evidence, contract length and renewal visibility, NPS or churn proxies, and price sensitivity vs value-in-use
- Peer-anchored: UK/Europe-focused concentrated investors (the reader profile) typically care most about B2B customer dynamics where contract visibility supports EPS trajectory modelling; consumer-facing dynamics require different evidence
- Edge signal: customer dynamics analyses frequently under-weight the procurement function change signal — when a customer company changes its CPO or procurement head, historical evidence suggests contract renegotiation probability rises materially within 12-18 months

Dimension 3 — Competitive landscape (canonical):
- Standard: competitive positioning maps (price/quality), recent share shift evidence (revenue growth differentials), product/service differentiation durability, and new entrant threat assessment
- Peer-anchored: without cohort peer data (D-BATCH-25 gap flagged), competitive positioning cannot be peer-anchored; any grading will be A-through-F against sector base rates only
- Edge signal: competitive landscape analyses frequently over-weight the announced competitive threat (the new entrant with press coverage) vs the quiet competitive degradation from existing players improving their offering incrementally; the latter is often more damaging to the thesis

Dimension 4 — Regulatory environment (canonical):
- Standard: current regulatory framework, pending regulatory changes (consultation papers, draft legislation), enforcement history, and regulatory cost-of-compliance trajectory
- Peer-anchored: UK/EU regulatory environment is materially more active than US equivalents in several sectors (financial services, energy, telecoms, data); sector-specific regulatory risk must be assessed against the sector's regulatory trajectory, not a generic business-risk baseline
- Edge signal: regulatory analyses frequently miss the implementation gap — regulations are announced but enforcement lags by 18-36 months; this creates a window where the well-prepared incumbent can build compliance infrastructure as a competitive moat before smaller competitors can afford to

Dimension 5 — Technology / disruption (canonical):
- Standard: technology adoption S-curve position, disruption threat vector (substitution vs displacement), R&D intensity vs peers, and patent / IP moat durability
- Peer-anchored: without sector, no R&D intensity peer benchmarking is possible; resubmit with sector
- Edge signal: technology disruption analyses frequently mis-time the threat — the disruption is real but the timeline is 5-7 years not 2-3 years; the analyst who acts too early on a disruption thesis pays an opportunity cost; the analyst who acts too late owns the disrupted incumbent through the inflection

Dimension 6 — Historical precedent + cross-sector analogues (canonical):
- Standard: what has happened to comparable companies in comparable situations? Base rates from historical precedents are often the most robust anchors available when company-specific data is thin
- Peer-anchored: historical precedent analysis for a DD-stage KQ should draw on at minimum 3-5 analogues with named citations; without knowing the KQ, analogues cannot be selected
- Edge signal: cross-sector analogues are frequently the most informative source — the analyst focused on, say, UK industrials should look at what happened to US industrials 5-7 years earlier when similar dynamics played out; geographic lag is a systematic and exploitable pattern

Sceptical bullet: the bearish read on the dimension-mapping apparatus is that it imposes a false sense of comprehensiveness — covering 6 dimensions doesn't mean the 7th dimension (the one that actually matters for this specific KQ) has been identified; the analyst must resist the checklist comfort and ask "what dimension is entirely absent from this mapping?"

§4 — LANDSCAPE MAPPING: INVESTMENT CASE STRUCTURE + PILLAR INTERACTION

  • Structurally load-bearing: a DD-stage KQ is by definition on the critical path of the investment case; understanding how the KQ interacts with the five standard pillars (Business Quality, Management, Financials, Valuation, Risk) is essential before the landscape mapping begins

Pillar interaction (generic DD-stage pattern):

Business Quality pillar:
- Standard: KQs that land under Business Quality typically concern moat durability, market share trajectory, or structural demand drivers; these are the most consequential KQs for a concentrated long-only investor because they determine whether the thesis has a 3-year or a 10-year runway
- Adjacent: Business Quality KQs are often adjacent to Management KQs — the quality of the moat is often a reflection of the quality of the capital allocation decisions that built it

Management pillar:
- Standard: KQs under Management typically concern track record reliability (do they deliver on guidance?), incentive alignment (are compensation structures aligned with long-term value creation?), or succession (is the key-person risk priced in?)
- Edge signal: management KQs are frequently resolved not by what management says but by what management does under pressure; capital allocation during a downcycle is the most informative signal, and it's often buried in 5-7 year old annual reports that no one has read recently

Financials pillar:
- Standard: KQs under Financials typically concern earnings quality (cash conversion, working capital dynamics, capitalisation policies), balance sheet resilience (leverage at trough earnings), and margin trajectory (what's the exit-rate margin at the end of the investment horizon?)
- Peer-anchored: without ticker, no specific financial metrics can be cited; the framework is ready

Valuation pillar:
- Standard: KQs under Valuation typically concern relative value (what multiple does the market assign to this earnings trajectory, vs what multiple is warranted given quality and growth?), or absolute value (what's the IRR under base/bull/bear scenarios?)
- Note: a concentrated long-only investor with a 25%+ IRR hurdle and 12-24 month hold has a specific valuation lens — they need the stock to re-rate AND/OR deliver earnings upgrades within the hold period; KQs that only resolve on a 3-5 year horizon are less actionable

Risk pillar:
- Standard: KQs under Risk typically concern tail risks — regulatory action, customer concentration loss, technology displacement, balance sheet stress; these KQs are often the most important to answer before sizing a position
- Edge signal: risk KQs are systematically under-weighted in bull-market research cycles; the analyst who spends disproportionate time on risk KQs at DD stage is doing the work that generates asymmetric returns when the tail risk doesn't materialise (optionality on avoiding loss)

Sceptical bullet: the pillar interaction framework above assumes the KQ fits cleanly into one pillar; the most dangerous KQs are those that cross pillars — a Business Quality issue that is also a Management issue and also a Financials issue simultaneously; these compound KQs are harder to resolve and more likely to generate false confidence when only one dimension is mapped

Second-order (generic pattern — to be populated with KQ-specific content on resubmission):

  • Second-order — pillar cascade: if the KQ resolves negatively (e.g. moat is weaker than consensus believes), the second-order implication is not just lower earnings but a re-rating of the valuation multiple — the market will simultaneously discount both the earnings trajectory AND the quality premium, producing a compounding downside

  • Second-order — management response: if the KQ reveals a structural headwind, the second-order implication is what management does in response — capital reallocation, M&A, cost restructuring; management response often creates as much value or destruction as the original headwind

  • Second-order — investor base response: if the KQ resolution is negative and widely flagged by sell-side, the second-order implication is forced selling by quality-oriented institutional holders whose mandate prohibits holding companies below a quality threshold; this creates overshoot to the downside that is actually an opportunity for the concentrated investor who can hold through the selling pressure

Third-order (generic pattern):

  • Third-order — competitive response: if the KQ reveals a structural tailwind for the company, the third-order implication is competitor response — increased investment, price aggression, product acceleration; the tailwind erodes as competitors respond, and the investor who enters late pays the full tailwind-priced multiple while capturing only the residual

  • Third-order — regulatory attention: if the KQ reveals the company is taking significant market share (a positive first-order), the third-order implication is regulatory attention — CMA or EU competition authority scrutiny; dominant market position attracts regulatory friction with a 2-4 year lag

M/M/O applications (Means/Motive/Opportunity):

  • M/M/O application 1 (generic — activist scenario): activist investor with Means (capital + execution capability) + Motive (undervalued asset with identifiable value-unlock levers) + Opportunity (passive institutional ownership concentration that can be mobilised) has all three legs; the KQ's resolution determines whether the activist thesis is coherent; for most UK mid-caps, the Opportunity leg is the binding constraint (UK passive penetration is lower than US, making activist campaigns harder to win)

  • M/M/O application 2 (generic — strategic acquirer scenario): strategic acquirer with Means (balance sheet capacity + synergy rationale) + Motive (the KQ reveals a capability gap the acquirer cannot build organically) + Opportunity (standalone company is at a valuation trough that makes acquisition economics compelling) — this is the M&A optionality scenario that the concentrated long-only investor should always price; it is systematically under-weighted in bear-case scenarios

  • M/M/O application 3 (generic — management capital misallocation): management team with Means (free cash flow + revolving credit facility) + Motive (empire-building incentive, tenure extension) + Opportunity (board that hasn't refreshed its challenge function in 3+ years) — this is the destruction scenario; the KQ's resolution on management quality is load-bearing for sizing how much M/M/O risk to assign to capital misallocation

Cross-pillar implication:

  • Cross-pillar: if the KQ sits under Business Quality but its resolution has direct implications for the Financials pillar (e.g. customer concentration loss reduces revenue visibility which requires balance sheet stress-testing), the analyst should explicitly map the cross-pillar pathway before APM grades

Cross-thematic:

  • Cross-thematic: many DD-stage KQs have cross-portfolio implications — if the same structural dynamic (regulatory change, technology shift, macro inflection) applies to another portfolio position, the KQ resolution should trigger a parallel review of that position's thesis

Sceptical bullet: the second and third-order implication the analyst is most likely flinching from is the one where the investment case is simply wrong at the foundational level — not wrong-in-detail but wrong-in-framing; the obstacle is the way: the most productive question is "what would have to be true for the entire thesis to be misconceived from the start?" and to answer it with the same rigour applied to the bull case; per Marcus Aurelius, the impediment to action is the action — surface the foundational challenge explicitly rather than burying it in scenario weighting
