Section 3 – What the company does 14 findings

Section summary
  1. LSEG operates an incredibly sticky financial infrastructure platform where 73% of revenues are recurring, protected by massive switching costs and strict regulatory moats.

Segment Breakdowns and Offerings

1.
Core Data offering: The Data & Analytics division represents 47% of group revenues, delivering real-time pricing feeds, reference datasets, and the flagship Workspace desktop platform
2.
Specialized indices business: FTSE Russell is a high-margin, top-three global index provider that generates 11% of group revenues through multi-asset benchmarking licenses
3.
High-growth compliance database: Risk Intelligence delivers 6% of revenues through financial crime and screening databases, featuring the industry-standard World-Check asset
4.
Broad Markets division: The Markets segment represents 36% of revenues, operating capital raising venues and leading electronic trading platforms like Tradeweb
5.
Vital clearing solutions: Within the Markets division, LSEG operates the systemically important London Clearing House clearing venues for OTC derivatives

Value Chain and Core Customers

6.
Dominant value positioning: LSEG sits in a highly profit-favoured position in the financial data value chain, controlling proprietary symbology and complex data normalization
7.
Exceptional customer retention: LSEG maintains longstanding strategic relationships with 99% of the top 100 global banks and 75% of the top 100 asset managers
8.
Low concentration risk: LSEG's top 250 customers account for approximately 45% of total revenues, ensuring that no single client can easily threaten the business
9.
Sticky long-term agreements: Over 16% of run-rate Data & Analytics revenue is now locked into multi-year LSEG Data Access agreements of up to seven years

Competitive Moats and Industry Structure

10.
High switching barriers: Data feeds are deeply embedded into bank trading algorithms and risk management systems, making switching costs extraordinarily high
11.
Regulated clearing moat: LCH SwapClear holds over a 90% market share of outstanding global interest rate swap clearing, creating an almost insurmountable network effect
12.
Consolidated market structure: The financial market data and infrastructure industry is highly consolidated, dominated by LSEG, Bloomberg, and S-P Global
13.
Other side of the story: Standardized desktop interfaces face structural threats as artificial intelligence models could allow clients to process raw feeds directly, potentially bypassing physical terminals

Section 5 – The possible investment case 14 findings

Section summary
  1. The forward investment case looks highly robust due to structural data acceleration, exclusive Microsoft integrations, and a massive share buyback tailwind.

Key Structural Growth Drivers

1.
Driver One: Subscription acceleration: Organic subscription revenue is projected to accelerate to 7% by FY27, supported by large multi-year Data Access agreements
2.
Strong plausibility evidence: Plausibility is high because LSEG secured £1.9bn in long-term contracts in Q4 2025 alone, indicating deep institutional commitment
3.
Potential failure mechanism: Competitive displacements by FactSet and S-P Capital IQ in banking desktop segments could impair pricing power
4.
Driver Two: Microsoft partnership: Co-developed products like Teams-based Workspace, Excel add-ins, and Copilot tools are designed to drive high-margin upsell
5.
Medium plausibility evidence: Plausibility is medium because early client feedback was mixed due to usability hurdles, although recent enhancements have boosted engagement
6.
Potential failure mechanism: Slower-than-expected cloud migration spend or delayed product rollouts could defer the anticipated £5bn long-term revenue synergy

AI Innovations and Capital Returns

7.
Driver Three: AI data distribution: LSEG's Model Context Protocol server distributes high-integrity, AI-ready datasets directly to clients' large language models
8.
High plausibility evidence: Plausibility is high as connected and onboarding customers surged to over 150 by Q1 2026, defying fears of AI disintermediation
9.
Potential failure mechanism: Slower adoption of proprietary AI agents by conservative compliance departments could limit initial volume-based add-on yields
10.
Driver Four: Share buyback tailwind: LSEG is executing a massive £3bn share buyback program through February 2027, accelerating EPS growth
11.
High plausibility evidence: Plausibility is high because LSEG completed £1.1bn in Q1 2026 alone, with its balance sheet well-positioned at 1.8x leverage
12.
Potential failure mechanism: Increased borrowing costs or larger-than-expected capital expenditure on core infrastructure could reduce excess cash available for repurchases
13.
Other side of the story: The bull case would fail if macroeconomic headwinds shrink financial sector headcounts, causing consolidated data contract contractures

Peer multiples premium: This represents an unjustified discount to US peers like S-P Global trading at 21.3x 2026 P/E and Moody's at 26.5x 2026 P/E

4.
Strong upside potential: The sell-side median price target sits at 11,900p, implying an attractive 22% upside to LSEG's current share price of 9,748p
5.
Inaccurate market pricing: Reverse free cash flow methodology indicates the market is pricing in a 7.0% CAGR, underappreciating LSEG's projected 8.2% CAGR
6.
Other side of the story: LSEG's multiple may remain compressed if the broader European exchange sector de-rates due to listings bleeding to US markets

Section 7 – Key risks and the other side 9 findings

Section summary
  1. The bear case on AI disruption is overdone because LSEG's proprietary taxonomic moats and systemic clearing venues are extremely difficult to replicate.

Primary Operational and Competitive Threats

1.
Threat of AI disintermediation: Bears argue clients will utilize generative AI to clean and ingest raw data directly, bypassing premium aggregators
2.
Earnings transmission mechanism: A shift to open-source scraping could compress LSEG's Data & Feeds pricing power, directly hitting high-margin recurring segments
3.
Aggressive desktop competition: FactSet continues to capture market share, forcing LSEG to make significant margin-concessions to protect large banking contracts
4.
Earnings transmission mechanism: Contract concessions and desktop seat losses at major banks like Citigroup would directly drag Workflows revenue growth
5.
Post-Brexit clearing threat: The European Union could restrict access to LCH SwapClear if permanent regulatory equivalence is not granted to UK clearing houses
6.
Earnings transmission mechanism: Migration of interest rate swaps to EU exchanges would severely reduce Markets transaction volume and clearing fees

Early Warning Indicators and Safeguards

7.
Crucial monitoring metrics: Investors should closely watch sequential deceleration in organic Annual Subscription Value growth and Workflows contract churn
8.
Other side of the story: The risks are overstated because 98% of revenues are derived from proprietary IP and regulated environments, making AI duplication highly implausible
