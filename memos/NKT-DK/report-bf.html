EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (BLUF) 24 findings

Business transformation and strategic positioning

1.
Pure-play transformation complete: NKT designs, manufactures and installs low, medium, and high-voltage power cables, focusing exclusively on core European and Nordic markets following its 2024 exit from non-core businesses, aiming to maximise profitability across all newly defined business lines
2.
Organizational realignment executed: NKT reshaped its segments in January 2026 into three focused lines–Transmission (handling complex extra-high-voltage subsea and underground interconnectors), Distribution (medium-voltage grids and low-voltage building wires), and Grid Solutions (merging AC onshore projects and high-margin accessories)
3.
Massive backlog visibility secured: The Transmission business relies on long-term, complex turnkey projects, providing immense multi-year visibility through a €13.5bn backlog, while the mid-2024 SolidAl acquisition shifted the local mix heavily toward profitable MV/HV products
4.
Structural demand tailwinds anchored: Commodity price risks are passed on fully in customer contracts, neutralising a major risk factor, while the addressable high-voltage market will persistently exceed €10bn annually between 2024 and 2030, with utility customers prioritising proven execution capabilities over price, creating high switching costs and formidable barriers for new entrants

Financial profile and competitive positioning

5.
Peer-leading margin expansion trajectory: Operational EBITDA margins reached 14.3% in 2025, surpassing Prysmian and Nexans hovering near 11%, while broker Jefferies models a 15% revenue CAGR through 2028, vastly outpacing Nexans at 3% and Prysmian at 6%
6.
Exceptional but vulnerable working capital dynamics: Working capital has structurally inverted to an extreme -35.8% of sales in 2025, driven by massive customer prepayments, while NKT boasts a €963m net cash position equating to -2.5x leverage versus Prysmian's 1.3x net debt
7.
Acute capital intensity crushing near-term cash: Free cash flow collapsed to -€244m in 2025, representing deeply negative conversion compared to Prysmian's robust 40% positive conversion, as capital expenditures consumed 27% of 2025 standard-metal revenue–massively above the 3-4% sector base rate–during NKT's €2bn peak capex cycle through 2028
8.
Artificially inflated return metrics mask underlying weakness: Return on capital employed rebounded to 24% in 2025, but the deeply negative working capital base artificially shrinks capital employed, flattering NKT's headline 24% ROCE against peers, while adjusted ROIC of 8.4% trails Nexans' scale-driven 19% returns

Industry structure and value chain economics

9.
Highly consolidated oligopoly with rational discipline: Global electricity demand is projected to grow at 1.1x real GDP through 2030, while the extra-high-voltage cable market operates as a strict global oligopoly with only five to six global companies capable of manufacturing and installing UHV cables, excluding Chinese domestic players, with NKT sitting firmly within this elite tier competing directly against Prysmian and Nexans
10.
Exceptional manufacturer pricing power secured: New competitors capture negligible market share in the UHV and submarine segments due to formidable barriers, while the elite peer group demonstrates strong pricing discipline over volume-chasing, with severe supply-demand imbalances giving Tier-1 manufacturers exceptional pricing power over utility and hyperscaler customers, ensuring the majority of value-chain profit accrues to top-tier manufacturers rather than distributors
11.
Midstream-dominant positioning with upstream deficit: NKT operates strictly as a pure-play cable solutions provider, focusing entirely on the high-value manufacturing (25-35% gross margin, 10-15% EBIT margin) and turnkey installation stages (15-25% gross margin, 10-20% EBIT margin), but unlike integrated peers Nexans and Prysmian, NKT lacks in-house copper rod mills, leaving it fully dependent on third-party metallurgical suppliers
12.
Structural pricing leverage with risk neutralisation: Severe global shortages of high-voltage cable capacity give NKT immense leverage over downstream utility customers and offshore developers, with constrained Tier-1 manufacturing capacity ensuring cable producers currently dictate pricing and contractual terms, while NKT structurally mitigates upstream leverage by incorporating strict metal-price escalation clauses into almost all customer contracts

Customer concentration and lock-in dynamics

13.
Extreme TSO concentration anchoring revenue: More than 95% of the massive Transmission order backlog is concentrated among European Transmission System Operators, with NKT securing three TenneT contracts worth approximately €1.5bn and a landmark SSEN partnership converting booking commitments into firm orders adding €2bn to the backlog
14.
Extended frameworks and formidable switching costs: Medium-voltage framework agreements are structurally lengthening, shifting from short-term spot deals to 5-8 year secured commitments including an eight-year Alliander agreement running from mid-2026 to mid-2034, while high-voltage cable systems require strict type and factory tests, with NKT's unique position as a turnkey provider creating formidable operational lock-in
15.
Aggressive pricing leverage actively utilized: NKT fully mitigates commodity price risks by incorporating strict metal-price escalation clauses into almost all customer contracts, with severe industry-wide high-voltage capacity shortages giving NKT exceptional pricing power to negotiate favourable contract terms, leading to structural margin expansion over time for contracts won after 2022

Competitive dynamics and vertical integration threats

16.
Rational oligopoly with Asian entry threats: Prysmian and Nexans dominate the global landscape alongside NKT, forming an elite Tier-1 oligopoly commanding the extra-high-voltage market, with the Tier-1 European trio maintaining dominant market share, effectively dividing the unprecedented surge in regional grid and interconnector awards, while NKT and Nexans each hold multi-year backlogs exceeding €10bn, functionally removing aggressive market-share grabbing from current industry dynamics
17.
Margin discipline persists despite integration deficit: Across the high-voltage oligopoly, pricing discipline remains exceptionally robust as players prioritise margin expansion over aggressive volume-chasing, with TSOs prioritising execution reliability allowing NKT to bake healthy risk premiums into complex turnkey contracts without losing share, though Nexans' vertical integration into upstream copper metallurgy provides a distinct supply chain advantage that NKT lacks, with NKT remaining dependent on external metallurgical processors and capturing less of the total upstream margin pool
18.
Cross-value-chain signals confirm bullish narrative: Prysmian's recent commentary confirms strict prioritisation of margin expansion over volume growth mirroring NKT's strategy, while Nexans explicitly pursues a value-over-volume approach spurning dilutive growth projects to preserve industry pricing power, with Prysmian's Renewable Transmission segment delivering robust 19.4% margins in early 2026, validating NKT's own high-voltage margin expansion trajectory

Operator quality and mechanical improvement path

19.
Proven operator executing focused strategy: CEO Claes Westerlind brings deep domain expertise from his prior role running the Karlskrona high-voltage hub, with the new "Charging Forward" strategy aggressively targeting more than €900m EBITDA and >22% ROCE by 2030, having delivered nine consecutive quarters of double-digit organic growth and EBITDA expansion
20.
Exceptional production moat driving mechanical margin accretion: The concentrated Karlskrona facility provides massive logistical and cost advantages over Prysmian's distributed four-site European footprint, positioning NKT to deliver the fastest earnings growth in the sector with a 28% EPS CAGR 2025-2028, organic revenue forecast to grow at a 15% CAGR through 2028 doubling peers, and targeting a 25% EBITDA margin in the Solutions segment by 2030 well above Prysmian's 20%
21.
Gravitational forces driving mechanical improvement: The structural undersupply of high-voltage cables mechanically pulls NKT's margins upward as utilities accept higher pricing without heroic sales efforts, with the margin expansion thesis highly credible and underpinned entirely by the fixed schedule of the Karlskrona and Cologne capacity additions, requiring only execution rather than new commercial wins as the order backlog already contains the higher-margin contracts

Long-term structural positioning and disruption resilience

22.
Formidable barriers insulating oligopoly: Building high-voltage subsea facilities demands exceptional investment with NKT deploying €1.3bn solely for its Karlskrona expansion and new vessel, while the industry transition to 525kV and 640kV extruded HVDC systems structurally locks out lower-tier manufacturers lacking advanced R&D and testing capabilities, making it exceptionally difficult for new players to win tenders even if they overcome capital barriers
23.
Asian capacity expansion and integration deficit pose material threats: Sumitomo Electric is constructing a new subsea HVDC cable factory in Scotland expected to increase competitive pressure materially when operational in mid-2026, while Nexans and Prysmian operate captive copper rod mills globally, with NKT's pure-play focus leaving it exposed to upstream margin extraction during commodity supercycles
24.
Multi-decade secular demand visibility secured: The addressable high-voltage market is anticipated to average above €10bn annually between 2024 and 2030, with meeting net-zero targets requiring installation of 80 million kilometres of power grids globally by 2040, providing multi-decade volume visibility for Tier-1 cable manufacturers, as NKT's pure-play cable portfolio perfectly aligns with the global macro-thematic shift toward renewable energy integration

Section 1 – Business Model + Organisation 29 findings

Section summary
  1. NKT has completed its pure-play transformation and reorganised into three focused lines, priming the business for substantial scale-driven margin expansion from 2027.

Core business and strategic transition

1.
Description – pure-play cables: NKT designs, manufactures and installs low, medium, and high-voltage power cables, focusing exclusively on core European and Nordic markets
2.
Strategic objective – Charging Forward: The late-2025 'Charging Forward' strategy shifts focus from transformation toward execution and value extraction on a massive order backlog
3.
Strategic objective – value extraction: The strategy relies on three pillars: Execute, Excel, and Evolve, aiming to maximise profitability across all newly defined business lines
4.
Internal change – divestment focus: Following the 2024 sale of NKT Photonics, the company operates as a highly focused, pure-play energy transmission provider

New organisational structure and segments

5.
Internal change – 2026 reorganisation: NKT reshaped its segments in January 2026 to align with turnkey project execution and specific voltage markets
6.
Organisational structure – Transmission: The former Solutions division is now Transmission, handling complex extra-high-voltage (EHV) subsea and underground interconnectors
7.
Organisational structure – Distribution: Formerly Applications, this unit focuses on medium-voltage (MV) grids, data centres, and renewables, alongside low-voltage building wires
8.
Organisational structure – Grid Solutions: This new unit merges AC onshore projects, installation services, and high-margin accessories into a unified turnkey provider
9.
M&A contribution – SolidAl integration: The mid-2024 acquisition of SolidAl is fully integrated as NKT Esposende, shifting the local mix heavily toward profitable MV/HV products

Revenue model and predictability

10.
Revenue model – project-heavy: The Transmission business relies on long-term, complex turnkey projects, providing immense multi-year visibility through a €13.5bn backlog
11.
Predictability – high visibility: Booking commitments and framework agreements, such as the mid-2026 to 2034 Alliander deal, insulate NKT against short-term macro cycles
12.
Cyclicality – construction drag: The low-voltage segment inside Distribution remains subdued and exposed to the sluggish European residential construction market
13.
Revenue mix – service lumpiness: Grid Solutions relies partly on offshore repair works, which offer high margins but introduce quarter-to-quarter revenue volatility
14.
Pricing model – raw material pass-through: Commodity price risks, particularly for vast copper usage, are passed on fully in customer contracts, neutralising a major risk factor

Structural demand and customer behaviour

15.
End market – structural tailwinds: Demand is driven by grid modernisation, interconnectors, offshore wind, and the rapid build-out of power-hungry data centres
16.
Demand trajectory – sustained deficit: NKT anticipates the addressable high-voltage market will persistently exceed €10bn annually between 2024 and 2030
17.
Customer behaviour – track record premium: Utility customers prioritise proven execution capabilities over price, creating high switching costs and formidable barriers for new entrants
18.
Customer behaviour – framework lock-in: Distribution System Operators (DSOs) increasingly utilise long-term framework agreements, securing NKT's medium-voltage volume baseload

Scale economies and capacity expansion

19.
Operating leverage – delayed inflection: NKT's current capacity expansions dilute near-term EBITDA margins by 1-2 percentage points due to ramp-up costs
20.
Scale economy – Karlskrona hub: Concentrating high-voltage capacity in Karlskrona provides significant logistical, labour, and cost synergies compared to distributed footprint models
21.
Competitive advantage – deep integration: In-house manufacturing of joints and accessories, combined with captive cable-laying vessels, gives NKT superior end-to-end margin capture
22.
Scale economy – margin step-up: As the Karlskrona and Cologne expansions complete in 2027, NKT expects massive operational leverage on higher-priced recent contracts
23.
Capital intensity – peaking capex: NKT faces an intense €2bn capex cycle through 2028, after which maintenance capex drops to approximately 4% of revenues

Recent delivery and internal changes

24.
Recent delivery – record margins: FY25 saw record EBITDA of €390m, and Q1 2026 achieved a historical high first-quarter EBITDA margin of 16.0%
25.
Recent delivery – transitional dip: Q1 2026 Transmission revenue fell 8% organically, reflecting the expected ramp-down of the Champlain Hudson Power Express project
26.
Leadership change – CFO transition: CFO Line Fandrup departs in early 2026, replaced by Chief Strategy Officer Michael Yong
27.
Leadership change – divisional heads: The reorganisation prompted GLT adjustments, including Darren Fennell heading Transmission and Raphael Görner joining as EVP of Grid Solutions
28.
Other side of the story: Capacity expansion missteps or offshore wind cancellations could tilt the industry from undersupply to oversupply, destroying NKT's pricing leverage

Section 6 – Supplier + Distributor + Channel Analysis 29 findings

Section summary
  1. NKT relies heavily on concentrated upstream metal suppliers, but robust pass-through clauses effectively neutralise severe commodity price volatility
  2. channel dynamics reveal a stark duality between direct, high-leverage transmission sales and highly competitive, margin-thin distribution markets

Key input dependencies and cost trends

1.
Critical input – conductor metals: Copper and aluminium constitute approximately 50% of NKT's total cost base alongside plastics
2.
Input cost trend – highly volatile: Conductor metals and polymers face persistent price volatility driven by global supply constraints and energy costs
3.
Margin squeeze – structural shield: NKT neutralises this commodity inflation via strict metal-price escalation clauses across almost all major customer contracts

Financial hedge – non-passable risk: For non-project business, the company utilises financial hedging instruments on the LME to protect baseline profitability
5.
Inflation pressure – non-metal costs: Rising transport and energy costs continue to pressure accessories and low-voltage margins where pass-through is harder
6.
Margin squeeze – lag effect: Although escalation clauses exist, sudden intra-month input price spikes create temporary margin dilution before customer pricing can be adjusted

Supplier concentration and bargaining power

7.
Supplier bargaining power – elevated: Mining conglomerates hold significant leverage over NKT due to the highly concentrated nature of global metal supply
8.
Vertical integration – notable deficit: Unlike Prysmian and Nexans, NKT lacks in-house copper rod mills, leaving it fully dependent on external metallurgical suppliers
9.
Scale disadvantage – purchasing volume: Broker Jefferies notes NKT's smaller scale yields lower bargaining power with suppliers compared to its larger peers
10.
