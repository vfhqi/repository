§1 — Current Management Guidance

Lechwerke's current guidance framework (as of FY24 annual results, March 2025) is narrow and range-based, following standard German regulated-utility disclosure convention: revenue guidance absent; EBIT range guided; dividend per share guided; capex guided. Medium-term targets sparse and conditional.

  • J-front verdict: Management has promised a modest EBIT range for FY25 and a stable dividend, with no explicit medium-term EPS CAGR — guidance is credible in its conservatism but thin in its informativeness for a growth-thesis investor [#J]

  • FY current — EBIT: Management guided FY25 EBIT in the range of €135-155m (FY24 actual: €147m), implying flat-to-marginal growth at midpoint; range width of ~€20m is c.14% of midpoint, wider than the c.8-10% range typical of comparable German regulated utilities (E.ON Networks, Bayernwerk) [#A]

  • Sub: Range width has been consistent at 12-16% of midpoint across L4 periods, suggesting management does not tighten guidance as the year progresses until H1 results

  • FY current — Net income: Guided net income €90-105m for FY25; no explicit EPS number provided; at midpoint €97.5m, broadly flat vs FY24 net income of ~€99m [#A]

  • FY current — Dividend per share: Management confirmed €13.50/share dividend intention for FY25 (FY24: €13.50/share), representing a maintained payout with zero nominal growth; dividend has been stable at €13.50 since FY22, signalling capital allocation conservatism consistent with regulated-utility norms [#J]

  • FY current — CapEx: Guided €120-135m for FY25, up from FY24 actual of €118m; driven by grid-hardening and EV-charging infrastructure rollout mandated under the German Energiewende framework [#A]

  • Sub: Capex guidance has been met or exceeded (in actuals terms) in 3 of last 4 years, consistent with a utility in active grid investment cycle

  • FY current — FCF: No formal FCF guidance issued; implied FCF (net income minus capex delta, adjusted for D&A) is approximately €25-45m depending on working capital swings; analysts model it separately with a wide dispersion range [#I]

  • Medium-term — N2Y: No explicit FY26 EBIT target; management referenced "continued investment in network infrastructure" and "regulatory environment broadly supportive" at FY24 call but issued no numeric N2Y guidance [#A]

  • Medium-term — N3Y: Absent; no formal 3-year financial plan in public domain; closest proxy is the published capex envelope (€350-400m over FY24-27) implying average annual capex of €87-100m, lower than FY25 guided capex — internal tension not explained [#I]

  • Medium-term targets: Lechwerke's 2024 Capital Markets Day (October 2024) reiterated commitment to maintaining dividend but gave no return-on-equity, EBIT-margin, or EPS-growth target for the N3Y horizon — atypical relative to E.ON, RWE Networks, and Bayernwerk who all provide 5-year medium-term EPS CAGR targets [#A]

  • Source — earnings call: Primary guidance source is the FY24 results press release (March 2025) and accompanying analyst call; H1 interim results (August 2024) contained a guidance confirmation without revision [#J]

  • Source — written: Annual Report 2024 repeats EBIT range and capex guidance; no additional granularity vs press release [#I]

  • Caveat: Management explicitly conditioned FY25 guidance on "no material change in regulatory framework" and "weather-normalised network load volumes"; energy price assumptions not disclosed [#A]

  • Conditional assumption: The implied assumption of stable BNetzA tariff settlement for H2-25 is load-bearing; if Q3-25 regulatory review surfaces an adverse adjustment, management has reserved the right to revise guidance downward mid-year (noted in risk disclosures) [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read is that the wide guidance range (14% of midpoint) and conditional caveats give management substantial room to miss the bottom of the range while technically claiming guidance delivery; the absence of FCF and EPS targets means the most investible metrics are unanchored, and the N3Y guidance vacuum makes medium-term modelling dependent entirely on analyst assumptions rather than management commitment [#I]

§3 — Guidance Pattern: Sandbagging vs Over-Promising

  • J-front verdict: Lechwerke is a mild sandbagger — management consistently guides the lower-mid of achievable range, closes above midpoint in benign macro periods, and has only missed once in L4 periods; posture is structurally conservative but not aggressively low-balling [#J]

  • Sandbagger signal: EBIT guidance midpoints have been set approximately 3-5% below the eventual outcome in FY22, FY23, and FY24; consistent direction suggests deliberate range-setting conservatism rather than forecast error [#J]

  • Sub: German regulated utilities commonly set guidance at the lower-mid of the regulatory allowed-return corridor, creating a systematic upward skew vs midpoint

  • Beat consistency: 3 of 4 EBIT periods beat midpoint; 3 of 4 net income periods beat midpoint; this 75% beat rate is broadly in line with what Warburg Research characterises as "typical German DSO guidance discipline" — neither exceptional nor weak [#A]

  • Magnitude — beat: Average beat magnitude on EBIT is +3.7% above midpoint; stripping FY22's land-gain one-off, underlying beat magnitude is c.+2.5%; modest and sustainable — not a large-gap sandbag that risks disappointment on mean reversion [#A]

  • Magnitude — miss: Single miss (FY21) was -3.3% below midpoint; within the stated range; not a guidance breach; magnitude is low relative to utility-sector miss norms (sector misses outside range typically 5-8% in adverse macro years) [#A]

  • Mixed pattern — capex: Capex is an inverse pattern: management guides a midpoint and consistently exceeds it (spend > guidance midpoint in 3/4 periods); this is not a credibility problem per se but signals capex discipline is weaker than EBIT discipline — relevant for FCF modelling [#I]

  • Sub: Hauck & Aufhäuser (2025) flagged this capex overrun pattern as a "mild FCF depressant" relative to initial modelling assumptions [AS-Hauck]

  • Honest tracker — dividend: Dividend guidance is a perfect honest-tracker: guidance = actual, every period, no variance; management treats dividend as a commitment, not a range [#J]

  • Pattern shift: No material shift observed in guidance posture across L4 periods; FY22's elevated beat was an outlier driven by non-recurring items, not a step-change in sandbagging philosophy; the underlying pattern is stable [#A]

  • Improving accuracy: EBIT variance vs midpoint has tightened from FY22's wide +10.2% beat to +5.0% in FY24 (excluding the FY21 miss outlier), suggesting management has recalibrated range-midpoints upward post the energy-price surge; this is accuracy improving toward tighter midpoints [#J]

  • Deteriorating accuracy — FCF: The absence of formal FCF guidance across all four periods means there is no improving/deteriorating trend to track on this metric — it is a structural data absence, not a deterioration [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on the sandbagging pattern is that the apparent conservatism is exhausted: FY24's EBIT actuals of €147m are already near the top of a range (€130-150m) that was set when the energy macro was more favourable; FY25's guidance midpoint of €145m implies essentially no growth, and if the true ceiling of sandbagging capacity has already been revealed, there is no further cushion available for beats — the pattern runs out precisely when investors most need it [#I]

§4 — Trend in Guidance Accuracy

  • J-front verdict: Guidance accuracy has improved modestly from FY21-22 to FY23-24 in the sense that range-midpoints are better calibrated; however, accuracy is narrowing partly because management is tightening ranges in a period of more stable energy prices, not because forecasting skill has structurally improved [#J]

  • Recent accuracy: FY24 EBIT variance vs midpoint was +5.0%; FY23 was +3.0%; both are tighter than the FY22 outlier (+10.2%) and the FY21 modest miss (-3.3%); trend is converging toward a ±3-5% band [#J]

  • Historical accuracy: FY21-22 range was wider (variance band ±3.3% to +10.2%) driven by macro volatility in energy markets; the high standard deviation of FY22 distorts any simple trend line [#A]

  • Accuracy improving: The post-energy-crisis period (FY23-24) shows tighter guidance accuracy; this is structural to the extent that energy market volatility has subsided; it is cyclical to the extent that any future energy price spike would re-widen the variance [#J]

  • Volatility driver — macro: The FY21 miss and FY22 large beat are both macro-driven (weather / energy prices) rather than execution-driven; this limits the signal value of both extremes for assessing management's intrinsic forecasting skill [#A]

  • Volatility driver — execution: No evidence of execution-driven guidance misses in the L4 sample; all variance is traceable to external inputs (weather, energy prices, regulatory timing) [#J]

  • Metric — best accuracy: Dividend per share — 0.0% variance all four periods; net income accuracy is second-best (average ±3.2% vs midpoint) [#A]

  • Metric — worst accuracy: FCF — no guidance issued, no variance trackable; effectively the lowest-quality disclosure metric in Lechwerke's suite; among guided metrics, capex has the widest systematic directional bias (consistently above midpoint) [#I]

  • Accuracy deteriorating — risk: The BNetzA RP3 regulatory period beginning FY26 introduces a new structural uncertainty that could widen guidance accuracy materially; management has not yet modelled or disclosed how an adverse WACC revision would affect its guidance-setting methodology [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on accuracy trend is that recent tightening (FY23-24) is a calm-weather mirage — both years benefited from above-average grid load and stable energy procurement costs; the FY26+ guidance accuracy will be stress-tested by regulatory transition, not by management's improved forecasting craft, and there is no disclosed evidence of improved internal forecasting processes [#I]

§6 — Key Assumptions Behind Guidance

  • J-front verdict: The guidance assumption stack is partially transparent — regulatory WACC and network volume are the load-bearing inputs, both externally determined; internal cost assumptions are opaque; the single most fragile assumption is RP3 regulatory WACC, and management has not published a sensitivity for it [#J]

  • Revenue assumption — market: Lechwerke does not guide revenue explicitly; implied revenue is driven by (a) regulated network tariffs set by BNetzA, (b) electricity and gas distribution volumes, and (c) a small non-regulated services segment; regulation accounts for an estimated 70-75% of EBIT [#A]

  • Revenue assumption — pricing: Tariff increases for FY25 assumed consistent with the approved regulatory period 2 (RP2) tariff schedule; no adverse adjustment assumed; BNetzA's RP2 tariff decisions are locked — this is a known, low-risk assumption for FY25 [#J]

  • Revenue assumption — M&A: No M&A assumed in guidance; Lechwerke has not made a material acquisition since 2019; guidance is explicitly organic [#A]

  • Margin assumption — cost: Management assumes network operations costs broadly flat in real terms for FY25; H2-24 actual costs came in slightly above internal budget (the driver of H2-24 consensus downgrade), suggesting cost inflation may be understated in the FY25 assumption [#I]

  • Margin assumption — leverage: Operational leverage is low in a regulated utility — cost base is largely fixed (grid maintenance, personnel); EBIT sensitivity to volume changes is mainly through the allowed return on RAB, not through operating leverage in the traditional sense [#A]

  • Margin assumption — pricing power: Pricing power is regulatory — Lechwerke cannot raise tariffs above BNetzA-approved levels; any assumption of pricing power is therefore a proxy for regulatory period outcomes, not commercial negotiation [#J]

  • EPS assumption — tax: Effective tax rate assumed at approximately 30-31% (consistent with German corporate tax + solidarity surcharge + trade tax); no structural change assumed; this is low-risk and well-anchored [#A]

  • EPS assumption — share count: Share count is stable (no buyback programme, no dilutive issuances in recent years); EPS assumption effectively passes through net income directly [#A]

  • Macro assumption — commodity: Energy procurement costs for the non-regulated segment assumed at market forwards; management has not disclosed explicit energy price floors in its FY25 guidance, introducing commodity price risk for the ~25-30% of EBIT from non-regulated activities [#I]

  • Macro assumption — regulatory: The RP3 regulatory period (starting FY26 for electricity distribution) will reset the allowed WACC downward from current levels given falling risk-free rates in BNetzA's reference period; management has not incorporated RP3 risk into FY25 guidance but has flagged it as a medium-term headwind in risk disclosures [#A]

§7 — Credibility Assessment

  • J-front verdict: Credibility is MEDIUM-HIGH — the track record is clean in the controllable dimensions (EBIT beat rate, dividend delivery, cost stability) but structurally exposed to a single external driver (regulatory WACC) that management cannot influence and does not hedge; the credibility rating would drop to MEDIUM if RP3 outcomes disappoint [#J]

  • Credibility — verbal verdict: MEDIUM-HIGH — three consecutive beats on EBIT against a conservative range-midpoint, stable dividend delivery, and transparent caveat structure all support credibility; the one weakness is the opacity of the RP3 risk [#J]

  • Green flag — recent beat: EBIT above midpoint in FY22, FY23, FY24 — 3-year consecutive delivery is a credibility asset; only FY21 (adverse weather year) interrupted the streak [#J]

  • Green flag — conservative assumption: Guidance ranges set wide (12-16% of midpoint) and midpoints set conservatively; consistent with "promise low, deliver high" utility governance [#J]

  • Green flag — dividend commitment: Four-period perfect dividend delivery at €13.50/share signals management treats dividend as a hard commitment; credibility on income component is high [#J]

  • Red flag — recent miss: No recent outright miss (all actuals within range), but FY21 below-midpoint delivery is a reminder that single adverse weather year can break the beat streak; not a serious red flag but not dismissable [#A]

  • Red flag — aggressive assumption: The implicit assumption that RP3 regulatory outcomes will be benign is the most aggressive embedded assumption; it is aggressive by omission — management has not stress-tested it publicly or adjusted guidance framework for RP3 risk [#I]

  • Red flag — FCF opacity: No FCF guidance is a structural weakness for investors modelling 18M-3Y FCF yield; management's disclosure of capex guidance but not FCF targets means investors are on their own for the most important cash-generation metric [#I]

  • Margin of safety — high: If guidance is off by 10% adverse (EBIT €135m vs guided midpoint €145m), LEC-DE at current valuation (~15x EV/EBIT) would still represent a modestly-priced regulated utility; the investment case is not binary on a single guidance print [#A]

  • Margin of safety — low: If RP3 WACC cut is combined with a -10% EBIT miss (€135m) in FY26, and the cut is structural rather than cyclical, the medium-term earnings power embedded in the current valuation is overstated — margin of safety on the N3Y trajectory is low under that scenario [#A]

  • Thesis sensitivity: The investment thesis (18M-3Y EPS growth + 25%+ IRR) is highly sensitive to RP3 regulatory outcome; this is a binary-ish external event, not a continuous management-driven variable; the credibility assessment for internally-controlled metrics is MEDIUM-HIGH, but the overall IRR thesis is hostage to a regulator [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: The credibility verdict would be invalidated if BNetzA's RP3 provisional WACC decision (expected H2-25) comes in below 6.0% — at that level, the EBIT headwind for FY26-27 would be large enough to make management's current FY25 guidance irrelevant as a forward indicator; the scenario where apparent credibility proves brittle is thus not a management failure but a regulatory reset, and management's silence on this risk in guidance framing is itself a credibility issue [#I]
