- Sub: Finnish nuclear waste fund rules require periodic revaluation; this is a structural feature, not a temporary one-off

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on quality is that comparable earnings are management-constructed — Fortum's IAC exclusions strip out items that, while volatile, are economically real costs (nuclear decommissioning, carbon obligations); an investor relying solely on comparable EPS is accepting management's framing of "normal" profitability, which may structurally overstate sustainable free cash earnings [#J]

J-front verdict: Margin expansion observed but primarily cyclical — EBIT margin improvement from ~12.7% (FY23) to ~14.0%e (FY25e) reflects power price recovery and operational normalisation more than structural cost transformation [#J]

Revenue CAGR: FY23 continuing-ops ~€6,150m → FY25e ~€6,700m = ~+4.4% 2-year CAGR; broadly in line with Nordic electricity price inflation partially offset by volume constraints [#A]

Profit CAGR: Comparable EBIT ~€780m (FY23) → ~€940m (FY25e) = ~+9.8% 2-year CAGR; profit CAGR ~2.2x revenue CAGR [#A]
- Sub: this positive operating leverage reflects fixed-cost absorption — Fortum's hydro/nuclear cost base is largely fixed; incremental revenue (from higher prices) flows through at high margin

Operating leverage: Generation segment EBIT margins in high-price environments approach 40–50% incremental margin on volume/price upside [#J]
- Sub: this cuts both ways — earnings compress rapidly on price downside, as FY23 demonstrated; operating leverage is a double-edged sword for a commodity-price-tethered utility

Gross margin: Comparable gross margin improved from ~22–23% (H1 2023 trough) to ~25–26% range (FY24/H1 2025); this reflects lower fuel/variable costs as a % of revenue and power price recovery [#A]

EBITDA margin: 17–18% comparable EBITDA margin range in FY24/FY25e — consistent with investment-grade Nordic utility norms; not exceptional but stable [#A]

Cost cut driver: Fortum's €150m+ cost efficiency programme (announced FY23, running through 2025) contributed ~€50–70m per year in savings; modest relative to €1bn+ EBITDA but directionally positive [#A]

Mix shift: Heat segment (district heating in Nordic cities) is lower-margin but stable; generation (hydro + nuclear) is higher-margin but price-volatile; no significant mix shift across L3Y [#J]

Pricing driver: The primary margin expansion driver is Nordic/European wholesale power price — this is cyclical, not structural; 2022 price spike inflated comparable FY22 earnings; 2023 price normalization compressed margins; 2024–2025 partial recovery supports current ~14% EBIT margins [#A]

Scale driver: No meaningful scale improvement — revenue base is ~€6–7bn post-Uniper; growth is organic and modest; Fortum is not a compounding growth business at current strategic positioning [#J]

Sustainability: Margins sustainable at current power price levels (~€55–65/MWh Nordic) but not expanding further without price increase or volume growth; the ~14% EBIT margin is a price-level-dependent equilibrium, not a floor [#J]

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on margin sustainability is that FY23–25 margin improvement is being read as structural operational improvement when it is predominantly a power price reflation from the 2023 trough; Nordic wholesale prices at €40–50/MWh would compress EBIT margins back to sub-12%, erasing ~200bp of apparent "improvement" [#J]

§4 — Seasonal Patterns

J-front verdict: Pronounced H2-skewed seasonality — Nordic hydro, district heating, and nuclear maintenance scheduling create consistent H2 > H1 earnings pattern; material and predictable [#J]

H1 pattern: Structurally weaker — hydro reservoir levels build in spring, limiting generation; heating demand declining April–June; nuclear scheduled maintenance typically timed for Q2 [#A]
- Sub: H1 typically accounts for ~40–42% of full-year comparable EBIT in a normal year; FY23/FY24 data confirm this split

H2 pattern: Structurally stronger — peak heating demand October–March; hydro discharge season (autumn precipitation and drawdown); higher spot power prices in winter; nuclear back to full capacity post-maintenance [#A]

Strongest quarter: Q4 (October–December) is typically the single strongest quarter for generation margin and heat contribution [#J]

Weakest quarter: Q2 (April–June) is structurally weakest — shoulder heating season, spring hydro build, nuclear outage season [#A]

Seasonality direction: Stable and consistent across L3Y; H1/H2 split has not meaningfully changed, suggesting no structural shift in business mix or operating model [#J]

Cause — weather: Hydrology is the dominant swing factor within the seasonal pattern — anomalously dry years (like H1 2023) compress H1 earnings below the structural average; anomalously wet years flatter H1 [#A]
- Sub: Nordic hydro deficit/surplus swings can move comparable EBIT by €50–150m in a half-year period

Cause — production: Nuclear maintenance outage (Loviisa Unit 1 and Unit 2, typically 2–4 weeks each in Q2) removes ~300–400 MW of capacity during H1, suppressing H1 output [#A]

Seasonality changing: No evidence of structural change in seasonality across L3Y; H2/H1 skew has been ~58%/42% consistently [#J]

Investor implication: Weak H1 prints should not be read as run-rate deterioration — H2 earnings recovery is structural; the bear trap is selling on a poor H1 number that misses the seasonal uptick [#J]

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on seasonal patterns is that the H2 strength is increasingly dependent on high winter power prices, which have been supported by European gas price volatility since 2021–2022; if gas prices normalise structurally to pre-2021 levels, winter power price uplift diminishes, compressing the H2 advantage and making the seasonal pattern a weakening rather than stable feature [#J]

J-front verdict: Modest sandbagger tendency — Fortum has beaten comparable EBITDA consensus in 4 of 6 L3Y periods, consistently by 3–8%; management's wide guidance ranges embed cushion rather than precision; credibility is moderate-to-good on EPS delivery [#J]

Beat/miss frequency: 4 beats, 1 miss, 1 in-line across 6 L3Y periods (EBITDA basis) [#A]
- Sub: H1 2023 was the miss period — hydrology deficit and sharper-than-expected power price decline caught consensus below management's wide range; FY23 slight miss on revenue but comparable EBIT in-line

Beat magnitude: Average beat ~+3.5% on comparable EBITDA across beat periods; not exceptional by European utility norms (typical beat frequency among European regulated/semi-regulated utilities ~60–65% of periods, average magnitude ~2–4%) [#A]

Miss magnitude: H1 2023 miss ~(6)% on comparable EBITDA — driven by hydro deficit; not a management guidance failure per se, as hydrology is explicitly excluded from guidance certainty [#A]

Direction of surprise: Beats are predominantly driven by power price hedge realisation above consensus modelling; management is more consistently right on costs than on power price outcomes [#J]
- Sub: cost line surprises (positive) in 5 of 6 periods; power price/volume mix is the swing variable

Sandbagging signal: Modest evidence — guidance ranges are deliberately wide (e.g., FY25 guidance €700–900m comparable EBIT = ±12% range around midpoint); this width creates structural beat opportunity against consensus that clusters below midpoint [#A]

Guidance accuracy: FY24 guidance of comparable EBIT €750–950m; actuals ~€870m = within range but above midpoint; this pattern (actual vs consensus below midpoint) has appeared in FY23 and FY24 [#A]

Trend in accuracy: Improving — FY23 guidance was exceptionally wide given post-Russia uncertainty; FY24–25 guidance ranges have narrowed to ~±12%, reflecting greater operational visibility as the business stabilises [#J]

Sell-side revision pattern: [AS-Berenberg][AS-Nordea] estimates revised upward post-H1 2024 results (~+3–5% to full-year EBIT); [AS-Citi][AS-Deutsche Bank] more conservative, revised up only ~+1–2%; [AS-Jefferies] maintained cautious stance on power price trajectory through FY24 [#A]

Honest-tracker assessment: Management communicates honestly about uncertainty but uses wide ranges as a protective mechanism — not deceptive, but the ranges are wide enough to be nearly non-informative in power-price-volatile environments [#J]

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on management credibility is that consistent beats vs consensus may reflect sell-side conservatism (anchored by 2022–23 price fears) rather than management sandbagging; as the business normalises and sell-side models re-anchor to recovery, the beat cushion compresses and the 3–8% beat frequency may revert to zero; additionally, Fortum's guidance explicitly excludes nuclear provision volatility and carbon MTM, meaning management can beat "comparable" guidance even when total economic earnings are below expectations [#J]
