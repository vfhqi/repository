[C] Q2 Change Forces — Alfen NV (ALFEN-NL)

Cohort: Energy Transition Equipment Recovery (ALFEN-NL / S92-DE / KEMPOWR-FI / LAND-CH)
Stage: IG | Source: [C] Claude | Date: 2026-05-15
CQs addressed: CQ-C1, CQ-C2, CQ-C4, CQ-C5

§1 — Key Change Forces: Brief Recap + Summary

  • J-front verdict: Alfen's dominant force is a structural EV charging market demotion — that division is now a value detractor, not driver — partially offset by a cyclical Smart Grid recovery that is finally materialising in Q1 2026 (+25% group revenue). [#J]

  • Dominant force 1 — EV Charging demand collapse: Revenue fell from €153m (2024) to ~€120m (2025), with Q1 2025 -27% YoY; home-charging segment structurally smaller than peak as European OEM CO2 relief reduces urgency; competitive pricing pressure from low-cost entrants has compressed unit economics. [#I]

  • Dominant force 2 — Energy Storage commoditisation headwind: ESS fell -24% in 2024, -27% in H1 2025; Chinese cell-price deflation (LFP packs -13% in China 2025) is structural for the system-integrator model; Alfen's SEM platform differentiation is being tested by price anchors set by Chinese-linked BESS competitors. [#I]

  • Dominant force 3 — Smart Grid cyclical trough turning: SGS grew +12% in 2024 and is +3.9% in H1 2025; Q1 2026 group recovery driven primarily by storage backlog delivery and grid project execution; SGS backlog at €126.8m end-2025, with €122.1m expected to deliver in 2026. [#I]

  • Dominant force 4 — Management and strategic reset: New CEO (October 2025) and CFO (January 2025) implementing company-wide transformation — workforce -15%, cost base -€13.1m; previous management guided €590–660m for 2024, delivered €487.6m; credibility was severely impaired, now rebuilding. [#I]

  • Dominant force 5 — Regulatory tailwind building but lagging: AFIR mandate (EV charging coverage obligation) live from January 2026; Repower EU grid investment mandates and European energy independence framing provide long-term demand underpinning but have not yet translated to order intake acceleration in EV charging. [#I]

  • Component map:

Force Type Direction Magnitude
EV Charging demand / competitive pressure Structural Headwind High
Energy Storage price deflation (Chinese) Structural Headwind Medium
Smart Grid cyclical recovery Cyclical Tailwind Medium
Management/strategy reset Transitional Tailwind (if executed) Medium

European energy independence policy Structural Tailwind (long-duration) Medium

  • Sceptical reversal: If AFIR demand uplift materialises slower than expected AND ESS price deflation accelerates, all three divisions face headwinds simultaneously; the Q1 2026 recovery could be a timing/backlog delivery event rather than a cycle inflection. [#J]

§2 — Leadership and Management Changes

  • J-front verdict: Management credibility is at a nadir after two consecutive guidance cuts in 2024; the new CEO and CFO are externally recruited turnaround/transformation profiles, not energy-sector veterans — execution risk is elevated for 18M. [#J]

  • CEO — Michael Colijn: Joined October 2025; previously CEO of Heliox (EV fast-charging for public transport and trucks); no prior CEO role at an Euronext-listed industrial of Alfen's complexity; appointment signals board intent to rebuild commercial credibility and digital orientation. [#I]

  • CFO — Onno Krap: Appointed May 2024 (statutory board effective January 2025); background in tech/SaaS CFO roles (Exact Software, Kinly, Crucell); not a traditional energy equipment CFO; joined during the downturn and has overseen the cost restructuring and reporting normalisation. [#I]

  • COO — Bart Kempen: Appointed February 2024; manages manufacturing and operations; the operational rationalisation (workforce -15%, agile R&D, reduced management layers, consolidation of overlapping activities) is under his remit. [#I]

  • Previous CEO exit: The prior management regime guided FY2024 revenue at €590–660m (original guidance) before cutting to €485–520m mid-year; net loss of €27m was delivered against prior-year profit of €29.7m — a €57m net income swing; guidance credibility was destroyed. [#A]

  • Management team stability: Four out of five executive committee members appointed in 2024–2025; only CCO Michelle Lesh (since 2021) has continuity; the team is effectively a new set of operators, which increases execution uncertainty but removes the incumbents who presided over the peak-to-trough mismanagement. [#A]

  • Recovery execution track record: Too early to assess Colijn's delivery — he has been CEO for only 7 months; Q1 2026 beat is encouraging but one quarter is not a track record; the restructuring (€13.1m cost savings) appears to be landing as guided. [#A]

  • Sceptical reversal: New CEO with no listed-industrials CEO experience + new CFO with SaaS background + new COO = no one at the top has cycled through Alfen's specific order-book volatility before; turnaround narrative is plausible but unproven. [#J]

§3 — Strategy and Structure Changes

  • J-front verdict: Strategy has shifted from volume-at-all-costs growth to focused top-3 positioning in north-western Europe; the divisional structure is unchanged but go-to-market has been tightened and the scope of addressable geographies narrowed — a necessary correction but not a new growth engine. [#J]

  • Strategic focus sharpening: External strategy validation exercise in late 2024 concluded Alfen should concentrate on product-market combinations where it has or can attain top-3 market position; geographic priority now explicitly north-western Europe (NL, BE, DE, FR, Nordics) — retreat from broader European and non-European ambitions. [#I]

  • No divisional structure change: Three-division model (SGS / EVC / ESS) intact; management chose to retain the integrated platform rather than divest EV Charging despite its underperformance; rationale appears to be cross-selling potential and AFIR-driven future demand. [#I]

  • Operational restructuring: Workforce reduced by ~15% (from 1,053 FTE at December 2024); management layers reduced; R&D moved to agile methodology; overlapping activities consolidated; manufacturing capacity in Netherlands and Finland retained. [#I]

  • Three strategic pillars for 2026 transformation: Customer centricity (closer to key accounts), product excellence (fewer, higher-quality SKUs), digitalisation (connected charging, smart grid software, remote monitoring subscription revenues). [#I]

  • ESS strategy — hold or exit question: ESS contributed €123.7m in 2024 (down from ~€163m in 2023); Alfen has retained ESS as a division and cited €126.8m backlog end-2025; no divestment signal, but the strategy document is ambiguous about whether Alfen is a cell-agnostic system integrator or wants deeper proprietary differentiation. [#A]

  • Sceptical reversal: "Focus on where we have top-3 position" is a rational post-hoc rationalisation of a shrinking addressable market rather than a genuine offensive strategy; the geographic narrowing reduces the TAM underpinning the recovery thesis. [#J]

§4 — Priorities and Ambition

  • J-front verdict: Management's stated targets are deliberately conservative and 2026 is explicitly framed as a transformation/reset year, not a recovery year; profitable growth is deferred to 2027 — the ambition level is appropriately de-risked relative to 2022-era targets but offers thin financial upside in the near term. [#J]

  • 2025 actuals vs guidance: FY2025 revenue €435.6m vs guidance €445–505m (mid-point €475m) — missed the midpoint by ~8%; adjusted EBITDA margin 5.8% vs guidance "high single digit" — missed; revenue declined -10.7% vs 2024; net loss continued for second consecutive year. ❌ [#I]

  • 2026 guidance (February 2026): Revenue €435–475m (flat to -8% vs 2025 at midpoint); adjusted EBITDA margin 4–7%; CAPEX <4% of revenue; described as a "transformational year." The midpoint implies revenue essentially flat and margin potentially declining from 5.8% — conservative but still requires execution. [#I]

  • Medium-term ambition (2027 and beyond): 5–10% revenue growth rate beyond 2025 (originally given in February 2025, subsequently revised down in H1 2025 to 0–5% growth); year-on-year margin improvement towards low double-digit adjusted EBITDA; no specific EPS target given. [#I]

  • KPIs management is guiding to: Adjusted EBITDA margin (primary); revenue (secondary); CAPEX as % of revenue; and implicitly headcount and cost base (the €13.1m savings programme). No order intake KPI is publicly disclosed, which is a notable gap for investors assessing cycle timing. [#A]

  • Prior ambition vs reality comparison: At peak in 2022, management guided €590–660m for 2024 and double-digit EBITDA margins; actual 2024 was €487.6m with sub-3% reported gross margins and a net loss; the peer group (S92, Kempower, Landis+Gyr) also missed peak targets, but Alfen's delta was among the widest. ❌ [#A]

  • Sceptical reversal: 2026 guidance at essentially flat revenue while describing it as a "transformation year" means financial recovery is entirely a 2027+ event; if macro or AFIR delays persist, the low double-digit EBITDA margin target gets pushed to 2028+, invalidating the 18–24M recovery thesis. [#J]

  • FY2023: €504.5m (+15%, peak)
  • FY2024: €487.6m (-3.3%)
  • FY2025: €435.6m (-10.7%)

  • Q1 2026: €129.7m (+25% YoY vs Q1 2025 trough) — first positive inflection [#I]

  • Divisional revenue evolution (2024 actuals):

  • Smart Grid Solutions: €210.6m (+11.8% vs 2023) — resilient
  • EV Charging: €153.3m (flat vs 2023) — then deteriorated to ~€120m in 2025

  • Energy Storage Systems: €123.7m (-24.1% vs 2023) — significant decline [#I]

  • Gross margin trajectory:

  • FY2023: ~30% (adjusted)
  • FY2024 reported: 23.7% (impacted by €15.4m SGS moisture provision + €6.5m EVC obsolete inventory + €1.6m ESS impairment)
  • FY2024 adjusted: 28.6%
  • H1 2025: improving with cost cuts; Q3 2025: EVC gross margin 43.1% (segment-level)

  • Q1 2026: gross margin 26.2% (down from 29.8% in Q1 2025 on revenue mix shift) [#I]

  • EBIT and EBITDA trajectory:

  • FY2024: net loss €27.0m vs net profit €29.7m FY2023 — complete profitability reversal
  • FY2024 adjusted EBITDA: €28.5m
  • FY2025 adjusted EBITDA: €25.5m (5.8% margin)

  • Q1 2026: adjusted EBITDA €8.2m (6.3% margin, +50% YoY) — operating leverage emerging ⚡ [#I]

  • Cost base restructuring impact: Personnel costs -15.2% and other OPEX -21.1% in 2025 vs 2024; cost savings of €13.1m materialising in 2025 as guided; fixed cost base has been rightsized but remains meaningful — at €435m revenue, overhead absorption is thin. [#A]

  • Operating leverage mechanics: Estimated ~40–45% incremental EBITDA margin on revenue above the current run-rate (based on cost structure disclosed); i.e. each €10m additional revenue above current base likely generates ~€4–4.5m incremental EBITDA; at €500m revenue, low-double-digit EBITDA margins are plausible. [#A]

  • Working capital: One-off inventory provision (EVC obsolete inventory €6.5m) and SGS moisture-related provision (€15.4m) distorted 2024; CAPEX held below 4% of revenue as guided, limiting cash burn; no balance sheet stress signals but net cash position not disclosed in available sources. [#A]

  • Sceptical reversal: Q1 2026 gross margin at 26.2% is actually below Q1 2025's 29.8% despite the revenue recovery — suggesting mix shift towards lower-margin ESS backlog delivery; if SGS margins are structurally lower than pre-2023, the margin recovery path is not as linear as the operating leverage argument implies. ❌ [#J]

§6 — Demand Changes

(CQ-C1 EXPLICIT ADDRESS — Structural vs Cyclical)

  • J-front verdict (CQ-C1): Verdict is differentiated by division. SGS demand is cyclical — constrained by Dutch grid operator permitting/labour, not secular decline; ESS demand is structurally challenged by Chinese price deflation but absolute volumes are growing; EV Charging home segment faces structural demotion from market share loss and OEM CO2 target relief — the clearest case of structural impairment in the portfolio. [#J]

EV Charging sub-market:

  • Home segment — structural concern: Revenue declined -27% in Q1 2025 and full-year 2025 to ~€120m; decline driven by (1) intensified competition from low-cost entrants in home chargers, (2) BEV registrations in Germany -27% following abolition of EV incentives, (3) European OEM CO2 target relaxation removing fleet electrification urgency. [#I]

  • Commercial/public charging — more stable: AFIR regulation mandating EV charging at motorway service areas every 60km and in urban areas became operational January 2026; Alfen's new Eve Double Plus / Eve Single Plus launched Q4 2025 are AFIR-compliant and Plug&Charge (ISO 15118-20) ready; commercial/CPO segment should benefit. [#I]

  • AFIR demand timing: AFIR compliance deadline was 8 January 2026 for existing installations; CPO (Charge Point Operators) retrofit and expansion demand should be visible in order intake H1 2026; this is the primary regulatory demand catalyst for EVC — but Q1 2026 EV Charging revenue still declined, suggesting AFIR effect has not yet arrived in revenue. [#A]

  • Cyclical vs structural verdict on EVC: Home charging = structurally smaller market than 2022–2023 expectations implied; commercial/public = cyclical lag with AFIR tailwind; the mix shift towards commercial is strategically correct but Alfen is not the natural incumbent in public CPO infrastructure (that is more Kempower/ABB/Siemens territory). [#J]

Smart Grid Solutions sub-market:

  • Demand drivers are structural (grid congestion, electrification): Netherlands, Belgium and Germany face severe grid congestion; private industrial customers cannot get grid connections; utilities must upgrade transformer infrastructure to handle distributed generation and EV load. [#I]

  • Cyclical execution constraint — Dutch grid operators: Two of three main Dutch grid operators reduced 2025 order quantities vs previously communicated volumes; reason: labour shortages (installation crews) and nitrogen-related permitting delays (a Netherlands-specific regulatory issue); this is a cyclical delay, not structural demand destruction. [#A]

  • Order book recovery in progress: SGS revenue +3.9% H1 2025; Q1 2026 SGS participating in group +25% revenue; SGS backlog likely rebuilding; the Finnish manufacturing facility (355 substations in H1 2025) provides geographic diversification of production. [#I]

  • Cyclical verdict on SGS: Demand is structurally driven (electrification, grid modernisation) but cyclically constrained by Dutch permitting/labour issues that are 12–24M in resolution; grid operator budget cycles are visible multi-year; this is the most recoverable division. [#J]

Energy Storage Systems sub-market:

  • Volume direction is positive, but price is deflationary: ESS backlog at €126.8m end-2025 (up from ~€100m end-2024), with €122.1m expected to deliver in 2026; Q1 2026 storage is a primary growth driver; absolute order volumes are recovering. [#I]

  • Chinese price competition — structural headwind on margins: Global LFP battery pack prices fell to ~$84/kWh in China (2025), -13% YoY; Chinese BESS competitors (CATL's commercial ESS products, Hithium, Envision) are pricing aggressively in European markets; Alfen's SEM (Smart Energy Management) system integration adds software value but cannot fully offset the hardware price anchor. [#A]

  • Verdict on ESS: Volume demand is cyclical recovery (project delays from 2023–24 reversing); margin on that volume is structurally challenged by Chinese cell pricing; net-net, ESS should deliver revenue recovery in 2026 but margin contribution may not recover to 2022 levels. [#J]

  • Sceptical reversal on CQ-C1: The optimistic reading (SGS structural growth, ESS volume recovery) requires Dutch permitting to ease and grid operator budgets to normalise in 2026–2027; if nitrogen/labour constraints persist into 2027, the "cyclical" SGS narrative becomes structural by duration. ❌ [#J]

§7 — Competition Changes

  • J-front verdict: Competitive intensity has increased materially across all three divisions since 2022; Alfen has lost pricing power in home EV charging, faces Chinese price anchoring in storage, and competes with larger-capitalised European peers (Siemens, ABB) in smart grids — the competitive moat has narrowed, particularly in EVC home segment. ❌ [#J]

EV Charging competition:

  • Home segment — Alfen losing share: Competition from lower-cost European and Asian home charger manufacturers has taken market share; Alfen acknowledged "increasing competition in the home segment" as primary cause of EVC revenue decline; the home charger market has commoditised faster than management anticipated. [#I]

  • Kempower / ABB / Siemens in public charging: Kempower (KEMPOWR-FI, cohort peer) is a direct competitor in AC and DC commercial charging; ABB and Siemens have scale distribution advantages in the CPO and fleet charging segments; Alfen's competitive advantage (integrated smart charging network software) provides differentiation but is not unique. [#A]

  • ChargePoint / EVBox: Platform-first competitors in commercial charging have moved to hardware-agnostic models, potentially commoditising Alfen's hardware further. [#I]

Smart Grid competition:

  • Alfen holds strong position in Dutch market: Long-standing relationships with Dutch DSOs (Stedin, Liander, Enexis) give Alfen incumbency advantage; the moisture-related quality issue in 2024 (€15.4m provision) is a one-off reputational risk but the relationships appear intact (SGS backlog growing). [#A]

  • Huawei / Chinese grid equipment: Huawei is active in European smart grid components (inverters, grid management); cybersecurity concerns about Chinese grid equipment are growing in EU policy circles — this could benefit Alfen as a European trusted supplier, though no regulatory restriction is yet in force. [#A]

  • Finnish manufacturing (Alfen's new capacity): The Espoo, Finland facility adds 355 substations/half-year production; this is partly a hedge against concentration in NL but also positions Alfen for Nordic utility orders. [#I]

Energy Storage competition:

  • Chinese BESS system integrators: CATL (largest global battery manufacturer, 38% global share) is expanding BESS commercial offerings in Europe; Hithium, Envision, and other Chinese players offer full-stack BESS at prices Alfen's SEM platform cannot match on hardware cost alone; this is the most structurally threatening competitive force. ❌ [#I]

  • Price competition evidence: Alfen cited "battery system price declines" as an expected ongoing headwind into 2026; this is management's language for margin compression from competitive pricing, not just input cost benefit. [#I]

  • SEM differentiation — software integration: Alfen's SEM platform offers software-defined energy management, grid code compliance, and multi-vendor compatibility; this value-add is real but not priced at a premium sufficient to offset Chinese cell-cost advantages fully. [#A]

  • Sceptical reversal: Alfen's incumbent SGS position in Netherlands is the most defensible moat; but NL is a small market, and Alfen's attempt to expand into broader European and storage markets has exposed it to competition it is not structurally advantaged to win. [#J]

§8 — Disruption

  • J-front verdict: V2G technology is the most credible near-term disruptive force — Alfen is positioning early (Eve Twin 5, ISO 15118) but V2G adoption is still pre-commercial at scale, and platform consolidation by OEMs/energy retailers could disintermediate hardware suppliers within 3–5 years. [#J]

  • V2G (vehicle-to-grid) — strategic positioning: Alfen's Eve Twin 5 and the new Eve Double Plus/Single Plus (launched Q4 2025) are V2G-ready with ISO 15118-20 bidirectional charging capability; V2G enables EV batteries to discharge to grid, creating demand for bidirectional charger hardware — a market that doesn't yet exist at scale but could differentiate Alfen from single-direction hardware peers. [#I]

  • V2G adoption constraints: OEM participation (all major EV platforms must support ISO 15118-20) is required; only a handful of vehicle models (Nissan Leaf, some Hyundai/KIA, VW ID-series from 2025) currently support V2G; grid operator tariff structures need regulatory adjustment to monetise V2G discharge; 2–4 year lag before V2G generates material order volume. [#A]

  • Software/platform disintermediation risk: Tesla Energy (Powerwall, Megapack), Shell Recharge, and large energy retailers are moving towards turnkey EV + storage + grid solutions; if these platforms disintermediate hardware specialists by bundling charger + energy management + grid services, Alfen's stand-alone hardware becomes a price-competed commodity. [#A]

  • Battery chemistry changes: Solid-state batteries (expected commercial scale ~2028–2030) could reduce stationary storage system cost further; sodium-ion batteries are already in commercial production from CATL; LFP has likely peaked in cost-reduction velocity, but new chemistries could restart price deflation for ESS. [#A]

  • Digital/software transition: Alfen's 2026 transformation explicitly targets digitalisation — connected charging, remote monitoring, subscription revenues; if this generates a software-recurring revenue layer, it partially hedges hardware commoditisation; but no financial guidance on software revenue is provided, and the current revenue base is >95% hardware. [#A]

  • Sceptical reversal: V2G, software subscriptions, and grid digitalisation are all 3–7 year opportunities; Alfen needs to survive and compete through the next 18–24M on existing hardware economics before these disruptive bets mature. [#J]

§9 — Political and Regulatory Changes

  • J-front verdict: The regulatory tailwind is building — AFIR is live, Repower EU is committed, and European energy independence has become a political priority post-2025 energy crisis escalation — but the translation from regulatory mandate to Alfen's order book is lagged by 12–24M and not yet visible in EVC revenue. [#J]

  • AFIR (Alternative Fuels Infrastructure Regulation) — key detail: Regulation effective 1 January 2026; requires minimum 0.66kW per battery electric vehicle registered in each EU member state at public charging points; combined with motorway coverage obligations (every 60km minimum fast-charge point); Alfen's commercial and CPO customers must invest in new and upgraded charging infrastructure to comply. [#I]

  • AFIR demand translation timing: The compliance deadline was January 2026; CPO procurement decisions typically lag 6–12M; therefore AFIR-driven hardware orders should appear in backlog H1 2026 and convert to revenue H2 2026 / FY2027; Q1 2026 EVC still declining — AFIR effect not yet in revenue. [#A]

  • Repower EU / EU grid investment mandates: EU has committed to accelerating grid investment under the Net-Zero Industry Act and REPowerEU; total EU grid investment requirement estimated at €584bn by 2030 (EU Commission figures); Alfen's SGS division is a direct beneficiary through substation supply to DSOs; however, actual national government capex deployment has been slow relative to commitments. [#I]

  • European energy independence framing (2025–2026): Iran conflict and continuing energy price volatility have elevated energy independence as a political priority; EU is restricting Chinese telecoms equipment and expanding restrictions to energy infrastructure; Huawei is being excluded from grid management contracts in several EU member states; a formal EU cybersecurity restriction on Chinese grid equipment would benefit Alfen as a European-origin manufacturer. [#A]

  • Netherlands-specific — nitrogen permitting crisis: Dutch grid operator order reduction in 2025 is partly attributable to the Netherlands' ongoing nitrogen compound deposition regulations (PFAS/nitrogen case law), which restrict permitting for new infrastructure; this is a Dutch-specific regulatory headwind, not an EU-wide issue; resolution timeline uncertain (2026–2027). [#I]

  • Dutch national energy policy: Netherlands has committed to rapid grid capacity expansion (net-zero 2050 pathway); Tennet/DSO investment plans remain large; the permitting constraint is a pace limiter, not a demand cancellation. [#A]

  • Cybersecurity regulation (potential Alfen beneficiary): EU Cyber Resilience Act and NIS2 Directive increase requirements for critical infrastructure equipment security; Chinese grid equipment suppliers face growing scrutiny; if/when formal restriction comes, Alfen as a European-origin supplier with an established DSO relationship base benefits; timing uncertain, likely 2027+. [#A]

  • Sceptical reversal: EU mandates often lack enforcement bite — AFIR compliance is monitored at member-state level and penalties for non-compliance are light in early years; grid investment commitments are fiscal, dependent on national budget cycles, and have historically been delayed. [#J]

§10 — Supply Side Changes

  • J-front verdict: Supply chain normalised post-2022 and is no longer a constraint; the primary supply-side pressure is now margin-compressive rather than capacity-limiting — copper/labour cost inflation in Netherlands, and Chinese component competition setting floor prices for power electronics. [#J]

  • Component availability normalised: Post-2022 transformer component shortages (driven by global grid infrastructure demand surge) have eased; lead times for power electronics components have normalised; the SGS concrete housing supply constraint cited in mid-2024 has been resolved. [#I]

  • Manufacturing capacity: Alfen operates its primary manufacturing in Almere, Netherlands, with a secondary facility in Espoo, Finland (commenced operations for SGS substations); Q1 2025: 1,273 substations in NL + 355 in FI = 1,628 total; Q3 2025: 733 total (reflecting lower demand in H2 2025); capacity exists for recovery. [#I]

  • Labour cost — Netherlands: Dutch labour market remains tight; Alfen reduced headcount by ~15% (from 1,053 to estimated ~900 FTE post-restructuring); labour is a meaningful fixed cost for a Dutch industrial; wage inflation in Netherlands running at ~4–5% per annum, though EUR rates are easing. [#A]

  • Raw materials — copper: Copper is a key input for substations and charging hardware; copper prices have been elevated post-2022 but have partially normalised; LME copper at ~$9,000–9,500/tonne in 2025–2026, vs ~$10,500 peak; not a major swing factor given Alfen's cost structure. [#A]

  • Lithium / battery cells for ESS: LFP cell prices falling structurally — BNEF data shows $108/kWh global average, $84/kWh China average in 2025; this reduces Alfen's ESS hardware input cost but simultaneously depresses system prices (customers expect the savings); net margin effect is approximately neutral to slightly negative. [#A]

  • Supply chain risk — Chinese component dependency: Alfen's ESS division uses lithium cells from third-party suppliers (cell suppliers not publicly disclosed); if EU imposes tariffs on Chinese battery components (as with solar panels), ESS system costs could rise; the EU Battery Regulation supply chain traceability requirements (2024+) add compliance cost for battery system integrators. [#A]

  • Sceptical reversal: Normalised component supply means the capacity constraint excuse is gone — any further revenue misses in SGS will be demand/permitting driven, not supply driven, making them harder to explain away. [#J]

§11 — Revenue and Demand Cycle

(CQ-C4 EXPLICIT ADDRESS — % services/recurring, trajectory 2020–2025)

  • J-front verdict: Alfen is overwhelmingly a hardware business with minimal recurring revenue; services and software subscriptions are <5% of group revenue and have not grown materially through the 2023–25 downturn; the 2026 transformation explicitly targets growing recurring/digital revenue but no financial KPIs are disclosed. ❌ [#J]

  • Revenue mix — hardware vs services (CQ-C4):

  • Hardware (substations, chargers, battery systems): estimated >95% of revenue
  • Service contracts, remote monitoring, software subscriptions: <5% of revenue (no segment disclosure)

  • Services revenue trajectory 2020–2025: no specific data disclosed by Alfen; the company does not break out services as a separate line in quarterly trading updates [#I]

  • Evidence from downturn behaviour: During 2023–25 downturn, no "services cushion" was visible — revenue declined proportionally across all divisions; if a material recurring services base existed, it would have dampened the top-line decline; its absence is the clearest evidence that recurring revenue remains immaterial. [#A]

  • 2026 digitalisation initiative — nascent: Alfen has stated digitalisation as a 2026 transformation pillar, including connected charging platforms and remote monitoring subscriptions; CEO Colijn's Heliox background is relevant here (Heliox offered fleet management software); but no revenue guidance for digital/services is provided, and commercialisation is at least 2–3 years from meaningful contribution. [#A]

  • Order book visibility — ESS: ESS backlog at €126.8m end-2025, with €122.1m expected to deliver in 2026; this is the strongest order-book visibility in the group and is the primary driver of Q1 2026's +25% revenue growth. [#I]

  • Order book visibility — SGS: No specific SGS backlog figure disclosed in available sources; SGS operates on shorter lead times (substations are typically ordered 6–12M out) vs ESS project lead times (12–18M); SGS recovery is more dependent on grid operator procurement cycles re-opening. [#A]

  • Order book visibility — EVC: No order book disclosed; home charging is spot/distribution channel business (minimal lead time visibility); commercial/CPO charging has longer lead times but Alfen does not disclose EVC backlog; EVC is the lowest-visibility division. [#A]

  • Book-to-bill ratio: Not publicly disclosed; the fact that ESS backlog grew from ~€100m to €126.8m in 2025 while ESS revenue was also declining implies book-to-bill >1.0 for ESS in 2025 — a positive cycle signal. ⚡ [#A]

  • Sceptical reversal (CQ-C4): Alfen's <5% recurring revenue means the entire investment thesis depends on hardware cycle recovery; there is no defensive services floor; a second demand air-pocket (e.g. AFIR implementation delays + Dutch permitting extension) has no buffer to absorb. ❌ [#J]

§12 — Macro Changes

  • J-front verdict: The macro environment is mixed for Alfen — European rate cuts in 2025 are modestly positive for utility capex, but lower European gas prices reduce urgency of electrification investment for industrial customers; the Iran-driven energy price volatility (Q1 2026) is a net positive for energy independence investment sentiment. [#J]

  • Interest rates — European DSO capex sensitivity: ECB cut rates from 4% to ~2.5% through 2025; lower rates reduce the hurdle rate for utility infrastructure investment and make grid modernisation projects more fundable; Dutch grid operators (Stedin, Liander, Enexis) are regulated utilities with RAB-based returns — their capex is less rate-sensitive than merchant capex, but lower WACC helps the investment case for new connections. [#A]

  • Interest rate effect on commercial EV charging customers: Corporate fleet electrification capex and commercial charging station build-out is more rate-sensitive; elevated rates in 2023–24 contributed to project postponements; ECB rate cuts in 2025 should partially reverse this; however, the benefit was not visible in EVC order intake through 2025, suggesting rate sensitivity is secondary to EV adoption pace. [#A]
