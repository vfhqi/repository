Section 2 – The 80/20 (bottom line up front) 10 findings

Section summary
  1. Irish Continental Group offers an exceptionally predictable 18-month earnings trajectory driven by strategic fleet ownership and Dover-Calais capacity sharing.
  2. With record cash flow conversion and a conservative balance sheet, the stock represents a highly attractive idea-generation candidate.
1.
Diverse segment operations: The company makes money through two core divisions, Ferries and Container & Terminal, carrying cars, passengers, RoRo freight, and LoLo containers. It has dominant positions in both Irish ferry and container markets
2.
Predictable investment case: A highly predictable EPS trajectory is supported by the transition to full fleet ownership and deep structural cost-rationalisation on the Channel. This transition to full fleet ownership reduces lease expenses and enhances competitive scale
3.
Critical swing factor: The key variable for forward earnings is the successful pass-through of carbon surcharges and fuel cost stabilization. Passenger yields are also highly sensitive to cross-channel price wars
4.
Resilient revenue growth: Total revenue grew by 10.4% in FY25 to €666.7m, up from €603.8m in FY24, reflecting resilient consumer demand. This performance was achieved despite early operational headwinds
5.
Robust margin expansion: Group EBITDA increased by 12.8% to €150.6m in FY25, while basic EPS rose by 28.4% to 46.6c. Operating cash flow reached €162.2m
6.
Primary downside risk: Intense price wars and capacity oversupply from rival ferry operators on the Short Straits represent the primary risk. This price pressure limits visibility on near-term volume trends
7.
Biometric border delays: The implementation of the biometric Entry/Exit System could cause lengthy border crossing delays and depress passenger traffic. This represents a key travel documentation challenge
8.
Favourable initial verdict: A closer look is strongly warranted because ICG is uniquely positioned to capture high margin growth while trading at a low multiple. Capital returns also remain robust

Section 3 – What the company does 15 findings

Section summary
  1. The group operates an integrated, asset-backed maritime platform that holds defensive, market-leading positions within Ireland's core trade corridors.
  2. Long-term port concessions and strategic berthing windows provide high barriers to entry that protect ICG's structural profitability.

Core Segment Operations and Logistics Integration

1.
Extensive ferry services: Under the Irish Ferries brand, the company operates passenger and Roll-on/Roll-off (RoRo) freight services on corridors linking Ireland, Britain, and France. These routes operate as extensions of road networks to facilitate trade
2.
Ferries division dominance: The Ferries Division is the primary earnings driver, generating €465.5m in FY25 compared to €234.6m in the Container and Terminal division. This represents approximately 66% of group revenue
3.
Integrated LoLo shipping: Under the Eucon brand, the company operates a flexible fleet of chartered container ships linking Ireland with major European hub ports. Eucon transported 370,000 teu in FY25, providing door-to-door and quay-to-quay container services across 20 European countries
4.
Dominant terminal operations: The group operates Dublin Ferryport Terminals and Belfast Container Terminal, handling 46% of all lift-on/lift-off movements across Ireland. Terminal concessions provide high operational stability and long-term earnings visibility
5.
Predictable ticket revenue: Passenger transport is purely transactional, with revenues recognized over the short duration of the voyage, ensuring high cash conversion. Booking procedures capture cash prior to departure to minimize receivables risk
6.
Commercial freight contracts: Cargo and RoRo freight revenues are earned through a mix of direct commercial freight contracts and transactional spot bookings. Customer concentration is exceptionally low with no single customer over 10% of revenue

Competitive Advantages and Value Chain Position

7.
Essential island infrastructure: The group occupies a critical infrastructure position within the Irish economy, as Ireland relies entirely on resilient maritime links. This supports long-term structural demand and cash flow predictability
8.
Exceptional lease protections: Secure long-term port concessions and leases, including a 150-year lease in Dublin Port, prevent competitors from duplicating terminals. This concession runs from 1972 to secure multi-decade operations
9.
Entrenched customer stickiness: Long-term relationships with global shipping lines and freight forwarders create high customer stickiness at container terminals. This mitigates the risk of customer churn in periods of economic softness
10.
Scarce berthing access: Ownership of scarce port berthing windows at highly congested ports like Dover and Calais acts as a major barrier. These windows support high schedule reliability and protect passenger market share
11.
Extensive scale advantages: Operating the largest LoLo container terminal in Ireland allows ICG to achieve unmatched operational efficiency and cost advantages. This facilitates over 1,000 truck moves per day at Dublin terminal
12.
Consolidated industry structure: The maritime transport sector remains highly consolidated, with a few large operators controlling key routes and terminal access. Competitors face high capital expenditure requirements for vessel compliance and decarbonisation
13.
Other side of the story: Severe price-cutting from competitors on the Dover-Calais route could lead to sustained yield dilution, offsetting ICG's fleet expansion. This price war limits the near-term profitability of the Channel expansion

Section 5 – The possible investment case 13 findings

Section summary
  1. The possible investment case is highly compelling, supported by fleet ownership savings and capacity rationalisation catalysts.
  2. Full asset control and optimized vessel deployment are expected to drive a highly predictable multi-year EPS trajectory.

Driver 1 – Shift to Full Fleet Ownership

1.
Predictable charter savings: Purchasing previously chartered vessels eliminates high bareboat charter fees and improves operating margin visibility. This simplifies the capital structure and reduces overall leverage volatility
2.
Fleet ownership milestones: The purchase of the James Joyce in April 2025 and the final Oscar Wilde payment in May 2026 secure full fleet ownership. This completes a long-term strategic priority of transitioning away from leased ships
3.
Owned vessel maintenance: This driver would fail if owned vessel maintenance capital expenditure significantly exceeds former charter lease costs. However, the probability of failure is low as the fleet remains highly modern and reliable

Driver 2 – Dover-Calais Capacity Rationalisation

4.
Strategic capacity sharing: The space charter agreement with P&O Ferries allows shared capacity, optimizing sailing schedules and frequency. This space charter agreement with P&O Ferries improves freight network flexibility and enhances competitive differentiator
5.
Deep structural savings: Active sailings fell from 13,153 in FY24 to 11,290 in FY25, driving massive fuel and payroll cost reductions. This was achieved without compromising service availability or schedule integrity
6.
Capacity driver failures: The driver would fail if anti-social dumping regulations or minimum wage laws erode the cost benefits of capacity rationalisation. This remains a key watchlist item for the European short-sea shipping industry

Driver 3 – Container Terminal Capacity Expansion

7.
Strategic terminal growth: Long-term concession extensions and terminal capex programs in Dublin expand container handling capacity. This supports the growing door-to-door and quay-to-quay trade lanes
8.
Long-term concession security: The Belfast Container Terminal concession was extended to 2032, securing multi-year high-margin terminal revenues. This provides long-term runway visibility and stable cash flow projections
9.
Softening global volumes: The container expansion could face headwinds if European macroeconomic volumes soften or enter recession. However, the business is well-positioned to leverage its strong balance sheet
10.
Compound driver independence: These drivers operate independently, allowing the company to grow EPS even if terminal cargo volumes soften. This mitigates compound probability risks for forward earnings forecasts
11.
Other side of the story: A severe European macroeconomic slowdown could depress freight volumes across both segments, crushing operational leverage. This represents the largest macro risk to EPS forecasts over the next 18 months

Section 6 – Valuation 8 findings

Section summary
  1. ICG shares trade at a severe valuation discount compared to high-barrier European infrastructure peers and logistics companies.
1.
Multiple expansion potential: Based on DNB Carnegie estimates (Dec 2024), ICG trades at forward multiples of P/E 7.7x FY24e, 6.9x FY25e, and 6.2x FY26e. This represents an attractive entry point, particularly at a P/E of 6.9x FY25e and 6.2x FY26e
2.
Attractive EV/EBITDA multiples: The forward EV/EBITDA multiples stand at 15.1x FY24e, 12.9x FY25e, and 11.4x FY26e, while EV/EBIT is 2.8x FY24e, 2.5x FY25e, and 2.2x FY26e. These multiples are well below peer averages
3.
Pronounced peer discount: These multiples represent a massive discount to logistics peers and infrastructure play Getlink SE. This persists despite ICG's superior returns
4.
Support from buybacks: Valuation is strongly supported by aggressive share buybacks, with €97.7m returned to shareholders via buybacks in FY25. This reflects management's belief in share under-valuation
5.
Mispriced market fears: The current market valuation appears to over-index on temporary cross-channel price wars and transition capital expenditure. This creates a pricing disconnect for patient investors
6.
Market disconnect: Some analysts note that current share prices reflect a persistent price war on the Channel. This creates an attractive entry point
7.
Other side of the story: A permanent multiple derating could happen if long-term competitive price pressure on the English Channel persists, diluting passenger yields. This price war limits near-term multiple expansion

Section 7 – Key risks and the other side 10 findings

Section summary
  1. The risk profile is dominated by English Channel price competition, fuel price volatility, and biometric travel delays.
  2. Regulatory compliance costs and port infrastructure disruptions present watchpoints for forward earnings forecasts.

Regulatory and Operational Risks

1.
Material fuel volatility: Fuel expenses account for 26% of total ferry operating costs, making margins sensitive to global oil price spikes. Hedging and fuel surcharges help mitigate this impact
2.
Growing carbon taxation: EU ETS carbon surcharges are rising, with expectations of carbon costs increasing from ~€6 in 2025 to ~€11 in 2026. This EU ETS carbon surcharges increases overall ferry operating costs
3.
Impending border friction: The implementation of the EU biometric Entry/Exit System could cause severe border delays, disrupting passenger schedules. This represents a key travel documentation challenge
4.
Escalating wage laws: New UK and French seafarer minimum wage laws could increase labor costs, which represent 51% of operating expenses. This remains a key regulatory risk

Market Structure and Monitoring Signals

5.
Critical bottleneck reliance: Severe weather or berthing incidents at Holyhead Port present significant operational risks that depress passenger revenues. These disruptions create multi-week negative impacts on seasonal earnings
6.
False friend volumes: Growing passenger numbers could mask structural margin decay if they are driven by heavily discounted, low-yield tickets. Yield analysis is vital to assess true earnings health
7.
Principal monitoring signal: Investors must closely track monthly freight market share splits between short-sea ferries and Getlink Shuttle. This serves as an early-warning indicator for price wars on key corridors
8.
Other side of the story: These regulatory cost increases could force financially fragile ferry competitors out of the Channel market, restoring rational pricing. Cost pressures favor large-scale, owned-fleet operators

Section 8 – Management: what is changing 5 findings

Section summary
  1. Corporate leadership is highly stable, but active board compensation and corporate secretary transitions are currently underway.
1.
Important secretary transition: Brian Holland was appointed Company Secretary in 2025, succeeding Thomas Corcoran who had held the post since 2001. Holland previously served as Finance Director of the Ferries Division
2.
Exceptional leadership longevity: Chief Executive Eamonn Rothwell has been a Director for 39 years and CEO since 1992, ensuring deep continuity. His long tenure has anchored the company's strategic vision
3.
Resilient chairman tenure: Chairman John McGuckian continues to lead since 2004, despite minor shareholder pushback regarding long tenure and succession planning. The board has reaffirmed his critical role in guiding strategic expansion
4.
Board fee realignment: Non-executive Director fees were increased effective January 2026 to align with comparable UK listed companies. This was approved following a benchmarking review
