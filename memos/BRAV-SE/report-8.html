§3 — Barriers to Entry + Exit + Disruption Forces

  • Industry verdict: Barriers to entry in Nordic TIS are moderate — not prohibitive to a single-trade local entrant but substantial for a credible multi-trade national operator; disruption forces are real but slow-moving over a 3-5 year horizon [#J]

  • Capital requirement: Capital requirements to start a small TIS business are low (tools, van, licence) but to build a 300-branch multi-trade Nordic network requires SEK 1-2bn+ of accumulated investment and 10-15 years of operational build [#A]

  • Sub: Bravida has SEK ~1.5bn of goodwill + intangibles on balance sheet from acquired branch networks; replicating this organically from scratch would require decades

  • Scale economy: Scale economies accrue via materials procurement rebates (estimated 2-5% advantage vs small operators), IT/systems amortisation, management overhead leverage, and geographic density [#A]

  • Sub: Instalco's counter-model (decentralised, SEK 10bn) suggests scale economies are real but not overwhelming — a SEK 10bn operator is viable without Bravida-level centralisation

  • Regulatory barrier: Trade licensing requirements in Nordics are meaningful barriers — electricians require certified FSE (Auktoriserad Elinstallationsföretag), HVAC requires F-gas certification, and fire-safety requires SP/RISE compliance certification [#A]

  • Sub: Public-sector framework tenders require demonstrated quality management systems (ISO 9001, ISO 14001), health and safety documentation, and financial reference cases — filtering out micro-operators

  • Switching cost: Installed-base knowledge (system drawings, maintenance history, component specifications) is a genuine switching cost for service contracts — a new operator must invest 3-12 months to rebuild the documentation knowledge that an incumbent holds [#A]

  • Sub: For complex HVAC or fire-safety systems in hospitals or data centres, the re-commissioning risk is high enough that customers default to incumbency unless price differential is substantial

  • Distribution barrier: Branch network density is a distribution barrier — Bravida's 300+ branches enable next-day dispatch for service calls across Sweden/Norway/Denmark; a new entrant without equivalent density cannot credibly offer multi-region framework service SLAs [#A]

  • Brand: Bravida brand carries moderate weight for complex project tenders — reference cases and certifications matter more than brand per se in TIS; brand is not a primary economic moat [#A]

  • Structural exit: Barriers to exit for existing operators are low — TIS businesses have minimal fixed assets; a struggling operator can simply decline new work and let contracts run off; industry does not have stranded-asset dynamics [#A]

  • Sub: This low-exit-barrier dynamic means capacity remains in the market through downturns, sustaining price pressure; operators do not face catastrophic losses from exiting

  • Disruption force — FM aggregation: Integrated FM platforms (CBRE, JLL, Sodexo Technical Services, Siemens Smart Infrastructure) are expanding in Nordic commercial property — aggregating TIS procurement into bundled FM contracts that may squeeze standalone TIS operators out of direct customer relationships [#A]

  • Sub: Bravida has partially mitigated this by building its own service/FM capability; risk is that FM aggregators capture the margin-rich service layer

  • Disruption force — technology/digitisation: IoT-enabled predictive maintenance, digital BMS, and automated fault-detection reduce emergency call-out work over time; shifts demand from reactive service toward planned preventive maintenance contracts [#A]

  • Sub: This is a partial threat and partial opportunity — operators with digital capability can increase contract value per customer; those without will see reactive work volumes decline

  • Disruption force — prefabrication/modularisation: Growing use of off-site-fabricated MEP modules (mechanical, electrical, plumbing) in construction reduces on-site labour content; could structurally reduce the labour-hours-per-project for traditional installers [#A]

  • Sub: Currently a trend in data-centre construction (modular UPS/cooling pods) and hospital fit-outs; penetration in standard commercial/residential is limited; horizon is 5-10 years

  • Adjacency entry: Electrical utilities (Fortum, Vattenfall) have expanded into electrical services in some Nordic markets; however, their scale in TIS remains small and they focus primarily on utility grid work [#A]

  • Sub: Security/alarm companies (Securitas, Verisure) have adjacency interest in fire/safety installation — but labour-intensive MEP installation is not their model

  • Innovative entrant: No technology-native TIS disruptor has emerged at scale in Nordics; apps for booking small trades (Hantverkarborsen, Fixa) serve B2C micro-jobs and are not competitive in B2B framework or project work [#A]

  • Sub: Labour dependency of TIS inherently limits the platform-disruption vector — you cannot Uber-ise complex multi-trade project installation requiring certified technicians

  • Vanilla entrant: Vanilla new entrant risk is low — replicating the branch network takes too long; meaningful entrants come via acquisition rather than organic build [#A]

  • Substitutes: Direct substitutes are limited — buildings require physical installation of electrical, HVAC, plumbing, and fire systems; there is no digital substitute for physical MEP infrastructure [#A]

  • Sub: The partial substitute is in-house facilities management teams (large industrial operators with internal electricians) but this model is declining as companies outsource FM

  • Sceptical bullet: The regulatory-barrier read could weaken if Nordic governments simplify trade licensing in response to labour shortages (a politically live debate in Sweden), lowering the quality-certification hurdle for non-Nordic-trained tradespeople and enabling Eastern European sub-contractors to compete more directly on certified work through the mechanism of mutual recognition of EU trade qualifications under Posted Workers Directive [#J]
