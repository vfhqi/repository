[C] VEI-NO | Triaging #4 | Earnings & Fundamental Trends | 04-May-26

KEY FINDINGS

  • Margin recovery is real but concentrated in one segment. The full-year 2024 pre-tax margin of 4.1% (up from 3.3% in 2023) is predominantly an Infrastructure Norway story — specifically the asphalt operation turnaround. Construction Norway, the largest revenue segment, remains under volume pressure with thin margins, limiting the durability of the group-level improvement narrative.

  • Revenue is shrinking, not growing. Group revenue fell from NOK 43.1bn (2023) to NOK 41.4bn (2024), a decline of approximately 4% — driven by Construction Norway and Construction Sweden contracting. The order book at NOK 41.0bn at year-end 2024 is essentially flat against LTM revenue, implying no volume growth buffer entering 2025. This is a fragility signal: order coverage of circa 1.0x revenue with 60% converting within 12 months leaves limited margin for error on new order intake.

  • Order book acceleration in Q1 2025 is encouraging but not confirmed recovery. Q1 2025 order intake of NOK 12.0bn (up 18% on Q1 2024) and a backlog rebuild to NOK 44.4bn is structurally positive and signals genuine residential market stabilisation. However, 30% of Q1 2025 orders relate to residential buildings — a segment that contracted severely in 2022–2024. Residential re-entry carries margin risk if clients retain pricing leverage in a demand-recovery environment.

  • Peer relative positioning is mediocre. VEI's 4.1% pre-tax margin in 2024 sits below AF Gruppen (5.2%), well below Strabag (6.1%), and broadly in line with Skanska Construction (3.5%). VEI is not a margin leader in its peer group. The fulcrum driver needed for above-peer EPS growth (CQ-C4) is not yet visible — infrastructure margins are not structurally superior enough to lift the group while construction volumes decline.

  • False Friend warning on backlog. The backlog build from NOK 41.0bn to NOK 44.4bn through Q1 2025 is superficially constructive. However, the margin quality of newly-won residential and infrastructure orders has not been disclosed at a level that allows investors to verify whether backlog growth translates to margin expansion. Given input cost complexity and industry fixed-price norms, backlog growth alone is a false friend.

  • Cash conversion declined materially in 2024. Operational cash flow fell from NOK 2.9bn (2023) to NOK 2.2bn (2024), a 24% decline, despite pre-tax profit rising 17%. This working capital divergence — where earnings rose but cash fell — is a quality-of-earnings red flag requiring explanation. Net interest-bearing assets declined from NOK 2.8bn to NOK 2.6bn over the same period.

  • EPS of NOK 9.3 and full-payout dividend of NOK 9.0 signals balance sheet discipline but limited reinvestment ambition. A dividend that equals 97% of EPS is extraordinary in the context of a business with volume decline challenges. This is either a statement of confidence in FCF sustainability or a capital allocation policy that forecloses growth investment. Either interpretation is not obviously bullish for EPS compounding.

  • 2025 momentum is strengthening but the base case is modest. Q3 2025 showed revenues up 10% year-on-year to NOK 10.7bn with a 6.8% margin — the highest quarterly margin in recent years. If this trajectory persists through Q4 2025, full-year 2025 could meaningfully beat 2024. But the revenue growth is partly catch-up from the volume trough, and margin upside depends on sustained infrastructure execution and a housing recovery that is still early-stage.

  • Norweigan construction market cycle points to 2026 as the earliest structural upturn. Market output contracted approximately 3.9% in 2024 and is forecast down a further 2% in 2025 before a 3.4% AAGR recovery through 2026–2028. VEI is positioned for this cycle but the timing and extent of residential volume recovery — its largest construction sub-segment — will determine whether EPS trajectory is structural or merely operating-leverage catch-up.

  • No immediate disqualifiers, but no compelling catalyst for Stage 3 progression yet. VEI clears the basic screens: no leverage stress, no fraud signals, no catastrophic project impairments. But it lacks an identifiable above-peer EPS growth driver in the N18-36M window. The case at this stage is mean-reversion / cycle recovery, not structural outperformance. That is not a triage kill, but it does not justify fast-tracking to ESA without further work on competitive positioning and margin trajectory.

1. Executive Summary

Section Rating: Yellow

  • Peer stack-rank by 2024 EBIT/pre-tax margin: Strabag 6.1% > AF Gruppen 5.2% > Veidekke 4.1% > Skanska Construction 3.5%. By this single metric, VEI is in the second quartile of its peer group — a reasonable position but not a standout.

  • VEI's pre-tax margin improvement from 3.3% to 4.1% year-on-year is meaningful. This is the first year of material margin recovery after a three-year period of compression. The improvement is 80bps in a single year, which in the context of a low-margin, high-volume construction business is noteworthy.

  • Revenue contraction is the dominant fundamental concern. The group shrank 4% in 2024. No peer in the cohort showed comparable revenue contraction: AF Gruppen grew revenue, Strabag held output volumes flat, Skanska rebuilt its backlog to record highs. VEI is a laggard on volume momentum.

  • The Infrastructure Norway segment is carrying the group. Asphalt operations specifically drove the 2024 profit improvement. These are semi-recurring, maintenance-type revenues with reasonable pricing power from public road authorities. This is structurally more attractive than lump-sum building contracts. But it is a relatively small proportion of group revenue.

  • Construction Norway is the swing factor. It is the largest segment by revenue, exposed to the residential and institutional market that has been deeply depressed since 2022. Any material recovery here would be highly significant for group-level earnings power.

  • The sector tailwind is real but delayed. Norwegian construction market output forecast to begin recovering in 2026. Swedish infrastructure investment pipeline (SEK 354bn road + SEK 210bn additional) provides multi-year civil engineering visibility for the Sweden operations.

  • Skanska is the most relevant pure-play benchmark. Both are Nordic multi-segment contractors without material developer exposure. Skanska's Construction margin of 3.5% in 2024 is actually below VEI's group margin, suggesting VEI's infrastructure mix provides a margin premium. This is a genuine positive differentiator.

  • AF Gruppen at 5.2% pre-tax margin is the benchmark to aspire to. AF's superior margin is partly a function of its decommissioning / offshore segment (higher-margin specialty work) and a more selective project book. VEI lacks an equivalent high-margin anchor segment.

  • Strabag is an outlier in this peer group. Strabag's 6.1% EBIT margin in 2024 reflects its scale, central European construction market strength (notably Germany/Austria), and cost efficiency from vertical integration. It is not a directly relevant benchmark for VEI given different market geographies.

  • The order book entering 2025 is flat at approximately 1.0x LTM revenue. This is adequate but not expansionary. Peers such as AF Gruppen at NOK 44.7bn backlog against NOK ~30bn revenue show considerably stronger coverage ratios. VEI's order book provides only 12 months of confirmed revenue visibility.

  • Sector headwinds: Rising material costs, skilled labour shortages in Scandinavia, and tight public-sector budget pressures in Norway post-hydrocarbon revenue moderation are structural challenges. Infrastructure spend is the offset, but this is a competitive market.

  • The key sector tailwind is energy transition infrastructure. Statnett's NOK 150–200bn grid investment plan by 2035 and Sweden's grid development plan create a substantial civil engineering pipeline directly relevant to VEI's Infrastructure Norway and Infrastructure Sweden segments.

  • VEI's cooperative ownership model (employee-owned) creates a cultural advantage in talent retention and alignment, but also constrains capital flexibility. Management cannot issue equity freely for acquisitions, limiting inorganic growth optionality versus listed peers.

  • Overall fundamental momentum: Improving from a low base in 2024 but still in recovery mode. 2025 momentum through Q3 is clearly stronger. The sector cycle is not yet confirmed as a full upturn. VEI is well-positioned for the recovery but not obviously mis-priced relative to a still-uncertain macro backdrop.

2. Revenue Growth & Organic Dynamics

Section Rating: Orange

  • Full-year 2024 revenue of NOK 41.4bn represents a 3.9% year-on-year decline from NOK 43.1bn in 2023. This follows revenue growth of approximately 11% in 2023 (from NOK 38.7bn in 2022). The 2024 decline is therefore a reversal after a period of strong nominal growth — significant because the 2022–2023 growth was partly inflation-driven, so real volumes contracted more than the nominal number implies.

  • Q4 2024 revenue of NOK 11.5bn was down 4.9% from NOK 12.1bn in Q4 2023. The quarterly decline was explicitly attributed to Construction Norway and Construction Sweden — the two largest revenue segments. Infrastructure Norway and Denmark held up better.

  • Construction Norway is the primary revenue drag. This segment is exposed to the residential construction market, which experienced some of the sharpest volume contractions in Europe in 2023–2024 due to Norway's interest rate cycle. Norwegian housing starts fell materially from 2022 peaks, and Veidekke's construction backlog in residential tracks this closely.

  • Construction Sweden is a secondary revenue drag. Sweden's construction market experienced analogous pressures: rising interest rates, falling residential starts, and a very weak new-build market through 2023–2024. Veidekke's Swedish construction operation wrote down NOK 37m of goodwill in Q4 2024, consistent with an operation facing structural volume challenges.

  • Infrastructure Norway is the revenue stabiliser. The segment benefited from public road maintenance contracts and asphalt work, which is less cyclical than residential construction. Road maintenance is annual-tender, high-utilisation work with relatively predictable volumes. This segment is the revenue quality anchor.

  • Revenue by geography: Norway is approximately 60–65% of group revenues, Sweden approximately 30–35%, Denmark the balance. This geographic distribution means VEI is highly exposed to two markets that both contracted in 2024. There is no geographic diversification into higher-growth markets.

  • Organic revenue versus acquired revenue is difficult to disaggregate from public disclosures. The acquisition of Euromining by Infrastructure Sweden (referenced in Q3 2025 commentary) adds some inorganic revenue in 2025 but is not material at the group level.

  • Q1 2025 revenue of NOK 9.0bn was down 5.3% from NOK 9.5bn in Q1 2024, continuing the contraction trend. However, Q1 is a seasonally weak quarter in Nordic construction. Management explicitly flagged that the revenue decline is primarily driven by Construction Sweden.

  • Q3 2025 revenue of NOK 10.7bn was up 10% from NOK 9.7bn in Q3 2024. This is the first meaningful year-on-year revenue growth in multiple quarters and is highly significant. It suggests the volume trough may have been reached in H1 2024 and that the recovery is materialising.

  • Order intake dynamics are improving markedly. Q1 2025 order intake of NOK 12.0bn versus NOK 10.2bn in Q1 2024 represents 18% growth. For a business generating approximately NOK 40bn of annual revenue, a NOK 12bn single-quarter order intake (nearly 1.2x quarterly revenue) is a genuine positive signal.

  • 30% of Q1 2025 order intake is residential. This is notable because residential was effectively frozen in 2023–2024. The reopening of the residential order pipeline suggests client developers are returning to the market — almost certainly catalysed by Norwegian interest rate cuts and improved affordability.

  • Pricing power in construction is structurally limited. On fixed-price lump-sum work (a material portion of construction contracts), contractors absorb input cost risk. VEI, like all Scandinavian contractors, has faced material input cost headwinds since 2022. Price escalation clauses have become more common but coverage is incomplete. The risk of fixed-price contract losses remains live even as volumes recover.

  • Revenue guidance for 2025 is not explicit. Management's Q4 2024 commentary was that approximately 60% of the NOK 41.0bn order book would convert to revenue in 2025 — implying approximately NOK 24.6bn of secured revenue from the backlog, leaving approximately NOK 15–16bn to be won and executed. This is a higher-than-normal reliance on new awards for annual revenue.

  • Consensus estimates (10 analysts, May 2026) project revenue growth of approximately 3.4% per annum. Given 2024 at NOK 41.4bn, this implies NOK ~43bn by 2026. This is a modest and plausible recovery profile but does not require heroic residential market assumptions.

  • Sceptical view on revenue: The recovery narrative is dependent on (a) residential construction volumes recovering on schedule in 2026, (b) no deterioration in public infrastructure spend in Norway (which is sensitive to the oil fund's draw-down rate), and (c) Construction Sweden finding volume stabilisation. None of these is guaranteed. A 12-month delay in housing recovery alone would put the consensus revenue trajectory under pressure.

  • Market share signals are ambiguous. VEI's revenue fell 4% in 2024 while the Norwegian construction market contracted approximately 4% (and Swedish similarly). This implies broadly market-rate performance — neither gaining nor losing significant share. But AF Gruppen, a direct Norwegian competitor, appears to have maintained revenues better. Market share loss to a better-capitalized or lower-cost competitor is a live risk.

3. Margin Profile & Evolution

Section Rating: Yellow

  • The L3Y pre-tax margin trajectory is: 2022 = ~3.8%, 2023 = 3.3%, 2024 = 4.1%. This shows a dip and recovery pattern. The 2023 trough coincided with peak input cost inflation. The 2024 recovery reflects both lower input cost pressure and specific operational improvements in Infrastructure Norway.

  • The reported margin metric is pre-tax profit margin (profit before tax / revenue), not EBIT margin in the strict sense. VEI reports profitability as profit before tax. For comparability, this is broadly equivalent to pre-tax EBIT margin given minimal below-EBIT items. Using this metric, the peer comparison stands: AFG 5.2% > VEI 4.1% > SKA 3.5%.

  • Infrastructure Norway drove the 2024 margin recovery. Management explicitly attributed "the profit increase in 2024 primarily to infrastructure operations, and improved profitability of the Norwegian asphalt operation in particular." This is a specific operational improvement rather than a broad market-driven margin expansion. It matters for the durability question: can asphalt margins be sustained, and can other segments close the gap?

  • Construction Norway margins are below group average. The segment experienced lower capacity utilisation in Q4 2024 and has been through a period of volume contraction. Thin construction margins (likely 1.5–2.5% in building work) create significant operating leverage exposure in both directions: volume recovery would expand margins, but fixed cost absorption remains poor in the current volume environment.

  • Construction Sweden is a structural concern. Goodwill impairment of NOK 37m in Q4 2024 signals that management internally assessed the value of the Swedish construction operation as impaired relative to acquisition cost. This is an admission that expected profitability has declined. The Swedish market is expected to remain weak through 2025 with only gradual improvement.

  • Infrastructure Sweden has mixed dynamics. An asset sale (warehouse in Stockholm) generating a NOK 29m loss on joint ventures in Q4 2024, plus the broader Euromining acquisition, indicate an operation in restructuring mode. This is normal corporate activity but adds execution noise to the margin picture.

  • Q4 2024 quarterly margin of 4.9% versus 5.0% in Q4 2023 shows sequential resilience. Q4 is seasonally the strongest quarter for Nordic construction. The near-flat margin year-on-year despite revenue declining 4.9% implies that cost management partially offset volume deleveraging.

  • Q3 2025 margin of 6.8% versus 6.4% in Q3 2024 is the most impressive recent data point. A 40bps year-on-year improvement in a seasonally peak quarter, combined with 10% revenue growth, suggests genuine operating leverage is beginning to manifest from the volume recovery. This is not a margin illusion.

  • Operating leverage analysis: In a construction business, fixed costs (labour overheads, equipment leases, administrative functions) create meaningful operating leverage. VEI's workforce is partly unionised and the cooperative structure limits aggressive headcount reduction. When volumes fell in 2023–2024, margins compressed. As volumes recover in 2025, the reverse leverage is visible in Q3 2025.

  • Input cost environment is normalising. Norwegian construction cost inflation was approximately 3.7% in 2024 (construction cost index for residential buildings), down from higher peaks in 2022–2023. Normalising input costs reduce the margin headwind from fixed-price contract exposures in the active backlog.

  • The asphalt business is the margin quality anchor. Norwegian road maintenance contracts are typically annual, re-tendered, and priced to reflect current input costs. This means the asphalt segment has better pricing pass-through than fixed-price building contracts. As the infrastructure segment grows as a proportion of group revenue, the underlying margin profile improves structurally.

  • N2Y consensus margin trajectory: With 3.9% EPS growth and 3.4% revenue growth implied by consensus (roughly flat margin), sell-side does not appear to be pricing in meaningful further margin expansion beyond 2024. This is either conservative or reflects genuine uncertainty about whether the 4.1% margin is repeatable. Given Q3 2025's 6.8% quarterly margin, full-year 2025 looks likely to exceed 2024.

  • Margin decomposition: Asphalt and civil engineering operations likely operate at 4–6% margins. Building construction likely at 1.5–3%. Group blended margin of 4.1% suggests a revenue mix that is approximately 40–50% infrastructure / 50–60% construction. Margin improvement requires either (a) growing infrastructure to a larger share, (b) recovering building construction margins through volume, or (c) exiting structurally impaired operations.

  • Sceptical margin view: The risk is that the 2024 margin improvement is an asphalt-specific windfall tied to benign raw material pricing and a high-volume season, not a structural step-change. If bitumen prices (the primary asphalt input cost, linked to oil) normalise upward — which is possible in the current Iran-risk environment — the Infrastructure Norway margin could retrace. The construction segments have not demonstrated margin recovery capability independently.

  • Peer margin comparison and gap: AFG's 1.1 percentage point margin advantage over VEI is attributable to its offshore decommissioning and energy services work, not its Norwegian construction operations per se. VEI cannot easily replicate this via organic means — it would require M&A into adjacent higher-margin segments, for which there is no stated strategy.

4. EPS Trajectory & Delivery

Section Rating: Yellow

  • Full-year 2024 EPS of NOK 9.3 per share. This compares to what can be inferred as approximately NOK 7.9–8.0 per share in 2023 (pre-tax profit of NOK 1,444m in 2023 growing to NOK 1,683m in 2024, roughly 17%). The year-on-year EPS growth rate is approximately 16–18%, driven by profit improvement rather than share count changes.

  • The absolute EPS level in 2024 is at or near multi-year highs. The recovery from the 2022–2023 margin compression trough means that current EPS probably represents a cycle peak rather than a structural floor. The key question is whether EPS can grow from here or whether 2024 was the inflection high.

  • Consensus EPS growth of approximately 4% per annum. If 2024 EPS is NOK 9.3, consensus 2025E and 2026E EPS is approximately NOK 9.7 and NOK 10.1 respectively. These are modest growth rates — not the kind of above-peer EPS trajectory that would justify a premium multiple. At these growth rates, the stock is likely priced as a yield play, not a growth play.

  • However, Q3 2025 profit per share of NOK 4.0 for a single quarter suggests full-year 2025 EPS could materially exceed consensus. Q3 is the peak quarter. If Q1 2025 EPS was approximately -NOK 0.1 (given -NOK 21m pre-tax), Q2 and Q4 are unknown but likely constructive. A back-of-envelope 2025 EPS estimate using Q3 alone implies full-year might be NOK 11–12, well ahead of the approximately NOK 9.7 consensus.

  • The dividend of NOK 9.0 per share (97% payout ratio) is extraordinary. In a European construction context, most peers maintain 40–60% payout ratios to preserve capital for working capital swings and growth investment. VEI's near-100% payout implies the board has extremely high confidence in recurring cash generation, or is returning capital because growth investment opportunities are limited.

  • Net interest-bearing assets of NOK 2.6bn at year-end 2024. VEI is in a net cash position — there is no debt servicing drag on EPS. This is a structural positive and means EPS is not at risk from rising interest rates. The net cash position also provides a buffer for potential working capital swings in a market recovery environment (residential construction typically absorbs working capital ahead of revenue).

  • Share repurchase authority of up to 10% of share capital exists but has not been actively utilised. Management conditions buybacks on the share price being "below actual market value" — a subjective threshold. There is no visible history of consistent buyback execution. EPS accretion from buybacks is therefore not a reliable component of the EPS growth story.

  • Operational cash flow of NOK 2.2bn in 2024 versus pre-tax profit of NOK 1.68bn gives a cash conversion ratio of approximately 1.3x profit — superficially excellent. However, cash flow fell 24% from NOK 2.9bn in 2023, while profit grew 17%. This suggests a significant working capital outflow in 2024 that absorbed profitability gains. If working capital continues to build as volumes recover in 2025, operating cash flow could disappoint even if reported profits improve.

  • Capital expenditure of approximately NOK 956m in 2024 (net investments in machinery, equipment, and acquisitions) versus NOK 1.4bn in 2023. The reduction in capex is partly a volume response — lower activity requires less equipment investment. As volumes recover, capex should normalise upward, partially constraining FCF growth.

  • EPS delivery versus stated guidance: Veidekke does not provide explicit EPS guidance. The company provides qualitative commentary on market conditions and order book outlook. This makes formal delivery versus guidance analysis difficult. However, the 17% profit growth in 2024 against a backdrop of market-level revenue contraction is a genuine delivery beat versus external expectations.

  • Below the line items to watch: VEI's profit before tax includes gains and losses from joint ventures (e.g. the NOK 29m JV loss in Q4 2024). These are normal but variable items. The Infrastructure Norway Q4 2024 non-recurring reorganisation costs of NOK 32m are a modest drag that should not recur in 2025.

  • Goodwill write-down of NOK 37m in Construction Sweden. This is an accounting charge with no cash impact but signals that the expected future profitability of the Swedish construction business has been revised downward. It is a modest negative for the quality of the balance sheet and confirms the structural challenges in that segment.

  • EPS sensitivity to a residential construction market recovery is high. Given the operating leverage in Construction Norway, a volume recovery from a deeply depressed base would add significant profit even at modest incremental margins. A 10% revenue recovery in Construction Norway at a 2% margin would add approximately NOK 60–80m to group profit — roughly 4–5% of current pre-tax profit.

  • Sceptical EPS view: The risk to the EPS story is that 2024's improvement is a single-factor (asphalt margin) event that does not repeat. If Infrastructure Norway margins normalise, Construction Norway remains volume-challenged, and Construction Sweden does not recover, the group could deliver flat-to-declining EPS in 2025 despite rising revenues. The Q3 2025 data point challenges this bear case but does not eliminate it.

5. Guidance & Forecast Changes

Section Rating: Yellow

  • Veidekke does not provide quantitative earnings guidance. Management provides qualitative market commentary and order book statistics. This is standard for European mid-cap contractors. The primary forward indicator is the order book and the 12-month conversion rate.

  • Management Q4 2024 guidance language was cautiously optimistic. CEO Bengtsson: "We have taken on 2025 from a good starting point, with a robust and balanced portfolio of quality projects, and with risk under control." The language of "risk under control" is notable — it signals that management is focused on margin quality of won work, not just volume.

  • The 60% order book conversion guidance for 2025 (approximately NOK 24.6bn from the NOK 41bn end-2024 backlog) establishes a minimum revenue floor for the year. With Q1 2025 revenues of NOK 9.0bn already executing against this, the conversion pace appears broadly on schedule.

  • Q1 2025 order intake guidance language: VEI explicitly noted that the construction market is "approaching a turning point" following the publication of a market report. This is a qualitative signal of management's view that the volume trough is near or passed.

  • The 2025 outlook hinges on three variables: (1) speed of residential construction market recovery in Norway; (2) Infrastructure Norway's ability to sustain 2024's asphalt operational improvements; (3) Construction Sweden's stabilisation trajectory. Management has given constructive commentary on all three but provided no numeric targets.

  • Sell-side consensus trajectory: 10 analysts cover VEI with a consensus 12-month price target of approximately NOK 179.50 (range NOK 165–194). Revenue growth consensus of 3.4% per annum and EPS growth of 4% per annum imply a modest, gradual recovery scenario with no dramatic inflection. This consensus was likely set before Q3 2025's 10% revenue growth data point.

  • Q3 2025 results (revenues +10%, margin 6.8%) are materially ahead of the implied consensus trajectory. If Q3 is not an anomaly, there is meaningful upside to consensus 2025 estimates. The key question is whether Q4 2025 maintained this momentum or reverted.

  • The Q4 2025 full-year results (available January/February 2026) will be the critical data point for confirming or challenging the recovery narrative. Management's language at Q3 2025 — "2025 ended strongly, with increased revenues and higher profits than in Q4 of last year, with a profit margin of 6.8%" — appears to reference full-year 2025 guidance.

  • Guidance credibility assessment: Veidekke's management track record on guidance is reasonable. The 2023–2024 period demonstrated that they navigated a difficult market without issuing profit warnings on the scale seen by some European construction peers. The 2024 profit growth of 17% versus market expectations (given revenue contraction) was a positive surprise.

  • Risk to guidance from Swedish operations. Construction Sweden is the most uncertain segment. Goodwill impairment in Q4 2024 and ongoing volume weakness make this the most likely source of negative guidance revision. If Swedish construction markets remain weak through 2025, the recovery timeline for group revenue shifts to the right.

  • Norwegian interest rate cycle is the macro catalyst. Norges Bank began cutting rates in 2024. Further easing through 2025 should improve residential construction affordability and pull forward housing starts. The speed of this cycle is the key upside driver to guidance for Construction Norway.

  • No profit warning history in the review period. VEI has not issued a formal profit warning in 2022–2024. This is a positive indicator of management discipline in project selection and cost management. However, the absence of warnings in a difficult market may also reflect the cushion provided by the infrastructure segment, which could mask deterioration in the construction business.

  • Sceptical guidance view: The transformation from NOK 1.44bn pre-tax profit (2023) to NOK 1.68bn (2024) was driven by a single segment (asphalt). If the company guides for NOK 1.8–2.0bn in 2025 based on market recovery assumptions, the key risk is that residential construction volumes do not accelerate as expected, leaving the company dependent on another exceptional Infrastructure Norway year. This is not a wide-base earnings recovery; it remains segment-concentrated.

6. Comparative Peer Ranking

Section Rating: Yellow

  • Peer universe for this analysis: AF Gruppen (AFG-NO), Strabag (STR-AT), Skanska (SKA-SE), Balfour Beatty (BBY-GB). VEI is ranked against each on the core metrics of revenue growth, margin, EPS momentum, and order book quality.

  • Revenue growth 2024 ranking (best to worst): Strabag (flat, holding at ~EUR 17.4bn) > AF Gruppen (slight growth) > Skanska (record order intake, positioning for revenue growth) > Veidekke (-4%) > Balfour Beatty (modest growth). VEI is the weakest on revenue momentum in the recent period.

  • Pre-tax / EBIT margin ranking 2024: Strabag (6.1%) > AF Gruppen (5.2%) > Veidekke (4.1%) > Skanska Construction (3.5%). VEI sits in the middle of the pack. Strabag's leadership is partly structural (Central European market strength, scale). AF Gruppen's leadership reflects its diversified higher-margin offshore and decommissioning work.

  • Order book coverage (backlog / LTM revenue): AF Gruppen (NOK 44.7bn / ~NOK 30bn = ~1.5x) > Strabag (EUR 25.4bn / EUR 17.4bn = ~1.46x) > Skanska (book-to-build >1.0x with 123% B2B ratio over L12M) > Veidekke (NOK 41bn / NOK 41.4bn = ~1.0x) > Balfour Beatty (adequate but UK-specific). VEI is the weakest on order book coverage — a direct read on revenue visibility and growth optionality.

  • EPS growth L12M ranking: Veidekke (+17% pre-tax profit growth) = best in class on this single metric in 2024. However, this growth is from a low base after a deep trough, making it a recovery metric rather than structural growth evidence.

  • Forward EPS growth (consensus): Strabag targeting EBIT margin >4.5% in 2025 and output volume ~EUR 21bn (growth) places it well ahead on forward earnings growth. AF Gruppen's NOK 44.7bn backlog provides strong forward visibility. VEI's 4% consensus EPS growth is the most modest in the peer group.

  • Balance sheet quality: VEI (net cash NOK 2.6bn), AF Gruppen (net cash positive), Skanska (conservative balance sheet), Strabag (strong balance sheet). The peer group is broadly well-capitalised. No leverage concerns for VEI. However, VEI's near-100% payout ratio means the balance sheet is not being rebuilt for growth.

  • Dividend yield as a differentiator: VEI's NOK 9.0 dividend against a consensus price target of approximately NOK 180 implies a dividend yield of approximately 5%. This makes VEI a high-yield construction stock in Scandinavian context. For income-oriented investors, this is attractive. For growth investors, the near-100% payout constrains reinvestment.

  • Rank change L3M: VEI has improved relative to peers primarily due to Q3 2025 performance becoming known. Revenue growth of 10% in Q3 2025 versus flat-to-negative peers in the same period suggests market share recovery or sector-specific acceleration.

  • Rank change L6M: Broadly flat in relative positioning. The fundamental story has been consistent: VEI recovering from a trough, peers also recovering but from less severe bases.

  • Rank change L12M: VEI has improved most on margin momentum (3.3% to 4.1%) but remains last on order book coverage and forward revenue growth visibility. Net rank movement: upward on quality metrics, stable on volume metrics.

  • AF Gruppen is the most comparable peer — both are Norwegian multi-segment contractors, both are employee/cooperative in ownership culture, both are exposed to the same residential market cycle. AF Gruppen's consistent margin advantage over VEI is a strategic puzzle worth investigating: is it sustainable, and what does VEI need to do to close it?

  • Strabag is the aspirational benchmark. Its 6.1% EBIT margin in 2024 shows what is achievable in European construction at scale. But Strabag operates in different geographies (Germany, Austria, Central/Eastern Europe) with different competitive dynamics. Direct comparison should be cautious.

  • Sceptical peer view: VEI's relative improvement on margin in 2024 could be reversed if the asphalt business reverts to average. Peers like Skanska and AF Gruppen have more diversified margin drivers. VEI's relative outperformance in 2024 was partly luck of segment mix; sustained peer-beating performance requires multiple segments improving simultaneously.

7. Sector & Value Chain Context

Section Rating: Yellow/Green

  • The Norwegian construction market contracted approximately 3.9% in real terms in 2024, following a contraction of approximately 1.8% in 2023. This was the deepest consecutive contraction in recent memory, driven by the fastest interest rate cycle in a generation. The market trough appears to have been reached in 2024.

  • Recovery is forecast to begin in 2025 (modestly) and accelerate through 2026–2028. Independent market forecasters project Norwegian construction output growth at 2% in 2025 and a AAGR of approximately 3.4% from 2026 to 2028. This is a meaningful medium-term tailwind for VEI's core market.

  • Residential construction is the laggard but the most powerful recovery driver. Norwegian residential building permits fell approximately 16.8% in 2023 and remained suppressed in 2024. But early 2025 data shows a clear uptick in permits, consistent with the interest rate cutting cycle feeding through to developer confidence. When residential starts recover, VEI's Construction Norway segment has the highest operating leverage to benefit.
