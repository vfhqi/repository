§2 — Change in Leadership

[J] Leadership instability is the single most concerning signal at AFX — two CEO transitions in seven months suggests parent-company dissatisfaction, not routine succession planning.

CEO timeline:

  • Dr. Markus Weber — CEO for multiple years, departed by mutual agreement effective 31-May-25. 22-year ZEISS veteran. Credible operationally; oversaw the DORC deal and the digital ecosystem strategy. Departure cited as personal ("right time for a new challenge") but the timing — mid-margin compression cycle, immediately post large acquisition — reads as a managed exit.
  • Maximilian Foerst — appointed 01-Jun-25, terminated by mutual agreement effective 31-Dec-25. Six-month tenure only. Previously Head of ZEISS Greater China since 2009, responsible for ~€2bn Greater China revenues. The appointment thesis was explicit: AFX's biggest problem is China, so appoint the China expert. The removal after six months is deeply concerning — either the China problems are more structurally entrenched than Foerst's playbook could fix, or there was a parent/board cultural mismatch.
  • Andreas Pecher — interim from 01-Jan-26. Simultaneously CEO of Carl Zeiss AG (the 70% parent). Running two entities. Background is corporate operations/M&A, not ophthalmology. No defined AFX-specific turnaround strategy on public record. Promised update "no later than 12-May-26."

CFO and C-suite:

  • Justus Felix Wehmer — CFO, appears to be continuing. Presented the Q1 FY26 results. His language was unambiguous: "geopolitical uncertainties, noticeable restraint in investment decisions, changes in regulatory framework." No heroic cost-saving narrative was presented.
  • Board composition not changed publicly in L12M. Parent Carl Zeiss AG controls ~70% of shares — the Supervisory Board is effectively controlled by the parent.

Governance and activism:

  • No external activist involvement. With 70% parent control, there is no realistic activist pathway.
  • The parent has strong incentives to stabilise AFX given its own balance sheet exposure and the strategic fit of the ophthalmology platform within the ZEISS group.

❌ The "Animal CEO" WL model test fails at AFX today. No permanent CEO. Interim is running two organisations. No defined change plan. The absence of an animal CEO is itself a thesis risk — until one is named, it is premature to underwrite a corporate change scenario.

§3 — Change in Strategy/Structure

[J] The strategic vision (full-line ophthalmology + digital ecosystem + recurring revenue) is coherent and differentiated, but execution capability has been insufficient for two consecutive years.

Growth plan and stated targets:

  • Medium-term target: EBIT margin "sustainably above 20%" — reiterated as recently as FY25 annual report but not put on a specific timeline.
  • Revenue ambition: become the "top player in ophthalmology globally" — clearly aspirational rather than a hard financial target.
  • Consumables/services targeting ~50% of revenue — not yet achieved; equipment mix remains dominant.

R&D prioritisation:

  • R&D spend ~12-14% of revenues (well-funded vs peers).
  • Stated reprioritisation of R&D activities as part of the upcoming restructuring plan (May-12). No specifics yet disclosed.
  • Focus on three integrated platforms: (1) surgical workflow (ARTEVO 850/VisuMax 800/CALLISTO eye); (2) diagnostic imaging (Cirrus OCT family, Humphrey Field Analyzer); (3) vitreoretinal (DORC EVA NEXUS + ZEISS instruments).

Geographic strategy — China:

  • China previously ~20-25% of revenues and higher-margin (premium IOL mix, consumables).
  • Localisation strategy announced: two existing production sites in Guangzhou and Suzhou; plan to shift more manufacturing into China to mitigate VBP and geopolitical risk.
  • VisuMax 800 (SMILE Pro) received China NMPA approval — a genuine positive signal.
  • ❌ Localisation is a 3-5 year capital programme, not a near-term fix. The VBP IOL pricing shock and bifocal product quality failure land in FY26 before any local manufacturing advantage accrues.

DORC integration:

  • Closed April 2024 for ~€985m (equity value). Full consolidation from FY24/25.
  • DORC's EVA NEXUS platform: leading vitreoretinal surgical system. High consumable content (DORC specialises in retinal instruments and single-use consumables).
  • Strategic logic: AFX was weak in posterior/vitreoretinal; DORC fills the gap. Combined with ARTEVO 800/850 surgical microscope, creates a full-room posterior surgery offer.
  • Revenue contribution: Ophthalmology SBU grew +8.5% in FY25 but only +2.3% organic — the delta is almost entirely DORC.
  • [J] DORC is strategically right but financially timed poorly. Bought at top-cycle for ophthalmology equipment, funded with debt, integrating while the core business is under pressure.

Cost structure:

  • "Cost control programme" initiated in FY24 — described as achieving "sideways trend in operating costs."
  • Savings "in the low to mid-double-digit millions of euros" flagged for FY25 — modest relative to the revenue base.
  • Full restructuring plan deferred to 12-May-26. Commentary references "accelerating product localisation, reprioritising R&D activities, and implementing additional efficiency initiatives."

§5 — Change in Shareholder Returns

[J] Capital allocation is constrained by DORC debt and margin collapse — there is no credible near-term shareholder return story here.

  • Dividend: €0.55 proposed for FY25 (vs €0.60 FY24, vs higher prior years). ~2.2% yield at a ~€25 price. Not a yield play.
  • Buyback: No evidence of a buyback programme in the search record. Given the balance sheet absorption from DORC, buybacks are not a near-term prospect.
  • Capital allocation philosophy: The DORC acquisition at €985m was a sizeable bet (~4-5% of market cap at transaction prices). The timing, in hindsight, was poorly judged — bought at the top of an equipment cycle.
  • Peer comparison: Alcon has been executing a differentiated capital allocation — selective M&A (Aerie, Regener-Eyes), progressive dividend, modest buybacks. EssilorLuxottica (EL.PA) has maintained strong FCF conversion. AFX is at a disadvantage post-DORC on capital optionality.

§6 — Change in Demand/Revenue Dynamics

[J] The demand picture is bifurcated: procedure volumes are growing (structural demographic tailwind intact) but capital equipment spend is being deferred, and China consumables pricing is being permanently repriced lower.

Cataract surgery — equipment:

  • Underlying procedure volumes: globally resilient, driven by ageing populations. The Covid-era deferral bump has largely normalised.
  • Hospital capex: Management guidance explicitly states "no quick recovery in the investment climate for equipment" — this is their own assessment of their core market.
  • Phacoaemulsification platforms: AFX (CATAYST, CINERA series) competes directly with Alcon (Centurion/Phacoemulsification), J&J (Veritas). Alcon's Unity VCS platform integrates cataract and vitreoretinal on one console — direct threat to AFX's standalone equipment sell.
  • New products: Micor 700 handpiece introduced in US April 2025 — incremental improvement, not a platform reset.

Refractive surgery (SMILE Pro / VisuMax 800):

  • Genuine positive: SMILE Pro received FDA approval for VisuMax 800 platform in mid-2025. This opens the US market for the fourth-generation SMILE platform.
  • SMILE Pro hyperopia extension: pending FDA approval — if cleared, materially expands addressable market.
  • VisuMax 800 China NMPA approval: confirmed — positive for the domestic China refractive market (less VBP-exposed than IOL market as SMILE is procedure-based, not device-based procurement).
  • Competition from STAAR EVO ICL (implantable collamer lens): growing share in younger myopia patients who prefer non-ablative correction. Different modality but competes for the same patient pool.
  • LASIK commoditisation: multiple vendors now offer femtosecond lasers (Alcon WaveLight, J&J iDesign, AMO). SMILE's advantage is flapless — a genuine clinical differentiator, not just marketing.
  • Market growth: SMILE campaign drove +15% lift in refractive laser sales in FY25 — standout performance in an otherwise weak year.

Glaucoma (ARTEVO 800/850 surgical microscope):

  • ARTEVO 850 evolution announced. Digital heads-up 3D display with intraoperative OCT.
  • Glaucoma surgical volumes growing with MIGS procedure adoption (Micro-Invasive Glaucoma Surgery) — ARTEVO is the visualisation platform of choice.
  • Less VBP-exposed than IOL market.

Diagnostics (Cirrus OCT, Humphrey Visual Field):

  • Recurring revenue profile (software subscriptions, probe/consumable replacement) — the most defensible segment.
  • AI-guided progression analysis (Carl Zeiss Meditec AI) — genuine moat; FORUM data management platform creates stickiness.
  • Humphrey Field Analyzer: the global standard for glaucoma perimetry. Near-monopoly position in a must-have diagnostic.
  • Competition from Topcon (3D OCT family), Heidelberg Engineering (Spectralis) — both credible but AFX's installed base is massive.

China — IOL and consumables (the key risk segment):

  • VBP Round 1 (national IOL tender, 2022-23): successfully navigated — AFX maintained significant volume.
  • Q4 FY25: Bifocal IOL withdrawn from current VBP tender after NMPA quality inspection found spectral transmittance failure. The suspension cascaded across Shanghai, Zhejiang, Guangdong, Ningxia, Guangxi — major ophthalmology markets.
  • VBP Round 2 (national IOL, 2026): ongoing tender process. Expected outcome: significant price erosion, expanded domestic competitor participation in multifocal categories.
  • Inventory "scrap risk" on bifocal IOL: €8m one-off hit flagged for Q2 FY26.
  • The bifocal IOL quality failure is particularly damaging: (a) it raises regulatory compliance credibility questions; (b) it creates a gap in the premium IOL portfolio during the critical tender period; (c) it will likely shift volume to domestic Chinese suppliers for whom the tender design already creates advantages.
  • [J] China IOL revenue is in structural repricing, not just cyclical weakness. This is the most important change force in the memo — it represents a permanent reduction in the earnings power of the China IOL business.

§7 — Change in Competitive Dynamics

[J] AFX is losing ground in surgical equipment to Alcon's platform bundling strategy while maintaining strong moats in diagnostics and refractive. The net competitive position is deteriorating.

Alcon — primary surgical competitor:

  • Unity VCS launch: integrated vitreoretinal and cataract platform on a single console. Directly challenges AFX's separate-device portfolio model.
  • Bundle advantage: Alcon sells IOLs + phaco equipment + surgical instruments + VR systems. A hospital that standardises on Alcon's ecosystem has structural barriers to switching to AFX's point solutions.
  • AFX's DORC acquisition is partly a response to this — the EVA NEXUS platform is AFX's attempt to create a comparable posterior/anterior bundle.
  • Alcon has significantly higher ophthalmic surgery consumables revenue (~$2.5bn+) than AFX — Alcon's model is blade+consumable recurring; AFX's is more capital equipment-heavy.
  • [J] The bundle war is the structural risk. If Alcon successfully converts hospitals to integrated platforms, AFX's standalone devices face pricing pressure. DORC buys AFX time in posterior; the anterior/phaco battle is still unresolved.

STAAR Surgical — refractive:

  • EVO ICL is a premium alternative to LASIK and SMILE for high myopia and thin corneas. Growing rapidly in Asia, US, Europe.
  • Different modality (surgical implant vs laser) — not a direct substitute for VisuMax/SMILE but competes for market growth.
  • SMILE Pro's competitive response: faster procedure time, broader indication range, established surgeon training ecosystem.

J&J Vision (TECNIS IOL platform):

  • Strong premium IOL competitor in cataract. Trifocal, EDOF lens portfolio.
  • In China VBP context: J&J's IOL business faces the same VBP pricing risk as AFX. Not a company-specific AFX problem.

Topcon / Haag-Streit in diagnostics:

  • Topcon DRI OCT Triton family: competitive but AFX's Cirrus HD-OCT 6000 (swept source) is widely regarded as the clinical gold standard.
  • Haag-Streit: strong in slit-lamp, tonometry. AFX's strength is in imaging/OCT, not traditional slit-lamp markets.
  • No evidence of meaningful diagnostic share loss at AFX. The Humphrey Field Analyzer and Cirrus OCT positions remain dominant.

Chinese domestic competitors :

  • Eyebright, HumanOptics (now part of Chinese entities), Vision Pro — expanding capability in premium IOL (multifocal, toric).
  • VBP design specifically advantages Chinese manufacturers through local procurement preferences.
  • This is not a 2-3 year risk; it is a live threat in the 2026 VBP tender cycle.

§8 — Change in Macro Environment

[J] The macro environment has shifted from broadly favourable (post-Covid recovery, pent-up procedure demand) to structurally challenged (China policy risk, US tariff uncertainty, hospital capex restraint) — all three macro tailwinds of 2021 are now headwinds.

Demographics (positive, structural):

  • Global cataract procedures: 30m+ annually, growing ~5-6% driven by ageing.
  • Myopia epidemic: 30-40% global prevalence, rising in Asia — durable refractive surgery demand driver.
  • Diabetic retinopathy: expanding DORC's addressable market in posterior surgery.
  • These tailwinds have not been repealed; they operate over a 10-20 year horizon.

China macro (materially negative, structural):

  • Post-Covid hospital capex catch-up cycle completed by late-2023. Hospital equipment budgets now constrained by government fiscal restraint.
  • VBP expansion: ophthalmology was targeted after cardiovascular and orthopaedics. The "dynamic delisting" mechanism (quality inspection failures leading to removal) adds operational risk beyond price erosion.
  • Government policy to support domestic medical device manufacturers — structural not cyclical.
  • Geopolitical risk: EU-China trade tensions; US export controls on semiconductor-adjacent technologies could theoretically affect diagnostic imaging systems.

US tariffs (negative, quantified):

  • 10% base tariff on EU medtech imports to the US (announced 2025).
  • AFX cost impact: >€10m flagged in Q1 FY26 commentary.
  • Additional investigations by US DoC ongoing — potential for further levies.
  • US hospital capex environment: budget uncertainty reducing discretionary equipment purchases.

FX:

  • EUR reporting currency. Significant USD and CNY revenues.
  • CNY weakness vs EUR = headwind on China translation.
  • FX headwind >€20m in Q1 FY26.

§9 — Regulatory/Political Environment

[J] China VBP represents the most significant regulatory change force — it is a permanent reduction in AFX's China earning power on the IOL segment, not a transition period.

China VBP mechanism:

  • Volume-Based Procurement is a national tender system for medical devices.
  • Round 1 for IOLs was navigated — but AFX maintained volume partly through premium product positioning that is now being eroded by domestic competition.
  • Bifocal IOL quality failure (Dec-25): NMPA cancelled AFX's VBP selection status after spectral transmittance failure in quality inspection. This is a process-level failure, not a design failure — but the commercial damage is immediate.
  • Round 2 (2026): more domestic players now registered in multifocal categories. Historical high-margin nature of premium IOL business makes this a disproportionate EBIT impact.

EU MDR compliance:

  • European Medical Device Regulation: ongoing compliance burden. AFX has large legacy device portfolio requiring re-certification.
  • Incremental cost, not a strategic threat; manageable for a company of AFX's size.

FDA approvals (positive catalysts):

  • VisuMax 800 + SMILE Pro: FDA approval achieved mid-2025 — opens US refractive market.
  • SMILE Pro hyperopia extension: pending FDA clearance — meaningful TAM expansion if cleared.
  • ARTEVO 850: presumably following ARTEVO 800's existing FDA clearance pathway.

§10 — Technology/Disruption Changes

[J] AFX's technology moat is genuine in imaging AI and refractive laser, but is being eroded in surgical equipment by Alcon's platform strategy and in IOL consumables by Chinese domestic manufacturers.

AI-guided surgical workflow:

  • CALLISTO eye: intraoperative guidance system integrating with ARTEVO microscope. Provides real-time overlay of pre-operative measurements onto the surgical field.
  • Zeiss VERACITY Surgery Planner: AI-based IOL power calculation — differentiates from commodity biometry.
  • FORUM platform: data management ecosystem connecting all Zeiss devices across a clinical pathway. Creates genuine switching costs in installed-base hospitals.
  • [J] AI is embedded in AFX's platform, not a bolt-on. This is a legitimate long-term moat. However, the short-term revenue contribution from software/AI is modest vs the capital equipment decline.

Femtosecond laser commoditisation:

  • Multiple femtosecond cataract surgery (FLACS) platforms now available: Johnson & Johnson Catalys, Alcon LenSx, Ziemer. AFX does not sell a FLACS system (it uses standard phaco).
  • FLACS market share has stabilised at ~3-5% of cataract procedures globally — hasn't cannibalised conventional phaco as feared.
  • Less of a threat than anticipated; not a near-term disruption.

Retinal gene therapy:

  • Emerging therapies (Spark/Roche, Applied Genetic Technologies) targeting IRDs, wet AMD.
  • Medium-term risk: if gene therapy reduces the pool of repeat vitreoretinal surgical procedures, DORC's EVA NEXUS consumable revenue could be pressured.
  • 10+ year horizon. Not a near-term DORC thesis killer.

Digital pathology/connected diagnostics:

  • Zeiss is investing in AI-based progression detection for glaucoma (FORUM + AI analysis).
  • Strong competitive moat; Topcon and Heidelberg are also investing but AFX's installed base is larger.
  • Remote diagnostic potential (SaaS-based OCT analysis) — longer-term recurring revenue opportunity.

Surgical visualisation evolution:

  • ARTEVO 800/850 heads-up 3D display: genuine clinical advance. Reduces neck/eye strain for surgeons; enables wider surgical field view; integrates intraoperative OCT.
  • Leica (Microsystems) and Haag-Streit compete but AFX is widely considered the innovation leader in ophthalmic surgical microscopy.

§13 — Historical Track Record on Change Delivery

[J] Management credibility is impaired. The gap between the "above 20%" EBIT margin commitment and the 1.7% Q1 FY26 EBITA reality is not a market-driven phenomenon alone — it reflects consistent operational under-delivery.

Peak guidance vs actual:

  • FY22 guidance: EBIT margin "around 19-21%." Actual: 20.9% — on target.
  • FY23 guidance: similar range. Actual: approximately 15% — missed on execution.
  • FY24: margin compression accelerated as China channel inventory destocking hit. Management initially characterised the China inventory issue as "transitory." It was not transitory — it presaged the structural VBP repricing.
  • FY25: guided "low-to-mid double-digit millions" of cost saves. Actual: margins barely recovered. EBIT margin at 10.0% vs a 20%+ medium-term target.
  • FY26: guided €2.3bn revenue + 12.5% EBITA. Withdrawn after Q1.

Specific credibility failures:

  • China inventory destocking (FY23): characterised as a temporary channel correction. In reality, it marked the beginning of a structural repricing cycle.
  • "Above 20% EBIT medium term" — this guidance was given with declining certainty; it is now effectively unachievable in the near term without a fundamental restructuring.
  • The decision to appoint Foerst as CEO in Jun-25 and remove him six months later is the most damaging credibility event. The parent/board appears uncertain about the strategic direction.

COVID-era messaging:

  • Post-COVID, AFX sold the narrative of pent-up cataract demand driving a multi-year equipment supercycle. The 2021 peak validated this. However, the company did not adequately prepare for the post-supercycle normalisation — cost structures remained elevated, DORC was acquired at the top of the cycle, and China concentration was not reduced.
