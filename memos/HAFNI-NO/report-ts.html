Section 2 – The 80/20 (bottom line up front) 7 findings

Section summary
  1. Hafnia is a highly profitable, cash-generative shipping leader with a low cash breakeven and a shareholder-friendly distribution policy that warrants deeper due diligence.
1.
Core operational model: Hafnia generates seaborne shipping revenue by deploying a fleet of 118 product tankers across global spot markets and commercially managed pool structures
2.
Compelling investment case: Sustained structural supply deficits from low global shipyard capacity combine with elevated sailing distances from geopolitical trade redirections to lock in high spot charter rates
3.
Critical swing factor: The primary swing factor for forward earnings is the daily spot charter rate, which remains highly volatile and is directly impacted by OPEC+ export volumes
4.
Strong historical performance: Hafnia captured high earnings during the upcycle, generating $793.28 million in net profit in 2023 and $774.04 million in 2024
5.
Primary bear threat: The core risk is a global economic recession that dampens refined product demand, or a rapid de-escalation of Middle East conflicts that opens Suez transit
6.
Positive initial verdict: Deeper institutional work is highly warranted because Hafnia trades at 0.93x P/NAV, presenting an attractive asset-backed entry point relative to shipping peers

Section 3 – What the company does 15 findings

Section summary
  1. Hafnia utilizes a dominant commercial pool structure to optimize spot market exposure and capture premium freight rates across four major vessel classes.
1.
Long Range Two segment: The LR2 segment consists of vessels between 85,000 and 124,999 deadweight tons, providing seaborne transportation of clean petroleum oil products globally
2.
Long Range One segment: The LR1 segment operates vessels between 55,000 and 84,999 deadweight tons, transporting both clean and dirty petroleum products, where Hafnia holds a leading global market share
3.
Medium Range segment: The MR segment handles vessels between 40,000 and 54,999 deadweight tons, carrying clean fuels, vegetable oils, and easy chemicals, with Hafnia acting as the third-largest player globally
4.
Handysize tanker operations: The Handy segment covers smaller vessels between 25,000 and 39,999 deadweight tons, carrying specialized chemical and refined product parcels
5.
Pool management model: Hafnia operates eight commercial pools that bring together similar third-party vessels to exploit operational efficiencies and optimize vessel utilization
6.
Disponent owner pools: In 2023, the company transitioned major pools to a disponent-owner model, allowing Hafnia to act as principal and recognize gross pool revenue
7.
Spot vs charter mix: Approximately 93% of shipping revenue is derived from the spot market or spot-oriented pools, leaving only 7% locked under fixed-rate time charters
8.
Value chain positioning: Hafnia sits at the midstream of the energy supply chain, transporting products from refineries to regional distribution terminals
9.
Customer concentration profile: The customer base is relatively concentrated, with the top five customers accounting for $675.40 million, or 47.50% of total revenue in 2025
10.
Scale competitive advantage: Scale provides a formidable competitive advantage, as operating over 200 vessels allows Hafnia to secure bulk purchase discounts and minimize ballast legs
11.
Fleet age superiority: The average age of Hafnia's owned fleet is 9.60 years, which is significantly younger than the global average of over 13 years for MR and LR segments
12.
Environmental regulatory barriers: Evolving Carbon Intensity Indicator rules and Energy Efficiency Existing Ship Index regulations discourage speculative new ordering, capping global fleet growth
13.
Wind down of segments: Hafnia intends to wind down its Handy and LR2 pool operations, focusing on LR1 and MR segments where it possesses peak market muscle
14.
Other side of the story: Extreme spot exposure means Hafnia is highly vulnerable to severe rate collapses, as a drop in spot rates below its cash breakeven of $13,000 per day would instantly wipe out operating profitability

Section 5 – The possible investment case 11 findings

Section summary
  1. The investment case is highly compelling due to structural supply constraints and sustained geopolitical disruptions that inflate global shipping distances.
1.
Historically low orderbook: The global product tanker orderbook remains near historical lows, with manageable deliveries of ~4% of fleet capacity over 2025-2027
2.
Shipyard capacity bottlenecks: Shipyards are heavily booked with container and LNG vessels, preventing any rapid supply response for product tankers until at least 2029
3.
Accelerating fleet aging: The global product tanker fleet is aging rapidly, with the potential for accelerated scrapping of older, non-eco vessels as regulations tighten
4.
Strait of Hormuz threat: Disruptions in the Middle East place nearly 30% of global seaborne oil trade at risk, reshaping international trading routes
5.
Red Sea rerouting: Rerouting around the Cape of Good Hope has increased average shipping distances significantly, driving a massive increase in ton-mile demand
6.
Regional refinery destruction: Refinery closures in Europe and the Middle East force product imports from more distant refining centers in the US and China, permanently increasing ton-miles
7.
Consistent payout history: Hafnia has distributed dividends for 17 consecutive quarters, highlighting a resilient and highly predictable capital return framework
8.
Share buyback flexibility: The board's authorization of share buyback programs provides management with an additional lever to return capital when shares trade below NAV
9.
Compound probability requirement: The investment case does not require all drivers to fire simultaneously, as supply-side tightness is sufficient on its own to sustain profitable rates
10.
Other side of the story: A rapid de-escalation of Middle East conflicts would trigger immediate route normalization, eliminating the Cape routing distance premium and causing spot rates to fall precipitously

Section 6 – Valuation 5 findings

Section summary
  1. Hafnia is attractively valued on an asset-backed basis, trading at a discount to both peer averages and its intrinsic net asset value.
1.
Valuation multiples context: Hafnia trades at a forward P/E of 8.20x for 2026, compared to peer Scorpio Tankers at 8.00x
2.
EV/EBITDA multiple: The company trades at an EV/EBITDA multiple of 7.10x for 2026, which is higher than peer Scorpio Tankers at 5.80x
3.
Discount versus peers: Hafnia trades at 0.93x P/NAV, compared to its closest peers who trade at an average of 1.03x P/NAV
4.
Other side of the story: This valuation framework is highly dependent on vessel values remaining elevated, meaning a 20% drop in secondhand ship prices would erase the NAV discount and expose investors to downside

Section 7 – Key risks and the other side 7 findings

Section summary
  1. The risk profile is balanced but highly sensitive to macroeconomic deceleration and the unwinding of geopolitical trade distortions.
1.
Demand destruction from recession: A global economic recession could severely reduce refined product consumption, leading to a surplus of shipping capacity
2.
Peak oil demand horizon: The transition to renewable energy sources raises the risk of peak oil demand, potentially creating a deflationary shipping environment
3.
Export volume restrictions: Major refined product exporters such as China and India could restrict exports to secure domestic supplies, severely lowering ton-mile demand
4.
Fleet renewal capital requirements: Given that a significant portion of Hafnia's fleet was built in 2015, the company faces rising drydocking and fleet replacement capex
5.
Operating expense inflation: High fuel costs, which represent Hafnia's most material expense, could compress TCE margins if bunker prices spike
6.
Other side of the story: The bear case is highly overstated because the absolute lack of shipyard supply for tankers provides an impenetrable supply-side backstop that keeps charter rates highly profitable

Secondary diligence action: Monitor weekly spot freight rate indices across LR1 and MR segments to detect any early signs of refined product export weakness
