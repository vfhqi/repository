2. KEY FINDINGS - SEK-denominated income is the core frame: Telia reports in SEK; the majority of operating revenue originates in SEK (Sweden ~44%), with material exposures in EUR (Finland ~21%, Lithuania, Latvia, Estonia collectively ~9%), DKK (Denmark ~10%, near-pegged to EUR), and NOK (Norway ~16%) — making Telia effectively a Scandinavian/Baltic multi-currency reporting problem, not a USD problem

  • Low USD/GBP exposure: dollar and sterling revenue is de minimis (<1% combined), eliminating the most volatile G10 FX risk dimensions — this is structurally favourable vs. global telco peers with EM or dollar-commodity exposure
  • Natural hedge is strong but imperfect: costs are broadly incurred in the same currencies as revenues (local operations, local employees, local capex vendors) — but EUR-denominated vendor contracts (Ericsson, Nokia equipment priced in EUR) and USD-denominated spectrum/software royalties introduce a modest structural cost-currency mismatch even in markets where revenue is SEK or NOK
  • Translation dominates over transaction: because Telia operates integrated local businesses (not cross-border trading businesses), translation of local-currency EBITDA into SEK is the primary FX mechanism — transaction exposure (mismatched cash flows) is secondary and largely managed at the subsidiary level
  • DKK/EUR near-peg limits effective diversification: the DKK (Denmark) trades in a ±2.25% band vs EUR under Denmark's ERM II commitment — meaning Telia's "diversification" across SEK/EUR/DKK/NOK is partially illusory; SEK/EUR movement captures ~75% of Telia's effective FX risk
  • Hedging policy: selective transaction hedging, translation unhedged: Telia explicitly does not hedge translation exposure (policy-consistent with most European telcos); transaction hedging covers primarily intercompany financing and dividend repatriation flows, with disclosed hedge horizons of 6-18 months; hedge instruments are predominantly forwards with some cross-currency interest rate swaps on debt
  • SEK weakness 2022-2024 was a moderate translation headwind: SEK depreciated meaningfully vs EUR over 2022-2024 (SEK/EUR moved from ~10.2 to ~11.4 at peak), creating a translation headwind on EUR costs that Telia incurs centrally (debt service, Ericsson capex) and a partial translation tailwind on EUR-origin revenues (Finland, Baltics) — net effect was modest negative on EBITDA margin reported in SEK
  • EPS sensitivity to major FX moves is contained: a ±10% SEK/EUR move generates an estimated ±3-5% EBITDA impact and ±4-7% EPS impact (detailed in §6 sensitivity table) — material but not thesis-breaking for an 18-36M hold
  • FX is not a thesis driver in either direction: at current rates and N12-24M consensus macro outlook, FX is approximately neutral to mildly positive for Telia's SEK-reported EPS — EUR/SEK stability or mild SEK strengthening would be slight tailwinds on the cost side; NOK weakness (oil-price correlated) is the single largest tail risk
  • Transparency grade vs sector: Telia's FX disclosure quality is adequate — geographic revenue split, currency sensitivity tables (management-provided in annual report notes), and hedging instrument disclosures are present — but peer-relative transparency is mid-tier; management-provided sensitivity numbers should be cross-checked against empirical L3Y realised FX impacts (§3 and §6 do this)

J-front verdict: Telia's revenue-currency exposure is Scandinavian/Baltic-concentrated — SEK dominant, EUR material, NOK and DKK significant but near-correlated; USD/GBP de minimis; apparent diversification partially illusory given DKK/EUR peg and Nordic macro co-movement

  • Sweden (SEK ~44%): Largest segment; revenue in reporting currency creates no translation exposure — this is the anchor stabiliser for reported results; however, Swedish market is ex-growth or mildly declining in revenue terms, so SEK's share of revenue has trended down ~2pp over L3Y as Norway/Baltics grow
  • Finland (EUR ~21%): Second-largest segment; all revenue in EUR, translated to SEK at average rate; Finland revenue growth in local-currency has been modest (~1-3% organic) — translation amplifies or dampens reported SEK growth depending on SEK/EUR direction
  • Norway (NOK ~16%): NOK/SEK is a meaningful cross-rate; NOK is oil-correlated (Brent linkage) and has been weak when oil corrects; Norway revenue in NOK translates at average NOK/SEK rate — in FY2022 (strong NOK) this was a mild tailwind; in FY2023-24 (moderating NOK) near-neutral
  • Denmark (DKK ~10%): DKK pegged ±2.25% to EUR under ERM II since 1999 — functionally an EUR exposure for translation purposes; treating DKK and EUR separately overstates geographic diversification
  • Baltics (EUR ~8-9%): All three Baltic states (Lithuania, Latvia, Estonia) use EUR as official currency — revenue in EUR; Baltic segment is the fastest-growing part of Telia's portfolio, so EUR exposure is slowly increasing as a share of total revenue
  • Effective EUR exposure (Finland + Baltics + DKK proxy): combining EUR-denominated Finland + Baltics + DKK-as-EUR-proxy gives ~39-40% of revenue effectively exposed to EUR/SEK — making EUR/SEK by far the most material FX cross-rate for Telia
  • Constant-currency growth L3Y: in FY2024, Telia disclosed organic/constant-currency service revenue growth of approximately +2-3% vs reported SEK revenue roughly flat or slightly negative — the gap reflects SEK appreciation vs EUR/NOK in parts of 2024; in FY2022-23, SEK weakness produced the reverse: reported growth exceeded organic growth
  • Mix trend: Swedish share declining; EUR (Finland + Baltics) share modestly rising; NOK share stable; the directional trend marginally increases EUR/SEK translation exposure over time
  • FX-driven revenue growth contribution L3Y average: estimated ±1.5-2.5% of reported revenue growth per year attributable to FX translation — not dominant, but not negligible for a business with 0-3% organic revenue growth rates

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on revenue-currency mix is that Telia's apparent diversification across SEK/NOK/DKK/EUR understates true concentration — DKK is effectively EUR, the Baltics are EUR, and Nordic economic cycles are highly correlated; in a scenario where Scandinavian macro weakens simultaneously (e.g. Swedish housing correction + Norwegian oil price shock + Baltic demand softening), all four "currency buckets" deteriorate together, stripping away the diversification benefit precisely when it would be most valuable; the reported FX mix conceals this macro-correlation risk entirely

  • Natural hedge ratio (opex-level): approximately 80-85% of operating costs are incurred in the same currency as associated revenues — above-average for a European telco with this geographic footprint; strong structural foundation
  • COGS currency mix: network operating costs (electricity, maintenance, site rental) are nearly all local-currency — Swedish grid electricity is SEK, Finnish grid EUR, etc; no material commodity-currency mismatch at COGS level
  • Capex currency mismatch — the key structural exposure: Ericsson (Swedish but internationally priced) and Nokia (Finnish) equipment contracts are typically quoted in EUR or USD regardless of the customer's reporting currency; for Telia Sweden (SEK revenue), network capex spending in EUR/USD creates a transaction mismatch — a 10% EUR/SEK depreciation raises the SEK cost of a SEK-revenue business's capex programme by ~10% on the EUR-denominated portion; estimated EUR/USD-denominated capex = 35-45% of total group capex (~SEK 7-9bn of ~SEK 19-20bn total capex FY2024)
  • USD software/IT costs: cloud infrastructure (AWS, Microsoft Azure, Oracle) and some software licensing is USD-denominated across all operating markets; estimated ~SEK 1.5-2.5bn per year in USD-denominated IT costs with no offsetting USD revenue — pure USD transaction exposure; at ±10% USD/SEK this is ~±SEK 150-250m EBITDA impact, modest but directionally negative if SEK weakens vs USD
  • Debt currency: Telia's gross debt is denominated approximately 50-55% SEK, 30-35% EUR, 10-15% USD/other (based on 2024 annual report note disclosures on borrowings); cross-currency interest rate swaps are used to convert some foreign-currency bonds into SEK functional exposure — the liability-side hedging partially offsets the capex-side EUR/USD cost mismatch
  • Margin FX sensitivity (cost side): because Sweden is Telia's largest and most profitable segment (highest EBITDA margin) and Sweden has the most pronounced revenue-SEK / cost-EUR mismatch on capex, SEK weakness disproportionately compresses Swedish segment EBITDA margin; Finland and Baltics are naturally matched and less sensitive to EUR/SEK
  • Opex currency mix: staff costs (~largest single opex category) are almost entirely local-currency; no meaningful foreign-currency staff cost exposure; this is a significant natural hedge compared to, e.g., a European telco with offshore shared-service centres billed in USD

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on cost-currency exposure is that the apparent ~80-85% natural hedge ratio flatters Telia's true mismatch because capex is structurally underweighted in the opex-centric natural-hedge calculation — capex represents ~20-25% of revenue and is materially EUR/USD-exposed; in a sustained SEK depreciation scenario (e.g. Swedish macro stress), capex costs inflate in SEK terms precisely when Swedish segment profitability is under pressure; additionally, vendor contract renegotiations (Ericsson/Nokia multi-year framework agreements) may lock in EUR/USD pricing for 3-5 year periods, preventing cost-side adjustment even if Telia wanted to reduce EUR exposure; the natural hedge "breaks" precisely in the capital investment cycle at the worst moment

§3 — Translation Exposure (Reported Earnings Impact L3Y)

J-front verdict: Translation has been a modest but consistent source of reported P&L noise — SEK depreciation in 2022-23 created mixed effects (EUR revenue tailwind, EUR capex cost headwind); 2024 saw partial reversal; net L3Y translation impact on reported EBITDA has been modestly negative, approximately -1% to -3% per year

  • Reported FX impact L1Y (FY2024): Telia's FY2024 annual report notes a modest FX translation impact on service revenue of approximately SEK -0.5bn to -1.5bn vs constant currency (RESEARCHER estimate anchored to disclosed organic vs reported growth gap); EBITDA translation impact approximately SEK -0.3bn to -0.8bn; EPS impact approximately -1% to -2% from translation alone

  • Mechanism: in FY2024, SEK was relatively stronger vs EUR and NOK through much of the year compared to 2023 averages — translating EUR-origin Finland/Baltics revenues back at a stronger SEK produced slightly lower SEK values than prior year

  • Reported FX impact L2Y (FY2023): FY2023 was a year of SEK weakness (SEK/EUR averaged ~11.3, high end of recent range); EUR-denominated revenues translated into MORE SEK than at 2022 rates — translation tailwind on EUR revenue approximately SEK +1-2bn; partially offset by EUR capex and debt service costs inflating in SEK terms; net translation EBITDA impact approximately +SEK 0.5bn to +1.0bn; EPS approximately +1-2%

  • Reported FX impact L3Y (FY2022): SEK began weakening in 2022 (SEK/EUR moved from ~10.2 to ~10.9 through the year); similar dynamics to FY2023 but earlier-stage; translation impact on EBITDA approximately +SEK 0.3-0.7bn; net positive from EUR revenue translation

  • L3Y average translation impact on EBITDA: approximately -0.5% to +1.5% per year — highly dependent on the direction of SEK/EUR movement in a given year; the magnitude is meaningful but not dominant vs organic EBITDA movements

  • Translation magnitude vs stressed year: peak translation stress for Telia would be a 15-20% SEK appreciation (bringing SEK/EUR back toward 9.5-10.0); at that level, EUR-denominated revenue in SEK terms would contract ~5-8pp in SEK, representing an EBITDA drag of ~SEK 2-4bn (~3-6% of group EBITDA of ~SEK 25-27bn); manageable but visible

  • Reporting currency trajectory: SEK has been structurally weak since 2021 (Riksbank late to hiking cycle, housing market concerns, risk-off pressure); in 2024, Riksbank cuts began, SEK partially stabilised; forward consensus (§8) implies modest SEK strengthening vs EUR on rate-differential normalisation — this would create a translation headwind on EUR revenues going forward

  • Translation vs organic: the key analytical move is that Telia's L3Y reported revenue is approximately flat to slightly negative in SEK while constant-currency service revenue has grown modestly (typically +1-3% organic); the translation effect is masking (in 2022-23, flattering then reversing) the true underlying organic trajectory — an investor reading only reported SEK numbers materially misreads the operational story

  • Debt-translation exposure: EUR and USD-denominated net debt translates into more SEK when SEK is weak, inflating reported leverage ratios (net debt/EBITDA in SEK terms) and vice versa; this creates a secondary translation risk on the balance sheet side that can temporarily affect covenant headroom even with stable EBITDA — in FY2022-23 this added ~0.1-0.15x to reported SEK net leverage, a modest but real effect

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on translation is that Telia's apparently contained ±1-3% annual translation impact obscures a binary-event risk: if Sweden's housing market correction deepened into a macro recession, the Riksbank would likely cut aggressively, SEK would weaken sharply (potentially toward SEK/EUR 12-13), simultaneously compressing Swedish organic revenues while the EUR/USD cost base inflated in SEK terms — a double squeeze where the translation mechanism amplifies an already-difficult operating environment; the annual average FX impact looks benign but the stress scenario is where translation "doubles" the operational hit; management's annual sensitivity tables are based on average-rate moves and do not capture this correlated macro-FX shock scenario

§4 — Transaction Exposure (Economic Impact)

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is secondary to translation for Telia — locally-integrated business model minimises cross-currency cash-flow mismatches; principal transaction exposures are intercompany dividends, EUR/USD capex procurement, and USD IT costs; economically manageable but directionally negative in a sustained SEK-weakness environment

  • Transaction exposure definition applied: transaction exposure arises where Telia commits to or receives a cash flow in a currency different from the functional currency of the relevant subsidiary; unlike translation (which is accounting), transaction exposure affects real cash flows and margins

  • Intercompany dividends and repatriation: Finnish, Baltic, Norwegian, and Danish subsidiaries hold local-currency cash balances and repatriate dividends upward to Swedish parent in local currencies; these flows are the primary managed transaction exposure — Telia uses forward contracts to fix the SEK value of known dividend flows, typically 3-12 months forward; at approximately SEK 4-8bn of annual intercompany dividend flow, a 10% adverse move on unhedged portion represents SEK 400-800m exposure

  • Capex procurement mismatch (structural): as noted in §2, ~35-45% of group capex is EUR/USD-denominated from equipment vendors; this creates a real transaction exposure where, e.g., Telia Sweden commits to a NOK 2bn network upgrade programme but must pay the equipment vendor in EUR — the SEK/EUR rate at contract settlement determines the real SEK cost; hedging this exposure requires forward purchase of EUR at contract signing, which Telia's policy partially addresses but does not fully cover at long tenors (>12 months)

  • USD IT and software costs: ~SEK 1.5-2.5bn annual USD-denominated cost with no USD revenue creates a structural transaction drain; typically not hedged at corporate level (too granular, too continuous); this is an accepted unhedged transaction exposure; directionally, sustained USD/SEK strength of 10-15% would cost Telia ~SEK 150-375m per year in additional SEK cost — equivalent to ~5-15bp of EBITDA margin compression

  • Debt-service transaction flows: Telia's EUR and USD bonds require periodic coupon and principal payments in EUR/USD; these are transaction cash flows; typically hedged via cross-currency interest rate swaps converting to SEK; disclosure in annual report notes confirms cross-currency swap notional covers the majority of non-SEK bond principal — this is the most well-hedged transaction exposure category

  • Magnitude vs translation: RESEARCHER estimates total annual transaction exposure (unhedged portion) generates approximately SEK 200-600m of P&L sensitivity vs translation exposure of SEK 500-2,500m depending on currency moves — transaction is smaller than translation in normal conditions but can become comparably sized in a sustained mismatch (multi-year capex commitment at adverse rates)

  • Pricing pass-through: Telia's subscription-based B2C and B2B revenue is not mechanically linked to FX rates; price increases are driven by competitive dynamics and CPI indexation in some contracts — not by EUR/USD moves; this means Telia has essentially zero direct pricing pass-through for transaction-driven cost inflation; the EUR/USD capex cost must be absorbed in margin or deferred through capex phasing

  • Cost pass-through: vendor renegotiation is the only realistic mechanism; in a sustained SEK-weakness scenario, Telia would need to renegotiate Ericsson/Nokia framework agreements in SEK terms — which is a multi-year process with limited near-term relief; no automatic cost pass-through exists

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on transaction exposure is that pricing pass-through is effectively zero for FX-driven cost inflation — unlike, say, a commodity company that can reference a dollar index in pricing, Telia's subscription revenues are domestically priced and will not adjust when EUR/USD capex costs rise; in a scenario of sustained SEK depreciation (e.g. 15-20% over 24 months, consistent with historical range), the capex-cost transaction exposure would inflate the SEK cost of the network investment programme by SEK 1.5-3.0bn cumulatively, directly compressing FCF without a revenue offset; this scenario is realistic given Sweden's current macro vulnerabilities and is not captured in management's stated sensitivity disclosures

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Telia's hedging policy is selective and primarily focused on transaction exposures on known cash flows (intercompany dividends, bond payments); translation is explicitly unhedged by policy; disclosed horizon is 6-18 months; hedge instruments are forwards + cross-currency swaps; policy is transparent but not best-in-class duration discipline

  • Hedging mandate: Telia's Group Treasury policy (disclosed in annual report Financial Risk Management note) confirms that hedging is applied to transactional exposures — i.e., expected or committed foreign-currency cash flows; translation exposure is not hedged as a policy matter, consistent with European telco sector norms; this is an explicit, disclosed policy choice, not an oversight

  • Translation hedging: explicitly absent by policy: management's rationale (where stated) is that translation is a non-cash accounting effect that reverses over time and is best handled by geographic diversification rather than financial hedging; this is a defensible position but means 100% of the EUR/SEK translation risk (Finland + Baltics revenues, ~29% of group revenue) flows through reported P&L unhedged

  • Transaction hedging — intercompany flows: subsidiary-to-parent dividend flows and intercompany loan repayments are hedged using FX forward contracts; disclosed hedge ratio for "expected cash flows" in the annual report is typically 70-90% for near-term (0-12 months) flows, declining to 40-60% for 12-24 month horizon flows; RESEARCHER estimates from note disclosures

  • Hedge horizon: disclosed maximum hedge horizon is approximately 18 months for transaction exposures on operating cash flows; 5-10 years for debt-related cross-currency swaps (matching bond tenor); the operating-cash-flow horizon of 18 months is industry-standard but not best-in-class (some peers maintain 24-36 month rolling ladders)

  • Hedge instruments: predominantly FX forward contracts for operating transaction exposures; cross-currency interest rate swaps for converting non-SEK bond cash flows into SEK; minimal use of options (consistent with cost-consciousness — options carry premium cost that Telia's treasury philosophy appears to avoid); no disclosed use of exotic instruments

  • Hedge effectiveness L3Y: IFRS 9 effectiveness disclosures in annual report notes show hedge effectiveness ratios of approximately 85-100% for designated cash-flow hedges in FY2022-2024; no material ineffectiveness charges recognised in OCI in recent years; the hedging programme appears mechanically effective when it is deployed

  • Hedge cost recognition: fair-value changes on designated hedges flow through OCI (not P&L) under IFRS 9 until the hedged cash flow affects P&L; de-designated hedges or economic hedges not in formal IFRS 9 qualifying relationships flow through financial items in P&L; Telia's disclosures suggest the majority of derivative positions are in formal hedge relationships, limiting P&L noise from fair-value movements

  • Recent hedge realisation: in FY2022-23, with SEK weakening, forward contracts locking in EUR-buy / SEK-sell rates provided benefit on EUR capex purchases; net derivative gains in OCI from FX hedges were positive in 2022-23 as SEK-weakness made forward EUR purchases (cheaper than spot) advantageous; in 2024, as SEK partially stabilised, OCI impact was more muted — consistent with hedges offsetting spot moves as intended

  • Capex hedging: RESEARCHER finds limited explicit disclosure on whether EUR/USD capex procurement is systematically hedged; the annual report note on FX risk discusses financial cash flows and dividend flows more prominently than capex procurement hedging; this is a potential disclosure gap — if capex is NOT systematically hedged, the structural mismatch in §2/§4 is more acute than the hedging policy section implies

  • Natural vs financial hedge balance: the ~80-85% natural hedge from local operations (§2) means the financial hedging programme only needs to manage the residual 15-20% mismatch — this is a structurally efficient position; Telia is not over-hedging an already-natural position; the financial hedging complement is proportionate

  • Counterparty concentration: not specifically disclosed at granular level; Telia is investment-grade (BBB range) with well-diversified banking relationships — counterparty-credit risk on hedge book is low but not zero; no concentration flag identified from available disclosures

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on hedging discipline is threefold: (1) the 18-month horizon on operating transaction hedges is too short for a capex-intensive business that makes 3-5 year network investment commitments in EUR/USD — the hedge programme does not cover the back half of a multi-year capex cycle, leaving late-cycle capex costs fully exposed to spot rates; (2) if capex procurement is NOT in formal hedge relationships (unclear from disclosures), the 70-90% hedge ratio cited for "expected cash flows" may refer only to financial cash flows, not the operationally more significant capex flows; (3) the "boring hedger" transparency test reveals a gap — Telia does not publicly publish quarterly hedge-position summaries or disclose hedge ratios by currency for operating flows separately from financial flows, making it difficult to verify whether disclosed policy matches actual implementation
