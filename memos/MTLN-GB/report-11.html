KEY FINDINGS - Structural shape: Metlen operates across a multi-hub value chain — two partially overlapping chains (integrated power + metals) converging at a shared balance sheet, creating cross-subsidisation optionality that most single-sector peers structurally cannot replicate [#J]

  • Positional concentration: Metlen is a rare integrated operator spanning raw material procurement → power generation → electricity trading → aluminium smelting → downstream metals products, occupying 4-5 of 8 mapped stages simultaneously [#J]

  • Margin-pool gravity: In European integrated utilities, regulated/quasi-regulated generation and proprietary trading books are the two margin-dense nodes; Metlen sits astride both via its RES + hydro portfolio and Elpedison trading arm [#J]

  • Pricing power verdict: Asymmetric — strong on the generation/trading side (liberalised Greek market, captive industrial offtake), weak on the metals side where LME aluminium prices are the binding constraint [#J]

  • Supplier leverage: Lignite and bauxite supply chains carry oligopolistic upstream risk; natural gas supply depends heavily on DEPA/LNG spot, an exposure that repriced violently in 2021-22 [#J]

  • Vertical integration depth: Metlen's aluminium segment is near-fully vertically integrated from bauxite mining (Delphi-Distomon) through alumina refining (AoG) to smelting and rolled products (Alumil partial overlap), placing it among top-decile integrated metals producers in the European mid-cap universe [#I]

  • Adjacent-stage threat: Greek energy market liberalisation and EU renewable build-out are structurally compressing captive generation margins; the long-term threat is margin migration from generation to grid/storage/flexibility services where Metlen has thinner footing [#J]

  • ⚡ RARE structural duality: The combination of a regulated-adjacent power platform and a fully integrated metals smelter within a single ~€3-4bn enterprise is effectively unique in European mid-cap — no direct structural peer operates both at meaningful scale [#I]

  • Single largest positional risk: Aluminium smelter energy cost pass-through — if Greek power market prices normalise upward or LME aluminium softens simultaneously, the smelter margin can compress from both sides, with no hedge within the same corporate perimeter [#J]

  • ESA signal: Value-chain position is net positive for the investment case — integration depth creates structural cost advantages that pure-play peers cannot easily replicate; the bear case centres on regulatory and commodity cycle rather than structural displacement [#J]

§1 — Value Chain Map + Stage Table

Metlen's industry context is genuinely dual: it operates across an integrated power/utilities chain and a vertically integrated aluminium/metals chain. These chains intersect at the smelter (power is the primary input to aluminium smelting), making the structural map more complex than a single-industry linear chain. The stage table below maps both chains, noting Metlen's presence at each.

  • Structural shape: The combined value chain is best described as two-hub spoke-and-integrated, not purely linear — power feeds metals, metals revenue cross-funds power capex [#J]

  • Linear chain (power): Fuel procurement → generation → transmission/grid → distribution → retail/industrial supply — classical regulated utility structure with margin concentrated at generation and increasingly at flexibility/balancing [#J]

  • Linear chain (metals): Bauxite mining → alumina refining → primary smelting → rolling/forming → downstream fabrication/sales — classical vertically integrated metals structure with margin concentrated at smelting when LME is high and energy costs are contained [#J]

  • Intersection node: Electricity supply to smelter is the critical value-chain junction — Metlen's power generation partially feeds its own smelting demand, creating an internal transfer-pricing dynamic that is both a structural advantage and a valuation complexity [#I]

  • Platform element: Elpedison (Metlen's retail energy arm) introduces a platform-like B2B/B2C customer layer atop the generation chain, adding margin capture at the distribution/retail stage [#A]

  • Geographic footprint: Power chain is primarily Greek domestic + Balkan regional; metals chain has global commodity exposure via LME pricing but physically concentrated in Greece and southeast Europe [#J]

  • Capital intensity gradient: Both chains are heavily capex-intensive at the generation/smelting nodes; distribution and trading are relatively capital-light [#J]

  • Operator fragmentation: Greek power generation is an oligopoly (PPC dominant, Metlen/Elpedison second-tier, growing renewables independents); European aluminium smelting is even more concentrated (Hydro, Alcoa, Rio Tinto/ELYSIS, TRIMET, Metlen among the handful of European primary smelters) [#I]

§2 — Metlen's Position Within the Chain

  • J-front verdict: Metlen is a genuinely multi-stage integrated operator spanning raw materials through retail across two value chains — positional concentration risk is low but execution/complexity risk is high [#J]

  • Stage occupied (power): Stages 3, 5, and 7 — generation, trading, retail — constituting the core integrated utility model similar to a mini-vertically-integrated European utility (comparable structural model to early-stage Enel or EDF at regional scale) [#J]

  • Stage occupied (metals): Stages 1, 2, and 6 — bauxite mining, alumina refining, primary smelting — constituting a fully vertically integrated aluminium producer from ore to molten metal [#J]

  • Upstream integration (power): Metlen does NOT own upstream gas production or LNG terminals; it is a buyer in the gas market via DEPA and spot LNG, which is a structural vulnerability when TTF/NBP prices spike [#J]

  • Sub: Hydro generation (Acheloos cascade) provides partial upstream energy self-sufficiency, an important structural hedge [#I]

  • Upstream integration (metals): Metlen's Delphi-Distomon bauxite mine covers roughly 35-45% of AoG's alumina refinery feed (estimated from public capacity disclosures); the remainder is imported bauxite — partial, not complete, upstream self-sufficiency [#I]

  • Downstream integration (power): Elpedison provides meaningful downstream reach into retail/B2B energy supply; this is above-average integration depth vs most European IPPs (independent power producers) which stop at the wholesale/trading level [#I]

  • Downstream integration (metals): Limited — smelter output is largely sold as primary aluminium and billets into the open market or to Alumil (related but separate entity); Metlen does not control the downstream fabrication value chain [#A]

  • Peer integration vs: European IPP peers (Energean, Greenvolt) are single-stage specialists; European integrated utilities (Enel, Iberdrola) are larger but transmission-absent like Metlen; European aluminium peers (TRIMET, Alro) are smelter-only without upstream mining [#I]

  • Integration asymmetry: The power chain integration (gen-trade-retail) is more complete than the metals chain integration (mining-refining-smelting without downstream fabrication) — strategic optionality is higher on the power side [#J]

  • Cross-chain synergy: The most distinctive positional feature is the intra-group power supply to smelter — Metlen's generation fleet partially fulfils the smelter's energy demand, effectively creating a captive industrial offtaker that stabilises generation utilisation rates [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on Metlen's multi-stage integration is that it creates operational complexity without proportional margin uplift — managing mining, refining, smelting, generation, trading, and retail simultaneously requires significant management bandwidth; single-stage specialists like TRIMET (smelting-only) can optimise a single node more efficiently [#J]

§4 — Margin Distribution Across the Chain

  • J-front verdict: In both the power and metals chains, Metlen occupies margin-rich nodes (generation and primary smelting when conditions favour) but faces structural thin-margin exposure at the retail and refining stages [#J]

  • Most-profitable stage (power): Hydro and RES generation — EBIT margins 20-35% in European markets (Iberdrola RES EBIT margin ~25-30%; hydro historically higher); Metlen's hydro cascade should deliver structurally above-average generation margins [#I]

  • Most-profitable stage (metals): Primary smelting during LME high-cycle with contained energy costs — EBIT margins can reach 15-20% for low-cost European smelters (Hydro's aluminium metal segment EBIT margin 12-18% across cycles); Metlen's AoG smelter is among the highest-cost European smelters due to Greek energy prices [#J]

  • Margin-thin stage (power): Gas thermal generation — EBIT margins 5-12% (spark spread compression in liquid European markets; reference: RWE gas fleet EBIT margin ~6-8%); structurally undermined by carbon costs and gas price volatility [#J]

  • Margin-thin stage (metals): Alumina refining — EBIT margins 8-15% when alumina/LME ratio is compressed (alumina prices historically ~13-15% of LME; compression years produce margin squeeze); Metlen's AoG refinery is not the lowest-cost globally [#I]

  • ❌ Retail energy: B2B/B2C energy retail generates EBIT margins of 2-6% structurally (reference: EDF retail ~3-5%; Enel retail ~4-7%); Elpedison retail is value-added for customer stickiness but not a margin-driver [#J]

  • Margin driver — regulatory (power): Greek RES contracts (feed-in tariffs, CFDs under current framework) provide revenue visibility that elevates effective margins above merchant equivalents; Metlen's RES book benefits from this [#I]

  • Margin driver — scale (metals): AoG's ~165kt/yr aluminium output is mid-size by European standards (Hydro Sunndal ~300kt; Aluminium Dunkerque ~280kt); scale disadvantage vs Nordics means higher unit costs [#I]

  • Margin driver — switching cost (power): Industrial and commercial retail customers on multi-year supply contracts create sticky revenue at the Elpedison retail stage, moderately elevating retail margins above pure commodity equivalents [#A]

  • Margin trend (power): Thermal generation margins contracting structurally as EU ETS carbon cost rises and RES proliferates; RES/hydro margins expanding as capacity market payments and balancing services premiums grow [#J]

  • Margin trend (metals): Primary aluminium smelting in Europe faces secular cost pressure from rising energy prices; long-term trend is margin compression unless smelters access green/cheap power (Norway, Iceland model); Metlen's Greek location is disadvantaged vs Nordic peers [#J]

§5 — Power Dynamics + Pricing Leverage

  • J-front verdict: Pricing power is strongly asymmetric across Metlen's segments — generation/trading is moderately price-setter, smelting is a full price-taker, retail is contested [#J]

  • Pricing power (generation): Hydro and RES output benefits from CfD/FiT regulatory frameworks in Greece that provide floor pricing; merchant exposure exists above the floor, creating upside optionality without full downside risk [#J]

  • Sub: Thermal generation is fully merchant-priced via the Greek day-ahead market , meaning Metlen is a price-taker at the margin for its gas fleet [#J]

  • Pricing power (metals): Zero proprietary pricing power at the primary smelting stage — LME aluminium prices are globally set; Metlen's AoG smelter sells at LME +/- regional premium [#J]

  • Sub: The alumina/bauxite upstream integration provides modest cost-side pricing influence (internal transfer prices can be optimised), but the top-line is fully LME-governed [#I]

  • Customer concentration (power): Greek retail energy market has high concentration of large industrial customers; Elpedison's top industrial accounts (aluminium smelter being one) represent a concentrated but captive base [#A]

  • Sub: The smelter is simultaneously Elpedison's own largest customer — an intragroup relationship that reduces external customer concentration risk but creates transfer-price complexity [#I]

  • Switching cost (retail energy): B2B industrial customers have medium switching costs (multi-year contracts, infrastructure dependencies); residential customers have low switching costs in liberalised Greek market [#J]

  • Differentiation (generation): Hydro generation is inherently differentiated — low marginal cost, dispatchable, increasingly valuable for grid balancing as RES penetration rises; Metlen's hydro cascade on the Acheloos river system is a non-replicable physical asset [#I]

  • Substitutes (power): Battery storage and demand response are emerging substitutes for dispatchable generation; 10-15 year horizon before material threat; intermediate-term hydro premium is structurally secure [#J]

  • Supplier leverage (gas): Metlen has limited leverage over gas suppliers — DEPA is a regulated entity, LNG spot is market-priced; 2021-22 TTF spike demonstrated this structural exposure viscerally [#J]

  • Sub: The only structural mitigation is volume flexibility (ability to reduce thermal dispatch when gas costs spike) and partial displacement by hydro/RES [#A]

  • Supplier leverage (bauxite/alumina): Delphi-Distomon provides partial self-supply; imported bauxite sourced from Guinea, Ghana, Jamaica (typical European refinery feedstock geographies) — political risk is low-medium [#I]

  • Customer leverage (smelter): Primary aluminium buyers (automotive, packaging, construction) are price-takers on LME — they cannot dictate price below LME but compete on regional premium and product spec [#J]

  • Segment-specific power (hydro): Hydro is the single highest-pricing-power asset class in Metlen's portfolio — Greek hydro output commands premium dispatch priority and benefits from capacity payments as a balancing resource [#I]

  • Regulatory barrier (generation): Greek grid connection permits, environmental licensing, and water-use rights for hydro are high-barrier-to-entry moats that protect Metlen's existing generation margin [#J]

  • ❌ Dictated terms : Primary aluminium smelting has no pricing power whatsoever — Metlen's AoG is a global price-taker with no ability to influence LME; margin depends entirely on cost management and LME cycle [#J]

  • Scale economy (generation): Metlen's ~2.5GW total generation capacity is mid-scale by European IPP standards (reference: Greenvolt ~1.5GW, Voltalia ~2GW, EDPR ~28GW); insufficient for top-tier trading book optimisation but adequate for domestic leadership [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on power dynamics is that Greek energy market liberalisation, ongoing PPC privatisation, and EU single market pressures will structurally erode Metlen's quasi-captive Greek market advantages; the domestic pricing power that appears structural today could be a transitional regulatory artefact [#J]

§6 — Competitive Map at Metlen's Stage

  • J-front verdict: At every stage Metlen operates, it faces clearly identifiable tiers; Metlen is a domestic leader in Greek power but a global cost-disadvantaged mid-tier player in European aluminium [#J]

  • Competitor — leader (Greek power generation): PPC (Δ.Ε.Η.) is the dominant incumbent with ~35-40% of Greek electricity generation; Metlen/Elpedison is a clear second-tier domestic challenger with ~10-15% share [#I]

  • Competitor — challenger (Greek power): Mytilineos/Protergia (now Metlen), Motor Oil (Coral/NRG), Terna Energy — oligopolistic challengers in Greek generation; combined with PPC they constitute ~70-80% of Greek thermal/hydro capacity [#I]

  • Scale-cost dimension (power): PPC's scale advantage in thermal generation is structurally large; Metlen's competitive differentiation is in RES growth velocity and the integrated metals offtake, not pure scale [#J]

  • Differentiation dimension (metals): Among European primary aluminium producers, Metlen's AoG competes on proximity to Mediterranean markets and integrated upstream (own alumina) rather than energy cost (where Nordic/Icelandic peers dominate) [#J]

  • Competitor — leader (European aluminium): Hydro ASA (Norway) is the global scale leader in European primary aluminium; Alcoa Europe, TRIMET (Germany), Aluminium Dunkerque (Liberty/GFG), Alro (Romania) are mid-tier peers [#I]

  • Sub: Metlen's AoG at ~165kt/yr is below mid-scale; Hydro Sunndal alone produces ~300kt — a 2x scale disadvantage at the smelting stage [#I]

  • Geographic dimension: Greek geography gives Metlen logistics advantage for Mediterranean construction/packaging markets but structural cost disadvantage vs Northern European green-power smelters [#J]

  • Customer-segment dimension (power): Elpedison's industrial B2B book is structurally stickier than residential-heavy peers; industrial customers require complex supply contracts, metering, and demand-management services that create switching friction [#A]

  • Stage concentration (power): Greek generation is an oligopoly — 4-5 players control >80% of capacity; Metlen's position is competitively defensible within this structure [#J]

  • Stage concentration (smelting): European primary aluminium is highly concentrated — fewer than 10 active smelters; the competitive set is narrow but cost-curve differentiation is extreme (Norwegian hydro-powered smelters vs Greek gas-exposed smelters) [#I]

  • Competitor — niche (Greek RES): Independent RES developers (Terna Energy, Volterra, Greenvolt Greece) are growing fast in Greek renewables; their pure-play RES focus allows tighter project economics than Metlen's diversified model [#J]

  • Stage fragmentation (metals downstream): Downstream aluminium fabrication (Alumil's territory) is far more fragmented than primary smelting — dozens of European extruders and rollers; Metlen's partial exposure here is in the less concentrated, lower-margin end of the metals chain [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on Metlen's competitive position is that pure-play RES specialists are growing faster in Greek renewables than Metlen's diversified entity; Terna Energy and Volterra have superior renewable development pipeline optionality relative to their market caps, and could outpace Metlen's RES growth in the 3-5 year horizon [#J]

§7 — Competitive Maps at Adjacent Stages + Vertical Integration Threats

  • J-front verdict: Adjacent-stage competitive structure presents moderate-to-high vertical integration threat from customer-side consolidation and low-to-moderate threat from supplier-side integration, but the most credible forward risk is customer self-generation [#J]

  • Supplier — leader (gas supply): DEPA Commercial (now privatised) and LNG spot via Revythoussa terminal dominate Greek gas supply; Energean as domestic E&P operator is the only credible domestic gas supplier growing its upstream base [#I]

  • Sub: Energean's upstream integration into Greek/Israeli gas could eventually bypass DEPA's intermediary role, but this is a supply-system evolution, not a direct threat to Metlen [#A]

  • Supplier consolidation (alumina/bauxite): Global bauxite supply is increasingly concentrated in Guinea (60%+ of seaborne trade); AoG's partial domestic supply via Delphi-Distomon is a structural hedge against Guinea geopolitical risk [#I]

  • Supplier consolidation (equipment/EPC): RES equipment suppliers (Vestas, Siemens Gamesa, GE) are experiencing supply chain consolidation; turbine lead times and pricing power have shifted toward suppliers in 2023-25, squeezing RES developer margins [#J]

  • Customer — leader (aluminium): Greek and Balkan aluminium buyers (construction profiles, automotive stamping, packaging converters) are highly fragmented; no single customer approaches market power over AoG [#A]

  • Sub: Broader European aluminium buyers (Novelis/Hindalco, Constellium) are large and consolidated — if AoG were to seek direct OEM relationships, it would face sophisticated buyers with strong LME-based negotiating leverage [#J]

  • Customer consolidation (power): Greek industrial power consumers are consolidating and self-generating; large industrial buyers (including Metlen's own smelter, steel plants) are increasingly pursuing self-generation through PPAs with RES developers [#J]

  • Customer vertical integration threat: Large industrial energy consumers (steel, cement, chemicals) are signing direct PPAs with RES developers, bypassing Elpedison's retail intermediary function — this is the most credible near-term vertical integration threat to Metlen's retail/trading margin [#J]

  • Supplier vertical integration (upstream gas): E&P companies (Energean) could theoretically integrate downstream into power generation; Energean has already signalled gas-to-power ambitions in Israel — Greek market equivalent is possible but not yet actioned [#A]

  • Adjacent threat (government integration): ADMIE (Greek transmission operator, state-controlled) is expanding into energy storage and smart-grid services — a state-led vertical integration into the balancing/flexibility stage that could squeeze Metlen's trading margin over time [#J]

  • Supplier vertical integration (RES equipment): Turbine OEMs have historically been pure equipment suppliers; some (Vestas, GE) have moved into project development and ownership — a long-term adjacency threat to Metlen's RES pipeline [#A]

  • Customer vertical integration (aluminium): Downstream aluminium processors (Constellium, Novelis) have historically not integrated upstream into primary smelting due to energy cost complexity; this threat is structurally low [#J]

  • ❌ Customer self-generation: The Greek industrial sector is actively pursuing self-generation via corporate PPAs — effectively bypassing Elpedison retail; if 20-30% of Elpedison's industrial book migrates to direct PPAs over 5 years, the retail margin contribution would compress materially [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: The most credible vertical integration vector threatening Metlen is not supplier or competitor integration but buyer self-generation — large industrial customers who sign PPAs directly with solar developers, cutting Elpedison out of the margin chain entirely; this is already happening at the European level (reference: Volkswagen, ArcelorMittal direct RES PPAs) [#J]

§8 — Structural Changes + Evolution

  • J-front verdict: The value chain is structurally evolving away from thermal generation and toward flexibility services, storage, and green metals, both of which create opportunity and risk for Metlen's current integration model [#J]

  • Stage consolidation (European power): European power generation is consolidating toward large renewables-integrated utilities (Iberdrola, RWE, EDF) and away from mid-size thermal-heavy operators; Metlen's thermal portfolio faces structural headwinds from this consolidation trend [#J]

  • Stage fragmentation (RES project development): Simultaneously, RES project development is fragmenting — small specialist developers, infrastructure funds, and corporate PPAs are entering, disrupting the oligopolistic generation structure [#J]

  • Vertical integration trend (utilities): European utilities are integrating vertically into battery storage, EV charging, and demand-response — Enel, E.ON, Iberdrola all have significant downstream energy-services businesses; Metlen does not yet [#A]

  • Disintermediation: The energy retail intermediary is being structurally disintermediated by direct corporate PPAs and virtual PPAs; Elpedison's B2B book is at risk from this trend over a 5-10 year horizon [#J]

  • New entrant : Tech and financial new entrants (Amazon, Microsoft via corporate PPAs, infrastructure funds via project finance) are entering the RES ownership stage, increasing capital competition and potentially compressing RES project IRRs [#J]

  • Pricing power shift — upstream: As RES penetration increases and thermal is displaced, pricing power is migrating toward flexibility providers (pumped hydro, batteries, demand response) and away from baseload thermal generators; Metlen's hydro cascade is positively positioned in this shift [#I]

  • Pricing power shift — downstream (metals): Green aluminium premium is an emerging structural shift — EU taxonomy, automotive OEM sustainability requirements, and packaging recycled-content mandates are creating a green premium for low-carbon-intensity aluminium that Metlen's Greek-solar/hydro-powered smelter could potentially access [#I]

  • Technology disruption (smelting): Inert-anode smelting technology (eliminating carbon anode CO2 emissions) is 10-15 years from commercial scale; ELYSIS (Rio/Alcoa JV) is the leading project; if it scales, it redefines the smelter cost and carbon structure, creating obsolescence risk for conventional smelters like AoG [#A]

  • Business model innovation (energy-as-service): The shift from commodity energy sales to energy-as-a-service (managed load, demand response, flexibility contracts) is restructuring utility business models; Metlen's current model is primarily commodity-sales, not service-based [#J]

  • Stage consolidation (aluminium): European primary aluminium smelting is gradually consolidating via closures (high-energy-cost smelters shutting in UK, Germany during 2021-23 energy crisis) rather than M&A; the competitive set is thinning, which is structurally margin-supportive for surviving low-cost operators [#I]

  • ⚡ RARE — green metals premium: The convergence of EU taxonomy capital flows, automotive OEM Scope 3 targets, and CBAM (Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism) is creating a structurally new pricing tier for green aluminium that did not exist 5 years ago; Metlen's ability to power AoG with its own RES/hydro generation is a potentially transformational competitive repositioning that the market has not yet fully priced [#I]

  • DTC trend (power): Corporate PPA growth is effectively a direct-to-customer shift bypassing retail intermediaries; Metlen could participate as a PPA provider (seller) or be disrupted as a retail intermediary (buyer) — the strategic positioning is ambiguous [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on structural evolution is that Metlen's window for green metals premium extraction is short — if Nordic smelters (with already near-zero-carbon hydro power) are first to secure automotive OEM green aluminium contracts, Greek-based AoG may be permanently locked out of the premium tier regardless of Metlen's own RES build [#J]

§9 — Competitive Advantages + Strategic Implications + Sceptical Synthesis

  • J-front verdict: Metlen's value-chain position creates genuine structural advantages in Greek power and partial advantages in European metals, but the bear case is structurally coherent: thermal fleet obsolescence + smelter cost disadvantage + retail disintermediation are simultaneous headwinds [#J]

  • Structural advantage (hydro): The Acheloos hydro cascade is a non-replicable physical asset — licensed, permitted, and operationally proven; it provides low-marginal-cost dispatchable electricity that is increasingly valuable in a high-RES penetration grid [#I]

  • Sub: No new large hydro will be built in Greece — environmental and permitting barriers make this effectively an indefinite moat at the generation stage [#I]

  • Structural advantage (vertical metals integration): The bauxite-alumina-smelter integration creates structural cost visibility denied to pure-play smelters that buy alumina at spot — during alumina price spikes (e.g., 2018 Alunorte disruption), Metlen's integrated structure provided significant margin protection [#I]

  • Position-based moat (Greek power market): Metlen's established generation + trading + retail integration in the Greek market creates multi-layer switching barriers — replacing Metlen as a supplier requires competitors to replicate the generation fleet, trading infrastructure, and retail relationships simultaneously [#J]

  • Strategic option — upstream (gas): Metlen could theoretically pursue upstream gas equity (e.g., participation in Greek E&P via Energean partnerships, or Prinos field revival) to reduce gas cost exposure — structurally logical but not yet actioned [#A]

  • Strategic option — downstream (green metals): The single most value-creative strategic option is certifying AoG's output as green aluminium using internally generated RES power, accessing the EU taxonomy green premium — this is an RES capex-to-metals-margin arbitrage play [#I]

  • Strategic option — adjacent (flexibility/storage): As hydro becomes the Greek grid's primary balancing resource, Metlen could develop pumped storage or grid-scale battery assets to extend the hydro moat into the flexibility-services stage [#A]
