Financial Performance & Regional Divergence
Group Financial Performance Overview

Vodafone's broader financial trajectory has shown a mixed but gradually improving profile across recent reporting periods. In the first half of fiscal year 2025, the group reported a 1.6% year-over-year increase in reported revenues to €18.30 billion, alongside an organic service revenue growth rate of 4.8%. However, adjusted EBITDAaL for the same period was slightly weaker, declining 0.3% to €5.40 billion on a reported basis. The adjusted free cash flow metric for 1H25 stood at -€0.95 billion, representing an improvement from the -€1.38 billion recorded in the prior year.

Full-year 2025 results demonstrated solid overall performance despite pronounced regional variations, with group revenue reaching €37.40 billion and organic service revenue expanding by 5.1% year-over-year. Full-year adjusted EBITDAaL grew by 2.5% on a like-for-like basis to €10.90 billion, while adjusted free cash flow came in at €2.54 billion, effectively in line with the previous year's €2.60 billion. Moving into the first half of fiscal year 2026, the company posted accelerating organic service revenue growth of 5.7% and group revenue of €19.60 billion. Adjusted EBITDAaL for 1H26 reached €5.70 billion, marking a robust 6.8% like-for-like increase. For the full year 2026, management guidance targets adjusted EBITDAaL between €11.00 billion and €11.30 billion, excluding the impact of the UK merger, alongside €2.60 billion in adjusted free cash flow.

Germany: Regulatory Headwinds and Turnaround Efforts

The German market has been a significant focal point for sell-side analysts due to its pronounced underperformance and subsequent turnaround initiatives. In 1H25, Germany experienced a 3.9% organic decline in service revenues and a notable 9.3% contraction in adjusted EBITDAaL. This weakness persisted into the second half of the year, where EBITDAaL in Germany plummeted by 15.9%. Analysts attribute this severe top-line and profitability drag primarily to structural and regulatory changes surrounding cable television. Effective July 1, 2024, a legislative change prohibited landlords from bundling cable TV costs into the ancillary costs of tenant rental agreements, forcing Vodafone to transition end-users to direct, individual contracts.

Despite the severe initial impact, analysts note that the headwinds from the TV contract unbundling have largely concluded. The company successfully secured four million customers, achieving a retention rate of approximately 50.00%, which aligned with management's prior expectations. Furthermore, the German unit has engaged in strategic partnerships to stabilize its position, including signing new wholesale fibre contracts that expanded its marketable gigabit-capable footprint by nearly 5.00 million households. Mobile network quality is widely viewed as a close second to Deutsche Telekom, and a new roaming contract with 1&1 successfully commenced in August 2024. Consequently, 2Q26 results indicated a return to revenue growth in Germany, contributing positively to the broader group's solid 1H26 performance.

Africa: Sustained Organic Growth

In stark contrast to the structural challenges in Germany, Vodafone's African operations have consistently served as the group's primary growth engine. Analysts highlight that the region delivered an impressive 9.9% organic service revenue growth in the first half of 2025. This robust top-line expansion was matched by strong profitability, with the African segment generating 13.6% EBITDAaL growth in the latter half of 2024. The sustained momentum in Africa has provided a crucial counterweight to the weak top-line and earnings trends observed across Vodafone's European footprint.

The UK: Mergers and Market Consolidation

Sell-side commentary regarding the UK market heavily emphasizes the strategic necessity and financial impact of the Vodafone and Three UK merger. Prior to the consolidation, analysts argued that the standalone business cases for both operators were highly challenged. Following the transaction, the combined entity is guiding for significant cost synergies to improve long-term profitability. However, the near-term financial reporting has seen some friction; in 1H26, operating profit decreased by 9.2% year-over-year to €2.20 billion, a drop primarily driven by higher depreciation and amortisation expenses associated with the consolidation of Three UK.

The structural landscape of the UK telecommunications market was fundamentally altered when the Vodafone-Three merger officially launched on May 31, 2025. The combined operator immediately ascended to the number-one market position, overtaking legacy incumbents O2 and British Telecom by assembling a base of 29.00 million subscribers. As a regulatory concession to secure approval for the transaction, the new entity committed to a massive capital expenditure program, pledging to invest £11.00 billion into 5G Standalone networks over the next decade.

Capital Allocation, Shareholder Returns & Credit Profile
Leverage Targets and Balance Sheet Flexibility

ING reports that Vodafone's net debt-to-adjusted EBITDAaL ratio stood at 2.6x for the first half of fiscal year 2025, which represents a slight increase from the 2.5x leverage recorded at the end of fiscal 2024. Excluding the Spanish and Italian operations, the telecommunications group carried €31.80 billion in net debt into the period. In response to its shifting operational footprint, management has instituted a firm leverage policy targeting 2.25x to 2.75x net debt-to-adjusted EBITDAaL. According to ING, the company's explicit goal is to comfortably operate within the bottom half of this guided range going forward.

Rothschild & Co Redburn models a more aggressive de-leveraging trajectory as a direct result of the company's asset optimization program, estimating that post-deal pro forma leverage will organically drop to 2.0x. If the company opts to target a 2.3x net debt-to-EBITDAaL ratio, analysts project it would unlock approximately €3.70 billion in surplus capital. Redburn suggests this substantial liquidity cushion grants the operator significant balance sheet flexibility to execute large-scale tender offers while preserving sufficient dry powder for infrastructure investments in key markets like Germany.

Credit Profile and Debt Spreads

ING analysts observe that while Vodafone has made commendable progress in sharpening its business profile and strengthening its core credit metrics, imminent credit rating upgrades are not expected. The front end of the operator's credit curve currently trades at fair value and actively benefits from cash influxes via asset sales, which management is expected to deploy toward targeted debt tender offers. However, the longer end of Vodafone's credit curve appears broadly unattractive to analysts, as it features noticeably wider spreads when compared to the curve of its higher-rated peer, Orange.

ING further evaluates the company's hybrid debt positioning, warning that robust market technicals have driven Vodafone's hybrid notes to trade at historically rich spreads. These new hybrid instruments are deemed somewhat expensive due to an unusually flat hybrid curve, effectively trading at similar spread levels to Telefonica hybrids with durations of under four years. Consequently, credit analysts maintain a clear relative-value preference for the hybrids and longer-dated corporate notes of Orange over those issued by Vodafone.
