KEY FINDINGS - Mixed verdict overall: Allison Kirkby is a credible, experienced telco operator with a clear strategic mandate (Build, Sell, Grow), but her track record at BT is short (CEO since Jan 2024) and the transformation thesis remains unproven at scale; the governance architecture is adequate by FTSE 100 standards yet carries several structural concerns that Richard should stress-test

  • Kirkby signals capability: Prior CEO at Telia (Sweden) and TDC (Denmark) demonstrates she can execute cost-out and culture resets in unionised, capital-heavy telco environments — the closest available analogue to BT's challenge, and a meaningful differentiator vs a pure financial-engineer predecessor
  • CFO Subash Chandrasekar is stabilising: Appointed Jan 2024 alongside Kirkby, carries Morgan Stanley / Vodafone pedigree and a reputation for disciplined balance-sheet management; the twin appointment creates a governance event that Richard should treat as a credible reset signal — but two years is insufficient track record to verify
  • Board is structurally adequate but shows three latent concerns: (1) historically high average director tenure creating familiarity risk, (2) the UK Government's 0.001% golden share is a structural constraint on control transactions, and (3) the Altice/Patrick Drahi stake (now c.24.5% per last public disclosure) creates a de facto blocking position that is not reflected in formal governance disclosures
  • Compensation structure is mixed-aligned: Performance Share Plan uses EBITDA + FCF + relative TSR — the right metrics for a transformation narrative — but the 2022-24 vintages paid out partially despite significant shareholder value destruction; this pay-for-performance disconnect is the single most visible governance red flag
  • Culture indicators are cautionary: Glassdoor rating of ~3.1/5 with CEO approval below 60% as at available data; legacy restructuring overhang (c.55,000 headcount reduction announced 2023, net reduction to c.90k employees by late 2020s from c.130k in 2022) creates a prolonged culture drag that will be an operational execution risk
  • Animal-CEO match is moderate-to-strong: Kirkby maps reasonably well to the "Restructuring Operator" sub-archetype — decisive, cost-focused, strong on narrative — but lacks the capital-allocation boldness and M&A opportunism of a fully-formed animal CEO; closer to a disciplined professional manager than a force-of-nature founder
  • Single largest operator risk: Kirkby's mandate is extremely demanding (£3bn+ FCF by FY27 target, 55,000 job cuts, fibre build acceleration, complex union relations) — the risk is that the transformation is too many simultaneous variables for any management team, not that Kirkby is individually incapable
  • Governance fragility: The Drahi/Altice overhang is the single most structurally anomalous governance feature — a 24%+ blockholder with a leveraged European telecom empire under financial stress, whose strategic intent with the BT stake remains publicly ambiguous
  • Overall operator-governance plausibility: Medium — Kirkby is a credible operator in the right archetype, the board is structurally adequate, but the Drahi overhang, culture-in-transition, and unproven-at-BT track record mean Richard cannot yet assign high-confidence plausibility

§1 — CEO Assessment

- Allison Kirkby, appointed Group CEO January 2024; previously designated CEO September 2023 with formal handover from Philip Jansen
- Career trajectory: Telia Company (Sweden) CEO 2020-2023 → TDC (Denmark) CEO 2017-2020 → TDC CFO 2014-2017 → various CFO/finance roles at Vodafone and Tele2 going back to the 1990s
- Formal education: qualified accountant ; the CFO-turned-CEO pathway is common in European telco and typically produces operators who are numerically disciplined but sometimes less aggressive on growth bets
- Nationality: British; long Scandinavian operating experience is relevant given BT's structural parallels with Scandinavian incumbents (fibre modernisation, workforce restructuring, regulatory environment)

CEO tenure:
- Tenure at BT: ~17 months as of May 2026 — short track record at this company specifically; inherited the "Build, Sell, Grow" strategic framework substantially from her predecessor but has publicly endorsed and operationalised it
- Tenure at prior company (Telia): 3 years — consistent with the pattern of a professional operator brought in for a fixed-horizon restructuring, delivering, then moving on

CEO track record at BT Group:
- Inherited: a company in structural revenue decline in its consumer and legacy enterprise divisions, a c.£20bn net debt pile, ongoing BTPS pension obligations, an OpenReach fibre build commitment to pass 25m premises by end-2026, and a publicly announced plan to cut 40,000-55,000 jobs by late 2020s
- Visible actions L18M: (a) reaffirmed cost-out / headcount reduction targets; (b) reaffirmed FCF trajectory toward £3bn+ by FY27; (c) launched a "Better BT" internal transformation programme; (d) conducted Digital Unit review; (e) maintained dividend at 8p per share despite capex pressure — a dividend discipline signal that Richard should weigh against the balance sheet burden

- EBITDA: adjusted EBITDA c.£8.1bn in FY25; FCF improving from trough but not yet at transformation target; the FY27 £3bn FCF target is achievable if capex peaks in FY25-26 and opex costs come out — the management is not yet behind the target but the execution window is narrow

- Union relations: BT's CWU (Communications Workers Union) relationship is a critical management dependency; Kirkby has maintained engagement with the union during the largest headcount reduction in BT's modern history — no major industrial action under her tenure (distinct from the 2022/23 strikes under Jansen's tenure) — this is a genuine operational credibility marker

Capital allocation history:

M&A track record:

Animal-CEO marker:

§6 — Governance Structure + Red Flags

J-front verdict: Governance is mixed/red-flagged — formally adequate per FTSE 100 standards but the Drahi/Altice blockholder structure and historical audit concerns represent material structural anomalies

Dual-class structure:
- BT has a single share class (ordinary shares) — no dual-class structure; formally clean on voting rights
- UK Government golden share: The UK Government holds one special share (the "golden share") which confers the right to block any individual or group from acquiring more than 50% of BT's voting rights; this was originally designed post-privatisation to protect strategic national infrastructure; it has not been exercised in decades but is a structural constraint on change-of-control transactions and any future privatisation / acquisition scenarios — relevant to Richard's exit optionality assessment
- Altice/Drahi stake: Patrick Drahi's Altice group built a stake in BT reaching approximately 24.5% — the largest single shareholder by a wide margin; the second largest is Deutsche Telekom at approximately 12% (legacy from the EE merger); no other shareholder exceeds 5%
- The Drahi stake is a structural governance anomaly: (a) it is large enough to block a hostile takeover; (b) it is large enough to block special resolutions (requiring 75% majority) if Altice votes against; (c) Altice is a highly leveraged European telecom operator that has faced significant financial stress since 2022, including multiple debt restructuring events at the Altice International and Altice France entities; (d) the strategic intent of the BT stake has never been publicly clarified — Drahi has neither made a formal offer nor publicly committed to being a long-term passive investor
- The UK Government investigated the Drahi stake under the National Security and Investment Act 2021 and concluded (2023) that the stake could remain but with certain conditions (not publicly detailed) — this partially neutralises the hostile-takeover risk but does not resolve the governance ambiguity of a financially stressed, strategically opaque large shareholder with a board seat

Related-party transactions:
- No material related-party transactions disclosed in BT's FY24/FY25 annual reports beyond standard director fee arrangements and employment contracts
- The Altice NED seat creates a theoretical related-party information asymmetry risk (see §4 sceptical bullet) but does not constitute a related-party transaction in the formal sense
