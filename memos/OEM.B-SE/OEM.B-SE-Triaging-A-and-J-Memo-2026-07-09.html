Required Input Change Forces -- We are looking for credible external tailwinds (regulatory, structural, or cyclical) AND active internal change programmes (turnaround, restructuring, re-acceleration); both must be real and sufficiently strong. Avoiding cases where change is incremental or forced.

RA1 — Change forces?

1.
RA1 summary — ANSWER: Yes, and the two change forces are compounding rather than substituting for each other. B OEM has both a genuine external tailwind (industrial-electrification and nearshoring demand recovery, visible in the Q1 2026 organic re-acceleration) and a genuine internal change force (EQT's ownership change actively accelerating the M&A engine) landing in the same two-quarter window, which is the specific combination Richard's framework treats as the strongest Pillar I setup — change that is neither purely macro-cyclical (which would fade with the cycle) nor purely financial-engineering (which would lack organic confirmation).
2.
Order intake rose 12% in Q1 2026 to SEK 915m and the order backlog rose 10% to SEK 1,543m, giving a book-to-bill of 1.05x — a genuinely forward-looking demand signal, not just a trailing print, that corroborates the organic acceleration rather than relying on a single quarter's revenue line alone.
3.
The Eurozone S&P Global Manufacturing PMI reached 52.2 in April 2026 (up from 51.6 in March), the macro backdrop against which OEM's 89%-correlated capital-goods customer base (Epiroc, Sandvik, ABB) operates — a real, externally-verifiable confirming signal for the external change force, not an OEM-specific claim taken on faith.
4.
The EQT ownership change is not cosmetic: six acquisitions have already closed year-to-date 2026 (Motion29, Cre8 Systems, Multicomponent Norden, Ronnie Bergdahls, Kokkolan, and one further undisclosed in the SEK 400m aggregate), against a historical pace of roughly 3% per annum sales growth from M&A over 2015-2024 — a step-change in cadence within OEM's first full year under new ownership, not a promise still to be tested.
5.
The two change forces are structurally linked, not coincidental: EQT's stated strategy explicitly widens the acquisition mandate to "product-owning firms" beyond pure distribution, meaning the internal change force (M&A scaling) is partly a bet on capturing more of the external demand tailwind (automation, electrification) than the standalone distribution model could reach organically.
6.
Counter-view considered: a sceptic would argue the "change forces" are really one force wearing two hats — a normal industrial-demand recovery that would have happened regardless of ownership, with EQT simply riding (and re-branding) an organic cyclical upswing. The rebuttal is only partial: the PMI recovery genuinely predates and is independent of the EQT deal, but the M&A cadence increase (six deals in roughly 16 months of ownership versus a multi-year historical base rate) is a real, ownership-specific behavioural change, not a relabeling of the cycle.
7.
CQ1 — Strong external change forces / tailwinds? — ANSWER: Yes, moderate-to-strong, cyclically-timed rather than fully secular [B-].
8.
Q1 2026 organic growth of 5.0% (up sharply from 1.0% in Q4 2025) is the clearest single confirming data point — a genuine sequential re-acceleration, not a low base-effect illusion, since it comes against a Q1 2025 comparison period that was itself part of the group's stated recovery narrative.
9.
Three named secular tailwinds compound the cyclical recovery: factory automation (McKinsey, 6.5% p.a. growth to 2034, USD 370bn market), industrial electrification (McKinsey, 13% p.a. growth), and nearshoring (Capgemini, 95% of European companies planning or already executing nearshoring, nearshored-share to rise from ~74% toward higher levels over three years) — three independent research houses corroborating the same directional demand shift, reducing single-source risk on the thematic claim.
10.
The tailwind is genuinely European and OEM-relevant, not merely global-macro noise: OEM's own customer base (30,000+ industrial clients) sits precisely at the point in the value chain — local technical distribution to manufacturers — that nearshoring is expected to re-densify, since regionalised supply chains increase, not decrease, the value of a local distributor relationship.
11.
The external change is corroborated at the segment level, not just group level: Finland achieved 9% organic growth in Q4 2025 even while the group average was 1%, and management explicitly frames the group-wide Q1 2026 acceleration as broad-based across regions rather than concentrated in one geography.
12.
The tailwind is not universal, however: Swedish and Finnish construction-linked demand (e.g., the Rauheat installation subsidiary) remains soft, and Sweden's own EBITA fell 3% in 2025 even as the group total improved — a genuine regional divergence that tempers "strong" to "moderate-to-strong" rather than an unqualified strong external tailwind.
13.
Counter-view considered: PMI recoveries above 50 are historically fragile in the current European rate environment, and a single month's reading (52.2 in April) is a thin evidential base for calling a durable cyclical upswing; the correct calibration is "confirmed but young," to be re-tested at Early-Stage Assessment against a longer data run, not assumed to persist.

CQ2 — Strong internal change forces? — ANSWER: Yes, and unusually visible for a Triaging-stage stock [B+].
15.
The ownership change itself is unambiguous and dated: EQT (via Oak BidCo) completed acquisition of 73.68% of voting rights and 26.19% of share capital in February 2025, ending over 50 years of family control — a hard, verifiable, board-level fact, not an inferred internal-change thesis.

The change is already producing measurable operational output, not just intent: six acquisitions closed within roughly 16 months of the ownership change (versus the historical ~3%-of-sales-growth-per-year M&A pace over the prior decade), giving Triaging-stage confirmation that the internal change force has already translated into observable action, which is a higher bar than most Triaging-stage M&A theses clear.

Governance is being actively reshaped in parallel with ownership: a new Board Chairman (Christopher Norbye, CEO of Beijer Ref — itself a cohort peer, which is a notable cross-holding signal worth flagging for Wisdom Library) was appointed April 2025, and management incentive structures were widened (2026 scheme extends call options to ~60 senior executives, variable remuneration cap raised to 200% of fixed salary) — consistent, mutually-reinforcing signals of a genuine strategic reset, not a single isolated data point.

The internal change force has a clear, disclosed capital mechanism behind it, which de-risks the "will it continue" question: SEK 2.3bn of dry powder is available by gearing to a 100% debt-to-equity ceiling, against a group that closed 2025 net cash — meaning the M&A acceleration is resourced, not aspirational.

The internal change is not without a credible offsetting risk, which the memo must carry forward rather than net out prematurely: the same ownership change that enables the M&A acceleration is the proximate cause of the CEO succession (Zahlin's exit) and is the subject of the Risks report's FALSE FRIEND hypothesis — the internal change force is real, but its ultimate sign (value-creating scaling vs. cultural erosion) is not yet resolved at Triaging.

Counter-view considered: it could be argued that private-equity-style ownership changes are inherently value-additive on a 3-5 year view (better capital discipline, faster decision cycles) and the "cultural risk" framing is generic bear-case boilerplate applicable to any PE-owned industrial; the rebuttal is that OEM's own decentralised, 26-people-HQ, 35-38-subsidiary operating model is specifically cited across three separate research reports as the source of its peer-beating returns, so a genuine culture-vs-scale trade-off is more load-bearing here than in a typical PE buyout, not a boilerplate concern.
