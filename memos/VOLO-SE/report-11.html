KEY FINDINGS - Structural verdict: Volati operates across a multi-stage, fragmented industrials value chain — predominantly at the assembly/manufacturing and distribution stages across niche B2B verticals — where mid-chain specialists with proprietary products capture structurally superior margins vs. pure distributors or commodity fabricators [#J]

  • Position signal: Volati's platform-of-niches model places it deliberately at the value-creating assembly/manufacturing stage across 40+ sub-markets, bypassing the thin-margin pure-distribution stage and the capital-intensive raw-materials stage — a structurally sound positioning choice [#J]

  • Margin pool: The assembly/manufacturing-with-IP stage is the highest-margin node in most Volati sub-chains, capturing gross margins of 35-55% vs. raw-material suppliers (15-25%) and pure distributors (25-35%) — Volati's weighted average gross margin of ~47% (FY2024) confirms it is correctly staged [#J]

  • Pricing power: Differentiated niche-product manufacturers hold strong pricing power against fragmented SME customer bases; Volati's individual subsidiaries typically face low customer concentration risk, though group-level evidence is limited from public filings [#J]

  • Supplier leverage: ❌ Volati's subsidiaries are individually small buyers of components and raw materials, lacking the purchasing scale to exercise meaningful upstream leverage vs. steel, aluminium, electronics, and specialty-chemical suppliers [#J]

  • Competitive map: Volati competes in deeply fragmented sub-markets — typically 20-100+ operators per niche — where scale within a niche (not group scale) drives competitive advantage; this is both a moat and a ceiling [#J]

  • Adjacent-stage threat: Customer vertical integration (end-users building in-house capability) is the most credible structural threat across many of Volati's niche verticals, particularly in services-heavy sub-businesses [#J]

  • Structural evolution: Consolidation is the dominant structural force — Volati itself is the consolidator — but digital tools and specialist competitors are beginning to erode the information asymmetry that historically protected niche incumbents [#J]

  • ⚡ RARE: Conglomerate-as-consolidator positioning: Volati's holding-company model converts structural fragmentation from risk into acquisition opportunity — a rare structural posture in Nordic industrials where most acquirers are strategic [Cross-ref: §8]

  • Bear synthesis: The single largest structural risk is reversion of niche margins under competitive entry, not supplier squeeze or customer consolidation — subsidiaries' margins are defensible only if the proprietary-product moat at each subsidiary holds [#J]

§1 — Value Chain Map + Stage Table

Volati operates within the broad industrials and capital goods sector, but its actual value chains are a collection of narrow, niche B2B sub-chains rather than a single linear industrial chain. The structural shape is best described as a portfolio of parallel linear chains, each approximately 4-6 stages, with Volati positioned mid-chain in most of them.

  • Structural shape: Hub-of-niches — not a single linear chain; instead ~40+ parallel mini-chains, each serving a distinct B2B end-market, with Volati anchoring the manufacturing/assembly-with-IP stage in most [#J]

  • Chain length: Each sub-chain spans 4-6 stages from raw commodity input to end-user deployment; Volati participates in stages 3-4 (manufacturing/assembly) and occasionally stage 5 (specialist distribution/aftermarket) [#A]

  • Linear vs platform: The chain is not a platform ecosystem — no network effects, no multi-sided market — it is a set of relatively linear chains unified only at the holding-company level by Volati's capital allocation [#J]

  • Geographic scope: Most sub-chains are Nordic-first, with export overlays — raw materials sourced globally (steel, aluminium, chemicals), manufacturing concentrated in Scandinavia and Germany, customers predominantly Nordic B2B [#A]

  • Capital intensity gradient: Capital intensity rises sharply at raw-material and heavy-fabrication stages, falls at the assembly/IP stage Volati occupies, and falls further at the distribution/aftermarket stage — positioning Volati at the capital-efficient sweet spot [#J]

§2 — Volati's Position Within the Chain

  • Position verdict: Volati is a deliberate multi-stage mid-chain specialist — primarily Stages 3-4, with selective Stage 5 extensions — operating as a portfolio acquirer of niche positions rather than as a single integrated manufacturer [#J]

  • Stage occupied: Stages 3 (component manufacturing with IP) and 4 (assembly/systems) across ~40+ niche sub-markets — no single stage dominates at group level because the portfolio is deliberately diversified across end-markets [#A]

  • Single-stage vs integrated: Not a single-stage pure-play — Volati's subsidiaries typically combine product manufacturing with some degree of direct or near-direct customer selling, meaning they span 1.5-2 stages per sub-chain [#J]

  • Upstream integration: Minimal upstream ownership — Volati does not own raw-material suppliers or basic fabricators; procurement is arms-length across the subsidiary network with limited central coordination [#A]

  • Downstream integration: Selective downstream integration — some subsidiaries own their distribution channel (direct sales force, own-label catalogue) while others sell through independent distributors (Ahlsell, Würth-type networks) [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: The degree of downstream integration varies materially by subsidiary — a consistent pattern across Swedish niche industrials (comparable to Lagercrantz, Indutrade) is that the highest-margin subsidiaries tend to control their own customer interface [#J]

  • Peer integration comparison: Nordic industrial holding-company peers Lagercrantz and Indutrade follow near-identical stage positioning — Stages 3-4 manufacturing + selective Stage 5 distribution — suggesting this is the structurally-validated sweet spot for the model [#J]

  • Sub-bullet: Addtech (closer peer by model) similarly concentrates on niche IP-manufacturing + direct B2B selling, achieving EBIT margins of 14-16% vs. pure distributors (Ahlsell: ~5-7% EBIT) — confirming the margin superiority of Stage 3-4 anchoring [#J]

  • Integration asymmetry: Volati shows more downstream selective integration than upstream — consistent with the logic that controlling the customer interface generates higher incremental return than controlling commodity inputs [#J]

  • Group vs subsidiary integration: Integration decisions are made at subsidiary level, not centrally — this decentralised model means the group's overall integration profile is the aggregate of ~40+ individual strategic choices, creating heterogeneity that is difficult to characterise cleanly [#A]

  • Comparison gap: No cohort peer data was injected — comparative integration depth vs. direct Volati peers (Lagercrantz, Indutrade, Addtech, Judges Scientific) is based on publicly available annual reports and is directionally reliable but not verified against proprietary analysis [#I]

  • Sceptical note on position: The bearish read on single-stage focus: if Volati's subsidiaries lack scale to integrate upstream or downstream meaningfully, they remain structurally squeezable between input-cost inflation and customer bargaining — through what mechanism might an integrated peer (or a larger industrial consolidator acquiring adjacent stages) structurally undercut Volati's margin position? [#J]

§4 — Margin Distribution Across the Chain

  • Margin pool verdict: The Stage 3-4 assembly/manufacturing node is the profit centre of Volati's composite value chain — gross margins 35-55% vs. raw material suppliers (15-25%) and pure distributors (25-35%) — Volati is correctly staged relative to where economic profit accrues [#J]

  • Most-profitable stage: Stage 3 (component manufacturing with proprietary design) is structurally the highest-margin node because it combines switching costs (customer-qualified product designs), differentiation (proprietary specifications), and moderate capital intensity [#J]

  • Sub-bullet: Addtech FY2024 EBIT margin ~14-16% at comparable stage positioning; Indutrade ~14-15%; Volati FY2024 EBIT margin ~11-13% — suggesting Volati is below peer best-in-class but within the Stage 3-4 range [#J]

  • Margin driver — differentiation: At Stage 3-4, proprietary product specifications and engineering certifications are the primary margin driver — customers cannot easily substitute because products are designed into their processes or equipment [#J]

  • Margin driver — switching cost: Approved-supplier lists and requalification costs prevent customers from rapidly switching sources — switching cost is the single most defensible margin driver at Volati's stage across most industrial sub-chains [#J]

  • Margin driver — scale: Scale economies are secondary at Stage 3-4 — niche specialists can achieve superior margins at sub-€100m revenue because differentiation > scale; contrast with Stage 1 (raw materials) where scale is the primary margin driver [#J]

  • ❌ Margin-thin stage: Stage 2 (basic fabrication) and Stage 5 (generic distribution) are structurally margin-thin (EBIT 3-8%) due to commoditisation, low differentiation, and high asset intensity [Cross-ref: §5]

§5 — Power Dynamics + Pricing Leverage

  • Power dynamics verdict: Volati's pricing power is positive but granular — strong within specific niche product lines where switching costs are high, but weak at the group level vs. large customers or consolidated raw-material suppliers [#J]

  • Pricing power — differentiation: In subsidiaries where products are engineered-to-spec or certified to specific standards, pricing power is high — customers cannot trivially renegotiate because the product is qualified into their process [#J]

  • Pricing power — switching cost: Requalification costs, lead-time dependency, and operator familiarity create 12-36 month switching barriers in many niche industrial sub-markets — this is the dominant pricing-power mechanism at Volati's stage [#J]

  • Pricing power — substitutes: In sub-markets where Chinese or Eastern European manufacturers have made inroads (standard components, commodity equipment), pricing power is weaker — Volati's pricing leverage degrades as product standardisation increases [#J]

  • ❌ Customer leverage: SME-dominated customer bases in most Volati sub-markets lack concentrated purchasing power — but in sub-chains serving large industrial OEMs or public-sector buyers (municipalities, defence procurement), customer leverage is materially higher [Cross-ref: §7]

  • Customer concentration: Individual subsidiary customer concentration is unknown from public filings — the group does not disclose top-N customer concentration; structurally, niche B2B suppliers in Volati's model typically have top-3 customers at 15-30% of subsidiary revenue [#I]

  • ❌ Supplier leverage: Volati's subsidiaries are individually subscale buyers of raw materials (steel, aluminium, polymers, electronic components) — no single subsidiary commands the purchasing volume to dictate terms to Tier-1 commodity suppliers [#J]

  • Sub-bullet: Group-level procurement coordination is limited — decentralised model means cross-subsidiary purchasing synergies are largely unrealised; contrast with Indutrade, which has begun centralising purchasing in high-volume commodity categories [#J]

  • Segment-specific power — infrastructure/municipal: Subsidiaries serving municipal infrastructure customers (water, district heating) face concentrated public-sector buyers with procurement rules that limit price escalation — below-average pricing power in this segment [#J]

  • Segment-specific power — maintenance/MRO: Subsidiaries in maintenance, repair, and operations supply benefit from critical-path urgency pricing — availability is often more important than price for small-value but operationally critical components [#J]

  • Segment-specific power — niche OEM supply: OEM supply relationships involve annual pricing negotiations and multi-year contracts; pricing power exists at contract renewal (switching costs) but is exercised infrequently compared to spot/catalogue pricing [#A]

  • Regulatory barrier: In regulated product categories (fire suppression, pressure systems, ATEX-rated equipment), mandatory type-approvals and certification regimes raise switching costs structurally — a form of regulatory pricing leverage that is non-negotiable [#J]

  • Pricing-power transmission: Post-COVID pricing actions (2021-2023) demonstrated above-inflation price increases across Nordic niche industrials — Volati's reported revenue growth above volume growth confirms some pricing power was exercised [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: The test for durable pricing power is 2025-2026 — with input-cost inflation easing, will customers extract rollbacks? Peer evidence (Addtech, Indutrade) suggests partial normalisations are occurring in 2024-2025 [#J]

  • Dictated terms — distribution channel: Where Volati subsidiaries sell through dominant distributors (Ahlsell, Würth, Hagemeyer), the distributor captures negotiating leverage — shelf-access and working-capital terms may compress margins at the sub level [#J]

  • Sceptical note on power dynamics: The bearish read on pricing power: if Volati's subsidiaries rely on information asymmetry and customer inertia rather than genuine switching costs, pricing power is more fragile than it appears — through what mechanism might digital procurement transparency (e-auctions, specification databases) erode the pricing premium that underpins Stage 3-4 margins? [#J]

§6 — Competitive Map at Volati's Stage

  • Competitive intensity verdict: Volati's stage (niche industrial manufacturing) is characterised by deep fragmentation at the global level but near-oligopoly within individual niches — the relevant competitive set is always the niche, not the macro-stage [#J]

  • Stage fragmentation: At the global industrials manufacturing stage, there are tens of thousands of operators — fragmentation is extreme; but within any given Volati niche (e.g., heating cables, material handling fittings), the competitive set is typically 5-20 credible operators [#J]

  • Competitor — leader: In each Volati sub-market, one to three dominant niche players typically capture 30-60% of the relevant market — Volati's subsidiaries are usually the leader, second, or a strong regional player within their niche [#I]

  • Competitor — specialist: Family-owned niche manufacturers (German Mittelstand, Nordic engineering SMEs) are Volati's most common direct competitors at the Stage 3-4 level — similar product focus, similar scale, often less financially optimised [#J]

  • Competitor — challenger: Larger industrial groups with divisional strategies (ABB, Parker Hannifin, Rexnord) can deploy superior R&D and purchasing scale in adjacent niches — the risk is adjacency creep into Volati's sub-markets from above [#J]

  • Scale-cost dimension: Volati's subsidiaries are not scale-cost leaders in most niches — they compete on differentiation, certifications, and customer relationships rather than cost; scale advantage resides with commodity-adjacent operators at Stage 1-2 [#J]

  • Differentiation dimension: Proprietary product specifications, engineering heritage, and regulatory certifications are the primary differentiation weapons at Stage 3-4 — this favours incumbents with long product histories [#J]

  • Geographic dimension: Most Volati subsidiaries compete primarily in Scandinavian/Northern European geographies — domestic market leadership in a small geography can constitute a durable competitive position even without global scale [#J]

  • Sub-bullet: The geographic constraint is both moat and ceiling — Nordic leadership insulates from global commoditisation but limits growth runway without export expansion [#J]

  • Customer-segment dimension: B2B-only exposure across Volati's competitive set — no B2C risk; the relevant competitive battleground is specification-in and procurement relationships, not brand marketing [#A]

  • Stage concentration — trend: Consolidation is slowly increasing concentration at Stage 3-4 in most Volati niches — acquirers (Volati itself, Addtech, Lagercrantz, private equity) are rolling up family-owned operators, reducing the fragmentation that once made entry easy [#J]

  • New-entrant threat — low-cost manufacturing: Chinese and Southeast Asian manufacturers have entered standard industrial components at Stage 3 pricing, but have not yet credibly threatened certified/engineered niche products where qualification and service are required [#J]

  • Sceptical note on competitive map: The bearish read on competitive leadership: Volati reports subsidiary leadership positions anecdotally — independent verification of niche market-share claims is absent from public filings; through what mechanism might claimed leadership positions actually be regional/local rather than national, making them more fragile than presented? [#J]

§7 — Competitive Maps at Adjacent Stages + Vertical Integration Threats

  • Adjacent-stage verdict: Supplier-stage consolidation is moderate and manageable; the more credible structural threat is customer-stage forward integration or platform substitution, not supplier vertical integration into manufacturing [#J]

  • Supplier — leader (Stage 1-2): Key raw-material and component suppliers to Volati's subsidiaries include SSAB/Outokumpu (steel), Hydro (aluminium), BASF/Neste (polymers/chemicals), ABB/Schneider (electrical components) — all are large, diversified suppliers for whom any single Volati subsidiary is immaterial [#A]

  • Supplier consolidation: Steel and aluminium supply chains have consolidated significantly over 2000-2020 (SSAB acquisitions, Tata/Rautaruukki, etc.) — this increases supplier leverage over small buyers, though commodity pricing is market-driven rather than relationship-driven [#J]

  • Supplier vertical integration — threat: Large component suppliers (ABB, Siemens, Honeywell) have historically integrated forward into finished niche products in segments they consider strategically important — this is a credible but slow-moving threat in Volati's more technology-adjacent niches [#J]

  • Sub-bullet: The most credible supplier-integration vector is in electrification/automation-adjacent niches where large electronics/electrical suppliers have both the technology and distribution to absorb a niche manufacturer's position [#J]

  • Customer — leader (Stage 6): Volati's end customers are typically construction contractors, industrial plant operators, municipalities, agricultural operations, and maintenance contractors — highly fragmented, operationally-oriented buyers without sophisticated procurement functions [#A]

  • Customer consolidation: Nordic construction sector has been consolidating (NCC, Skanska, Peab gaining share), creating larger procurement entities — this modestly increases customer leverage in construction-adjacent sub-chains [#J]

  • Customer vertical integration — threat: Manufacturing customers (industrial plants, OEMs) with in-house engineering capability could potentially internalise simple component manufacturing — the risk is highest in lowest-IP, highest-volume sub-categories [#J]

  • Sub-bullet: The practical barrier to customer vertical integration is low volumes — most Volati subsidiaries supply too many variants in too small quantities for any single customer to justify in-house production [#J]

  • Adjacent threat — platform substitution: Digital B2B marketplace operators (Amazon Business, Würth Online, specialist procurement platforms) could disintermediate Volati's Stage 5 distribution-adjacent subsidiaries by aggregating product availability across competing suppliers [#J]

  • Adjacent threat — private equity platform: PE-backed industrial roll-ups (EQT-backed, Nordic Capital-backed industrial platforms) could consolidate Volati's direct competitors and then pursue Volati's niches — this is an acquisition risk more than an operational threat [#I]

  • Adjacent threat — OEM insourcing: In several Volati sub-markets, OEM customers that historically sourced sub-components externally are bringing production in-house as automation reduces the skill threshold — a structural demand headwind in the most commoditised sub-chains [#J]

  • Sceptical note on adjacent-stage threats: The bearish read on vertical integration: Volati's subsidiaries appear too small to attract deliberate supplier forward-integration, but through what mechanism might a large industrial player (ABB, Siemens, Atlas Copco) decide to acquire and absorb a Volati sub-market as part of a broader vertical strategy? The risk is not zero and historical Mittelstand acquisition by large strategics is well-documented [#J]

§8 — Structural Changes + Evolution

  • Evolution verdict: The dominant structural force shaping Volati's value chain is accelerating consolidation at Stages 3-4 combined with digital-procurement pressure at Stage 5 — these trends simultaneously create acquisition opportunity and margin-normalisation risk [#J]

  • Stage consolidation: Stage 3-4 niche manufacturing is consolidating faster than at any prior point in Nordic industrials history — driven by family-business succession vacuums, PE capital availability, and the Addtech/Lagercrantz/Indutrade/Volati model's demonstrated success [#J]

  • Sub-bullet: Price multiples for niche industrial acquisitions have compressed from 8-10× EBIT (2021-2022 peak) toward 5-7× EBIT (2024-2025) as capital costs rose — creates better acquisition economics for Volati but also signals increased competition for targets [#I]

  • Stage fragmentation — simultaneous: New specialist startups and spin-offs continue to fragment Stage 3-4 in digitalisation-adjacent niches (IoT sensors, industrial software, edge analytics) — the net result is consolidation of traditional niches alongside fragmentation of new digital-industrial niches [#J]

  • Vertical integration trend — downstream: Niche manufacturers are selectively integrating toward direct customer interfaces (online configurators, digital catalogues, subscription-based service contracts) — this reduces distributor dependency and expands EBIT margins at Stage 3-4 [#J]

  • Disintermediation: Traditional B2B distributors (Stage 5) are under structural pressure — industrial e-procurement, digital catalogues, and manufacturer direct-to-customer portals are eroding the distributor's information-advantage; this is net positive for Volati subsidiaries that own their own channel [#J]

  • Sub-bullet: Subsidiaries relying on third-party distributors for route-to-market are structurally exposed to disintermediation pressures — the distributor may be squeezed out but so is the manufacturer's indirect sales model [#J]

  • New entrant — technology disruption: Additive manufacturing (3D printing) represents a structural new-entrant risk in low-volume, high-mix component categories — as AM costs fall, OEM customers may self-supply complex small components currently sourced from niche manufacturers [#J]

  • New entrant — Asian competition: Chinese and Korean manufacturers have achieved Stage 3-4 quality levels in standard components (fasteners, fittings, standard hydraulics) while retaining 20-40% cost advantages — the risk to Volati's standard-product sub-chains is increasing [#J]

  • Business model innovation: Outcome-based and service-contract models (pay-per-output, guaranteed uptime) are emerging in more complex Volati sub-markets — subsidiaries that capture service revenue create stickier customer relationships and more recurring revenue profiles [#J]

  • Pricing power shift — downstream: Pricing power in industrials is gradually shifting downstream toward the customer-proximity stage as end-customers gain procurement sophistication and digital alternatives; Volati's downstream-selective integration strategy is directionally correct [#J]

  • Pricing power shift — upstream: In electrification-driven niches, raw-material suppliers (copper, lithium, rare earths) are accumulating structural pricing leverage — sub-chains dependent on these commodities face upstream squeeze [#J]

  • DTC trend: Direct-to-customer selling is growing across Nordic industrial B2B — Volati subsidiaries with direct field-sales forces are gaining vs. distributor-dependent competitors; the post-COVID acceleration of digital quoting/ordering reinforces this structural trend [#J]

  • ⚡ RARE: Succession-vacuum timing: Nordic industrial SME ownership is disproportionately held by 60-75-year-old founders with sub-optimal succession planning — this creates an unprecedentedly large pool of high-quality Stage 3-4 acquisition targets over 2024-2030, precisely when Volati's consolidation model is most mature [Cross-ref: §9]

  • Sceptical note on structural evolution: The bearish read on structural evolution: Volati's own consolidation model depends on continued fragmentation at Stage 3-4 — if larger strategics (ABB, Atlas Copco, Hexagon) enter the roll-up game with superior cost of capital and distribution synergies, the acquisition pipeline Volati relies on could be both depleted and priced out of reach [#J]

§9 — Sceptical Synthesis + Competitive Advantages + Strategic Implications

  • Positional synthesis verdict: Volati occupies a structurally well-chosen position — Stage 3-4 niche IP-manufacturing, the highest-margin node in most of its sub-chains — but the margin gap vs. closest peers (Addtech, Indutrade) suggests execution, not position, is the key variable [#J]

  • Structural advantage — portfolio diversification: 40+ parallel niche positions means no single sub-chain failure threatens the group — this is a structural resilience advantage unavailable to single-niche specialists [#J]

  • Structural advantage — consolidator positioning: Volati's holding-company model allows it to acquire niche operators at capital-efficient multiples while retaining the operational autonomy that makes these businesses attractive to founders — a structural recruitment advantage in the M&A market [#J]

  • Structural advantage — switching-cost stack: Across most sub-markets, Volati's position benefits from product certification, customer-approved-supplier status, and operator familiarity — a switching-cost stack that is sticky at the subsidiary level even without group-level brand recognition [#J]

  • Position-based moat: The moat is real but narrow — it operates at the individual subsidiary level, not at the group level; the Volati brand itself does not generate pricing power or customer stickiness [#J]

  • Strategic option — downstream: Selective downstream integration (own-digital-channel, service-contract bundling) is the highest-ROI strategic option available to subsidiaries — captures margin currently shared with distributors and deepens customer lock-in [#J]

  • Strategic option — upstream: Upstream integration is unattractive — raw-material ownership would add capital intensity without proportionate margin gain; selective component co-design partnerships are a better upstream move than ownership [#J]

  • Strategic option — adjacent: Adjacency expansion within existing customer relationships (cross-selling across subsidiaries, bundled product lines for common end-markets like HVAC or district heating) is under-exploited at group level relative to Addtech's cross-selling model [#J]

  • Reverse-engineered bear — margin compression: The bear case is subsidiary margin mean-reversion — if 5-8 subsidiaries with elevated post-COVID margins normalise simultaneously, group EBIT could contract 15-20% without any revenue decline; this is the single most credible bear pathway [#J]

  • Position fragility — IP depth: The proprietary-specification moat at each subsidiary is only as strong as the R&D and applications-engineering investment maintaining it — if subsidiaries under-invest in product development, the moat erodes quietly and rapidly [#J]

  • Single largest positional risk: Simultaneous niche-entry by well-capitalised industrial platforms (PE-backed roll-ups, Addtech/Lagercrantz adjacency creep) into Volati's specific niches, combined with cost-of-capital disadvantage at the acquisition stage, is the single most damaging structural scenario [#J]

  • Reverse-engineered bear — acquisition quality: Volati's EPS trajectory depends on continued value-accretive acquisitions; if the quality of available targets degrades (best businesses taken by better-capitalised competitors, remaining targets more cyclical or lower-margin), the capital-allocation engine stalls [#J]

  • Cross-cutting structural positive: Structural fragmentation at Stage 3-4 + demographic succession vacuum + Volati's proven model create a multi-year acquisition opportunity that is structurally superior to most European industrials compounder alternatives [#J]

Confidence in positional advantage durability: MEDIUM — Volati's stage choice and model are structurally sound, but the execution gap vs. best-in-class peers (Addtech, Indutrade) and limited public transparency on subsidiary-level positioning leave meaningful uncertainty about how durable the moat is at the individual niche level [#J]

AGGREGATE WEAK SIGNALS / DOWNSIDE

❌ Supplier scale leverage: Individual subsidiaries are sub-scale buyers vs. Stage 1-2 commodity suppliers — no purchasing leverage on steel, aluminium, or electronic components; group-level procurement coordination is unrealised. Sector median Nordic industrial compounder (Indutrade) has begun centralising purchasing by 2023-2024. [Cross-ref: §2 + §5]
