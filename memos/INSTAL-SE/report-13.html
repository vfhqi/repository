INSTALCO AB: KEY UNKNOWN DRIVERS OF SHARE PRICE (24-MONTH HORIZON)

Equity Research Memo | April 2026

Company: Instalco AB (INSTAL.ST, OMX Stockholm)
Current Price: ~SEK 38 (as of early April 2026)
Market Cap: ~SEK 15bn (est.)
Analyst: [C]
Date: 8 April 2026

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Instalco enters 2026 at an inflection point. The company delivered Q4 2025 results (released 12 Feb 2026) showing material margin improvement (EBITA margin 7.2%, up from 6.0% in Q3) and positive organic growth returning for the first time since autumn 2023. Management is executing Instalco 2.0, a disciplined operational excellence programme designed to lift EBITA margins to 8% while maintaining a decentralised acquisition model.

However, consensus pricing (SEK 27 target, Hold rating from SEB) suggests the market is sceptical about execution risk and remains uncertain on several macro and micro drivers that will materially determine returns over 24 months. This memo identifies six to eight high-impact, under-researched drivers whose realisation probability and direction of surprise will dominate share price performance. These sit beneath sell-side consensus and deserve deeper scrutiny.

KEY UNKNOWN DRIVERS — STACK RANK BY MATERIALITY

1. INSTALCO 2.0 EXECUTION RISK & MARGIN TRAJECTORY — 60–65% Probability of Success

What's at stake: The entire bull case rests on management's ability to lift EBITA margins from 6.9% (FY2025 average) to 8%+ sustainably, without sacrificing acquisition-driven revenue growth. This is the core unknown.

The Driver:

Instalco 2.0 is a top-down operational excellence programme launched in autumn 2025. It targets:
- Standardisation of costing/pricing methodologies across the decentralised portfolio
- New governance framework for underperforming companies (hands-on support + rigorous evaluation)
- Operational baseline-setting in electrical, HVAC, and industrial segments
- d cash conversion and working capital discipline

Q4 2025 delivered the first proof point: EBITA margin rose to 7.2% (vs. 6.0% in Q3), with CEO Per Sjöstrand emphasising "Margin, margin, margin" and CFO Christina Kassberg stating the company is "prioritizing margins over volume."

Probability Assessment — 60–65% Success Likelihood:

The positive signals are real but partial:
- Q4 2025 margin improvement is genuine and reflects operational discipline
- Positive organic growth returned (+0.5–1.0% est., Q4 2025), suggesting the market is stabilising
- Management tone is credible: decentralised model preserved, but standards imposed

However, material execution risk exists:

1. Margin-growth trade-off: The programme prioritises profitability over volume in a weak market. As demand recovers in 2026, will management resist the temptation to trade margin for top-line growth? Historical precedent (2021–2023) suggests Instalco has been acquisition-aggressive during recoveries. If the company pursues M&A growth before hitting 8% margins, the 2.0 programme falters.

2. Sustainability in cyclical downturns: Q4 2025 margin expansion occurred near the trough. As the market recovers and pricing normalises (less need to compete on cost), will margins hold or expand further? Conversely, if market conditions deteriorate again, will Instalco defend margins or revert to volume-chasing to maintain utilisation?

3. Underperformer turnaround success: The new framework for struggling portfolio companies is untested. Management has identified ~10% of the portfolio as structurally underperforming. Successfully turning these around without draconian measures (closure, consolidation) is non-trivial. If these assets continue to drag, margin expansion stalls at 7–7.2%.

4. Labour cost inflation: Swedish and Nordic wage inflation is accelerating (4.2% YoY as of August 2025, with further upward pressure expected in 2026). Installation services are labour-intensive. If labour costs rise faster than pricing power, margin gains evaporate.

Forward Guidance & Consensus:

- Analyst consensus EPS forecast: SEK 2.38 for next financial year (38.8% YoY growth)
- Consensus EBITA margin implied by EPS forecast: ~7.3–7.5% (vs. management's 8% target)
- SEB target: SEK 27 (only sell rating; implies downside from ~SEK 38)
- HOLD consensus suggests lukewarm conviction on execution

Changes in Guidance Over Last 12 Months:

Guidance has narrowed — management shifted from growth/profitability balance to profitability-first. This is a material reframing. However, consensus EPS forecasts have remained relatively stable, suggesting sell-side has not fully priced in either upside (if 8% margins achieved) or downside (if margin expansion stalls).

Sell-Side Sceptical Case:

A short seller would argue: "Instalco 2.0 is a rebranding of poor execution, not a structural fix. The company operates 800+ decentralised entities with limited central control. Imposing costing standards and rigorous evaluation on this structure will trigger management turnover, integration costs, and resistance. Furthermore, the trough-like market conditions of Q4 2025 are artificially suppressing pricing/volume trade-offs. Once the market recovers, Instalco will snap back to growth-mode acquisitions and margin expansion will reverse."

Market Context & Macro Headwinds:

  1. Labour scarcity: Nordic construction is facing acute skilled labour shortages. Instalco's labour-intensive business model is vulnerable. Wage inflation of 4–5% annually could erode margin gains unless pricing can keep pace.

2. Swedish market weakness: The Swedish market remains challenged. While Norway and Finland showed strength in late 2025, Sweden — Instalco's largest market — continues to face demand weakness. Recovery timing is uncertain.

3. Competitive pressure: Assemblin-Caverion merger (completed Apr 2024) created a ~EUR 3.8bn combined entity with ~30,000 employees across 10 countries. This larger competitor is better positioned for multinational contracts and can absorb margin pressure. Instalco's decentralised model may struggle to compete on scale-driven synergies and automation.

Analyst Granularity on Margin:

- ABG Sundal Collier (not seen specific 2026 forecast, but historical tone supportive of operational narrative)
- SEB: Most sceptical, Sell rating, SEK 27 target (implies 7–7.2% EBITA margins at best)
- Nordea/Danske Bank/DNB (limited recent data from search, but consensus Hold suggests neutral stance)

Plausibility of Beating/Missing Consensus:

- Upside case: If margins reach 7.5–8.0% by H2 2026, driven by pricing power + operational leverage, EPS could surprise 15–20% to upside (consensus implies 7.3% margins). Bull target: SEK 42–45.
- Downside case: If 2.0 programme falters and margins remain at 6.5–7.0%, or if labour cost inflation outpaces pricing, EPS misses by 10–15%. Bear target: SEK 20–22.

2. FABRI GERMANY PLATFORM ACQUISITION & MAJORITY STAKE PATH — 45–55% Probability of Achieving Step 2

What's at stake: Instalco's long-term value proposition hinges on geographic diversification beyond the Nordic market (saturated, <3% of target addressable market). Fabri is the bet. Failure to expand Fabri materially, or overpayment at majority-stake step-ups, could destroy value.

The Driver:

In H2 2024, Instalco acquired a 24% minority stake in Fabri AG, a German installation services company with ~EUR 70m in annual revenue. The deal structure is phased:

- Step 1 (completed H2 2024): 24% stake acquired
- Step 2 (expected Q1 2026 – Q2 2027): Further 27% acquisition, conditional on agreed earnings thresholds
- Step 3 (conditional on continued performance): Remaining 17%, reaching 68% majority stake

Strategic Rationale:

Germany's installation services market is ~5x larger than the Nordic market. Properties are ageing, energy efficiency renovation mandates (EU EPBD, effective 2025–2030) will drive demand. Fabri is positioned to capitalise. Instalco gains a platform for rapid bolt-on M&A in Germany under local management.

Current Status (as of Apr 2026):

- Fabri now comprises 20 companies (up from 17 at H2 2025)
- Recent acquisitions added ~EUR 14–19m in net sales
- Management flagged "continued momentum" in Germany
- Step 2 conditional thresholds not publicly disclosed; triggering timeline remains Q1 2026–Q2 2027

Probability Assessment — 45–55% Probability of Smooth Step 2 Execution:

Bull case (55% probability):

  1. German market fundamentals are strong. The EPBD renovation targets create a structural tailwind. Residential buildings must meet Energy Class E by 2030, Class D by 2033. This is a legislated multi-billion-euro renovation boom.

  2. Fabri has local brand equity and relationships. Bundling this with Instalco's M&A playbook (decentralised, capital-light roll-ups) is strategically sound.

  3. Acquisition add-ons in Germany are available and reasonably priced. Instalco has announced 3+ acquisitions already, suggesting deal flow is healthy.

  4. Fabri's profitability is improving. If EBIT margins are tracking toward Instalco's target, Step 2 thresholds are likely to trigger.

Bear case (45% probability — execution and valuation risks):

  1. Valuation creep: Instalco has a history of paying up for acquisitions during bull markets. If Fabri's valuation multiples have risen since the initial minority stake, Step 2 and Step 3 prices could be eye-watering. At what point does the German platform no longer make financial sense?

  2. Integration execution: Fabri comprises 20 fragmented entities. Imposing Instalco 2.0 discipline on a foreign-language, culturally distinct market is harder than in the Nordic region. Management turnover risk is elevated.

3. Macro risk in Germany: German economy is slowing (GDP growth forecast 0.7–1.0% in 2026 vs. historical 2–3%). If recession hits, residential renovation budgets shrink and valuations collapse. Instalco could overpay at Step 2 for an asset that deteriorates.

4. Hidden liabilities: German labour law, environmental regulations, and accounting practices differ from Nordic norms. Due diligence risk is non-trivial.

  1. Capital deployment trade-off: Every euro spent on Fabri majority ownership is a euro not spent on Nordic acquisitions or shareholder returns. Is the IRR on German expansion higher than returning capital via dividends or share buybacks? Unclear from public commentary.

Forward Guidance & Analyst Expectations:

- Instalco has not guided on Step 2 probability or timeline beyond "Q1 2026 – Q2 2027, subject to thresholds"
- Consensus does not explicitly model Fabri earnings or assume Step 2 completion
- Fabri contribution to group EBITA estimated at 50–80 basis points by 2027 (rough calculation: EUR 70m sales * 7% EBITA margin = EUR 5m = ~1–2% of group EBITA)

Changes in Guidance/Expectations:

Management tone on Fabri has remained consistently positive. However, there has been no upward revision of earnings expectations tied to Fabri expansion. This suggests sell-side is conservatively assuming Fabri stays a minority holding or underweighting synergy assumptions.

Sell-Side Sceptical Case:

A short seller would argue: "Fabri is a value trap. Instalco paid a rich valuation for a minority stake in an untested market. Step 2 and Step 3 will be even richer, trapping capital in a low-ROIC asset. German labour costs are rising faster than Nordic equivalents. Regulation is creating a pipeline of 'low-margin, high-volume' renovation work, not the high-quality, recurring-revenue business Instalco targets. The minority ownership structure also suggests the founders/management want to retain control, implying governance friction and limited operational leverage for Instalco."

Market Context & Macro Trends:

  1. EU Energy Performance Buildings Directive : Transposition deadline 29 May 2026. Starting 2030, residential buildings must meet Class E. This is a genuine structural tailwind. However, the mandate also creates competitive intensity — contractors will commoditise pricing to capture volume.

  2. German labour costs: Construction wages in Germany are rising 4–5% annually and are already 20–30% higher than in the Nordic region on a PPP basis. Wage inflation will be a persistent headwind for margins.

3. Residential property cycles: German residential property market is entering a slowdown. New build is down 15–20% YoY (as of early 2026) due to mortgage rate sensitivity. Renovation demand is more resilient, but replacement cycles are longer than new build. Growth expectations should be conservative.

  1. Competitive landscape: German installation services market is highly fragmented (unlike Scandinavia). Consolidators (Instalco, Bravida, Assemblin-Caverion) are all competing for bolt-on acquisition targets. Prices are rising.

Plausibility of Meeting/Missing Expectations:

- Upside: If Step 2 triggers and Fabri grows 20–25% organically + 10–15% M&A, and margins expand to 7–7.5%, Fabri could contribute EUR 100–120m in sales and EUR 8–10m in EBITA by 2027. This would surprise consensus upside materially (perhaps 5–10% to group EPS).

- Downside: If Step 2 does not trigger, or if macro deterioration forces Fabri valuation resets, the asset becomes a drag. Minority stake write-downs are possible if profitability disappoints. Group EPS impact: -5–10%.

3. NORDIC MARKET RECOVERY TIMING & ORGANIC GROWTH SUSTAINABILITY — 50–60% Probability of Achieving +3–5% Organic Growth in 2026–2027

What's at stake: Instalco's entire acquisition-driven growth model is premised on a recovering Nordic market that allows portfolio companies to grow organically while Instalco layers on bolt-on M&A. If organic growth stalls, acquisition-driven growth alone is insufficient to deliver 10%+ revenue CAGR.

The Driver:

Instalco reported positive organic growth for the first time since autumn 2023 in Q4 2025 (~+0.5–1.0% est., driven by industrial segment). Management expects "market stabilization in 2026" with recovery in Q2 or later. The nordic market contracted sharply in 2024 (–11% per industry sources) and was flat-to-slightly negative in early 2025. Recovery would be a material inflection.

Current Status:

- Order backlog: SEK 9.5bn, stable book-to-bill at ~70%; backlog grew 5.6% (7.5% organic)
- Regional variation: Norway and Finland strong; Sweden weak and lacks visibility
- Segment variation: Industrial segment performing (positive organic growth), Residential segment softer
- Organic growth: Turned slightly positive Q4 2025 after 5+ quarters of decline

Probability Assessment — 50–60% Probability of Sustainable +3–5% Organic Growth:

Bull case (60% probability):

1. Structural recovery drivers: Monetary easing (ECB rate cuts, potential rate cuts by Sweden's Riksbank in H2 2026) is improving borrowing conditions. Mortgage rates are declining from 2024 peaks. This should unlock residential renovation budgets.

  1. Labour market stabilisation: Swedish unemployment has plateaued. Real wage growth, though modest, is positive. Consumer confidence is improving from late 2024 lows.

3. Order backlog conversion: SEK 9.5bn of backlog at 70% book-to-bill suggests strong forward visibility. If converted, this supports 2–3% organic growth in 2026.

  1. Infrastructure investment: Nordic governments (Sweden, Norway) are committing to infrastructure and public building projects (schools, hospitals, energy infrastructure). Instalco's industrial and public-sector exposure should benefit.

5. Industrial segment strength: Industrial, electrical, and HVAC segments are less cyclical than residential new build. Continued strength here (even if residential remains tepid) could sustain organic growth.

Bear case (40% probability — macro and competitive headwinds):

  1. Swedish market remains depressed: Sweden accounts for 50–55% of Instalco's Nordic revenue. Swedish residential market has not recovered as sharply as Norway/Finland. If Swedish market remains flat-to-slightly-negative, group organic growth is capped at +1–2%.

2. Competition and price pressure: Market recovery creates bidding intensity. Assemblin-Caverion (larger, pan-European) and Bravida (dominant in Sweden, 15% market share) will compete aggressively. Instalco's smaller scale and fragmented structure may result in margin-eroding price wars to win market share.

3. Labour cost inflation outpaces pricing: Wage inflation of 4–5% annually is outpacing pricing power in weak markets. Even if demand recovers, margin compression could offset top-line growth benefit.

4. Customer consolidation: End-customers (property managers, developers) are consolidating and exerting pricing pressure on installation contractors. Larger customers demand volume discounts and extended payment terms.

5. Uneven regional recovery: Not all Nordic regions are recovering in sync. If only Norway/Finland recover while Sweden remains weak, group organic growth is masked by geographical volatility.

Forward Guidance & Consensus:

- Management guidance on 2026 organic growth: Implicit +2–3%, stated as "cautious confidence"
- Consensus analyst forecasts: Revenue growth of ~5.5% annually (per PitchBook), but this includes acquisition contributions; organic growth implied at +1–2%
- Strategic target: ≥10% average sales growth (mix of organic + M&A)

Changes in Guidance/Expectations:

Management's tone has shifted from "market stabilization expected in H2 2025" (Q2 2025 guidance) to "market stabilization expected in 2026, possibly Q2 or later" (Q4 2025 guidance). This is a one-quarter delay. If market recovery delays further, organic growth misses could persist.

Sell-Side Sceptical Case:

A short seller would argue: "The Nordic market is structurally impaired. High mortgage rates have reset residential demand lower; many owners are deferring renovations indefinitely. Commercial/industrial demand is also stalling due to energy cost concerns and investment cutbacks. Order backlog is lagging; new orders are declining. Instalco's order backlog of SEK 9.5bn is down-left seasonally volatile and does not imply sustainable growth. The company is kidding itself that 'organic growth' is returning — what it's seeing is a rebound from the trough, not a sustainable recovery."

Market Context & Macro Trends:

1. Nordic GDP growth: Forecast 0.7–1.5% in 2026 (post-2024 contraction). This is anaemic. Construction spending typically lags GDP growth, implying 0–1% construction growth even if GDP recovers.

  1. Energy cost shock aftermath: Energy-intensive industries (manufacturing, data centres, heating) are still absorbing the 2022–2024 energy crisis. Capital investment in energy efficiency and renovation is occurring, but unevenly.

3. Residential property market: Swedish residential prices have declined 10–15% from 2024 peaks. This dampens both new-build and renovation demand (lower equity for homeowner-initiated upgrades).

4. Public sector spending: Historically, Instalco's industrial and public-sector work is more resilient in downturns. If public budgets are constrained, this tailwind is muted.

5. Demographic shifts: Ageing Nordic population supports residential renovation (downsizing, accessibility upgrades). However, this is a slow structural trend, not a cyclical recovery driver.

Segment-Level Insights:

- Industrial: Positive organic growth already evident (Q4 2025). Expected to sustain if energy transition investments continue.
- Residential: Weakest segment. No clear recovery visible. Highly dependent on interest rate environment and household sentiment.
- Commercial/Public: Mixed. Some public infrastructure projects are underway; commercial real estate is softening.

Plausibility of Meeting/Missing Expectations:

- Upside: If Nordic market recovers strongly (stimulus, rate cuts, confidence snap-back) and Instalco wins market share through competitive intensity, organic growth could reach +3–5% by H2 2026. This would support EPS upside of 10–15% vs. consensus.

- Downside: If recovery stalls and organic growth reverts to –1 to 0%, and Instalco sacrifices margins to defend volume, EPS misses by 15–20% vs. consensus. Bear case: SEK 20–22.

4. PRICING POWER vs. LABOUR COST INFLATION — 55% Probability of Maintaining 100 bps+ Margin Advantage

What's at stake: Labour cost inflation is a binary issue for Instalco. Installation services are 60–70% labour costs. If wage inflation (4–5% annually) outpaces pricing power (2–3%), margins compress 50–100 bps annually. Over 24 months, this is material (100–200 bps of EBITA margin loss).

The Driver:

Nordic construction wages are rising at 4–5% YoY (August 2025 data). This is above inflation and well above productivity growth. Instalco's pricing power is constrained by competition and customer pressure. Management's Instalco 2.0 programme attempts to solve this via "costing standardisation and pricing discipline," but this is soft language for "we need to raise prices."

Current Status:

- Q4 2025 EBITA margin: 7.2% (up from 6.0% in Q3)
- Estimated labour cost inflation: 4–5% annually
- Estimated pricing power: 2–3% annually (conservative, given market weakness)
- Implied margin compression if unaddressed: 100–200 bps annually

Probability Assessment — 55% Probability of Maintaining Pricing Discipline:

Bull case (55% probability):

1. Improving market conditions lift pricing: As demand recovers, contractors have more pricing power. Instalco's decentralised model allows individual portfolio companies to adjust pricing dynamically.

2. Operational efficiency gains offset labour costs: Instalco 2.0 targets higher utilisation, faster project cycles, and reduced rework. If successful, labour productivity improves and labour cost inflation is partially offset.

3. Customer mix shift: Instalco is targeting larger, more stable customers (property managers, facility managers, governments). These customers are less price-sensitive than fragmented residential customers.

  1. mation potential: Some installation services (plumbing, electrical, HVAC) can be partially automated. Instalco has invested in prefabrication and modular solutions (e.g., Inmatiq, its building automation start-up). Scale-up could reduce labour intensity.

5. Strategic acquisition focus: If Instalco acquires high-margin, recurring-revenue businesses (facility management, maintenance contracts), this dilutes labour-intensive new-install work and raises blended margins.

Bear case (45% probability — labour cost headwinds persist):

1. Wage inflation is structural, not cyclical: Skilled labour shortages in Nordic construction are real and worsening. Demographic headwinds (ageing workforce, fewer young entrants) support sustained wage growth. Even with improving demand, wage inflation is likely to persist at 3–5% for 5+ years.

2. Pricing power is limited: Consolidation of Instalco's customers (property managers, real estate firms) is increasing. Larger customers have leverage to demand volume discounts. Pricing power is +2–3% at best, not enough to offset 4–5% wage inflation.

  1. mation is slow and capital-intensive: Instalco is not a manufacturing-scale technology company. mation of installation services is incremental, not transformative. Capex requirements could depress free cash flow and dividend capacity.

4. Competitive intensity: Assemblin-Caverion and Bravida will also be fighting labour cost pressures. If the larger competitors achieve economies of scale faster, Instalco's pricing power erodes further.

5. Residential segment vulnerability: Residential work (lower-margin, labour-intensive) is Instalco's largest segment. If labour cost inflation is acute here, and pricing power is weakest, margins compress materially.

Granular Analysis — Labour Costs by Region:

- Sweden: Average electrician wage ~SEK 620–680/hour (2025), rising 4–5% annually.
- Norway: Higher cost base (wage inflation 3–4% annually); Instalco's Norwegian operations (10–15% of group) are profitable but margin-constrained.
- Finland: Intermediate cost base; Finnish market showing better organic growth, implying better pricing/demand conditions.

Sell-Side Sceptical Case:

A short seller would argue: "Instalco 2.0 is a marketing façade for an insoluble problem: labour cost inflation will persist, pricing power is limited, and automation is a decade away. The company's FY2025 EBITA margin of 6.9% is unsustainable if labour costs inflate 4–5% annually. Management's 8% margin target is a fantasy. Over the next 24 months, margins will compress toward 6.0–6.5%, forcing either acquisition pauses (killing growth) or dividend cuts (killing valuation multiples)."

Market Context & Macro Trends:

  1. Nordic labour scarcity: Skilled installation labour is in acute supply shortage across Scandinavia. Apprenticeship rates are down 30–40% from 2015 peaks. This is a structural, long-term shortage.

2. Wage growth expectations: Trade unions are targeting 3–4% annual wage growth in 2026–2027 negotiations. This is above Nordic central bank inflation targets (1.5–2.0%), implying real wage growth and structural margin compression.

  1. Immigration policy: Stricter immigration policies in Sweden and Denmark are constraining labour supply. Foreign workers (who previously accepted lower wage growth) are increasingly scarce.

4. Energy cost benefits: Some labour cost pressures may be offset by lower energy costs (if energy inflation moderates in 2026–2027). However, energy is a smaller cost component than labour.

Plausibility of Meeting/Missing Expectations:

- Upside: If labour cost inflation moderates to 2–3% in 2026 (due to macro slowdown and easing labour market), and pricing power improves to 3–4%, margin expansion to 7.5–8.0% is achievable. EPS upside: +10–15%.

- Downside: If labour cost inflation accelerates to 5–6% (tight labour market in recovery scenario) and pricing power remains at 2%, margins compress to 6.0–6.5%. EPS downside: –15–20%.

5. CAPITAL ALLOCATION & SHAREHOLDER RETURNS POLICY — 50% Probability of Maintaining 30% Dividend Payout & Avoiding Capex-Driven Constraints

What's at stake: Instalco's valuation multiples are sensitive to capital allocation. If management deploys excess cash into low-ROIC Fabri expansion or Capex automation initiatives, dividend growth stalls and valuation multiples compress (P/E multiple expansion is limited if FCF yield is constant).

The Driver:

Instalco has articulated capital allocation targets:
- ≥10% average sales growth (organic + M&A)
- 8% EBITA margin
- 100% cash conversion
- 30% dividend payout ratio

FY2025 payout ratio was 38.92% (above target), reflecting weak earnings and management's commitment to dividend stability. Current dividend yield is 1.93%.

Current Status:

- Dividend per share: SEK 0.68 (FY2025)
- Payout ratio: 38.92% (above 30% target)
- Cash generation: SEK 1,010m from operations (FY2025), solid
- Net debt: Stable (estimated SEK 3–4bn, ~25–30% LTV)

Probability Assessment — 50% Probability of Achieving 30% Payout Target while Funding Growth:

Bull case (50% probability):

1. EBITDA growth outpaces dividend growth: If margins expand to 7.5–8.0% while revenue grows 5%+, EBITDA could grow 8–12% annually. At constant payout ratios, dividend per share would grow 6–10%, providing attractive dividend growth and yield expansion.

  1. Acquisition funding via equity: Instalco has historically used share issuance for major M&A (e.g., Fabri minority stake involved 4.6m new shares). This preserves leverage capacity and allows dividend maintenance.

  2. Capex requirements are modest: Installation services do not require massive capex (unlike manufacturing/infrastructure). Capex/revenue is typically 2–3%, leaving ample free cash for dividends.

  3. Asset-light model: Decentralised acquisition strategy is capital-light. No major capex for integration/consolidation (unlike scale-driven models), freeing cash for shareholder returns.

Bear case (50% probability — capex and M&A trade-offs):

1. Fabri expansion is capex-intensive: If Instalco pursues Step 2 and Step 3 acquisitions in Germany, and then invests in integration/automation infrastructure, capex could spike to 3–5% of revenue. This would crowd out dividend growth.

2. mation investments: Building automation (Inmatiq) and prefabrication platforms require significant capex. If Instalco is serious about automation-driven margin expansion, capex will increase.

3. Payout ratio trade-off: If earnings growth is modest (3–5% organically due to weak Nordic market), and capex needs rise, management will face payout ratio choice: cut dividends (shareholder backlash) or reduce M&A (growth miss).

4. Debt capacity constraints: If Instalco takes on debt to fund Fabri majority stake acquisition, leverage rises (est. 35–40% LTV), limiting dividend capacity and reducing financial flexibility.

5. Acquisition valuation discipline: If Instalco pursues 10% sales growth targets aggressively, it may overpay for acquisitions (as it has historically). This would reduce ROIC and depress free cash flow, forcing payout ratio cuts.

Forward Guidance & Consensus:

- Management target: 30% payout ratio
- Current situation: FY2025 payout was 38.92% (above target due to earnings weakness)
- Consensus expectation: Payout ratio normalises to 30–35% by 2027 as earnings recover
- Analyst dividend forecasts: Limited granularity in search results; SEB (Sell rating) implies downside risk to dividends

Sell-Side Sceptical Case:

A short seller would argue: "Instalco's dividend target of 30% payout is unrealistic given capex needs and M&A ambitions. The current 38.92% payout (FY2025) is the new normal. If Fabri acquisitions proceed, and operational capex rises for automation, payout ratios will rise to 40–50%, compressing shareholder returns and multiple expansion potential. Furthermore, if earnings growth disappoints (due to margin/organic growth misses), management may cut the dividend to maintain investment-grade debt metrics. This would be a major re-rating event."

Market Context & Investor Expectations:

1. Dividend growth expectations: Nordic industrial companies typically offer 3–5% annual dividend growth. Instalco's target (nominal, not growth) does not provide much upside. If growth misses, dividend yields compress and valuation multiples fall.

2. Capital allocation disclosure: Instalco does not provide detailed capex guidance or M&A budget guidance. This opacity creates uncertainty for dividend investors.

  1. Share buyback question: Instalco has not committed to share buybacks. If leverage rises (due to Fabri acquisition), buyback capacity is constrained. This is a headwind for EPS-per-share growth.

  2. Peer comparison: Bravida and Caverion (pre-merger) offered higher dividend yields (2–3%) and more aggressive buyback programmes. Instalco's shareholder return profile is conservative, which may weigh on valuation multiples.

Plausibility of Meeting/Missing Expectations:

- Upside: If earnings grow 10–15% annually and capex remains 2–3% of revenue, payout ratio normalises to 30% and dividend per share grows 8–12% annually. This supports P/E multiple expansion from 15–17x to 18–20x. Bull target: SEK 45–50.

- Downside: If earnings growth stalls (2–3%) and capex requirements spike to 4–5%, management cuts payout ratio to 25% (or dividend per share stalls). This depresses dividend yield and multiple expansion, supporting only SEK 30–35 by end-2027.

6. COMPETITIVE POSITIONING vs. ASSEMBLIN-CAVERION & BRAVIDA — 55% Probability of Maintaining/Gaining Market Share

What's at stake: Instalco operates in a consolidating industry with larger, scale-driven competitors (Assemblin-Caverion, Bravida) now dominant. If Instalco's decentralised, acquisition-light model is outcompeted by scale benefits (cross-selling, automation, customer leverage), the company's growth trajectory falters.

The Driver:

Competitive landscape has shifted materially since 2024:

  1. Assemblin-Caverion merger (Apr 2024): Created ~EUR 3.8bn (SEK 43.5bn) combined entity across 10 countries, 30,000 employees. Positioned as pan-European technical services platform with scale advantages in electrical, HVAC, building automation.

  2. Bravida dominance in Sweden: ~15% market share in Sweden (Instalco's largest market), substantial presence in Norway/Denmark.

  3. Instalco scale: <3% of ~SEK 170bn Nordic market; growing via M&A but still a mid-sized, decentralised player.

Current Status:

- Instalco revenue: SEK 13.6bn (FY2025); 41 acquired companies to date
- Assemblin-Caverion pro forma revenue: ~EUR 5.5bn (~SEK 63bn); 500+ companies in portfolio
- Bravida revenue: ~SEK 13bn; dominant position in Sweden
