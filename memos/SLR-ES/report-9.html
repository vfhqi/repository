  • Cash conversion: OCF / EBITDA has been approximately 75–85% L3Y — reasonable for an IPP with high depreciation but manageable working capital [#A]

  • Sub: Base-rate for European renewable IPPs: OCF/EBITDA typically 70–90%; SLR-ES sits in the middle of that range, not concerning

  • ❌ FCF conversion: FCF (OCF minus growth CapEx) has been materially negative L3Y — SLR-ES is investing €200–300m+ per year in new capacity; FCF is not a usable quality metric at this stage [#A]

  • Sub: This is expected for a growth-phase developer but means the "earnings quality" case rests entirely on EBITDA not free cash flow

  • NWC tailwind: Working capital changes are modest and broadly neutral — SLR-ES is a B2B energy seller with limited trade receivables complexity [#A]

  • Aggressive accounting signal: Capitalisation of development costs is a watch point — common in Spanish renewable developers but inconsistently disclosed; if projects are abandoned post-capitalisation, impairments follow [#J]

  • Conservative accounting signal: SLR-ES has not taken material impairments L3Y, suggesting project portfolio quality has held; but this may also reflect early-stage write-off absence rather than confirmed quality [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: Bearish read on earnings quality: the "recurring one-off" disposal gain is structurally baked in as a business model feature, but as the development pipeline matures and available sell-downs shrink, this contribution will fade, compressing reported EPS below the EBITDA recovery line [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: Bearish read on margin sustainability: if merchant power prices in Spain decline further (oversupply from rapid PV build-out, cannibalisation effects during peak solar hours), revenue per MWh falls without a corresponding cost reduction — margin compression at the EBITDA level becomes possible for the first time [#J]

  • Weakest half: H1 consistently weakest, but not dramatically so; the H1/H2 split is relatively balanced compared to, for example, hydropower or wind assets with more pronounced seasonal variance [#A]

  • Seasonality direction: Seasonality appears stable and slightly moderating as the portfolio diversifies geographically within Iberia (east-west spread reduces seasonal concentration) [#J]

  • Cause — weather: Primary driver is solar irradiation calendar — Spain's peak sun hours H2-weighted; secondary driver is H2-weighted commissioning cadence in the growth plan [#A]

  • Seasonality changing: No material shift in seasonal pattern L3Y — the H2 premium is structural and unlikely to change absent significant geographic diversification outside Iberia [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: Bearish read on seasonal patterns: if commissioning slips from H2 into the following H1, apparent H2 outperformance can mislead full-year expectations; also, H2 spot power prices are not always higher than H1 in Spain (winter demand peak can offset solar seasonality in some years), creating price vs volume offsetting effects [#J]

§8 — Forward Plausibility

J-front verdict: L3Y track record partially supports the forward thesis on capacity growth but does NOT support a clean 25%+ IRR path without power price assumptions above current forward curves [#J]

  • Track-record support: The capacity addition capability is demonstrated — SLR-ES commissioned ~1.4 GW net over FY2022–FY2024 (+78% installed base), confirming project execution and financing capability [#A]

  • Forward EPS implied: Consensus FY2025E EPS approximately €0.45–0.50 (estimated from broker notes), implying +15–28% growth vs FY2024 ~€0.39; achievable if ~400 MW commissioned and power prices stable at €80–90/MWh [#A]

  • Required CAGR: A 25%+ IRR from current valuation (~€10–12 per share) requires approximately 20–25% EPS CAGR over 3 years; L3Y delivered EPS CAGR was -19% (peak-distorted) — this creates a significant forward-vs-historical tension [#J]

  • Achievable from L3Y: The MW-growth-driven EBITDA path is plausible; the EPS accretion is more fragile because it requires simultaneously: stable/higher power prices, on-time commissioning, declining interest costs (leverage is high), and reducing disposal gain dependency [#A]

  • Acceleration required: Forward consensus implies acceleration in MW commissioning pace — ~500 MW per year vs ~350–400 MW in L3Y average; this has not been demonstrated in L3Y execution [#A]

  • Sub: [AS-Berenberg] November 2024 note explicitly flagged this acceleration assumption as the single biggest execution risk in their bull case

  • Margin runway: EBITDA margin runway is limited — already 69–73%; incremental margin expansion from here requires either higher power prices (exogenous) or lower financing costs (manageable via refinancing cycle) [#J]

  • Quality runway: OCF quality (75–85% OCF/EBITDA) can sustain as the asset base matures; but FCF generation (positive FCF) requires commissioning spending to slow post target-capacity achievement [#A]

  • Inflection plausibility: The FY2025 inflection (positive FCF for first time + EPS recovery) is plausible under base-case MW delivery and €80–85/MWh power prices; it is fragile under miss scenarios [#J]

  • Power price sensitivity: A 10% change in Spanish power prices has approximately 8–9% impact on EBITDA (high operating leverage to revenue); forward curves matter more than operational execution for the bull/bear EPS range [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: Bearish read on forward plausibility: the forward thesis requires an acceleration in MW delivery that has not been demonstrated, at power prices that are not guaranteed, to produce an EPS path that also requires declining disposal gain dependency; the combination of three simultaneous "all goes right" assumptions is the core fragility [#J]

§9 — Sceptical Synthesis
