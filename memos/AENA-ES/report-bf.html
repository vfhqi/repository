EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (BLUF)

Core business model and structural positioning

Aena operates the world's largest airport network by passenger volume, managing 46 airports and two heliports in Spain under a highly profitable dual-till regulatory model where stable regulated aeronautical assets anchor high-margin unregulated commercial growth, with the dual-till structure providing highly predictable cash flows from aviation that subsidize and de-risk high-margin commercial investments. The Spanish network generates 88% of consolidated group revenues historically, operating across four segments including the core Spain Airport segment, Real Estate, SCAIRM, and International. The commercial segment leases terminal retail spaces to third-party operators using a hybrid model of fixed minimum rents and variable royalties , with contracts featuring Minimum Annual Guaranteed rents creating a solid revenue floor that insulates Aena against traffic downturns. Aena maintains best-in-class unit costs per passenger at about half of peer operators , with high profitability protected by outsourcing most ground operations and creating significant positive operating leverage as traffic expands. The group achieved record traffic of 384.8 million passengers in 2025 , representing a 4.2% increase compared to 2024, with net profit increasing 10.5% year-on-year to EUR 2,136.7 million driven by strong commercial and international asset performance.

Value chain position and margin accrual dynamics

Aena sits in a uniquely privileged position within the airport value chain, acting as a natural monopoly infrastructure provider that captures superior returns, with upstream ground operations completely outsourced to insulate Aena from labor volatility and asset-heavy capital expenditures, while downstream commercial partnerships operate under highly favorable hybrid lease terms including high variable royalties and strong MAG protection. Aena leases terminal floor space to downstream global retailers employing a hybrid model of variable royalties and MAGs , with the Minimum Annual Guaranteed rents protecting commercial revenues from sudden passenger volume shocks. Global travel concessionaires operate on relatively thin margins as airport landlords capture the bulk of travel retail economics, with concession rents paid by retailers historically doubling from 17% of sales in 2005 to 30% by 2019 . Car parks are operated directly in-house retaining 100% of the cash flow without paying concession royalties , with directly managed car parks generating exceptional EBITDA margins exceeding 80% due to low incremental operating expenditure. Quantitative value-chain mapping confirms that pure infrastructure and direct commercial services accrue the highest operating margins, while travel retail operators carry high gross margins of 65% to 70% but airport concession rents compress their final EBIT margins to single digits.

Major structural risks and regulatory headwinds

The aeronautical business relies on the DORA framework which is facing regulatory headwinds from the CNMC's proposed tariff cuts, with Spain's competition regulator CNMC proposing a pre-tax WACC of 7.44% and an annual tariff decline of -0.59% compared to Aena's proposal of 9.0% WACC and +3.82% annual increases. Spanish airport operating expenditure increased 20.1% year-on-year in Q1 2026 driven by a 12.5% increase in wages, with rising staff costs and intense maintenance schedules threatening to dilute the group's best-in-class EBITDA margins. Major airlines consistently engage in administrative and legal litigation to block tariff increases, with IATA, Ryanair, and ALA unsuccessfully appealing previous tariff increases before the Spanish High Court, while the CNMC proposed declining tariffs of -0.59% per annum under DORA III rejecting Aena's +3.82% proposal, with Ryanair actively lobbying the government to enforce the CNMC's proposed 3.00% cut in airport charges . Aena is entering an unprecedented construction cycle planning EUR 13.00 billion in capex over DORA III , with executing massive airport expansions under active traffic conditions creating severe operational and flowdisruption risks, while escalating material costs and labor shortages threaten subcontractor solvency and timeline execution.
