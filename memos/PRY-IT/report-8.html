Prysmian SpA: Company Overview & Business Model
Business Segments and Product Portfolio

Prysmian SpA operates as a global leader in the energy transition and digital transformation, having evolved its business model from a traditional cable manufacturer into an integrated, end-to-end solutions provider. The company’s operations are structured around four primary operating segments, each designed to capture specific macroeconomic tailwinds such as grid modernization, electrification, and broadband expansion.

Transmission: This high-tech, high-value segment focuses on the transmission of renewable energy through submarine power systems and land High Voltage Direct Current (HVDC) infrastructure. The portfolio includes subsea telecom, offshore specialties, and 525kV HVDC submarine cable systems.

Power Grid: This segment supports the modernization of terrestrial power grids through High Voltage Alternate Current (HVAC) systems, power distribution solutions, and overhead lines.

Electrification: Representing the largest volume of sales, this segment provides energy-related cables for Industrial and Construction applications, alongside specialized products for OEMs, renewables, elevators, automotive, and downhole technologies.

Digital Solutions: Focused on telecom networks, this division manufactures optical fibre, connectivity components, optical ground wire (OPGW), and data center inside-plant cables.

Revenue Streams and Financial Scale

Prysmian operates with significant global scale, generating total revenues of €19.65 billion in FY25. The company benefits from a diversified geographic footprint, heavily weighted toward North America and the EMEA (Europe, Middle East, and Africa) regions.

Customer Segmentation and Go-to-Market Strategy

Prysmian aligns its go-to-market strategy with the specific needs of its distinct customer categories. In the Transmission and Power Grid segments, the company primarily serves Transmission System Operators (TSOs), Distribution System Operators (DSOs), large utilities, and public infrastructure agencies. The commercial approach here heavily relies on long-term, "turnkey" Engineering, Procurement, and Construction (EPC) contracts. Under this model, Prysmian guarantees end-to-end project management, from cable design to installation, testing, and maintenance.

In the Digital Solutions and Electrification segments, the customer base broadens to include telecommunications operators, construction firms, and high-tech enterprises. To capture the explosive growth in the data center market, Prysmian has pivoted toward securing long-term commercial agreements directly with hyperscalers. By leveraging its strategic position as one of the few domestic fiber producers in the US, alongside recent acquisitions like Channell, Prysmian acts as a unique one-stop shop providing both digital connectivity and energy distribution solutions for inside-building data center architectures.

Project Execution Capabilities

A core pillar of Prysmian’s business model is its full vertical integration, allowing the company to control quality, timelines, and costs throughout the value chain. For high-voltage submarine projects, project execution relies heavily on the company's proprietary fleet of advanced cable-laying vessels, which includes the Leonardo da Vinci and the newly introduced Monna Lisa. To cement its installation dominance, Prysmian recently acquired ACSM, a premier subsea surveying and seabed preparation firm. This acquisition brings in-house the expertise of over 350 subsea specialists and three dedicated vessels, optimizing offshore installation speeds and ensuring robust cable protection. By 2028, Prysmian plans to have eight vessels in operation, giving it unmatched maritime execution capabilities compared to its peers.

Structural Competitive Advantages and Technological Leadership

Prysmian’s structural moat is underpinned by its massive manufacturing and engineering scale, comprising 109 production facilities, 30 R&D centers, and approximately 34,000 employees globally. This scale allows the company to absorb high capital expenditures and maintain geographic proximity to major localized grid projects.

The company continuously distances itself from competitors through proprietary technological breakthroughs. In the high-voltage energy space, Prysmian successfully qualified a 525kV submarine cable system capable of operating at 90°C, increasing maximum transmission power by 25% (up to 2.5GW) without requiring changes to existing HVDC network infrastructure. In the telecom sector, Prysmian has partnered with Relativity Networks to co-manufacture hollow-core optical fibers. This technology transmits data nearly 50% faster than conventional fiber, enabling cloud hyperscalers to drastically reduce latency and flexibly locate their AI data centers farther from power providers.

Raw Material Sourcing and Circular Economy Integration

As a massive consumer of base metals and polymers, Prysmian has aggressively integrated circular economy principles into its supply chain. The company aims to generate 55% of its total revenues from sustainability-linked solutions by 2028. To mitigate raw material constraints and reduce its Scope 3 emissions, Prysmian has set strict procurement targets, successfully reaching a 23.3% share of recycled content across addressable materials by early 2026.

The company routinely collaborates with utility partners, such as Enedis in France, to supply cables comprising over 12% recycled aluminum, 30% recycled copper, and recycled polyethylene jackets. Furthermore, Prysmian has partnered with Rio Tinto to integrate ELYSIS™ technology into its building wire, utilizing a revolutionary aluminum smelting process that emits oxygen instead of greenhouse gases. Cementing its ESG leadership, Prysmian has also patented the world’s first negative-carbon-footprint cable, leveraging bio-polymers and recycled materials to absorb more carbon during sourcing and manufacturing than it releases.

Order Backlog and Visibility

Prysmian’s project-based segments enjoy exceptional multi-year revenue visibility. As of early 2026, the Transmission segment's order backlog reached a staggering €16.8 billion to €17.0 billion. Additionally, the company has roughly €2 billion in awarded contracts that have not yet been formally assigned to the backlog. This robust pipeline, dominated by massive offshore wind contracts (e.g., DolWin4, BorWin4, IJmuiden Ver) and cross-border interconnectors (e.g., NeuConnect, Eastern Green Link), secures the company's manufacturing and installation asset utilization well into 2028.

Sector Primer: Energy Transition & Connectivity Infrastructure
Macroeconomic Drivers: Electrification and Grid Modernization

The global power sector has entered a multi-decade structural investment upcycle driven by decarbonization, electrification, and energy security policies. This grid expansion is directly linked to the large-scale integration of renewable energy sources, which require extensive extra-high-voltage (EHV) and high-voltage direct current (HVDC) transmission corridors to evacuate power from remote wind and solar farms to major demand centers.

More than half of the existing energy infrastructure in developed markets was built before the 1970s, necessitating immediate modernization and replacement efforts.

According to the International Energy Agency (IEA), global power grid investment is projected to reach approximately $490 billion annually between 2025 and 2030.

Offshore wind energy acts as a particularly intensive driver for submarine cable demand, with global offshore capacity expected to expand significantly to 275 GW by 2030.

The AI Data Center Supercycle and Fibre Optics Boom

Artificial intelligence has introduced an unprecedented vector for both power and connectivity infrastructure demand. The IEA projects that global electricity demand from data centers will more than double to roughly 945 TWh by 2030, a figure equivalent to the entire current annual power consumption of Japan. To support this massive computing load, McKinsey estimates that the global AI-related data center ecosystem could require up to $6.7 trillion in cumulative investment through 2030, heavily weighted toward power generation and transmission capacity. As power density per rack escalates, data center architectures are transitioning toward medium-voltage distribution systems to improve scalability and reduce resistive losses.

As power density per rack in data centers increases, the transition to 800V DC makes specialized power equipment like solid-state transformers essential.

Geopolitics and Energy Security Policies

Geopolitical fragmentation and regional conflicts have fundamentally reshaped the strategic importance of energy and digital infrastructure. Following supply shocks stemming from the Russia-Ukraine conflict, European nations have accelerated their transition toward renewable energy to reduce hydrocarbon dependency and ensure long-term supply stability.

Digital sovereignty is also becoming a critical policy objective, with governments mandating localized data residency for "Sovereign AI" to insulate domestic data from global volatility. These trends are compounded by trade protectionism, such as elevated U.S. tariffs and "Buy America" supply requirements, which are forcing a restructuring of global supply chains and increasing capital costs. In the optical communications sector, heightened data security scrutiny has accelerated the shift of manufacturing bases toward Southeast Asia under "China Plus One" strategies to mitigate data export review risks.

Barriers to Entry, Supply Chain Dynamics, and Raw Material Constraints

The high-voltage and submarine cable industries are characterized by formidable barriers to entry, primarily due to extreme capital intensity, complex permitting, and the need for highly specialized manufacturing capabilities. These projects require extensive research and development, rigorous performance certifications, and proprietary maritime execution assets like specialized cable-laying vessels.

Currently, the industry is grappling with severe supply chain bottlenecks, particularly concerning raw materials like copper and aluminum. Electrification has made copper so critical that it is increasingly referred to as "the new oil," yet the development of new copper mines faces lengthy timelines and strict environmental regulations. Furthermore, grid interconnection queues for new capacity can now extend from three to five years in major markets due to bureaucratic permitting processes and acute shortages of transformers and switchgear.

Competitive Landscape & Peer Analysis
Global Market Structure and Barriers to Entry

The global cable industry exhibits a highly bifurcated competitive structure, characterized by an intensely concentrated oligopoly in the high-voltage (HV) and submarine markets, alongside a more fragmented landscape in the low- and medium-voltage (LV/MV) segments. The HV and submarine sectors are defined by formidable barriers to entry, requiring complex manufacturing technologies, specialized maritime installation vessels, and rigorous pre-qualification track records. Only a select group of manufacturers, primarily European players like Prysmian, Nexans, and NKT, alongside Asian leaders like LS Cable & System and Sumitomo Electric, possess the capabilities to execute massive cross-border interconnector and offshore wind export projects. Conversely, the LV/MV markets—which serve commercial construction, standard grid distribution, and localized data center routing—feature lower technological barriers and face increasing supply additions, driving a more competitive pricing environment.

Nexans: Strategic Pivot to Pure-Play Electrification

Nexans has undergone a radical strategic transformation, divesting non-core assets to pivot entirely into a pure-play electrification provider. Historically, Nexans struggled with profitability in the highly commoditized North American utility markets, prompting the company to temporarily retreat from the U.S. high-voltage land cable space to focus heavily on the high-margin submarine interconnector business. This disciplined focus on "value over volume" has structurally improved the company's profitability, allowing it to achieve record Adjusted EBITDA margins of 11.9% in late 2025.

To capture the surging demand from U.S. data centers and grid modernization, Nexans is now re-entering the North American low- and medium-voltage markets through targeted M&A. The company's recent acquisitions of Republic Wire in the U.S. and Electro Cables in Canada provide an expanded regional manufacturing footprint and crucial distributor relationships. In the submarine space, Nexans remains a formidable technological rival to Prysmian, having recently completed a world-record installation of a 500kV HVDC subsea cable at a depth of 2,150 meters for the Tyrrhenian Link.

Capital Allocation and Strategic M&A

Prysmian's capital allocation strategy balances aggressive, value-accretive M&A with internal capacity expansion and shareholder returns. The company has successfully executed a string of strategic acquisitions designed to verticalize its capabilities and expand its geographic footprint. The €169 million acquisition of ACSM brings crucial submarine cable installation and seabed preparation capabilities in-house, mitigating reliance on third-party maritime contractors. In the digital space, the acquisitions of Channell and Xtera have fortified Prysmian's position in North American connectivity and long-haul subsea telecom networks, respectively.

Simultaneously, Prysmian is deploying approximately €800 million in capital expenditures for 2026 to support organic capacity expansions across its Transmission, Power Grid, and Digital Solutions facilities. This aggressive cash generation has allowed the company to rapidly deleverage, bringing net financial debt down to €3,818 million by March 2026, with expectations to push leverage below 1.0x by year-end. Consequently, Prysmian continues to reward shareholders, proposing a 13% dividend increase to €0.90 per share for 2026.

Forward Outlook and 2028 Targets

Visibility into future revenue is secured by an order backlog that reached approximately €17 billion by the end of Q1 2026, supplemented by an additional €2 billion in awarded projects awaiting final assignment. Bolstered by this pipeline, Prysmian has confirmed its 2026 full-year guidance, projecting an Adjusted EBITDA between €2,625 million and €2,775 million and free cash flow generation of up to €1,400 million.

The company's "Accelerating Growth" business plan outlines ambitious targets for 2028, including an Adjusted EBITDA of up to €3.15 billion and a Return on Capital Employed (ROCE) climbing to between 20% and 22%. By consistently delivering on early milestones, management has signaled that the current trajectory is already outpacing these long-term structural targets.

Sustainability and Eco-Friendly Innovations

Prysmian has embedded decarbonization directly into its revenue model, targeting 55% of total sales from sustainability-linked solutions by 2028. In Q1 2026, the company successfully reached 42.6% in sustainability-linked revenues while reducing Scope 1 and 2 greenhouse gas emissions by 40.8% against its 2019 baseline.

Product innovation remains a core driver of this ESG strategy. In 2026, Prysmian patented and launched the world's first negative-carbon-footprint cable, which utilizes bio-polymers and recycled materials to absorb up to 1 ton of CO2 per kilometer manufactured. Furthermore, the company has partnered with Rio Tinto to incorporate ELYSIS™ zero-carbon aluminum into its building wire products, significantly reducing the embodied carbon of its electrification portfolio.

Operational and Macroeconomic Risks

Despite its robust financial health, Prysmian navigates a complex matrix of operational and macroeconomic risks. The execution of massive, multi-year turnkey submarine projects exposes the company to potential delays, technological failures, and warranty liabilities, which Prysmian mitigates through rigorous pre-installation testing and extensive insurance coverage.

Supply chain vulnerabilities also remain a persistent threat, particularly regarding the sourcing and pricing of critical base metals like copper and aluminum. Additionally, shifting geopolitical dynamics, including elevated U.S. trade tariffs on imported aluminum products, require constant monitoring, though Prysmian's localized North American manufacturing footprint via Encore Wire provides a structural hedge against protectionist policies.
