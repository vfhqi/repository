GYM-TRIO Q11 VALUE CHAIN MAP [C]

Date: 27-Apr-26 | Stage: ESA | Source: Claude [C]

KEY FINDINGS

  • The European low-cost gym value chain runs across four primary stages: real estate owners → fit-out contractors and equipment suppliers → gym operators → end members and B2B purchasers. Gym operators occupy the critical central stage, capturing the largest share of industry value creation through membership fees.
  • Margins accrue non-uniformly. Landlords earn 60-70% net operating income margins on their space. Equipment suppliers (Technogym, Life Fitness) earn 50%+ gross margins. Gym operators at the budget end earn 35-50% EBITDA pre-IFRS 16 at mature club level, but must service lease obligations that consume the bulk of this. Members pay a recurring fee with no residual ownership; the economic relationship is entirely in the operator's favour in a growing market.
  • Power dynamics are shifting in favour of gym operators vs landlords. Post-2019 retail vacancy explosion in Europe created a buyer's market for commercial space. Budget gym operators are now actively courted as anchor tenants by retail park owners and shopping centre landlords looking to replace failed fashion retailers. This has driven improved lease terms: longer rent-free periods, landlord fit-out contributions, and in some cases turnover-linked rent caps.
  • BFIT, GYM, and SATS are all pure-play operators occupying the mid-chain position. None owns real estate (all lease). None manufactures equipment (all buy from Technogym, Life Fitness, Matrix, Precor). All sell directly to consumers via app/web and in-club. SATS is the most differentiated at the downstream stage via corporate wellness B2B.
  • The digital intermediary has not emerged as a significant value extractor. Unlike in hospitality (Booking.com, Airbnb) or transport (Uber), no platform has inserted itself between gym operators and members. Members join directly via operator apps or websites. This is a structural advantage for operators vs the hotel or taxi industry.
  • Consolidation is happening at the operator stage, not at the landlord or equipment stage. BFIT-Clever Fit is the clearest example. The equipment market is also gradually consolidating (Life Fitness, Precor under Arden) but the gym operator remains the dominant margin capturer.
    - BFIT has the most defensible value chain position due to scale (cost advantages vs landlords and equipment suppliers), franchise model diversification (reduces capex dependence), and 24/7 technology leadership. GYM has the best asset-light scalability at the current stage of development. SATS has the strongest B2B downstream moat.
    - Rating summary: BFIT position sustainability — YELLOW (scale advantaged but integration and leverage risks); GYM position sustainability — GREEN (clean position, technology differentiator, improving returns); SATS position sustainability — YELLOW-GREEN (strong Nordic moat, B2B defensible, CMD execution risk is the primary concern).

1. END-TO-END VALUE CHAIN

The European low-cost gym value chain in ASCII diagram form:

Capital flows: Lease payments → Landlords | Equipment purchases + maintenance → Suppliers | Membership fees ← Members

BFIT/GYM/SATS position: Pure-play operators at the OPERATOR stage. No upstream ownership, no downstream intermediary.

Each stage has distinct characteristics. The landlord stage is capital-intensive and long-cycle. The equipment/fit-out supplier stage is project-based and moderately recurring (maintenance, equipment refresh). The operator stage is recurring-revenue, high-operating-leverage. The end-customer stage is monthly or annual subscription-based.

2. GYM OPERATORS' POSITION IN THE VALUE CHAIN

BFIT, GYM, and SATS are all pure-play operators. They do not own real estate, do not manufacture equipment, and do not control the property layer of the value chain. This is a deliberate choice: gym operators maximise ROIC by avoiding the capital intensity of property ownership.

BFIT's upstream relationships: leases from a diversified landlord base across Netherlands, Belgium, Luxembourg, France, Germany (Clever Fit), Spain. Equipment primarily from Life Fitness and Matrix (higher volume, lower price point than Technogym). Fit-out managed through standardised contractor relationships developed through 25 years of rollout. BFIT's scale (1,710 owned clubs + 454 Clever Fit) gives it exceptional procurement leverage with all upstream suppliers.

GYM's upstream relationships: UK commercial property (retail parks, high streets, mixed-use developments). Equipment from Life Fitness and Matrix. Fit-out through a limited panel of UK-based specialist gym fit-out contractors. GYM's 245-club estate is growing but is not yet at the scale that gives BFIT's procurement leverage. However, management has targeted fit-out cost reduction as a lever, with total capitalised cost per new site running at approximately £1.3m.

SATS's upstream relationships: Norwegian, Swedish, Finnish, and Danish commercial property. Equipment mix includes Technogym (premium positioning justifies higher-grade equipment). Fit-out through Scandinavian contractors. SATS's larger clubs (2,000-5,000 sqm) mean higher total fit-out cost per site (NOK 8-15m), but per-sqm costs are similar to UK budget peers. SATS's property portfolio skews toward established urban centres with lower landlord incentive availability than the retail-park-secondary locations favoured by BFIT.

3. ADJACENT VALUE CHAIN STAGES

Customers' customers (downstream extensions):

Direct members represent approximately 85-90% of revenue for BFIT and GYM. Members are individual consumers who purchase memberships directly through operator apps, websites, or in-club kiosks. There is no intermediary between operator and member in the consumer channel. This is a structural advantage: the operator captures 100% of member economics, unlike hotel operators who pay 15-25% commission to online travel agents.

Corporate wellness (B2B) is the most strategically important adjacent channel. Under corporate wellness, an employer subsidises or fully funds employee gym memberships as a health and productivity benefit. The gym operator invoices the corporate account monthly and receives guaranteed volume. This channel accounts for approximately 10-20% of revenue for SATS (the strongest B2B operator of the three) and a smaller share for BFIT and GYM. Corporate wellness members are structurally lower-churn than individual members (the employer-subsidy provides stickiness) and generate higher predictable revenue.

Insurance partnerships represent a growing channel in the Netherlands and Germany specifically. Dutch and German health insurers are increasingly subsidising gym memberships as preventive health intervention. BFIT's Netherlands/Belgium position benefits from this; it is a meaningful incremental demand driver in these markets. For GYM and SATS, insurance partnerships are less developed but represent a mid-term growth opportunity.

4. MARGIN DISTRIBUTION ACROSS THE CHAIN

Stage Key Players Gross Margin % Net Margin / ROIC Value-Add to Chain

Equipment suppliers Technogym, Life Fitness, Matrix, Precor 50-55% gross ~12-18% net Design, manufacturing, maintenance
Fit-out contractors Local/regional specialists 20-30% gross 5-10% net Installation, project management
Gym operators (budget) BFIT, GYM, SATS 35-50% EBITDA (pre-IFRS 16, mature) 5-15% net; 25-40% mature ROIC Brand, experience, technology, mgmt
Insurance/employer (B2B) Health insurers, employers N/A (cost centre) N/A Subsidised demand aggregation

Key observations:

Landlords earn high NOI margins but on very long-cycle assets with limited operational skill. Their value-add is purely location provision and lease structuring. Power dynamic: diminishing post-2019 in secondary/retail park locations; still strong in prime high-street and central urban locations.

Equipment suppliers earn excellent gross margins on premium products (Technogym) and moderate margins on volume products (Matrix, Life Fitness). The after-market (maintenance, service contracts, software) is the margin engine. Switching costs are moderate — equipment is interchangeable between brands — but volume contract relationships with major operators create multi-year supply stability.

Fit-out contractors are the lowest-margin participants. They execute standardised blueprints on a project-fee basis. At scale, operators like BFIT tender fit-out contracts across a region, creating significant competitive pressure among contractors. This has been a key capex reduction lever: GYM Group has reduced per-site capex through scale procurement of fit-out services.

Gym operators at mature scale earn extraordinary economics in terms of ROIC: a £1.2m investment earning £500k EBITDA is a 40%+ cash-on-cash yield before lease amortisation. The lease is the complicating factor: strip out the lease cost and the true operating economics are excellent; include it (as one should) and the ROIC settles in the 25-40% range for the best operators.

5. POWER DYNAMICS

Landlord power. Historically high, based on scarcity of commercial space in good catchment areas. Post-2019, retail vacancy dramatically increased the supply of appropriately sized units. Landlords shifted from a "choose your tenant" dynamic to a "find me a good tenant" dynamic. Gym operators, with their stable long-term leases, regular footfall, and low tenant failure risk, have become preferred tenants alongside supermarkets, healthcare providers, and drive-through food operators. The result: rent-free periods extended from 3-6 months to 6-12 months; landlord fit-out contributions increased; base rents in secondary locations decreased. For prime urban locations, landlord power remains significant.

Equipment supplier power. Moderate. The 3-4 dominant suppliers (Technogym, Life Fitness, Matrix, Precor) have genuine product differentiation and R&D investment. However, gym operators at the scale of BFIT can and do multi-source, mix equipment brands, and run competitive tender processes. The maintenance/service contract relationship creates medium-term stickiness but not long-term lock-in. Digital connectivity features (equipment-to-app integration) are creating nascent switching costs as operators invest in unified data ecosystems, but this is early-stage.

Gym operator power over members. Moderate and proximity-dependent. Members choose their gym primarily on location (within 2km of home or workplace). Pricing at the budget end is relatively undifferentiated; the decision is: go to gym (any budget gym) or not go. The operator must then compete on facility quality, cleanliness, equipment availability, and digital/class offering to reduce churn at the margin. Switching costs within the budget gym category are low (monthly contracts, no fees for cancellation). SATS's corporate wellness channel creates higher switching costs: an employer-linked membership is harder to cancel unilaterally.

B2B/insurance power. Growing. Health insurance reimbursements for gym memberships create a new intermediary with negotiating power over operator pricing. In the Netherlands, this channel is developed enough that insurers can negotiate membership rates. In Germany, it is growing. This is not yet a dominant value extractor, but over a 5-10 year horizon, institutional B2B buyers could compress operator margins if they grow to represent 30%+ of revenue. Currently, at 10-15% of revenue, they are value-additive rather than margin-compressive.

7. COMPETITIVE DYNAMICS AT ADJACENT STAGES

Equipment suppliers. Technogym (listed on Italian Stock Exchange) is the premium European leader, dominant in higher-end gym segments. Life Fitness (owned by KPS Capital Partners following acquisition from Brunswick) is the dominant mid-tier commercial supplier globally. Precor was acquired by Amer Sports (now partly owned by ANTA Sports). Matrix is the volume brand of Johnson Health Tech (Taiwanese manufacturer). The equipment market is oligopolistic but not monopolised; operators have genuine choice. Consolidation is occurring through private equity roll-ups (various regional suppliers being acquired), but the major brands remain independent.

Landlords. UK retail REITs (Land Securities, British Land, Hammerson) have explicitly shifted strategy toward alternative tenants including gyms, healthcare, and food. Hammerson's portfolio has seen increased gym operator tenancy. Continental European retail REITs (Unibail-Rodamco-Westfield, Klépierre) are following the same playbook. This structural shift is positive for gym operators: it locks in long-term lease availability at reasonable terms and provides a stable supply of suitable locations.

Insurance partnerships. Dutch health insurer partnerships with BFIT are a developed market. German health insurance (Krankenkasse) partially reimburses gym memberships for certain plan members. UK health insurance partnerships (AXA Health, Bupa) are nascent for budget gyms but growing. The insurance industry's shift toward preventive health creates a structural tailwind for gym operators over the medium term.

8. STRUCTURAL CHANGES AND EVOLUTION

24/7 staffless technology. BFIT pioneered the 24/7 staffless model in Benelux and is now rolling it out in France (Q1-26 approval). GYM Group has always operated 24-hour access; staffing levels are already low. SATS operates staffed peak-hour periods but is not pursuing pure staffless. The 24/7 staffless model removes the largest variable cost (staff) and provides a material EBITDA margin uplift. France approval alone saves BFIT at least €10m/year. As this model propagates across Europe, it is a permanent structural uplift to operator margins.

Digital fitness and app-based member management. All three operators have invested in proprietary app platforms. These apps serve three functions: member management (check-in, payments, account management); engagement (class booking, workout tracking, virtual classes); and commercial (PT marketplace, premium tier upsell). The best-in-class app (arguably GYM Group's) creates incremental switching costs and reduces churn. Investment in digital is ongoing and accretive to long-term value.

Consolidation at the operator stage. BFIT-Clever Fit is the defining consolidation transaction of the European budget gym market in 2025. It combines the largest owned-club operator with the largest franchise network, creating a genuinely pan-European business with scale across 12 countries. More consolidation is likely: mid-tier operators (McFIT in Germany, Fitness World in Denmark, smaller regional chains) face the squeeze between scale operators (BFIT, PureGym) and premium boutique studios. M&A opportunities for BFIT and PureGym to acquire distressed mid-tier assets at attractive multiples are expanding.

Direct-to-consumer digital substitution risk. This risk has materially declined since the Peloton collapse. The consensus view (backed by post-pandemic data) is that digital fitness complements rather than substitutes physical gym membership. The hybrid model — gym for equipment, community, and accountability; digital for convenience and supplementary training — is the emerging consumer behaviour pattern.

9. BFIT, GYM, AND SATS COMPETITIVE ADVANTAGES

Basic-Fit:

Scale is the primary moat. 2,184 clubs (Q1-26) with 6.0m members creates procurement leverage over every upstream supplier. BFIT negotiates club leases, equipment contracts, and energy supply at a scale that no competitor can match in continental Europe. The standardised operating model (same fit-out specification, same membership software, same app) reduces per-club operating complexity and drives predictable economics.

Technology leadership in 24/7 staffless operation. BFIT pioneered this model in Benelux and has built the regulatory expertise, technology infrastructure (access control, CCTV, alarm systems, emergency protocols), and operational knowledge to deploy it across markets. France approval (Q1-26) gives BFIT a regulatory first-mover advantage that will take 12-24 months for competitors to replicate.

Franchise model entry (Clever Fit). This adds a capital-light growth vector absent before October 2025. Franchise expansion in Germany, France, and Spain can proceed without BFIT deploying equity capital. If executed well, this model becomes BFIT's highest-ROIC growth lever for the period 2027-2030.

The Gym Group:

Technology differentiation in the UK budget segment. GYM's app-based class booking, PT marketplace, and digital member engagement represent a genuine competitive advantage in the UK. PureGym's app is less developed. JD Gyms has no comparable digital platform. For members who care about classes, virtual workouts, and PT access, GYM is the superior product at the budget price point.

Financial discipline and clean balance sheet. Non-property net debt of £59.3m (1.0x leverage), £38.3m FCF, £10m buyback. This financial profile means GYM can invest in growth, return capital, and absorb shocks simultaneously. No competitor in the UK budget segment is as financially lean.

Lowest-cost national chain brand positioning. Standard membership at £25.10 is competitively priced relative to PureGym (~comparable) and far below mid-market operators. This price position attracts price-sensitive consumers and creates a structural floor under volume.

SATS:

Premium-budget positioning in the highest-penetration markets in Europe. Norwegian and Swedish consumers are willing to pay NOK 600-700/month for a superior gym experience. SATS's offering — full group exercise timetable, staffed reception, large well-equipped facilities, SATS app with class booking and wellness tracking — justifies the price premium over BFIT's €24.91 or GYM's £21.60. SATS earns approximately 2.5x more revenue per member per month than BFIT on a currency-adjusted basis.

Corporate wellness B2B moat. SATS has developed institutional relationships with Norwegian and Swedish corporates that provide low-churn, high-LTV membership revenue. This B2B channel is not easily replicable by a new entrant: it requires years of institutional relationship development, corporate HR integration, and employer brand trust.

Dominant brand in the highest-loyalty fitness markets. Norwegian and Swedish consumers have the highest gym attendance rates in Europe. SATS has a 25+ year brand history. Brand loyalty is genuinely higher than in markets with shorter fitness culture histories.

10. VULNERABILITIES

All three operators share:
- Lease-liability exposure. All three operate 100% leased club estates. In a deep recession or major operational disruption, lease obligations are fixed while revenues are variable. IFRS 16 makes this visible on the balance sheet but does not eliminate the economic exposure.
- Energy cost vulnerability. Large facilities with HVAC, lighting, and cardio equipment are energy-intensive. Energy represents approximately 8-12% of revenue. A prolonged energy spike (as in 2022-23) compresses margins directly.
- Member acquisition cost risk. Budget gym membership requires constant new member inflows to replace churn. Marketing costs (primarily digital acquisition) are rising as Google/Meta advertising prices increase. Any significant increase in customer acquisition cost would compress FCF.

BFIT-specific:
- Clever Fit integration risk. 454 franchise partners are the responsibility of a team that has no prior franchise management experience. Integration failures (franchisee defection, brand quality deterioration, German underperformance) would directly damage the acquisition thesis.
- Leverage. At 2.7x net debt/EBITDA (ex leases), BFIT has limited financial flexibility if trading deteriorates. The June 2026 put option on the €303.7m convertible adds a specific liquidity management requirement.
- Single-CEO concentration. René Moos as founder-CEO and major shareholder is a governance concentration risk. The company's strategy is substantially driven by one person's vision and operational decisions.

GYM-specific:
- ARPMM ceiling. At £21.60 average and £25.10 headline price, GYM is approaching the upper end of what its budget brand positioning supports. Further price increases risk competitive response from PureGym co-locating near GYM sites.
- UK market saturation risk. In primary UK cities (London, Manchester, Birmingham), budget gym density is already high. The 75-site-over-3-years programme relies on identifying adequate locations outside the primary urban cores.
- PureGym competitive pressure. PureGym is larger, better capitalised (private equity backed), and aggressively targeting 300+ more UK sites. Co-location with PureGym is the most damaging risk scenario for GYM's membership economics.

SATS-specific:
- CMD execution dependency. The NOK 1.1bn EBITDA target is the most important near-term narrative for SATS. Any credible slip in execution triggers a valuation de-rating.
- NOK currency. For non-Norwegian institutional investors, SATS's NOK earnings are a structural FX risk that is unhedgeable through the share.
- Treningshelse competitive growth. Treningshelse's 93+ Norwegian locations directly compete with Fresh Fitness (SATS's budget sub-brand). If Treningshelse accelerates with external capital, Fresh Fitness market share is at risk.

11. STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS

Which company has the most defensible value chain position?

BFIT's scale and geographic diversification give it the most defensible position against landlord and supplier power shifts. No other European gym operator can replicate BFIT's procurement leverage across 12 countries and 2,184+ clubs. The franchise model addition further increases capital-light defensibility. However, BFIT's defensibility against competitor gym operators (PureGym in France/Spain, McFIT in Germany) is less clear. The Clever Fit integration execution risk is the primary near-term defensibility test.

GYM's position is most defensible against the technology disruption vector (strong app) and financial stress (clean balance sheet, high FCF). However, it is the most exposed to competitive co-location risk given PureGym's aggressive UK expansion.

SATS's position is most defensible against consumer price sensitivity (premium product justifies premium price) and B2B customer loss (corporate wellness creates institutional stickiness). It is most exposed to the ARPM ceiling risk in Norway and the currency risk for investors.

Who has the best opportunity to move up or down the value chain?

BFIT is best positioned to move upstream into partial real estate ownership in its most strategic markets (potentially acquiring the freehold on anchor clubs in France/Spain as the franchise model matures). BFIT is also best positioned to expand the franchise model downstream, earning ongoing royalty income rather than one-time membership revenue. This is structurally similar to a platform model.

GYM is best positioned to deepen the digital layer — expanding the PT marketplace, virtual class library, and data analytics to drive higher ARPMM from the existing member base. The digital layer is GYM's most differentiated asset and the one with the highest potential ROIC.

SATS is best positioned to deepen the B2B corporate wellness channel, moving from gym membership subsidisation to full employee wellness service provision (nutrition, mental health, physiotherapy integration). This adjacent expansion would increase ARPM and reduce churn simultaneously.

What must change for any to be structurally threatened?

BFIT would be structurally threatened by: (1) a pan-European digital fitness platform that genuinely substitutes physical gym attendance (not on the horizon); (2) regulatory reversal of 24/7 staffless models across its key markets; (3) a major credit market contraction that prevents refinancing of the lease liability and corporate debt stack.

GYM would be structurally threatened by: (1) PureGym successfully launching a sustained price war below £20/month (destroys budget economics for all UK operators); (2) A well-capitalised entrant (Amazon, Apple, JD Sports) acquiring the budget gym space and subsidising membership growth through non-gym revenue.

SATS would be structurally threatened by: (1) A major Norwegian recession leading to structural membership decline; (2) Treningshelse or an equivalent competitor achieving SATS-comparable scale in Norway/Sweden; (3) Permanent oil price weakness ($40/barrel) that structurally degrades Norwegian consumer spending power.

12. SCEPTICAL VIEW

What could erode each company's position most rapidly?

For BFIT: The Clever Fit franchise model may prove structurally incompatible with BFIT's owned-club culture. BFIT has spent 25 years building a lean, standardised, low-cost operated network. Franchising requires entirely different skills: franchisee recruitment, brand standards enforcement, ongoing training and support, franchisor economics modelling. If BFIT's franchise model underdelivers (franchisee attrition, brand dilution in Germany, failure to attract new franchise partners), the €175m Clever Fit acquisition is a write-down risk and the diversification thesis fails.

For GYM: ARPMM plateauing at £21-22 in an environment where PureGym expands aggressively is a slow-bleed scenario. Revenue growth slows to 3-4%/year (pure volume). EBITDA margins plateau as energy and staff costs rise faster than revenue. The 30% ROIC target for new sites is not achieved. GYM goes from a growth investment to a steady-state operational business — which is not terrible, but is not what the current premium valuation implies.

For SATS: The CMD target proves to be management aspiration rather than operational roadmap. ARPM increases above NOK 630 trigger meaningful churn in 2026-27. Net member count stagnates. EBITDA grows at 8-10%/year rather than the 15-20% implied by the CMD bridge. The stock re-rates to a lower multiple on lower growth expectations.

Sustainability ratings:
- BFIT position sustainability: YELLOW — scale advantage is real and durable; integration and leverage risks are the near-term constraints
- GYM position sustainability: GREEN — cleanest competitive position, most defensible near-term balance sheet, technology-led differentiation
- SATS position sustainability: YELLOW-GREEN — strong market position in loyal markets, but CMD execution risk and ARPM ceiling create genuine uncertainty
