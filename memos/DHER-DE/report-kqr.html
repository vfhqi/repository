Section 2 – Shareholder base changes and voting power dynamics 24 findings

Section summary
  1. The shareholder structure of Delivery Hero has consolidated under Uber's rapid stake accumulation to a 36.83% economic interest, while regulatory mandates have forced Prosus to reduce its holdings.

Mandatory sell-down and regulatory extensions

1.
Mandated stake reduction: To secure antitrust clearance for its Just Eat Takeaway acquisition, Prosus committed to the European Commission in August 2025 to reduce its holding to a low single-digit percentage
2.
Enforced governance restrictions: The regulatory remedy prevents Prosus from recommending or appointing board members or participating in Delivery Hero management
3.
Confidential deadline extension: On May 29, 2026, the European Commission extended the original August 2026 divestment deadline to allow strategic options to mature amid potential takeover bids
4.
Specific extended timeline: The regulatory extension effectively shifts the mandatory divestment deadline for Prosus to October 11, 2026, though the exact date remains confidential in official annual reports
5.
Relief from forced selling: The extension mitigates technical selling pressure by shielding Prosus from becoming a forced seller before a potential tender process finishes

Transacted stake sales and price execution

6.
Initial Uber acquisition: In April 2026, Prosus executed its first divestment step by selling 13,582,342 shares to Uber at €20.00 per share, yielding €270 million in gross proceeds
7.
Execution premium levels: The transaction price represented a c.22% premium to the one-month volume- weighted average price of Delivery Hero shares
8.
Subsequent activist transfer: In May 2026, Prosus sold an additional 15,188,284 shares to activist investor Aspex Management for €22.00 per share
9.
Substantial block premium: This secondary transaction generated €335 million in gross proceeds for Prosus and represented a c.10% premium to the closing spot price
10.
Activist stake escalation: The block purchase from Prosus allowed Aspex Management to increase its holding in Delivery Hero to approximately 14%
11.
Residual overhang reduction: Following both major block transactions, the residual equity interest held by Prosus in Delivery Hero declined from 26.6% to 17.1%

Uber's rapid consolidation and ownership thresholds

12.
Strategic stake tripling: Uber initially built its position from a 2.5% stake linked to the blocked foodpanda Taiwan deal to a 19.5% equity position by May 19, 2026
13.
Activist buyout execution: In late May 2026, Uber bought out the entire 14% holding of Aspex Management to rapidly accelerate its equity building
14.
Acquisition pricing levels: Uber acquired the Aspex holding for a price of just below €40.00 per share, representing a significant markup from previous block trades
15.
Current economic consolidation: The buyout lifted Uber's total economic interest to approximately 36.83% of Delivery Hero, establishing it as the dominant equity stakeholder
16.
Averting takeover triggers: Uber restricted its direct voting rights to 24.99% by holding the remaining 11.84% in derivatives, staying just below the 30% German mandatory takeover threshold
17.
Takeover probability increase: The rapid consolidation of over a third of the equity has significantly raised the probability of a full takeover offer by Uber

Voting power concentration at general meetings

18.
Neutralized voting blocks: In compliance with the European Commission mandates, Prosus has committed not to exercise its voting rights on its remaining 17.1% stake
19.
Independent trustee oversight: The voting rights associated with the Prosus shares are managed by an independent trustee tasked with acting in the best interest of Delivery Hero

Concentrated active franchise: Because Prosus's voting power is neutralized and typical AGM attendance was 78.34% in 2025, Uber's 24.99% voting stake wields immense real influence

Section 3 – CEO succession and activist pressure 18 findings

Section summary
  1. Activist pressure from Aspex Management directly accelerated the structured departure of CEO Niklas Östberg, while the strategic return of Roger Rabalais strengthens supervisory oversight during the long transition.

Activist escalation and executive departure

1.
Intensifying public campaign: Hong Kong-based activist investor Aspex Management launched a vocal public campaign in March 2026 demanding the immediate removal of co-founder and CEO Niklas Östberg
2.
Existential liability concerns: Aspex vocally criticized management over a massive €1.5 billion bill for legal provisions and contingent liabilities, threatening to appoint a special auditor to investigate these existential liabilities
3.
Style and performance critique: The activist asserted that the issues with Östberg's management style and performance went far beyond a hesitation or inability to divest underperforming assets
4.
Rapid succession catalyst: Although Delivery Hero denied that the resignation was activist-driven, the board announced the CEO's structured exit just 24 hours after Aspex acquired an additional 5% stake
5.
High-premium stake build: Aspex purchased a 5% equity block from Prosus at €22.00 per share, representing a 10% premium to spot and raising its total holding to 14%
6.
Defined transition window: Under the agreed terms, Östberg will step down by March 31, 2027, but will continue to lead negotiations for the ongoing strategic review
7.
Accelerated search timeline: To ensure organizational stability, the Supervisory Board initiated a formal search process with the goal of appointing a permanent successor by the end of 2026

Supervisory board reinforcement and Rabalais return

8.
Strategic board appointment: Delivery Hero appointed Roger Rabalais, a former Prosus operating partner, to the Supervisory Board on April 30, 2026, to succeed the resigning Warren Jenson
9.
High-caliber financial oversight: Following his formal re-election at the June 23, 2026 AGM, Rabalais was positioned to serve as Vice Chairman and Chairman of the Audit Committee
10.
Decoupled corporate independence: Despite his history with Prosus, Rabalais is classified as independent under the German Corporate Governance Code because he holds no active roles at Prosus
11.
Strategic execution support: Deutsche Bank argues that Rabalais' extensive corporate finance background and operational knowledge of food delivery actively support the ongoing strategic review
12.
Activist agenda mitigation: Jefferies notes that the return of Rabalais to the board serves as a powerful mechanism to potentially stymie the aggressive activist agenda of Aspex

Leadership transition friction and operational distraction

13.
Protracted operational distraction: Jefferies warns that the long transition period, combined with intense competition from Coupang in Korea and Keeta in MENA, poses severe risks of operational distraction
14.
Talent pool limitations: Finding a replacement is challenging because the global food delivery sector remains highly founder-led, significantly limiting the available C-suite executive talent pool
15.
Segment autonomy risk: Former division leaders point out that centralizing power in Berlin has historically led to replacing local founders with corporate executives, diluting critical local market expertise
16.
Leadership vacancy concerns: Sceptical observers point out that a long-running search during a period of heavy M&A speculation could leave key operating segments without a clear strategic mandate
17.
side of the story: Sceptics argue that despite governance adjustments, a transition period spanning nearly a year under an outgoing CEO will create prolonged strategic paralysis, hampering the firm's ability to fend off aggressive market share grabs by well-capitalized competitors in South Korea and Saudi Arabia

Section 4 – Strategic review, asset sales, and portfolio valuation 21 findings

Section summary
  1. The ongoing strategic review focuses on crystallizing value through the orderly divestment of non- core assets like foodpanda Taiwan and the partial monetization of high-margin MENA operations.
  2. Activist demands for leverage reduction are driving portfolio pruning, yet regulatory hurdles and heavy reinvestment requirements create execution uncertainty for long-term margin targets.

Divestment execution and foodpanda Taiwan monetization

1.
Aborted transaction: Delivery Hero originally agreed in May 2024 to sell its Taiwanese operations to Uber for $950 million in cash, which represented an attractive 0.60x enterprise value to gross merchandise value multiple
2.
Regulator block: The Taiwan Fair Trade Commission officially prohibited the Uber transaction in January 2025 due to severe market concentration and monopoly concerns in the local duopoly market
3.
Contractual protection: Following the deal termination, Uber paid a substantial cash breakup fee of €211.9 million or approximately $242 million to Delivery Hero in April 2025
4.
Restructured deal: Delivery Hero reached a subsequent agreement in March 2026 to sell foodpanda Taiwan to Grab Holdings for $600 million in cash
5.
Valuation discount: Deutsche Bank reports that the Grab transaction implies a lower valuation multiple of 0.35x enterprise value to gross merchandise value for 2025, which still exceeds Delivery Hero's group trading multiple of 0.15x
6.
Regulatory clearance: Jefferies highlights that because Grab is a new market entrant in Taiwan, the deal carries extremely low regulatory risk and is highly likely to close in the second half of 2026

7.

Consortium optionality: Deutsche Bank reports that Naver has explored an 80-20 consortium with Uber to acquire Woowa Brothers, though Naver stated in regulatory filings that no definitive decision has been made

Section 5 – M&A optionality and takeover defense dynamics 21 findings

Section summary
  1. Ongoing M&A speculation surrounding Delivery Hero is driven by Uber's conditional 33 euros per share offer and interest from DoorDash and local players in Middle Eastern assets.
  2. Regulatory antitrust barriers and complex change-of-control provisions that fully vest executive stock options create significant execution risk for any potential full takeover.

Takeover proposals and asset interest

1.
Indicative offer: Delivery Hero officially confirmed receipt of a non-binding proposal from Uber to acquire the entire business for 33 euros per share
2.
Shareholder expectations: Major institutional investors are reportedly holding out for a transaction price exceeding 40 euros per share before agreeing to a deal
3.
Middle East targeting: Sector peer DoorDash has expressed separate strategic interest in acquiring Delivery Hero's highly profitable Middle Eastern operations
4.
Local bidding competition: Saudi Arabian quick-commerce platform Ninja has evaluated an independent proposal to acquire the Hungerstation unit and partner on Talabat
5.
Asset separation complexity: Sceptical division experts suggest that separating regional businesses would complicate any global transaction due to deeply integrated regional tech infrastructure

Prosus positioning and tech sovereignty

6.
Strategic blocking optionality: Dutch internet conglomerate Prosus obtained a regulatory deadline extension to October 11, 2026, which prevents forced selling and permits moves to block US buyers
7.
Counter-bid expectation: Research from Jefferies indicates that a full counter-bid by Prosus to consolidate Delivery Hero remains the most plausible strategic outcome
8.
European tech sovereignty: Proponents argue that the European Commission has a strong incentive to block a US-led takeover of a major European digital champion
9.
Retained equity stake: Prosus currently retains a 17.1% equity holding and could leverage its massive scale to challenge any foreign acquisition attempts

Change-of-control and LTIP 2.0 triggers

10.
Executive departure rights: Delivery Hero's Management Board members can resign within three months of a change of control and automatically terminate their service contracts
11.
Accelerated option vesting: A change of control immediately triggers the accelerated vesting of all unvested restricted stock units and outstanding stock options
12.
Financial payout commitments: Management Board members Niklas Östberg and Pieter-Jan Vandepitte will have their incentive instruments fully vested regardless of cliff provisions
13.
Employee plan acceleration: Under the employee share purchase program, all rights to free matching shares immediately become due upon a change of control
14.
Credit facility acceleration: The existing 1.4 billion euro syndicated credit agreement permits participating banks to terminate commitments and accelerate repayment upon change of control

Antitrust barriers and regulatory reviews

15.
Significant market overlap: Uber and Delivery Hero have overlapping food delivery footprints in 22 global markets, which creates massive antitrust hurdles
16.
European concentration risks: The combination would face severe regulatory pushback in Europe due to competitive overlap in countries like Poland, Portugal, Spain, and Sweden

Legacy antitrust precedents: Regulatory authorities previously blocked Delivery Hero's attempted 950 million dollar sale of foodpanda Taiwan to Uber due to monopoly concerns
18.
Joint bid complexity: Analysts at Jefferies suggest that a joint bid from Uber and DoorDash to carve up the assets could bypass antitrust but introduces immense execution risk

side of the story: Sceptical advisors at Rothschild and Company Redburn argue that recurrent regulatory investigations into rider classification and high market overlap will prevent any strategic buyers from executing a full takeover, leaving Delivery Hero to execute its turnaround alone
