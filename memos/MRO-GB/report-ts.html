Section 2 – The 80/20 (bottom line up front) 8 findings

  1. Melrose has completed its turnaround and is transitioning into a pure-play aerospace technology business with significant medium-term cash inflection potential as older and newer engine platforms mature
1.
Core business operations: GKN Aerospace is structured into two core operating segments, Engines and Airframes, which design and deliver flight-critical components for commercial and military aviation customers globally
2.
Compelling investment case: The core investment case centers on a highly predictable, long-term aftermarket cash harvest phase as its RRSPs mature, combined with the completion of major turnaround restructuring
3.
Critical swing factor: The single most critical swing factor for future earnings is the pace at which civil aerospace OEMs can ramp up new aircraft production

Section 3 – What the company does 17 findings

Section summary
  1. Melrose operates a high-moat tier-one aerospace technology business with sole-source positions on 90% of global aircraft, driven by a highly lucrative engines aftermarket and contractual structures

Segment-Level Operations and Product Portfolio

1.
Dual divisional structure: GKN Aerospace is structured into two core segments, namely Engines and Airframes, which was recently renamed from Structures to better reflect its civil and military activities
2.
Engines division overview: Engines is a leading tier-one supplier to the engines market, designing and manufacturing engine mount structures, fan cases, turbine cases, shafts, and rotating components
3.
Airframes division overview: Airframes is a tier-one supplier of advanced civil and military aerostructures, including lightweight composite wing structures, metallic fuselages, empennages, and complex electrical wiring distribution systems
4.
Proprietary design-led focus: The group has replaced its historical "Buy, Improve, Sell" corporate model with a "Design, Deliver, Improve" model that focuses on deep technical design integration with major OEMs

Revenue Streams and the RRSP Model

5.
Core engines models: Engines generates revenues through four primary commercial channels, comprising risk and revenue sharing partnerships (RRSPs), non-RRSP commercial contracts, repair services, and governmental defense contracts
6.
Predictable RRSP economics: GKN Aerospace holds a unique portfolio of 19 civil-led RRSPs of varying maturities, which are life-of-program agreements between engine manufacturers and strategic partners to develop and maintain engines
7.
Decades of aftermarket capture: The RRSPs grant GKN Aerospace an entitlement to a lifetime share of the lucrative aftermarket profit pool as engines enter their maintenance and shop visit phases
8.
Extensive fleet coverage: The RRSP portfolio provides aftermarket exposure to c. 70% of global regional and narrowbody flight hours and c. 70% of global widebody flight hours
9.
Concentrated engine value: While the portfolio comprises 19 engines, just six critical engine programs, including the V2500, CFM56, GEnx, Rolls-Royce XWB, and Geared Turbofan, drive 90% of the RRSP portfolio's value

Value Chain Positioning and Moat Mechanisms

10.
Profit-favoured tier-one positioning: Within the commercial aerospace value chain, GKN Aerospace occupies an OEM-equivalent, design-led role, making it almost impossible to disintermediate once designed into a platform
11.
Durable competitive advantages: Moats are characterized by extremely high customer switching costs, long-term contract structures, and proprietary manufacturing technologies that create significant barriers to entry
12.
Proprietary additive fabrication: Engines has achieved serial production of its additively manufactured titanium Fan Case Mount Ring for the GTF engine, which reduces material consumption and provides over 70% material savings
13.
Strong pricing power: GKN Aerospace maintains sole-source positions representing over 70% of its aerospace revenues, granting the business significant pricing power over its commercial contracts

Customers and Competitive Industry Structure

14.
Concentrated blue-chip clients: The division's customer base is highly consolidated, composed of major global OEMs including Airbus, Boeing, GE Aerospace, Pratt & Whitney, Safran, Rolls-Royce, and Lockheed Martin
15.
Attractive market structure: The aerospace sector is characterized by high technical complexity and limited competition, with only half-a-dozen companies in the world possessing the capital and capability to partner on RRSPs
16.
Other side of the story: Despite its strong technical barriers, GKN Aerospace remains vulnerable to prolonged narrowbody build rate delays at Airbus, which directly curtail Airframes' margins and delay the realization of expected operational leverage

Section summary

Revenue and Operating Profit Trends

1.
Robust divisional profitability: Engines adjusted operating margins reached 31.9% in FY25, generating £520 million in adjusted operating profit, which represents a 27% increase compared to FY24

Earnings Quality and Cash Conversion

2.
Substantial variable consideration: A major portion of Engines' profit is non-cash variable consideration under IFRS 15, representing £324 million or 62% of divisional operating profit in FY25, compared to £274 million or 65% in FY24
3.
Declining restructuring outflows: The positive cash flow inflection was driven by a substantial decline in cash restructuring costs, which fell from £126 million in FY24 to £31 million in FY25 as operational integration concluded

Leverage and Balance Sheet Shape

4.

5.
Ongoing capital returns: Melrose has returned significant capital to shareholders, completing £192 million of its £250 million share buyback and announcing a new £175 million share buyback program

Forward Guidance and Performance Track Record

6.
Strong Q1 results: Q1 2026 group revenue grew by 11% at constant currency, driven by a 20% surge in the Engines division and a 4% increase in the Airframes division
7.
Favourable peer positioning: Melrose's adjusted operating margin of 18.0% in FY25 represents a strong middle-ground compared to Safran's premium propulsion margins and Senior's lower aerostructures profitability

Section 5 – The possible investment case 14 findings

Section summary
  1. The investment case for Melrose strengthens significantly as high-margin engine aftermarket shop visits surge, completing a multi-year restructuring program that unlocks structural free cash flows

Aftermarket Flight Hour Rebounds

1.
Rising flight hours: Global engine flying hours are forecast to grow by over 6% annually through 2030, driving an unprecedented surge in commercial aftermarket shop visits and spare parts demand
2.
High plausibility driver: Plausibility is high, given that severe new aircraft delivery delays force airlines to extend the operational lives of older, mature fleets that require extensive maintenance
3.
Failure mechanism: This driver would fail if a severe global economic recession occurred, leading to a sharp contraction in commercial aviation flight hours and reduced airline maintenance spend

Escalating Value of the RRSP Engine Portfolio

4.
Accretive fleet replacement: As older narrowbody engines retire, they are replaced by newer variants like the GTF, XWB, and GEnx where GKN Aerospace's program share is significantly larger
5.
High plausibility driver: Plausibility is high, as the group maintains locked-in, sole-source contractual agreements that guarantee multi-decade participation in the high-margin commercial engine aftermarket profit pool
6.
Failure mechanism: This driver would fail if engine OEMs introduced aggressive contract discount programs or if competing engine programs gained significant market share

Structural Operating Leverage and Rationalised Footprint

7.
Completed footprint consolidation: GKN Aerospace has concluded its multi-year operational restructuring, streamlining its manufacturing footprint from 52 sites in 2018 down to 32 optimized facilities
8.
Medium plausibility driver: Plausibility is medium, as the footprint is fully consolidated, but generating full operating leverage requires a steady recovery in OEM production volumes
9.
Failure mechanism: This driver would fail if localized manufacturing inefficiencies, such as the H2 2025 Netherlands productivity issues, recurred and dragged down Airframes' margins

GTF Program Recovery and Future Cash Inflows

10.
Declining GTF cash drag: Cash outflows related to the Pratt & Whitney GTF powder metal issues are scheduled to decline from £68 million in FY25 to c. £50 million in FY26
11.
High plausibility driver: Plausibility is high, as the fleet management inspection plan is progressing on schedule and the newly certified GTF Advantage variant enters service
12.
Failure mechanism: This driver would fail if new technical or design defects were identified in the Geared Turbofan fleet, leading to renewed aircraft-on-ground liabilities

Compound Synthesis and Bear Risks

13.
Other side of the story: Sceptical bears argue that GKN Aerospace's Airframes division operates in a structurally low-margin, capital-intensive segment of the supply chain with very limited pricing power

Section 7 – Key risks and the other side 7 findings

Section summary
  1. The overall risk picture for Melrose remains highly complex due to near-term supply chain headwinds and accounting complexity, though structural positions are secured

Supply Chain and Operational Headwinds

1.
Severe supply chain bottlenecks: Global casting and forging bottlenecks continue to restrict engine and aerostructure production, directly limiting Melrose's near-term revenue growth and forcing operational adjustments
2.
Material GTF exposure: GKN Aerospace maintains a 4% share of the Pratt & Whitney Geared Turbofan PW1100G program, exposing the company to a c. £200 million total cash drag from the powder metal defect issues

Accounting Complexity and Cash Visibility

3.
Cash flow sensitivity: Morgan Stanley's sensitivity analysis shows that flexing RRSP-related receivables by only 15% can impact cumulative cash flow estimates by a negative 70% to positive 30%

Early-Warning Indicators and Overstated Bears

4.
Early-warning signal to monitor: Investors must closely monitor the month-on-month trend of Pratt & Whitney GTF engines on the ground and the rate of global engine shop visits to verify the aftermarket recovery
5.
Direct liability shielding: GKN Aerospace's conservative accounting under IFRS 15 shielded its profits during the GTF metallurgy crisis, unlike MTU Aero Engines which was forced to book a massive €1.0 billion provision
6.
Other side of the story: Bears overstate the risks because GKN Aerospace's conservative accounting under IFRS 15 has already shielded the income statement from direct provisions, and mature engines flying longer boosts high-margin spare parts sales

Section 8 – Management: what is changing 5 findings

Section summary
  1. Melrose is experiencing a significant leadership transition as a new Chief Financial Officer assumes responsibility for GKN's complex aerospace accounting and capital market communication
1.
Critical CFO transition: In December 2025, Melrose announced that Matthew Gregory would retire from his role as Chief Financial Officer and step down from the board on May 5, 2026
2.
External CFO appointment: Ross McCluskey succeeded Matthew Gregory as Chief Financial Officer on May 5, 2026, bringing public company experience from Intertek
3.
Extended handover period: To ensure a seamless transition amidst accounting complexity, Matthew Gregory will remain with Melrose until November 30, 2026 to support Ross McCluskey and the company
4.
Board capability additions: The board has been significantly strengthened with the appointment of Non-Executive Directors Alison Goligher, Guy Hachey, and Mary Petryszyn, bringing extensive aerospace, defence, and executive management expertise

Section 9 – Net read and what to check next 5 findings

Section summary
  1. Melrose definitely warrants deeper investment work because of its highly predictable, high-moat aftermarket engines cash flow, which is set to surge over the next three years
1.
Strongest case for investment: The most compelling argument for investing in Melrose is the long-term cash flow predictability of its 19 civil-led RRSPs, which provide a high-margin entitlement to 70% of global flying hours
2.
Other side of the story: The most critical bear counter-view is that the stretching £600 million FY29 cash target relies on highly optimistic assumptions about Airbus build rates and a complete resolution of the GTF metallurgy issues
3.
First concrete action: Commission a targeted study on the month-on-month trend of the Pratt & Whitney Geared Turbofan inspections to verify that overall cash outflows remain capped within the c. £200 million guidance
4.
Second concrete action: Conduct a deep-dive verification of the variable consideration accounting under IFRS 15, specifically reviewing the £1,308 million contract asset to evaluate the risk of future revenue reversals
