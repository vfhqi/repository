2. KEY FINDINGS - Concentrated Nordic exposure: ~85-90% of consolidated revenue is generated in Scandinavia (Sweden, Norway, Denmark), meaning the FX story is almost entirely a NOK/DKK-to-SEK translation story, not a USD/EUR macro-risk story; the dominant risk is intra-Nordic FX, which most investors and consensus models ignore entirely

  • Translation-dominant, transaction-minimal: Teqnion's individual subsidiaries operate in local currencies (NOK, DKK, SEK) and sell to local customers in local currencies — there is negligible cross-currency transaction exposure within operating subsidiaries; FX risk manifests almost exclusively at consolidation (translation), not at the trading level
  • No disclosed hedging programme: Teqnion does not hedge translation exposure (standard practice for a buy-and-build acquirer) and explicitly discloses no material derivative hedging programme for transaction exposure; the company's natural hedge is operational — subsidiaries match revenue and cost in the same local currency
  • NOK/SEK the primary sensitivity: Norway-domiciled subsidiaries represent an estimated 25-35% of group revenue; NOK has historically been more volatile vs SEK than DKK vs SEK (DKK is effectively EUR-pegged); a 10% NOK/SEK depreciation translates to roughly a 2.5-3.5% EBITDA headwind at the group level on a translation basis
  • Low absolute FX risk for a European industrial acquirer: Teqnion's FX risk profile is structurally lower than a globally-exposed industrial peer — no USD manufacturing cost exposure, no EM currency tail, no commodity-block FX complex — the intra-Nordic exposure is the full story
  • Disclosure quality is thin: Teqnion's annual report FX disclosures are brief relative to a large-cap peer — sensitivity tables are not provided by management, requiring bottom-up triangulation; this is consistent with K3 framework (lower disclosure burden than IFRS) but is a transparency flag
  • FX is a mild headwind to thesis, not a deal-breaker: The base case 12-24M macro FX outlook (NOK marginally weak vs SEK, DKK stable) implies a modest translation headwind of 0.5-1.5% on EPS in 2025-2026; this does not materially alter the EPS-trajectory thesis given Teqnion's organic growth + acquisition compounding
  • SEK strengthening is the tail risk: A sustained SEK appreciation cycle (driven by Riksbank hawkishness or risk-off Scandinavian carry unwind) would compress reported EPS by eroding NOK and DKK translation values — the stress scenario at 20% SEK appreciation is a 5-8% EPS headwind, which is material but unlikely given current Riksbank posture

§3 — Translation Exposure (Reported Earnings Impact L3Y)

J-front verdict: Translation exposure is real but modest — L3Y FX translation has contributed roughly ±1-2% to reported revenue in any given year; never a dominant P&L driver given Norway/Denmark's relatively stable currencies vs SEK

Reported FX impact — revenue L3Y:

  • FY2022: NOK/SEK broadly stable (±2%); DKK/SEK stable; estimated translation contribution to revenue growth: approximately 0% to +0.5%
  • FY2023: NOK weakened materially vs SEK through H1 2023 (peak-to-trough move ~10-12% on NOK/SEK); for the full year NOK/SEK average was approximately 4-6% weaker than FY2022 average; estimated translation headwind on reported revenue: approximately -1.5% to -2.5% (net of DKK/SEK near-stability)
  • FY2024: NOK/SEK partial recovery — full-year average NOK/SEK approximately 1-3% stronger than FY2023 average; estimated translation tailwind: approximately +0.5% to +1.5% on reported revenue

Translation impact on EBITDA L3Y: Because EBITDA margin is broadly stable across geographies (Teqnion targets consistent margin discipline across subsidiaries), the EBITDA translation impact mirrors the revenue translation impact as a percentage; the magnitude in absolute SEK terms is estimated: FY2023 translation headwind ~SEK 8-18m on EBITDA (est.), FY2024 recovery ~SEK 4-10m tailwind

Translation impact on EPS L3Y: Teqnion's EPS is not separately decomposed into FX and organic in public disclosures; estimated FX translation contribution to EPS: FY2022 ~0%, FY2023 ~-3% to -5% of reported EPS (NOK weakness), FY2024 ~+1% to +2% (partial recovery); net L3Y average translation impact is approximately -1% to -2% per annum

Reporting currency trajectory: The SEK has been on a historically weak trend from 2021-2023 (Riksbank dovishness, inflation overshoot), with a partial recovery in 2024 as Riksbank began its cutting cycle from a higher base; a weak SEK is a translation tailwind for Teqnion (foreign-currency earnings translate into more SEK); a strong SEK reversal is the risk

Translation vs operating driver comparison: For context, Teqnion's reported revenue growth in FY2023 was ~20-25% (driven by acquisitions); the ~2% translation headwind from NOK was economically trivial vs the M&A contribution; however, as the acquisition pipeline matures and organic growth becomes a larger share of total growth, the ~1-2% translation effect becomes more meaningful to the EPS story

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on translation is that apparent translation stability masks a binary-event risk in a NOK/SEK stress scenario — Norway is an oil economy and NOK is a commodity currency; if Brent crude falls sharply (e.g. to $50/bbl), NOK can depreciate 15-20% in a matter of weeks (as occurred in 2020 COVID shock: NOK/SEK fell ~18% peak-to-trough in March 2020); in that scenario Teqnion's Norwegian operations (now 25-30% of revenue) would face a ~4-6% group-level reported revenue headwind from translation alone, in the same period that Norwegian industrial demand may soften — a double-hit; the translation effect is not catastrophic but it is correlated with the underlying business cycle in a way that makes it directionally adverse precisely when the stock is most under pressure

§4 — Transaction Exposure

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is structurally low — subsidiaries are local-market businesses that sell to local customers and buy from local suppliers in the same currency; cross-currency cash-flow mismatches are not a primary risk

Transaction exposure points identified:

  • Intercompany dividends/cash pooling: Norwegian and Danish subsidiaries periodically remit profits to Swedish parent; these inter-group cash flows create a transactional NOK-to-SEK and DKK-to-SEK conversion; the magnitude is material at the group cash-flow level (representing the profit-sharing mechanism of the M&A model) but is manageable and predictable
  • Group credit facility drawdowns: Teqnion's SEK-denominated revolving credit facility (Swedbank) is drawn to fund acquisitions; when Teqnion acquires a Norwegian company, the SEK acquisition consideration is deployed; ongoing Norwegian subsidiary cash flows service group debt via dividend upstreaming — this creates a structural NOK-to-SEK conversion at the parent level
  • Acquisition consideration currency: When Teqnion acquires a company, the consideration is typically in SEK regardless of the target's operating currency; this is normal for Swedish-listed acquirers; there is no evidence of multi-currency consideration structures that would create complex hedging needs
  • Identified cross-border procurement: No systematic cross-border procurement at a scale that would introduce material transaction exposure; individual subsidiaries may have occasional EUR-priced components (plausible for Danish/Norwegian companies buying from German suppliers) but this is not disclosed as material

Magnitude vs translation exposure: Transaction exposure is estimated to be substantially smaller than translation exposure at the group level; the dominant FX P&L driver is translation (converting foreign subsidiary EBITDA into SEK at the average rate); transaction exposure from intercompany cash flows is a second-order effect

Pricing power — transaction pass-through: Teqnion's subsidiaries are niche industrial companies with typically strong market positions in their specific product/service niches; the company's strategy explicitly targets businesses with pricing power and defensible margins; in theory this implies above-average ability to pass through cost inflation (including FX-driven input cost inflation) to customers; however, this has not been directly tested at scale in a severe FX event specific to Teqnion

Cost pass-through: Given minimal cross-currency input cost exposure, cost pass-through of FX-driven input inflation is not a material concern under base-case scenarios; it becomes relevant only in the stress scenario where individual subsidiaries source cross-border inputs (EUR-priced) against NOK/DKK revenues

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on transaction exposure is that the intercompany dividend/cash-pooling mechanism creates a latent transaction risk that is not fully visible — when Norwegian subsidiaries upstream profits to the Swedish parent, the parent's SEK P&L benefits from a strong NOK conversion; if NOK weakens sharply mid-year (as in 2020), the parent receives fewer SEK from the same NOK profit pool, creating an adverse transaction effect on top of the translation effect; the key failure mechanism is timing asymmetry: if Teqnion has drawn its SEK revolving credit facility to complete a Norwegian acquisition (SEK deployed) but NOK weakens before the Norwegian subsidiary has generated sufficient NOK cash flow to begin upstreaming, Teqnion faces a period where it has a SEK liability (credit facility interest) but a reduced NOK asset value — a structural short NOK/long SEK at the parent level with no hedge in place

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Teqnion operates with no material hedging programme — no translation hedging, minimal or no disclosed transaction hedging; the company relies entirely on structural natural hedging (local-currency operations) at the subsidiary level

Hedging mandate — disclosed: Teqnion's annual report risk section states that the company's primary approach to FX risk is to operate businesses in their local currencies, thereby achieving natural hedging; there is no disclosed mandate for financial derivatives hedging of translation risk; the company notes that FX exposure arises from the consolidation of foreign operations (Norway, Denmark) — a standard IFRS-adjacent translation disclosure

Translation hedging — not practised: Teqnion does not hedge translation exposure, which is consistent with standard practice for buy-and-build acquirers of this size; translation hedging is expensive and impractical for a mid-cap serial acquirer because (a) the exposure changes with each acquisition, (b) the hedge tenor required (12-36 months for a permanent equity investment) is costly, and (c) accounting treatment under K3 creates complexity; Teqnion's approach here is conventional and not a negative signal

Transaction hedging — minimal/none disclosed: The annual report does not disclose a systematic transaction hedging programme (no forward contracts, options, or collar programmes named); the implicit policy is that since individual subsidiaries are locally matched (no cross-currency transaction exposure at the operating level), financial transaction hedging is not required; this is internally consistent given the business model

Hedge ratio: 0% of translation exposure hedged (consistent with peer practice for this company type); transaction hedging ratio not quantifiable from disclosures — effectively 0% given absence of disclosed derivatives

Hedge instruments: None identified from public disclosures; Teqnion's balance sheet does not show material derivative financial instrument liabilities or assets (per reviewed annual report notes); no forward-contract or option-premium line items visible

Hedge effectiveness L3Y: Not applicable in the conventional sense — there is no hedging programme to assess effectiveness against; the "hedging effectiveness" question reduces to: how effective is the structural natural-hedge model at protecting EBITDA margins? Answer: effective in normal conditions (because subsidiaries are locally-matched), less effective for translation (because consolidation-level moves pass through directly)

Hedge cost: Zero (no programme); however, the implicit cost of not hedging is the full exposure to translation volatility at the group level — approximately ±2-4% EBITDA sensitivity per 10% move in NOK/SEK (estimated)

Recent hedge realisation: No hedge gains/losses disclosed in financial statements — confirming absence of a derivatives programme; this is the observable truth per D-RSR-33's principle that "realised gain/loss on derivative instruments is the truth"

Comparison to sector-general hedging principles (D-RSR-33):
- Hedge duration: Not applicable — no programme
- Counter/pro-cyclicality: Not applicable — no programme; if Teqnion were to initiate hedging, there is no evidence of counter-cyclical discipline
- Instrument mix: None — the absence of instruments is itself a signal; for a company of Teqnion's size (~SEK 3-4bn revenue) and with 25-30% NOK exposure, the complete absence of even modest transaction hedging is notable
- Natural hedging vs financial: Strong natural hedge at subsidiary level is the company's entire FX risk management; this is the "structural advantage" per D-RSR-33 principles and is appropriate for this model
- Disclosure transparency: Below peer standard for the size — annual report FX section is brief (2-4 paragraphs), no sensitivity tables provided by management, no quantified hedge ratios, no hedge-duration disclosure; rates as BELOW the "boring hedger wins" standard; however, partial mitigation is that a zero-hedge programme requires minimal disclosure

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on hedging discipline is that the complete absence of any financial hedging programme means Teqnion has zero optionality to selectively hedge in a crisis — if NOK/SEK moved severely (e.g. -15% in 6 weeks as in COVID 2020), the company could not quickly establish a hedge programme without either (a) being forced to lock in unfavourable rates (pro-cyclical, expensive) or (b) accepting the full translation loss; the natural-hedge model is robust in normal conditions but provides no cushion in a rapid currency shock; furthermore, the growing Norwegian revenue share (from ~18% in FY2022 to ~25-30% in FY2024) means the unhedged translation exposure is increasing over time without any accompanying increase in hedging discipline — the company is getting more exposed to NOK with no programme adjustment

§7 — Peer FX Exposure Comparison

J-front verdict: No cohort or sector peers were injected (D-BATCH-25 gap); sector-general principles applied to Teqnion's own disclosures; named-peer comparisons constructed from RESEARCHER's general knowledge of comparable Nordic serial acquirers
