§6 — Governance Structure + Red Flags

J-front verdict: Clean on structural red flags — no dual-class, no restatement, no material investigation; Engie residual stake and AFEP-MEDEF code limitations are structural nuances, not acute red flags

Voting rights structure:
- No dual-class shares: Veolia operates a standard one-share-one-vote structure for ordinary shares; no super-voting rights, no founder control mechanism
- Double voting rights (fidélité loyalty shares): French law (Florange Act, 2014) provides for double voting rights for shares held ≥2 years; Veolia's statutes incorporate this; effectively creates mild entrenchment for long-term holders (including Caisse des Dépôts, which holds ≈5–6%) but is industry-standard in France and does not create controlling-bloc governance risk
- Engie residual stake (≈10%): Engie sold its majority Suez stake to Veolia in 2020 but retained a residual position; this creates a potentially conflicted shareholder with board representation; risk: Engie could block or complicate strategic decisions where Veolia/Engie compete (energy services); mitigant: Engie's stake has been declining and there is a credible path to full divestiture; watch for board vote conflicts

Related-party transactions:
- Veolia's key RPT is the Engie relationship (former shareholder/supplier): some legacy service contracts between Veolia and Engie-group entities in energy services and utilities; disclosed in URD as related-party transactions; quantum is not "material" as a percentage of revenue but the structural conflict warrants monitoring
- No family/founder RPT risk (no founder family on register)
- Government/municipal customers: a substantial portion of Veolia's revenue is from public-sector entities (municipalities, regional governments); this creates policy and political risk but not governance RPT risk in the formal sense

Audit history:
- Auditor: Veolia uses a dual-auditor structure (French statutory requirement for major companies): Deloitte + Mazars (historically); Deloitte has been a long-standing auditor; no recent auditor change
- No material weaknesses disclosed in public record L5Y
- No restatement history identified; accounting under IFRS; Veolia's use of "current net income" as alternative measure is disclosed and consistent year-on-year
- Audit committee independence: confirmed majority independent; audit committee charter available in URD

Legal / regulatory issues:
- No SEC investigations (Veolia is a French company; dual-listed on NYSE via ADR historically but primarily regulated under AMF/SEC Form-20F framework; no AMF enforcement action publicly reported)
- Environmental regulatory risk: Veolia operates in highly regulated environmental services; periodic regulatory and compliance actions are part of normal operations (water quality violations, waste permit breaches at specific sites); no single regulatory issue of investment-thesis-breaking scale identified
- Suez acquisition litigation: the acquisition involved legal challenges (French courts, competition regulators in multiple jurisdictions, employee/union challenges); all primary litigation concluded with the acquisition completion and EC-mandated carve-outs; no open material litigation that threatens the combined entity
- Class actions: no US-style securities class action identified in public record; French securities litigation is structurally less common
- FCPA / anti-corruption: Veolia operates in emerging markets (Middle East, Africa, Latin America) where corruption risk is structurally elevated; no specific FCPA investigation disclosed; Veolia has a compliance programme (Code of Ethics, anti-corruption training) disclosed in URD; this is an ongoing monitoring item, not an acute red flag

Whistleblower / governance complaints:
- Veolia has a disclosed whistleblower mechanism (required under French Sapin II legislation); no public disclosure of material whistleblower event affecting the governance thesis
- Glassdoor reviews occasionally reference bureaucratic culture and post-merger confusion, but no systemic governance complaint pattern visible

Anti-takeover provisions:
- Double-voting (loyalty shares) provides mild entrenchment — see above
- No poison pill structure identified
- Caisse des Dépôts ≈5–6% stake acts as a quasi-strategic anchor (French state-adjacent); provides political stability but also implies French government de-facto influence on major strategic decisions — not governance-negative per se but limits full market discipline

Sceptical bullet: The bear read on governance is that the combination of Engie residual stake, Caisse des Dépôts quasi-government influence, French loyalty-share entrenchment, and AFEP-MEDEF's weaker independence thresholds creates a structural environment where a poorly performing management team is insulated from shareholder-driven accountability for longer than equivalent Anglo-Saxon structures would permit; the red flag that would invalidate the operator-quality thesis is evidence of the Compensation Committee repeatedly granting above-target ESG scores while TSR underperforms, signalling board capture rather than genuine performance management
