Plausible problems to monitor 5 findings

1.
Structural core service revenue erosion: Swisscom's domestic Swiss telecommunications service revenues declined by CHF 122 million during 2025, driven by B2C mobile erosion of CHF 52 million and B2B mobile erosion of CHF 70 million, significantly underperforming the European sector peer median of flat-to-positive service revenue growth and highlighting intensifying competitive pressure that completely neutralizes strategic tariff hikes. [Cross-ref: Section 1]
2.
Excessive post-acquisition leverage burden: Swisscom's net debt-to-EBITDA ratio reached 2.4x following the Vodafone Italia acquisition, marking the regulatory ceiling mandated by the Swiss Federal Council, while Redburn Atlantic estimates underlying starting leverage closer to 2.9x when including full lease obligations, substantially exceeding peer median leverage of 1.8x-2.0x for comparable European incumbents and constraining future capital allocation flexibility. [Cross-ref: Section 2]
3.
Severe Italian mobile market deflation: The Italian mobile service revenue pool contracted by 18.10% since FY2020 to €10.70 billion, representing one of the worst structural declines across major European markets and materially worse than the European sector base rate of 5-8% mobile revenue erosion, exposing Swisscom's largest growth investment to persistent pricing deflation that threatens synergy realization. [Cross-ref: Section 3]
4.
Unprecedented tower infrastructure switching costs: Swisscom's unilateral termination of the INWIT Master Service Agreement risks €640 million in direct switching costs and extensive network disruptions, with migration costs estimated at €50,000 per point of presence, substantially exceeding the European sector base rate of €15,000-€25,000 per site and representing a severe execution risk that could materially erode projected Italian synergies. [Cross-ref: Section 4, Section 5]
5.
Dilutive return profile from Italian consolidation: Swisscom's consolidated ROCE is pressured downward by expensive Italian asset acquisitions and flat Swiss margins, failing to meet the BB#2 Standard threshold of 25-30%+ post-tax RONIC, while group capital intensity reached 20.36% in 2025, materially worse than peer median capital intensity of 15-16% for comparable European convergent operators, reflecting suboptimal capital allocation that dilutes the high-quality Swiss asset base. [Cross-ref: Section 2, Section 9]

Outliers worth noting 3 findings

1.
Unique European infrastructure ownership model: Swisscom stands as the only major European incumbent operator that retains full ownership of its domestic physical telecommunications infrastructure, contrasting sharply with asset-light peers like Orange, Deutsche Telekom, and Vodafone that have divested passive tower and fiber assets to specialized infrastructure funds. This vertical integration creates exceptional capital intensity but delivers unmatched wholesale pricing power and strategic network flexibility. [Cross-ref: Section 9]
2.
Extreme Swiss telecom spending premium: Swiss telecommunications expenditure per capita reached CHF 85 per month in 2023, representing a 119% premium over the EU-15 average of CHF 39. Despite this extraordinary absolute spending level, the figure represents only 1.40% of Swiss disposable income, aligning closely with the historical EU-15 benchmark of 1.10%. This substantial price premium reflects both superior purchasing power and Swisscom's ability to extract exceptional pricing in a concentrated domestic market structure. [Cross-ref: Section 3]
3.
Unprecedented electromagnetic emission restrictions: Switzerland enforces electromagnetic field limits ten times stricter than WHO international recommendations, creating a regulatory ceiling on competitive tower deployment that lands in the global bottom 5% for permissive mobile infrastructure planning. These extreme emission standards artificially constrain network densification, preventing competitors from matching Swisscom's coverage quality and effectively functioning as a sovereign-enforced barrier to infrastructure competition. [Cross-ref: Section 9]
