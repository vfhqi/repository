Seven drivers determine the 24-month outcome.

  • Setup archetype: Peak-capex / FCF-inflection + regulated-infrastructure re-rating — pattern-matches a recognisable setup, but the number of co-required conditions makes it multi-variable rather than single-fulcrum [#J]

§2 — Key Driver #1: FCF Inflection and Deleveraging

  • Verdict: FCF inflection from FY26-27 is the thesis linchpin — if it delivers, re-rating is material; if it slips again, leverage and dividend credibility both break simultaneously [#J]

  • Driver definition: BT is in a peak-capex cycle driven by FTTP rollout (£15bn committed through FY29); FCF has been suppressed; the investment thesis hinges on capex peaking c.FY25-26 and FCF normalising to £1.5bn+ by FY27 [#A]

  • Sub: Mechanism to SP — FCF inflection supports dividend sustainability, enables net debt/EBITDA reduction below 3.0×, and drives EV-to-FCF re-rating from current depressed multiple [#A]

  • Sub: Current trailing FCF (normalised) estimated c.£1.0-1.1bn FY25; guided step-up to c.£1.5bn by FY27 implies c.35-45% improvement [#A]

  • Company guide: Management has guided "normalised FCF of £1.5bn+" by FY27; capex guided to peak FY25 at c.£5.1bn and step down thereafter as FTTP build intensity reduces [#A]

  • Sub: Conservatism vs optimism — guidance has been moved back once already (originally FY26, now FY27); credibility of "this time" guidance is consequently lower [#J]

  • Track record: BT has a history of guided FCF improvement slipping by 1-2 years; FY24 normalised FCF of c.£1.1bn was below the FY24 guidance range set 2 years prior [#A]

  • Sub: The pattern of 1-year guidance slippage is embedded in consensus — most brokers build in a 6-12 month delay to management's FCF timeline [#A]

  • SS consensus: Brokers broadly model FCF reaching £1.3-1.5bn by FY27, with bulls (e.g., those with Outperform ratings referencing infrastructure value) at the high end and bears modelling c.£1.1-1.2bn [#A]

  • Sub: Consensus FCF estimates have been revised down modestly over L12M, reflecting higher capex stay-costs and slower Enterprise stabilisation [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: BT's capex/revenue ratio (~25-28%) is elevated vs European incumbent peers (Proximus, KPN, Telefonica) at 15-22% — the FTTP intensity is genuinely higher-capex than most peers, but so is the future upgrade avoidance (no-coax-overhaul problem that Comcast faces) [#A]

  • Macro context: Higher-for-longer UK interest rates increase the cost of BT's £20bn+ net debt; every 100bps move in rates impacts interest cost by c.£100-150m on floating/refinancing tranches — a meaningful drag on FCF [#I]

  • Sub: BoE rate trajectory is thus a secondary but non-trivial FCF driver; base case assumes gradual cuts to c.3.5-4.0% by FY27 [#I]

  • Leverage: Net debt/EBITDA at c.3.4× (FY25E) vs management comfort range of 2.5-3.0×; deleveraging to comfort range requires both FCF accretion and EBITDA growth [#J]

  • Sub: Pension: IAS19 net pension liability c.£4-5bn; triennial valuation will be 2026 (next); adverse outcome could resurface as a cash drain [#J]

  • Bear case: FCF remains stuck at £1.0-1.2bn through FY27 if (a) FTTP take-up disappoints, (b) cost savings partially reinvested, (c) Enterprise revenue declines faster than modelled; in this scenario, leverage stays above 3×, dividend is at risk, and the stock de-rates toward 100-110p [#J]

  • Sub: Miss magnitude — failure to inflect FCF plausibly drives 25-35% SP downside from current levels (c.155-165p range) [#J]

  • Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — guided FCF step-up is achievable but dependent on 3-4 concurrent sub-drivers, and BT's own track record embeds a slippage risk of 12+ months [#J]

§3 — Key Driver #2: FTTP Build Trajectory and Take-Up

  • Verdict: FTTP is BT's structural reinvention asset — build coverage is on track, but take-up penetration and ARPU uplift per FTTP premises are the under-tested variables that will determine whether capital has been allocated well [#J]

  • Driver definition: BT/Openreach is building FTTP to 25m premises by Dec 2026 (previously 25m by end 2026; updated target maintained); take-up (number of homes actually subscribing to FTTP) and ARPU uplift vs legacy copper are the revenue mechanisms [#A]

  • Sub: Mechanism to EPS — FTTP premises passed generates incremental wholesale revenue for Openreach; retail FTTP ARPU is £3-5/month higher than ADSL equivalents; copper switch-off (2027+ target) eliminates stranded legacy cost [#A]

  • Company guide: BT guided 25m premises passed by end-2026; as of early FY26 reporting (FY25 full-year), build was tracking c.22-23m premises passed [#A]

  • Sub: FTTP take-up guided to reach "over 30%" of passed premises by FY26/27; current take-up is c.26-28% of passed premises (vs. Openreach's own longer-run target of 50%+) [#A]

  • SS consensus: Broadly aligned with build guidance; the controversy is on the revenue uplift timeline — bulls model ARPU uplift materialising FY26-27, bears argue wholesale pricing pressure from Ofcom limits upside [#A]

  • Sub: Named brokers vary — Barclays and BofA among constructive voices on FTTP monetisation; Citi and HSBC have flagged take-up risk relative to altnets' competitive build [#A]

  • Altnet competition: Alternative network operators (CityFibre, Nexfibre, Hyperoptic, Virgin Media O2) are building competing FTTP in overlapping geographies — Openreach's addressable footprint where it faces no competitor may be shrinking [#J]

  • Sub: Estimates suggest altnets now pass 8-10m premises with c.15-20% overlap with Openreach FTTP; in overlap areas, Openreach take-up is structurally lower [#A]

  • Recent delivery: Build velocity has averaged c.70,000-80,000 premises/week in FY25, tracking to hit 25m target; the take-up rate of 26-28% is slightly below original bull-case projections of 30%+ at this stage of build [#A]

  • Sub: ARPU uplift per FTTP sub vs ADSL sub has been £2-4/month realised vs £4-6 aspirational — modest but real [#A]

  • Copper switch-off: DCMS/Ofcom copper switch-off programme is running (pilot areas active); national switch-off by 2027 would force migration to FTTP and drive take-up; delays to this timeline are a medium-term take-up risk [#I]

  • Sub: The copper switch-off timeline is in part a government/regulatory decision — not fully in BT's control [#I]

  • Macro context: FTTP is a structurally defensive demand driver (connectivity is non-cyclical); however, altnet funding stress (several altnets facing refinancing difficulties in 2024-25) is a potential tailwind — if altnets consolidate or slow build, Openreach's overlap competitive pressure eases [#I]

  • Bear case: Take-up stalls at 25-28% if altnets sustain competitive pricing in overlap areas; ARPU uplift fails to cover incremental cost of FTTP vs copper; copper switch-off delayed beyond 2028; capex returns on FTTP prove below WACC at scale [#J]

  • Sub: Miss magnitude — if FTTP monetisation disappoints through FY27, the market may strip infrastructure premium from Openreach valuation, compressing BT's SOTP by 15-25% [#J]

  • Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — build is on track, but take-up and ARPU monetisation are at the low end of original guidance; altnet competitive intensity is a persistent and underappreciated headwind [#J]

§4 — Key Driver #3: Openreach Regulatory Environment (WFTMR / Charge Control 2026)

  • Verdict: The 2026 Openreach Charge Control review is the single highest-impact binary event in the 24-month window — adverse pricing could structurally compress Openreach EBITDA by mid-single digits with immediate permanence [#J]

  • Driver definition: Ofcom sets wholesale prices for Openreach's copper and FTTP access products under a multi-year regulatory framework (WFTMR — Wholesale Fixed Telecoms Market Review); the next charge control review is due 2026, covering the period to c.2031 [#A]

  • Sub: Mechanism to EPS — Openreach generates c.50-55% of group EBITDA; a 1% change in Openreach regulated wholesale pricing translates to c.£30-40m EBITDA impact (approximate, based on disclosed revenue structure) [#A]

  • Current framework: The WFTMR 2021-26 framework set relatively supportive pricing for FTTP deployment — Ofcom explicitly allowed a "fair bet" return on FTTP investment; key question is whether the 2026 review continues this or tightens returns post-build [#A]

  • Sub: "Fair bet" language is under regulatory scrutiny — consumer groups and CPs (competing providers) have lobbied for tighter FTTP wholesale pricing once build is largely complete [#A]

  • Company guide: BT management has publicly stated confidence in continued regulatory support for FTTP; management references Ofcom's stated FTTP investment promotion objectives as protective [#A]

  • Sub: Conservatism vs optimism — management guidance on regulatory outcomes is systematically optimistic (regulated entity bias); sceptical discount warranted [#J]

  • SS consensus: Brokers broadly model a "neutral-to-modest-positive" regulatory outcome; Deutsche Telekom's Telekom Deutschland experience (where regulatory tightening post-build surprised markets) is a cautionary peer [#A]

  • Sub: Named brokers with regulatory sensitivity analysis include Deutsche Bank and JPMorgan (referenced in public broker commentary); Barclays and Citi have both flagged this as a key swing factor [#A]

  • Track record: Ofcom has historically been more protective of infrastructure investment incentives than Continental European regulators; UK regulatory framework is broadly viewed as supportive vs France/Italy benchmarks [#A]

  • Sub: However, Ofcom is under political pressure from government on broadband affordability — the regulatory risk is asymmetric: upside limited, downside material [#J]

  • Macro/political context: UK government's "build to 25m FTTP" objective creates political incentive to maintain Ofcom's supportive stance through build completion; post-completion, political calculus may shift toward affordability [#I]

  • Sub: A Labour government with retail-price sensitivity may apply softer political pressure for tighter wholesale pricing than a Conservative predecessor — subtle risk, not consensus view [#I]

  • Bear case: Ofcom 2026 charge control (a) cuts FTTP wholesale headline pricing by 5-8%, (b) removes or narrows the "fair bet" carve-out, (c) requires greater equivalence of access for competitors; collectively this could remove £100-150m EBITDA from Openreach, reducing group EBITDA by 2-3% permanently [#J]

  • Sub: In a downside regulatory scenario, FTTP investment returns are retroactively impaired, triggering sell-side multiple compression and potentially a dividend review [#J]

⚡ RARE: Regulatory asymmetry — Openreach's regulatory event is unusual in that it is simultaneously the largest potential upside catalyst (if "fair bet" extended to new FTTP products) AND the largest potential downside risk; very few FTSE 100 stocks have a single regulatory event with this magnitude of symmetric impact within a 24-month window [Cross-ref: §4, §10]

  • Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility for a broadly neutral outcome — base case is Ofcom maintaining supportive framework, but the probability distribution has fat tails in both directions; a materially adverse outcome has c.20-25% probability [#J]

§5 — Key Driver #4: Cost Transformation and EBITDA Margin Expansion

  • Verdict: BT's £3bn gross cost savings programme (FY24-FY28) is the near-term EPS lever most in management's control, but gross/net confusion and reinvestment decisions have repeatedly made headline savings less valuable than they appear [#J]

  • Driver definition: BT has committed to removing £3bn+ gross costs from the business by FY28, including workforce reductions (targeting c.40,000-55,000 net headcount reduction vs FY23 peak), network simplification (copper retirement), and IT/digital transformation [#A]

  • Sub: Mechanism to EPS — every £100m net cost saving translates to approximately 3-4p EPS at BT's current tax rate and share count (~10bn shares) [#A]

  • Company guide: FY25 results (H1/H2 2024-25) confirmed annualised in-programme savings of c.£2bn+ achieved to date; management reaffirmed £3bn gross target for FY28 [#A]

  • Sub: Critical distinction — gross vs net: BT has historically reinvested a portion of gross savings into FTTP capex-support, digital transformation, and customer experience — net EBITDA benefit is c.50-60% of gross [#A]

  • Track record: Programme is broadly on pace on gross metrics; FY24 and H1 FY25 results showed EBITDA in line with or marginally above consensus — one of the few positive delivery signals in recent reporting history [#A]

  • Sub: Workforce reduction: BT headcount has fallen from c.130,000 (FY23) toward c.90,000 target; execution appears credible [#A]

  • SS consensus: Analysts broadly credit the cost programme but debate reinvestment rate; consensus EBITDA margin expansion forecast is c.200-300bps over FY25-FY28 (roughly in line with management) [#A]

  • Sub: The sceptical broker view (referenced in public Citi/Morgan Stanley commentary) is that network inflation and IT migration costs will consume a larger share of gross savings than management guides [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: European telecom margin expansion programmes (Proximus, KPN, DTAG) have historically delivered 60-70% of gross savings as net EBITDA; BT's guided 50-60% net conversion is towards the low end of peer range, providing limited upside surprise potential [#A]

  • Macro context: UK wage inflation (CPI-linked union agreements) adds cost pressure offsetting some gross savings; BT's CWU (Communication Workers Union) relationship historically has led to cost overshoot when redundancy/reorganisation programmes collide with collective bargaining [#I]

  • Sub: Energy costs (significant for network infrastructure) have partially normalised from 2022-23 peaks, providing a modest tailwind to gross savings delivery [#I]

  • Bear case: Gross savings achieved but reinvestment rate increases to 50%+ net; net EBITDA improvement stalls at £1.0-1.5bn rather than £1.5-2.0bn guided; margin expansion falls short by 100-150bps vs consensus [#J]

  • Sub: Miss scenario — Union industrial action or IT migration failure (BT has a complex multi-year ERP transformation running in parallel) could cause cost overshoot [#J]

  • Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — gross savings delivery is tracking; the risk is specifically on the net conversion rate and whether reinvestment obligations absorb the benefit before it reaches FCF [#J]

§6 — Key Driver #5: BT Enterprise Revenue Stabilisation

  • Verdict: Enterprise division revenue decline is a persistent multi-year drag — stabilisation has been guided as "imminent" for 3 consecutive years; each year it slips; this driver is low-conviction positive with meaningful downside risk if decline accelerates [#J]

  • Driver definition: BT Enterprise serves UK businesses (SME and corporate); revenue has been in structural decline (c.3-5% YoY) driven by legacy product sunset (ISDN, copper circuits), competitive pressure from cloud-native alternatives, and contract repricing [#A]

  • Sub: Mechanism to EPS — Enterprise is c.30% of BT group revenue; each 1% revenue decline at c.30% margin structure removes c.£30-35m EBITDA; stabilisation is worth c.£100m+ EBITDA per annum vs the current trajectory [#A]

  • Company guide: Management has repeatedly guided Enterprise revenue stabilisation by "FY25/26"; FY25 H1 results showed continued decline of c.3-4% revenue YoY — guidance timeline has slipped at least twice in L3Y [#A]

  • Sub: Management now characterises Enterprise as moving toward "managed decline plus digital transformation"; the language shift from "stabilisation" to "managed decline" is notable and bearish [#J]

  • Track record: ❌ Enterprise has not stabilised on any guided timeline; every annual report for FY22-FY25 has contained some variant of "revenue declines expected to reduce, with stabilisation in the near term" — a credibility-destroying pattern [#J]

  • SS consensus: Sell-side is broadly sceptical on near-term stabilisation; most brokers model c.2-3% decline through FY26, with some modelling flat-to-slightly-negative even in FY27 [#A]

  • Sub: Brokers with more negative Enterprise views (Barclays, Morgan Stanley per public commentary) model a longer tail of decline that meaningfully pressures group EBITDA [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: No cohort peer data available; however, European incumbent enterprise divisions (Deutsche Telekom Germany Business, Orange Enterprise) have all experienced similar legacy product sunset headwinds — typical stabilisation took 4-6 years from ISDN/copper initiation [#A]

  • Product mix shift: BT Enterprise's shift to managed services and security (BT Security, cloud connectivity) is the intended offset; however, managed services margins are lower than legacy circuit margins, so revenue stabilisation may not mean EBITDA stabilisation [#J]

  • Macro context: UK SME capex intentions are soft (BoE surveys show SME investment intentions below trend); a UK recession or prolonged stagnation would accelerate enterprise churn and delay managed services growth [#I]

  • Bear case: Enterprise revenue decline accelerates to 5-6% pa through FY27 if managed services growth disappoints and legacy sunset accelerates; this scenario removes an incremental £100-150m EBITDA vs consensus [#J]

  • Sub: Combined with any cost programme underdelivery (Driver #4), Enterprise acceleration could produce a 20-25% group EBITDA miss vs current consensus [#J]

  • Plausibility verdict: Medium-low plausibility for stabilisation within 24 months — the guidance track record is dismal; stabilisation in FY26 is possible but requires managed services inflection that is not yet visible in the data [#J]

§7 — Key Driver #6: Consumer ARPU and Pricing Power

  • Verdict: Consumer ARPU has been a positive surprise in FY23-FY24 via CPI-linked price indexation, but regulatory and political pressure on indexation is building; this driver may be in terminal deceleration by FY26 [#J]

  • Driver definition: BT Consumer (EE mobile + BT broadband) drives ARPU via annual price increases, upselling to higher-tier products, and FTTP premium capture; the CPI+3.9% annual price increase mechanism has been a major earnings support [#A]

  • Sub: Mechanism to EPS — each 1% ARPU improvement across c.10m consumer broadband/mobile subs equates to c.£100-120m annual revenue uplift at high incremental margin [#A]

  • Company guide: BT Consumer guided mid-single-digit revenue growth for FY25; reported c.4-5% growth in H1 FY25, largely price-index driven; management confident in ARPU trajectory [#A]

  • Sub: Importantly — Ofcom and CMA have opened investigations into mid-contract price increases across telecoms; BT and EE are directly in scope [#I]

  • Regulatory risk on indexation: CMA provisional findings (published 2024) recommended phasing out CPI-linked mid-contract increases by 2025-26; if implemented, BT loses c.£200-300m pa of annualised ARPU benefit — a material EPS headwind not fully priced in consensus [#J]

  • Sub: BT management has pushed back publicly on CMA findings; a negotiated outcome (annual increases capped at CPI flat, no additive percentage) is the current expected scenario [#A]

⚡ RARE: CMA indexation risk is underweighted in consensus — public broker notes broadly assume CPI+ indexation continues in modified form; the CMA's stated preference for pure CPI-cap (zero additive) would reduce BT Consumer revenue growth by c.3-4% pa vs consensus, a magnitude that is not in the central scenario of any broker I can identify [Cross-ref: §7, §10]

  • EE mobile: EE is the UK's largest mobile network by subscribers; mobile ARPU has been recovering post-pandemic; 5G upsell provides a modest ARPU lever (premium 5G plans £3-5/month above 4G equivalents) [#A]

  • Sub: UK mobile market remains rationally competitive (4 MNOs post-consolidation); no indication of pricing war, but MVNO growth (Sky Mobile, Tesco Mobile) caps the upside on premium pricing [#I]

  • Recent delivery: Consumer revenue growth of c.4-5% in H1 FY25 was in line with consensus; churn has been controlled; FTTP premium uptake tracking management expectations at low end [#A]

  • Bear case: CMA enforces pure CPI-cap on mid-contract increases from FY26; BT Consumer revenue growth drops to 1-2% pa; combined with mobile competition, ARPU momentum reverses; this scenario removes c.£200m+ EBITDA vs current consensus and pressures FCF [#J]

  • Sub: This scenario is directly correlated with Driver #1 (FCF inflection) — ARPU headwind delays FCF normalisation by 12-18 months [#J]

  • Plausibility verdict: High plausibility near-term (FY25-26 ARPU delivery is visible) but medium plausibility over the full 24-month window given CMA/Ofcom regulatory pressure on the indexation mechanism [#J]

§8 — Key Driver #7: Non-Core Disposals and Portfolio Simplification

  • Verdict: Non-core asset disposals are optionality positive with real execution risk — BT Global (sold), BT Sport (JV with Warner Bros Discovery), and potential future Openreach partial monetisation are live levers; magnitude is significant but timing is uncertain [#J]

  • Driver definition: BT has been simplifying its portfolio: sold BT Global (2023), created a JV with Warner Bros Discovery for BT Sport/TNT Sports; remaining potential levers include further international assets and the long-debated Openreach infrastructure monetisation [#A]

  • Sub: Mechanism to SP — disposals at premium multiples above carrying values accrete NAV; proceeds reduce net debt; SOTP uplift if Openreach is valued on infrastructure multiple [#A]

  • BT Sport JV: The TNT Sports JV (BT Sport + Eurosport merged into a JV with Warner Bros Discovery) was completed 2023; BT retains a 50% stake in the JV; the strategic outcome is modestly positive (sport cost removed from P&L) but cash benefit has been limited [#A]

  • Sub: JV profitability has been under pressure from Warner Bros Discovery's broader financial restructuring; BT may seek to monetise its 50% stake if an acceptable offer emerges [#A]

  • Openreach infrastructure monetisation: Partial IPO or infrastructure fund stake sale in Openreach has been periodically discussed; SOTP analysis by brokers suggests Openreach could be valued at £20-25bn as a standalone infrastructure asset vs BT's current market cap of c.£15bn [#A]

  • Sub: BT management has consistently rebuffed Openreach partial monetisation under current structure, citing regulatory complications and FTTP investment rationale; this lever is optionality, not base case [#J]

  • Track record: BT Global disposal was executed cleanly; BT Sport JV was strategically sound but financial benefits have been modest vs original expectations; disposal execution is credible but value realisation has been below initial market expectations [#A]

  • SS consensus: Brokers broadly include modest disposal/simplification value in BT SOTP models; Openreach infrastructure value is a consistent "upside case" item rather than base case [#A]

  • Sub: Deutsche Bank and Jefferies have historically flagged Openreach SOTP uplift as a re-rating catalyst; current consensus is not pricing this in base-case models [#A]

  • Macro context: Infrastructure fund appetite for telecom assets has cooled since 2022 (rate-driven decompression of infrastructure multiples); the window for Openreach monetisation at peak multiples has probably closed; if rates normalise lower, a new window may open FY27+ [#I]

  • Bear case: No further material disposals; BT Sport JV generates no cash for BT; Openreach monetisation remains off the table; the disposal driver reduces to background noise with minimal 24M impact [#J]

  • Sub: Risk of being stuck with a 50% stake in a loss-making sports JV if Warner Bros Discovery faces financial distress is a low-probability but non-trivial tail risk [#J]

  • Plausibility verdict: High plausibility for incremental portfolio simplification (minor disposals); low-to-medium plausibility for a value-accretive Openreach monetisation in the 24M window [#J]

§10 — Driver Synthesis + Sceptical Synthesis (Cross-Cutting)

  • Cross-cutting verdict: The strongest bearish case for BT is a correlated multi-driver failure scenario where the three biggest variables (FCF inflection, regulatory environment, Consumer ARPU) all disappoint simultaneously — a plausible scenario, not a tail event [#J]

  • Driver dependency: Drivers 1, 2, 3, 4, and 6 are materially inter-dependent — not independent [#J]

  • Sub: FTTP take-up (Driver #2) directly feeds Openreach revenue, which feeds FCF (Driver #1)
  • Sub: Regulatory outcome (Driver #3) sets the wholesale pricing ceiling that determines whether FTTP capex generates adequate returns — it preconditions Driver #2's revenue realisation

  • Sub: Consumer ARPU (Driver #6) and CMA outcome feed directly into Consumer EBITDA, which is the second-largest FCF contributor after Openreach

  • Correlation across drivers: Four of seven drivers have a common regulatory nexus — Ofcom/CMA actions can simultaneously impair FTTP wholesale pricing, Consumer ARPU indexation, and Enterprise service pricing; this is a high-correlation cluster [#J]

  • Sub: Only Driver #7 (disposals) and Driver #5 (Enterprise) are largely independent of the regulatory nexus; Driver #4 (cost transformation) is partially insulated but not fully

  • Cumulative plausibility: Applying rough independent probabilities (2× high at ~80%, 3× medium at ~60%, 2× medium-low at ~45%), joint probability of ALL drivers delivering is c.80% × 80% × 60% × 60% × 60% × 45% × 45% ≈ 7-9% — illustrating why multi-variable turnarounds are structurally difficult [#J]
