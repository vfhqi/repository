EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (BLUF)

Telecom Plus operates a capital-light multi-utility reseller model that bundles energy, broadband, mobile, and insurance services onto a single billing platform for UK domestic households, leveraging a unique word-of-mouth partner distribution network to deliver structurally superior customer lifetime values. The business consistently generates returns on capital employed exceeding thirty percent, significantly outperforming asset-heavy infrastructure peers, driven by its ability to support multiple revenue streams through one central back office. However, the foundational bundling thesis faces mounting pressure as organic service take-up per net new customer has declined from two point nine to one point eight over recent periods, forcing increased reliance on low-margin mobile additions and inorganic customer book acquisitions to sustain growth momentum.

Business Model and Structural Position

Telecom Plus operates as a pure-play reseller without owning capital-intensive physical infrastructure, shielding the company from direct infrastructure capital expenditure risks. The business remains strictly concentrated on the UK domestic household market, addressing a fifty-billion-pound opportunity across approximately thirty million domestic households, with market share under three percent per service leaving substantial organic growth runway largely untapped. The company enjoys a major cost advantage by supporting up to five distinct services per customer with one central back office, generating unique cost efficiencies unavailable to single-

service competitors. Recent operational delivery included ten point three percent organic customer growth in the year ending March 2026, reaching one point four three million total customers and three point eight million total services provided. Customer growth was accelerated by acquiring and migrating one hundred ninety-three thousand TalkTalk broadband customers, with successful cross-sell efforts upgrading fourteen point five thousand acquired customers to additional services.

Distribution Network and Customer Economics

Customer acquisition relies entirely on a national network of over seventy-seven thousand active and registered independent Partners who operate under a word-of-mouth model that avoids expensive traditional brand advertising and marketing campaigns. Distributor commission is structured almost entirely as variable cost tied directly to customer sign-ups, with Partners receiving initial payments upon successful service migration and ongoing monthly trailing commissions based on active bill payments, while forgoing residuals if referred customers default or churn prematurely. Multi-service bundling extends customer lifetime from under five years to over fifteen years when households switch all three core services simultaneously, creating high structural switching costs that drive superior unit economics. However, Deutsche Bank analysis indicates that core service take-up per net new customer fell from two point nine to one point eight over three years, dropping further to one point two in the first half of financial year 2026, with mobile growing to ninety-seven percent of organic service adds despite carrying lower unit gross profit.

Industry Structure and Competitive Dynamics

The UK domestic energy market is heavily dominated by six large suppliers controlling approximately ninety-one percent of the dual-fuel customer market. E.ON Next is acquiring OVO Energy for a reported five hundred fifty million pounds, with the merged entity capturing a market-leading twenty-six percent share with nine point six million customers, consolidating back-office infrastructure and customer service centers to drive cost savings.

The UK fixed broadband sector is experiencing slower organic growth as it reaches structural saturation, though Openreach reached twenty-three million premises with full fiber while recording thirty-eight percent take- up. Alternative networks are actively building competing fiber footprints, with CityFibre reaching seven hundred thirty thousand customers across four point six million passed premises, though smaller overbuilders face severe financial pressure from rising interest rates and high capital requirements.

Value Chain Position and Upstream Dependencies

Telecom Plus secures electricity and gas wholesale supply through a long-standing agreement with E.ON until December 2033, with standard variable retail tariffs structurally tied to quarterly Ofgem price cap limits, while E.ON remains fully responsible for energy volume procurement and executing market hedging strategies that completely protect the company from short-term commodity price spikes. Under the contract, E.ON funds the principal seasonal working capital requirements for energy retail customers, preventing cash deficits from customers paying in equal monthly instalments while consuming more in winter. A strategic contract refinement in October 2023 closely matched payment costs to actual Ofgem price cap components, replacing legacy proxy percentage discounts with highly accurate cost credits, though accounting phasing shifts temporarily compressed gross margins to twenty-one point two percent in first half financial year 2026. Telecom Plus resells mobile services by leveraging BT's EE national network infrastructure and sources fixed-line broadband from Openreach and CityFibre, with telecom networks actively supporting the reseller model because it delivers highly incremental customer volumes by reaching disengaged consumers who typically do not buy through price comparison sites. 23

Customer Quality and Retention Challenges

Telecom Plus targets creditworthy homeowners with above-average incomes relative to the broader UK population, delivering lower default rates and bad debt while homeowners typically consume more gas and electricity than tenants. The partner-led referral model naturally filters out less creditworthy customer segments, as Partners only earn commissions if their referred customers consistently pay bills, with personal face-to-face contact avoiding the high bad debt typical of price comparison site channels. However, competitive fixed energy tariffs pushed customer churn to fourteen point two percent in financial year 2026, compared to thirteen point seven percent in financial year 2025 and eight point seven percent in financial year 2024, underperforming historic benchmarks. Organic customer growth slowed from fourteen percent in financial year 2024 to nine percent in financial year 2026, with total customer numbers supported by migrating broadband customers from TalkTalk. 29

Structural Threats and Long-Term Evolution

The development of localized generation and distribution technology may trigger disruptive peer-to-peer energy trading, reducing aggregate energy volume sourced from nationwide utility networks with the group's long-term energy sales results potentially adversely affected. The building of local monopoly fiber networks represents a notable threat to broadband reseller economics, as Telecom Plus could find its physical connection access structurally limited in specific geographic pockets, restricting the reseller's capacity to compete effectively for local subscribers. Software innovations are reducing the proprietary systems advantage historically held by integrated resellers, with Kraken Technologies licensing its utility operating software to manage over seventy million customer accounts globally, including large rival energy retailers such as EDF and E.ON Next, while successfully extending into broadband and water billing capabilities. The UK energy retail landscape is evolving toward zero-margin commodity supply, with baseline energy provision increasingly serving as a customer acquisition gateway for secondary services while retail pricing for new customers remains highly competitive to capture long-term transition value.

Strategic Outlook and Key Risks

Telecom Plus is targeting the UK micro-business sector comprising approximately five point three million firms as a natural extension since small business owners prefer using their residential providers, while expanding deeper into financial services including motor cover or mortgages provides high-margin growth potential though constrained by consumer comfort with consolidating multiple financial liabilities. While the multi-utility model should mechanically drive improving financial outputs as customer density scales, organic service take-up per net new customer has steadily trended downward from two point nine to one point two, with mobile service additions representing ninety-seven percent of organic adds carrying lower unit gross profits. The company increasingly relies on inorganic customer book acquisitions like TalkTalk to maintain growth, with single-service broadband customers representing significantly lower lifetime values than organic multi-service customers, suggesting that wider margins require active management intervention rather than occurring via mechanical gravitational forces. The central investment risk remains whether management can reverse service take-up dilution and restore bundling economics before competitive intensity and software commoditization permanently erode the reseller's structural cost advantage.
