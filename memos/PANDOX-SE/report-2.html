Pandox AB: Transformational Growth Through Strategic M&A Reshapes European Hotel Property Leader
Key Takeaways

Historic Dalata acquisition fundamentally transforms scale and geography: The EUR 1.7 billion Dalata Hotel Group acquisition represents Pandox's largest-ever transaction, adding 31 investment properties across the UK and Ireland and pivoting the portfolio's center of gravity decisively away from the Nordics toward higher-RevPAR markets.

Sell-side earnings upgrades driven by acquisition accretion and operational strength: Analysts have upgraded 2026 cash earnings per share estimates by approximately 20%, primarily reflecting the massive income contribution from Dalata (SEK 1.2 billion in annual rental income) combined with stronger-than-expected margins in the Own Operations segment and more favorable interest cost assumptions.

Aggressive leverage deployment remains within policy limits but marks major shift: Net interest-bearing debt surged from SEK 34.4 billion to SEK 48.3 billion in a single year, pushing the LTV ratio from 45.2% to 52.7%, representing a substantial utilization of previously untapped balance sheet capacity to fund inorganic growth while remaining comfortably within the company's 45-60% target range.

Complex operational carve-out with Scandic demonstrates sophisticated M&A execution: The Dalata transaction involves a multi-stage separation where Scandic will acquire the operating platform for EUR 500 million while Pandox retains the real estate under new long-term, revenue-based leases, showcasing management's ability to execute highly complex, cross-border restructuring transactions.

Expanded leadership team supports heightened operational complexity: Key executive additions including Mikael Hultqvist (Property Management), Joakim Andersson (Treasury), and Jacob Rasin (Transactions) to Group Management formalize critical functions specifically designed to support aggressive M&A growth and the integration of the massively expanded portfolio.

Regulatory and ESG pressures accelerate capital deployment toward sustainability: The EU Taxonomy, Energy Performance of Buildings Directive, and Science Based Targets initiative validation are systematically forcing Pandox to deploy substantial capex (including a EUR 29 million climate transition program) to retrofit older properties and avoid regulatory obsolescence and "brown discounts."

Structural supply constraints and normalized demand create favorable operating backdrop: European hotel supply growth remains constrained at less than 1% annually, sitting roughly 10% below historical averages, while demand has normalized into a steady 3-6% RevPAR growth trajectory in Pandox's core markets, creating a durable tailwind for pricing power and occupancy rates.

Brief company descriptions

Pandox AB is a leading European hotel property company founded in 1995 and listed on the Nasdaq Stockholm Large Cap exchange.

The company exclusively invests in hotel properties, owning one of the largest hotel property portfolios in Europe.

As of year-end 2025, Pandox's portfolio encompasses 193 hotel properties with approximately 42,761 rooms.

The total market value of Pandox's property portfolio stands at approximately SEK 91.7 billion at the end of 2025.

Pandox operates across 11 countries, primarily maintaining a strong geographical focus on Northern Europe.

The United Kingdom represents Pandox's single largest geographical market, accounting for 25 percent of the total portfolio market value.

Germany and Sweden represent the next largest markets, each accounting for 18 percent of the portfolio's market value.

Ireland has grown significantly in prominence, representing 14 percent of the portfolio following recent strategic acquisitions.

Pandox's business model is divided into two mutually supportive operating segments: Leases and Own Operations.

The Leases segment forms the core of the business, representing approximately 84 percent of the total portfolio market value.

Within the Leases segment, Pandox owns hotel properties and leases them to skilled external hotel operators.

These leases are characteristically long-term and revenue-based, often incorporating guaranteed minimum rent levels to provide downside protection.

Revenue-based leases align the incentives of Pandox and its tenants, sharing both the risks and the upside of hotel performance.

The Own Operations segment involves Pandox acting as both the property owner and the hotel operator.

Own Operations represent approximately 16 percent of the total portfolio market value.

This segment serves as a strategic transformation tool, allowing Pandox to acquire underperforming assets and reposition them rapidly.

Once a hotel in the Own Operations segment is successfully repositioned, Pandox often seeks to lease it to an external operator.

Pandox generates its revenue primarily through rental income from its leased properties and direct operational revenue from its owned hotels.

The company has demonstrated consistent revenue growth over the past three years, with total revenues reaching SEK 7,469 million in 2025.

Over the next three years, Pandox's footprint will be heavily influenced by the massive integration of the Dalata Hotel Group portfolio.

The Dalata acquisition added 31 investment properties across Ireland and the UK, significantly boosting Pandox's exposure to these higher-RevPAR markets.

Pandox plans to divest the Dalata hotel operating platform to Scandic in 2026, transitioning these properties firmly into the Leases segment.

Looking ahead, DNB Carnegie expects Pandox's strong cash flow generation and wide geographical footprint to enable continued activity in the M&A market.

Pandox's portfolio predominantly targets the upper mid-market segment, focusing on hotels with strong domestic and regional demand.

The company's tenant base includes major operators such as Scandic, Fattal, Leonardo, and NH Hotel Group.

Degree of Change: OrangeThe core dual business model encompassing Leases and Own Operations remains structurally stable, but the geographical and scale mix is undergoing a significant transformation. The historic acquisition of Dalata Hotel Group has massively pivoted the portfolio's weight toward the UK and Ireland, which now collectively represent a much larger share of the company's asset base. While the underlying strategy of acquiring, repositioning through Own Operations, and ultimately transitioning assets to revenue-based Leases persists, the sheer magnitude of the recent capital deployment and the impending operational handover to Scandic marks a substantial evolutionary step for the company's footprint over the next three years. A higher rating would be warranted if Pandox fundamentally altered its revenue-based lease structure or expanded outside of Northern Europe, while a lower rating would apply if the company paused its M&A agenda entirely.

Key components summary

Leadership transitions and strategic hires: The company's leadership team has undergone targeted restructuring over the last 12 to 18 months to strengthen property management and commercial execution. Most notably, Mikael Hultqvist was appointed as the new Senior Vice President of Property Management. This appointment followed the departure of Erik Möller, who left the company to pursue external opportunities.

Expanded commercial responsibilities: Concurrently, internal promotions have been utilized to broaden the commercial focus of the executive team. Jonas Törner, who served as acting Head of Property Management in the Nordics, was officially appointed as Chief Commercial Officer to coordinate commercial matters across business areas. Furthermore, Mattias Bernunger was elevated to Senior Vice President of Asset Management for the Group.

Historic M&A transformation via Dalata Hotel Group: Pandox executed its largest acquisition to date by purchasing Dalata Hotel Group for an enterprise value of approximately EUR 1.7 billion. This transaction added 31 investment properties across the United Kingdom and Ireland to the company's portfolio. This acquisition is expected to contribute significantly to the company's long-term earnings, adding an estimated SEK 1.2 billion in rental income annually.

Strategic separation of Dalata's operations: A critical component of the Dalata transaction is the planned divestment of its hotel operating platform to Scandic Hotels Group for EUR 500 million. During the ongoing separation phase, Scandic is managing the operations of all 56 Dalata hotels. Once finalized in the second half of 2026, Pandox will retain the 31 real estate assets under long-term, revenue-based leases with Scandic.

Morgan Stanley's view on the Dalata integration: Analysts view the Dalata acquisition positively for the broader ecosystem, noting that the well-invested, upscale portfolio skews toward higher-RevPAR markets like the UK and Ireland. This transaction allows Pandox to capitalize on structurally higher occupancy and average daily rates compared to standard Nordic properties. The underlying real estate assets were acquired at an implied value of roughly SEK 13.7 billion, yielding approximately 8.40 percent.

Continued bolt-on acquisitions in Northern Europe: Beyond Dalata, the company has remained highly active in acquiring strategic assets over the last 18 months. Notable additions include the Radisson Blu Hotel Tromso in Norway for MNOK 750 and the Elite Hotel Frost Kiruna in Sweden for SEK 347 million. These purchases deepen Pandox's exposure to the rapidly growing northern Scandinavian leisure destinations.

Expansion in international target markets: The company has also executed targeted acquisitions in broader international markets. Recent deals include the purchase of the DoubleTree by Hilton in Montreal for CAD 80 million and the DoubleTree by Hilton Edinburgh City Centre for GBP 49 million. Additionally, Pandox acquired the Hotel Pullman Cologne for EUR 66 million, expanding its footprint in the resilient German market.

Active capital recycling and divestments: To fund high-yield acquisitions and optimize the portfolio, Pandox has selectively divested non-core assets. The company successfully divested the Quality Hotel Winn in Gothenburg and Scandic Imatra during the third quarter of 2025. Furthermore, the Crowne Plaza Antwerp was sold in February 2026 for approximately EUR 19 million to free up capital for higher-return investments.

Shifts in balance sheet leverage: The aggressive M&A agenda has fundamentally altered the company's financial profile, driving net interest-bearing debt to SEK 48.3 billion at the end of 2025. The net loan-to-value ratio increased from 45.2 percent to 52.7 percent year-over-year. Management maintains that this remains comfortably within the company's stated financial policy range of 45 to 60 percent.

DNB Carnegie's perspective on financial profile: Sell-side analysts expect Pandox to be less net-acquisitive in the short term as it digests the Dalata deal. The current LTV of 53 percent sits near the midpoint of the company's policy range, leaving adequate headroom. The reported yield requirement of 6.4 percent creates a healthy yield spread over the company's funding costs.

Refinancing execution and credit margins: Pandox has actively managed its debt maturity profile, completing new financings and refinancings totaling nearly SEK 20 billion over the last 12 months. The average interest rate on debt declined slightly to 3.9 percent at the end of 2025. This improvement was driven by a constructive financing climate and gradually lower credit margins from banking partners.

ments to shareholder returns: Despite the substantial capital outlay for acquisitions, the company remains committed to rewarding shareholders. The Board of Directors proposed a dividend increase to SEK 4.50 per share for 2025, up from SEK 4.25 in the previous year. This aligns with the corporate financial policy of distributing 30 to 50 percent of cash earnings.

Demand resilience amid tough comparisons: Revenue growth for comparable units has remained positive, though growth rates have normalized from the post-pandemic surge. During the summer of 2025, the company faced exceptionally tough comparative figures due to major 2024 events like the UEFA European Championship in Germany and Taylor Swift's Eras Tour. Nevertheless, demand proved stable, driven by an active leisure segment and a recovering business travel market.

DNB Carnegie's view on earnings upgrades: Sell-side analysts have upgraded their estimates for the company's cash earnings per share by roughly 3 percent for the 2026-2028 period. These upgrades are driven by stronger-than-expected profitability in the Own Operations segment and more favorable assumptions regarding financial costs. Analysts project that the Dalata acquisition will act as a major catalyst for 20% year-over-year cash earnings growth in 2026.

Evolution of sustainability goals: The company has firmly embedded sustainability into its corporate targets, receiving validation from the Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi). The core objective is to reduce Scope 1 and 2 greenhouse gas emissions by 42 percent, and Scope 3 emissions by 25 percent, by 2030. To achieve this, the Board approved a EUR 29 million climate transition program targeting energy efficiency across multiple properties.

Disruption from alternative accommodations: The company acknowledges ongoing competitive threats from disruptive business models, including online travel agencies (OTAs) and home-sharing platforms like Airbnb. To mitigate this disintermediation risk, Pandox relies on its diversified portfolio of over 30 distinct hotel brands and operators. The company also utilizes a Group-wide Revenue Management Centre to optimize pricing and distribution strategies against digital disruptors.

Technological disruption and AI: Industry research commissioned by Pandox suggests that artificial intelligence and digitalization will drastically alter hotel operations by 2055. However, the company views this disruption as an enabler rather than a replacement for human capital. Management believes that automating administrative tasks will free up resources to focus on personalized guest experiences and premium service delivery.

Tenant insolvency risks: Supply chain and tenant risks occasionally materialize, demonstrating the vulnerability of certain operators to cost inflation. In January 2026, Pandox disclosed that one of its German tenants, Revo Hospitality Group, entered self-administration under German insolvency law. The company maintains that the financial impact will be limited, as it holds bank guarantees covering one year's rent and is already exploring alternative options for the affected properties.

Macroeconomic tailwinds: The broader macroeconomic landscape is shifting in favor of the hotel real estate sector. Decreasing inflation, stable interest rates, and accelerated economic growth across Europe are fostering a conducive environment for both business and leisure travel. Management expects these macro factors to sustain robust consumer willingness to pay for experiential travel throughout 2026.

Morgan Stanley's view on macroeconomic supply dynamics: Analysts note that European hotel supply growth remains constrained at less than 1 percent, with total market supply only sitting roughly 5 percent above 2019 levels. This subdued construction pipeline serves as a significant macroeconomic tailwind for existing property owners. The lack of new supply limits competition and structurally supports continued average daily rate growth across the continent.

Currency translation headwinds: Despite strong underlying operational performance, the company faces macroeconomic headwinds from foreign exchange volatility. A sustained strengthening of the Swedish Krona against the Euro and the British Pound has mechanically depressed reported revenue and asset values. This translation effect masked the true magnitude of the 5 percent like-for-like revenue growth achieved in fixed currency terms during late 2025.

Jefferies' view on industry deal flow: Sell-side analysis indicates a broader macroeconomic recovery in the European hotel transaction market. Hotel investment volumes rebounded strongly to exceed EUR 27 billion in 2025, representing the highest level of liquidity since the pre-pandemic era. This active transactional environment validates the high valuations of premium hotel portfolios and provides property owners with favorable conditions for asset recycling.

Change in leadership

CEO Liia Nõu has provided stable and continuous leadership since assuming the permanent chief executive role in August 2021.

Nõu's extensive tenure at Pandox, which includes serving as the Chief Financial Officer from 2007 until her promotion, ensures deep institutional knowledge across the company's operations.

Reflecting her growing external profile and operational expertise, Nõu was proposed as a new non-executive board member for the Swedish gaming and entertainment company Modern Times Group in 2025.

CFO Anneli Lindblom, who joined the company in 2021, has notably shifted the finance department's operational focus over the past 12 to 18 months.

Lindblom describes the modern financial leadership role as increasingly "visionary," requiring a pivot away from traditional, backward-looking manual accounting toward forward-looking strategic planning.

The massive and highly complex acquisition of the Dalata Hotel Group in 2025 profoundly challenged the CFO to adapt to new cross-border collaborations and complex carve-out processes.

To handle the company's expanding scale and developmental ambitions, Pandox appointed Mikael Hultqvist as the new Senior Vice President of Property Management in December 2024.

Hultqvist, who officially joins the executive management team by June 2025, brings extensive construction and real estate experience from his previous role as CEO of Serneke Sverige.

Chief Executive Liia Nõu emphasized that Hultqvist's combined technical and commercial expertise will be instrumental in driving profitable property development across the portfolio.

Further expanding the executive layer, Pandox elevated Joakim Andersson to Group Management as Senior Vice President of Treasury in February 2026.

Andersson's promotion reflects the critical strategic importance of maintaining a stable capital structure and managing relationships with European banks amid aggressive acquisition activity.

Concurrently, Jacob Rasin was also appointed to Group Management as Senior Vice President of Transactions.

Rasin's elevation officially recognizes the Transactions team's central role as a primary driver of the company's growth journey and portfolio optimization over the last decade.

Investor Relations continues to be led by Anders Berg, who serves as the Senior Vice President of Communications and IR.

At the board level, the 2025 Annual General Meeting saw the departure of Ann-Sofi Danielsson, who declined re-election to the Board of Directors.

For the 2026 Annual General Meeting, the Nomination Committee proposed the election of Linda Eriksson as a new board member to add fresh commercial perspectives.

Eriksson brings highly relevant hospitality experience from her previous roles at Nordic Hotels & Resorts (Strawberry) and Scandic Hotels.

Veteran Chairman Christian Ringnes remains a stabilizing force, having been proposed for re-election alongside Vice Chair Bengt Kjell for the 2026 term.

Internally, Pandox undertook comprehensive initiatives throughout 2024 and 2025 to formalize its corporate culture and leadership principles.

Management mapped historical strengths and weaknesses to define five core values: Collaboration, Curiosity, Courage, Competence, and Clarity.

These newly codified values were rolled out as the central theme during a company-wide kick-off event held in the Faroe Islands during the autumn of 2025.

The cultural framework promotes a "hands-on, pragmatic and results-oriented" environment that empowers employees to act without relying on rigid corporate hierarchies.

In tandem with core values, the company established five actionable leadership principles designed to foster inclusive leadership and continuous improvement.

Pandox maintains a remarkably balanced workforce, with the total employee base consisting of 51 percent women and 49 percent men in 2024.

Gender representation within the executive management team has remained stable over the past year, comprising 30 percent women.

The company actively rolls out unconscious bias and inclusive leadership training to general managers and department heads to support its diversity strategy.

To align management incentives with shareholder returns, the board proposed extending the "LTIP 2026" performance-based cash incentive program.

This long-term incentive program targets up to 17 key executives and links payouts to total share return and the reduction of the net asset value discount.

Change in strategy/structure

Pandox AB's foundational corporate strategy revolves around owning large hotel properties in strategic locations and leasing them to strong operators under long-term revenue-based leases.

The Own Operations segment serves as a deliberate strategic transformation tool, allowing the company to acquire underperforming assets, reposition them, and eventually sign new leases or divest them once value has been created.

Management describes disciplined capital allocation as the absolute heart of its value creation engine, focusing stringently on expected returns rather than rigid geographical targets.

The company positions itself as a net buyer over time, aggressively utilizing M&A to generate immediate revenue growth while laying the foundation for profitable joint investments with tenants.

The most transformative structural shift over the last 12 months is the historic acquisition of the Dalata Hotel Group, valuing the target's share capital at approximately EUR 1.4 billion.

This transaction drastically scales the company's footprint in the United Kingdom and Ireland, which management notes represents the fifth-largest hotel market in Europe by total guest nights.

To maintain its strategic focus purely on property ownership, Pandox structured the Dalata deal alongside its long-standing operational partner, Scandic Hotels Group.

The structural integration plan involves separating Dalata into a property-owning entity and an operating entity, with Scandic slated to acquire the entire operating platform of 56 hotels for EUR 500 million.

During the ongoing separation phase, which is expected to officially conclude in the second half of 2026, Scandic is managing all 56 Dalata hotels under a temporary management agreement.

Following the final carve-out, Pandox will permanently retain 31 high-quality freehold investment properties, seamlessly integrating them into its core Leases segment.

These 31 retained properties will be operated by Scandic under new long-term, revenue-based leases that include guaranteed minimum rent levels, perfectly aligning with Pandox's traditional risk-sharing business model.

Morgan Stanley notes that the Dalata portfolio skews heavily toward structurally higher RevPAR markets than the Nordics, providing Pandox with significant income diversification and robust new avenues for growth.

Management highlighted to investors that the Dalata properties were acquired at an implied yield of approximately 8.40 percent, generating a massive tentative value uplift of approximately SEK 3.4 billion.

DNB Carnegie analysts estimate that the Dalata acquisition will serve as a massive structural catalyst, projecting it to boost Pandox's adjusted cash earnings per share by roughly 20 percent in 2026.

While highly acquisitive, Pandox explicitly views asset sales as a crucial strategic tool to free up capital, continuously evaluating the portfolio to recycle funds into higher-yielding opportunities.

Demonstrating this strict capital discipline, the company sold the Crowne Plaza Antwerp for EUR 19 million, specifically citing limited long-term development opportunities due to an expiring land lease.

Further streamlining the portfolio, Pandox completed the divestment of the DoubleTree by Hilton Montreal for CAD 80 million, effectively reallocating capital away from non-core North American assets.

Management also recently disposed of smaller, lower-value properties such as the Quality Hotel Winn in Gothenburg and Scandic Imatra to continuously optimize long-term return potential.

Looking ahead to 2026, the company has clearly communicated to investors that it expects to be increasingly active in divesting additional non-core properties, particularly within the mature Nordic region.

Management views the current macroeconomic environment as highly favorable for transactions, signaling an intent to utilize strong liquidity to keep the acquisition wheels spinning faster.

Jefferies highlights that Pandox's proactive M&A strategy has historically allowed it to gain significant market share and navigate competitive pressures successfully, noting its successful track record of partnering with operators for expansion.

Degree of Change: RedThe degree of change in Pandox's strategy and structure is profound, driven almost entirely by the historic, multi-billion-euro acquisition of the Dalata Hotel Group. This transaction fundamentally alters the company's geographic weighting by aggressively expanding its footprint into the UK and Ireland, decisively pivoting the structural center of gravity away from the Nordics. Furthermore, the complex, multi-stage carve-out strategy—involving a temporary management phase followed by a massive EUR 500 million operational spin-off to Scandic—demonstrates a highly sophisticated evolution in how the company executes M&A. Coupled with an active capital recycling program that is actively shedding non-core assets across Europe and North America, the corporate structure is undergoing a rapid, high-impact transformation. A lower rating would only be appropriate if the company was merely executing bolt-on acquisitions, but the sheer scale of the Dalata deal warrants the highest degree of change rating.

Change in priorities/ambition

Pandox's overarching corporate ambition is clearly articulated by CEO Liia Nõu, who emphatically defines the organization as a "company driven to succeed" with a paramount focus on financial strength.

The executive team places a heavy emphasis on maintaining a "high business tempo" as a core operational priority.

This intense operational pace was highly visible throughout 2024 and 2025, a period characterized by continuous strategic transactions, new share issues, and active business development across the existing portfolio.

Looking forward, management's ambition is to actively accelerate this growth trajectory, with the CEO stating during an earnings call that the goal is to "keep the wheels spinning quicker and quicker."

The company has adopted an aggressive, opportunistic posture regarding market consolidation, with leadership explicitly telling investors that they are "on the hunt" for value-accretive acquisitions despite broader macroeconomic uncertainties.

A critical priority in Pandox's investment philosophy is the willingness to embrace operational difficulty to generate alpha.

The CEO noted that the company specifically prefers to "buy things that are a little bit complicated," as this complexity deters competition and allows Pandox to utilize its operational expertise to drive outsized returns.

In tandem with embracing complexity, management views "deal certainty" and the company's strong reputation for successfully closing difficult transactions as a primary competitive edge that must be prioritized and protected.

While prioritizing rapid expansion, leadership also recognizes the need for balanced intensity.

The CEO reflected in the 2025 Annual Report that while the team likes to "work at a fast pace and challenge ourselves," the greatest corporate insights often emerge during deliberate moments of reflection rather than from blindly chasing financial figures.

Culturally, Pandox's ambition is to fiercely protect its historical "entrepreneurial spirit" and its relentless "focus on results."

At the same time, management acknowledges the necessity of evolving the corporate culture to successfully navigate the demands of operating within an increasingly complex and rapidly changing international market.

To facilitate this evolution, executives engaged in a series of rigorous self-assessment workshops during 2024 and 2025 to actively map the company's strengths alongside its historical downsides.

This intense introspective process prioritized identifying internal "behavioural patterns that can sometimes limit us," demonstrating a deliberate ambition to mature the organization's psychological framework to support its massive scale.

The execution of massive, transformative transactions like the Dalata deal has intentionally forced senior leaders outside of their historical comfort zones.

CFO Anneli Lindblom candidly noted that the recent acquisitions challenged her directly to "think in new ways, collaborate across borders and sometimes dare to let go of what I am used to," highlighting the stretching of executive capabilities.

To ensure the intense workload does not lead to burnout, one of the newly codified behavioral priorities is an explicit mandate to "work smart, see opportunities and have fun along the way."

Operationally, the company has prioritized a highly pragmatic and data-driven behavioral model, telling investors that management strictly aims to "focus on the data and the things we can affect" rather than becoming distracted by external noise.

To combat inflationary pressures and rising employment costs, the company has prioritized aggressive productivity enhancements across its Own Operations segment.

These enhancements focus heavily on driving structural efficiency, digitalization, and automation, a process that the CEO admits puts "everybody up on the toes" to continuously find internal savings.

Integrating technological advancement into the company's daily operations is a long-term priority, with Pandox actively exploring AI solutions to optimize property performance.

The overarching ambition for the property portfolio has definitively shifted from passive holding to active optimization, with Pandox prioritizing its role as an "active owner with deep hotel expertise focused on creating value across the value chain."

Environmental sustainability has been elevated from a compliance necessity to a core operational ambition, highlighted by the firm commitment to slash Scope 1 and Scope 2 CO2 emissions by 42 percent by 2030.

Ultimately, management attributes the company's high-intensity execution capabilities to the unique "Pandox spirit," consistently prioritizing the cultivation of a super strong team as the ultimate driver of its financial success.

Financial profile

Sell-side analysts have significantly upgraded their earnings estimates for Pandox over the past six months, primarily driven by the transformative acquisition of the Dalata Hotel Group.

Analysts project that this historic acquisition will fundamentally boost the company's adjusted cash earnings per share by 20 percent year-over-year in 2026.

Forecasts indicate that the Dalata transaction alone could add approximately SEK 2.4 per share to cash earnings, assuming a marginal cost of debt of 4.5 percent and an initial yield of 6.8 percent.

Furthermore, sell-side earnings upgrades are heavily supported by stronger-than-expected profitability and margins within Pandox's Own Operations segment.

Analysts have also cited more favorable, lower financial cost assumptions as a key driver for upgrading 2026-2028 CEPS estimates by a further 3 percent.

Pandox achieved robust total revenue of SEK 7,469 million in 2025, representing a 5 percent increase from the previous year.

The Leases segment, which forms the core of the business, generated SEK 4,098 million in revenue, demonstrating a 6 percent year-over-year expansion.

The Own Operations segment contributed SEK 3,371 million to total revenue, growing by 3 percent compared to 2024.

The company delivered an EBITDA of SEK 4,086 million in 2025, which reflects a 3 percent increase over the prior year.

When adjusted for transaction costs specifically related to the Dalata acquisition, underlying EBITDA actually increased by an impressive 9 percent year-over-year.

The total property portfolio operates with a highly attractive average valuation yield of 6.37 percent.

This high valuation yield, combined with lower debt costs, ensures a strong and positive yield spread of nearly 250 basis points.

Aggressive M&A activity has fundamentally shifted the company's leverage profile, pushing net interest-bearing debt to SEK 48,342 million at the end of 2025.

Consequently, the net loan-to-value ratio increased substantially from 45.2 percent in 2024 to 52.7 percent by year-end 2025.

Despite this rapid increase in leverage, management highlights that the LTV remains comfortably within the company's stated financial policy target range of 45 to 60 percent.

The interest coverage ratio remained resilient at 2.6 times on a rolling twelve-month basis, demonstrating strong debt serviceability despite higher borrowing levels.

The ratio of net interest-bearing debt to EBITDA expanded significantly to 11.8 times, up from 8.7 times in the previous year, reflecting the immediate debt load of the Dalata deal.

Pandox successfully managed its debt maturity profile by completing new financings and refinancings totaling nearly SEK 20.9 billion during 2025.

The average interest rate on the company's debt portfolio improved slightly, ending the year at 3.9 percent compared to 4.0 percent in 2024.

Adjusted cash earnings per share amounted to SEK 11.34 in 2025, adjusting for SEK 241 million in transaction costs and SEK 59 million in preparatory financial costs.

Supported by strong underlying cash flows, the Board of Directors proposed increasing the annual dividend to SEK 4.50 per share for 2025.

This dividend aligns perfectly with the company's stated financial policy of distributing between 30 and 50 percent of total cash earnings to shareholders.

Pandox's financial profile remains highly competitive when benchmarked against global hotel property peers, many of which operate with substantially lower EBITDA margins.

Value chain - Competition

The traditional hotel industry value chain is increasingly dividing into three distinct and highly specialized components: the property owner, the hotel operator, and the brand franchisor.

Property owners focus their specialized skillset on maximizing property income, improving asset returns on investment, developing the underlying land or buildings, and optimizing the capital structure.

Hotel operators manage the day-to-day performance of the asset, including staffing, housekeeping, and revenue management, to maximize the gross operating profit margin.

Brand franchisors control the intellectual property, brand standards, loyalty programs, and central reservation systems, typically representing the highest-margin and most scalable part of the value chain.

While the structural separation of property ownership and daily operations is highly advanced in the United States, the European market has historically remained far more vertically integrated.

However, the pace of structural disaggregation in Europe is rapidly accelerating, evidenced by massive transactions such as Pandox's acquisition of the Dalata Hotel Group portfolio where the real estate and operations were formally split.

Pandox operates a highly flexible business model that allows it to move freely across this evolving value chain, acting as an active owner in both property management and direct hotel operations.

By maintaining the internal capability to operate hotels directly, Pandox fundamentally reduces its operational risk, as it can seamlessly step in to manage properties if external lease conditions become unprofitable.

The barriers to entry for the commercial hotel property market are structurally high, requiring comprehensive specialist expertise and substantial capital outlays to build a scalable, defensive portfolio.

Well-established hotel properties situated in strategic, central locations possess natural, location-based competitive advantages that are exceptionally difficult for new market entrants to replicate.

New market entrants face significant structural hurdles in obtaining the necessary regulatory approvals, zoning permits, and operational licenses required to develop and open new hotel properties from the ground up.

Elevated construction costs, exacerbated by inflationary pressures and selective bank lending, are currently severely restricting new hotel supply growth and new competitor developments across Europe.

Barriers to exit in the hotel real estate sector are also notably high, primarily driven by the statutory difficulty and complexity involved in changing a hotel's designated zoning purpose to alternative commercial uses.

Furthermore, exiting the industry often requires navigating the cancellation or termination of complex, long-term rental and hotel management agreements, which can trigger significant financial penalties.

The European hotel property market remains highly fragmented, creating a continuous pipeline of inorganic growth and consolidation opportunities for well-capitalized institutional players like Pandox.

Pandox competes fiercely for prime hotel assets against a diverse pool of well-capitalized entities, including institutional pension funds, private equity investors, sovereign wealth funds, and other specialized real estate investment trusts.

Covivio Hotels represents a major European competitor in the hotel investment space, managing a highly competitive portfolio of 278 hotels valued at approximately EUR 6.7 billion.

Similar to Pandox, Covivio utilizes a hybrid operating model, with roughly 59 percent of its portfolio leased to operators under long-term agreements averaging 11 years in duration.

Covivio's hotel portfolio delivered a solid average valuation yield of 6.2 percent in 2025, demonstrating the robust financial health and attractive return profile currently available to scaled European hotel property owners.

Despite intense underlying competition for assets from private equity and institutional funds, recent macroeconomic shifts and higher funding costs have actually resulted in lower competition during specific bidding processes, allowing Pandox to secure highly attractive acquisition opportunities.

To maintain its competitive edge against alternative accommodation disruptors like Airbnb and Vrbo, Pandox actively partners with approximately 30 different hotel brands to optimize market positioning and defend its tenants' market share.

While this trend has been deeply established in the US for years, the European market is now rapidly catching up, driving complex, multi-layered M&A activity like Pandox's historic Dalata acquisition.
