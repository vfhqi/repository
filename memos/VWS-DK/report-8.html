§1 — Industry Overview + Value Chain Economics

The global wind turbine industry is a structurally growing, policy-dependent, capital-intensive sector where margin pools concentrate in services, not equipment manufacturing.

  • Verdict: Industry is large, fast-growing, and structurally driven by energy policy — but margin pools sit in services/O&M, not turbine manufacturing, creating a tension for OEMs [#J]

Industry Size + Growth

  • Industry size: Global wind turbine market (equipment + installation + service) ~$100bn total annual value chain spend (2024E); equipment segment ~€65-75bn [#A]
  • Sub: Onshore turbine OEM revenue ~€40-45bn; offshore ~€15-20bn; O&M/service ~€20-25bn globally
  • Sub: IEA targets ~320GW annual additions by 2030 vs ~120GW installed in 2023 — ~2.6× acceleration required
  • Growth trajectory: Global wind capacity additions forecast ~8-10% CAGR through 2030 (IRENA World Energy Transitions Outlook 2023); onshore ~7%, offshore ~15-20% from lower base [#A]
  • Sub: EU alone targets 510GW wind by 2030 (REPowerEU) vs ~220GW installed end-2023 — requires >40GW/year additions vs ~20GW recent pace
  • Sub: US IRA (2022) unlocks ~$369bn energy/climate spend over decade; wind beneficiary via PTC extension — significant demand catalyst
  • Geographic split: China ~65% of global annual installations (but largely served by domestic OEMs); ex-China market ~35% (~40-50GW/year) is Vestas's primary battleground [#A]
  • Sub: Europe ~15GW/year (2023), US ~8-10GW/year, Rest of World ~8-10GW/year — Vestas strongest in EU + US + emerging markets
  • Sub: China market effectively closed to Western OEMs — Goldwind/Envision/CSSC dominate domestically; competitive risk is Chinese OEMs moving outward

Structural Drivers

  • Structural driver: Energy transition policy is the dominant demand driver — binding renewable energy targets across EU, US, UK, India create non-cyclical demand floors [#A]
  • Sub: EU ETS carbon price ~€60-70/tonne (2024) makes wind economics attractive vs gas peakers
  • Sub: Corporate PPA demand (RE100 signatories — Apple, Google, Microsoft, etc.) adds private-sector demand layer
  • Structural driver: Technology cost deflation — levelised cost of onshore wind ~$25-50/MWh (2023, LAZARD) vs $80-180/MWh in 2010 — demand elasticity improves as costs fall [#A]
  • Volume secular: Grid investment lagging — bottleneck is permitting + grid connection, not turbine supply — demand is real but installation pipeline has absorption constraints [#A]

Fragmentation vs Consolidation

  • Consolidation: Top 5 global OEMs control ~65-70% of ex-China market installations; consolidation occurred 2015-2020 (Siemens + Gamesa merger 2017, Nordex + Acciona 2016) [#A]
  • Sub: Consolidation driven by scale economics in R&D and supply chain — minimum viable scale ~3-5GW/year installs
  • Sub: Chinese OEMs remain fragmented among themselves (~8-10 significant players) but each gaining scale rapidly
  • Fragmentation (service): O&M market more fragmented — Vestas, Siemens Gamesa, independent service providers (ISPs), and owner-operators all compete; ISPs gaining share at contract renewal [#A]

  • LT growth: Energy transition requires ~3× current global wind installation rate by 2030 — structural volume demand is one of the clearest secular growth narratives globally [#J]
  • Adjacency: Offshore wind is Vestas's highest-conviction adjacency; MHI Vestas offshore (now Vestas 100% post-2022 acquisition) provides deepwater capability — offshore TAM ~€15-20bn/year growing to €40-50bn by 2030E [#A]

§3 — Barriers to Entry + Exit + Disruption Forces

  • Verdict: Barriers to entry are moderate-to-high for Western markets but structurally compromised by Chinese OEM state-backing; disruption risk is real but decade-scale, not near-term [#J]

Barriers to Entry

  • Capital requirement: New wind OEM requires €2-5bn capital investment in R&D, manufacturing, and certification — high absolute barrier; but Chinese OEMs cleared this with state support [#A]
  • Sub: Type certification (IEC/DNV) for a new turbine platform takes 3-5 years and €200-500m — genuine regulatory barrier
  • Scale economy: R&D costs for a new turbine platform ~€500m-1bn (Vestas invests ~€300-400m R&D/year); must be amortised over large volume — significant scale-economy barrier for standalone entrant [#A]
  • Technology barrier: Blade aerodynamics, drivetrain engineering, control software (SCADA), and reliability data represent 20-30 years of cumulative engineering knowledge — hard to replicate quickly [#A]
  • Sub: Vestas's failure mode database (~169GW installed, 40+ years operating data) is a genuine knowledge moat — no shortcut
  • Distribution barrier: Relationship-based sales to large utilities with 2-5 year procurement cycles — established relationships (Ørsted, RWE, Iberdrola, Enel Green Power) create incumbency advantage [#A]
  • Regulatory barrier: Grid codes, noise regulations, blade disposal regulations, and country-specific certification vary — Vestas's multi-country regulatory approval library is a barrier [#A]
  • IP: ~1,500+ active patents (est); control systems and blade geometry are key — IP barrier is real but litigating against Chinese state-backed OEMs is practically limited [#A]

Barriers to Exit + Lock-In

  • Lock-in mechanism: AOM (Active Output Management) service contracts, typically 10-30 years duration, create strong lock-in once turbines are installed — Vestas's 156GW service-managed fleet is a recurring revenue moat that no entrant can replicate [#A]
  • Sub: Switching cost for O&M provider mid-contract: €50-200k per turbine in retraining, data migration, and warranty risk
  • Structural exit: Siemens Gamesa consolidation with Siemens Energy effectively removed it as independent OEM; Enercon (private) has contracted; Senvion exited bankruptcy 2019 — exits have happened but slowly and painfully, suggesting high exit barriers [#A]
  • Lock-in mechanism: Proprietary SCADA/monitoring system (Vestas VestasOnline) creates data lock-in — customer switching costs increase with fleet age and data history [#A]

Disruption Forces (BB#2 TC3 RA11)

  • Disruption force: Chinese OEM internationalisation is the most material near-term disruption — not technology disruption but geopolitical/cost disruption [#J]
  • Sub: EU is considering carbon border mechanism and reciprocal tariffs on Chinese turbines; outcome uncertain; timeline 2025-2027
  • Disruption force: Energy storage + solar cost deflation could reduce wind's competitive advantage in some markets — but wind/solar complementarity means substitution is partial, not full [#A]
  • Disruption force: Floating offshore wind — if FOW cost curves fall faster than expected, deeper water sites open and Vestas's bottom-fixed offshore platform advantage partially erodes [#A]
  • Innovative entrant: Airborne wind energy (AWE — Makani, Ampyx, etc.) — speculative; no commercial scale; decade-plus away from threat [#A]
  • Vanilla entrant: New conventional turbine OEM entry from adjacent industrial companies (eg: ABB, Mitsubishi heavy — already exited via JV) — unlikely given capital requirements and Chinese incumbent advantage [#A]
  • Substitute: Utility-scale solar + BESS replacing onshore wind in sun-belt markets — ongoing substitution in Middle East/India; structural limit in Northern Europe/US where wind has capacity factor advantage [#A]
  • Disintermediation: Large utilities self-sourcing turbine component procurement direct — theoretical; practically limited by complexity; Ørsted/RWE still buy complete systems from OEMs [#A]

Adjacencies Entering (BB#2 TC3 RA10 CQ46)

  • Adjacency entry: Power electronics/inverter specialists (SMA Solar, ABB) entering wind control systems — nibbling at margins but not yet threatening full OEM position [#A]

  • Adjacency entry: Digital platform players (IBM, AWS, Microsoft Azure) entering wind analytics — competing with Vestas Digital/monitoring but not replacing physical OEM role [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: Inversion case on barriers — the apparent barriers look stronger on paper than in practice; Chinese OEMs have already penetrated South Africa, Brazil, and Eastern Europe despite the barriers described above; the "40-year knowledge moat" framing conflates accumulated data with irreplaceable advantage — Chinese OEMs are accumulating operational data at scale domestically and can license missing IP or hire European engineers; the most dangerous scenario is a Chinese OEM (Goldwind or CSSC) winning a significant EU offshore project circa 2026-2028, which would crystallise the threat and potentially trigger a new price war; regulatory protection via carbon border mechanism/EU procurement rules is the only credible counter-barrier, and that is a political bet, not a structural one
