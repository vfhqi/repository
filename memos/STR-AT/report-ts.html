Section 3 – What the company does 13 findings

Section summary
  1. STRABAG's fully vertically integrated business model secures high-margin raw materials and insulates its regional civil and building operations from supply chain shocks that frequently disrupt asset-light competitors.

Segmental Operations and Value Chain

1.
Core business segments: STRABAG organizes its construction activities into three primary geographic segments, with North + West focusing on Germany and Switzerland, South + East covering Austria and CEE, and International + Special Divisions handling global tunneling, where segments are managed independently by divisional heads who report directly to the Management Board
2.
AprStrabag SE – 2025 Annual ReportSTRABAG's vertically integrated value chain and core business activities overviewRevenue generation model: Revenue is earned primarily through long-term, public-sector construction contracts, with over 60% of output volume coming from public customers, providing high cyclical stability compared to residential-focused peers such as Skanska or Peab
3.
Vertical raw materials integration: A massive competitive moat is created by STRABAG's dense network of 556 in-house production facilities, which secures a high self-sufficiency rate of 86% in asphalt and effectively hedges against volatile raw material inflation
4.
Downstream service expansion: STRABAG covers the entire construction life cycle by expanding its downstream capabilities in Building Solutions, which bundles facility management and mechanical, electrical, and plumbing services to drive recurring revenues
5.
Cement joint ventures: In addition to self-producing the vast majority of its asphalt, the group operates strategic joint ventures with industry leaders like LafargeHolcim to secure direct access to cement across key Central and Eastern European core markets

Customer Concentration and Moat

6.
Public sector dominance: Customer concentration is low with over 14,000 active construction sites worldwide, while public sector contracts make up 69% of the 2025 output volume, insulating the order book from private developer defaults
7.
Technical competitive advantage: Technological leadership is anchored by Zentrale Technik and the STRABAG Innovation and Digitalisation division, employing over 2,400 specialized engineers to deploy BIM 5D digital modeling and robotics across complex projects
8.
Industry market structure: STRABAG is the absolute market leader in Central and Eastern Europe, maintaining the number one position in Germany, Austria, and Poland, where it benefits from massive economies of scale
9.
Capital-light concession assets: The company operates a robust portfolio of 44 public-private partnership concession projects, utilizing a capital-light construction model that generates long-term, predictable cash flows
10.
Strategic regional focus: The operational footprint is highly focused on regional proximity, which utilizes local teams and local know-how as a key pillar to manage operational risks on construction sites
11.
Infrastructure asset classes: The group's output is diversified across infrastructure types, with structures of all kinds representing 85% of output, encompassing transport routes, bridges, tunnels, rail systems, and water infrastructure
12.
Other side of the story: The high-exposure public sector contract mix leaves STRABAG vulnerable to sudden government fiscal austerity measures or delayed public budget approvals, as seen in late 2025 when regional German road construction projects were temporarily postponed due to delayed federal budget adoption

Section 5 – The possible investment case 10 findings

Section summary
  1. STRABAG's investment case is strengthening significantly as the group transitions into a high-margin full-service provider, leveraging secular European infrastructure and energy transition trends to drive a highly predictable earnings trajectory.

Infrastructure and High-Tech Drivers

1.
Mobility infrastructure driver: The primary forward earnings driver is the German transport infrastructure modernization offensive, with Germany alone planning to invest up to 500.00 billion euros in infrastructure, which directly impacts STRABAG as Germany represents its largest core market, accounting for 47% of group output
2.
High-tech facilities expansion: A major margin-accretive driver is the rapid expansion of high-tech facility construction, such as semiconductor fabs and data centers, which currently make up 6% of the group's 31.40 billion euro order backlog
3.
Energy and water infrastructure: Following the establishment of the dedicated Energy Infrastructure division in 2025, STRABAG is capturing massive clean energy grid investments, including key contracts for the German SuedLink power transmission lines
4.
Bolt-on acquisition catalyst: Forward earnings will be catalyzed by high-margin bolt-on acquisitions, notably the integration of the German water infrastructure specialist WTE Group, which expands STRABAG's water portfolio from 100.00 million euros to 400.00 million euros in annual output, and the acquisition of specialist civil engineering company Van Elle in the United Kingdom

Portfolio Diversification and Targets

5.
Geographical diversification moat: STRABAG has successfully diversified its geographical risk through its March 2025 acquisition of the Georgiou Group, which added 800 million euros to the order backlog and established a robust operating footprint in Australia
6.
Multi-market independent drivers: The forward growth drivers are largely independent rather than consolidated, meaning the investment case can succeed if either the German public roads budget, the UK water infrastructure programs, or high-tech fab investments fire
7.
Clear 2030 target visibility: Under its Strategy 2030, titled "People. Planet. Progress.", STRABAG aims to sustainably increase its EBIT margin to 6.0% by 2030, supported by record order visibility reaching through 2028
8.
Strong backlog coverage: The record-high order backlog of 31.40 billion euros represents a highly visible book-to-bill ratio of approximately 1.54x 2025 output volume, providing robust capacity utilization through 2028
9.
Other side of the story: The bull case would fail if severe labor shortages and high wage inflation in Central and Eastern Europe prevent STRABAG from executing its record backlog on time, as rising labor costs are already actively curbing construction momentum in key core markets like Slovakia

Section 7 – Key risks and the other side 9 findings

Section summary
  1. While the legal dispute over the sanctioned Russian shareholder introduces short-term headline volatility, the operational risks are well-mitigated by STRABAG's robust compliance framework and the complete freezing of all Russian share capital.

Sanctions and Russian Overhang

1.
Sanctioned shareholder freeze: The most prominent overhang is the 24.1% stake held by MKAO Rasperia Trading Limited, a firm previously controlled by the sanctioned Russian billionaire Oleg Deripaska, whose shares, voting rights, and dividend entitlements remain completely frozen under EU sanctions
2.
Russian litigation verdict: The conflict escalated in January 2025 when a Russian court ordered STRABAG and its Austrian core shareholders to pay 2.04 billion euros to Rasperia, ruling that the verdict can be enforced against Raiffeisen Bank International's Russian assets, though STRABAG maintains the ruling is unenforceable in Austrian courts
3.
Ownership transfer dispute: The core syndicate shareholders have filed a claim with a court of arbitration in Amsterdam to exercise their right of first refusal and legally purchase Rasperia's frozen shares, with a final ruling expected by the end of 2026

Operational Risks and Warnings

4.
Climate and cost transition: Operating margins face risk from volatile energy and fossil fuel prices linked to CO2 pricing, as STRABAG's heavy machinery fleet remains highly dependent on fossil fuels, prompting a gradual transition to alternative HVO 100 fuel
5.
Legal and cartel risks: STRABAG faces ongoing litigation and claims for damages in Austria following its €100.63 million competition cartel fine increase, which represents a persistent drag on legal expenses even though the company has since achieved group-wide ISO compliance certification
6.
Early warning signal: The key early-warning signal to monitor is any meaningful contraction in customer prepayments, which have remained stable and supported the cash position but could decline if higher interest rates finally pressure public budgets
7.
Supply chain human rights: The group's complex supply chain, involving direct raw material extraction and numerous subcontractors, exposes STRABAG to systemic risks of working hour, occupational safety, and child labor violations in high-risk regions
8.
Other side of the story: The risks are significantly overstated because the Russian court's 2.04 billion euro verdict has zero legal standing or binding effect in Austria, and the strictest sanctions compliance measures have successfully insulated STRABAG's operational business from any direct financial impact
