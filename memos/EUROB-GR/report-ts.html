Section 3 – What the company does 18 findings

Section summary
  1. The group operates a highly resilient and diversified regional business model that leverages scale in Greece and high-growth segments in Cyprus and Bulgaria

Regional Business Footprint and Products

1.
Geographic footprint: Eurobank stands as the most geographically diversified Greek bank, with approximately half of its total assets and net profits originating from operations outside Greece
2.
Greece asset footprint: The home market of Greece remains the group's primary segment, representing 58% of total assets, where the bank focuses on large corporate investments
3.
Cyprus asset footprint: The Cyprus division forms the second-largest geography, representing 27% of group assets following the legal merger of Hellenic Bank in September 2025
4.
Bulgaria asset footprint: The Bulgarian subsidiary, Postbank, accounts for 12% of total group assets and ranks as the fourth-largest bank in that market
5.
Luxembourg asset footprint: Luxembourg operations, focused strictly on cross-border private banking and wealth management, account for approximately 3% of the group's total asset base
6.
Product diversification strategy: The group actively cross-sells retail banking products, wealth management advisory, and insurance services across all three core geographic markets

Revenue Model and Value Chain Position

7.
Diversified revenue streams: Eurobank generates robust revenues from a mix of net interest income on lending books and high-margin wealth management fees
8.
Corporate lending margins: The group occupies a highly profitable value chain position in Greece, supported by corporate lending spreads of 178 basis points in early 2026
9.
Non-interest fee expansion: Non-interest revenues are a major driver of growth, with group fee and commission income expanding 19.9% year-on-year in 1Q26 to €203m
10.
Segment customer base: The bank serves a highly diversified customer base, spanning retail households, small-to-medium businesses, and large corporate entities with minimal client concentration
11.
Strategic bancassurance asset: Eurobank is the only Greek bank besides Piraeus with a fully integrated insurance operation across both Greece and Cyprus

Competitive Advantage and Industry Structure

12.
Market share position: Eurobank enjoys market dominance in Cyprus, ranking first or second across all segments, and holds the fourth position by assets in Bulgaria
13.
Switching cost barrier: High switching costs, proprietary digital platforms, and deep corporate customer relationships protect the bank's core deposit and lending franchises
14.
Highly consolidated industry: Greece's banking market is highly consolidated, with the four largest banks controlling 75% of assets, which maintains rational pricing behavior
15.
Stable pricing environment: Corporate lending spread repricing is expected to stabilize over the 2026-2028 horizon as Greek GDP growth remains structurally robust
16.
Cost efficiency advantage: Extensive digital investments have helped the bank achieve a best-in-class cost-to-core income ratio of 37.9% in 2025, outperforming European peers
17.
Other side of the story: Core interest margins would face compression if intensifying local competition forces lower corporate loan pricing or drives higher retail deposit betas

Section 5 – The possible investment case 15 findings

Section summary
  1. The investment case is highly compelling, driven by credit growth, regional wealth expansion, and transformational M&A synergies in Cyprus and Greece

Core Structural Drivers of Forward Earnings

1.
Cyprus consolidation driver: The operational integration of Hellenic Bank in Cyprus is projected to yield €120m in annual synergies, with 40% captured as of late 2025
2.
Cyprus failure mechanism: This catalyst would fail to materialize if IT integration or personnel restructuring faces significant implementation delays beyond the 18-month timeline
3.
Bulgaria Euro adoption: Bulgaria's Eurozone entry on January 1, 2026, releases €1bn of liquidity, which is expected to boost annual net interest income by €20m
4.
Bulgaria failure mechanism: The earnings benefit would fail if intense local competition compresses asset yields and prevents effective redeployment of the released liquidity
5.
Greek corporate pipeline: Robust investment activity in Greece, supported by the Recovery and Resilience Facility, drove €1.1bn in organic loan growth in 1Q26
6.
Pipeline failure mechanism: Corporate credit expansion would stall if persistent inflation or elevated geopolitical uncertainty delays capital expenditure projects
7.
Regional insurance acquisition: Reacquiring Eurolife's life insurance operations will expand commission income by €100m, adding €0.02 to earnings per share
8.
Insurance failure mechanism: Bancassurance fees would disappoint if insurance penetration levels across Greece and Cyprus fail to converge toward the Eurozone average
9.
Synergies capital relief: Over the medium term, Eurobank is well-positioned to secure capital relief from the Danish Compromise, providing another capital deployment catalyst

Valuation Re-rating and Compound Probabilities

10.
High sustainable returns: These drivers are collectively projected to increase the bank's return on tangible book value to approximately 17.0% by 2028
11.
Low compound probability: The earnings drivers are largely independent, meaning the investment case does not require all strategic catalysts to succeed simultaneously
12.
Multi-market growth correlation: Credit expansion is diversified across three distinct economic cycles, insulating the top line from specific single-country downturns
13.
Surplus capital flexibility: Maintaining a Common Equity Tier 1 ratio above 14% provides €950m of excess capital for selective regional M&A opportunities
14.
Other side of the story: A severe global macroeconomic recession would compress margins across all three regional segments, leading to rising credit provisions and lower loan demand

Section 7 – Key risks and the other side 12 findings

Section summary
  1. While the near-term risk profile is highly managed, macroeconomic slowdowns and geopolitical conflicts pose downside risks to credit spreads and asset quality

Operational and Geopolitical Risk Factors

1.
Geopolitical risk channels: Escalation of military conflicts in the Middle East could disrupt energy supply lines and raise inflation, reducing economic growth in Greece
2.
Greek spread compression: Corporate lending yields remain subject to downward pressure due to intense local bank competition, with lending spreads dropping in Greece
3.
Conservative provisions overlay: Eurobank has proactively shifted its macro-scenario weights, increasing the adverse scenario to 40% and recognizing a €24m impairment charge
4.
Swiss franc retail risk: Swiss franc denominated mortgage portfolios represent operational and litigation risks, although €170m has been converted to Euros
5.
Regulatory capital requirements: Rising regulatory capital or minimum reserve requirements could restrict organic credit growth or limit the bank's planned shareholder payouts
6.
Tourism macroeconomic dependency: The Greek corporate lending book remains highly sensitive to tourism, which contributes up to 20% of Greek gross domestic product

Shareholder Overhang and Mitigation

7.
No short-seller presence: There are currently no active short-seller campaigns or unusually elevated short interest metrics targeted against Eurobank shares
8.
Fairfax overhang risk: Long-term share price performance faces overhang risks if the reference shareholder, Fairfax Financial, decides to trim its 33% stake
9.
Early-warning indicators: Investors should closely monitor monthly credit growth figures and divisional cost-of-risk ratios for any signs of asset quality deterioration
10.
Slower insurance sales: Bancassurance fee generation could lag expectations if the integration of CNP and Eurolife insurance products faces consumer pushback
11.
Other side of the story: Downside risks to earnings are mitigated by Eurobank's non-performing exposure ratio of 2.6% and conservative coverage of 94.1%
