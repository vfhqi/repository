Section 3 – External Change: Value-Chain Forces (Demand, Competition, Disruption, Regulation, Supply, Cycle, Macro) 43 findings

Section summary
  1. structural biosimilar adoption is accelerating, though PBM rebate walls hinder near-term access
  2. competitive intensity is surging as multiple biosimilar pairs launch simultaneously
  3. continuous manufacturing platforms and autoinjectors are structurally reducing costs and improving convenience
  4. regulatory streamlining is waiving costly Phase III trials, accelerating time to market
  5. aggressive vertical integration is securing supply chains against rising global API risks
  6. a looming 2027-2028 patent-expiry trough threatens to temporarily stall sector growth momentum
  7. geopolitical tariffs have triggered adverse API price dumping, compressing specific segment margins

Demand

1.
Structural demand pulse: global biosimilar sales are forecast to reach USD 74 billion by 2030, driven by aging demographics and cost-containment mandates
2.
Strengthening customer behaviour: ustekinumab biosimilar penetration has significantly outpaced historical adalimumab adoption curves, indicating rising physician and payer comfort
3.
Temporary demand pulse: GLP-1 therapies represent a rapidly expanding self-medication market requiring autoinjectors, providing a substantial volume tailwind for device-enabled biosimilars
4.
Weakening customer behaviour: pharmacy benefit managers are leveraging originator rebate strategies to block broader prescription biosimilar adoption despite lower list prices
5.
Improving sentiment: Jefferies notes that Medicare may increasingly favour biosimilars due to IRA discount structures, leading to broader coverage

Competition

6.
Rising intensity: the US denosumab market is crowded, with six biosimilar pairs already approved, including entrants from Celltrion and Samsung Bioepis
7.
Consolidating structure: originator companies like JNJ use aggressive contracting and private-label biosimilar strategies to defend market share
8.
Fragmenting structure: by July 2025, there were 119 biosimilars approved in Europe and 72 in the US, diluting single-asset market shares
9.
Expanding competitor margin: Biocon leverages deep expertise in synthesis and manufacturing at scale for large molecules at a fractional cost
10.
Easing intensity: Sandoz retains a leading 35% global share with Omnitrope, demonstrating exceptional stability in a highly competitive 20-year-old category

Disruption

11.
Accelerating technology disruption: continuous biomanufacturing platforms like J.POD can reduce production costs to under USD 50 per gram versus USD 150 traditionally
12.
Falling disruption cost: continuous production enables around-the-clock output with flexible capacity scaling from 5kg to 2,500kg per run
13.
Innovative new entrant: subcutaneous autoinjector formulations for products like Pyzchiva represent a major step forward in patient convenience and retention
14.
Strong incumbent response: Sandoz acquired the Just-Evotec Biologics Toulouse site for USD 300 million to internalise continuous manufacturing technology
15.
Fast disruption pace: AI and data analytics are transforming drug discovery and manufacturing supply chains under the Pharma 4.0 paradigm

Regulation

16.
New regulatory shift: EMA guidance increasingly waives Phase III comparative clinical efficacy studies for well-characterised biological medicines
17.
Supportive political actor: Formycon secured FDA alignment on a Phase III waiver for its Keytruda biosimilar, accelerating potential timelines
18.
Hostile political actor: the US Inflation Reduction Act imposes new price caps and mandatory rebates, reshaping the economics of drug pricing
19.
Pending regulatory shift: proposed EU legislation changes may shorten the standard ten-year period of marketing exclusivity for originator biologics
20.
Removed tariff: the US Commerce Department recently confirmed that generic and biosimilar medicines are exempt from Section 232 tariffs

Supply

21.
Increased vertical integration: Sandoz is investing USD 440 million in a new Slovenian sterile biosimilars plant to secure end-to-end European supply
22.
Improving supplier economics: the JEB Toulouse acquisition provides an indefinite license to continuous-manufacturing technology to capture production economics
23.
Rising supply risk: the pharmaceutical industry remains heavily dependent on globally integrated supply chains for APIs and packaging materials
24.
Rising concentration: consolidation in contract manufacturing, such as the acquisition of Catalent by Novo Holdings, is widening the demand-supply gap
25.
Weakening supplier health: geopolitical tensions and extreme weather have exacerbated supply chain vulnerabilities and increased logistics costs globally

Cycle

26.
Late cycle stage: the US generic pharmaceutical market experienced 5.8% price deflation in 2025, reflecting a mature and highly competitive environment
27.
Trough behaviour: Morgan Stanley anticipates a growth deceleration risk due to fewer biosimilar launch opportunities over the 2026-2028 period
28.
Lengthening cycle: the highly anticipated Keytruda biosimilar opportunity is targeted for approval in 2027 or 2028 at the earliest
29.
Early cycle stage: GLP-1 receptor agonists are expanding from diabetes into obesity, projected to exceed USD 70 billion by 2026
30.
Inventory cycle destocking: post-pandemic destocking recovery is providing a tailwind for the injectable packaging and device sector

Macro

31.
Adverse macro shift: US tariffs diverted Chinese API supply to Europe, prompting severe price dumping and margin compression in anti-infectives
32.
Supportive FX: a weaker US dollar against the EUR and CHF provided a 2% tailwind to reported net sales in 2025
33.
Falling rates impact: major economies have begun monetary easing cycles, providing additional support to domestic demand and capital access
34.
Rising inflation: persistent inflation has increased operating costs and made investors more selective, favouring companies with established commercial operations
35.
Geopolitical risk: complex international conflicts and trade disputes have disrupted transportation routes and elevated active pharmaceutical ingredient prices

Other side of the story: PBM consolidation and originator rebate walls could completely negate regulatory tailwinds; accelerated biosimilar supply might compress margins without capturing volume
