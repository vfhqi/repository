2. KEY FINDINGS — BLUF

  • LEAD VERDICT: Three questions dominate diagnostic value — the gross margin bridge walk, the churn cohort disclosure, and the M&A/capital-return optionality question; management candour on all three simultaneously would be extraordinary
  • Top-3 most-diagnostic questions:
  • Q §2.1 — "Walk me through the gross margin per installed unit in your three largest country markets — what is the exit-rate spread?" (tests whether the business model is fundamentally sound or geographically fragmented beyond repair)
  • Q §3.1 — "What is the EBITDA contribution of the alarm-central (monitoring) segment vs the device-hardware segment, and how is that mix shifting?" (tests operating-leverage credibility vs hardware drag hypothesis)
  • Q §6.3 — "In the markets where you've lost contracts in the last 24 months, what was the primary stated reason from the customer?" (tests churn vector: price, tech, or incumbent stickiness failing)
  • Bull hypothesis at stake: Careium re-rates as a recurring-revenue, operating-leverage story as hardware mix fades; margins normalise 14-17% EBITDA; FCF inflects by FY26
  • Bear hypothesis at stake: Revenue is sticky only because switching costs are regulatory not economic; pricing power is structurally low; the Swedish/Nordic public-sector customer base reprices on contract renewal cycles at unfavourable rates
  • False-friend risk: Management narrates a "technology transformation" story (connected care, IoT platform) that is real optically but immaterial economically for 3-5Y; the stock re-rates on the narrative before the P&L catches up, then de-rates when it doesn't
  • Highest-uncertainty pillars pre-meeting: (1) unit economics per geography, (2) renewal pricing dynamics in the Swedish public-sector customer base, (3) timeline and credibility of the platform/software margin uplift
  • Go-to-most-different imperative (Concept B CA1): The question the analyst does NOT want to ask — "What has gone wrong with the technology platform rollout and what specifically have you de-prioritised?" — ask it anyway; §8 addresses this directly
  • Power-of-incentives note: CEO and CFO incentive structure at Careium (LTI vesting metrics, EBITDA vs revenue weighting) is load-bearing for whether the "margin recovery" narrative is incentive-aligned or aspirational; §6 probes this

3. §1 — EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (top-3 most-diagnostic questions)

  • VERDICT — diagnostic hierarchy: Three questions materially discriminate bull/bear/false-friend; remainder are confirming or clarifying at the margin
  • Q-1 discriminator (gross margin geography walk): Answer determines whether the operating-leverage story is structurally intact or whether low-margin geographies are structurally drag; bull = management can cite expanding GM in Sweden/Norway core; bear = GM deteriorating in core as legacy hardware cycles linger
  • Q-2 discriminator (monitoring vs hardware EBITDA split): Answer tests whether the "high-quality recurring revenue" frame is real or mixed; bull = monitoring segment EBITDA margin materially above group; bear = they can't or won't split it (itself a red flag)
  • Q-3 discriminator (lost-contract churn vector): Answer surfaces whether contract losses are idiosyncratic or systematic; bull = losses concentrated in non-core, replaced by higher-value wins; bear = losses in core Swedish market on price or tech grounds
  • Post-meeting back-brief hook: Map every answer against the three hypotheses; a single internally-contradictory answer (e.g. "margins are fine" + "we've adjusted renewal pricing down to retain volume") invalidates the bull case on pricing power
  • What the analyst does NOT want to ask — and must: "Which specific technology commitments made to customers in 2022-2023 have you not yet delivered?" — uncomfortable because it implies prior management missed targets; exactly the question that surfaces false-friend risk; flagged again in §8
  • Sceptical close: Fluent answers in a CEO/CFO meeting are not comfort — Careium management has had ample time to rehearse the "transformation" narrative; look for hesitation on specific numbers, not polished narrative delivery

4. §2 — THEME 1: Revenue Drivers + Visibility

  • VERDICT — revenue-side uncertainty: High; public filings show top-line stability but the quality of that stability (price-led vs volume-led vs contract-tenure-led) is underspecified; this theme carries the most diagnostic weight pre-decision

Q 2.1 — Gross margin per unit by geography

  • Question: "Can you walk me through the gross margin per installed device or per alarm-central subscriber in Sweden, Norway, and the UK separately — and what is the exit-rate spread across those three?"
  • Expected answer: Management will offer consolidated GM directionally; may resist per-geography disclosure citing competitive sensitivity
  • Good answer: Specific per-geography numbers or tight ranges; trend direction stated; management comfortable with the spread discussion
  • Bad answer: Deflects to group-level; says "we don't manage the business that way" (almost certainly false for an operator of this type)
  • Follow-up probe: "Even directionally — is Sweden above or below group average GM? By how many points?"
  • Diagnostic note: Bull hypothesis requires Sweden/Norway core to be high-GM and improving; bear hypothesis requires discovering the UK or newer geographies are dilutive and growing as a share of mix; false-friend = management gives a directional narrative without the numbers (leaves the false impression of transparency)

Q 2.2 — Contract renewal pricing dynamics

  • Backlog visibility / Pricing power: "In the last 12 months of contract renewals with Swedish municipality customers, have you renewed at higher, flat, or lower per-unit pricing — and what's the average renewal uplift or haircut?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely say "broadly stable" or "modest increases in line with CPI"; the real answer may be below-CPI if municipal procurement pressure is intensifying
  • Good answer: Quantified renewal pricing uplift (e.g. +2-4% per annum); evidence that renewal pricing is positively correlated with CPI; no reference to "competitive pressure requiring price concessions"
  • Bad answer: Vague on quantum; admits pricing flat to down on a volume-weighted basis; references "some competitive pressure" without quantifying
  • Follow-up probe: "What share of your Swedish AUM (annual recurring revenue or equivalent) comes up for renewal in the next 24 months — and what's your expectation on pricing at renewal?"
  • Diagnostic note: This is THE most important revenue-quality test; a public-sector customer base renewing below inflation is structurally value-destructive even if headcount/volume holds

Q 2.3 — Churn rate and cohort retention

  • Churn / retention: "What is your gross subscriber churn rate on an annual basis — and can you separate involuntary churn (death/care-home transition) from voluntary (customer contract switch to a competitor)?"
  • Expected answer: Total churn probably 8-15% annually given the elderly care market demographics; management may not have the split
  • Good answer: Clear split between demographic churn and competitive churn; competitive churn below 3-4% annually; trend stable or improving
  • Bad answer: Cannot or will not split; total churn above 15%; rising trend in competitive churn
  • Follow-up probe: "In markets where a competitor has actively marketed against you in the last 12 months, what has been the churn rate in those specific accounts?"
  • Diagnostic note: Demographic churn is structurally embedded in the elderly care model and does NOT imply competitive weakness; competitive churn is the bear-case signal; conflating the two in management's answer is itself a yellow flag

Q 2.4 — New contract pipeline and conversion rate

  • Forward order book: "What is the current value of your new-contract pipeline and what is your close rate — and how does 2025 pipeline compare to 2023 pipeline in value terms?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely give directional commentary; hard numbers may require pushing
  • Good answer: Pipeline growing in value terms; close rate stable; named expansion into a new geography or segment with specific customer examples
  • Bad answer: Pipeline "healthy" with no specifics; close rate declining; no new geography wins in the last 18 months
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the average time from initial RFP or tender to contract signature in a new municipality win — and has that lengthened or shortened?"
  • Diagnostic note: Lengthening sales cycles in public-sector markets is a leading indicator of procurement headwinds; bull case requires a compressing or stable cycle

Sceptical close §2: A fluent revenue narrative from management — with precise churn numbers, smooth pipeline commentary, and CPI-plus renewal pricing — is not unambiguously positive; it may signal that management has perfected the presentation of stability rather than the reality of growth; the analyst should probe for a specific recent contract loss and ask management to narrate it in detail

5. §3 — THEME 2: Margin Trajectory + Levers

  • VERDICT — margin uncertainty: Moderate-to-high; public filings show EBITDA margins recovering from a trough but the source of margin recovery (operational efficiency vs revenue mix vs cost deferral) is opaque; the operating-leverage thesis requires verification of the mechanism, not just the direction

Q 3.1 — Segment EBITDA split (monitoring vs hardware)

  • GM driver / Mix shift: "What is the EBITDA margin of the alarm-central monitoring segment versus the device-hardware segment — and how is that split shifting as a percentage of revenue?"
  • Expected answer: Management knows this internally but may resist full disclosure; directional answer likely (monitoring higher-margin); quantum is the load-bearing information
  • Good answer: Monitoring segment margin materially above group (e.g. 20%+ EBITDA); hardware declining as share of total; management can articulate a credible 3Y mix-shift trajectory
  • Bad answer: Refuses to split; or split reveals hardware is a larger share than the "recurring revenue" narrative implies; or monitoring margin is not materially above group
  • Follow-up probe: "As you expand the software/platform layer on top of the monitoring service, where does that revenue sit — does it improve monitoring margin, or is it a separate cost centre at this stage?"
  • Diagnostic note: The bull case for re-rating requires a demonstrated high-margin monitoring core; if management can't or won't show the split, the "recurring revenue premium" multiple is not earned

Q 3.2 — Cost structure walk: what is fixed vs variable?

  • Op leverage: "Of your total cost base, what percentage is truly fixed (irreducible in a 12M horizon) versus variable or semi-variable — and at what revenue increment does the next tranche of fixed cost get absorbed?"
  • Expected answer: Management may give a rough ratio; alarm-central operators typically have high fixed cost in the monitoring centre (labour, real estate, connectivity) and variable cost in device logistics and field service
  • Good answer: ≥60% fixed cost base clearly articulated; incremental revenue margin on the next SEK 50-100M revenue explicitly stated and credible; management has done the operating-leverage math internally
  • Bad answer: Vague on the fixed/variable split; no incremental margin estimate; or implies incremental margins are only modestly above current group margins (suggesting no real operating leverage)
  • Follow-up probe: "If revenue grew 10% organically next year, what would EBITDA margins do — holding cost structure constant?"
  • Diagnostic note: This is the operating-leverage crystalliser; a company with genuine operating leverage should be able to answer this in 90 seconds; hesitation or deflection is a structural red flag

Q 3.3 — R&D spend: adequacy and capitalisation policy

  • R&D adequacy / Capex pacing: "What is your annual R&D spend as a percentage of revenue — and what share is capitalised versus expensed? Has that ratio changed in the last 3 years?"
  • Expected answer: Careium spends on IoT platform and connected-care software; capitalisation ratio likely elevated during platform build; management may be shifting toward more expensing as the platform matures
  • Good answer: R&D spend stable or growing as % of revenue; capitalisation policy conservative (lower); management can articulate specifically what the R&D is building and when it becomes revenue-generative
  • Bad answer: R&D as % of revenue declining (underinvestment risk); or capitalisation ratio elevated and rising (earnings quality concern); or management cannot link R&D spend to specific product deliverables and timelines
  • Follow-up probe: "In the last 12 months, what specifically has been de-prioritised in the product roadmap — and why?"
  • Diagnostic note: De-prioritisation disclosure is the most honest test of R&D adequacy; a company that claims to be investing at full capacity with no trade-offs is either over-resourced (unlikely) or not being candid

Q 3.4 — Personnel cost trajectory

  • Productivity: "Personnel costs are your largest cost line — what is your revenue per FTE trend over the last 3 years, and what is the target ratio you're managing toward?"
  • Expected answer: Revenue per FTE likely improving modestly as automation and remote monitoring reduce field-service headcount intensity; management may not have a stated target ratio
  • Good answer: Revenue per FTE visibly improving; management has an explicit productivity target; headcount growth lagging revenue growth in percentage terms
  • Bad answer: Revenue per FTE flat or declining; headcount growing in line with or faster than revenue; no explicit productivity framework
  • Follow-up probe: "What is your field-service technician utilisation rate — and how does that compare to 2 years ago?"
  • Diagnostic note: Field-service technician utilisation is a leading indicator of margin recovery; if utilisation is below 80%, there is structural margin drag from idle field capacity that revenue growth alone may not fix

6. §4 — THEME 3: Capital Allocation + Balance Sheet

  • VERDICT — capital allocation clarity: Low; Careium's balance sheet is not highly leveraged but the capital allocation framework (M&A vs organic vs return to shareholders) is underspecified in public filings; management's revealed preferences on capital will be more informative than their stated preferences

Q 4.1 — RONIC discipline and M&A hurdle

  • RONIC discipline / Returns hurdle: "What is your internal hurdle rate for M&A — and what RONIC do your last three acquisitions show at year 3 post-close versus your underwriting case?"
  • Expected answer: Management will state a hurdle rate (likely WACC + some premium, perhaps 10-15% IRR); the realised RONIC question is the harder one and may produce deflection
  • Good answer: Explicit hurdle rate stated; management can name the last 2-3 acquisitions and give a specific realised RONIC vs underwriting case; honest about any miss and what drove it
  • Bad answer: No post-mortem culture on M&A; "all acquisitions are on or ahead of track"; or RONIC realised is not tracked at the deal level (implies M&A is growth narrative-driven rather than return-driven)
  • Follow-up probe: "If you acquired a tuck-in alarm-central operator in a new geography today, what would the acquisition multiple be — and how long until RONIC exceeds WACC?"
  • Diagnostic note: M&A return discipline is a key differentiator between compounder-quality operators and serial acquirors whose ROE is eroded by deal premium leakage; this question separates the two

Q 4.2 — Free cash flow conversion and working capital

  • Working capital management: "Your reported EBITDA has grown — but what is your free cash flow conversion rate on EBITDA, and what is driving any gap between EBITDA and operating cash flow?"
  • Expected answer: FCF conversion in alarm-central / connected-care operators should be 60-80% of EBITDA in a steady state; if lower, working capital or capex is consuming the surplus
  • Good answer: FCF conversion ≥70%; management can walk through the bridge (EBITDA → EBIT → operating CF → FCF) with specific numbers; working capital is not a structural drag
  • Bad answer: FCF conversion below 60%; management deflects to EBITDA as the "right" measure; working capital absorbing cash at a rate management cannot fully explain
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the cash conversion cycle for your device-hardware business — and is the trend improving?"
  • Diagnostic note: Low FCF conversion relative to EBITDA is an earnings-quality red flag in a B2B services company with recurring revenue; it implies either capex is higher than the asset-light narrative suggests, or receivables are building

Q 4.3 — Capital return optionality

  • Dividend policy / SBB cadence: "At what leverage level, or at what FCF-per-share threshold, would you consider returning capital via buyback or special dividend — and is that a current board-level discussion?"
  • Expected answer: Management likely to say growth investment is the priority; no near-term SBB; dividend likely stable or modestly growing
  • Good answer: Explicit leverage target stated (e.g. net debt/EBITDA <1.5x); board has discussed and defined the capital-return trigger; FCF above a stated threshold would trigger capital return consideration
  • Bad answer: Vague; "we'll evaluate when appropriate"; no articulated framework; dividend policy driven by precedent rather than capital structure logic
  • Follow-up probe: "If M&A pipeline dried up for 18 months and FCF continued at the current rate, what specifically would you do with the surplus?"
  • Diagnostic note: Management that cannot answer this question fluently either has a live deal in the pipeline (non-disclosure reason) or has not done the capital allocation framework work (governance concern); both are informative

7. §5 — THEME 4: Strategy + Competitive Position

  • VERDICT — competitive position: Moderate uncertainty; Careium operates in markets with entrenched incumbents (Tunstall, Doro, Vitaphone equivalents) and municipal procurement cycles; the "platform differentiation" narrative requires stress-testing against the actual competitive dynamic in tenders

Q 5.1 — Win rate in competitive tenders

  • Competitive response / Win condition: "In the last 12 months, how many competitive tenders have you participated in — and what was your win rate? Against which specific competitors did you lose, and on what grounds?"
  • Expected answer: Management likely knows this precisely; may be reluctant to name competitors explicitly; win rate above 50% in core markets is the baseline expectation
  • Good answer: Win rate ≥60% in core markets; losses concentrated in non-core geographies or segments; competitive losses predominantly on price (addressable) rather than technology (structural)
  • Bad answer: Win rate declining; losing in core Swedish/Nordic markets; losing on technology or integration grounds (signals platform is not competitive)
  • Follow-up probe: "When you lose a tender, does the winner typically offer a lower price, a different technology, or both?"
  • Diagnostic note: Losses on technology grounds in the core market are the single most damaging bear-case signal; they imply the platform investment has not yet translated into competitive parity or advantage

Q 5.2 — Platform differentiation — what does the customer actually value?

  • Moat trajectory / Adjacency expansion: "When you win a contract, what is the customer's stated primary reason — is it the technology platform, the alarm-central response quality, the price, or the implementation track record?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely say "combination of factors" — push for a rank order
  • Good answer: Technology platform cited in the top two factors by ≥40% of customers; management has customer survey data to back this up; implementation track record is a differentiator (high switching cost)
  • Bad answer: Price is the primary win factor; technology is an "also mentioned" rather than a primary driver; no customer survey evidence
  • Follow-up probe: "Do you have any NPS or customer satisfaction data from the last 12 months you can share?"
  • Diagnostic note: If price is the dominant win factor, the business is not earning a platform premium and the re-rating thesis depends on a narrative rather than a structural moat

Q 5.3 — Regulatory tailwinds and risk

  • Regulatory positioning / Disruption risk: "How dependent is your growth trajectory on the EU's Accessible Products & Services directive or national digital-care mandates — and what happens to your model if regulatory timelines slip by 2-3 years?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely frame regulatory change as a tailwind; push them to quantify how much of the growth plan is regulatory-dependent vs organic demand-led
  • Good answer: Regulatory tailwinds contribute meaningfully but growth is demonstrable even on a base case of no new regulation; management has modelled the regulatory-slip scenario explicitly
  • Bad answer: Growth plan is heavily regulatory-dependent; management has not modelled a slip scenario; no demonstrated organic demand beyond current regulatory minimum
  • Follow-up probe: "In a jurisdiction where there has been no regulatory push in the last 3 years, what has your market growth rate been?"
  • Diagnostic note: A business whose growth requires regulatory catalysts is a political/policy risk vehicle, not a structural compounder; the false-friend profile arises precisely here

8. §6 — THEME 5: Management Quality + Operator Track Record

  • VERDICT — management quality signals: Mixed in public record; communication has improved post the Doro/Careium restructuring, but sandbagging discipline and operator track record on platform commitments are unverified at DD depth

Q 6.1 — Sandbagging discipline — prior guidance vs outcome

  • Sandbagging discipline / Operator track record: "Over the last 3 years, how often has the company met, exceeded, or missed its own guidance on EBITDA and revenue — and what were the primary drivers of any miss?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely cite external macro factors for any miss; push for an honest post-mortem on internal execution failures
  • Good answer: Track record is one of meeting or moderately exceeding guidance; misses are explained by identifiable one-off factors; management can name an internal execution failure and describe what they changed
  • Bad answer: Consistent over-promising on margins or revenue; misses attributed entirely to external factors; no evidence of organisational learning from misses
  • Follow-up probe: "What was the single largest internal execution miss in the last 24 months — and who was accountable?"
  • Diagnostic note: A management team that cannot describe an internal failure in specific terms either has not had one (rare) or has not internalised accountability culture (more common and concerning)

Q 6.2 — Communication clarity — what do you not know?

  • Communication clarity / Crisis playbook: "What is the single biggest uncertainty in your own business that you do not have a clear answer to — something that keeps you up at night?"
  • Expected answer: CEOs often deflect this with macro answers (inflation, geopolitics); push for something business-specific
  • Good answer: CEO surfaces a specific operational or competitive uncertainty (e.g. "the conversion rate of our IoT platform upsell in legacy accounts"); is candid that they don't yet know the answer; has a plan to resolve it
  • Bad answer: Macro-only answer; or a clearly-rehearsed vulnerability ("our biggest risk is talent, which we address by X") — sanitised, not genuine
  • Follow-up probe: "Of the things you don't know today, which one, if it resolved against you, would most materially change your revenue guidance?"
  • Diagnostic note: Willingness to surface genuine uncertainty is the strongest single signal of management quality; a CEO who cannot or will not do this in a private investor meeting is not trustworthy as a disclosure partner

Q 6.3 — Incentive structure alignment (Power of incentives)

  • Power of incentives / Animal-CEO probe: "Can you walk me through your LTI vesting metrics — what specific targets trigger full vesting, and what weighting is given to revenue growth versus EBITDA versus ROIC or FCF?"
  • Expected answer: LTI likely tied to revenue growth and EBITDA; ROIC or FCF less commonly weighted in Scandinavian mid-cap structures; management may not expect this level of detail
  • Good answer: LTI explicitly includes FCF or ROIC metrics with meaningful weighting (≥25%); management articulates why the metrics were chosen; CEO equity holding is significant relative to total comp
  • Bad answer: LTI exclusively revenue and EBITDA weighted with no FCF/ROIC component; CEO equity holding modest; management seems surprised that the question is being asked
  • Follow-up probe: "What percentage of your total 2024 compensation was in equity — and at what price was the last grant made?"
  • Diagnostic note: EBITDA-weighted LTI with no FCF component is a structural incentive to grow reported EBITDA through accounting choices (capitalisation, working capital management) rather than genuine cash generation; this is an earnings-quality risk at the incentive-structure level

Q 6.4 — Hiring posture — who is joining and why

  • Hiring posture / Culture archetype: "In the last 12 months, what has been the most senior hire you've made — what was their background, why did they join, and what was the competing offer they turned down?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely highlight a tech or commercial hire; the "why they joined" answer is the diagnostic one
  • Good answer: Senior hire left a larger, higher-paying role to join; articulated reason was mission or growth opportunity; competing offer was credible (e.g. a larger Nordic tech firm)
  • Bad answer: Most senior hire was lateral from a similar company with no premium; or management cannot answer the competing-offer question (implies no competition for the hire)
  • Follow-up probe: "What is your average tenure for the top-15 leadership roles — and what is the attrition rate in that cohort over the last 2 years?"
  • Diagnostic note: Attrition in senior leadership is the strongest leading indicator of cultural decay; a management team with high top-15 attrition is operating in a structurally unstable environment regardless of what the strategy slides say

9. §7 — THEME 6: Risk + Downside (Pre-mortem cross-ref)

  • VERDICT — downside probe: The Q19 ACH matrix failure modes cluster around three vectors: (1) pricing erosion in public-sector renewals, (2) technology platform non-delivery, (3) M&A value destruction; this theme tests all three directly

Q 7.1 — Bear-case probe: pricing erosion scenario

  • Bear-case probe / Pricing fragility: "If Swedish municipality procurement consolidated further and three large customers demanded a 15% unit-price reduction on renewal, what would that do to group EBITDA — and do you have cost flexibility to offset it?"
  • Expected answer: Management will resist the premise ("that's not our expectation"); push for the hypothetical sensitivity; the answer reveals pricing fragility and cost flexibility simultaneously
  • Good answer: Management can run the sensitivity in real time or has done it; can identify specific cost offsets (automation, field-service efficiency); total EBITDA impact in the scenario is manageable (<15% EBITDA hit on a 15% pricing cut — implying meaningful gross margin buffer)
  • Bad answer: Refuses to engage with the scenario; "that won't happen" framing; or sensitivity reveals that a 15% pricing cut would be >25% EBITDA impact (implying near-zero variable cost offset and high fixed cost leverage operating against the company)
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the minimum unit price at which you would not renew a Swedish municipality contract — and have you ever walked away from a renewal on price grounds?"
  • Diagnostic note: Willingness to walk away from a renewal at a minimum price is the strongest signal of pricing discipline; a company that has never walked away is likely not setting a floor — it is pricing to retain volume at any cost

Q 7.2 — Technology platform failure mode

  • Failure mode test / False-friend probe: "What is the current status of your IoT platform rollout against the roadmap you had 24 months ago — specifically, what has been delayed and what was the reason?"
  • Expected answer: Some delays almost certain; management will likely frame as prioritisation rather than failure; the specifics of what was delayed are the diagnostic
  • Good answer: Named specific delays with specific causes; course-corrected with re-prioritised roadmap; customer commitments associated with the delayed features have been renegotiated honestly; no revenue recognised on undelivered commitments
  • Bad answer: "On track" with no specifics; or platform delays that have already caused customer contract non-renewals that management has not disclosed; or capitalised development costs that should have been impaired
  • Follow-up probe: "Are there any customers who were sold capabilities that the current platform does not yet deliver — and if so, how are those relationships managed?"
  • Diagnostic note: This is the most direct false-friend probe — the scenario where the stock re-rates on a platform narrative that is real optically but economically immaterial or delayed; customer contracts written against undelivered capabilities are a specific litigation/credit risk

Q 7.3 — Customer concentration and key-account risk

  • Customer churn vector / Customer concentration: "What percentage of your revenue is attributable to your top 5 customers — and what is the average remaining contract life of those relationships?"
  • Expected answer: In a public-sector dominated business, top-5 customer concentration may be 25-40% of revenue; contract life likely 2-5 years
  • Good answer: Concentration below 25%; remaining contract life across top-5 is ≥3 years on a weighted-average basis; no single customer >10% of revenue
  • Bad answer: Concentration above 35%; any top-5 customer contract renewing within 12 months with pricing uncertainty; or management cannot state remaining contract life for top accounts
  • Follow-up probe: "For your single largest customer, what is the renewal date, what is the pricing mechanism at renewal, and do you have any competitive risk on that contract?"
  • Diagnostic note: A near-term renewal of a top-3 customer at uncertain pricing terms is a binary event risk that is not reflected in consensus estimates if undisclosed

Q 7.4 — Regulatory exposure: analogue-to-digital sunset

  • Regulatory exposure / Bear-case probe: "The analogue telecom network sunset creates both an opportunity and an execution risk for your installed base — what percentage of your installed base has not yet migrated to digital, and what is the cost per migrated unit?"
  • Expected answer: Migration is likely ongoing; some percentage of legacy analogue base still unmigrated, especially in rural or lower-acuity accounts; migration cost is a capex or cost-of-revenue item
  • Good answer: Migration substantially complete (>80%) in core markets; per-unit migration cost declining as the process is systematised; no material installed base at risk of obsolescence
  • Bad answer: Large unmigrated base (>30%) with no clear migration timeline; per-unit migration cost rising; some customers refusing migration who represent meaningful revenue at risk
  • Follow-up probe: "What happens to a customer's service contract if they do not migrate before the analogue shutdown date in their region — is that a churn risk or a forced-migration opportunity?"
  • Diagnostic note: Analogue-to-digital migration is simultaneously a capex risk (Careium bears cost) and a competitive-displacement risk (migration events are the moment a competitor can intervene); both vectors need to be assessed

Sceptical close §7 — ACH diagnostic items NOT covered by any question in this memo:

  • Uncovered ACH item 1: Supplier concentration and component cost risk for hardware devices — if a single ODM or component supplier represents >40% of device COGS, a supply disruption is a margin risk not probed above; flagged for the analyst to add if hardware remains a material cost line
  • Uncovered ACH item 2: FX risk on non-SEK revenues (UK, Germany, BeNeLux) relative to SEK-denominated cost base — not probed in this memo; relevant if UK or other non-Nordic revenues are growing as a share of mix
  • Uncovered ACH item 3: Key-man risk on technology leadership — the CTO or head of product who owns the platform roadmap; if they leave, the platform delivery timeline is at risk; not covered in §6 questions

10. §8 — CROSS-CUTTING META + NON-OBVIOUS QUESTIONS

  • VERDICT — non-obvious angle: Management will arrive prepared for revenue and margin questions; the questions in this section are designed to surface information management would NOT volunteer — and where non-answer is itself diagnostic

Non-obvious angle 1 — the "silent majority" customer:

  • Edge-detection probe: "Of your customers who have been with you for more than 5 years and have never escalated a complaint, what do you actually know about whether they are satisfied — or are they simply stuck?"
  • Why non-obvious: Consensual customer relationships in public-sector contracted services are often inertia-based, not loyalty-based; management rarely distinguishes these two
  • Expected response: Management may conflate low-churn with high-satisfaction; push them to name a specific customer research effort that challenges their satisfaction assumptions
  • Diagnostic note: If management cannot distinguish inertia from loyalty, the "sticky revenue" narrative is not earned — it is assumed

Non-obvious angle 2 — the "what would make you sell the company" question:

  • ⚡ WH Smith probe / Cross-roads question: "If a strategic acquiror approached you with a 30% premium to the current share price, what would be the primary argument you would give the board for NOT accepting?"
  • Why non-obvious: Forces management to articulate the investment thesis in the negative — why is it worth more than 30% above current; the answer discriminates between a management team that genuinely believes in the long-term compounding case and one that would take the money
  • Expected response: CEO likely deflects; CFO may engage more candidly; the answer reveals conviction level
  • Good answer: Articulates a specific 3-5Y value creation pathway that is not priced at current levels; names specific milestones that, if achieved, would make 30% premium look cheap
