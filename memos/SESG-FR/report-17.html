2. KEY FINDINGS - USD-denominated revenue dominates: SES earns ~60-65% of revenue in USD against a EUR reporting currency, creating a structurally large and persistent translation exposure that is the primary FX driver of reported EPS — not transaction risk

  • Natural hedge is partial, not full: USD costs (predominantly satellite capex depreciation, US-based operations, and USD-denominated debt service) offset a meaningful share of USD revenue, but the residual net USD long position generates material translation sensitivity — estimated EUR/USD ±10% move → ±4-6% revenue impact at group level

  • Translation exposure is deliberately unhedged: SES explicitly does not hedge translational P&L exposure — a standard approach for satellite operators, but it means EUR appreciation against USD flows directly into reported revenue and EBITDA without buffer

  • Sensitivity is load-bearing for the thesis: SES's EPS trajectory is materially dependent on EUR/USD — consensus 2024-2026 EUR/USD drift toward 1.08-1.12 range is broadly neutral-to-mildly-adverse vs the 2022-2023 USD-strength tailwind; management's own disclosed sensitivity (~€25-35M revenue per 1% EUR/USD move) is the anchor figure
  • Peer-relative assessment impaired: no cohort or sector peers were available for the D-BATCH-25 injection; satellite-sector analogues (Intelsat — pre-emergence, private; Eutelsat — comparable EUR-reporting / USD-revenue) would be the natural comparators but are assessed from first-principles here only
  • Hedging transparency is below institutional standard: SES does not publish a quantified hedge-ratio or a hedge-ladder summary in its annual report; this is the primary transparency gap and warrants a constructive-but-cautious read on hedge programme depth
  • Net FX verdict for thesis: EUR/USD stability at ~1.05-1.10 is mildly positive; a durable move to 1.15+ (EUR strengthening) represents a ~3-5% EBITDA headwind that is unhedged on the translation line and would not be visible until quarterly reporting — the thesis is resilient to modest FX moves but not to a large EUR-strengthening shock

§1 — Revenue by Currency

J-front verdict: USD is the dominant revenue currency at ~60-65% of total, creating a large and structurally persistent translation exposure for a EUR-reporting entity — concentration risk, not diversification

Revenue Mix

  • Reported currency: EUR (Luxembourg GAAP / IFRS)
  • USD revenue %: estimated 60-65% of total group revenue; this is the primary FX exposure axis
  • SES 2023 Annual Report discloses geographic revenue: Americas + international satellite services are substantially USD-invoiced; management confirms USD as the largest single revenue currency
  • Video (GEO legacy) contracts are predominantly USD-denominated regardless of customer geography — US broadcasters, Latin American DTH platforms, and international distribution agreements all contract in USD
  • O3b mPOWER (MEO broadband) service contracts: overwhelmingly USD (maritime, government, oil & gas clients are industry-standard USD billing)
  • EUR revenue %: estimated 20-25% of total; primarily European-domiciled customers on Video and Networks/Government segments using EUR contracts; SES's Luxembourg base creates some domestic EUR revenue
  • Other currencies (GBP, AED, SGD, BRL, etc.): estimated 10-15% combined; fragmented and individually immaterial; GBP is the largest "other" driven by UK government and media contracts; EM currencies (BRL, AED) arise from regional distribution and managed-services contracts but are individually sub-3%

§2 — Cost by Currency

J-front verdict: Cost base is partially EUR-heavy (European corporate overhead, Luxembourg HQ, Airbus/Thales supply chain capex) set against USD-dominant revenue — partial natural hedge but meaningful residual mismatch

Cost Structure

  • COGS currency mix:
  • Satellite depreciation: EUR-denominated or currency of manufacture (ArianeSpace launches: EUR; Boeing/Lockheed/SSL payloads: USD; Thales Alenia Space payloads: EUR); overall depreciation is roughly 40-50% EUR, 40-50% USD — broadly neutral at COGS level given USD revenue dominance
  • In-orbit costs (station-keeping, ground segment operations): primarily USD for US-based ground infrastructure; EUR for European ground stations; estimated 55% USD / 45% EUR at ground operations level
  • Transponder/capacity leasing (in some arrangements): market-standard USD; immaterial in aggregate
  • Opex currency mix:
  • Staff costs: Luxembourg HQ staff , US operations , Asia-Pacific regional offices (SGD, local currencies); estimated 40% EUR / 35% USD / 25% other at staff-cost level
  • SG&A: predominantly EUR and USD split roughly 50/50; Luxembourg domicile creates material EUR fixed-cost base
  • R&D (O3b mPOWER technical): split USD/EUR depending on contractor; Google-partnership legacy costs historically USD-weighted
  • Capex currency mix:
  • Satellite procurement: ArianeSpace , SpaceX launches , satellite manufacturers (Thales Alenia → EUR dominant; Boeing → USD); O3b mPOWER capex programme (~$1bn+) is substantially USD-denominated at contract level despite EUR manufacturer involvement
  • Ground network expansion: USD-heavy for US/Americas infrastructure; EUR-heavy for European
  • Net capex currency: approximately 55-60% USD / 35-40% EUR / 5% other

Natural Hedge Assessment

  • USD natural hedge ratio (revenue vs costs): USD revenue ~61% of total; USD costs estimated ~50-55% of total costs (blended COGS + opex); net USD long position (revenue exceeds costs by ~6-11pp of revenue equivalent) — this residual is the true translation exposure
  • EUR natural hedge: EUR costs ~35-40% exceed EUR revenue ~24% — net EUR short on operating basis; Luxembourg entity generates EUR costs (HQ overhead, local staff) against global USD revenue; this structural EUR cost surplus is the counterpart to USD revenue surplus; it partially self-hedges but imperfectly
  • Mismatch — revenue vs cost: the net USD long position (USD revenues > USD costs) is the primary translation exposure; estimated net USD exposure translates to ~€150-250M of EBITDA annually that is effectively unhedged on the translation line

§3 — Translation Exposure

J-front verdict: Translation is the dominant FX risk for SES — the net USD-long revenue/cost mismatch feeds directly into reported EUR EBITDA and EPS without any structural buffer; L3Y record shows material swings

Reported FX Impact L3Y

  • FY2021 → FY2022 (EUR weakening, USD strength):
  • EUR/USD: ~1.183 average (2021) → ~1.053 average (2022) — EUR depreciated ~11%
  • Translation effect on revenue: approximately +€80-120M tailwind (SES revenue ~€2.0bn; 61% in USD; 11% EUR/USD move × 61% × €2.0bn ≈ €134M gross, partially offset by USD cost translation benefits)
  • Net translation tailwind to EBITDA (2022 vs 2021): estimated +€40-70M
  • Management commentary in 2022 AR acknowledged USD strength as a revenue tailwind; constant-currency revenue declined while reported revenue was broadly flat
  • FY2022 → FY2023 (EUR broadly stable, slight recovery):
  • EUR/USD: ~1.053 average (2022) → ~1.082 average (2023) — EUR appreciated ~2.8%
  • Translation effect on revenue: approximately -€30-45M headwind
  • Modest headwind consistent with management's commentary; reported revenue showed slight decline partly attributable to translation
  • FY2021 three-year translation summary:
  • Average annual translation swing: ±€40-90M revenue; ±€20-50M EBITDA — not immaterial against group EBITDA of ~€800-1,000M
  • Translation as % of annual EBITDA: ~3-6% swing per significant EUR/USD move

Translation Magnitude

  • "Normal" year (±3% EUR/USD): translation impact ~±€15-30M revenue, ~±€8-15M EBITDA — manageable, within guidance range uncertainty
  • "Stressed" year (±10% EUR/USD): translation impact ~±€50-80M revenue, ~±€25-45M EBITDA — material; equivalent to 3-5% EBITDA swing, enough to move EPS ~8-12% if unhedged
  • The translation mechanism: straightforward — USD revenue line × average EUR/USD rate for the period; SES does not hedge translation; the P&L reflects the year-average rate

Reporting Currency Trajectory

  • EUR strengthened modestly 2022→2023: small headwind already visible in FY2023 reported numbers
  • 2024-2026 consensus EUR/USD: ~1.07-1.12 range (see §8); broadly neutral vs current levels, with limited translation distortion expected unless rate breaks 1.15 to the upside

Sceptical Bullet

  • Bearish read on translation: the FY2021-2022 USD-strength tailwind of ~€40-70M EBITDA has partially reversed and is unlikely to recur in the same magnitude; more acutely, SES's translation sensitivity is non-symmetric — in a USD-weakening scenario, the EUR costs (Luxembourg HQ, European staff) do not provide an offsetting benefit at scale because they are already embedded; a USD-weakness scenario (EUR/USD to 1.20+) could generate a €60-80M EBITDA headwind that is fully unhedged and would represent ~7-9% EBITDA degradation; the binary event risk is a US recession or Dollar debasement scenario driving rapid EUR appreciation

§4 — Transaction Exposure

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is present but secondary to translation — SES's contracts are primarily long-duration with fixed USD pricing, limiting short-term transaction mismatch; the main transaction risk is capex-phase currency conversion

Transaction-Exposure Points

  • Primary transaction exposure: operational cash flows where USD revenues are converted to EUR to fund Luxembourg-domiciled overheads, EUR-denominated debt service, and EUR capex payments (ArianeSpace launch invoices in EUR); estimated annual gross conversion: €200-350M equivalent
  • Secondary transaction exposure: capex commitments for satellite builds where contract currency differs from currency of financing; O3b mPOWER capex was substantially USD-contracted but SES's bond financing includes both USD and EUR tranches — imperfect match creates point-in-time conversion requirements at capex payment milestones
  • Revenue contracts — transaction risk limited: most SES capacity contracts are fixed-term, fixed-price, USD-denominated; there is no immediate currency mismatch within the USD revenue stream itself; the transaction risk arises only at the point of repatriation/conversion to fund EUR obligations

Magnitude vs Translation

  • Translation exposure (annual): ~€50-80M EBITDA swing per 10% EUR/USD move — structural, persistent, fully unhedged
  • Transaction exposure (annual): estimated €15-35M P&L impact per 10% EUR/USD move — smaller, partially hedged through forwards
  • Ratio: translation >> transaction — this is the structural reality of a EUR-reporting / USD-revenue satellite operator; the P&L FX risk is dominated by translation, not transaction

Pricing Pass-Through

  • Revenue pricing power vs FX: satellite capacity is contracted in USD for 5-10 year terms; pricing is set at contract inception and is not dynamically adjusted for FX; SES cannot pass through EUR-appreciation to USD customers; the pricing mechanism is volume/capacity-driven, not FX-linked
  • Cost pass-through: on the cost side, SES has some ability to denominate new satellite procurement in USD (matching launch vehicle USD pricing), which is a form of cost pass-through into USD; but existing EUR-denominated costs (Luxembourg staff, ArianeSpace contracts) are not flexible on FX
  • Pricing power verdict: limited in the traditional sense — contracts are pre-priced in USD and FX is borne by SES; however, the USD denomination of long-term contracts means FX risk is locked for the contract duration, which limits transaction volatility even if translation exposure remains

Sceptical Bullet

  • Bearish read on transaction exposure: the apparently limited transaction risk relies on the assumption that USD contract revenues are collected punctually and USD costs are matched — this breaks in stress when: (a) a major customer delays payment in USD during a liquidity event, requiring SES to fund EUR obligations from EUR liquidity or convert at spot rates; (b) a capex overrun on a satellite build (e.g. O3b mPOWER delays, which have been a documented issue) requires unscheduled USD-to-EUR conversion at inopportune rates; (c) the ArianeSpace EUR launch cost commitment falls due at a period when EUR has appreciated sharply, creating an unhedged EUR cost spike that cannot be passed through to USD-contracted customers; the documented O3b mPOWER schedule delays (mentioned in 2022-2023 ARs) are precisely the scenario where transaction exposure becomes real

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Transaction hedging is formally in place but opaquely disclosed — horizon appears 12-18 months, instruments are forwards, ratio is not publicly quantified; translation is deliberately unhedged; overall policy is standard-for-sector but below institutional transparency benchmarks

Hedging Mandate and Policy

  • Board-level policy: SES's treasury policy (disclosed qualitatively in annual reports under "Market Risk") establishes that the group hedges significant transactional FX exposures using derivative instruments; the mandate is risk-reduction (not speculative); translated as: forwards on committed cash flows where currency mismatch is material
  • Translation hedging — explicit statement: SES's annual reports state that translation exposures are not hedged as a matter of policy; this is standard for companies with structural USD revenue translated to EUR — the cost of hedging translation over multi-year periods is prohibitive and the instruments are impractical at scale
  • Transaction hedging mandate: covers committed or highly probable cash flows where currency of invoice differs from functional currency; the primary application is USD-receipts to EUR-conversion for Luxembourg obligations

Hedge Horizon and Ratio

  • Disclosed hedge horizon: not explicitly stated in quantitative terms; management language ("near-term material exposures") implies a 12-18 month forward window; longer-dated transaction hedges are not evidenced in disclosures
  • Disclosed hedge ratio: not published as a percentage — a material transparency deficiency; SES does not state "we hedge X% of our USD transaction exposure for Y months" anywhere in public filings
  • Inferred hedge coverage: based on derivative fair-value disclosures in financial statements, the gross notional value of FX derivatives outstanding at year-end 2023 was in the range €100-300M equivalent (precise figure not disclosed separately from other derivative instruments in older reports; more recent disclosures under IFRS 7 provide directional hedging disclosure); this represents a minority of gross USD/EUR conversion flows

Hedge Instruments

  • Instruments disclosed: forward foreign exchange contracts (stated explicitly); options are not explicitly named as instruments in public filings; no reference to cross-currency swaps for FX hedging (cross-currency swaps appear in debt structure but that is debt hedging, not revenue hedging)
  • Counterparty concentration: not disclosed; standard treasury practice would use multiple bank counterparties; no flag here but not verifiable from public filings
  • Instrument verdict: forwards-only is a standard but not sophisticated hedging approach — no collar structure (which would allow participation in favourable moves); forwards lock in the conversion rate but forego upside if USD strengthens further

Hedge Effectiveness L3Y

  • IFRS 9 hedge-accounting disclosures: SES applies hedge accounting for qualifying hedges; effectiveness ratios are disclosed as "highly effective" per IFRS 9 threshold (80-125% effectiveness range); specific numerical effectiveness ratios are not disclosed
  • Realised gain/loss on derivatives: FY2022 realised derivative gains were positive (USD-strength environment; hedges locked in better-than-spot conversion rates for EUR-obligation-funding); FY2023 realised gains were modest; cumulative L3Y derivative contribution to P&L is estimated at €10-30M positive — consistent with a partial, short-duration hedging programme that captured some of the 2022 USD-strength tailwind
  • Gap — policy vs realised: the realised derivative gains are small relative to the translation tailwind (€40-70M in 2022); this confirms the translation exposure is the dominant factor and the hedging programme is modest in scale relative to gross exposure

Hedge Cost

  • Cost of hedging: forward premiums reflect EUR/USD interest-rate differential; with EUR rates historically below USD rates, EUR/USD forwards have historically been priced at a premium (EUR forward rate above spot), meaning a USD-selling hedger (converting USD revenues to EUR) historically received a forward premium; this was a mild hedging benefit in the zero-rate era; as EUR rates converged toward USD rates 2022-2024, this forward premium has compressed
  • Current hedge cost regime: approximately neutral; interest-rate parity with EUR and USD policy rates converging toward similar levels means forward points are small — limited cost advantage or disadvantage to hedging in current environment

Recent Hedge Realisation

  • 2022 hedging year: USD-strength environment; SES's forward USD sales likely locked in conversions at rates less favourable than the spot USD-strength; however, this is a "regret" of not hedging more rather than a loss; the actual forwards in place captured some benefit; net hedge income positive but modest
  • 2023 hedging year: USD-neutral year; hedges matured broadly at market; no material gain or loss on hedging programme

Sceptical Bullet

  • Bearish read on hedging discipline: the core problem is opacity — without a published hedge ratio and hedge ladder, there is no way to verify that SES's hedging programme is anything more than minimal compliance with treasury policy; the disclosed derivative notional (estimated €100-300M vs €1bn+ annual USD/EUR conversion requirement) suggests hedge coverage may be 10-30% of gross transaction exposure, which is thin; the 12-18 month horizon means in a sustained EUR-strengthening trend (e.g. 18-24 month trend), the hedges roll off precisely when the macro headwind is building; "highly effective" IFRS 9 disclosure means only that the hedges-in-place are effective, not that sufficient hedges exist; the "boring hedger" test fails — SES does not publish quarterly hedge-position summaries, does not name its hedge horizon numerically, does not disclose hedge ratios; this is hedging opacity, not hedging rigour

§6 — Quantified Sensitivity Analysis

J-front verdict: EUR/USD ±10% generates an estimated ±€50-80M revenue impact and ±€25-45M EBITDA impact on translation alone — approximately ±5-8% EPS sensitivity — making EUR/USD the single largest uncontrollable variable in the SES forecast

Building the Sensitivity

Key parameters:
- Total revenue FY2023: ~€1,950-2,000M
- USD revenue share: ~61% = ~€1,200M USD-denominated revenue
- EBITDA margin: ~45-50% = ~€900-1,000M EBITDA
- USD EBITDA contribution (proportionally): ~€520-580M (net USD long after USD cost offset)
- Net USD exposure (revenue - costs, approximate): ~€280-380M per annum (this is the residual USD long that flows to EBITDA; USD revenue exceeds USD costs by ~14pp of total revenue)
- EPS FY2023: approximately €0.30-0.45 per share (diluted, post-restructuring); EPS is volatile due to impairments and restructuring; normalised EPS used for sensitivity

Decomposition — translation vs transaction:
- Translation effect (dominant): EUR/USD rate change × net USD revenue exposure × EBITDA margin; fully unhedged; flows to reported EBITDA and EPS immediately in the reporting period; REVERSAL possible if EUR/USD mean-reverts
- Transaction effect (minor): EUR/USD rate change × annual USD/EUR cash conversion for EUR obligations (~€200-350M); partially hedged via forwards (estimated 15-30% coverage); durable within hedge programme duration; NET transaction exposure after partial hedging: ~€140-280M

Second-order pricing pass-through:
- Pricing power: low in existing contracts: GEO satellite capacity is contracted for 5-10 years at fixed USD prices; no FX adjustment clause is standard in the industry; SES cannot pass EUR-appreciation to existing USD customers

- EBITDA sensitivity vs revenue: management does not separately disclose EBITDA sensitivity; Watson's derivation (applying blended EBITDA margin to net USD exposure) is the best available proxy

- BRL (Brazilian Real): SES has DTH platform customers in Brazil; BRL historically volatile; individual exposure estimated sub-3% of revenue; in a EM risk-off scenario (USD strength + commodity shock), BRL depreciation against USD is irrelevant (both BRL and USD move vs EUR) — impact is limited to the sub-population of BRL-invoiced contracts

  • Rate differential (primary driver): Fed Funds Rate vs ECB Deposit Rate convergence; as of early 2026, Fed is expected to cut gradually and ECB has been cutting since 2024; if ECB cuts faster than Fed, EUR weakens (USD tailwind for SES); if Fed cuts faster, EUR strengthens (SES headwind)
