[W] AALB-NL — Short-Sellers + Bear Case [C] @ 19-May-26

KEY FINDINGS - Bear case breadth: No published short-seller reports from any named firm (Muddy Waters, Hindenburg, Citron, Gotham City, Viceroy, ShadowFall, QualityValue) against Aalberts have been identified; the bear case is therefore constructed from sell-side scepticism, accounting-pattern analysis, and structural industry arguments rather than activist short campaigns [#J]

  • Short interest: AALB-NL carries modest but non-trivial short interest in the 3-5% of float range; not extreme by European industrial standards (peer median ~2-4%), but reflecting genuine macro and cycle scepticism rather than stock-specific fraud allegations [#A]

  • Sell-side bearish bloc: A meaningful minority of covering analysts hold Neutral/Hold or equivalent ratings; the bear thesis is concentrated on (a) cycle exposure in semiconductor and construction end-markets, (b) margin plateau risk after the 2021-2024 restructuring, and (c) M&A integration risk from bolt-on strategy [#J]

  • Accounting: No material weaknesses, auditor changes, or published forensic-accounting attacks; however, non-GAAP adjustments for "restructuring and acquisition costs" have been persistent and growing — a pattern worth watching even absent fraud allegations [#A]

  • Governance: Board structure and compensation are Dutch-market standard; no related-party red flags identified; CEO tenure is long (Wim Pelsma since 2018, COO-to-CEO transition) but that cuts both ways [#A]

  • Structural bear thesis: The single most credible bear scenario is a prolonged semiconductor-capex downturn combined with European construction weakness, compressing Aalberts' Flow Control and Hydronic Flow revenues simultaneously and leaving margin expansion stranded at current levels rather than progressing toward management targets [#J]

  • Cycle exposure underappreciated: Bears argue Aalberts' portfolio repositioning toward "technology" end-markets (semiconductor, defence, nuclear) is chronologically late — the semiconductor capex boom has likely peaked for the 2024-2026 window, reducing near-term revenue tailwinds [#A]

  • M&A integration risk: Aalberts completed multiple bolt-on acquisitions since 2018; goodwill/intangibles as share of total assets is elevated versus European capital-goods peers; impairment risk in a downturn is non-trivial [#A]

  • Valuation risk: At ~14-16x forward P/E (May 2026 estimates), Aalberts trades at a modest premium to European industrial peers trading ~11-13x; if cycle downgrade materialises, re-rate to peer median alone implies 15-20% downside before EPS cuts [#A]

  • Overall bear coherence: The bear case is structurally coherent but not explosive — it is a "slower growth, margin plateau, valuation normalisation" story, not a fraud or governance crisis; probability of catastrophic downside is low, but probability of 20-35% drawdown in a cycle downturn is meaningful [#J]

§4 — Accounting + Governance Red Flags

Verdict: Accounting is broadly clean with no fraud allegations; however, persistent and growing non-GAAP restructuring adjustments and elevated goodwill warrant monitoring — not red flags but amber flags [#J]

  • Revenue recognition: Aalberts manufactures physical industrial components (no software, no SaaS, no complex multi-element contracts); revenue recognition follows standard point-of-delivery IFRS 15 methodology with minimal scope for manipulation [#I]

  • Sub: Flow Control products (hydronic components, HVAC) are sold at arms-length to distributors and contractors — low channel-stuffing risk given inventory is physical and customer returns are visible

  • Channel stuffing signal: No evidence of channel stuffing; distributor inventory days for European plumbing/HVAC components are publicly tracked and showed inventory normalisation (reduction) in 2023-2024, consistent with end-demand weakness rather than stuffing [#A]

  • Non-GAAP gap: Aalberts regularly adjusts for "acquisition-related costs" and "restructuring costs" in reported EBITA; these adjustments have been present in every annual report since the 2018 strategic restructuring [#A]

  • Sub: Adjustment magnitude: approximately €20-35m per year in "non-recurring" costs that recur annually; as a % of EBIT, this represents approximately 4-7% of adjusted EBIT being excluded from "clean" earnings
  • Sub: European industrial peer median for similar adjustments: approximately 3-5% of EBIT; Aalberts is at the upper end but not an outlier

  • Sub: The pattern of "recurring non-recurring" adjustments is a legitimate accounting amber flag — not fraud, but it means reported adjusted earnings overstate clean earnings capacity by a modest amount ❌

  • Adjustments increasing: Acquisition-related PPA amortisation is growing as bolt-on deal pace has increased since 2020; this mechanically increases the gap between adjusted EBITA (management's preferred metric) and IFRS EBIT [#A]

  • Sub: PPA amortisation in 2023 estimated at €50-65m versus ~€30-35m in 2019; delta of ~€25-30m represents dilution of true earnings power not visible in adjusted metrics

  • Sub: Peer anchor: DSM-Firmenich (acquisition-heavy) shows similar PPA amortisation growth; this is a sector-wide IFRS 3 pattern, not Aalberts-specific

  • Goodwill / intangibles concentration: Goodwill and acquired intangibles represent approximately 45-50% of total assets, elevated relative to European capital-goods peers at ~30-40% median [#A]

  • Sub: Goodwill impairment risk is real in a sustained downturn — if Hydronic Flow or Technology Solutions acquired businesses underperform, impairment charges could materially hit IFRS book value

  • Sub: No impairment has been recorded in recent years, but the headroom in CGU tests narrows as construction volumes fall ❌

  • Capitalisation aggressive: R&D capitalisation practices appear within IFRS norms; Aalberts capitalises product development costs selectively and the absolute amounts are not large relative to revenue [#I]

  • Sub: The company's products are evolutionary (fittings, valves, surface treatments) rather than breakthrough — aggressive capitalisation of development costs is not a meaningful risk here

  • Related-party transactions: No material related-party transactions flagged in recent annual reports beyond standard director remuneration; no Aalberts family interests, controlling shareholder tunnelling risk, or complex subsidiary structures that would be typical related-party red flags [#I]

  • Mgmt comp structure: CEO and Executive Board remuneration is aligned to EBITA growth and EPS targets; this creates an incentive to maximise adjusted EBITA (which excludes acquisition costs) rather than IFRS earnings [#A]

  • Sub: The incentive structure means management is incentivised to keep acquisition-related charges below the EBITA line — a legitimate compensation concern that reinforces the non-GAAP adjustment amber flag

  • Sub: Dutch corporate governance code compliance is standard; no egregious pay packages identified

  • Board independence: Supervisory Board composition appears to meet Dutch corporate governance code independence requirements; majority of Supervisory Board members are non-executive with no identified conflicts [#I]

  • Sub: No cross-shareholding relationships, no family-board overlap, no known activist-required board seat disputes

  • Auditor change: No auditor change detected in recent years; KPMG has been auditor for multiple years (standard for Dutch mid-cap); auditor rotation under EU audit reform rules will eventually apply but no forced change [#I]

  • Material weakness: No material weakness in internal controls has been disclosed in any annual report or audit opinion [#I]

  • Pending lawsuit / regulator action: No material pending litigation or regulatory enforcement action identified; standard product liability and commercial dispute references appear in risk factor disclosures but below materiality threshold [#I]

  • NWC liability: Working capital has been somewhat elevated during the 2021-2023 inventory build cycle (common across European industrials during supply chain disruption); signs of normalisation in 2023-2024 [#A]

  • Sub: Inventory days peaked at approximately 85-95 days in 2022 versus a historical norm of 70-80 days; modest improvement visible but not yet fully normalised

  • Sub: Elevated inventory is a cash drag and a cycle risk — in a harder downturn, inventory writedowns are possible

  • Receivables aging: DSO (days sales outstanding) has been broadly stable at 55-65 days — within normal range for European industrial distribution channel [#A]

  • Payables stretching: DPO appears stable at 45-55 days; no evidence of aggressive payables stretching that would signal cash pressure [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet (BULL rebuttal): The accounting amber flags (recurring non-recurring adjustments, goodwill concentration) are IFRS 3 structural artefacts common to all European acquisition-driven industrials; they do not suggest manipulation, and management has been transparent about the adjusted vs IFRS gap [#J]

  • Bear rebuttal #2: Margin improvement from restructuring has stalled; EBITA margin has been broadly flat at 13-14% since 2021 despite management targets of 15-16% by 2025 [#A]

    • Sub: The bear argument: restructuring benefits are real but have been fully absorbed; next leg of margin expansion requires volume leverage that isn't coming in the current end-market environment ❌

  • Bull thesis #3: Technology Solutions (semiconductor surface treatment) is a structurally growing, high-quality segment with durable competitive advantages
