§2 — Structural Dimension: The Dual-Source Architecture ([C] + [AS])

J-front verdict: the dual-source design is the single most material quality lever in Q21 — running [C] alone, as forced here, produces a structurally incomplete landscape

  • Market structure (template architecture): Q21's design assumes [C] and [AS] produce complementary, partially-overlapping evidence surfaces — [C] provides framework, base rate, cross-sector analogue, and public-filing inference; [AS] provides expert-call on-the-ground signal, sell-side channel checks, and management-commentary triangulation
  • Customer dynamics (of the template's intended user): the downstream APM is a concentrated long-only investor with 5-15 positions and a 12-24M hold horizon; this user has a very high signal-to-noise tolerance — they want J-front verdicts, peer anchors, and explicit confidence levels, NOT narrative prose; the template's bullet + signpost format is calibrated exactly to this preference profile
  • Supplier dynamics (evidence sources): [C] is the "wide-aperture" source — it can range across sectors, historical analogues, academic base rates, and regulatory frameworks without being constrained by proprietary data; [AS] is the "narrow-aperture, high-conviction" source — expert calls on the specific company or sector often carry 10x the informational weight of a sell-side note but require the researcher to distinguish expert opinion from expert knowledge
  • Competitor landscape (of Q21 vs Q22): Q21 and Q22 are complementary, not competing — the frequent error is running Q22 (judgement-forming) before Q21 (landscape-mapping) is complete, which produces premature closure; the correct sequence is Q21 → Q22 → APM pillar grade update; skipping Q21 and going straight to Q22 is the epistemic equivalent of writing a conclusion before reading the evidence
  • Regulatory: no regulatory dimension applies to the template architecture itself; however, in a live KQ on a regulated sector, this section would be the most evidence-dense — regulatory outcomes are often the single highest-variance input to an EPS trajectory, and they are systematically underweighted in sell-side models because regulators don't give guidance on the same cadence as company management
  • Technology / disruption (template evolution): Q21 is versioned (v2.1 current) — the most material evolution from v2.0 is the explicit ⚡ outlier marker mandate and the Concept B "multiple-truths-coexist" synthesis instruction; both address documented failure modes where researchers produced consensus-confirmation memos that missed the non-consensus signal that was actually load-bearing for the case
  • Historical precedent (template failure modes): the three most common Q21 failure modes observed across prior runs — (1) placeholder non-substitution treated as complete output, (2) confidence classification table populated with "High" across all sections without evidential anchoring, and (3) data gaps listed without resolution routing — all three are mechanical errors, not analytical errors, and all three are addressable with the validation gates (5A-5K, Q21-DM, Q21-CC, Q21-DG, Q21-SO)
  • Cross-sector analogue: Q21's structure is analogous to a due-diligence information memorandum in private equity — the IM maps the landscape, the investment committee memo makes the call; the two-document structure exists precisely because the researcher who maps the landscape should NOT be the same person who makes the judgement call without a second-pass analytical layer; Q22 is that second-pass layer
  • Geographic variance: in a live UK/Europe-focus KQ, this dimension would cover how the research question varies by geography — e.g., pricing power in UK vs German vs French market structures often follows different regulatory and competitive patterns, and the researcher must resist collapsing all three into a single "European" data point
  • Time-series trend: the template's "12-24M hold horizon" instruction is load-bearing for scope — a Q21 that maps a 5-year structural trend is out of scope unless the trend has 12-24M inflection points; the researcher should always ask "what in this landscape changes in the next 18 months?" and weight evidence accordingly
  • Non-consensus read (Concept A): ⚡ the most systematically missed signal in dual-source Q21 runs is disagreement between [C] and [AS] — when [C] framework inference points one direction and [AS] expert calls point the other, the contradiction is itself the most informative data point; researchers too often resolve the contradiction by deferring to [AS] (on the grounds that primary > secondary) without examining whether the expert call is subject to selection bias, recency bias, or motivated reasoning
  • Concept B synthesis: the elephant in the dual-source room is that [C] and [AS] are differently biased — [C] is biased toward public-information consensus (what is already priced in), while [AS] is biased toward recency and expert opinion (what the smart money currently believes); the composite truth is neither the [C] consensus view nor the [AS] expert view, but the gap between them — that gap is where alpha lives
  • Sceptical bullet: the bearish read on the dual-source architecture is that it produces a false sense of completeness — two sources feel like triangulation, but if both sources draw on the same underlying information pool (sell-side consensus + management IR), the "two source" structure is illusory; the researcher is not seeing disconfirming evidence because it exists in channels (short-seller reports, competitor IR, customer interviews, regulatory dockets) that neither [C] nor [AS] systematically surfaces

§3 — Structural Dimension: J-Front Verdict Discipline

J-front verdict: J-front formatting is the highest-leverage, most tractable quality gate — it forces epistemic commitment before hedging and is the most mechanically enforceable of all Q21 quality requirements

  • Market structure (of J-front in the output): every parent bullet in a Q21 output must open with a bolded verdict word — "Resolved:", "Partially resolvable:", "Indeterminate:", "Strong:", "Weak:", "Contested:", or any judgement-leading word that commits to a position before presenting evidence; this is not a stylistic preference, it is an epistemic posture requirement
  • Customer dynamics: the APM reader of a Q21 memo has limited attention bandwidth across 5-15 positions; J-front formatting allows the reader to skip to the verdict, assess whether it is surprising, and then read the evidence only where the verdict is non-consensus or changes their prior; narrative-front formatting forces the reader to parse the entire bullet to find the verdict, which is an allocation of cognitive cost that the template correctly refuses
  • Supplier dynamics (of evidence): J-front discipline has a supply-side effect on the researcher — committing to a verdict before writing the evidence bullet forces the researcher to have a view before presenting data; this is the correct epistemic sequence (hypothesis → evidence → update) and it catches the common failure mode where a researcher presents data and then avoids committing to what the data implies
  • Competitor landscape: the main "competitor" to J-front discipline is hedge-language — "it appears", "there are indications", "one could argue" — all of which are epistemic retreats that the Q21 template explicitly prohibits; when a researcher finds themselves reaching for hedge language, the correct response is to assign an explicit confidence level ("Low confidence:") rather than hedging the verdict word itself
  • Regulatory: no specific regulatory constraint; however, in regulated-sector KQs, the J-front verdict discipline is especially important because regulatory outcomes are binary (approved / not approved) and the researcher must commit to a probability estimate ("High probability approved:", "Contested regulatory outcome:", "Low probability on current trajectory:") rather than describing the regulatory landscape without a view
  • Technology: J-front discipline is format-stable across output media — it works in Notion bullets, in oral briefings, and in written memos; the APM can request a verbal summary of a Q21 and the researcher should be able to state the J-front verdict for each section in ≤10s, which is a practical test of whether the memo is actually J-front or merely J-front-labelled
  • Historical precedent: the most common J-front failure mode is the "stacked-evidence bullet" — a bullet that presents 3-4 data points and then ends without a verdict; this looks comprehensive but is epistemically empty because it passes the interpretive work to the reader; the researcher's job is to do the interpretation and commit to it
  • Cross-sector analogue: J-front discipline is structurally identical to the bottom-up-led investment process used by the best long-only managers — the PM states a thesis, then tests it against evidence, then updates the thesis; J-front is the memo-level implementation of that process
  • Non-consensus read (Concept A): the non-consensus read on J-front discipline is that it produces overconfidence — by forcing a verdict, the template creates the illusion of more certainty than the evidence warrants; the mitigation is the explicit confidence classification table (§N+1), which labels each section's verdict as High / Medium / Low plausibility; the verdict and the confidence level together are the correct epistemic unit, not the verdict alone
  • Time-series trend: J-front discipline degrades over long memos — researchers maintain it for the first 5-6 sections and then revert to narrative-front in later sections; the validation gate (5A-5K) should check J-front compliance across the entire memo, not just the opening sections
  • Sceptical bullet: the bearish read on J-front discipline is that it is a form of epistemic theatre — a researcher who is genuinely uncertain about a KQ can produce a memo that is perfectly J-front-formatted while being substantively empty; the quality gate is not the format but the quality of the verdict word, which requires the researcher to have genuinely grappled with the evidence before committing; format compliance is necessary but not sufficient

Confidence: High — anchor: the J-front requirement is explicitly stated in the template doctrine and is testable mechanically; the failure modes are well-documented from prior research runs

§4 — Structural Dimension: Confidence Classification Architecture

J-front verdict: the confidence classification table is the most frequently populated-but-not-anchored element — analysts assign "High" without naming the evidence anchor, which makes the table structurally present but epistemically hollow

  • Market structure: the confidence classification table requires three inputs per row — section name, dimension, and anchor (≤25w evidence summary); the anchor is the load-bearing element because it forces the researcher to name the specific evidence that justifies the confidence level; without the anchor, the confidence level is an assertion, not a judgement
  • Customer dynamics: the three confidence levels — High / Medium / Low — map to: (High) multiple independent sources confirm, primary data available, quantified and peer-anchored; (Medium) secondary sources confirm, framework inference consistent with data, no major contradicting evidence; (Low) framework inference only, single source, or significant contradicting evidence present
  • Supplier dynamics: the aggregate confidence verdict uses full phrasing ("high plausibility / medium plausibility / low plausibility") and is explicitly prohibited from shorthand ("HIGH/MED/LOW"); this is not pedantry — the full phrasing forces the researcher to think about the overall evidence pattern rather than averaging individual section scores
  • Competitor landscape: a common error is treating confidence as a fixed attribute of a section rather than a conditional one — e.g., "Market structure confidence: High" is less useful than "Market structure confidence: High IF the expert call on channel dynamics is confirmed; Medium if contradicted"; conditional confidence assignments are more informative for the APM's decision-making
  • Regulatory: in regulatory dimensions, confidence is almost always Low or Medium at DD stage — regulatory outcomes are inherently uncertain, and any researcher who assigns High confidence to a regulatory verdict before the ruling is made is either anchoring on prior rulings (base rate reasoning) without flagging that regulators have discretion, or is not accounting for the political/contextual factors that differentiate this case from precedent
  • Technology: confidence levels should be time-stamped — "Medium confidence as of 2026-05-19, contingent on [event/disclosure/ruling]" — because a KQ that is Low confidence today may become High confidence at an upcoming earnings call or regulatory decision date; the APM needs to know the confidence trajectory, not just the point estimate
  • Historical precedent: the strongest anchor for a confidence level is a quantitative peer base rate — e.g., "High confidence — anchor: 8 of 10 comparable transactions in the 2018-2024 period resolved in favour of the acquirer under this regulatory framework"; the weakest anchor is "consistent with sector experience", which is unfalsifiable
  • Cross-sector analogue: when cross-sector analogues are used as confidence anchors, the researcher must explicitly state the analogy's load-bearing assumptions and where the analogy breaks down; analogies are evidence of plausibility, not probability, and conflating the two is a systematic error in landscape mapping
  • Non-consensus read (Concept A): ⚡ the non-consensus read on confidence classification is that Low confidence sections are often the most informative for the APM — a section where evidence is genuinely thin tells the APM exactly where to direct the next research resource (expert call / field visit / primary channel check); a memo with all-High confidence sections is either genuinely well-evidenced or is a researcher who is not looking hard enough for disconfirming evidence
  • Concept B synthesis: the multiple-truths-coexist read on confidence classification is that a KQ can simultaneously have High confidence on the landscape facts and Low confidence on what those facts imply for the investment outcome; researchers frequently conflate these two — "I know the market is structured this way (High confidence)" vs "I know what this structure implies for EPS trajectory (Low confidence)" are different questions
  • Sceptical bullet: the bearish read on confidence classification is that it creates false precision — assigning "Medium confidence" implies a calibrated epistemic position, but in practice it often means "I'm not sure and I'm hedging"; the discipline of requiring an anchor ≤25w forces the researcher to name the specific evidence that defines the Medium, which is the correct mitigation

Confidence: High — anchor: confidence classification architecture is fully specified in the template with explicit gating (Q21-CC); failure modes are documented and addressable via the anchor requirement

§5 — Structural Dimension: Data Gaps and Resolution Routing

J-front verdict: the data gaps section is chronically underpowered — analysts name gaps but do not route them to resolution methods, leaving the APM with a problem list rather than a next-step agenda

  • Market structure: a well-formed data gap entry has four components — (1) name the gap, (2) state why it is material (what decision rides on resolving it), (3) route it to a resolution method (Expert call / Field visit / Primary filing / Wait-and-monitor), and (4) estimate resolution timeline; most Q21 data gap sections have only component (1) and sometimes (2)
  • Customer dynamics: the canonical signpost vocabulary for data gaps is: "Critical data gap:" (blocks the pillar grade), "Material gap:" (materially affects the grade but doesn't block it), "Minor gap:" (affects confidence level but not the verdict), "Resolvable via:", "Unresolvable from desk:", "Expert call would resolve:", "Field visit would resolve:", "Wait-and-monitor:"; researchers should use these labels consistently because the APM uses them to triage research spend
  • Supplier dynamics: "Unresolvable from desk:" is the most important label — it tells the APM that additional desk research will not close the gap, and that a different research method is required; researchers who do not use this label often produce follow-up Q21 memos on the same gap that add no new information
  • Competitor landscape: the distinction between "Critical data gap:" and "Material gap:" matters for the APM's decision tree — a Critical gap means the APM cannot close the pillar grade without resolving it; a Material gap means the APM can close the grade but with lower confidence; the researcher must make this distinction explicitly rather than leaving it to the APM to infer
  • Regulatory: regulatory data gaps are almost always "Wait-and-monitor:" or "Expert call would resolve:" — the underlying regulatory docket is often public but requires specialist interpretation; a generalist researcher who reads a regulatory filing without a specialist lens often misses the load-bearing details (procedural posture, commissioner signalling, comparable ruling trajectory)
  • Technology: technology data gaps often require "Field visit would resolve:" or "Primary channel check:" — e.g., whether a customer is actually adopting a new product is better resolved by talking to the customer or their systems integrators than by reading sell-side initiation notes or company IR materials
  • Historical precedent: the strongest data gap resolution method is a precisely scoped expert call — "Expert call with [former regulatory affairs VP at comparable company] would resolve [specific question about regulatory interpretation]" is more actionable than "Expert call would resolve regulatory uncertainty"; the researcher should scope the expert call target as specifically as the evidence landscape allows
  • Cross-sector analogue: cross-sector analogues are sometimes the most efficient gap-fillers — if the specific company's data is unavailable, a structurally comparable company's public filings may contain the same information; the researcher should name the specific analogue and the specific filing, not just assert that cross-sector data exists
  • Geographic variance: geographic data gaps are material in UK/Europe-focus research because market structure, regulatory framework, and customer dynamics often differ significantly across geographies; a gap that is resolved for the UK market may remain open for the German or French market, and the researcher must track this explicitly
  • Non-consensus read (Concept A): the non-consensus read on data gaps is that some gaps should not be closed — if closing a gap would require disproportionate research spend relative to its impact on the pillar grade, the correct decision is to hold the position with unresolved uncertainty rather than delay the investment decision; the APM makes this call, but the researcher must provide the decision tree
  • Sceptical bullet: the bearish read on the data gaps section — and the mandatory closing sceptical bullet per template — is: are the data gaps genuinely thin evidence, or is the analyst not looking in the right places? Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 1 says go to the most-different source — the source most likely to hold disconfirming evidence; if the data gaps section names only sources that the analyst has already checked, the section is a list of absences rather than a genuine gap analysis; the most-different source (short-seller reports, competitor IR, regulatory dockets, customer forums, trade press) is rarely in the standard analyst workflow and almost never empty

Confidence: Medium — anchor: data gap architecture is specified in template doctrine but failure modes are observed empirically across runs; the resolution-routing requirement is clear but not mechanically enforceable in the same way that formatting gates are

§6 — Structural Dimension: Second + Third-Order Implications and M/M/O Discipline

J-front verdict: the second/third-order section is the highest-value and most consistently under-delivered element — analysts do surface second-order, but third-order and M/M/O applications are rare and often tokenistic

  • Second-order (of template non-substitution): if a Q21 runs with unsubstituted placeholders — as in this run — the second-order implication is that the downstream APM receives a structural demonstration rather than investment-relevant evidence; the APM's decision is blocked, not informed; the correct response is to flag the gap immediately and re-run with substituted values rather than accepting the hollow output
  • Third-order (of null peer data): if the D-BATCH-25 peer injection is null — as in this run — the third-order implication is that every comparative judgement in the memo is anchored on public base rates rather than cohort-specific data; public base rates are systematically biased toward the median company in the sector, not toward the specific stock under analysis; the APM must apply an additional discount to all comparative claims in the memo
  • Cross-pillar: in a live KQ, the second/third-order section would map how the KQ resolution affects other pillars — e.g., if the KQ is about pricing power, the cross-pillar implications include: margin trajectory (Financials pillar), competitive moat (Business Quality pillar), and management's pricing credibility (Management Quality pillar); a KQ that resolves bearishly on one pillar often has correlated effects on adjacent pillars, and the researcher must name these explicitly
  • Cross-stock: in a live KQ, cross-stock implications would identify whether the KQ resolution has read-across to other positions in the portfolio — e.g., if the KQ is about a regulatory ruling, does the same ruling affect a comparable company in the portfolio?
  • Cross-thematic: in a live KQ, cross-thematic implications would identify whether the KQ resolution is part of a broader sector or macro theme — e.g., a KQ about input cost pass-through in one company may have read-across to the entire input-cost-inflation theme across the portfolio
  • M/M/O application #1 (Means/Motive/Opportunity): in the template architecture, the actor with means + motive + opportunity to drive the KQ toward non-consensus resolution is most often the company's management team — they have means (information asymmetry), motive (valuation management, option vesting, acquisition currency), and opportunity (IR communications, guidance cadence, disclosure timing); the researcher must ask whether the evidence landscape has been shaped by management's IR strategy, and discount accordingly
  • M/M/O application #2: the second actor with M/M/O in most Q21 KQs is the sell-side analyst community — they have means (model architecture, note distribution), motive (deal flow, management access, commission revenue), and opportunity (consensus estimate setting, sector coverage framing); sell-side evidence is systematically biased toward management-aligned narratives, and the researcher must weight [AS] expert calls above sell-side notes precisely because expert calls are less subject to this M/M/O dynamic
  • Cross-roads exposure: the cross-roads exposure that the standard landscape mapping most often misses is technology disruption at the margin of the core business — companies rarely disclose that a new technology is materially threatening their business model until the threat is already visible in revenue trends; the researcher must explicitly ask whether the KQ landscape contains early-stage disruption signals that are visible in the cross-sector analogue or technology dimension but not yet in the company's financials
  • Power-of-incentives: the incentive structure of the research process itself is a second-order factor — the APM who has already formed a thesis will (consciously or not) frame the KQ in a way that is more likely to confirm the thesis; the researcher must be alert to this and should explicitly check whether the KQ is framed in a way that presupposes the answer
  • Inversion: the inversion test on any Q21 KQ is: "if the KQ resolves in the opposite direction to the consensus landscape view, what is the magnitude of the case impact?" — if the magnitude is small, the KQ may not be worth the research resource; if the magnitude is large (>1 pillar grade change, >15% valuation impact), the KQ is load-bearing and the full Q21 + Q22 pair is warranted
  • Sceptical bullet — the flinch surface: the second-order implication the analyst is most likely flinching from in any Q21 is the base-rate failure mode — the specific company looks exceptional on the landscape metrics, but the base rate for companies that look exceptional at DD stage is that roughly 40-60% disappoint within 24M of investment; the researcher must explicitly ask whether the exceptional landscape reading is genuine or is the product of selection bias (looking at the company from the perspective of a bullish case thesis rather than a neutral landscape probe)

Confidence: Medium-High — anchor: M/M/O discipline and second/third-order framework are well-specified in the template; the gap is that without a live KQ, these applications are illustrative rather than evidence-based
