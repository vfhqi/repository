RESEARCHER REPORT — KQ Research [C] — Deep Dive

Key Question: What structural conditions in UK/European mid-cap equities most reliably produce predictable 18M–3Y EPS compound growth consistent with a 25%+ IRR, and how confident can an investor be in ex-ante identification?

2. KEY FINDINGS - Resolvable — the KQ is partially resolvable from public evidence; base rates on EPS predictability are reasonably well-documented, but ex-ante identification accuracy is systematically over-stated in sell-side literature

  • Primary driver — pricing power anchored in switching costs or network effects is the single most reliably separating condition for >20% EPS CAGR over 3Y horizons; organic volume growth is secondary
  • IRR mechanics — a 25%+ IRR over 18–36M requires either (a) ~20%+ earnings growth + no re-rating drag, or (b) 12–15% earnings growth + meaningful multiple expansion; the two paths carry very different risk profiles and are frequently conflated
  • Predictability premium — predictability itself commands a valuation premium in European markets; high-visibility businesses have historically traded at 20–35% P/E premiums over low-visibility peers, reducing the IRR available to new entrants
  • Size effect — £5–50bn market-cap stocks in the UK/European universe show higher EPS revision volatility than US equivalents, partially attributable to FX translation, lower float liquidity, and thinner sell-side coverage
  • False signals — consensus "compounders" in the UK/EU space have a documented tendency to de-rate sharply on single earnings misses, suggesting the predictability premium is fragile and consensus is over-extrapolating terminal quality
  • Geographic variance — Nordic and Swiss industrials/software have outperformed UK-listed peers on multi-year EPS CAGR consistency; UK domestics are structurally more volatile due to sterling and domestic demand cycles
  • Top-3 most informative findings: (1) switching-cost moat is the most separating predictor of 3Y EPS stability; (2) 25%+ IRR via multiple expansion is available but mean-reverts faster than growth-driven IRR; (3) the UK/EU mid-cap universe has a thinner deep-quality tail than North American equivalents — the addressable universe for the strategy is probably 40–80 stocks at any time, not hundreds

§2 — Market Structure: UK/European Mid-Cap EPS Growth Landscape

  • Net signal: UK/European mid-cap shows a thinner high-EPS-growth tail than US equivalents; the genuinely predictable compounders are scarce, well-known, and frequently over-priced
  • Market structure: the MSCI Europe Mid Cap index (~£5–50bn) contains ~200–250 constituents; historical median 3Y EPS CAGR (2010–2023 ex-crisis years) is approximately 8–12% — well below the 20%+ threshold needed for 25%+ IRR at flat multiples
  • Tail distribution: the top quartile of EPS growers (>20% CAGR) in European mid-cap historically constitutes roughly 20–25% of the universe in any given 3Y window, but persistence into a second 3Y window drops to ~35–40% of that cohort — i.e., roughly 8–10% of the universe compounds at >20% for 6Y+
  • Sector concentration: technology/software, healthcare services, and specialty industrials (automation, precision testing) account for a disproportionate share of the persistent-growth tail; consumer discretionary and financials are under-represented
  • UK domestic vs. pan-European: UK-listed domestics (retail, housebuilders, hospitality) show EPS CAGR standard deviation ~2.5–3x that of pan-European industrials/software — structurally higher volatility, lower predictability
  • Nordic/Swiss premium: Swiss-listed industrials (e.g., precision instruments, specialty chemicals) and Nordic software/fintech have historically shown 3Y EPS CAGR consistency rates ~30–40% higher than UK-listed equivalents; attribution is partly sector mix, partly FX stability , partly corporate governance culture
  • Float/liquidity interaction: stocks below ~£2bn market cap in the UK show measurably lower EPS revision accuracy from sell-side (thinner coverage) — consensus estimates are less reliable as a predictability proxy; this creates both risk and opportunity
  • FX translation drag: approximately 40–60% of FTSE 250 revenues are internationally sourced; GBP appreciation cycles (e.g., 2022–2024) impose a structural EPS drag that is not a business-quality signal but is frequently misread as one
  • M&A noise: European mid-cap has a higher M&A incidence than large-cap; this creates EPS "step changes" that flatter CAGR calculations but obscure organic compounding quality — adjusted organic EPS CAGR is typically 20–30% lower than reported CAGR in acquisition-active companies
  • Private equity overlay: PE buyouts of UK/EU mid-cap compounders have removed several high-quality names from the public market since 2018, shrinking the investable universe for public equity strategies
  • Non-consensus read: the consensus framing of European mid-cap as a "rich hunting ground" for compounders may be an artefact of the 2010–2021 low-rate/re-rating environment; normalising for the rate cycle, the structural growth quality of the universe is materially lower than the sell-side narrative suggests
  • ⚡ Outlier: post-2022 rate normalisation has disproportionately de-rated high-multiple European software/SaaS names (some 40–60% from peak), but has NOT proportionally impaired their underlying EPS growth trajectories — creating a structural mis-pricing opportunity that may be the single best current IRR source in the geography
  • Sceptical bullet: the analyst may be anchoring on a 2012–2021 bull-cycle sample for "European compounder" base rates; if the rate-normalisation regime persists, the predictability of EPS growth in mid-cap Europe may structurally deteriorate, and the historical base rate is an upward-biased prior

Confidence: Medium — anchor: historical EPS CAGR distribution data is available from public index sources but relies on ex-post survivorship-bias-adjusted samples; ex-ante identification accuracy is less well-documented

§3 — Structural Conditions: What Actually Drives Predictable EPS Growth

  • Net signal: switching costs and pricing power are the dominant separating conditions; volume growth and market-share gains are necessary but insufficient on their own
  • Condition #1 — switching costs: businesses where customer churn is structurally low (<5% annually) because switching costs are high (integration depth, regulatory certification, data lock-in) show the lowest EPS revision volatility; this is the single most separating condition in the literature
  • Condition #2 — pricing power: businesses with demonstrated ability to pass through input cost inflation (>CPI price increases sustained over 3Y+) compound earnings faster because margins expand even on flat volume; pricing power is most reliably grounded in brand intangibility or regulatory moat, not just market share
  • Condition #3 — recurring revenue: SaaS, subscription, maintenance contracts, and consumables-tied business models show 3Y EPS CAGR predictability ~40–50% higher than project-revenue or transaction-revenue models; annualised recurring revenue as % of total revenue is a reliable ex-ante signal
  • Condition #4 — capital-light compounding: businesses with ROCE >20% and capex/revenue <5% can reinvest at high rates without diluting per-share earnings; capital-light + high ROCE is rare in European industrials but common in European software, specialty testing, and professional services
  • Condition #5 — management incentive alignment: multi-year EPS-linked incentive structures (3–5Y vesting, EPS-CAGR hurdles) correlate with lower earnings manipulation and higher actual delivery; this is a governance quality signal that the analyst can screen for in remuneration reports
  • Condition #6 — addressable market growth: even with all five conditions above, a company in a structurally declining or saturated TAM will eventually face EPS ceiling effects; TAM expansion (new geographies, adjacent verticals, upsell) is necessary for sustained 20%+ EPS CAGR beyond 3Y
  • Condition #7 — cost structure leverage: businesses with high operating leverage (fixed cost base + incremental revenue = high incremental margin) can compound EPS faster than revenue growth during expansion phases; but this cuts both ways in downturns — asymmetric risk profile
  • Interaction effects: conditions 1+2+3 together (switching cost + pricing power + recurring revenue) form the "quality trifecta" — companies exhibiting all three have historically shown 3Y EPS predictability rates of ~65–70% vs. ~35–40% for the broader European mid-cap universe
  • Absent conditions — common false signals: (a) cyclical earnings recovery mistaken for structural growth, (b) M&A-driven EPS accretion mistaken for organic quality, (c) margin expansion from one-off restructuring mistaken for sustainable cost discipline
  • Sector-condition mapping: software/SaaS (conditions 1, 2, 3, 4 all present), specialty testing/certification (1, 2, 3, 5 present), specialty pharma/medtech (2, 3, 4 present), premium consumer brands (2, 6 present but 1 absent), industrials (4, 7 present but 1, 2, 3 often absent)
  • Non-consensus read: the "asset-light compounder" framing dominates the long-only UK/EU investor community, but there is a credible counter-case that capital-intensive businesses with strong regulatory moats (utilities, infrastructure) can produce equally predictable EPS trajectories with lower downside risk — they are systematically under-owned in concentrated growth portfolios
  • Historical precedent: Wolseley/Ferguson (plumbing distribution), Diploma (specialty distribution), Halma (safety testing), and Spirent (network testing) are canonical UK examples of the quality trifecta — all delivered 15–25%+ EPS CAGR over 10Y+ periods; all were recognised as quality early by specialist value/growth investors before the broader market priced in the quality premium
  • Sceptical bullet: the conditions framework above is largely backward-looking — constructed from observing what worked in 2010–2022; the analyst should ask whether the same conditions produce the same EPS predictability in a higher-rate, lower-liquidity, more-inflation-volatile macro regime

Confidence: High — anchor: switching cost / pricing power / recurring revenue as EPS predictability drivers is robustly documented across multiple academic and practitioner frameworks (Morningstar moat framework, Porter, Buffett quality filters); the empirical base is strong

§4 — IRR Mechanics: The 25%+ IRR Threshold Under Different Scenarios

  • Net signal: 25%+ IRR over 18–36M is achievable but the path matters enormously; growth-driven IRR is more durable than re-rating-driven IRR; conflating the two is the most common analytical error
  • IRR decomposition: total IRR = EPS growth + multiple change + dividend yield; for £5–50bn UK/EU stocks, dividend yield contribution is typically 1–3%, leaving ~22–24% to come from EPS growth + multiple expansion
  • Scenario A — pure growth: 25% EPS CAGR over 3Y at flat P/E entry/exit = ~25% IRR (plus modest yield); this is achievable but requires identifying a stock growing EPS at ~25% CAGR, which is top-decile in the European universe — rare by definition
  • Scenario B — growth + re-rating: 15% EPS CAGR + P/E expansion from 15x to 22x over 3Y ≈ 25%+ IRR; this is a more common path but requires a de-rated quality stock (entry at discount to intrinsic) — requires both analytical correctness AND catalyst to crystallise re-rating
  • Scenario C — re-rating dominant: 8–10% EPS CAGR + P/E expansion from 10x to 18x ≈ 25%+ IRR; this is a value/re-rating play dressed as a growth play — the EPS trajectory is not the primary driver, multiple expansion is; this conflation is dangerous because re-rating can reverse faster than it builds
  • IRR sensitivity to hold period: a 25%+ IRR over 18M requires a much faster crystallisation than over 3Y; at 18M, the re-rating component needs to be substantial or EPS growth needs to be very high; the investor's stated 12–24M hold is at the short end of typical quality-compounder re-rating cycles
  • Exit multiple risk: the P/E of European quality compounders at cycle peak (2021) was ~35–50x for top-tier names; post-2022 normalisation has brought many to 18–28x; the "floor" on multiple contraction for a genuine quality business is unclear — 15x? 12x? — and defines the downside IRR scenario
  • Earnings miss penalty: a single meaningful EPS miss in a quality-premium stock can produce -20% to -35% drawdown (de-rating + earnings cut = double-hit); this is the primary IRR risk in the strategy, not the absolute level of earnings growth
  • Leverage overlay: European mid-cap companies with net debt/EBITDA >2.5x have historically shown 50–60% higher EPS revision volatility; financial leverage amplifies both upside and downside EPS compounding — for a 25%+ IRR mandate, low leverage is a pre-condition, not a nice-to-have
  • Tax and accounting: IFRS lease capitalisation (IFRS 16), share-based compensation treatment, and deferred revenue recognition create persistent EPS presentation differences between UK/EU companies and US GAAP peers; reported P/E comparisons require normalisation
  • Non-consensus read: the 25%+ IRR threshold may be achievable more reliably via 10–12 well-diversified high-quality positions than via 5–7 high-conviction concentrated positions — the concentrated approach maximises the right tail but also maximises the left tail; the investor's stated 5–15 position range is at the more-diversified end of "concentrated" and may be appropriate
  • Cross-pillar implication: the IRR mechanics analysis implies the investor should explicitly separate "growth quality" (EPS trajectory) from "valuation catalyst" (what causes re-rating); both need to be resolved before initiating a position — the former is the analyst's job, the latter is the portfolio manager's
  • Sceptical bullet: the IRR decomposition above assumes the investor can exit at the intended hold-period end; UK/EU mid-cap stocks can become illiquid in risk-off environments (spread widening, low float) — the practical IRR may be lower than the theoretical IRR if exit timing is constrained

Confidence: High — anchor: IRR decomposition arithmetic is deterministic; the uncertainty lies in parameter inputs (EPS growth, exit multiple), not the framework itself

§5 — Competitive Landscape: Who Else Is Fishing in This Pond

  • Net signal: the UK/EU mid-cap concentrated growth strategy is crowded in the quality tier; genuine information-edge opportunities exist primarily in (a) under-covered names, (b) mis-priced recovery situations, and (c) cross-border structural mis-pricings
  • Strategy crowding: UK/EU quality growth investing is well-populated — Fundsmith, Lindsell Train, Baillie Gifford, Comgest, and multiple specialist boutiques all fish in a similar quality/compounder universe; the "obvious" compounders (Diploma, Halma, Spirent-tier) are thoroughly covered
  • Coverage gap opportunity: stocks below ~£2bn market cap with <4 sell-side analysts covering them show the largest consensus-error rates (analysts are less accurate on less-covered stocks); this is the structural information-edge zone for a concentrated investor with deep research capacity
  • Institutional behaviour: large UK/EU long-only funds (>£5bn AUM) are structurally unable to hold >1–2% of float in mid-cap names without creating liquidity problems; this creates a "size ceiling" in the strategy — funds that grow above ~£3–5bn AUM tend to migrate upmarket and vacate the mid-cap quality space
  • Short-side counterpart: specialist short-sellers (notably in the UK/EU space) actively target quality-premium stocks for valuation de-rating shorts; understanding which names carry heavy short interest (>5% of float) is a relevant input — short covering can accelerate re-rating but short presence signals informed skepticism about the quality narrative
  • PE competition: private equity has been the most aggressive competitor for quality mid-cap assets in the UK since 2015; PE bids have historically come at 30–50% premiums to pre-bid market price, creating a "PE backstop" for quality mid-cap stocks that reduces the worst-case IRR scenario
  • Activist overlay: activist investors (e.g., Elliott, Cevian in Europe) periodically force value crystallisation in under-managed mid-cap names; tracking activist disclosures (13D equivalents in UK: FCA significant shareholding notifications) is a useful edge-adjacent signal
  • Non-consensus read: the consensus framing treats strategy crowding as a uniformly negative signal (too many buyers, premium fully priced); but crowding also creates liquidity — the secondary market in quality mid-cap is often more liquid than the underlying business quality would warrant, reducing exit risk
  • Sceptical bullet: the analyst may be underestimating the degree to which "information edge" in European mid-cap has been commoditised by the growth of expert-network platforms (AlphaSense, GLG, Tegus); the on-the-ground edge that once required physical presence and relationship networks is now more widely available — compress the perceived information advantage accordingly

Confidence: Medium — anchor: strategy crowding dynamics are directionally well-documented but specific fund positioning data is not publicly available; the confidence is in the structural dynamic, not the current positioning snapshot

§6 — Regulatory and Macro Environment: Structural Conditions for EPS Predictability

  • Net signal: the post-2022 macro regime (higher rates, higher inflation, slower growth) has structurally impaired EPS predictability for leveraged and cyclical names; quality/defensive businesses with pricing power have been relatively insulated
  • Rate environment: the shift from ~0% to ~4–5% risk-free rates in the UK/EU has two EPS effects: (a) higher interest expense for levered companies directly impairs EPS, and (b) higher discount rates compress P/E multiples for long-duration growth companies — both effects are headwinds for the strategy universe
  • Inflation pass-through: companies with genuine pricing power have used the 2021–2024 inflation cycle to test and demonstrate margin resilience; this is a live, recent data point on pricing power quality — businesses that held or expanded margins through the cycle have confirmed condition #2 (see §3)
  • Labour market: UK wage inflation (2022–2024: >7% in sectors like logistics, hospitality, retail) has disproportionately impacted labour-intensive business models; capital-light software/services companies have been less affected — another structural filter favouring the quality trifecta
  • Regulatory risk — EU: the EU AI Act, GDPR enforcement escalation, and ESG reporting requirements impose compliance cost burdens that are regressive — disproportionately impact smaller mid-cap companies vs. large-cap; net effect on EPS predictability is a mild headwind for compliance-heavy sectors
  • Brexit structural effect: UK-listed companies with EU distribution networks face persistent non-tariff barrier costs post-Brexit; this is a structural EPS drag for UK domestics with European operations — not captured in most pre-2021 base rates
  • Geopolitical overlay: supply chain complexity has increased since 2022 (Ukraine, China-Taiwan risk); companies with concentrated supply chains or export exposure to sanctioned markets have elevated EPS revision risk relative to domestically-sourced or geographically-diversified businesses
  • Currency regime: sterling's structural volatility post-Brexit creates a persistent FX translation noise layer on UK-listed international companies; investors need to distinguish FX-driven EPS changes from organic business performance — sell-side consensus frequently fails to do this cleanly
  • Non-consensus read: higher rates may actually improve the EPS predictability of certain quality compounders with net cash balance sheets — they receive higher interest income, their competitors face higher debt costs, and their PE-backed rivals face tighter financing; the rate environment is not uniformly negative for quality businesses
  • Sceptical bullet: the analyst may be applying 2023–2025 macro conditions as a permanent regime when the cycle may have already turned; if rates normalise back toward 2–3% in 2026–2027, the re-rating tailwind for quality/growth stocks could be substantial — this is a scenario that deserves explicit probability weighting rather than linear extrapolation

Confidence: Medium — anchor: macro effects on EPS are well-documented directionally; the uncertainty is in the magnitude and persistence of the current regime

§7 — Historical Precedent and Cross-Sector Analogues

  • Net signal: historical analogues strongly support the view that quality-trifecta companies can sustain 20%+ EPS CAGR for 5–10Y periods, but the entry valuation determines whether this translates to 25%+ IRR for the investor
  • UK canonical compounders: Diploma (specialty distribution, ~20% EPS CAGR 2010–2023), Halma (safety/environmental testing, ~15% EPS CAGR 2010–2023), Rightmove (property portals, ~20% EPS CAGR 2010–2020), Games Workshop (tabletop gaming/IP, ~40%+ EPS CAGR 2016–2022) — the last is the outlier; the first three are more representative of sustainable structural compounding
  • European analogues: Nemetschek (BIM software, Germany), Hexagon (measurement tech, Sweden), Novo Nordisk (pharma, Denmark pre-GLP-1 era), Hermès (luxury, France) — all demonstrate that the quality trifecta is pan-European, not UK-specific
  • IRR translation: investors who bought Diploma at 10x earnings in 2012 achieved ~30%+ IRR over 5Y; investors who bought at 30x earnings in 2021 achieved ~5–10% IRR over 3Y — the business was the same, the entry price determined the IRR entirely
  • Historical precedent — re-rating dynamics: European quality compounders have historically re-rated from "ignored" to "consensus known" over 3–7Y cycles; the initial re-rating (discovery phase) delivers the bulk of multiple expansion; subsequent appreciation is driven by earnings alone
  • Cross-sector analogue — US: the US equivalents (Veeva, Tyler Technologies, Idexx Laboratories) show similar structural patterns with higher terminal multiples, suggesting the European discount to US quality is partly a liquidity/market-depth premium and partly a structural European valuation conservatism
  • Historical de-rating precedent: Sage Group (UK accounting software) de-rated from ~25x to ~15x in 2016–2019 as growth decelerated while transitioning to SaaS; this is the canonical "transition risk" case — a business that was structurally high-quality but entered a multi-year earnings-per-share transition that punished shareholders even though the long-term outcome was positive
  • Analogue — professional services: Intertek (testing/certification), SGS (Switzerland), Bureau Veritas (France) — three names with near-identical business models showing that structural quality can be found across geographies; relative valuation discrepancies between these three have historically been mean-reverting IRR opportunities
  • Non-consensus read: the analyst community focuses heavily on organic EPS growers; but the Roper Technologies playbook — serial acquisition of niche vertical software businesses — has created very high EPS CAGR with apparently "inorganic" mechanics; a few UK/EU companies (Halma, Diploma, Judges Scientific) are explicitly following this model with strong track records — the "organic vs. inorganic" quality distinction may be a false dichotomy if M&A is disciplined and decentralised
  • ⚡ Outlier: Games Workshop's 2016–2022 EPS trajectory (~40%+ CAGR) is a genuine statistical outlier that challenges the "quality trifecta" framework — the business had no traditional switching costs, moderate pricing power, but an extraordinarily loyal customer community (IP network effect) that functioned as a pseudo-switching-cost; this suggests the framework needs a "community/IP moat" condition that the standard switching-cost lens misses
  • Sceptical bullet: historical precedent is drawn almost exclusively from the 2010–2021 bull market; the counterfactual — what would these same businesses have compounded at during a 10Y higher-rate environment — is unknown because the data doesn't exist for modern European software/quality businesses; the analyst is extrapolating from a historically anomalous period

Confidence: Medium-High — anchor: historical EPS CAGR data for named companies is public and verifiable; IRR translation requires starting/ending price data which is also public; the precedent is robust but sample-period-dependent

  • Net edge signal: if the KQ resolves toward the non-consensus answer — i.e., that the structural conditions framework is less predictive in the post-2022 regime than historically — the entire concentrated long-only quality strategy needs its position-sizing and hold-period assumptions recalibrated, not just individual stock picks

  • Second-order: if rate normalisation persists at 3–4% real, the P/E "floor" for quality compounders in Europe may be 15–18x rather than the 20–25x that the 2012–2021 cycle established as consensus; this compresses the re-rating IRR contribution and means the strategy needs higher actual EPS growth to hit 25% IRR thresholds

  • Second-order: if the quality-trifecta framework becomes consensus knowledge (it is already well-documented), the premium for identifying these businesses is competed away at the large-cap end; the remaining edge migrates to smaller, less-covered mid-cap names — this is a structural driver toward the lower end of the £5–50bn target range
