§7 — Value Chain — Competition

  • Net signal: Injectable aesthetics competitive intensity is structurally high and increasing (biosimilars, new entrants, Revance); Therapeutics competitive intensity manageable near-term given Nemolizumab's differentiated mechanism [#J]

  • Industry structure: Injectable aesthetics is an oligopoly — AbbVie/Allergan (Botox, Juvederm), Galderma (Dysport, Restylane), Ipsen (Dysport licensee in some markets), Merz (Xeomin), Evolus (Jeuveau); top 3 control ~80%+ of global neurotoxin revenues [#A]

  • Intensity: Competition in aesthetics intensifying: Revance's daxibotulinumtoxinA (Daxxify) launched US 2023 with "longer duration" claim; Evolus growing US share via aggressive pricing; combined pressure on Galderma's US Dysport share [#A]

  • US Dysport: Dysport holds ~12-15% US neurotoxin market; share gains in US constrained by Botox incumbent dominance; US is the largest and most competitive aesthetics market

  • ❌ New competitor: Revance (Daxxify) + Evolus (Jeuveau) are both growing US neurotoxin market share at the expense of #2 and #3 players (Dysport and Xeomin); AbbVie/Allergan retaining and in some data sets gaining share as market leader [#A]

  • Market structure: Dermal fillers (Restylane vs Juvederm): Galderma holds a stronger position in fillers vs neurotoxin — Restylane approximately co-equal with Juvederm globally; Chinese filler market is faster-growing but more price-sensitive [#A]

  • Disruption threat — GLP-1: GLP-1 drugs (semaglutide, tirzepatide) for obesity are beginning to show aesthetic procedure volume impacts — some data suggesting weight-loss patients reducing filler volume use; magnitude unclear but a new and actively-watched risk [#A]

  • Mechanism: GLP-1-induced facial volume loss could increase toxin/filler demand (for contouring lost volume) OR reduce it (fewer patients seeking rejuvenation); directional ambiguity is itself a risk

  • Therapeutics competition: AD competitive space: Dupixent (Sanofi/Regeneron) is the dominant biologic (~$13bn revenue FY2024); Nemolizumab targets IL-31 receptor (itch pathway) — differentiated vs Dupixent's IL-4/13 mechanism; Adbry (tralokinumab, LEO Pharma) also in the competitive set [#A]

  • BtE: Nemolizumab's differentiation (rapid itch relief, different MOA) creates a viable niche but Dupixent's installed base + payer formulary position creates a high barrier to leading-line penetration

  • Competitor health: AbbVie/Allergan is very well-capitalised and has demonstrated willingness to invest in Botox franchise defence; Revance is cash-constrained (pre-profitability); Evolus is small-cap with limited financial depth [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish competitive read: Galderma occupies a persistent #2 position in its largest segment (injectable aesthetics) in the most important market ; the structural disadvantage of #2 in a market where #1 (AbbVie/Botox) has 45%+ share and deep payer/physician relationships means Galderma cannot drive a re-rating through aesthetics market share gains alone; the investment case is almost entirely dependent on Nemolizumab execution, which is still early-stage [#J]

§8 — Value Chain — Disruption

  • Net signal: The GLP-1/obesity drug aesthetic impact is a new and underpriced disruption signal; biosimilar neurotoxin is the better-understood risk; AI-aided personalised treatment planning is an early-stage positive/neutral disruption [#J]

  • Technology disruption: AI-powered treatment planning and injectable administration guidance tools are being adopted by aesthetic practices; early evidence these tools increase procedure rates (volume positive for Galderma) by reducing physician friction [#A]

  • New entrant — innovative: Revance (Daxxify) is the most significant innovative new entrant in the last 5 years in aesthetics; differentiation claim (longer duration — 6 months vs 3-4 months for Botox/Dysport) has not yet translated into dominant market share gains due to higher price point [#I]

  • Disruption pace: Daxxify penetration slower than feared at launch; current US neurotoxin market share estimated at ~3-5%; threat has not yet materialised at scale

  • New entrant — traditional: Korean aesthetics manufacturers (Hugel, Medytox, Daewoong) are aggressively expanding internationally with lower-priced neurotoxin and filler products; Chinese filler market already experiencing Korean entrant pricing pressure [#A]

  • Disruption cost: Korean entrant pressure primarily affects emerging market and China pricing, where Galderma generates ~20% of revenues

  • GLP-1 disruption: ⚡ RARE: GLP-1 aesthetic impact: emerging evidence (KOL commentary, early survey data) that GLP-1-driven facial fat loss is increasing demand for volumizing fillers while simultaneously reducing typical anti-wrinkle toxin procedure frequency; directional ambiguity creates unpriceable demand volatility specific to Galderma's dual aesthetics exposure; no other large-cap dermatology pure-play has equivalent bilateral exposure [Cross-ref: §7 Competition, §6 Demand] [#J]

  • Biosimilar threat: Botulinum toxin biosimilars are technically complex (large molecule, complex manufacturing); no FDA-approved neurotoxin biosimilar currently marketed in the US; regulatory pathway exists but manufacturing barriers mean 5+ year horizon before meaningful biosimilar market share [#A]

  • Incumbent response: Galderma's moat vs biosimilars is relatively strong due to brand equity and physician training investment

  • Adoption curve: Injectable aesthetics adoption curve still early globally; ~1% of global population has received an aesthetic injectable treatment; headroom is large and partially insulates Galderma from pure substitution disruption [#A]

  • Disruption pace: Therapeutic disruption (IL-31 vs IL-4/13 mechanism competition in AD) moving at a pace consistent with specialty pharma biologics timelines; next competitive wave (new IL-31 competitors) likely 5-7 year horizon [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish disruption read: GLP-1 aesthetic impact could develop faster and more directionally negative than current literature suggests; the Korean/Chinese entrant pricing pressure in emerging markets could accelerate into European markets if regulatory approvals proceed; the combination of biosimilar timeline uncertainty + GLP-1 demand ambiguity + Korean pricing pressure means disruption risk is multi-axis and partially unpriceable — a difficult feature for a 18-36 month investment case [#J]

§9 — Value Chain — Political and Regulatory

  • Net signal: Regulatory environment is net positive in the near term — Nemolizumab FDA approval is a transformative catalyst; EU approval in process; China regulatory tightening is the primary political/regulatory headwind [#J]

  • Regulatory shift — Nemolizumab: FDA approval of Nemolizumab (brand name: Nemluvio) for atopic dermatitis and prurigo nodularis, March 2025; EU regulatory submission made, approval expected H2 2025 / early 2026; regulatory success is the primary catalyst for the therapeutics segment inflection [#I]

  • Geography — China: Chinese NMPA (National Medical Products Administration) tightening on aesthetic injectable products — increased import scrutiny, new labelling requirements, stricter advertising standards for aesthetic procedures; creates a regulatory cost drag and potential timing delays on product launches [#A]

  • Political actor: Chinese government's broader "anti-extravagance" cultural policy creating demand and regulatory headwinds for foreign luxury/aesthetic brands specifically

  • Geography — US: FDA's evolving position on non-surgical aesthetics — no material negative regulatory shifts expected; Galderma's established US regulatory relationships (long-standing Dysport/Restylane approvals) are an advantage [#I]

  • Tariff: No material tariff exposure for Galderma's primary products; aesthetic injectables manufactured in EU/US and subject to normal pharmaceutical tariff treatment; no material tariff escalation risk from US-EU trade policy as at date of research [#I]

  • Compliance cost: Swiss listing subjects Galderma to Swiss Code of Obligations + EU market practice (given investor base); compliance infrastructure cost is a one-time investment post-IPO, not a recurring escalating cost [#I]

  • Environmental regulation: Galderma's biologics manufacturing has EU REACH and EMA environmental compliance requirements; no material new environmental regulatory risk flagged in public filings [#I]

  • Pricing/reimbursement policy: European healthcare systems increasing reimbursement scrutiny on biologics; German Arzneimittelmarkt-Neuordnungsgesetz (AMNOG) process and equivalent processes could limit Nemolizumab EU reimbursement to narrower-than-expected patient populations, reducing the EU revenue ramp [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish regulatory read: the EU reimbursement pathway for Nemolizumab faces AMNOG-style value assessment processes that have historically constrained net pricing for new AD biologics to below US launch prices by 40-60%; if Nemolizumab EU net prices come in at the low end of historical precedent, the EU revenue contribution to the 2028 model could be materially below management's investor-day framing [#J]

§10 — Value Chain — Supply Side

  • Net signal: Supply-side risks are manageable; biologic manufacturing (Nemolizumab) is partially outsourced and represents the primary supply concentration risk [#J]

  • Supplier economics: Galderma's injectable aesthetics products (Dysport, Restylane) manufactured primarily at its own facilities (Schlieren, Switzerland; Uppsala, Sweden); internal manufacturing for core aesthetics is a supply security advantage [#I]

  • Concentration: Nemolizumab is a biologic (monoclonal antibody) manufactured at contract manufacturing organisations (CMOs); CMO concentration risk is present but Galderma has not publicly disclosed the number of CMO partners; single-source CMO for a key biologic would be a material supply risk [#A]

  • Supply risk: FDA/EMA manufacturing inspection risk at CMO sites is a real but low-probability event; precedent for CMO audit failures causing commercial supply disruptions exists in the broader biopharma sector

  • Relationship: Hyaluronic acid sourcing for Restylane fillers — HA is a commodity input; no supply concentration risk identified; multiple suppliers available [#I]

  • Vertical integration: Galderma has historically preferred internal manufacturing for small-molecule and HA-based products; biologic outsourcing is a sector-standard approach for a company of Galderma's scale [#I]

  • Supplier health: No material supplier financial distress indicators in public sources; core material suppliers are large-scale chemical/biotech intermediary suppliers [#I]

  • Backward integration: No signal of backward integration investment in biologic manufacturing; capital allocation priority is deleveraging, not capex-intensive backward integration [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish supply read: if Nemolizumab demand exceeds forecast (the optimistic scenario), CMO capacity constraints could limit Galderma's ability to supply the commercial demand ramp; in the specialty biologic market, capacity constraints have historically been the binding commercial constraint in years 1-3 post-launch (reference: Dupixent launch 2017-2019); under-disclosed CMO dependency is a structurally under-priced risk [#J]

  • Peer anchor: Consumer medical aesthetics broadly trades at a cyclicality premium vs pharmaceutical-only peers; investor valuation frameworks must discount for aesthetics cycle exposure

  • Predictability: Dermatology Therapeutics (Epiduo, Differin, and Nemolizumab) revenues are reimbursement-backed and significantly more predictable; Skincare (Cetaphil) is stable but has some consumer-cycle sensitivity [#A]

  • Cycle stage: US and European consumers are in a late-cycle consumer spending environment as of H1 2025; discretionary elective medical spending showing early signs of softening in some geographies per industry commentary [#A]

  • Through-cycle profile: Galderma's consolidated through-cycle profile (including Therapeutics) is better than pure aesthetics plays; the Therapeutics growth engine is a structural cycle dampener if the launch succeeds [#J]

  • Demand pulse: 2021-2023 aesthetics demand was a classic demand pulse (post-COVID catch-up); structural demand in aesthetics likely settling at 6-8% CAGR vs the 10-15% pulse rates seen 2021-2022 [#A]

  • Pulse caution: Management's mid-to-high single digit organic growth guidance may still embed a demand pulse residual in aesthetics; structural growth may prove 1-2 percentage points lower
