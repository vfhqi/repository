KEY FINDINGS

- Pure electrification player. Nexans completed a multi-year portfolio rotation by divesting non-core assets (Aginode, AmerCable, Lynxeo, and now electric to Motherson for €207m). The remaining business is 100% electrification: submarine HVDC cables, onshore HV/MV grids, and low-voltage connectivity.

- Structural demand tailwind. Europe needs ~80 million km of new or refurbished cable by 2040. Offshore wind interconnectors, grid modernisation, EV charging, heat pump rollout, and data centre power demand provide multi-decade secular growth. This is not cyclical demand.

  • Oligopoly economics in HVDC. Three players — Prysmian, Nexans, NKT — control ~75% of the global HVDC cable market. Lead times exceed 5 years. Nexans' Halden facility is sold out through 2028. Barriers to entry are extreme: specialised manufacturing, cable-laying vessels, and decades of project execution know-how.

- €8.1bn transmission backlog. Record adjusted backlog at end-2025, up 9.7% YoY. Provides exceptional revenue visibility through 2028+. Backlog quality is high: utility counterparties, framework agreements (e.g. >€1bn with RTE), milestone billing.

  • Accelerating financial profile. Standard sales +8.3% organic in 2025. Adjusted EBITDA €728m (+27.3% YoY). EBITDA margin 11.9% — all-time high. FCF €344m (up from €177m). Net debt collapsed to €266m from €681m. ROCE 21.6% group, 27.5% electrification.

- 2028 target: €1,150m EBITDA. Implies ~58% EBITDA growth from 2025 actual, or ~16% CAGR. Cumulative FCF 2025-28 guided at ~€1.4bn. ROCE >20%. €1.2bn capex investment. Credible given backlog visibility and secular tailwinds.

- 2026 guidance disappointed. €730-810m adjusted EBITDA vs consensus €782m. H1 expected softer. GSI (Great Sea Interconnector) excluded from guidance. Market sold the stock down — now trades at ~€118, well below consensus PT of €141.

- CEO transition risk. Christopher Guérin (architect of the transformation) departed October 2025. Replaced by Julien Hueber, an internal promotion. Continuity risk is real but mitigated by Hueber's 23-year tenure and deep operational experience.

  • GSI overhang. The €1.43bn Great Sea Interconnector (Greece-Cyprus) project has been frozen by both governments. Tender cancellation. Material backlog item in limbo. Resolution unclear — downside risk to backlog if permanently cancelled.

  • Valuation gap vs Prysmian. Nexans trades at ~12x trailing PE vs Prysmian at ~27x. The discount is wide even accounting for Prysmian's larger scale and broader scope. If Nexans executes on 2028 targets, the re-rating potential is significant.

SECTION 1 — GENERAL

1. Description

What it is. Nexans SA is the world's second-largest cable manufacturer by revenue, headquartered in Paris. The company designs, manufactures, installs, and services cable systems for electrification infrastructure. Its products range from high-voltage direct current submarine cables connecting offshore wind farms and national grids, through medium-voltage distribution cables for DSOs/TSOs, to low-voltage connectivity cables for buildings, data centres, and industrial applications.

Why it exists. The global energy system is being rewired. Renewable generation must connect to load centres. Ageing grids must be replaced and expanded. New demand sources — EVs, heat pumps, data centres, industrial electrification — are driving unprecedented cable demand. Nexans sits at the centre of this structural shift.

How it makes money. Three core business segments:

- PWR-Transmission: Submarine HVDC cables, onshore HV cables, and associated installation/services. Project-based, turnkey delivery including marine cable-laying via its vessel fleet (Nexans Aurora, Nexans Electra on order). Long-cycle, high-barrier, milestone-billed. €1,657m standard sales in 2025 (+29.8% organic). EBITDA margin 12.3%.

  • PWR-Grid: Medium-voltage cables for distribution grid modernisation and expansion. Shorter cycle but recurring demand from DSO/TSO upgrade programmes. €1,319m standard sales in 2025 (+5.5% organic). EBITDA margin 16.4% — the highest-margin segment.

  • PWR-Connect: Low-voltage cables for buildings, data centres, and infrastructure connectivity. Higher volume, more competitive, but benefiting from product mix improvement toward higher-value solutions. EBITDA margin 12.3%.

- Other Activities: Residual non-electrification operations (Industry & Solutions, copper wire sales being wound down via tolling). EBITDA €20m in 2025 (vs -€23m in 2024).

Strategy. Nexans calls its 2025-2028 plan "Sparking Electrification with Tech Solutions." The strategy rests on three pillars: (1) expand electrification leadership through organic growth and selective M&A (e.g. Electro Cables acquisition in Canada for ~€125m revenue), (2) technology differentiation via superconducting cables, digital grid solutions, and AI-enhanced operations, and (3) disciplined capital allocation with ROCE >20% target.

Organisational structure. Three business units (PWR-Transmission, PWR-Grid, PWR-Connect) supported by centralised functions. ~25,700 employees across 41 countries. Key manufacturing sites: Halden (Norway, submarine cables — sold out to 2028), multiple European MV/LV plants. Cable-laying vessel fleet enables turnkey project delivery. New Stella Nova Centre of Excellence in Hanover (superconductivity R&D, opened June 2025).

Leadership. Julien Hueber became CEO in October 2025, succeeding Christopher Guérin who led the 2018-2025 transformation. Hueber is a 55-year-old, 23-year Nexans veteran. Previously ran PWR-Grid & Connect Europe (€2.6bn, 23 plants). Jean-Christophe Juillard continues as Deputy CEO and CFO. The board includes Bpifrance (5.2% stake) and Invexans/Quiñenco (14.2%) as anchor shareholders.

5. Financials — Analysis

Margin expansion story is real. Adjusted EBITDA margin moved from ~5% (2018) to 11.9% (2025). This is a structural improvement driven by: portfolio rotation away from low-margin commodity cables, shift toward higher-value electrification segments, operational efficiency (Guérin's "simplify to amplify" — cutting 2/3 of customers), and favourable supply/demand dynamics in HV cables.

PWR-Grid is the margin star. At 16.4% EBITDA margin, this segment benefits from recurring DSO/TSO demand, framework agreements, less competitive intensity vs commodity cables, and lower project execution risk than submarine cables. This segment should be the focus for M&A.

Revenue growth acceleration is backlog-driven. PWR-Transmission grew +29.8% organically in 2025 as Halden expansion came online. This isn't sustainable at this rate — it reflects capacity additions being absorbed into a sold-out market. But the €8.1bn backlog provides 5+ years of visibility at current run-rates.

Balance sheet is clean. Net debt of €266m at end-2025 is negligible (0.4x EBITDA). The company has significant financial flexibility for M&A or shareholder returns. Net debt was €681m just a year earlier — the €415m reduction demonstrates strong cash generation.

FCF quality question. 2022-23 FCF was inflated by customer prepayments on large transmission projects. The 2024 dip (€177m) was partly a normalisation. 2025 bounced back to €344m. The 2028 target of >45% FCF conversion rate is the right metric to watch.

Capex intensity is increasing. €383m in 2025, guided at ~€300m/year average through 2028. Investments are going into Halden expansion, new Morocco MV plant (operational 2026), second cable-laying vessel, and grid/connect capacity in growing markets. Payback periods on HV capacity investments are attractive given the supply shortage.

Working capital management is a competitive advantage. Operating working capital at 0.5% of annualised sales is exceptional. Customer prepayments in transmission (milestone billing), combined with strict inventory management and receivables control, create a cash-generative operating model.

Bearish financial observations:
- 2026 EBITDA guidance (€730-810m) implies potential YoY decline at the midpoint vs 2025 actual (€728m). This is a yellow flag. Management attributes it to H1 softness and GSI exclusion, but the market read it as a momentum break.
- The 2028 EBITDA target of €1,150m requires significant acceleration from the 2026 base. The bridge from ~€770m (2026E) to €1,150m (2028) implies ~22% CAGR over two years. This requires both Transmission revenue growth (backlog-driven, credible) and Grid/Connect margin expansion (less certain).
- EPS growth has been strong but partly reflects favourable tax rates and discontinued operations. The sustainable EPS trajectory from continuing operations needs closer scrutiny.
- Copper price risk is structurally hedged via pass-through mechanisms, but timing mismatches can create quarterly noise.

SECTION 2 — COMPANY DETAILS

6. Revenue Model

Understanding how Nexans makes money requires separating three distinct business models that operate at different cycle lengths, margin profiles, and competitive intensities. The Group's strategic evolution has been to grow the highest-barrier, longest-cycle segments while exiting or deemphasising the most commoditised, shortest-cycle segments. This is the correct strategic direction.

Nexans generates revenue through three primary mechanisms:

Project-based turnkey delivery (PWR-Transmission). Large-scale submarine and onshore HV cable projects. Contracts are typically multi-year, milestone-billed, with performance retainers. Revenue recognition follows project completion percentages. Typical contract size: €100m-€1bn+. Customer base: TSOs (TenneT, RTE, National Grid), offshore wind developers, interconnector operators. The €1bn+ RTE framework agreement (March 2025) exemplifies the relationship-driven, repeat-order model.

Product sales with service overlay (PWR-Grid). MV/HV cables sold to DSOs and TSOs under framework agreements. More standardised than Transmission, but still technical. Revenue is product-driven with growing services component (installation, maintenance). Framework agreements provide multi-year volume visibility. Pricing is competitive but less commoditised than LV cables.

Volume product sales (PWR-Connect). LV cables for buildings, infrastructure, and data centres. Higher volume, more competitive. Differentiators: fire resistance ratings, energy efficiency, technical specifications for specialist applications (e.g. data centre power distribution). The Electro Cables acquisition (Canada, December 2025) expanded the North American footprint in this segment.

Metal pass-through. Copper and aluminium represent a significant portion of cable cost. Nexans strips this out via "standard metal prices" in its reporting. Actual metal costs are passed through to customers, meaning standard sales growth is the true organic revenue metric. This mechanism means revenue can swing dramatically with copper prices, but margins on a standard basis are stable.

Recurring vs one-off revenue. Grid and Connect revenues are relatively recurring — infrastructure spending programmes run for years/decades. Transmission revenue is lumpier — large projects create revenue concentration risk. But the €8.1bn backlog smooths this considerably.

Revenue quality assessment. The shift in revenue mix is positive for quality. Pre-2018 Nexans had significant exposure to short-cycle, commodity cable products where pricing power was weak, customer relationships were transactional, and demand was cyclically sensitive. Today's revenue base is dominated by long-cycle projects with contractual commitments, framework agreements with utility customers, and technical specifications that create switching costs. Revenue visibility has improved materially. The Transmission backlog alone provides 5+ years of revenue coverage at current run rates. Grid framework agreements typically extend 3-5 years. Connect is shorter-cycle but benefits from building code requirements and infrastructure mandates.

Geographic revenue distribution. Nexans generates the majority of its revenue in Europe, with France, Scandinavia (Norway is critical for Transmission), and Germany being the largest country markets. The company also has meaningful operations in the Middle East and Africa (driven by renewable energy projects), Asia-Pacific, South America, and North America (growing via the Electro Cables acquisition). European revenue concentration is both a strength (exposure to the strongest grid investment cycle globally) and a risk (dependency on European energy policy continuity).

7. Business Model

The business model combines manufacturing with engineering and services. Cable manufacturing is capital-intensive — extrusion lines, cross-linking equipment, and specialised testing facilities require sustained investment. The Halden plant (Norway) is the crown jewel: it produces submarine HVDC cables up to 525 kV, with continuous lengths exceeding 100 km. Few facilities globally can match this capability.

Vertical integration in Transmission. Nexans designs, manufactures, transports (via cable-laying vessels), installs, and commissions submarine cable systems. This end-to-end capability is a significant barrier to entry. Owning cable-laying vessels (Nexans Aurora operational; Nexans Electra on order, >$205m investment, mid-decade delivery) is a competitive moat — vessel availability is a bottleneck industry-wide.

Distributed manufacturing in Grid/Connect. Multiple plants across Europe and beyond serve local markets. Proximity to customers matters for logistics (cable is heavy and expensive to transport). Local manufacturing also helps with regulatory/procurement preferences in utility markets.

Technology moat. Patent coverage on insulation compounds, joints, and terminations. R&D spending supports next-generation capabilities: 525 kV HVDC systems, superconducting cables (Stella Nova centre), and digital monitoring solutions. The superconducting cable research is speculative but could be transformative for data centre power distribution.

Capital allocation discipline. The "simplify to amplify" strategy reduced the customer base by 2/3. This is unusual and impressive. It signals management willingness to sacrifice top-line for profitability. ROCE improvement from 9% (2018) to 21.6% (2025) validates the approach.

Project risk management in Transmission. Submarine cable projects carry significant execution risk — weather delays, seabed conditions, cable faults, and regulatory complications can cause cost overruns. Nexans manages this through: milestone billing (reducing working capital risk), fixed-price contracts with contingency allowances, weather window planning (installation typically during calmer summer months), and in-house vessel operation (reducing third-party dependency). Project margins can vary significantly — a well-executed project delivers high returns, while a troubled project can swing to losses. The key metric to monitor is the variability of Transmission segment margins across quarters, which reflects project mix and execution quality.

Aftermarket and services. A growing component of revenue comes from cable inspection, maintenance, and repair services. As the installed base of submarine cables grows, IMR revenue becomes more meaningful and more recurring. This is a strategically important revenue stream that improves customer lock-in and margin mix. Nexans' vessel fleet supports IMR operations alongside new installation work.

8. Secular Trends

The cable industry is experiencing a generational demand inflection. Multiple secular drivers are converging:

Grid modernisation. Europe's transmission and distribution grids are aged — much of the infrastructure dates from the 1960s-80s. The IEA estimates 80 million km of new or refurbished cable needed by 2040. DSOs and TSOs are accelerating capex programmes. This drives PWR-Grid demand.

Offshore wind. Europe's offshore wind capacity is planned to grow from ~30 GW (2023) to 120+ GW by 2030 and 300+ GW by 2050. Every offshore wind farm needs export cables (HV submarine cables to shore) and array cables (inter-turbine connections). This is the core driver for PWR-Transmission.

Grid interconnectors. Cross-border electricity interconnection is a European policy priority. Projects like the Great Sea Interconnector, Viking Link, NeuConnect, and many others require long submarine HVDC cables. The North Sea Energy Island concept alone could drive €10bn+ in cable demand.

Data centre power. AI-driven data centre expansion is creating unprecedented point-source power demand. A single hyperscale data centre may require 500 MW-1 GW of grid connection capacity. This drives demand across all three segments: HV cables for grid connection, MV distribution cables, and LV cables for internal power distribution.

EV charging and heat pumps. Electrification of transport and heating drives LV cable demand for charging infrastructure and building upgrades. European EV sales continue to grow, and heat pump installations are accelerating under decarbonisation mandates. The UK alone plans to install 600,000 heat pumps per year by 2028. Each heat pump installation typically requires electrical wiring upgrades. At scale, this is a material demand driver for PWR-Connect.

Reshoring and industrial electrification. Manufacturing returning to Europe/North America requires new grid connections and internal wiring. Industrial decarbonisation (electrifying industrial processes) adds further demand. Steel, cement, and chemical plants are increasingly evaluating electric arc furnaces, electrolysis, and other electric process routes. Each major industrial electrification project requires significant cable infrastructure.

Data centre demand acceleration. This deserves special attention. Global electricity demand from data centres is expected to more than double by 2030, from 415 TWh in 2024. Individual hyperscale data centres are now being planned at 500 MW-1 GW+ capacity — equivalent to a small city. Each facility requires: (1) HV grid connection cables (PWR-Transmission), (2) MV distribution cables within the campus (PWR-Grid), and (3) LV cables for rack power distribution (PWR-Connect). AI training clusters are particularly power-intensive, with single racks consuming 30-120+ kW. The data centre opportunity spans all three of Nexans' business segments. Nexans' superconducting cable technology (Stella Nova) is specifically targeting data centre power distribution as a first commercial application.

Net assessment: These trends are structural, not cyclical. They have multi-decade runways. The cable industry is rare in having virtually all secular tailwinds aligned simultaneously. Rating: A.

9. Customer Side

Nexans' customer base is concentrated among large institutional buyers:

- TSOs (Transmission System Operators): TenneT, RTE, National Grid, Elia, etc. These are regulated monopolies with multi-decade investment programmes. Credit quality is effectively sovereign. Contracts are long-term framework agreements.
- DSOs (Distribution System Operators): Local distribution utilities upgrading grid infrastructure. Regulated returns provide spending visibility.
- Offshore wind developers: Ørsted, Equinor, Vattenfall, RWE, etc. Project-by-project but increasingly via framework agreements.
- Data centre operators: Hyperscalers (Microsoft, Google, Amazon) and co-location providers. Growing customer segment for high-specification cables.

Customer concentration risk exists in Transmission — a single large project can represent a meaningful share of segment revenue. But the customer base is predominantly investment-grade or government-backed entities. Payment risk is low.

The customer base has become more focused following the "simplify to amplify" strategy. Nexans cut its customer count by 2/3, focusing on high-value relationships. This improves pricing power and reduces complexity.

10. Economies of Scale

Scale matters in cables, but differently across segments:

Transmission: Scale advantages are primarily in manufacturing capability (few plants globally can produce 525 kV submarine cables) and vessel fleet. Nexans is #2 globally. Economies of scale are moderate — what matters more is technical capability and capacity availability.

Grid: Scale advantages in procurement (copper/aluminium buying), manufacturing efficiency, and logistics. Local scale matters — being the largest producer in a region provides logistics advantages for heavy cable products.

Connect: Higher volume, more commoditised. Scale advantages in raw material procurement, production efficiency, and distribution. More exposed to price competition from Asian producers.

Overall, Nexans benefits from being a global scale player in a concentrated industry. Its ability to invest in cable-laying vessels (€200m+ each) and specialised manufacturing facilities creates barriers that smaller competitors cannot easily overcome.

The "platform" concept. Nexans is evolving from a pure product manufacturer to an integrated solutions platform. The combination of cable manufacturing, vessel-based installation, digital monitoring, and aftermarket services creates a fuller value proposition for utility customers. This integrated approach supports higher margins and stronger customer relationships. The risk is that platform investments (vessels, R&D, digital) increase capital intensity and fixed costs. If demand disappoints, the operating leverage works against the company. But in a structurally growing market, operating leverage amplifies the upside.

12. Internal Changes

The period 2021-2025 was transformative for Nexans:

Strategic repositioning. From a diversified cable conglomerate to a pure electrification player. This is the defining narrative. The "Winds of Change" (2021-2024) and "Sparking Electrification" (2025-2028) plans provide strategic coherence.

Portfolio rotation. Divested: Aginode (fibre optic), AmerCable (US mining/industrial cables), Lynxeo (specialty chemicals), and electric (automotive wiring harnesses, €749m revenue, ~14,000 employees, to Motherson for €207m EV). The electric sale removes a meaningful revenue/employee block but a low-margin, non-strategic business.

Acquired: Electro Cables Inc. (Canada, LV cables, ~€125m revenue, December 2025). Strengthens PWR-Connect in North America.

Operational efficiency. Reduced customer base by 2/3. This is a radical move that concentrates revenue on high-value, high-margin relationships. The "simplify to amplify" philosophy drove margin expansion.

CEO transition. Christopher Guérin was the architect of the transformation. His departure in October 2025 introduces execution risk. Julien Hueber provides continuity — he has been at Nexans for 23 years and was on the Executive Committee since 2018. His focus: "from execution to expansion."

Technology investment. Stella Nova Centre of Excellence (Hanover) opened June 2025 for superconducting cable R&D. Superconductors could be transformative for data centre power distribution (zero electrical resistance, ultra-high current density, compact footprint). Still early-stage but a genuine technology option.

ESG credentials. CDP Climate Change A-List. Net-Zero 2050 commitment (SBTi-aligned). Target to increase recycled copper from 5% (2023) to 25% (2028). ESG positioning supports access to sustainability-linked financing and meets institutional investor expectations.

SECTION 3 — INDUSTRY CONTEXT

13. Vs Peers

Prysmian (PRY.MI): Market leader. ~3x Nexans' revenue. Broader scope — telecom cables, energy cables, accessories. Acquired Encore Wire (US building wire) for ~$4bn. More diversified but less pure-play electrification (~47% electricity vs Nexans' ~100%). Significantly higher valuation (~27x PE). Prysmian's scale provides procurement advantages and project portfolio diversification. Nexans' response: purer focus, higher ROCE, and deeper specialisation in target segments.

NKT (NKT.CO): Smaller but more focused. ~90% electricity-related revenue. Dominant in HVDC — €10.2bn backlog exceeds Nexans'. Aggressive capacity expansion (>$1bn investment 2023-26, building new Cologne factory). Trades at a significant premium (~35x PE). NKT's pure HV focus commands a scarcity premium. Nexans has broader segment exposure but less extreme specialisation.

Sumitomo Electric / LS Cable: Asian competitors with growing HV capabilities. Pricing pressure on standard products. Less competitive in European HV projects due to qualification requirements and vessel fleet limitations. Long-term threat if they invest in European manufacturing capacity.

Leoni: Primarily automotive wiring. Less comparable to Nexans' electrification focus. Nexans' electric divestiture removes the overlap.

14. Industry Structure

The cable industry is oligopolistic at the high-value end and fragmented at the commodity end.

HVDC submarine cables: Three players (Prysmian, Nexans, NKT) control ~75% of the global market. Lead times exceed 5 years. Manufacturing qualification takes years. Cable-laying vessel availability is a binding constraint. This is effectively a protected oligopoly with structural pricing power. New entrants would need $1bn+ in investment and 5-10 years to qualify.

Onshore HV cables: Somewhat more competitive. Asian players (Sumitomo, LS Cable) participate in international tenders. But European qualification requirements and local content preferences create regional barriers.

MV distribution cables: Moderately concentrated. Regional players compete alongside the Big Three. Barriers are lower but still meaningful — product certification, utility qualification, local manufacturing presence.

LV cables: Most competitive segment. Many producers globally. Price competition from Asian manufacturers. Differentiation through technical specifications (fire resistance, energy efficiency), local service, and certification.

The industry structure is highly favourable for Nexans' strategic positioning. The shift toward Transmission and Grid (where concentration is highest) and away from commodity cables (where competition is fiercest) is exactly right.

Procurement dynamics. Cable procurement by utilities and offshore wind developers follows lengthy qualification processes. For HVDC submarine cables, qualification can take 3-5 years. TSOs typically maintain qualified vendor lists with 2-3 approved suppliers. Switching costs are high — requalification is expensive and time-consuming. This creates customer stickiness and repeat-order dynamics. Framework agreements (e.g. the €1bn+ RTE deal) formalise this lock-in.

Pricing dynamics. In HVDC, pricing has been strengthening as demand outstrips supply. Cable makers have been able to pass through cost inflation and capture margin improvement. In MV/LV segments, pricing is more competitive but product mix improvements (shifting to higher-specification cables) support margin expansion. The industry has moved away from pure price competition toward total-cost-of-ownership selling, which favours technically superior producers.

Capacity utilisation. The industry is operating at very high utilisation rates. Goldman Sachs estimates 90% utilisation for 2025-28 (up from 63% previously). At these levels, producers have pricing power and can be selective about which projects they pursue. The risk is that the current investment cycle (NKT Cologne, Prysmian expansion, Nexans Halden Phase 2) creates temporary overcapacity in 2028-29. History suggests demand will absorb new capacity, but the timing mismatch could create a margin headwind.

Antitrust history. The cable industry has a history of cartel behaviour. Nexans, Prysmian (then Pirelli), and others were fined €302m by the European Commission in 2014 for price-fixing in submarine and underground power cables (1999-2009). A Competition Appeal Tribunal case (London Array v Nexans, October 2025) reflects ongoing litigation legacy. The industry has since implemented extensive compliance programmes, but the historical context is relevant for understanding competitive dynamics and regulatory risk.

Currency risk. Revenues in multiple currencies (EUR, NOK, GBP, USD) but costs concentrated in EUR and NOK. Significant NOK exposure via Halden operations. Gas-insulated lines offer an alternative to cables for some underground transmission applications. Wireless power transmission remains science fiction for grid-scale applications.

Regulatory and competition law risk. The cable industry has a documented history of cartel behaviour (2014 EC fines). Ongoing litigation (London Array v Nexans, October 2025 Competition Appeal Tribunal) creates contingent liability risk. Tightened compliance procedures should reduce future risk, but the reputational and financial exposure from historical cases is not fully extinguished.

Customer concentration. While the overall customer base is diversified, individual large projects create concentration risk. A single submarine cable project can represent 5-10% of annual Transmission revenue. Contract disputes, payment delays, or project cancellations from a single major customer could impact quarterly results. The GSI situation illustrates this risk clearly.

Labour and skills shortage. Cable manufacturing requires specialised engineering talent. Submarine cable installation requires highly skilled marine operations crews. Competition for talent is intensifying as all three major players expand capacity simultaneously. Labour costs are rising. Nexans' ability to attract and retain skilled personnel is a key operational risk factor that is often underappreciated.

Nexans History and Heritage

Nexans was formed in 2000 as a spin-off from Alcatel's cable division. The legacy stretches back over a century to the French cable industry. The company listed on Euronext Paris and spent its first two decades as a diversified cable conglomerate with exposure to telecom, automotive, energy, and industrial cables. Performance was mediocre -- the business lacked strategic focus and suffered from commodity price volatility, competitive pressure, and underinvestment.

The turning point came with Christopher Guerin's appointment as CEO in July 2018. Guerin implemented a radical simplification programme: reducing the customer base by 2/3, divesting non-core businesses, and concentrating investment on electrification. The ROCE improvement from 9% (2018) to 21.6% (2025) tells the story. This is one of the more successful European industrial transformations of the past decade.

Detailed Revenue Bridge (2024 to 2025)

The headline standard sales figure of EUR 6,098m in 2025 (down from EUR 7,078m in 2024) is misleading. The decline reflects IFRS 5 reclassification of electric and Industry & Solutions disposals, not organic weakness. On a continuing electrification basis:

- PWR-Transmission: EUR 1,290m to EUR 1,657m (+29.8% organic)
- PWR-Grid: EUR 1,250m to EUR 1,319m (+5.5% organic)
- PWR-Connect: broadly stable with improving mix
- Electrification total organic growth: strong double-digit

The reported number understates the underlying momentum. Investors should focus on the three-segment electrification KPIs rather than total group standard sales.

Cable-Laying Vessel Economics

Cable-laying vessels are a critical and underappreciated asset. A purpose-built HV cable-laying vessel costs $200-300m. Build time is 3-4 years. Global capacity is severely constrained -- perhaps 10-15 capable vessels exist worldwide for HV submarine cable installation. Vessel day-rates have risen significantly.

Nexans Aurora (operational since 2021) was a strategic investment. The vessel can carry up to 10,000 tonnes of cable and is equipped for both cable laying and cable protection installation. Nexans Electra is on order and will expand the fleet, enabling parallel project execution and reducing scheduling constraints.

The vessel investment creates a vertically integrated model in Transmission: design, manufacture, transport, install, commission. This integration supports higher margins (Nexans captures the full value chain), reduces third-party dependency, and provides a competitive moat. Competitors without owned vessels must charter from a tight market, creating cost uncertainty and scheduling risk.

Copper as a Cost Input

Copper represents approximately 40-50% of cable costs (varying by product type). Nexans manages this through several mechanisms:

  1. Pass-through pricing. Standard sales reporting strips out metal price movements. Customer contracts include copper price adjustment clauses.
  2. Financial hedging. Nexans hedges non-ferrous metals exposure using futures and options. This smooths quarterly cash flow impacts.
  3. Physical inventory management. Just-in-time purchasing and lean inventory reduce copper price exposure.
  4. Recycled copper. Target to increase from 5% to 25% by 2028. Recycled copper is typically 15-20% cheaper than virgin copper and has a lower carbon footprint.

The net effect: copper price movements are largely neutral to Nexans' profitability over a full year. But quarter-to-quarter, timing mismatches between pass-through adjustments and actual metal cost can create P&L noise. Investors should look through this noise and focus on standard sales and adjusted EBITDA.

ESG and Sustainability Positioning

Nexans has strong ESG credentials that are increasingly relevant for institutional investor appetite:

- CDP Climate Change A-List (among ~260 companies globally)
- Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) commitment: Net-Zero by 2050
- 42% reduction in Scope 1 and 2 emissions since 2019
- Sustainability-linked financing: credit facilities tied to ESG KPIs
- Employee safety: lost-time injury frequency rate improved YoY

ESG positioning is not just virtue signalling. It provides access to sustainability-linked financing (lower cost of capital), supports inclusion in ESG indices (broadens the investor base), and increasingly matters in utility procurement decisions. Several European TSOs/DSOs now include sustainability criteria in cable tender evaluations.

Infrastructure Investment Cycle Context

The current infrastructure investment cycle is arguably the strongest in European history. Multiple policy frameworks converge:

These investment programmes are backed by legislation, regulatory mandates, and government commitment. They are not discretionary. They represent a multi-decade demand floor for cable products. The key risk is execution speed -- permitting, planning, and construction bottlenecks may delay but not eliminate the demand. For a cable manufacturer, these delays simply extend the demand runway further into the future.

Detailed Backlog Analysis

The EUR 8.1bn PWR-Transmission adjusted backlog at end-2025 deserves close scrutiny. It is the single most important asset on Nexans' balance sheet from an equity investor's perspective.

Composition. The backlog is primarily composed of: submarine HVDC export cables for offshore wind farms, grid interconnector projects (cross-border and island connections), and onshore HV cable packages. Major identified projects include contracts with TenneT (North Sea grid connections), RTE (French offshore wind and HVDC framework agreement, >EUR 1bn), Interconnect Malta (second interconnector), and the frozen Great Sea Interconnector (EUR 1.43bn, Greece-Cyprus). The backlog is geographically concentrated in Northern Europe, with growing representation from the Mediterranean and Middle East.

Quality assessment. The backlog is high quality by industrial standards. Counterparties are predominantly TSOs and government-backed entities with investment-grade credit ratings. Contracts are typically fixed-price with copper price adjustment clauses. Payment is milestone-based, providing cash flow visibility. Cancellation clauses exist but typically include compensation for work performed and materials procured. The risk of non-payment or arbitrary cancellation is low -- though the GSI situation demonstrates that politically-driven project freezes are a real risk even with strong counterparties.

Conversion timeline. At the current PWR-Transmission run-rate of ~EUR 1,657m standard sales, the EUR 8.1bn backlog represents approximately 4.9 years of revenue. However, backlog conversion is not linear. Projects ramp up and down over their lifecycle. Revenue recognition depends on manufacturing schedules, installation windows (seasonal), and customer milestone approvals. In practice, the backlog will convert over 5-8 years, with annual revenue varying based on project phasing.

Backlog replenishment. Nexans needs to add approximately EUR 1.5-2.0bn per year to maintain backlog levels at current production rates. The framework agreement model (e.g. RTE) provides some visibility on future order intake. The pipeline of announced European offshore wind and interconnector projects suggests replenishment opportunities are ample through 2030+. However, order timing is lumpy -- large framework agreements can shift quarterly intake figures significantly.

GSI impact assessment. If the Great Sea Interconnector (EUR 1.43bn) is permanently cancelled, the backlog falls to ~EUR 6.7bn. This is still substantial -- approximately 4 years of Transmission revenue visibility. However, the market would likely interpret cancellation negatively, both for the direct financial impact and the signal it sends about political risk on large interconnector projects. The probability of cancellation vs resumption is currently unclear. Greece and Cyprus jointly froze the project in early 2026. Nexans has cancelled subtender processes. The project had received EUR 657m in EU Connecting Europe Facility funding, which creates political pressure for eventual completion. But bilateral political tensions between Greece and Cyprus complicate matters. The base case should assume GSI is delayed rather than cancelled, with a 30-40% probability of permanent cancellation.

Competitive Moat Assessment

Nexans' competitive moat can be evaluated across five dimensions:

Manufacturing capability (STRONG). The Halden plant produces 525 kV HVDC submarine cables -- one of perhaps 5-6 facilities globally capable of this. The facility is sold out through 2028. Replicating this capability requires $500m+ investment and 3-5 years of construction and qualification. This is a durable moat.

Vessel fleet (MODERATE-STRONG). Nexans Aurora is operational. Nexans Electra is on order. Global cable-laying vessel capacity is severely constrained. Vessel ownership ensures installation capacity and enables end-to-end project delivery. But vessels depreciate and require ongoing maintenance investment. The moat is strong today but could weaken if vessel supply increases.

Customer relationships (STRONG). Multi-decade relationships with European TSOs. Framework agreements provide contractual lock-in. Qualification requirements create switching costs. The customer base is sticky. Guerin's "simplify to amplify" strategy actually strengthened this moat by deepening relationships with fewer, more important customers.

Technology and IP (MODERATE). Patent coverage on insulation compounds, joints, and terminations. Superconducting cable R&D is a potential future moat. But cable technology evolves slowly. The primary technology advantage is manufacturing know-how (tacit knowledge embedded in the workforce) rather than patent-protectable innovation.

Scale (MODERATE). Nexans is #2 globally but significantly smaller than Prysmian. In HVDC, the Big Three are roughly comparable. In MV/LV, scale advantages are regional. The scale moat is less decisive than manufacturing capability and customer relationships.

Overall moat rating: B+ to A-. The moat is strong in Transmission (manufacturing + vessels + customer lock-in) and moderate in Grid/Connect (regional scale + qualification requirements). The moat is strengthening as the industry consolidates around electrification and barriers to entry increase with capacity constraints.
