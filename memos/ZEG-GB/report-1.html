§1 — GENERAL

Net signal: Zegona is a small UK-listed vehicle now operating a large, structurally challenged Spanish telco; financial profile reflects acquisition-stage leverage, not mature telco economics — do not conflate with normalised sector comparables [#J]

Description: Zegona Communications plc is a UK AIM-listed investment company repurposed as an operating holding company following the acquisition of Vodafone Spain (rebranded Vodafone España / operating as "Vodafone Spain") completed July 2024 [#I]

- Sub-bullet: The sole operating asset is now Vodafone Spain — a full-service integrated telco offering mobile, fixed broadband, TV, and enterprise services across Spain

Strategic objective: Value creation via the "buy-fix-sell" PE playbook applied to listed equity — acquire subscale/distressed European telco at a discount to replacement value, restructure costs, stabilise revenue, then exit via trade sale or IPO of the operating company [#J]

- Sub-bullet: Leverage is deliberately high at inception; deleveraging through EBITDA growth and FCF generation is the primary financial objective

Organisation: Lean UK holdco (Zegona plc, AIM-listed in GBP) sits above Spanish OpCo (Vodafone Spain, unlisted, reports in EUR) [#I]

§2 — COMPANY DETAILS

Net signal: Revenue model is a standard integrated telco with structurally declining trends and concentrated competitive pressure; business model value rests almost entirely on execution of a cost-transformation thesis, not on an inherent moat [#J]

Revenue source: Four primary revenue streams — mobile (consumer + enterprise), fixed broadband (consumer + B2B), TV/content, and enterprise/B2B services [#I]
- Sub-bullet: Mobile historically ~55-60% of service revenues; fixed/broadband ~25-30%; TV/content ~5-7%; enterprise solutions ~8-12% (estimates from Vodafone Spain segment disclosures under Vodafone plc)
- Sub-bullet: B2B/enterprise is a strategically important segment — typically higher-margin, stickier; Vodafone Spain has historically been relatively strong in enterprise vs consumer

Revenue mix — geography: 100% Spain; zero geographic diversification at the operating level [#I]
- Sub-bullet: Spain is the 4th largest EU economy with ~47m population; telco penetration is high (fixed broadband ~78% household penetration, mobile ~120%+ SIM penetration)
- Sub-bullet: Concentration in a single, highly competitive market with historically aggressive pricing is a structural risk, not a feature ❌

Cyclicality: Telco revenues are modestly defensive in recession — mobile and broadband are utility-adjacent; enterprise is more cyclical [#A]
- Sub-bullet: Spanish GDP growth was approximately 2.5% in 2023 and approximately 3% in 2024 (above EU average), providing a benign macro backdrop at acquisition
- Sub-bullet: Consumer telco ARPU in Spain has been under structural pressure from converged bundle competition (mobile+fixed+TV bundles), which has defied cyclical recovery ❌

Predictability: Revenue is relatively predictable quarter-to-quarter (contract-based) but with a structural downward drift over the L3Y — not the predictability a long-only investor normally values [#J]
- Sub-bullet: Churn rate in Spanish telco is approximately 1.5-2.5% per month for prepaid; postpaid churn approximately 1.0-1.5% — broadly at market, not differentiated
- Sub-bullet: Postpaid contract lock-in is 12-24 months; convergent bundle customers have higher switching friction but also lower willingness to pay a premium vs pure-play alternatives

Customer behaviour: Spanish consumers are highly price-elastic in telco — one of the most price-competitive markets in Western Europe [#A]
- Sub-bullet: NPS for Vodafone Spain has been below Telefónica España historically; brand perception has weakened following multiple years of strategic uncertainty under Vodafone plc's review process
- Sub-bullet: Customer base under Vodafone Spain: approximately 9-10m mobile SIMs, approximately 3m fixed/broadband customers — scale is meaningful but declining

Switching costs: Low-to-moderate for consumer; moderate-to-high for enterprise [#A]
- Sub-bullet: Spanish number portability regulations are efficient; consumer switching is relatively frictionless
- Sub-bullet: Enterprise customers have higher integration dependencies (MDM, VPN, IoT), creating genuine lock-in — this is Vodafone Spain's strongest moat segment

Scale economies: Limited at current scale — Vodafone Spain's absolute scale (~€3.2bn revenue) is dwarfed by Telefónica España (~€12.5bn); scale disadvantage is meaningful for content purchasing, supplier negotiations, and national infrastructure amortisation [#A]
- Sub-bullet: However, at 9-10m mobile SIMs, Vodafone Spain has minimum viable scale for 5G network economics in Spain — it is not subscale to the point of structural unviability
- Sub-bullet: Network sharing with MásMóvil (pre-merger) partially addressed infrastructure scale disadvantage — the future of that sharing arrangement post Orange-MásMóvil merger is a material uncertainty [#J]

Competitive advantage: Residual competitive advantages are: enterprise relationships, Vodafone Group's global brand (licensed but not owned), IoT/B2B technology capabilities, and partial network infrastructure [#A]
- Sub-bullet: The Vodafone brand licence from Vodafone Group plc creates dependency and potential brand risk if the licence is renegotiated or Vodafone Group's brand deteriorates globally
- Sub-bullet: There is no customer moat equivalent to a Telefónica-level network quality differentiation — Vodafone Spain competes primarily on price and bundle value ❌

Recent delivery — acquisition execution (Jul-24 to present): Completion of the Vodafone Spain acquisition was the primary recent milestone — deal closed Jul-24, financed by a combination of debt at the Spanish OpCo and equity raised at Zegona plc level [#I]
- Sub-bullet: Post-acquisition, management communicated a cost transformation programme targeting €300-400m of annual run-rate EBITDA improvement through workforce restructuring, IT rationalisation, and property/procurement savings
- Sub-bullet: First post-acquisition financial update showed some initial positive signs on cost trajectory but the revenue line remains under pressure — the key watch is whether revenue stabilises before cost savings mature [#A]

- Sub-bullet: Spain's convergent market dynamics have shifted post MásMóvil-Orange merger approval (late 2024/early 2025) — the 3-player market is structurally more favourable for ARPU discipline

Internal change — L12M: The most significant internal change is the shift from investment vehicle to operating company — entirely new operating management team, governance structure, and financial reporting framework [#I]
- Sub-bullet: Eamonn O'Hare and Robert Samuelson both joined pre-acquisition with specific mandate to execute this transformation; both have Liberty Global heritage

§3 — INDUSTRY CONTEXT

Net signal: Spanish telecoms market has structurally improved competitive dynamics post consolidation (4→3 MNOs) but Vodafone Spain enters this improved environment from a position of brand, cost, and commercial weakness — structural tailwind but company-specific headwinds [#J]

Market structure: Spain has transitioned from a 4-MNO to 3-MNO market following the Orange-MásMóvil merger approval (confirmed late 2024) — creating Masorange, alongside Telefónica España and Vodafone Spain [#I]
- Sub-bullet: Top 3 combined mobile market share approximately 85-90% (Telefónica ~38%, MásMóvil/Orange ~30%, Vodafone ~18-20%) with remainder split across MVNOs
- Sub-bullet: This 3-player structure is more analogous to the UK post-consolidation dynamic (BT, Virgin Media O2, Vodafone UK) where pricing discipline has historically improved [#A]

Concentration: Spanish mobile market Herfindahl index has increased meaningfully post Orange-MásMóvil merger — structurally positive for ARPU [#A]
- Sub-bullet: Top 3 market share of >85% places Spain in line with Germany (~3 players, ~88% top 3) but still below UK (~3 players, ~92% post-merger if VMO2/Vodafone UK deal proceeds)
- Sub-bullet: Fixed broadband is more concentrated — Telefónica España has ~40% fixed broadband share via its Movistar network; Vodafone Spain approximately 13-15% [#A]

Competitive intensity: Historically extremely high — Spain has been one of the 2-3 most price-competitive telco markets in Western Europe, with aggressive MVNO penetration and high multi-SIM household behaviour [#A]
- Sub-bullet: Average mobile ARPU in Spain approximately €13-15/month (service revenue basis) vs European average approximately €16-20/month — persistently below-average monetisation
- Sub-bullet: Post consolidation, the trajectory of ARPU is the single most important macro variable for Zegona's investment case [#J]

New entrant risk: Low in the near term — spectrum costs, infrastructure requirements, and market consolidation make new MNO entry economically irrational [#A]
- Sub-bullet: MVNO segment remains active (~15-20 active MVNOs in Spain) but MVNOs are dependent on MNO infrastructure and typically compete at the low end; they are a competitive pressure on prepaid not on converged bundle [#A]
- Sub-bullet: Fixed broadband has more new entrant risk via alternative fibre operators (altnet model), though Spain's open-access fibre infrastructure partially mitigates this

BtE (Barriers to entry): High — spectrum licences, national infrastructure, universal service obligations, and regulatory compliance create meaningful BtE for a new MNO [#A]
- Sub-bullet: Vodafone Spain's own position is somewhat protected by these same barriers; it is unlikely to be competitively displaced in an absolute sense
- Sub-bullet: The risk is not displacement but continued margin compression — a different form of competitive erosion [#J]

Regulatory shift: Spanish regulator approved the Orange-MásMóvil merger with remedies (MVNO wholesale access obligations) in 2024 — this maintains some competitive pressure at the low end but removes a fourth facilities-based MNO [#I]
- Sub-bullet: Remedy obligations on Masorange may limit its ability to aggressively undercut Vodafone Spain in certain market segments
- Sub-bullet: EU DMA and DSA compliance obligations add regulatory cost but are broadly sector-wide and not uniquely burdensome for Vodafone Spain

Political SWOT: Spain's government has historically been interventionist on telco pricing (domestic roaming, broadband access pricing) — regulatory risk is real but not acute in the near term [#A]
- Sub-bullet: Universal service obligations and coverage requirements in rural Spain create capex obligations that are partially offset by public co-funding programmes
- Sub-bullet: Geopolitical stability in Spain is solid; no material political risk to telco ownership structures

Macro shift: Spanish GDP growth of ~2.5-3% pa (2023-2024) outpaces EU average; rising employment and household income provide a constructive backdrop for telco spending [#A]
- Sub-bullet: Inflation has driven energy and labour cost increases — both are meaningful cost items for Zegona's restructuring programme
- Sub-bullet: Interest rate environment: ECB rate cuts (2024 cycle) are modestly positive for Zegona's floating-rate debt obligations at the Spanish OpCo level [#A]

Thematic trend — 5G: Spain's 5G rollout is advanced (~70% population coverage); Vodafone Spain has 5G infrastructure in major cities [#I]
- Sub-bullet: 5G monetisation remains limited in Spain — consumers have not yet demonstrated willingness to pay a premium for 5G, compressing the ARPU uplift from the network investment
- Sub-bullet: Enterprise 5G (private networks, IoT, Industry 4.0) is the medium-term monetisation opportunity — this aligns with Vodafone Spain's enterprise strength [#J]

Thematic trend — fibre: Spain has among the highest FTTH penetration in Europe (~80%+ premises passed) — infrastructure overbuild risk is lower than in markets still building fibre [#A]
- Sub-bullet: Vodafone Spain relies partly on access to Telefónica's fibre network via regulated access and partly on its own/co-investment infrastructure — this creates dependency but avoids full capex burden [#A]

Peer divergence: No direct listed peer for Zegona's specific structure (PE-style holdco over single-country telco); closest comparables are Liberty Global (multi-country cable/telco holdco) and Masmovil pre-merger (Spanish challenger) [#A]
- Sub-bullet: Liberty Global comparison is instructive: similar "buy underperforming assets, restructure, exit" playbook, but Liberty operates across multiple geographies, providing diversification Zegona lacks
- Sub-bullet: No cohort or sector peer research was available at the staleness threshold — this analysis relies solely on publicly available data and AlphaSense sourced research [#I]

Sceptical bullet: Bearish cross-cutting read on industry context — consolidation to 3 MNOs is structurally positive but the remedy conditions on Masorange may preserve more competitive intensity than the headline implies; Telefónica España's structural advantages (network quality, scale, content) mean the improved market structure benefits it disproportionately; Vodafone Spain's weak starting position means it may be slower to capture ARPU recovery than the market's "rational oligopoly" assumption implies [#J]
