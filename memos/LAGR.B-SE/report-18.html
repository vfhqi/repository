- Lagercrantz's annual report governance section does not disclose a detailed CEO succession plan — standard Nordic corporate governance practice is less prescriptive on this than UK/US governance codes
- Board is aware of succession risk — compensation committee and board nominations committee responsibilities include succession planning — but no public signal of active process

Recruitment risk if CEO departs:
- The model's reputation is strongly linked to Wigh personally in investor perception
- A replacement CEO from inside would need to be credible on M&A discipline — this is learnable but requires the market to trust the new CEO's capital-allocation instincts before the trust is earned
- External hire risk: Lagercrantz's model is specialised enough that an external CEO with general industrial background but no serial-acquirer experience would face a steep learning curve on deal origination and integration

Quantified departure-risk estimate:
- Based on valuation patterns at Nordic serial acquirers where CEO transitions occurred (e.g., Indutrade, Addtech historical transitions), a CEO departure at Lagercrantz without a well-signalled succession plan would likely produce a 10-20% immediate share-price decline; sustained de-rating possible if incoming CEO is perceived as lower quality

Bearish read on key-person risk:
- The decentralised model is frequently cited as the structural mitigation for key-person risk ("the model survives the CEO"); this argument is partially correct at the operational level but incorrect at the capital-allocation level; M&A deal origination, pricing discipline, and the decision of when to walk away from deals are all deeply personal-judgement functions that depend on the specific experience and temperament of the Group CEO; a new CEO inheriting the model could follow the playbook operationally while gradually deteriorating the M&A quality without the decline being immediately visible in financials — the quality of acquisitions made in years 1-3 of a new CEO's tenure will only show in ROIC data 3-5 years later

§8 — CEO Quality Verdict + Animal-CEO Archetype + Sceptical Synthesis

J-front verdict (cross-cutting bear synthesis): Wigh is a genuine operator-track CEO with 18-year compounding evidence; the bear case is not capability — it is model-maturity and key-person concentration in a model that claims not to have key-person risk

Animal-CEO archetype assessment:

The animal-CEO framework tests six markers: force, agency, dynamism, capital-allocation discipline, communication clarity, outlier standards.

Force marker (drive / energy / push):
- Present, but expressed through institutional persistence rather than personal dynamism; Wigh has maintained the same acquisition pace and operational targets for 18 years without visible energy decline
- Not a visibly charismatic or high-energy communicator in the theatrical sense; force is expressed through execution consistency rather than motivational presence
- Animal-CEO match: moderate — consistent force, not dramatic force

Agency marker (decision velocity / boldness):
- High on M&A execution (once a deal is priced correctly, he moves); low on strategic pivots (the model has not been meaningfully redesigned in 18 years)
- The decision to consistently walk away from overpriced deals is itself an expression of agency — it requires resisting the pressure to deploy capital that most serial acquirers eventually capitulate to
- Animal-CEO match: high on capital-allocation agency; moderate on broader strategic agency

Dynamism marker (adaptability / strategic clarity):
