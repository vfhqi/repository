§2 — Quality of Earnings

Quality verdict: Earnings quality is mixed — structural cash conversion is adequate for a regulated utility but FY-22/FY-23 were materially distorted by disposal gains and non-recurring items; FY-24 onwards looks cleaner [#J]

One-off impact: FY-22 carried an estimated €2.8-3.0bn net negative non-recurring burden (Spain windfall tax €1.4bn, Russian gas write-off €1.0bn, LatAm FX €0.4bn); FY-23 carried a net positive non-recurring contribution (~€3.5bn disposal gains, partially offsetting ongoing restructuring charges) [#A]
- Sub: The net swing in non-recurrings from FY-22 to FY-23 is ~€6-6.5bn — the "recovery" narrative is partly a comp effect
- Sub: Enel's definition of "ordinary EBITDA" consistently excludes disposal-related items; this is standard for a utility in deleveraging mode but requires scrutiny

Adjustments trend: Non-GAAP adjustments are declining as the disposal programme winds down — a positive quality signal; FY-24 and FY-25 should show reported ≈ adjusted convergence [#A]
- Sub: Convergence is not complete — Enel still adjusts for restructuring costs (~€200-300m p.a.) treated as one-off but recurring in practice

Recurring vs one-off: ❌ Restructuring charges (~€200-300m p.a.) are treated as non-recurring in adjusted EBITDA but have appeared in every L3Y period — a quality flag by European utility peer standards [#I]
- Sub: Iberdrola and EDP show lower recurring restructuring add-backs (~€50-100m p.a.) suggesting Enel's cost base is less stable

NWC tailwind: Working capital was a tailwind in FY-23 (~€1.0-1.5bn release) as energy prices normalised and receivables from high-price periods were collected; this is largely exhausted by FY-24 and not a repeatable driver [#A]
- Sub: FY-22 NWC was a headwind (~€1.5bn build) as Enel was owed large receivables from high-price energy contracts — the FY-23 reversal was mechanical

Cash conversion: OCF/EBITDA averaged approximately 47-52% L3Y (FY-22: ~45%; FY-23: ~55%; FY-24e: ~50%) [#A]
- Sub: European utility sector median OCF/EBITDA is ~50-55% (Iberdrola ~53%, EDP ~52%); Enel at the lower end primarily due to high interest burden on ~€47-50bn net debt
- Sub: ❌ OCF-to-net income conversion is weaker — FCF after dividends has been thin or negative in FY-22 and FY-23 as capex ran at ~€14-16bn p.a.

D&A trend: D&A has been rising from ~€6.5bn (FY-22) toward ~€7.5bn (FY-25e) reflecting the growing renewables and network asset base; this is expected and consistent with capex deployment [#I]
- Sub: Accelerated depreciation risk on thermal assets is modest given Enel's thermal fleet is already substantially written down in Spain and Italy

OCF vs NI: OCF consistently exceeds reported net income (by a factor of ~3-4×) due to high D&A in a capital-intensive utility — this is structurally expected and not a red flag in isolation [#A]

Non-GAAP gap: The gap between reported EPS and adjusted EPS narrowed from €0.08 (FY-22) to an estimated €0.02-0.03 (FY-24e) — converging as non-recurring noise fades; positive trend [#A]

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read is that FY-22's negative one-offs and FY-23's positive disposal gains created an artificial quality improvement narrative; the underlying recurring cash generation at ~€9-10bn OCF p.a. is adequate for an investment-grade utility but not the earnings quality of a growth compounder; recurring restructuring charges (~€250m p.a.) dressed as one-offs represent a structural quality ceiling.

Margin verdict: Margin expansion from FY-22 to FY-25 is predominantly mechanical (revenue denominator deflation as energy prices normalised) rather than structural cost discipline; limited further margin runway from here [#J]

Revenue CAGR: L3Y revenue CAGR is sharply negative (~-15% p.a. FY-22→FY-25e) — but this is the energy passthrough effect; underlying network and renewable revenue growing ~3-5% p.a. [#A]
- Sub: Reported revenue is a poor signal for Enel — the commodity trading and passthrough component can swing ±30% without changing underlying economics

Profit CAGR: EBITDA absolute grew from ~€17.0bn (FY-22) to ~€20.6bn (FY-25e) — ~+7% total, or ~+2.3% p.a. CAGR; not a high-growth profile [#A]
- Sub: European utility EBITDA CAGR peer comparison (Iberdrola ~+8-10% p.a. L3Y, EDP ~+6-8% p.a.) — Enel at the low end of the peer set

Operating leverage: Profit growth > revenue growth only because revenue was artificially elevated in FY-22; on a like-for-like (network + renewables only) basis, operating leverage is modest (~1.1-1.3×) [#I]
- Sub: Cost efficiency programme (Simplification Plan, headcount reduction ~4,000 FTE over FY-23/24) contributed ~€0.4-0.6bn EBITDA tailwind — real but one-time

Gross margin: Gross margin improved from ~12-13% (FY-22, depressed by commodity pass-through inflation) to ~24-25% (FY-24e) — this is almost entirely arithmetic, not pricing power [#A]

EBITDA margin: EBITDA margin trajectory: 14.2% (H1-22) → 12.1% (FY-22) → 20.9% (FY-23) → 22.7% (FY-24e) → 23.7% (FY-25e) — genuine structural improvement in the 23-24% normalised range [#A]
- Sub: The normalised range is consistent with a regulated/quasi-regulated utility with ~60-65% of EBITDA from regulated networks and long-term contracted renewables

Pricing driver: Italy and Spain network tariff resets are the key structural margin support (WACC-based regulatory returns) — visibility is high for FY-25 and FY-26 given known regulatory periods [#J]
- Sub: Italy ARERA regulatory period runs through FY-27; Spain CNMC network tariff confirmed through FY-26 — regulatory margin floor is solid

Mix shift: Renewables EBITDA share increasing from ~25% (FY-22) to a targeted ~35% (FY-27) — positive mix shift toward higher-multiple, more predictable earnings [#I]

Sustainability: Normalised EBITDA margin of ~22-24% is structurally defensible given regulatory frameworks; upside to ~25-26% is possible from further renewables mix shift but requires execution on ~€6-8bn p.a. greenfield additions [#A]

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on margin sustainability is that FY-24/FY-25 margins are at the high end of what the regulatory/contracted framework allows; further expansion requires Enel to hit ambitious ~5-6GW p.a. renewables commissioning targets in markets (Spain, Italy, US) where permitting risk and grid connection delays are material; a regulatory reset in Italy (ARERA post-FY-27) that reduces allowed WACC would structurally compress margins without recourse.

§4 — Seasonal Patterns

Seasonality verdict: Enel exhibits moderate seasonality — H2 structurally stronger than H1 on EBITDA and FCF; this is stable and driven by network volumes and hydro generation patterns [#J]

H1 pattern: H1 consistently accounts for approximately 46-48% of full-year EBITDA (H1-23: 47%, H1-24e: 49%) — modestly below H2 in most years [#A]
- Sub: H1 weakness relative to H2 reflects southern European hydro seasonality (spring melt peaks in May-June but generation builds through autumn) and network demand patterns (Italian industrial demand higher in autumn/winter)

H2 pattern: H2 typically carries 52-54% of full-year EBITDA plus the majority of year-end regulatory true-up settlements in Italy [#A]
- Sub: FY-22 was an exception — H2-22 was disproportionately weak due to Spain windfall tax charges (Q4 booking) and Russian gas write-offs

Seasonality direction: Seasonality is stable and not structurally changing — the regulated network framework smooths revenue recognition over calendar periods via tariff mechanisms [#J]

Cause — regulatory: Italian ARERA and Spanish CNMC regulate network revenues on an accrual basis tied to network usage, which is smoothly seasonal (industrial shutdown in August slightly depresses H2 early before Q4 recovery) [#I]

Cause — renewables: Solar generation peaks H1 (Mediterranean) while wind peaks H2 in Spain and Italy — creating a natural partial offset that moderates seasonality in the growing renewables segment [#A]

Cause — working capital: Enel historically receives energy bill payments in Q1 (January utility bills for H2 prior-year consumption) — creating a cash seasonality that is more pronounced than EBITDA seasonality [#I]

FCF seasonality: Free cash flow is heavily H2-weighted (historically ~60-65% of annual FCF generated in H2) partly due to capex timing (Enel typically back-loads capex spending) [#A]

Sceptical bullet: Apparent seasonality stability may mask a changing pattern — as Enel's renewables mix shifts toward ~35% of EBITDA (from ~25% in FY-22), the solar/wind seasonal offset should increase; if the Spanish wind resource continues its declining-capacity-factor trend (observed FY-23 and FY-24), H2 seasonality could weaken, reducing full-year FCF predictability.

Beat/miss frequency: EBITDA beat in 6 of 8 L3Y reporting periods (75% beat rate); EPS beat in 5 of 8 (63%); revenue beat in 7 of 8 (88% — but revenue is mechanically hard to miss given passthrough pricing) [#A]
- Sub: European utility peer beat rate on EBITDA ~60-65% historically (Iberdrola ~70%) — Enel's 75% is above peer median, consistent with conservative guidance setting

Beat magnitude: Average EBITDA beat of +2.3% vs consensus over L3Y (FY-22 miss of -6.6% excluded from average — macro shock, not management miss); consistent with a ~€300-400m cushion per period [#A]

Miss events: FY-22 was the single significant miss (-€1.2bn EBITDA vs consensus) — attributable to macro shocks (Russia energy crisis, Spain windfall tax) not management execution failure; H1-22 also a mild EBITDA miss (-€0.3bn) for the same reason [#J]
- Sub: Sell-side consensus was too slow to cut estimates for FY-22 energy headwinds — miss was partly a consensus failure, not purely a company failure

Sandbagging signal: Moderate sandbagging is present — FY-23 guidance (set at CMD Nov-22) for €18.6bn EBITDA was beaten by €0.6bn (FY-23 actual ~€19.2bn); FY-24 guidance (CMD Mar-23 plan: €20.4bn EBITDA) was beaten by ~€0.4bn [#A]
- Sub: The degree of sandbagging is consistent with Enel's historical pattern pre-crisis (FY-19, FY-20, FY-21 each beat EBITDA guidance by 1-3%)

Guidance accuracy: Management's formal CMD plan targets are met or exceeded in 7 of 8 periods where a formal target existed; the exception is FY-22 (macro) [#A]
- Sub: Net debt guidance has been the most accurate metric — Enel met FY-23 net debt target (€51bn) and FY-24 target (~€47bn) within €0.5bn

Trend in accuracy: Guidance accuracy improved markedly post-FY-22 as the strategic simplification reduced the number of moving parts (fewer LatAm FX variables, fewer commodity-trading positions) [#J]

EPS credibility: EPS guidance is less reliable — financing cost line and tax line introduce ~5-8% EPS variance even when EBITDA is on track [#A]
- Sub: Enel's average net debt ~€47-50bn at ~4% average cost = ~€1.9-2.0bn financing costs; a 30bps rate deviation costs ~€0.01-0.02 EPS — meaningful for a stock trading on yield

Sell-side revision pattern: Consensus EBITDA estimates for the following 12M period were revised upward on the back of FY-23 and H1-24 results (post-release revision +1-3%); FY-22 results triggered large downward revisions of -8-10% for FY-23 at the time [#A]

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on management credibility is that consistent EBITDA beats are sandbagging running out of cushion — the disposal programme is ~90% complete, leaving fewer one-off levers; future "beats" must come from organic operations where visibility is lower; the CMD habit of setting ambitious 3Y targets (e.g., €21bn EBITDA by FY-26) but with historical actual delivery ~2-4% below the 3Y plan creates a credibility ceiling.
