EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (BLUF)

Subsea 7 presents a compelling structural opportunity driven by managed leadership succession, transformative Saipem merger ambitions, and exceptional operational execution, though the company faces meaningful integration risks and potential antitrust headwinds that could disrupt the forward thesis. The company is converting structural pricing power into peer-leading 23% margin guidance and generating sector-topping 7-9% FCF yields with a net-cash balance sheet. The net external signal reveals a structural demand upcycle and tight vessel supply constraining capacity into 2028, driving pricing power, while the Saipem merger promises unprecedented market consolidation.

While Jefferies views Evans' retirement as a slight negative given his strong execution record, they view Fitzgerald's succession as providing important positive continuity.

The core executive leadership team remains unchanged under the transition, with key figures including CFO Mark Foley and EVP Projects & Operations Phil Simons.

Employee turnover increased slightly to 9.9% in 2025 from 9.6% in 2024, while total headcount dropped from 15,072 to 13,821 in 2025, primarily driven by project portfolio shifts in Angola and Singapore.

proposed merger of equals with Saipem to create "Saipem7" by H2 2026 represents a transformative strategic shift, with the combined entity targeting over €300 million in annualised synergies within three years of completion and combining Subsea 7's 41 vessels with Saipem's fleet to create a 60-vessel powerhouse.

However, Brazil's antitrust regulator, CADE, has declared the Saipem-Subsea7 merger "complex", prompting demands for potential remedy proposals, and regulators may require the divestment of key deepwater vessels, such as Saipem's FDS and Subsea 7's Seven Oceans, to preserve competition.

Broker views diverge sharply, with Morgan Stanley viewing the combination as making strategic sense given the fragmented market, while Jefferies notes Subsea 7 has already cut costs drastically since 2011, meaning further synergies must come entirely from Saipem, and a former executive views massive subsea mergers as potentially destructive, risking the loss of specific operational focus.

development demand remains robust as operators prioritise deepwater tie-backs and long-cycle projects to sustain production through 2035, with Subsea 7's backlog reaching a record $13.9bn in Q3 2025, providing unprecedented revenue visibility into 2027 and 2028.

The proposed Saipem-Subsea7 merger will fundamentally alter industry dynamics, creating a dominant global leader in subsea installation, with Saipem and Subsea 7 combined having recently secured approximately 60% of large subsea projects in Brazil.

High- end subsea installation relies heavily on a limited global fleet of specialised J-lay, S-lay, and Reel-lay vessels, and there is virtually no new-build vessel capacity entering the deepwater installation market, insulating incumbents from sudden supply-driven disruption.

The global enabler vessel fleet is severely constrained, with ships spending significant time in transit between international regions, and the lack of new vessel construction, driven by high capital costs and cautious lending, restricts overall market capacity. 2025 reported revenue grew 4% year-on-year to $7.1 billion, driven by sustained deepwater offshore activity, with the core Subsea and Conventional unit delivering 5% revenue growth in 2025, reaching $5.8 billion, and group adjusted EBITDA margins expanding 500 basis points year-on-year, reaching 21% for the full year 2025, with the core Subsea segment reaching an impressive 23% EBITDA margin, up from 16% in 2024.

Free cash flow more than doubled year-on-year to $1.2 billion in 2025, demonstrating exceptional operational leverage, with the company achieving a 1.1x cash conversion ratio in 2025, up from 0.9x in 2024, confirming high-quality earnings.

Unmatched by most peers, the group ended 1Q26 with $198 million in net cash, inclusive of $337 million in lease liabilities, with gross gearing (borrowings divided by total equity) falling to a highly conservative 13.1% by year-end 2025.
