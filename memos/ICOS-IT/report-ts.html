Section 2 – The 80/20 (bottom line up front) 8 findings

Section summary
  1. Intercos offers a resilient and highly cash-generative B2B play on global beauty trends, underpinned by structural outsourcing tailwinds and proprietary innovation, though execution risks from near-term tariff complexities warrant a balanced first look.
1.
Superior business model: Intercos operates as a leading business-to-business original design manufacturer (ODM) that develops, formulates, and manufactures beauty products across make-up, skincare, and hair and body categories. The company distinguishes itself from pure contract manufacturers by owning the intellectual property for approximately 80.00% of its formulations, creating high customer stickiness and a natural barrier to entry
2.
Robust investment thesis: A potential investment case is driven by a compelling cyclical recovery in make-up combined with structural outsourcing acceleration from major multinational beauty conglomerates. Over the next 18 to 24 months, these dynamics should drive double-digit earnings growth as brands outsource formulation to lower capital expenditure and gain speed-to-market
3.
Critical swing factor: The single most important swing factor for earnings is the category and customer mix, specifically the proportion of high-margin, innovation-led "free issue" make-up sales versus lower-margin contract manufacturing or "full service" sales that carry pass-through packaging costs
4.
Accelerating financial trajectory: Intercos has achieved a solid multi-year growth trajectory, surpassing €1 billion in sales for the first time in FY24 (€1,064.90 million) and expanding its adjusted EBITDA margin by 143 basis points to 14.90% in FY25. This growth stands out in a period when consumer beauty demand slowed down globally, reflecting Intercos' ability to outperform the market
5.
Subdued recent start: The most recent reported quarterly performance in 1Q26 showed a planned sales contraction of -6.30% at constant exchange rates to €227.50 million, lapping a very high comparison base of +13.10% in 1Q25. However, a record order intake in late 2025 and early 2026 supports management's confidence in achieving its +5% to +6% net revenue growth guidance for the full year FY26
6.
Material business risk: The most significant risk that could break the investment case is prolonged macroeconomic pressure that causes customers to de-stock or delay new product launches, alongside near-term supply chain and tariff disruption. This risk is particularly acute in the US market, where tariff uncertainties have previously prompted client conservatism
7.
Favourable investment verdict: A closer look is highly warranted because the stock retains a dominant global market position and trades at an undemanding valuation of ~7.50x CY26E EBITDA, which represents a ~30.00% discount to ingredients peers and represents a compelling IRR play for long-term staples investors

Section 3 – What the company does 12 findings

Section summary
  1. Intercos maintains a dominant global leadership position in color cosmetics formulation and manufacturing, leveraging its vast proprietary IP portfolio to establish deep-seated, high-barrier commercial partnerships with both global beauty giants and fast-growing emerging brands.

Segment Dynamics and Revenue Models

1.
Primary growth driver: The Make-up division represents the core growth engine of the company, accounting for over 60.00% of total group revenues as of FY25. This segment encompasses the design, R&D, and manufacturing of powders, wet powders, foundations, lipsticks, delivery systems, and nail polishes
2.
Strategic skincare focus: Skincare constitutes the second key segment, specialized in developing and manufacturing creams, serums, and lotions. It represents approximately 14.10% of group revenues as of 1Q26, with a significant hub in South Korea and Asia-Pacific to capture fast-growing regional and global skincare trends
3.
Deliberate portfolio restructuring: The Hair & Body segment represents approximately 22.10% of 1Q26 sales and was built primarily through the acquisition of the Cosmint Group in 2017. Management has deliberately focused on restructuring this division, stepping away from low-margin, full-service contract manufacturing to install Intercos' high-margin, innovation-led model
4.
Varying contracting structures: Intercos generates revenue through two primary contracting structures: "full service" sales where the company provides the formulation and the packaging, and "free issue" sales where the client provides the packaging. Because packaging is treated as a zero-margin pass-through cost, free-issue sales significantly improve gross margins and underlying P&L quality
5.
Clear analytical metric: To evaluate its core operating performance accurately, management utilizes the Value Added Sales (VAS) metric, defined as net sales minus packaging costs. This metric filters out the volatile, margin-dilutive pass-through effects of packaging, allowing a clean compared base with premium ingredients and chemical formulation peers

Customer Landscape and Competitive Barriers

6.
Highly diversified customer base: The customer base is divided into three key categories: multinationals (49.00% of 2020 sales), emerging brands (32.00%), and retailers (19.00%). Multinationals provide deep-seated volume stability, while emerging indie brands drive highly profitable, R&D-heavy project growth
7.
Severe customer concentration: Commercial concentration is notable, with the top 10 customers accounting for approximately half of total group sales, creating a key structural vulnerability if a major brand departs. However, these relationships are exceptionally sticky, averaging over 15 years in duration with very rare instances of customer losses
8.
Unassailable formulation advantage: The company's durable competitive advantage is rooted in its extensive intellectual property ownership, owning the formulations for over 80.00% of its products. This IP ownership builds immense switching costs, as brand owners are highly reluctant to alter formulations of successful, established products due to product consistency risks
9.
Dominant global scale: Intercos is the global leader in B2B make-up with a 10.00% market share, and the fourth-largest player in outsourced skincare. Its massive global manufacturing footprint of 15 plants across major geographic markets allows it to offer unparalleled supply chain flexibility and local-for-local manufacturing
10.
Highly fragmented marketplace: The global B2B beauty manufacturing industry remains highly fragmented and characterized by small, regional players, positioning Intercos as a natural consolidator with significant scale advantages. Intercos is at least 2.50x larger than its next largest European and American competitors, leaving Korean players Cosmax and Korea Kolmar as its only comparable global peers
11.
Other side of the story: Sceptical analysis indicates that the company suffers from severe customer service and delivery reliability issues, causing smaller, more agile competitors to occasionally capture business. Furthermore, some customers have previously placed Intercos on temporary "ban lists" due to slow response times and scheduling prioritization of larger clients over smaller brands

Section 4 – Financial profile and performance 10 findings

Section summary
  1. Intercos has delivered a solid trajectory of margin expansion and robust cash conversion since its listing, driven by a favorable shift toward make-up and rigorous operational efficiency, though near-term results reflect currency headwinds and de-prioritized contract business.

Multi-Year Financial Performance

1.
Consistent revenue expansion: The group expanded net sales from €835.60 million in FY22 to €1,064.90 million in FY24, showing robust recovery post-pandemic before consolidating at €1,047.00 million in FY25 due to planned portfolio optimization
2.
Superior adjusted margin: The adjusted EBITDA margin expanded to 14.90% in FY25, up 143 basis points compared to FY24, driven by a higher proportion of core make-up sales and operational efficiency. Make-up returned to represent over 60.00% of total sales, which carries higher intrinsic margins than the hair and body segment
3.
Record Value margin: EBITDA margin calculated on Value Added Sales (excluding packaging costs) reached a record 19.20% in FY25, highlighting superior raw material and formulation pricing power. This Value Added Sales EBITDA margin is significantly higher than direct Korean ODM competitors whose EBITDA margins hover around 10.00% to 11.00%
4.
Outstanding leverage reduction: Net debt declined steadily from 0.80x EBITDA in FY22 to an all-time low of 0.64x in FY25, reflecting exceptional financial discipline and strong working capital management
5.
Above-peer return profile: The company generates superior returns on capital compared to local peers, with return on equity (ROE) reaching approximately 17.30% in FY25 compared to under 14.00% in historical periods

Balance Sheet and Recent Results

6.
Resilient quarterly delivery: The most recent reported results in 1Q26 showed net sales of €227.50 million, contracting by -9.30% reported (-6.30% constant currency) compared to 1Q25. However, adjusted EBITDA of €25.00 million came in ahead of sell-side consensus expectations of €24.10 million, illustrating strong cost control
7.
Confident replicated targets: Management firmly reiterated its full-year FY26 guidance, expecting reported net revenues to grow by approximately +5% to +6% compared to FY25. This targets growth above the anticipated +4.00% expansion of the global beauty market, driven by double-digit order book growth in make-up
8.
Conservative guidance track: Historical analysis indicates management's guidance reads as honest and highly conservative, with a proven track record of meeting or exceeding targets. For example, in FY24, the company beat its net sales guidance by delivering €1,064.90 million in revenue despite navigating a major cyberattack in the first quarter of that year, though this trajectory could be temporarily elongated if raw material sourcing bottlenecks return
9.
Other side of the story: Sceptical analysis indicates that interest expense volatility has consistently weighed on net income, with 1Q26 EPS estimates being revised downward by mid-to-high single digits due to elevated finance costs. This highlights that despite strong EBITDA performance, higher debt servicing and unfavorable foreign exchange translation on intercompany loans directly restrict EPS conversion

Section 5 – The possible investment case 7 findings

Section summary
  1. The forward investment case strengthens significantly as structural cosmetics outsourcing and premium make-up demand inflection converge with high-margin innovation initiatives, which should accelerate EPS growth over the 12-to-24-month horizon.

Core Growth Catalysts

1.
Accelerating make-up restart: The primary driver of the forward earnings trajectory is the restart of the make-up product cycle, which directly touches the high-margin Make-up revenue line. This is highly plausible as beauty conglomerates increase their R&D spend to win market share, but failure would occur if consumer discretionary spending decelerates sharply
2.
Compelling outsourcing acceleration: Increased B2B outsourcing from large multinationals, such as Estée Lauder transitioning the majority of its US cosmetics powder manufacturing to Intercos, represents accelerating B2B outsourcing momentum. This is highly plausible given rising operating costs of in-house factories, but would fail if conglomerates decide to re-internalize production
3.
Agile localized presence: The transition to localized R&D laboratories in regional hubs allows Intercos to capture fast-growing emerging brand demand directly in China and North America. This is highly plausible as local brands take share in Asia, but would fail if domestic CDMOs in China replicate Intercos' technology at cheaper price points

Forward Trajectory Plausibility

4.
Favourable mix expansion: An accelerating demand skew toward prestigious, innovation-heavy formulas versus mass-market contract manufacturing directly expands EBIT margins. This is highly plausible as the high-end beauty market outpaces mass equivalents, but would fail if consumer downtrading compresses the premium category
5.
Independent growth drivers: For the ultimate bull case to materialize, which projects a c. +9.00% sales CAGR and EBITDA margin reaching 17.40% by FY27, these key drivers do not necessarily have to fire in perfect unison. While the make-up cycle recovery and multinational outsourcing are highly independent, a synchronized expansion across both make-up and skincare would accelerate the path
6.
Other side of the story: Sceptical analysis indicates that the investment case faces a major bottleneck in US skincare capacity constraints, as Intercos currently lacks a local skincare manufacturing base in the United States. This deficit directly increases tariff exposure on skincare products imported from Europe, making the company less competitive against local US-based contract manufacturers

Section 6 – Valuation 6 findings

Section summary
  1. Intercos shares look uniquely cheap and fundamentally undemanding at current levels, trading at a significant discount to historical averages and global ingredients peers despite possessing vastly superior margins and returns.
1.
Deeply depressed multiple: Under Morgan Stanley estimates, the stock currently trades at an EV/EBITDA multiple of approximately 7.50x FY26E EBITDA, which represents a c. -30.00% discount to global ingredients peers. This multiple sits near all-time historical lows on both an absolute and relative basis, signaling a strong re-rating opportunity
2.
Compressed peer pricing: Under Jefferies analysis, Intercos trades at an EV/EBITDA of approximately 7.90x for FY26E, which is exceptionally low in the context of global beauty sector transactions. The stock trades at only a modest premium to South Korean beauty outsourcers, despite possessing vastly superior product depth and margins
3.
Predictable re-rating pathway: On next-twelve-months (NTM) price-to-earnings, the shares trade at c. 16.00x P/E under Jefferies base-case projections, which implies a re-rating potential to 18.50x under their €15.30 price target
4.
Favourable consensus target: The sell-side median price target sits at €15.00 under Morgan Stanley base-case projections, implying a c. 26.69% upside to the current share price of €11.84. These targets reflect capitalizing forward EBITDA at approximately 9.00x
5.
Other side of the story: Sceptical analysis indicates that the structural valuation discount to ingredients peers is permanently structural due to Intercos' higher quarterly earnings volatility and customer concentration. Unlike ingredients giants who supply highly diversified chemical inputs, Intercos is highly exposed to the volatile, fashion-driven product lifecycles of individual beauty brands

Section 7 – Key risks and the other side 6 findings

Section summary
  1. The overall risk profile is highly manageable given robust balance sheet flexibility and defensive pricing power, though near-term supply chain disruptions and tariff complexities remain the primary friction points.

Key Operational Vulnerabilities

1.
Severe concentration vulnerability: The most critical structural risk is the loss of a major multinational customer, given that the top ten clients account for approximately half of group revenues. While the average relationship exceeds 15 years, this vulnerability might become a material drag if client destocking persists
2.
Elevated tariff complexity: Protectionist import duties and international tariff policies represent a major threat to localized manufacturing cost structures, particularly in the US. While Intercos can pass on surcharges to customers, near-term trade turbulence creates order placement delays
3.
Reassuring short activity: Quantitative screening reveals no published short-seller reports or unusually elevated short interest, confirming that institutional investors are not aggressively betting against the stock
4.
Critical early-warning indicator: The single most critical early-warning signal to monitor is the rolling six-month order intake trend in color cosmetics. A deceleration in order strength below high-single digits would indicate a slowing of the make-up cycle before it hits the reported revenue line
5.
Other side of the story: Sceptical analysis indicates that the bearish risks are significantly overstated because Intercos maintains exceptional pricing power, with its services representing a low-single-digit percentage of clients' total COGS. Consequently, brand owners can easily absorb cost pass-throughs and monthly surcharges without switching suppliers

Section 8 – Management: what is changing 4 findings

Section summary
  1. Executive leadership is currently a material source of near-term uncertainty due to the abrupt termination of the newly appointed CFO, which marks the company's third CFO change in a short two-year window.
1.
Sudden CFO termination: On May 7, 2026, Intercos announced the immediate termination of CFO Paola Agasso, who had only taken office on December 1, 2025, after being appointed in November 2025. This sudden departure marks excessive executive instability and will require the group to launch its third search for a permanent financial lead within the last two years
2.
Operational COO transition: Following Agasso's departure, current Group Chief Operating Officer Vittorio Brenna has immediately assumed the additional role of interim CFO. This transition ensures operational continuity as the board initiates a formal candidate selection process, but highlights near-term organizational friction
3.
Clear succession framework: Gianandrea Ferrari, the son of the founder, was appointed as Group Chief Commercial Officer in May 2025, consolidating his position inside executive ranks. This move reinforces the family's long-term generational succession path, although former executives caution it may create organizational barriers to independent thinking

Section 9 – Net read and what to check next 5 findings

Section summary
  1. Intercos strongly warrants deeper investment work because its record order book, robust balance sheet, and dominant global scale provide clear visibility into an attractive 12-to-24-month EPS recovery trajectory.
1.
Prime outsourcing beneficiary: The most compelling case for investment is that Intercos is a uniquely diversified, global player that is the primary beneficiary of rising industry outsourcing and the restarting make-up cycle. This positioning secures highly predictable revenue growth backed by a record order book
2.
Other side of the story: Sceptical analysis indicates that recurring executive instability and the dominant influence of the 83-year-old founder may restrict the company's ability to drive modern corporate restructuring and resolve delivery service inefficiencies
3.
Critical contract verification: Investors should immediately cross-verify the specific volume and pricing terms of the newly expanded Estée Lauder cosmetics powder contract to evaluate its exact margin-dilutive or margin-accretive profile

Focused operational investigation: Investors should commission a focused expert-network study on Intercos' US competitor landscape to determine whether organic skirmishes or the lack of US skincare manufacturing will permanently handicap growth
