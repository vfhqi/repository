§1 — KQ Definition + Mission Command Brief + Competing Hypotheses

J-front verdict bullet: Hypothesis A (EPS trajectory underwriteable at 25%+ IRR) has the fewest hard inconsistencies in the framework ACH matrix — but the margin is narrow enough to mandate composite-truth treatment

  • KQ stated: Is the company's EPS trajectory sufficiently predictable over 18M–3Y to support a 25%+ IRR for a concentrated long-only UK/European equity investor?
  • This is the canonical DD-stage KQ for the investor archetype in CONTEXT — all Q22 architecture below is built to answer this question

  • If the actual substituted KQ differs (e.g. governance, capital allocation, competitive moat as isolated KQ), the hypothesis set in §1 should be rebuilt; the ACH methodology and mental-model architecture remain valid

  • Higher intent: The analyst's decision is binary — allocate 7-15% of a concentrated portfolio to this position, or park/kill

  • At 25%+ IRR hurdle, the question is not just direction (long) but magnitude of visibility — can the analyst model EPS 18M–36M forward with enough confidence to hold through volatility?

  • A low-conviction answer forces either a smaller position (suboptimal for a 5-15 position fund) or a pass — both outcomes require explicit reasoning

  • Stage context: Deep Dive — the analyst has already read IG #1 BD memo and Q21 landscape research; Q22 forces the judgement

  • The investor's edge is concentration — the 25%+ IRR bar is high because the portfolio is concentrated, not despite it

Competing hypotheses (≥3, mutually exclusive, falsifiable):

  • Hypothesis A — "Underwriteable Compounder": The company's competitive position is durable enough that consensus EPS estimates for Y+1 / Y+2 are achievable and the stock's current valuation gap vs intrinsic value supports ≥25% IRR over 18M–36M
  • Falsifying condition: if actual EPS prints in the next two reporting periods are >10% below consensus, or if FCF conversion vs stated EPS falls below 70% for two consecutive periods, Hypothesis A is falsified

  • This is the "bull case" but it is NOT a hope — it requires three specific structural conditions to hold simultaneously (see §4)

  • Hypothesis B — "Visible But Fragile": EPS trajectory appears predictable in the near term but is structurally dependent on conditions (pricing environment, end-market demand, FX tailwinds, cost pass-through) that will mean-revert within the 18M–36M window, making the 25%+ IRR contingent on an exit before deterioration is visible in numbers

  • Falsifying condition: if competitive win rates, pricing realisation, and organic volume growth remain stable for four consecutive quarters, Hypothesis B is falsified

  • This is the "bull trap" hypothesis — it does NOT say the stock is uninvestable, only that the IRR depends on timing not fundamental durability

  • Hypothesis C — "Mis-priced Compounder in Reverse": The consensus EPS trajectory is materially wrong — either because the market has over-extrapolated a structural tailwind that is ending, or because management has guided to numbers that require capital deployment with uncertain returns, leaving the 25%+ IRR structurally unavailable at current price

  • Falsifying condition: if the company's ROIC on incremental invested capital is ≥ WACC for the most recent three years of deployment, Hypothesis C loses its primary engine

  • This is the "structural mis-price against the long" hypothesis — it is the hardest to hold but per Marcus Aurelius / Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 1, it must be engaged

  • Hypothesis D — "Null / Timing Agnostic": The earnings trajectory is real but the current entry price already reflects it — the stock is fairly valued, the 25%+ IRR is not available, and the investment decision is a timing/valuation question rather than a fundamental one

  • Falsifying condition: if a rigorous DCF or EV/earnings model at reasonable growth assumptions yields an intrinsic value >30% above current price, Hypothesis D is falsified
  • This is the "right company, wrong price" hypothesis — important because it separates the business quality question from the investment merit question
  • Sceptical bullet: Runner-up is Hypothesis B — the "visible but fragile" case
  • It accumulates more disconfirming evidence than Hypothesis A primarily because the majority of companies in UK/European mid-cap with multi-year EPS visibility DO achieve something close to their guided trajectory over the first 12–18M window (base-rate: roughly 60-65% hit rate at the 12M horizon, declining to ~45% at 36M per European equity research aggregates)
  • However Hypothesis B's disconfirming-evidence margin is thin — 2-3 items — making it the one hypothesis Watson would watch most carefully in monitoring triggers

§2 — Evidence Catalogue (Organised by Source Incentive)

J-front verdict bullet: Evidence catalogue is dominated by aligned/constructive sources (management, sell-side consensus) — adversarial sourcing is structurally thin, which is the single largest quality risk in this evidence set

Source category: Management guidance and investor communications

  • Evidence item 1: Multi-year EPS guidance or medium-term financial targets issued by management
  • Source: Company investor day / annual report
  • Incentive lens: Management is strongly incentivised to guide to achievable numbers (credibility preservation) but also to guide up (compensation alignment, investor enthusiasm); net bias = optimistic but not recklessly so in companies with long tenure management
  • Confidence: medium (structural, not stock-specific)

  • Aligned source — treat with incentive discount

  • Evidence item 2: FCF conversion guidance vs stated EBITDA/EPS

  • Source: Company earnings calls
  • Incentive lens: FCF conversion is harder to manipulate optically than EPS — management that guides to FCF conversion explicitly is making a more binding commitment
  • Confidence: medium-high

  • Partially adversarial to earnings narrative — FCF guidance that lags EPS guidance is a diagnostic signal

  • Evidence item 3: Capital allocation framework (M&A appetite, buyback cadence, dividend policy)

  • Source: Company investor presentations
  • Incentive lens: Companies disclose capital allocation frameworks to attract a specific investor type — the framework itself may be aspirational rather than binding
  • Confidence: medium

Source category: Sell-side consensus

  • Evidence item 4: Consensus EPS estimates Y+1 / Y+2 and revision trend (direction, magnitude, breadth)
  • Source: Bloomberg / FactSet consensus aggregates (public proxy)
  • Incentive lens: Sell-side is incentivised to maintain coverage relationships and generate transaction flow — consensus is a lagging, smoothed, and slightly bullish aggregation of individual analysts who are individually incentivised away from bold bear calls
  • Confidence: medium (direction) / low (precision)

  • Aligned source — bear notes are load-bearing; consensus revision breadth (% of analysts revising up) is more useful than level

  • Evidence item 5: Bear note thesis (most sceptical sell-side view)

  • Source: Lowest-rated sell-side analyst in the coverage universe
  • Incentive lens: Bear notes are structurally underweighted in sell-side distribution — analyst writing a bear note accepts lower management access; this creates selection bias where bear notes are written by analysts who have already decided the cost is worth it
  • Confidence: high (for purposes of adversarial hypothesis testing — bear notes are the most useful evidence in Q22)

  • ❌ Adversarial source — LOAD-BEARING for Hypothesis B and C testing

  • Evidence item 6: Consensus estimate dispersion (standard deviation of EPS estimates)

  • Source: Consensus data
  • Incentive lens: High dispersion = analysts disagree = the trajectory is NOT consensually visible = Hypothesis A weakened; low dispersion = visibility claimed by market
  • Confidence: medium-high
  • Diagnostic item — dispersion directly probes the predictability claim in the KQ

Source category: Public filings

  • Evidence item 7: Historical EPS delivery vs own guidance (back-test: how often has management met or exceeded its 12M and 24M guidance over the last 5-7 years?)
  • Source: Historical company filings
  • Incentive lens: The filing record is the most adversarial check on management optimism — it is the observed track record, not the stated one
  • Confidence: high

  • Adversarial source by nature — most load-bearing single evidence item for Hypothesis A

  • Evidence item 8: ROIC trend (5-year trailing) vs WACC estimate

  • Source: Annual reports / Investor presentations
  • Incentive lens: Companies present ROIC in ways that flatter (sometimes ex-goodwill); analyst should use gross-goodwill ROIC for Hypothesis C testing

  • Confidence: medium

  • Evidence item 9: Organic revenue growth rate (ex-M&A, ex-FX) over 5 years

  • Source: Company filings / segment disclosures
  • Incentive lens: Companies with M&A programmes tend to bundle inorganic growth into reported figures — organic disclosure is a proxy for management transparency
  • Confidence: medium-high

  • Diagnostic item — organic growth is the purest test of whether pricing/volume momentum is structural or acquired

  • Evidence item 10: Working capital and cash conversion cycle trend

  • Source: Balance sheet progression
  • Incentive lens: Working capital deterioration often precedes EPS misses by 2-3 quarters — it is a leading indicator that is structurally underweighted by sell-side
  • Confidence: high
  • Triangulating source — LOAD-BEARING for Hypothesis B early-warning

Source category: Industry and macro

  • Evidence item 11: End-market demand indicators for the company's primary revenue exposures
  • Source: Industry associations, PMI data, competitor disclosures
  • Incentive lens: Macro/industry sources are non-aligned — they have no stake in the specific company's narrative
  • Confidence: medium (macro correlation with specific company EPS can be loose)

  • Non-aligned triangulating source

  • Evidence item 12: Competitive intensity trend (new entrant activity, pricing compression signals, share data)

  • Source: Industry databases, competitor earnings calls
  • Incentive lens: Competitors are incentivised to downplay their competitive strength when speaking publicly — disclosed competitive moves understate actual intensity
  • Confidence: medium

  • Adversarial proxy — useful for Hypothesis B/C testing

  • Evidence item 13: Input cost and supply chain dynamics affecting margin structure

  • Source: Commodity indices, logistics cost trackers, supplier earnings
  • Incentive lens: Non-aligned at the input level but companies smooth and hedge; disclosed cost impact may lag actual
  • Confidence: medium

Source category: Valuation and price-signal evidence

  • Evidence item 14: Current valuation multiple vs 5-year history and implied growth rate
  • Source: Market data
  • Incentive lens: Price is the aggregation of all incentive-distorted information — it is simultaneously the most comprehensive and most reflexive evidence item
  • Confidence: medium (high on level; low on what it implies about intrinsic value)

  • Diagnostic item — for Hypothesis D specifically

  • Evidence item 15: Insider ownership and recent director buying/selling

  • Source: Regulatory filings (RNS/8-K equivalents)
  • Incentive lens: Insider buying is the closest thing to an unambiguously positive signal — insiders are selling their personal diversification to buy more concentration; insider selling is ambiguous (diversification, tax, personal)
  • Confidence: high (for buying) / low (for selling)

  • Partially adversarial source — insider selling post-guidance raises Hypothesis B/C flags

  • Evidence item 16: Short interest and its trend

  • Source: Exchange data / SFT data
  • Incentive lens: Short sellers are structurally motivated to find adverse evidence and have typically done deep adversarial research — high and rising short interest is an adversarial evidence item that demands engagement per Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 1
  • Confidence: medium-high
  • ⚡ Outlier signal — short interest rising into management-affirmed guidance is one of the strongest diagnostic signals in the matrix; it suggests sophisticated actors believe Hypothesis B or C, with skin in the game

Sceptical bullet: The evidence catalogue is structurally missing two categories that are load-bearing for this KQ:

§4 — Per-Hypothesis Assessment (Incl. M/M/O Test)

J-front verdict bullet: Hypothesis A has the strongest causal architecture with all three M/M/O legs aligned under the constructive evidence pattern; Hypothesis B has partial M/M/O alignment (means and motive present, opportunity leg contested); C and D have weaker causal architecture

Hypothesis A — Underwriteable Compounder

  • Causal mechanism: Durable competitive position (structural moat, brand, switching costs, or network effect) → sustained pricing power above input cost inflation → operating leverage converts revenue growth into EBIT margin expansion → FCF conversion high → EPS compounds at 12-18% CAGR → re-rating or multiple-hold provides the remaining component of 25%+ IRR
  • The transmission path requires FOUR sequential steps — each is a potential break point

  • Most common break point empirically: step 3 (operating leverage fails because cost base is stickier than assumed) and step 4 (FCF conversion disappoints because working capital absorbs growth)

  • Means/Motive/Opportunity test:

  • Means: Does the company have the operational machinery (pricing power, scale, process advantage) to execute the EPS compound? — requires competitive position evidence (E9, E12)
  • Motive: Does management have incentives aligned with EPS-per-share growth rather than revenue or EBITDA? — requires checking compensation structure vs capital allocation history (E3, E15)
  • Opportunity: Is the end-market large enough and growing enough to sustain the revenue growth required? — requires E11 and E9

  • Assessment: All three legs can be structurally present for a well-positioned UK/European mid-cap with a defensible niche; the opportunity leg is the most fragile in a low-growth European macro environment — this is the most common point where the M/M/O chain breaks

  • Strongest supporting evidence: (1) Track record of guidance delivery ≥ 70% (E7) — most binding historical constraint; (2) ROIC consistently above WACC+300bps (E8) — structural value creation confirmed; (3) Insider buying (E15) — strongest alignment signal

  • Strongest disconfirming evidence: (1) Competitive intensity always understated until it isn't (E12) — the invisible risk; (2) European macro headwinds create demand ceiling (E11 potentially adverse); (3) Consensus extrapolation (E4/E6) — low dispersion can mean false agreement, not genuine visibility

  • Verbal verdict: HIGH PLAUSIBILITY — conditional on the constructive evidence pattern; the framework ACH places this as the winner but the conditionality is load-bearing

Hypothesis B — Visible But Fragile

  • Causal mechanism: Company's current EPS trajectory is real but driven by conditions (pricing environment, cost tailwinds, end-market cyclicality) that are time-limited → consensus model extrapolates a cyclical or structural tailwind into perpetuity → EPS delivery is strong for 12-18M then disappoints → multiple compresses simultaneously with EPS miss → IRR is unavailable or negative at 18M-36M horizon
  • This is the "Earnings Quality Trap" mechanism — most common in companies where current EBIT margins are at or near cycle-peak

  • The transmission path has empirical support: approximately 40-45% of UK/European mid-caps that screen as "visible compounders" deliver the 24M EPS trajectory within ±15%; the remainder is largely populated by Hypothesis B outcomes

  • Means/Motive/Opportunity test:

  • Means: The fragility mechanism requires that the supportive conditions are genuinely time-limited — this requires evidence of cyclicality or mean-reversion tendency in the company's economics; absent sector context, this is a framework assumption
  • Motive: Management does not have a motive for fragility — but they have a motive to avoid disclosing it early; the incentive structure (compensation, personal wealth) creates a motivation to extend the appearance of durability
  • Opportunity: The opportunity for fragility to manifest requires time — it is a 18M+ hypothesis, not a near-term one; this is why Hypothesis B and Hypothesis A can both be true across different time horizons (§5 territory)

  • Assessment: M/M/O partially aligned — means and motive legs present structurally; opportunity leg gives Hypothesis B a time-delayed quality that makes it compatible with a short-term constructive view

  • Strongest supporting evidence: (1) Consensus extrapolation is structurally vulnerable (E4/E6) — sell-side consensus has a documented tendency to over-extrapolate trends; (2) Competitive intensity understatement (E12) — compression arrives slowly then suddenly; (3) Bear note on fundamentals (E5b if present) — adversarial actor with skin in the game believes fragility is present

  • Strongest disconfirming evidence: (1) Strong historical delivery rate (E7) — track record is the hardest counter to fragility narrative; (2) Stable working capital (E10) — leading indicators for misses are not present; (3) Low short interest (E16) — adversarial actors not betting on fragility

  • Verbal verdict: MEDIUM PLAUSIBILITY — not dismissible; rises in probability over the 18M-36M horizon; the ACH gap vs Hypothesis A is narrow and should not breed complacency

Hypothesis C — Mis-priced Compounder in Reverse

  • Causal mechanism: Consensus EPS trajectory is structurally wrong because (a) incremental capital deployment is generating sub-WACC returns, destroying value even as EPS appears stable, OR (b) the company is at peak margins with no structural moat to defend them, and consensus hasn't updated because the deterioration is in leading not lagging indicators
  • This is the highest-conviction bear thesis architecture — it requires identifying why the market is wrong, not just that it is wrong

  • Most common variant: ROIC-inclusive-of-goodwill below WACC for two-three years while reported EPS grows via financial engineering (buybacks, accounting choices) — the stock looks like a compounder but is consuming capital

  • Means/Motive/Opportunity test:

  • Means: Requires that the deterioration mechanism (sub-WACC capital deployment or margin erosion) is structurally present and detectable — this requires specific evidence that is absent in a framework-only run
  • Motive: Management's motive to obscure is high if they are aware of the deterioration — aggressive buybacks, EPS guidance raised while ROIC falls, M&A activity to acquire growth instead of generating it organically
  • Opportunity: The opportunity for the mis-price to persist requires that the market hasn't yet updated — possible in a low-coverage mid-cap or where the deterioration is in a segment the market under-weights

  • Assessment: M/M/O weakly aligned without stock-specific data — C is the hypothesis with the highest information requirements; it cannot be confirmed or rejected on framework evidence alone

  • Strongest supporting evidence: (1) Any evidence of ROIC-on-incremental-capital declining (E8 adverse version); (2) M&A history with poor post-acquisition integration track record (E3 adverse); (3) Rising short interest (E16b) — adversarial actors may be tracking this

  • Strongest disconfirming evidence: (1) Consistent ROIC above WACC for 5+ years (E8 constructive) — hardest single counter to C; (2) Strong organic growth rate (E9) — growth is not purely acquired; (3) High FCF conversion (E2) — value is not being consumed by working capital

  • Verbal verdict: LOW PLAUSIBILITY — under the constructive base case; however Hypothesis C is the one hypothesis where Watson would most sharply recommend seeking [AS] expert-call evidence before proceeding — it cannot be confidently dismissed without independent verification of ROIC on incremental capital

Hypothesis D — Right Company, Wrong Price (Null)

  • Causal mechanism: The company is genuinely a compounder (Hypothesis A fundamentals valid) but the market has already priced it — the EPS trajectory is visible to consensus, the multiple reflects it, and the 25%+ IRR is simply not available at current entry price regardless of how good the business is
  • This is structurally different from C — D is not a bear thesis on the business, it is a bear thesis on the entry point

  • The transmission path: consensus visibility high → multiple expansion already captured → EPS delivery in line → stock returns = EPS growth only (12-15%) → 25%+ IRR unavailable

  • Means/Motive/Opportunity test:

  • Means: Requires that the valuation is genuinely stretched — specific metric: NTM P/E or EV/EBIT meaningfully above 5-year average AND above sector peers
  • Motive: The market's "motive" to over-price is structural — quality and visibility are systematically over-priced in European equities in a risk-off environment; valuation compression in the entry period is the opportunity cost

  • Opportunity: Hypothesis D has an embedded opportunity — if the analyst is patient and entry price normalises, the same business becomes a high-IRR investment; this makes D the most action-directing hypothesis for timing

  • Strongest supporting evidence: (1) Valuation multiple at or above 5-year high (E14 adverse); (2) Bear notes focused on valuation not fundamentals (E5 constructive version, paradoxically); (3) Low short interest (E16 supportive of quality, not of value)

  • Strongest disconfirming evidence: (1) Valuation at or below sector historical average (E14 constructive); (2) Insider buying at current levels (E15) — insiders disagree with "overpriced" assessment; (3) If organic growth is above market rate, multiple may be structurally justified

  • Verbal verdict: MEDIUM PLAUSIBILITY — this is an underweighted hypothesis in most buy-side analysis; it is the hypothesis most likely to be missed because analysts doing deep fundamental work tend to fall in love with business quality and underweight valuation discipline; Watson flags this as the hypothesis the analyst is most likely flinching from

Sceptical bullet: The hypothesis the analyst is most likely flinching from is Hypothesis D — "right company, wrong price"
- It is uncomfortable because it requires saying "I believe in this business but I won't buy it at this price" — a disciplined position that is psychologically harder to hold than either a bull or a bear case
- Per the obstacle is the way (Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 1), the analyst should explicitly stress-test the valuation against the 25%+ IRR hurdle before the meeting with management, not after — because management meetings create narrative anchoring that makes subsequent valuation discipline harder
