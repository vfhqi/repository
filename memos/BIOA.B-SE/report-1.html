§1 — General (Description, Financials, Peer Comparison)

J-front verdict: BioArctic is a founder-controlled Swedish CNS biotech trading at ~SEK 18-22bn market cap — almost entirely a Leqembi royalty story with a lumpy financials profile that looks deceptively profitable in milestone years but reverts to modest cash generation otherwise [#J]

Description: BioArctic AB was founded in 2003 by Prof Lars Lannfelt (Uppsala University) around protofibrils — a species of aggregated amyloid-beta implicated in Alzheimer's pathology earlier than plaques [#I]
- Sub: The company's core insight — targeting protofibrils rather than mature plaques — differentiated lecanemab from earlier amyloid antibodies (aducanumab, solanezumab) and is now validated by Phase 3 CLARITY AD data
- Sub: BioArctic retains rights outside the US/EU/Japan for lecanemab and holds a co-development agreement with Eisai; Biogen co-commercialises in the US

Strategic objective: BioArctic describes itself as a "research-based biotech focused on CNS diseases with high unmet need" — primary objective is to build a pipeline of novel CNS antibodies leveraging the protofibril platform [#I]
- Sub: Secondary objective is to maximise royalty income from lecanemab while self-funding pipeline R&D — not a traditional royalty-only vehicle
- Sub: Long-term ambition articulated at Capital Markets Days (2022, 2024) is to become an integrated CNS company, not solely a licensor — but this is 5-10 year horizon

Organisation: ~80-100 FTEs (as of 2024 annual report); heavily R&D-weighted; commercial function is minimal (Eisai owns commercialisation) [#I]
- Sub: Leadership team is stable and scientifically credentialled; no large operational infrastructure — this is a focused drug-discovery organisation
- Sub: Headquartered in Uppsala/Stockholm; no manufacturing; fully asset-light on production

Listing: BIOA B listed on Nasdaq Stockholm; A-shares (founders) carry superior voting rights and are not freely traded; B-shares are the investable instrument [#I]

§2 — Company Details (Revenue Model, Business Model, Recent Delivery, Internal Change)

J-front verdict: BioArctic's revenue model is almost entirely dependent on one asset (lecanemab), one partner (Eisai), and one indication (early Alzheimer's) — moat is IP-based but economic capture is structurally limited by the royalty rate and partnership structure [#J]

Revenue source: Three streams in principle — (1) upfront/milestone payments from Eisai, (2) royalties on Eisai/Biogen net sales, (3) minor collaborative R&D reimbursements; in practice stream (1) dominated FY2023 and stream (2) is the ongoing baseline [#I]
- Sub: Milestone payments are lumpy and non-recurring by nature; the major Phase 3 and FDA approval milestones have now been triggered; remaining milestones tied to label expansion (early-stage, subcutaneous formulation, Asian markets) are smaller
- Sub: Royalty rate on Leqembi is publicly reported as "low-to-mid single digit percentage" of net sales — BioArctic has not disclosed exact rate; this compares unfavourably to typical royalty structures in pharma deals where royalties of 8-15% are more common

Revenue mix: 100% CNS/neurology; 100% Alzheimer's disease (Leqembi); no diversification within current approved products [#I]

Geography: BioArctic retains rights in territories outside US/EU/Japan — i.e., rest-of-world including China, where lecanemab is under regulatory review [#I]
- Sub: If China approval occurs, milestone + royalty from that territory could be material (China AD patient population is very large — estimated 10m+ patients); but regulatory timing and pricing access remain uncertain
- Sub: US + EU + Japan commercialisation entirely controlled by Eisai/Biogen; BioArctic has no commercial presence in these markets

Cyclicality: Revenue is not economically cyclical in the traditional sense but is highly event-driven (clinical/regulatory milestones) — making it non-linear and difficult to model on a smoothed basis [#J]

Predictability: Low for milestone components; medium-low for royalty components (dependent on Leqembi ramp trajectory, which has disappointed consensus to date) [#J]
- Sub: Royalty income growth is linked to Leqembi net sales growth — which Carnegie, Nordea, and Berenberg have revised down from initial bull cases of USD 1-2bn pa US sales by 2025 to more modest USD 0.8-1.2bn pa by 2026E
- Sub: Eisai reported cumulative patient starts of ~16,000 globally (mid-2025 run rate data per Eisai/Biogen investor disclosures) vs pre-approval expectations of 50,000+ by end-2024 — a significant uptake lag

Driver count: Effectively one primary driver — Leqembi US (and to lesser extent EU/Japan) net sales volume × royalty rate [#J]
- Sub: This is critically low driver count vs European pharma peers (UCB has 3-4 major assets; Lundbeck 5+ marketed products) — any single-asset disappointment has full portfolio impact
- Sub: Diversification only comes from pipeline assets, the nearest of which is 5-7 years from potential market

Customer behaviour: BioArctic's "customer" is Eisai (the licensee); actual end-customers are neurologists/clinics administering Leqembi infusions to patients — BioArctic has no direct commercial relationship with physicians or patients [#A]
- Sub: Eisai's commercial execution quality is a key risk factor BioArctic does not control; the IV infusion protocol and ARIA monitoring requirements create physician/clinic friction that Eisai must overcome
- Sub: Switching costs at the physician level are meaningful once infusion centres are established and patients are on therapy — analogous to oncology biologics

Lock-in: Patients on Leqembi face limited switching options — the amyloid-clearing mechanism requires continuous therapy; discontinuation studies suggest rapid plaque re-accumulation; this creates a degree of patient stickiness once on therapy [#A]

Competitive advantage: IP estate (patents on lecanemab composition-of-matter, protofibril selectivity, manufacturing process) is the primary moat; these patents run to approximately 2034-2037 in major markets [#A]
- Sub: Scientific know-how in protofibril biology is a culture/knowledge moat — BioArctic's founding team (Lannfelt group) published the foundational papers and retains unique insights
- Sub: First-mover advantage in the approved anti-amyloid antibody class for early AD is meaningful but being challenged by Eli Lilly's donanemab (Kisunla, approved Jul-2024)

Recent delivery — last 3 years:

  • 2022 delivery: Phase 3 CLARITY AD data readout (Sep-2022) showed 27% slowing of cognitive decline vs placebo — landmark result that validated the protofibril hypothesis and drove the stock from ~SEK 150 to ~SEK 250-350; [AS-Carnegie, AS-Berenberg] [#A]
  • 2023 delivery: FDA traditional approval granted Jul-2023 (first-ever traditional approval for an amyloid-targeting Alzheimer's therapy); Eisai triggered major milestone payment (~SEK 2.5bn); Leqembi launched commercially in the US; EU approval received Jan-2024 [#A]
  • 2024 delivery: EU launch initiated; subcutaneous formulation programme progressing; Eisai began bimonthly maintenance dosing label expansion; China regulatory filing progress; quarterly royalty income starting to accrue to BioArctic P&L [#A]
  • Pipeline delivery: BAN2201 (separate GABA-A programme for CNS disorders — not Alzheimer's) advancing through preclinical; BAN1454 (anti-TDP-43 for ALS/FTLD) in Phase 1 — no readouts yet of significance [#I]

Internal change — L12M: CEO Gunilla Osswald reconfirmed as leader; Board composition stable; no major leadership or governance changes; focus shifted from regulatory milestone chasing to "commercial support of Eisai" + "pipeline R&D execution" [#I]
- Sub: BioArctic hired additional CNS scientists in L12M to deepen pipeline — consistent with stated ambition to build beyond lecanemab
- Sub: No activist involvement; founder control via A-shares insulates from external governance pressure

Internal change — N3Y: Anticipated milestones include subcutaneous Leqembi approval in US (FDA PDUFA date likely 2025-26), potential China approval, and Phase 1 data on BAN1454 — these are the most likely near-term catalysts [#A]

Sceptical bullet (§2): The revenue model's single-asset concentration means BioArctic's apparent "business model strength" is entirely derivative of Eisai's commercial execution and Leqembi's competitive positioning — if Eli Lilly's donanemab takes share or Eisai's infusion centre rollout remains slow, BioArctic cannot intervene; the royalty rate (low-to-mid single digit) means even a USD 2bn peak sales scenario generates only ~USD 60-120m pa to BioArctic, which at current EV (~USD 2bn) implies a very modest sustainable yield [#J]

§3 — Industry Context (Peer Comparison, Structure, Macro + Non-Macro Change)

J-front verdict: The Alzheimer's therapy market is entering a structural growth phase after decades of failure — but competitive intensity is rising sharply as Lilly's donanemab (Kisunla) competes directly with Leqembi, and market penetration remains far below bull-case scenarios as of mid-2025 [#J]

Market structure: The approved anti-amyloid antibody space for early Alzheimer's is currently a duopoly — Leqembi (Eisai/Biogen) and Kisunla (Eli Lilly/donanemab, approved Jul-2024) are the only fully FDA-approved options; aducanumab (Biogen/Aduhelm) was withdrawn from the US market in Jan-2024 [#I]
- Sub: Top 2 share is effectively 100% of the anti-amyloid antibody market for early AD — but this market is nascent and total penetration of the eligible patient population is <1% as of mid-2025 per Eisai/Lilly investor disclosures
- Sub: The broader "Alzheimer's drug market" including symptomatic agents (Aricept, Namenda generics, Exelon) is much larger by patient volume but those are low-value commoditised treatments; the "disease-modifying" segment is new and premium-priced

Concentration: Anti-amyloid antibody concentration is currently extreme (duopoly) but the pipeline suggests 3-5 competitors within 5 years — Roche/Genentech's remternetug is in Phase 3; other tau-targeting approaches from AbbVie, J&J in mid-stage trials [#A]
- Sub: If the market evolves to a 4-5 player oligopoly with similar mechanisms, pricing power of individual agents may erode; analogous to the PCSK9 inhibitor market (duopoly became price-competitive within 3 years of entry)

Competitive intensity: Currently moderate-high and rising — Leqembi and Kisunla are direct competitors; Lilly's marketing investment is substantially higher (Lilly has a large CNS/neuroscience commercial organisation vs Eisai's smaller US presence) [#A]
- Sub: Head-to-head data between Leqembi and donanemab does not exist; clinicians selecting between the two agents consider dosing convenience (donanemab is monthly until plaques clear, then discontinued; Leqembi is biweekly initially, bimonthly maintenance), ARIA risk profile, and price
- Sub: Leqembi's biweekly infusion burden is a structural disadvantage vs donanemab's monthly dosing; the subcutaneous formulation (pending approval) would partially address this

New entrant — pipeline threats: Roche's remternetug targets both amyloid and tau; Phase 3 data expected 2026-27 — if efficacy is superior, it could take share from both Leqembi and donanemab [#A]
- Sub: Oral anti-amyloid small molecules (no clinical-stage candidate with strong Phase 3 data as of 2025) represent a longer-term but potentially disruptive substitute
- Sub: Gene therapy approaches (AAV-based, targeting APOE4) are early-stage but could theoretically prevent amyloid accumulation upstream of any antibody mechanism

Market size: Global AD drug market estimated at USD 10-15bn by 2030 (sell-side consensus range; [AS-Guggenheim, AS-Jefferies]) driven by disease-modifying therapies; addressable population for anti-amyloid antibodies estimated at 6-10m patients globally in eligible (early) stage [#A]
- Sub: Current penetration is 16,000 cumulative global starts (Eisai data) vs 6-10m eligible — implying <0.3% penetration; even 5% penetration at current pricing would imply >USD 3-5bn peak sales
- Sub: The gap between theoretical TAM and actual uptake reflects: infusion centre capacity constraints, diagnostic infrastructure requirements (amyloid PET/CSF testing needed for patient selection), physician risk aversion to ARIA, and payer/reimbursement friction

Macro shift — reimbursement: US CMS expanded Medicare coverage for Leqembi under Traditional Medicare in Jul-2023 (concurrent with traditional approval) — this was the key enabling event removing the largest payer barrier; but IRA drug pricing negotiations and potential biosimilar pathways remain long-term headwinds [#A]
- Sub: EU reimbursement is proceeding market-by-market and slowly — Germany (AMNOG process), France , and Nordic markets all have different timelines; Berenberg and SEB both note EU royalty contribution remains negligible in 2025 estimates
- Sub: Japan pricing set in Feb-2024 at ~¥298,000/month (approx USD 2,000/month) — below US list price of ~USD 26,500/year; Japanese reimbursement expected to be a modest contributor

Non-macro shift — diagnostics infrastructure: Amyloid blood tests (plasma p-tau217 assays — Fujirebio, C2N Diagnostics Precivity) received FDA clearance in 2024 — this is a potential major enabler of patient identification at scale without PET requirement [#A]
- Sub: [AS-Carnegie] estimates blood-based biomarker adoption could accelerate eligible-patient diagnosis by 2-3x vs current PET-dependent pathway; if true, this is the most important near-term demand-side catalyst
- Sub: This shift from PET/CSF to blood test would structurally lower barriers for primary care physicians to identify and refer patients — transformative for market penetration if it plays out

Regulatory shift: FDA's approval of the lecanemab subcutaneous formulation is pending (BLA submitted by Eisai, PDUFA likely late 2025/early 2026) — approval would significantly reduce infusion burden and could re-accelerate uptake [#A]
- Sub: Regulatory risk: if FDA requires an additional ARIA monitoring REMS requirement for subcutaneous (due to different absorption kinetics), the convenience advantage may be partially offset

Competitor attitudes: Eli Lilly is competing aggressively — large DTC advertising spend, significant neurologist detailing, competitive pricing discussions with payers; Eisai is resource-constrained relative to Lilly in US commercial execution [#A]
- Sub: Lilly's strategy appears to prioritise volume over ROCE in the near term (heavy promotional investment) — consistent with a large-pharma "land-grab" mentality in a nascent but potentially large market; this is neutral to positive for market expansion but negative for Leqembi's market share

Sceptical bullet (§3): The structural tailwind (large unmet need, reimbursement access, blood-test diagnostics) is real but "market expansion" and "market share" are different questions — Lilly's superior commercial muscle in the US may mean that even if total market grows, Leqembi's share within it shrinks; the PCSK9 analogy (Repatha vs Praluent) where the second entrant (Lilly/donanemab as first-mover in the "convenient monthly dosing" framing) captures an outsized share is a plausible bear case that is underweighted by current sell-side models [#J]

§4 — BB#2 Foundation Quality

J-front verdict: BioArctic's foundation quality is mixed — operator and scientific culture are strong, IP-based moat is defensible but narrow, economic capture from the moat is constrained by partnership structure, and industry structure is moving from duopoly to oligopoly which is net-negative for pricing power [#J]

§4.1 — Operator (BB#2 TC1: Great Operator)

Operator: Gunilla Osswald (CEO since 2017, PhD pharmacology/neuroscience) is scientifically credentialled and has executed well on the key 2017-2023 milestones — Phase 3 completion, FDA approval, partner management with Eisai [#A]
- Sub: Osswald is not an "animal CEO" archetype (aggressive capital allocator, M&A-driven growth) — she is a science-led operator focused on pipeline development and partner relationship management; appropriate for the current stage but potentially a constraint as the company aspires to become a commercial entity
- Sub: No evidence of large bold strategic pivots or unorthodox operator behaviour; execution has been steady and reliable rather than aggressive

Strategic ambition: BioArctic's stated ambition is to become a fully integrated CNS company over the 5-10 year horizon — building a pipeline beyond lecanemab while leveraging the Eisai relationship [#A]
- Sub: At the 2024 CMD, management outlined three pipeline pillars: (1) lecanemab lifecycle management, (2) next-gen anti-protofibril antibodies, (3) non-amyloid CNS targets (TDP-43, GABA-A); this is a coherent scientific strategy
- Sub: Strategy congruence is moderate — the ambition to be integrated is genuine but requires capability-building (commercial, manufacturing) that BioArctic currently lacks; the 80-100 FTE organisation cannot execute integration without significant investment

CEO + leadership team: Founding chairman Lars Lannfelt (A-shareholder) provides scientific continuity; CFO Anders Martin-Löf joined from AstraZeneca background — adds big-pharma financial discipline [#A]
- Sub: No evidence of a high-velocity hiring/resourcing strategy; the company appears to be growing scientific headcount modestly and deliberately
- Sub: Culture appears collaborative/academic — the Uppsala University origins are visible in publication-heavy culture; this is appropriate for a discovery-stage organisation but may need to evolve for commercial aspirations

Guidance posture: Management has been consistently conservative on revenue forecasts — not quantitative guidance on royalties given the Eisai dependency; they have explicitly avoided giving specific royalty income forecasts, citing Eisai's control over commercialisation [#A]
- Sub: This communication approach is frustrating for analysts (ABG Sundal Collier and Nordea both note the lack of granular royalty guidance in their research) but is intellectually honest given information asymmetry
- Sub: Pipeline milestones have generally been delivered on time (Phase 3 CLARITY AD readout, BLA submission, EU MAA submission) — suggests operational reliability on the R&D execution side

Track record + behaviour: The single most important track record event is the CLARITY AD Phase 3 result — delivering a clean, statistically robust, clinically meaningful outcome after 20+ years of Alzheimer's drug development failures [#A]
- Sub: BioArctic deployed R&D capital at essentially 100% reinvestment rate into lecanemab through 2022 — the high-RONIC capital allocation decision (even though unrecognised until the Phase 3 result) is a positive indicator of research prioritisation
- Sub: No share buybacks, no excessive executive compensation reported in proxy materials; governance is founder-controlled which creates concentration risk but also long-termism

Shareholder alignment: Founders hold A-shares with superior voting rights — creates governance concentration but also aligns long-term interests vs quarterly pressures; Lannfelt family own ~15-20% of economic value and substantially more voting control [#A]
- Sub: Institutional shareholders include Swedish pension funds (AP1-AP4) and European biotech specialists — this is a credible institutional base for a company of this size

Governance: Dual-class share structure (A/B) means external shareholders have limited ability to influence strategic direction — this is common in Nordic tech/biotech founding structures (Spotify, Hexagon) but limits accountability mechanisms [#A]

§4.2 — Advantaged Business + SRCA (BB#2 TC2)

Customer moat (RA4): Patient stickiness once on Leqembi therapy is real — amyloid re-accumulation after discontinuation, established infusion centre relationships, and physician familiarity create a modest switching cost; however this is a young market with limited data on switching rates [#A]
- Sub: The patient relationship is mediated by Eisai, not BioArctic — BioArctic's "customer moat" is at the IP/partner level (Eisai's contractual commitment) rather than at the end-patient level
- Sub: Eisai's commitment to the Leqembi franchise is strong (it is their largest CNS programme by far) — this creates a de facto lock-in at the partner level

Production moat (RA4): BioArctic does not manufacture; the production moat is entirely IP-based — composition-of-matter patents on lecanemab running to ~2034-2037, process patents on manufacturing, and method-of-use patents [#A]
- Sub: The protofibril selectivity of lecanemab (vs mature plaques) is a scientific differentiation claim backed by multiple peer-reviewed publications and validated in Phase 3 — this is a defensible production moat anchored in reproducible science
- Sub: However, the manufacturing of the antibody itself is handled by a CMO (contract manufacturer) under Eisai's direction — BioArctic has no manufacturing IP advantage

Scale moat (RA4): BioArctic has negligible scale advantage — it is an 80-100 FTE organisation; scale advantages in this model accrue to Eisai/Biogen (manufacturing scale, distribution, commercial infrastructure) not to BioArctic [#A]
- Sub: This is a structural weakness vs integrated pharma competitors — BioArctic cannot leverage scale to lower unit costs, improve margins, or reinvest at higher velocity

Regulatory moat (RA4): FDA traditional approval (Jul-2023), EU approval (Jan-2024), and Japan approval create regulatory moats of 5-10 years before potential biosimilar entry (biologics exclusivity + patent estate) [#A]
- Sub: The FDA required post-approval confirmatory trial (AHEAD 3-45) to be conducted — this adds a regulatory overhang but also creates a further data moat if AHEAD confirms efficacy in pre-clinical populations

TAM (RA4): Global AD population is ~55m; early-stage (MCI-to-mild-AD) addressable population for disease-modifying therapy is estimated at 6-10m; at current US pricing (~$26,500/year) this implies a USD 160-265bn global TAM if fully penetrated — the TAM is very large relative to current revenue [#A]
- Sub: Realistic penetration in 2030 scenario is 3-8% of eligible US patients per sell-side models ([AS-Guggenheim] models 5% penetration by 2030 for ~USD 1.5bn US peak sales for Leqembi); this still implies meaningful royalty growth from current levels
- Sub: TAM realism note: the gap between theoretical and realistic TAM is very large in AD because of diagnostic infrastructure, payer access, and physician risk-tolerance constraints — not all TAM is equally accessible

OPERATE (RA5) — high-level, ESA depth in Q8: BioArctic's SCU concept is unusual — it has no traditional "unit economics" in the conventional sense; the closest analogue is "royalty per patient year," which is USD 800-1,500 at current royalty rate [#A]
- Sub: Pricing power resides with Eisai/Biogen (US list price decisions) not BioArctic; BioArctic cannot unilaterally raise royalty income without Eisai's commercial success
- Sub: ESA depth — covered in Q8 for full OPERATE (SCU/RSCU/pricing power) analysis

GENERATE (RA6) — EPS growth: EPS is highly volatile and milestone-driven; FY2023 EPS was SEK ~35-40 (milestone year); FY2024E EPS is likely slightly negative to marginally positive (royalty income offset by R&D spend); steady-state EPS when royalties mature (FY2027-30) could be SEK 5-15 pa [#A]

§4.3 — Value Chain (BB#2 TC3)

Customer demand (RA9): Demand from end-patients and neurologists is real but constrained by supply-side factors (infusion capacity, diagnostics) rather than willingness-to-pay at the patient level (Medicare covers >75% of eligible US patients following Jul-2023 CMS coverage decision) [#A]
- Sub: Route to market runs: BioArctic IP → Eisai manufacturing + commercialisation → US infusion centres (specialty neurology clinics, memory care centres) → screened patients; BioArctic has no direct influence on any step beyond the IP layer
- Sub: The diagnostic-infrastructure bottleneck (amyloid PET scans cost ~$3,000-5,000 and are not universally available; neurologist capacity for diagnosis and monitoring is limited) is the primary demand-side friction — a supply-of-physicians problem, not a demand-from-patients problem

Existing competition (RA10): Eli Lilly's donanemab (Kisunla) is the direct competitor — approved Jul-2024 for early symptomatic AD; the key differentiators vs Leqembi are: (1) monthly vs biweekly dosing, (2) treatment completion (once amyloid cleared, discontinuation possible), (3) slightly higher ARIA rates but similar cognitive outcomes [#A]
- Sub: Early market share data (per Eisai/Lilly quarterly disclosures) suggests Leqembi and Kisunla are roughly splitting new patient starts in the US, with slight Lilly advantage in academic centres; [AS-Bryan Garnier] notes Lilly's larger neuroscience salesforce as a structural commercial advantage

Adjacencies entering (RA10): No direct adjacency threat in the near term — the mechanism (anti-amyloid IV antibody) is not easily substituted by an adjacent drug class; tau-targeting antibodies (JNJ, AbbVie pipeline) represent the most plausible adjacency but are 3-5 years from approval [#A]

Disruptive substitutes: Oral small molecules targeting amyloid (e.g., BACE inhibitors failed historically but new approaches are in discovery); gene therapy; nucleotide therapeutics — all early-stage; the most credible near-term "disruption" is the oral route-of-administration advantage that none of the current antibodies have [#A]
- Sub: If an oral anti-amyloid agent reached Phase 3 with comparable efficacy, it would threaten both Leqembi and donanemab; likelihood of this in the 5-year investment horizon is low but not negligible

Regulatory SWOT (RA12): Regulatory environment is broadly positive — FDA's traditional approval (not accelerated) removed the most significant reimbursement barrier; but IRA provisions could subject Leqembi to drug price negotiation from 2027+ (9-year window for biologics from approval date) [#A]
- Sub: IRA pricing negotiation risk: if CMS negotiates a lower net price for Leqembi under IRA, the gross-to-net spread narrows and royalty income (which is typically on net sales) could be impacted — this is a medium-term risk (2027+ horizon) not currently priced in most sell-side models

Supplier SWOT (RA13): BioArctic has no meaningful supplier power issues — it does not manufacture and has no raw material dependencies; Eisai and its CMOs handle supply chain [#I]
- Sub: ESA depth — covered in Q8 for full supplier SWOT analysis
