3. PART A — FAILURE NARRATIVES

Scenario 1 — Sovereign FX Shock / Hyperinflation Cascade (Revenue Risk)

Narrative: A simultaneous FX devaluation across two or more of HTWS's core African markets (Tanzania, Ghana, DRC, South Africa) erodes USD-equivalent revenue. While contracts are often USD- or CPI-linked, local-currency billing creates a timing lag; MNO counterparties resist escalation clauses in stress environments, producing effective rent resets downward. Transmission: FX depreciation → local-currency MNO revenue pressure → MNO pushback on USD escalators → HTWS USD revenue misses → covenant headroom compression.

Early warning signals:
- Tanzanian shilling / Ghanaian cedi / South African rand 90-day trend vs USD — sustained >10% depreciation
- Central bank forex reserve levels in HTWS's top-3 markets falling below 3-month import cover
- MNO earnings calls flagging local-currency ARPU pressure or capex deferral in HTWS markets
- IMF Article IV consultation language hardening on fiscal sustainability in key sovereigns
- HTWS management commentary on escalation-clause enforcement shifting from "automatic" to "negotiated"

Plausibility: High plausibility — Ghana experienced severe currency stress 2022–2023 (cedi lost ~55% vs USD), and IHS Towers explicitly cited Nigeria/Egypt FX as a driver of EBITDA miss in that period; HTWS is structurally more diversified but not immune

M/M/O test:
- Means ✓ — African currency volatility is structurally embedded; multiple precedents
- Motive ✓ — MNOs have strong commercial incentive to renegotiate in stress
- Opportunity ✓ — contract escalation clauses have historically been renegotiated under sovereign pressure
- All three legs: FULL — not downgraded

Disconfirming evidence: Long-term USD-denominated master lease agreements with automatic CPI/USD indexation and strong judicial enforcement track record in HTWS operating markets; HTWS's Tanzania market has demonstrated relative stability vs West Africa peers; management's stated hedging and USD-billing architecture

Monitoring checklist:
- Monthly USD/TZS, USD/GHS, USD/ZAR spot rates and 12M forwards
- HTWS quarterly revenue reported in USD vs local-currency constant-exchange equivalent
- MNO counterparty (Vodacom, Airtel, MTN) Africa segment revenue growth in local vs USD terms
- Reported arrears / "past-due" receivables line in HTWS earnings releases
- Covenant headroom vs net leverage tests from semi-annual reporting

Interaction with other failure modes: Directly correlated with Scenario 3 (MNO financial stress) and Scenario 7 (leverage/covenant breach); acts as a trigger upstream of both; also amplifies Scenario 10 (false-friend EBITDA masking)

Disconfirming-evidence test: USD-indexed long-term contracts with automatic CPI indexation are the hard disconfirm — if enforcement is demonstrated consistently across a stress cycle, this scenario's plausibility falls materially

Scenario 2 — MNO Customer Concentration / Credit Deterioration (Revenue Risk)

Narrative: HTWS's revenue is heavily anchored to a small number of pan-African MNOs (Airtel Africa, Vodacom, MTN). If one anchor tenant faces balance-sheet stress — driven by competitive pressure, spectrum costs, or sovereign tax — it defers tower rent payments, renegotiates lease rates, or (extreme) enters administration/merger. Transmission: MNO credit event → delayed/reduced rent receipts → HTWS revenue and EBITDA miss → leverage metrics deteriorate.

Early warning signals:
- Airtel Africa / MTN / Vodacom net debt/EBITDA trends deteriorating beyond 2.5x
- MNO free cash flow yields turning negative in HTWS-relevant markets
- Airtel Africa in particular: parent Bharti's support signals; any commentary on African asset monetisation
- Regulatory spectrum auction outcomes increasing MNO capex obligations
- Credit rating actions on MNO counterparties (S&P, Moody's watch-negative)

Plausibility: Medium plausibility — Airtel Africa has historically operated with meaningful leverage and has been the subject of periodic balance-sheet concern; full credit event is low-probability but partial deferral/renegotiation is medium-probability within 3-year window

M/M/O test:
- Means ✓ — MNO sector in Africa has limited FCF headroom; leverage is real
- Motive ✓ — cost reduction is structurally motivated for MNOs under competitive pressure
- Opportunity: partial ✗ — HTWS contracts have minimum payment obligations and termination penalties that constrain MNO flexibility in the short term
- Two-of-three: WATCH ONLY — downgraded from full

Disconfirming evidence: Long-term (typically 10–15 year) contracted minimum lease commitments; MNO industry in Africa is oligopolistic with pricing power; data demand secular growth underpins MNO willingness to maintain tower payments; Airtel Africa has been reducing leverage post-African tower asset monetisation

Monitoring checklist:
- Airtel Africa, MTN, Vodacom semi-annual results: Africa EBITDA margins and net debt
- HTWS disclosed counterparty concentration (top-3 customers as % of revenue)
- Any disclosed arrears or receivable ageing deterioration in HTWS accounts
- MNO spectrum auction announcements in Tanzania, Ghana, DRC
- Rating agency actions on Airtel Africa or MTN Group

Interaction: Correlated with Scenario 1 (FX stress pushes MNO into arrears); partially correlated with Scenario 7 (covenant); null interaction with Scenario 5 (cost inflation is independent of MNO credit)

Disconfirming-evidence test: Minimum revenue commitments written into lease agreements with penalty clauses are the structural disconfirm — the question is whether courts in relevant jurisdictions have enforced these; Tanzania track record is the key test case

Scenario 3 — Tenancy Growth Narrative Breaks (Revenue Risk)

Narrative: HTWS's equity story is predicated on rising tenancy ratios (co-location growth) driving operating leverage on a largely fixed-cost asset base. If MNO network investment slows — due to capex fatigue, spectrum scarcity, or competitive rationalisation — incremental tenancy additions disappoint, and the operating-leverage flywheel stalls. Transmission: tenancy ratio plateau → revenue growth deceleration → EBITDA misses consensus → multiple de-rating.

Early warning signals:
- Quarterly tenancy additions falling below trailing-four-quarter average for two consecutive periods
- HTWS management guidance language on "pipeline" shifting from specific to vague
- MNO network rollout plans disclosed in annual reports showing ≥20% reduction in planned tower-related capex
- Industry data on African subscriber growth rate decelerating to <5% p.a.
- New tower build orders (a leading indicator of future co-location demand) falling YoY

Plausibility: Medium plausibility — African mobile data penetration remains structurally underpenetrated vs global peers, supporting long-run demand; but near-term tenancy additions have been lumpy and management has a history of guiding conservatively; consensus may be embedding an optimistic tenancy trajectory

M/M/O test:
- Means ✓ — MNO capex cycles are documented; underspend periods follow dense capex years
- Motive: partial ✗ — MNOs benefit commercially from densification; limited motive to permanently reduce
- Opportunity ✓ — capex deferral is a common MNO balance sheet management tool
- Two-of-three: WATCH ONLY

Disconfirming evidence: 4G/5G rollout mandates in HTWS markets tied to spectrum licence conditions; documented underpenetration of mobile broadband vs global benchmarks creates structural pull for continued densification; HTWS management's stated contracted pipeline

Monitoring checklist:
- Quarterly tenancy ratio (sites × average tenants/site) disclosed in HTWS earnings
- HTWS "committed tenancy" pipeline disclosure
- GSMA African mobile broadband penetration data (annual)
- MNO capex guidance for Africa-specific segments
- HTWS new site builds vs co-locations on existing sites ratio

Interaction: Sequential with Scenario 8 (operating leverage failure directly follows tenancy plateau); interacts with Scenario 1 if FX stress suppresses MNO investment appetite

Disconfirming-evidence test: Disclosed contracted-but-not-yet-installed tenancy backlog would be strong disconfirm; if HTWS shows 12–18 month forward visibility on tenancy additions, near-term narrative break becomes low plausibility

Scenario 4 — Input Cost Inflation / Energy Cost Spiral (Margin Risk)

Narrative: Tower operations are energy-intensive; African markets require diesel backup for grid-unreliable locations. A sustained spike in diesel/fuel costs — driven by global oil markets, local subsidy removal, or currency depreciation — compresses HTWS's direct operating margins. Even where fuel costs are partially pass-through under contracts, there is typically a lag and a cap. Transmission: energy cost increase → site-level EBITDA margin compression → group EBITDA miss → leverage ratio deteriorates.

Early warning signals:
- Brent crude sustained above $95/bbl for ≥3 months
- Local fuel subsidy removal announcements in Tanzania or Ghana
- HTWS disclosed "power and fuel" cost line as % of revenue rising ≥200bps YoY
- Management commentary on pass-through coverage shifting from "substantially covered" to "partially covered"
- Peer (IHS Towers, Eaton Towers) margin disclosures deteriorating on energy line

Plausibility: Medium plausibility — HTWS has invested materially in solar and hybrid energy solutions to structurally reduce diesel exposure; however, grid-unreliable markets remain a persistent risk; the 2022 energy price spike demonstrated the earnings sensitivity across the towerco sector

M/M/O test:
- Means ✓ — global energy markets are volatile; this is an external force
- Motive: N/A (macro driver — M/M/O slightly misfit for commodity scenarios)
- Opportunity ✓ — energy cost pass-through contracts have documented lag and cap limitations
- Partial fit — WATCH ONLY for M/M/O; treated as medium plausibility on base-rate grounds

Disconfirming evidence: HTWS's documented green energy programme (solar, battery storage) reducing diesel dependency; contractual pass-through provisions covering energy cost escalation; management stated target of majority-renewable energy across portfolio

Monitoring checklist:
- Brent crude 12-month forward curve
- HTWS semi-annual: power/fuel cost as % of revenue
- HTWS green energy coverage ratio (% sites with solar/hybrid — disclosed in sustainability reporting)
- Local fuel prices in Tanzania, Ghana (pump price indices)
- HTWS EBITDA margin by segment (looking for market-specific compression)

Interaction: Correlated with Scenario 1 (currency depreciation amplifies local fuel cost in USD terms); amplifies Scenario 7 (margin compression tightens covenant headroom); partially correlated with Scenario 6 (if capex deferred to manage costs, green energy rollout slows)

Disconfirming-evidence test: If HTWS's disclosed green energy programme has reached ≥70% site coverage with demonstrated cost reduction, this scenario's margin-impact severity falls substantially

Scenario 5 — Operating Leverage Failure / Fixed-Cost Creep (Margin Risk)

Narrative: The HTWS investment thesis is partly a leveraged-operating-model story — as revenue scales, fixed costs should be leveraged across a larger base, expanding EBITDA margins. If revenue growth disappoints (via Scenarios 1-3) AND operating cost base is sticky (headcount, site maintenance, ground lease inflation), the operating leverage works in reverse. Transmission: revenue deceleration + sticky fixed costs → margin contraction → EBITDA miss → consensus downgrade cycle → multiple compression.

Early warning signals:
- EBITDA margin flat or declining for two consecutive half-year periods despite revenue growth >5%
- Ground lease cost escalation (a key fixed-cost component) exceeding contractual CPI caps
- Headcount growth rate exceeding revenue growth rate for >2 quarters
- General and administrative costs as % of revenue inflecting upward after multi-year decline
- HTWS management shifting from "margin expansion trajectory" language to "margin stability" framing

Plausibility: Medium plausibility — ground lease cost inflation in African markets is a documented risk; HTWS has some fixed-price ground lease agreements but also has escalation exposure; the operating-leverage story requires continuous tenancy growth to materialise

M/M/O test:
- Means ✓ — fixed cost structures are inherent to towerco models
- Motive ✓ — landowners, employees, and maintenance contractors all seek inflation-linked increases
- Opportunity ✓ — contract renewal periods create re-pricing moments
- All three legs: FULL

Disconfirming evidence: HTWS's multi-year ground lease programme locking in long-term fixed-price agreements; demonstrated historical EBITDA margin improvement trajectory; fixed-cost leverage well understood by management team with explicit margin targets disclosed

Monitoring checklist:
- Semi-annual EBITDA margin by geography
- Ground lease / site rental cost as disclosed absolute and % of revenue
- Headcount disclosures (annual report or sustainability report)
- Revenue per tower vs cost per tower trend (if disclosed or calculable)
- Analyst consensus EBITDA margin estimates — watch for systematic cut cycle

Interaction: Directly amplified by Scenario 3 (tenancy plateau removes the revenue growth that makes fixed-cost leverage work); correlated with Scenario 8; partially correlated with Scenario 4 (energy cost inflation is a component of the fixed-cost base)

Disconfirming-evidence test: Multi-year ground lease cost disclosures showing fixed or below-CPI escalation would directly invalidate the cost-creep variant; long-term locked ground leases are the structural disconfirm

Scenario 6 — FALSE FRIEND: Capex Deferral Masking Margin Expansion (LOAD-BEARING)

Narrative: HTWS reports improving EBITDA margins and apparent FCF generation — the thesis appears to be confirming. However, the margin improvement is partly or wholly driven by deferrals of maintenance capex, green-energy conversion capex, or ground lease prepayments, rather than genuine operating leverage. Surface metrics confirm the thesis; underlying asset quality is quietly deteriorating, creating a future capex liability that will suppress FCF when it crystallises. Transmission: capex deferral → near-term EBITDA/FCF appears strong → analyst/investor confidence rises → actual capex catch-up wave arrives → FCF misses → thesis re-rates sharply.

Early warning signals:
- Maintenance capex as % of revenue declining YoY while asset age increases
- HTWS site churn or "decommissioning" rate increasing (aging assets being abandoned rather than maintained)
- Management language shifting from "investment" to "efficiency" framing for capex
- Third-party tower inspection reports or operator complaints about site quality
- Green energy rollout pace slowing despite stated targets (capex being deferred)

Plausibility: High plausibility as a false-friend dynamic — the structural pressure to show FCF improvement at this stage of HTWS's lifecycle is high; investors are demanding FCF positive proof; management incentive structure may reward near-term metrics; peer precedent (cell tower operators globally have periodically deferred maintenance in FCF pressure periods)

M/M/O test:
- Means ✓ — management controls capex categorisation and timing
- Motive ✓ — FCF demonstration is the current key investor demand; management incentives aligned to FCF milestones
- Opportunity ✓ — maintenance capex is discretionary in the short run without immediately visible P&L impact
- All three legs: FULL — elevated concern

Disconfirming evidence: Auditor scrutiny of capex vs opex classification; covenant packages requiring maintenance capex minimums; disclosed capex intensity stable or increasing in line with tower count growth; independent sustainability/ESG audit of green energy programme progress

Monitoring checklist:
- Capex-to-revenue ratio trend (maintenance vs growth capex split, if disclosed)
- Net PP&E growth rate vs tower count growth rate (divergence = potential deferral signal)
- Average tower age disclosed or calculable from asset register disclosures
- Auditor's note on useful economic lives and impairment testing assumptions
- Peer (SBA Communications, American Tower) maintenance capex intensity as cross-check

Interaction: ⚡ This is the most dangerous interaction scenario — it correlates with Scenario 10 (EBITDA beat masking) and can coexist with Scenarios 1-3 rather than excluding them; false-friend scenarios are additive to, not instead of, direct bear scenarios

Disconfirming-evidence test: If total capex intensity (maintenance + growth) has remained stable or increased as % of asset base over the past 3 years, the deferral hypothesis is weakened; the hard disconfirm is audited maintenance capex per tower staying flat in real terms

Scenario 7 — Leverage / Covenant Breach (Margin/Execution Risk)

Narrative: HTWS carries substantial net debt (historically 5-7x EBITDA range); the investment grade of this leverage depends on stable EBITDA generation and USD cash flows. If EBITDA misses (via any combination of Scenarios 1-5) or FX moves materially, net leverage ratios breach covenant thresholds or rating-agency upgrade assumptions, triggering a refinancing at higher cost or equity raise at depressed prices. Transmission: EBITDA miss or FX shock → leverage ratio breach → lender renegotiation / refinancing at higher cost → interest burden rises → EPS and FCF materially miss → equity de-rates.

Early warning signals:
- Net debt/EBITDA approaching or above stated covenant threshold (typically ~6x for HTWS structures)
- Refinancing maturities clustering within 18 months without refinancing announced
- Rating agency (S&P, Moody's) negative watch or outlook change
- Bond yield spread vs investment-grade comparators widening >150bps
- Management shift in capital allocation language — increased emphasis on "debt reduction" over "growth"

Plausibility: Medium plausibility — HTWS has historically operated close to leverage ceilings; the company's credit story has improved but is not bulletproof; a combined FX + EBITDA miss shock could push leverage to uncomfortable levels; the 2022–2023 period demonstrated how quickly towerco leverage can become a market concern

M/M/O test:
- Means ✓ — leverage is structurally embedded; covenant breach risk is real
- Motive: N/A for external credit markets — but creditors have incentive to protect their position
- Opportunity ✓ — covenant renegotiation typically occurs at refinancing or annual compliance testing
- Structural risk — full concern; M/M/O partially applicable

Disconfirming evidence: HTWS's stated net leverage target and demonstrated trajectory toward target; long-dated maturity profile (if refinancing is not near-term); EBITDA growth trajectory providing headroom improvement; management's explicit focus on leverage reduction as stated strategic priority

Monitoring checklist:
- Net debt/EBITDA as reported each half-year
- Debt maturity schedule (from annual report)
- Bond/loan spreads on HTWS's outstanding instruments
- Hedging ratios on USD debt vs USD revenue (currency mismatch risk)
- Rating agency reports and any watch/outlook changes

Interaction: This is the fulcrum scenario — almost every other failure mode in this pre-mortem feeds into leverage deterioration; Scenarios 1, 2, 4, and 6 all amplify leverage risk; it is both a standalone risk and a cascade amplifier

Disconfirming-evidence test: A disclosed leverage ratio comfortably below covenant threshold (>1.5x headroom) with a long-dated maturity profile would substantially reduce acute breach risk — the disconfirm is the covenant headroom quantum

Scenario 8 — Key Executive Departure / Management Disruption (Execution Risk)

Narrative: HTWS is a relatively small-cap company (~$1bn market cap range) where the CEO and senior management team carry significant institutional knowledge about complex multi-jurisdictional African operating environments, regulatory relationships, and customer relationships. An unexpected departure of the CEO or CFO — particularly to a competitor or via a conflict with the board — removes key execution capability at a critical stage of the FCF inflection story. Transmission: executive departure → strategy uncertainty → potential capital allocation shift → execution delays in tenancy ramp → investor confidence erosion → multiple de-rating.

Early warning signals:
- Senior management equity award vesting schedule cliff (common departure trigger)
- Board composition changes or disclosed shareholder-board tensions
- CEO/CFO compensation structure change (shift from long-term to short-term weighting)
- Glassdoor / LinkedIn signals of senior-level departures in HTWS's management tier
- Activist shareholder accumulation or disclosed shareholder engagement escalation

Plausibility: Low plausibility as an imminent risk, medium plausibility over 3-year window — CEO turnover in small/mid-cap telecoms infrastructure has a non-trivial base rate; HTWS has demonstrated management stability but the company is at an FCF inflection stage where management expectations from investors are high; miss vs high expectations increases departure risk

M/M/O test:
- Means ✓ — executives can leave; no structural lock-in
- Motive: partial ✓ — at FCF inflection, if performance disappoints, board pressure increases; also competing offers from larger towercos
- Opportunity ✓ — post-vesting windows are departure opportunities
- All three legs: conditional — FULL if FCF disappoints

Disconfirming evidence: Multi-year performance share plan with unvested tranches creating golden handcuffs; CEO equity ownership alignment (skin in the game); track record of management team tenure stability; stated board satisfaction with current strategy execution

Monitoring checklist:

- Board composition changes between AGM proxy statements
- Any HTWS disclosed "listening to shareholder feedback" language (activist signal)
- Senior management changes in RNS/stock exchange announcements
- External search mandates for C-suite roles (occasionally visible in headhunter/LinkedIn signals)

Interaction: Partially correlated with Scenario 9 (M&A integration failure — bad acquisition accelerates board/CEO conflict); null interaction with macro scenarios (Scenarios 1, 4); sequential with Scenario 3 if tenancy disappointment triggers performance review

Disconfirming-evidence test: Large unvested equity stake held by CEO/CFO with 3+ year vesting horizon would substantially reduce near-term departure risk — the disconfirm is disclosed unvested award quantum and schedule

Scenario 9 — M&A Integration Failure / Capital Allocation Error (Execution Risk)

Narrative: HTWS has a stated growth strategy that includes tower portfolio acquisitions in new or existing African markets. A poorly priced or poorly integrated acquisition — overpaying for a portfolio with embedded contract or asset quality problems, or entering a new market with regulatory complexity — could destroy capital and divert management attention. Transmission: overpaid acquisition → goodwill impairment risk → integration opex burden → leverage increase → FCF dilution → thesis re-rates.

Early warning signals:

- Post-acquisition EBITDA contribution below deal model within first 12 months (if visible)
- Integration headcount / restructuring charges materialising in H1 post-deal

Plausibility: Medium plausibility — HTWS has a track record of African market entries; however, the global towerco M&A market has been competitive and disciplined deal-doing is harder; the risk is higher in periods of management pressure to demonstrate growth

M/M/O test:

Scenario 10 — FALSE FRIEND: Earnings Beat from Unsustainable Contract Catch-Up (LOAD-BEARING)

Narrative: HTWS reports a strong earnings period with revenue and EBITDA beats. The bull thesis appears confirmed. However, the beats are driven by deferred contract escalations crystallising in a single period, one-time catch-up payments from an MNO arrears settlement, or a particularly favourable FX translation in the comparative period — none of which repeat. Analysts and investors anchor to the beat as a run-rate; guidance for forward periods is maintained or raised; the stock re-rates higher; the underlying business has not structurally improved. Transmission: non-recurring beats → consensus upgrade cycle → elevated multiple → subsequent period normalisation → miss vs elevated expectations → sharp de-rating from a higher multiple base.

Early warning signals:
- Management qualitative commentary describing a "catch-up" or "settlement" element in the period's results
- Revenue growth significantly exceeding tenancy ratio growth (non-tenancy revenue driving the beat)
- YoY comparison quarter had a known weak period (FX headwind base effect)
- Analyst estimates revision: if ≥3 brokers raise full-year estimates >5% after a single strong quarter, this is a red flag for extrapolation error
- FCF per share not following EBITDA per share higher (if the beat is non-cash or non-recurring, FCF won't follow)

Plausibility: High plausibility as a recurring dynamic in the towerco reporting cycle — periodic MNO settlements, escalation catch-ups, and FX base effects are structurally common in African towerco financials; this false-friend dynamic has caught investors in IHS Towers and American Tower Africa equivalents

M/M/O test:
- Means ✓ — IFRS/GAAP treatment of lease revenue and settlement income creates legitimate but non-recurring line items
- Motive ✓ — management and investors both want FCF proof; pressure to point to positive momentum is real
- Opportunity ✓ — reporting periods where a catch-up naturally crystallises provide the opportunity for narrative amplification
- All three legs: FULL — elevated concern; treat as load-bearing false-friend

Disconfirming evidence: Clean, consistent quarterly/half-year revenue growth with explicit management decomposition between recurring and non-recurring; FCF corroborating EBITDA trend without notable working capital swings; auditor sign-off on revenue recognition policy not changing period-on-period

Monitoring checklist:
- Reconciliation of reported revenue vs "organic" / "recurring" revenue in management commentary
- Working capital movement in cash flow statement (large inflows = potential catch-up payments)
- FCF vs EBITDA delta trend (FCF conversion ratio stability)
- Management explicit disclosure of any settlement or catch-up items
- Tenancy ratio growth vs revenue growth co-movement — persistent divergence is a red flag

Interaction: This is the primary FALSE FRIEND amplifier — it can coexist with any or all bear scenarios; it is most dangerous in combination with Scenario 6 (capex deferral + revenue catch-up = double false-friend in same period); interacts with Scenario 7 by temporarily masking leverage pressure

Disconfirming-evidence test: FCF conversion ratio (FCF/EBITDA) maintaining 40%+ consistently across 4+ periods would indicate the beat is structural; single-period FCF conversion outliers are the signal to scrutinise

Scenario 11 — Technology Disruption / Competitive Structural Risk

Narrative: Low-earth-orbit satellite connectivity (Starlink and successors) provides MNOs with an alternative to terrestrial tower infrastructure for low-density coverage areas, reducing the incremental tenancy demand for new sites and potentially enabling MNOs to decommission legacy rural sites. While urban densification remains tower-dependent, HTWS's rural coverage tower base faces a structural long-run headwind. Transmission: LEO satellite adoption → MNO reduced greenfield tower demand in rural areas → HTWS new site build slowdown → revenue growth rate structurally lower than historical → multiple de-rating on growth premium.

Early warning signals:
- Starlink or competitive LEO operator announcing commercial agreements with African MNOs as a wholesale backhaul/coverage layer
- HTWS disclosed rural site utilisation rates flattening or declining
- MNO investor day presentations reducing "tower count" targets in rural areas
- HTWS management commentary adding LEO as a "watch" or "competitive dynamic" for the first time
- New tower build rates by category (urban densification vs rural coverage) — rural deceleration is the early signal

Plausibility: Low plausibility within 3-year investment window; medium plausibility over 5-7 year horizon — LEO satellites face fundamental constraints (latency, capacity per beam, terminal cost) that limit displacement of terrestrial mobile infrastructure for dense urban and suburban markets; however, rural coverage in Africa is genuinely in the LEO competitive zone

M/M/O test:
- Means ✓ — LEO technology is commercially available and improving
- Motive ✓ — MNOs have capex efficiency motive for hybrid LEO + tower architectures
- Opportunity: partial ✗ — commercial deployment at scale in African rural markets is 3-5+ years away; near-term opportunity is limited
- Two-of-three: WATCH ONLY for investment horizon; flag for long-term monitoring

Disconfirming evidence: Structural density requirements for 4G/5G data delivery that LEO cannot replicate at scale; regulatory spectrum licensing structures that incentivise terrestrial coverage obligations; HTWS's portfolio concentration in urban/peri-urban corridors with highest tenancy ratios

Monitoring checklist:
- Starlink Africa commercial launch announcements and pricing
- MNO commentary on hybrid connectivity architecture in African market presentations
- HTWS rural vs urban/peri-urban tower count split (if disclosed)
- GSMA reports on non-terrestrial network adoption in Sub-Saharan Africa
- ITU/regulator pronouncements on spectrum allocation for NTN in HTWS markets

Interaction: Low direct interaction with other scenarios in the 18M–3Y window; long-run structural interaction with Scenario 3 (tenancy growth narrative); null interaction with financial/leverage scenarios in the near term

Disconfirming-evidence test: If HTWS's new site build programme is demonstrably urban-densification-led (low rural dependency), the LEO disruption scenario's impact on the near-term investment case falls to low plausibility

4. PART B — META-ANALYSIS

  • Net signal on scenario clustering: five of eleven scenarios (Scenarios 1, 2, 3, 4, 7) share the African macro / currency cluster as a common upstream driver — this is a high-density correlated cluster
  • Scenario cluster — African macro trigger: Scenarios 1, 2, 4 are first-order expressions; Scenario 7 (leverage) is the second-order cascade; if the macro cluster activates, these four scenarios fire approximately simultaneously
  • Scenario cluster — FCF demonstration risk: Scenarios 6 and 10 are the false-friend cluster — they are independent of macro conditions and can activate even in a benign environment; they are the most insidious cluster because they delay detection
  • Scenario cluster — Execution/management: Scenarios 8 and 9 are loosely correlated (management pressure from performance miss increases acquisition risk and departure risk simultaneously); they are downstream of the macro cluster
