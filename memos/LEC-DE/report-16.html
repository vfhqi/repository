§4 — Board Composition + Independence

Verdict: Structurally constrained independence — E.ON-nominee dominance means the Supervisory Board functions as an oversight layer, not a check on the controlling shareholder; adequate for a controlled subsidiary, inadequate for minority shareholder protection

  • Board size: Lechwerke operates under German codetermination law (Mitbestimmungsgesetz); for a company of its size (~2,000-3,000 employees), the Supervisory Board is typically 12-16 members split equally between shareholder representatives and employee representatives

  • Exact current size: most recent accessible disclosures indicate a 12-16 member Aufsichtsrat

  • Independent directors: In the German structure, "independent" must be understood in the codetermination context; shareholder-side seats are typically held by E.ON SE nominees (including senior E.ON executives) plus potentially a small number of nominally independent external members; Anglo-Saxon independence (non-executive, no material relationship) applies to perhaps 2-4 members at most

  • Key point: because E.ON holds ~89.9%, the shareholder-side Supervisory Board seats are controlled by E.ON; "independence" from the controlling shareholder is structurally very limited

  • Insider directors: E.ON executive or senior management nominees occupy multiple shareholder-side seats; this is expected and disclosed; it means the Supervisory Board cannot effectively challenge E.ON on intercompany terms, dividend extraction, or strategic direction

  • Independence ratio: Effective independence ratio (members with no material E.ON relationship) is estimated ≤25% of the full board; this is below median for comparable listed European utilities (sector median ~45-55% independent), acknowledging the controlled-subsidiary context makes direct comparison misleading

  • Director tenure pattern: Employee representatives are subject to Works Council election cycles (typically 4 years); shareholder-side nominees rotate with E.ON group career moves; no evidence of unusually long tenure creating capture risk beyond the structural E.ON control already embedded

  • Recent board changes L24M: No major restructuring of the Supervisory Board identified; composition changes consistent with normal E.ON group personnel rotations; no activist-driven or crisis-driven board changes

  • Audit committee composition: Lechwerke's Prüfungsausschuss (Audit Committee) includes shareholder-side and employee-side members; chaired by a shareholder-side nominee with finance background; KPMG has been Lechwerke's auditor (consistent with E.ON group audit framework); no auditor changes or material weaknesses identified

  • Comp committee composition: Personalausschuss handles executive compensation; dominated by shareholder-side (E.ON) nominees; compensation decisions are effectively set by E.ON's HR framework cascaded to subsidiaries; minority shareholder influence on CEO pay is nil

  • Nomination committee: Nominations are effectively controlled by E.ON's group HR/governance function; no independent nominations process

  • Director skills mix: Shareholder-side nominees bring energy sector, finance, and regulatory expertise; employee-side brings operational and works council perspective; legal and digital skills are present; no observable gap in required competencies for a regulated utility oversight function

  • Director equity ownership: E.ON SE owns the equity; individual Supervisory Board members do not hold Lechwerke shares directly (beyond any token holdings); no skin-in-the-game via equity for board members

  • Diversity profile: 50% employee representation (codetermination) is legislated; gender diversity on the Supervisory Board is partial — German large companies now target ≥30% women on supervisory boards; Lechwerke's specific current gender composition not confirmed precisely in available disclosures but is likely compliant with German legal minimums

§6 — Governance Structure + Red Flags

Verdict: Mixed — structurally concentrated but legally compliant; the dominant risk is not active misconduct but the structural minority protection limitation inherent in the controlled-subsidiary model; no acute red flags

  • Dual-class structure: Not applicable — Lechwerke AG is a standard Aktiengesellschaft; no dual-class shares or special voting rights; E.ON's control derives from economic ownership (~89.9%), not structural voting enhancement

  • Founder control: Not applicable — Lechwerke is a long-established regional utility with no founder; E.ON's control is that of an industrial majority shareholder, not a founder-entrepreneur

  • Anti-takeover provisions: The ~89.9% ownership by E.ON is the most effective anti-takeover provision possible; no squeeze-out threshold has been triggered (squeeze-out in Germany requires 95%+ for simplified Kapitalgesellschaft squeeze-out); the gap between 89.9% and 95% is observable and meaningful — squeeze-out risk is a live structural consideration for minority investors

  • Related-party transactions: This is the highest-risk governance area for minority shareholders

  • Lechwerke purchases services from and sells to E.ON group entities under intercompany agreements; these are disclosed in the Abhängigkeitsbericht (dependent company report) per §312 AktG, which requires the Vorstand to state that all transactions were on arm's-length terms or that disadvantages were compensated
  • The Abhängigkeitsbericht is audited by KPMG (the group auditor) and reviewed by the Supervisory Board; the structural conflict in having the group auditor audit the dependent company report is a governance concern that is standard practice in Germany but not comforting to minority investors

  • No specific related-party transaction identified as materially disadvantageous in available disclosures; the legal construct provides a floor but not a ceiling on minority protection

  • Audit history: KPMG is the statutory auditor; no auditor changes identified in the accessible record; no material weaknesses, audit qualifications, or emphasis of matter paragraphs identified; clean audit history

  • Restatement history: No restatements identified in available Lechwerke standalone accounts

  • Legal/regulatory issues: Lechwerke operates under BNetzA (Bundesnetzagentur) and Bayerische state regulatory oversight; regulatory compliance appears standard; no enforcement actions, significant fines, or regulatory censures identified in accessible record

  • Energy sector regulatory proceedings (e.g. cost-of-capital determinations in the ARegV framework) are ongoing structural elements of the business, not governance red flags

  • Class action / securities litigation: No class actions identified; Lechwerke's limited free float and narrow minority investor base make securities class actions unlikely in the US sense; no German Kapitalanleger-Musterverfahren identified

  • Whistleblower / governance complaint signals: No publicly accessible whistleblower-related events or significant governance complaints identified; E.ON group has a group-level whistleblower channel which applies to subsidiaries including Lechwerke

  • AGM / EGM history: AGM proceedings are not widely reported for subsidiaries at this level; no contested resolutions, significant withhold votes, or activist interventions identified

  • Squeeze-out optionality (⚠️ structural flag, not red flag): E.ON's 89.9% stake is structurally significant; if E.ON ever sought to squeeze out minorities, it would need to reach 95% first; the current gap means a squeeze-out offer (potentially at a premium or discount depending on structural approach) is a latent event risk that minority investors should model separately; this is a governance structure consideration, not a management-conduct red flag

  • Sceptical bullet — bearish read on governance: The governance architecture that would invalidate the operator-quality thesis is not a sudden scandal but a slow deterioration of minority position via intercompany mechanics: E.ON could extract value from Lechwerke through (a) increased intercompany service fees, (b) capex deferrals pushed to the subsidiary, (c) transfer of profitable energy services contracts to E.ON group entities, or (d) dividend extraction at the expense of reinvestment; the Abhängigkeitsbericht provides legal redress after the fact, not prevention; the single red flag that would trigger immediate reassessment is a revision to the Abhängigkeitsbericht that discloses disadvantageous transactions without full compensation — to date, no such revision has been identified
