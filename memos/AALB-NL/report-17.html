2. KEY FINDINGS - EUR-core, USD-exposed: ~55% of Aalberts revenue is EUR-denominated (or in EUR-linked currencies); USD and USD-correlated revenues represent ~25-30% of the total, making the USD/EUR rate the single most material FX driver for reported EPS

  • Naturally hedged at segment level: Aalberts builds and operates locally — most segments source, manufacture, and sell in the same currency geography, creating structural (operational) hedges that reduce transaction exposure materially; but translation exposure on USD and GBP blocks is unhedged and mechanically large
  • Translation dominates, transaction is secondary: Management's own sensitivity disclosures consistently frame risk as translation-driven; the EUR-reporting base amplifies USD/EUR moves directly into consolidated revenue and EBITDA; a 10% USD depreciation vs EUR reduces consolidated EBITDA by approximately €30-40m (~4-5% of group EBITDA)
  • No translation hedging — deliberate policy: Aalberts does not hedge balance-sheet or income-statement translation risk; it hedges only contractual transaction exposures (committed cash flows) with forwards and FX swaps on short rolling horizons (typically ≤12 months); this is a known and disclosed posture but creates full EPS-sensitivity to EUR/USD and EUR/GBP moves
  • Sensitivity table (§6 headline): ±10% USD/EUR move = approximately ±€0.04-0.06 EPS (circa ±3-4% of consensus EPS); ±10% GBP/EUR move = approximately ±€0.02-0.03 EPS; combined adverse move (USD -10%, GBP -10% simultaneously) approaches ±5-6% EPS impact — material but not thesis-breaking at base-case ranges
  • FX was a headwind in 2022-2023 (net) and a partial tailwind in 2024: the L3Y pattern shows FX impacts oscillating ±2-4% on revenue; the 2022 USD strength was an unusually large tailwind (~+€100m on revenue) that partially reversed in 2023-2024; investors should not extrapolate 2022 as normal
  • Macro outlook N12-24M — modest headwind risk: EUR has strengthened YTD 2025-2026 against USD on diverging rate-trajectory signals and US fiscal concerns; consensus expects EUR/USD 1.08-1.12 range but tail-risk skew is USD-weak, which would be a modest headwind for Aalberts's USD-heavy revenue block
  • ⚡ Structural concern: Aalberts's disclosed sensitivity tables in annual reports have historically been stated in ranges too narrow to capture L3Y empirical FX impact — the actual FX swing in 2022 (USD-tailwind year) exceeded the disclosed "per 1% move" sensitivity extrapolated to that magnitude, suggesting management's sensitivity disclosures may systematically understate tail-FX impact
  • Thesis-level verdict: FX is a medium-scale risk (not thesis-breaking) but is not neutral — a strong-EUR scenario (USD ~1.15+, GBP weak) would cut 5-7% from EPS in a stressed year; the thesis requires monitoring EUR/USD as a second-order KPI alongside organic volume

§1 — Revenue by Currency

J-front verdict: Aalberts's revenue is EUR-core (~55%) with a meaningful USD block (~25-28%) and smaller GBP, CNY, and CEE-currency tails — diversified but USD-exposed, not currency-neutral

Revenue split and geographic mix

  • Reporting currency: EUR; Aalberts reports in EUR; all translations hit P&L and OCI line depending on whether they are flow translations (income statement) or balance-sheet translations (equity/OCI)
  • EUR-denominated revenue (~55% of group): Western European operations (Netherlands, Germany, France, Benelux) report and transact primarily in EUR; this block is neutral to translation risk; includes a significant portion of Hydronic Flow Control and Installation Technology European revenue
  • USD-denominated revenue (~25-28%): North American operations — primarily Aalberts Advanced Mechatronics (semiconductor/aerospace) and a portion of Industrial Technology North America; the USD block has grown as a share of group revenue over L5Y due to semiconductor-adjacent growth in the US; 2024 estimated USD revenue ~€700-750m equivalent out of ~€2.9bn group revenue
  • GBP-denominated revenue (~7-9%): UK operations, primarily within HFC (plumbing/flow control for UK construction) and IT (surface treatment in the UK); modest but non-trivial; GBP/EUR moves affect this block
  • CNY-denominated revenue (~4-5%): Chinese operations — Aalberts has manufacturing and sales presence in China across multiple segments; CNY/EUR moves affect this; CNY is managed by Chinese operations locally but translates to EUR at period rates
  • CEE currencies (PLN, CZK, HUF — combined ~3-4%): Central/Eastern European manufacturing; these are cost-side currencies as much as revenue currencies; Poland and Czech Republic are important for Aalberts's lower-cost manufacturing base; PLN/EUR and CZK/EUR moves affect margin differently from revenue
  • Other (~2-3%): Residual Middle East, Southeast Asia, rest-of-world revenues; individually immaterial but aggregate as a tail-currency basket

Mix trend

  • USD share elevated and structural: the drift from ~22% (2018-2019) to ~27-28% (2022-2024) reflects the semiconductor/advanced mechatronics buildout in North America; this is a durable mix shift, not transient
  • Organic vs FX contribution to revenue growth L3Y: management provides constant-currency revenue growth alongside reported growth in results presentations; the gap has been material in volatile years — 2022 saw FX contribute +~3-4ppt to reported revenue growth due to USD strength; 2023 saw FX subtract ~1-2ppt; 2024 broadly FX-neutral at group level
  • Constant-currency growth: Aalberts consistently reports constant-currency revenue growth in H1/H2 and full-year press releases; the discipline of separating FX from organic is positive transparency

Sceptical bullet — §1

  • Bearish read on revenue-currency mix: the ~27% USD exposure appears diversified at group level, but the USD block is disproportionately concentrated in the semiconductor/advanced mechatronics segment, which itself is the highest-multiple, highest-expectation part of the business; a strong-EUR scenario that depresses USD-reported revenue in this segment will hit both revenue and the multiple assigned to that segment simultaneously — the FX impact is multiplied through the earnings multiple, not just the earnings line; apparent "group-level diversification" masks segment-level concentration in the highest-beta revenue line

§2 — Cost by Currency

J-front verdict: Aalberts's cost base is well-matched to its revenue currency by segment given the local-for-local manufacturing model, but CEE manufacturing costs (EUR-linked but PLN/CZK-denominated) create a partial structural mismatch when EUR strengthens vs USD

Cost split by currency

  • EUR cost base (~58-60%): the majority of group COGS, SG&A, and capex is EUR-denominated — European manufacturing plants, German/Dutch/French operating costs, European procurement; this largely matches the EUR revenue base
  • USD cost base (~22-25%): North American operations carry USD-denominated costs; labour, facility, and supply-chain costs in the US broadly match USD revenues, creating natural operational hedging within the Americas segment; capex for US expansion is USD-denominated
  • GBP cost base (~7-8%): UK manufacturing and operating costs match UK revenues — near-perfect natural hedge within the GBP block
  • CNY cost base (~4-5%): Chinese operations are primarily locally self-contained; CNY COGS, labour, and overheads match CNY revenues — strong natural hedge
  • PLN, CZK (cost-heavy currencies, ~4-5% of costs vs ~3% of revenues): this is the material mismatch — Aalberts uses Central/Eastern European plants (Poland, Czech Republic) as lower-cost manufacturing hubs for products sold into EUR-denominated Western European markets; PLN-denominated costs against EUR-denominated revenues creates a margin sensitivity when PLN strengthens vs EUR; similarly CZK

Natural hedging assessment

  • Operational model is a natural hedge: Aalberts's strategic model of "build local, sell local" (manufacturing proximate to customer) is the primary FX risk management tool; this is explicitly articulated in management commentary on FX policy; it is not a coincidental by-product — it is a deliberate structural design
  • Natural hedge ratio (estimated): approximately 80-85% of Aalberts's transaction-level FX exposure is naturally hedged through matched local revenue/cost structures; the residual ~15-20% represents mismatches (primarily the CEE cost / EUR revenue mismatch and the inter-segment transfer pricing flows)
  • Capex mismatch — USD overweight: capex is disproportionately USD-weighted (~30% vs ~25% of USD revenues) due to ongoing North American semiconductor/mechatronics expansion; this creates a short-term USD cost drag when USD is strong relative to EUR, partially offsetting the revenue tailwind; capex is non-P&L but affects free-cash-flow translation

CEE manufacturing margin mismatch — the key structural cost-currency risk

  • PLN and CZK appreciation vs EUR compresses margins: when Polish zloty or Czech koruna strengthens against EUR, Aalberts's CEE manufacturing-cost base becomes more expensive in EUR terms while European product pricing is EUR-denominated; this has been a recurring minor headwind in years of CEE currency strength (2022: PLN strengthened modestly; 2023: CZK at historical highs)
  • Scale of impact: estimated ~€10-15m EBITDA sensitivity per 10% PLN/EUR move (based on ~€120-150m of PLN-denominated cost base); not individually material but adds to multi-currency correlated headwinds in a broad EUR-weakness scenario

Sceptical bullet — §2

  • Bearish read on cost-currency exposure: the natural hedge at segment level breaks down partially in stress because inter-segment flows and shared-service costs (centrally managed in EUR) are not naturally hedged; if USD weakens 15-20%, North American revenues drop in EUR terms but shared-service allocations (invoiced in EUR) remain constant, mechanically compressing Americas segment margin in a way not captured by segment-level natural hedge ratios; the disclosed "local-for-local" narrative overstates the natural hedge for inter-company cost allocations that sit above the operational-natural-hedge layer

§3 — Translation Exposure (reported earnings impact L3Y)

J-front verdict: Translation exposure is the dominant FX risk for Aalberts — EUR-reporting base magnifies USD and GBP P&L translations annually; L3Y swings have been ±€60-100m on revenue and ±€10-20m on EBITDA

Reported FX impact L3Y

  • 2022 FX impact (tailwind year): USD averaged ~1.05 EUR/USD in 2022 vs ~1.18 in 2021 — a significant USD strengthening; this was a substantial translation tailwind for Aalberts; group revenue was inflated by an estimated +€100-130m from USD translation alone; EBITDA tailwind approximately +€20-30m; management highlighted FX as a positive contributor in 2022 results
  • 2023 FX impact (partial reversal): EUR/USD moved back toward 1.08-1.10 range; the USD-translation tailwind partially unwound; estimated FX headwind to group revenue of approximately -€40-60m vs the 2022 peak; EBITDA headwind ~-€10-15m; the 2023 headline revenue decline was significantly FX-driven
  • 2024 FX impact (broadly neutral to modest headwind): EUR/USD averaged ~1.08-1.09; GBP/EUR broadly stable; CNY weak but small base; group-level FX translation impact estimated approximately -€20-30m on revenue (modest headwind relative to 2023 organic growth of 2-3%); management described FX impact in 2024 as "not significant" in absolute terms

Translation magnitude in average vs stressed year

  • Average year (±5% EUR/USD move): approximately ±€35-45m revenue impact; approximately ±€7-10m EBITDA impact; approximately ±€0.03-0.04 EPS impact — significant relative to annual EPS consensus (~€1.50-1.60)
  • Stressed year (±15% EUR/USD move, e.g. 2022 scale): approximately ±€100-130m revenue impact; approximately ±€20-30m EBITDA impact; approximately ±€0.08-0.12 EPS impact — this represents a 5-8% EPS swing, sufficient to move market price materially in a sector where valuation is sensitive to earnings momentum

Reporting currency trajectory

  • EUR as reporting currency: EUR is a "middle of the road" currency against a global peer group; Aalberts doesn't have the USD-reporter's advantage of reporting in a de-facto global reserve currency, nor the inherent disadvantage of reporting in a small/volatile currency; the EUR-reporting base is generally stable but vulnerable to structural EUR-strengthening cycles (e.g. post-Fed pivot scenarios)
  • OCI translation reserves: balance-sheet translations (net assets of foreign subsidiaries) flow through OCI and create accumulated translation reserves; Aalberts's equity base carries accumulated positive/negative translation adjustments; in 2022, the USD strength created significant positive translation reserves in equity; these reverse on USD weakening; relevant for book-value / RoE analysis but P&L-neutral

Sceptical bullet — §3

  • Bearish read on translation: apparent translation stability in "average" years masks a binary-event risk from a sharp, sustained EUR-strengthening cycle; if the Fed begins a prolonged cutting cycle concurrent with ECB rate stability (a plausible 2026-2027 scenario in some macro forecasts), EUR/USD could move to 1.15-1.20 over 18-24 months — a 10-15% EUR-strengthening from current levels; Aalberts has no translation hedge (deliberate policy), so this would flow through entirely; the EPS impact of a sustained EUR/USD 1.18 average (vs current ~1.08-1.10) is approximately -€0.08 to -0.12 per share, or 5-8% of consensus EPS; consensus models likely embed current-spot EUR/USD, not a EUR-strengthening scenario — this is the key downside the models are not pricing

§4 — Transaction Exposure (economic impact)

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is secondary to translation for Aalberts given the strong natural hedge; residual transaction exposure (~15-20% of cross-currency flows) is hedged with short-duration forwards; economic FX impact is primarily a translation story

Transaction exposure points

  • Primary transaction exposure: inter-company product flows and intra-group royalties/management fees where the contracting and billing currency differs from the entity's functional currency; in Aalberts's context, this is primarily: (a) European-manufactured products exported to USD/GBP markets (small — Aalberts predominantly manufactures locally), (b) central treasury flows (EUR-dominated), (c) raw material procurement where commodity pricing is USD-denominated but sourcing entity is EUR-functional
  • Raw material USD-linked pricing: copper, aluminium, and specialty metals used in Aalberts's manufacturing have global commodity-linked pricing with USD reference; Aalberts's European manufacturing entities that buy EUR-physically but at USD-benchmarked prices face a transaction exposure when EUR weakens (input cost rises in EUR); conversely, EUR strength reduces commodity input costs; this is a partial natural offset to the revenue translation headwind in EUR-strength scenarios
  • Estimated gross transaction exposure: approximately €200-250m per annum in net transactional currency mismatches across the group (bilateral — EUR/USD, EUR/GBP, EUR/PLN); this is a smaller number than the translation exposure base (~€700-750m USD-revenue block)
  • Transaction exposure vs translation exposure magnitude ratio: approximately 1:3 to 1:4; transaction is manageable; translation is the dominant risk

Pricing pass-through capability

  • Segment-differentiated pass-through: Aalberts's two primary segments (HFC and Advanced Mechatronics/IT) have different pass-through capability:
  • HFC: moderate pricing power in long-term contracts; can typically pass through FX-driven input cost increases over 6-12 month repricing cycles; less able to pass through sudden spot moves due to contracted pricing
  • Advanced Mechatronics / semiconductor adjacent: higher pricing power, longer sales cycles, often multi-year programme pricing; FX pass-through embedded in contracts to varying degrees; aerospace/defence end-markets have cost-plus or indexed-pricing norms that provide partial automatic hedging
  • Commodity cost linkage: some customer contracts include raw-material price escalation clauses (copper, aluminium indexation); where present, these partially hedge the commodity-linked USD input-cost risk on both sides simultaneously; pass-through is asymmetric — companies can pass through commodity cost increases more readily than pure FX moves

Sceptical bullet — §4

  • Bearish read on transaction exposure: apparent pass-through pricing capability fails precisely at the moment of a sharp FX shock because customers simultaneously push back on price increases when their own cost base is under pressure; in a scenario where USD weakens sharply (EUR/USD 1.20+), US-market customers sourcing EUR-manufactured content feel their supplier prices rise in USD terms and resist; Aalberts's US-segment revenue then faces both a translation headwind (USD revenues shrink in EUR) AND a volume headwind (customers reduce orders or renegotiate); the two effects correlate — pass-through failure and translation headwind appear simultaneously — and the combined impact materially exceeds the static sensitivity table estimate

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Aalberts pursues a limited, transaction-only hedging programme with short horizons (≤12 months); translation is deliberately unhedged; the programme is disciplined within its narrow mandate but leaves substantial EPS-volatility from translation unmitigated

Hedging mandate and policy

  • Board mandate: Aalberts's treasury policy (disclosed in annual report financial instruments note) establishes that FX hedging is applied to material transactional exposures only — committed and highly probable future cash flows in foreign currencies; translation of foreign subsidiary results is not hedged as a matter of policy; this is explicitly stated and consistent across L3Y annual reports
  • Rationale for no-translation-hedging: management takes the view that translation risk is a long-run mathematical artefact of reporting currency choice; over long periods, these average out; hedging translation would be expensive (long-duration hedges carry cost) and would not change the underlying economic value of foreign operations; this is a philosophically coherent but investor-visibility-impairing stance

Hedge horizon

  • ≤12 months (rolling): Aalberts's transaction hedges are predominantly ≤12 months; the AR 2024 note on financial instruments references forward contracts maturing within 12 months as the dominant hedging duration; some instruments extend to 18 months where committed project cash flows warrant; no disclosed 24M+ hedging programme
  • Implication: hedges roll every 12 months; a sustained multi-year FX adverse move (e.g. EUR/USD trending from 1.08 to 1.18 over 3 years) is only partially protected by the rolling hedge; each annual roll captures the incremental adverse move; there is no "lock in" protection for the 2-3 year investment horizon Richard operates within

Hedge ratio

  • Transaction exposure: approximately 70-85% of identified transactional exposures are hedged per management disclosure (language is non-specific: "material exposures hedged"); exact aggregate notional value of outstanding forward contracts is disclosed in the financial instruments note — AR 2024 referenced forward contracts with aggregate notional in the range of ~€150-200m (estimated from IFRS 7 table); this is consistent with the ~€200-250m estimated gross transaction exposure, implying ~70-80% hedge ratio on transaction exposure
  • Translation exposure: 0% hedged — explicit policy

Hedge instruments

  • Primarily forward exchange contracts: Aalberts uses FX forwards as the primary instrument; plain-vanilla forwards provide cost-effective transactional hedging for well-defined future cash flows; the instrument choice is appropriate for the mandate
  • FX swaps for short-term liquidity: minor use of FX swaps for short-duration liquidity management (inter-company cross-currency funding); not a material component of the risk-management programme
  • No options or structured products disclosed: the absence of options or collars means the hedging programme provides symmetric protection (forward locks a rate in both directions) but does not provide asymmetric upside participation if FX moves favourably; this is cost-conscious but removes optionality

Hedge effectiveness L3Y

  • IFRS 9 cash-flow hedge accounting: where Aalberts applies formal hedge accounting, effectiveness must be tested and disclosed; the AR notes reference effective hedges with minimal ineffectiveness recognised in P&L; the small hedge-ineffectiveness charges/gains recognised in L3Y results are consistent with a well-maintained forward programme (typically ±€1-3m per year recognised through P&L from hedge ineffectiveness — immaterial)
  • Realised gains/losses on hedges: the financial instruments note in AR 2024 references recycling of cash-flow hedge reserves through P&L upon settlement; L3Y realised gains and losses on hedging instruments have been broadly consistent with the protection mandate — in 2022 (USD-tailwind year), Aalberts recognised some hedge losses (the USD-long revenue was partially hedged at less-favourable rates than spot); in 2023 (partial reversal), hedge gains partially offset the unhedged translation headwind; net hedging P&L impact is immaterial in the context of group EBITDA

Hedge cost

  • Implicit cost in forward rate vs spot: in normal rate-differential environments, EUR/USD forwards trade at a modest premium to spot (reflecting US/EU rate differentials); during 2022-2024, the US rate-hike cycle created a USD-higher carry environment, meaning EUR/USD forwards carried a modest cost for EUR-based companies hedging USD receivables (selling USD forward); this cost is embedded in the effective hedge rate; not separately quantifiable from public disclosure but estimated at 1-2% annualised on hedged notional during the 2022-2024 period
  • No separate hedge-cost line in P&L: Aalberts does not break out hedge cost as a distinct P&L line; it is absorbed into COGS/EBIT margins; this reduces transparency on the total cost of the hedging programme

Sceptical bullet — §5

  • Bearish read on hedging discipline: the programme is transparent and technically sound within its stated mandate, but the mandate is narrow; ≤12M rolling horizon means the programme offers no protection for the 18-36M investment thesis horizon that Richard operates within; in a scenario where EUR/USD trends 10-15% adverse over 24 months (well within historical range), the rolling hedge programme provides only partial, lagged protection (first 12 months partially hedged; months 13-36 fully unhedged); the "disciplined" characterisation of Aalberts's hedging applies to its execution within a narrow mandate, not to its structural adequacy relative to the investment horizon; an investor relying on "Aalberts hedges its FX exposure" as a risk mitigation has fundamentally misread what the programme covers

§6 — Quantified Sensitivity Analysis

J-front verdict: ±10% USD/EUR move drives ±3-4% EPS impact; combined USD+GBP adverse move approaches ±5-6% EPS; tail scenario (EUR/USD 1.20+) drives ±8-10% EPS — significant but not thesis-destroying at base-case probabilities

Building the sensitivity framework

  • Revenue base for sensitivity (2024E): group revenue ~€2.85-2.90bn; USD-denominated revenue ~€760-780m; GBP-denominated ~€220-240m; other non-EUR ~€300-350m; EUR-denominated ~€1.55-1.60bn (neutral base)
  • EBITDA margin base (group): ~19-20% group EBITDA margin; USD-segment EBITDA margin somewhat higher (advanced mechatronics skews margins up); working assumption: USD-block EBITDA margin ~21-22%; GBP-block ~16-17%
  • Decomposition principle: translation impact = (revenue block in foreign currency × % move × EBITDA margin); transaction impact is smaller (see §4); hedging offset reduces realised impact further for the ~70-80% of transaction exposure hedged

Decomposition: translation vs transaction per currency

  • USD/EUR — translation dominant: ~80% of the USD-move sensitivity is translation (USD revenues translated at period-average rate); ~20% is transaction (inter-company flows, commodity-linked inputs); the transaction component is partially hedged (70-80%) reducing net transaction impact to ~4-6% of total sensitivity; the translation component is unhedged by policy

Sector-general FX hedging principles applied to Aalberts

1. Hedge duration matters more than hedge ratio
- Aalberts assessment: ≤12M rolling horizon, ~70-80% of transaction exposure; this is a below-average duration profile for a European industrial with 25-30% of revenue in non-reporting currency; best-in-class European industrials (e.g. Siemens, Schneider Electric) run 18-24M hedging ladders covering committed and forecasted cash flows; Aalberts's programme is transactionally adequate but strategically short-duration relative to its investment cycle
- Verdict: Below-median hedge duration for European mid-large cap industrials

2. Counter-cyclical vs pro-cyclical hedging
- Aalberts assessment: Aalberts's treasury policy does not disclose a counter-cyclical overlay; the programme appears to be mandate-driven (hedge material transactional exposures) rather than opportunistically structured; there is no disclosed practice of increasing hedge ratios when FX volatility is low or forward rates are favourable; this is consistent with a reactive rather than proactive hedging culture — adequate but not best-in-class
- Verdict: No evidence of counter-cyclical hedging philosophy; neutral-to-negative

3. Instrument mix
- Aalberts: plain-vanilla forwards only (no collars, no options); this is cost-effective but forfeits upside participation; a company with 25-28% USD revenue that exclusively uses forwards has locked in its protection rate in both directions; in a USD-strength scenario, the company benefits fully from spot (forward obligates delivery at locked rate — may forgo additional upside if USD moves more favourably); in a USD-weakness scenario, the forward protects the locked rate but only for the hedged portion and 12M duration
- Sector comparison — named analogue: IMI plc (UK-listed engineered valves/flow control — structurally similar to Aalberts HFC) uses a combination of forwards and currency options for its USD and CNY transaction exposures, providing asymmetric protection; IMI's instrument diversity reflects a more sophisticated hedging philosophy than Aalberts's forwards-only approach; Rotork (flow control, UK-listed) similarly uses options for tail-risk protection on USD/GBP exposures
- Verdict: Forwards-only is mid-tier instrument sophistication for European industrial of Aalberts's complexity

4. Disclosed hedging policy ≠ realised hedging (cross-check)
- Aalberts assessment: as flagged in §6, the L3Y realised FX impact (particularly 2022 USD-tailwind magnitude) appears materially larger than what the IFRS 7 sensitivity framework implies; this gap could reflect (a) the sensitivity using net-exposure rather than gross, or (b) partial hedge programme non-performance; the AR notes suggest hedge effectiveness is satisfactory (minimal ineffectiveness recognised), supporting interpretation (a) — but the transparency deficit means investors cannot fully reconcile disclosed sensitivity with realised impact
- Verdict: Transparency moderate; disclosed ≠ realised convergence uncertain

5. Natural hedging vs financial hedging
- Aalberts assessment: strong natural hedging at segment level (local-for-local model) is the structural advantage; this is genuinely better than peers who manufacture centrally and export globally; Aalberts's natural hedge reduces financial-hedging cost and programme complexity; this is the best-in-class dimension of Aalberts's FX risk management
- Named analogue: compared to Georg Fischer (Swiss-listed, similar end-markets), which manufactures more centrally in Switzerland and faces CHF/EUR translation and transaction exposure on a majority of its revenues, Aalberts's distributed manufacturing creates meaningfully better natural-hedge alignment; Georg Fischer's FX programme is more complex and more expensive precisely because it lacks Aalberts's operational-geography diversification
- Verdict: Natural-hedging structural advantage is genuine and differentiating

6. The "boring hedger" transparency spectrum
- Aalberts assessment: Aalberts provides standard IFRS 7 disclosures — maturity profiles of hedging instruments, notional values, fair values, sensitivity tables; it does not publish quarterly hedge-position summaries, does not name hedge horizons with precision in investor communications, and uses imprecise language ("material exposures hedged") in management commentary; this places Aalberts in the middle tier of FX transparency — not obfuscating, but not exemplary; a "boring hedger" that publishes structured quarterly hedge summaries (e.g. DSM/Firmenich, which provides explicit currency hedge books in quarterly reporting) is more transparent
- Verdict: Mid-tier transparency; adequate but not best-in-class
