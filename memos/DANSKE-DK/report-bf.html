EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (BLUF)

Danske Bank operates a resilient universal banking model supported by its dominant Danish market share and structural IT cost takeouts under its Forward '28 strategy, though apparent cost efficiencies from AI and platform integration could be offset by severe retail customer erosion if competitive mortgage pricing pressures intensify. The bank demonstrates structural turnaround quality under CEO Carsten Egeriis, supported by resolved compliance liabilities and a highly optimized capital distribution framework. The Nordic banking industry exhibits high structural concentration, where pricing discipline is maintained by rigorous competitor ROE targets.

Business Model and Strategic Positioning

Danske Bank is Denmark's largest financial group, holding a market share above 25% in loans and deposits as of late 2025. Operating activities are concentrated across Denmark, Finland, Sweden, and Norway, alongside a leading regional position in Northern Ireland, with management completing the divestment of its Norwegian personal customer portfolio to Nordea in late 2024 to prioritize corporate clients.

Structural reporting is organised into Personal Customers, Business Customers, Large Corporates & Institutions, Danica Pension, and Northern Ireland.

Total income and pre-tax profits are distributed roughly evenly across the Personal, Business, and Large Corporates & Institutions divisions.

Core revenue is driven by Net Interest Income and Net Fee Income, alongside trading and net insurance results, with interest income delivering DKK 9,340 million in the first quarter of 2026 and fee revenue rising to DKK 3,918 million driven by everyday banking and asset management. Non-banking fees are growing through ancillary investment services and bancassurance penetration via the wholly-owned Danica Pension subsidiary.

Technology Transformation and AI Integration

Danske Bank aims to become a leading Nordic tech bank, with investments in tech and generative AI projected to deliver DKK 2 billion in annual productivity benefits by 2028, and structural efficiencies expected to improve the Cost-to-Income ratio to no greater than 43% by 2028.

Annual technology and core platform investment has been increased from DKK 4.0 billion to DKK 4.5 billion, with group operations leveraged across a unified IT platform enabling reusable data products and rapid cloud migration, having successfully migrated over 30% of total bank applications to the public cloud since 2023. Customer Quality and Retention Dynamics

Danske Bank maintains a highly diversified customer base of over 2.4 million clients, serving more than 2.4 million personal and business customers across its core Nordic markets with no single external customer generating more than 10% of the financial group's total operating income, while the Large Corporates & Institutions division serves more than 2,200 large corporate and institutional clients.

Danske Bank suffers from ongoing retail customer attrition within its Danish residential mortgage segment, with nominal private mortgage lending to Personal Customers declining by approximately 12% over five years due to Totalkredit's dominant market share of 47.5% and its formidable customer bonus pricing scheme, while competitors cannot easily match Totalkredit's pricing without facing unacceptable margin compression on existing portfolios.

Corporate mortgage relationships demonstrate strong retention offsetting the private mortgage volume decline, with nominal mortgage lending to Business Customers expanding by 11% over the same five-year period and mortgage lending to Large Corporates & Institutions customers increasing by 34% since early 2021, while the

bank is capturing market share within high-value affluent and Private Banking customer segments. customers giving the bank an average meeting satisfaction score of nine out of ten in early 2026. Danske Bank suffers from structurally weaker pricing power in the residential mortgage segment compared to Totalkredit, with Realkredit Danmark's administration margin cuts only benefiting new clients leaving existing borrowers on uncompetitive historical rates, while DNB Carnegie forecasts long-term lending and deposit margin pressure of 1.5 basis points annually.

Foundation Quality and Operator Excellence

Management upgraded its Forward '28 targets to target a Return on Equity exceeding 14.5% by 2028, with the strategy projecting total annual income to reach approximately DKK 63 billion by 2028 and operational initiatives targeting compressing the Cost-to-Income ratio to no greater than 43% by 2028. CEO Carsten Egeriis has delivered exemplary execution since April 2021, successfully resolving the bank's historical Estonia liabilities, joining the Executive Leadership Team in 2017 previously serving as Chief Risk Officer, with his previous tenure as Barclays Chief Risk Officer reinforcing a disciplined approach to balance sheet de-risking.

The bank holds a dominant 25% share of loans and deposits in Denmark, with core scale supporting over DKK 1,770 billion in total Nordic loans and mortgages, managing DKK 1,100 billion in deposits and over DKK 1,000 billion in assets.

Large scale allows the bank to maintain and run its proprietary core IT platform, with smaller domestic peers required to share BEC, SDC, or Bankdata utility platforms, while standalone platforms bypass the exit penalties and multi-year notice periods of shared utilities. Low deposit betas near 30% protect funding costs relative to historical rate cycles.

Disruption Forces and Long-Term Growth

Danske Bank's high entry barriers protect its core footprint while secular affluent growth and selective corporate expansion offset emerging digital platform disruption risks, with capital requirements and MREL decisions raising compliance complexity establishing a high entry threshold, while maintaining proprietary core IT platforms requires annual tech investments of DKK 4.5 billion which smaller challenger banks cannot replicate.

Digital challenger banks target transaction-heavy retail and SME segments but face structural difficulties scaling into capital-intensive mortgages, with neobanks like Lunar capturing fractional payment volumes from younger demographics threatening long-term primary customer retention, though incumbents have largely closed the digital experience gap by executing major technology investments and cloud migrations.
