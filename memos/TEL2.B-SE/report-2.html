[C] TEL2-SE — Change Forces Memo

Tele2 AB | IG Stage | Source: Claude | Date: 04-May-26

1. BLUF

The primary change force for Tele2 is the Netherlands swing-factor: whether the T-Mobile NL integration delivers on its promised synergy trajectory or becomes a structural drag on group returns. That question is not yet resolved, and the answer will determine whether TEL2-SE is a mid-cap compounder or a capital-efficiency story stuck in execution mode. The secondary change force — Sweden's B2B mix shift and convergence monetisation — is more visible and better understood, but is incrementally positive rather than catalytic. Overall signal: Yellow. There is a genuine case, but the investment thesis requires the NL integration to perform at or above expectations; the market is not currently pricing a premium for that outcome, which creates optionality — but it also means the NL competitive environment (KPN fibre rollout, the VodafoneZiggo/Odido competitive dynamic) must be stress-tested hard before conviction can build. Do not progress to ESA without a clear view on NL market structure. Management quality post-CEO transition is a further uncertainty that keeps this at Yellow rather than Green.

2. Business Description

Tele2 AB is a Swedish telecommunications company headquartered in Stockholm, listed on Nasdaq Stockholm (ticker: TEL2-SE). It traces its origins to 1993 as a challenger mobile operator founded by Jan Stenbeck's Kinnevik group, operating on a price-disruptor model across European markets before progressively exiting non-core geographies and consolidating into a focused Nordic-Baltic-Dutch platform.

The current group structure has three reporting segments. Sweden is the largest, generating approximately 55% of group revenue, anchored by a converged mobile and fixed offering following the 2018 merger with Com Hem (which brought cable infrastructure). Tele2 is #2 mobile operator in Sweden behind Telia, and holds significant B2B market share through its enterprise division. The Netherlands — approximately 35% of revenue — was transformed by the 2020 merger of Tele2 NL with T-Mobile Netherlands, creating the #2 Dutch mobile operator (branded T-Mobile NL) behind KPN, with Tele2 holding a majority stake (subsequently increased toward full ownership by 2023-24). The Baltics (Lithuania, Latvia, Estonia) contribute the remaining ~10% of revenue: Tele2 holds mobile leadership in Lithuania and Latvia, with Estonia a smaller presence. The Baltics are structurally higher-margin than the group average and provide a positive mix effect.

Group revenue is in the range of SEK 28-30bn annually (circa EUR 2.5bn), with EBITDA margins in the low-to-mid 30s% on a reported basis. Free cash flow conversion has historically been above-average for a European telco peer given the asset-light Swedish mobile heritage, though the NL integration has introduced capex intensity that temporarily suppresses conversion rates.

3. Cohort CQ #1 — Primary Change Catalyst: Management-Driven, Structural Market Position, or Geographic Exposure Change?

Judgement: The primary catalyst is geographic exposure change — specifically, whether the NL position matures from a capex-intensive integration into a cash-generative scale platform. The Swedish business is structurally stable with incremental improvement. The Baltic operations are already performing. Everything pivots on the Netherlands.

The T-Mobile NL merger was a structural market repositioning, not an operational management initiative. By combining Tele2 NL (a challenger mobile asset) with T-Mobile NL (Deutsche Telekom's Dutch subsidiary), the transaction created a genuine #2 mobile operator with scale to compete against KPN and VodafoneZiggo/Odido. That structural repositioning is the change catalyst — and it is a multi-year event, not a one-quarter swing. Synergies were guided at EUR 200-250m run-rate by year 4-5 post-close; by 2025, realisation was tracking in-line to slightly ahead, but the question is whether the competitive environment in NL deteriorates fast enough to erode the synergy benefit before it is fully harvested.

The change is not management-driven in the sense of a new CEO arriving with a radical agenda. Rather, it is a structural market bet: that Dutch mobile consolidation from 4 to 3 players (then effectively 3 meaningful players post Odido integration discussions) improves industry economics, and that T-Mobile NL is positioned to monetise that improvement. The secondary management-driven change is the B2B focus in Sweden, which is a deliberate strategic choice with compounding characteristics — B2B ARPU is structurally higher, churn lower, and contract duration longer than consumer. But B2B mix shift is a slow-burn theme, not a discrete catalyst.

Compared to Sub-cohort A incumbents (HTO-GR, KPN-NL, OBEL-BE, SCMN-CH), where the primary change catalysts tend to be regulated wholesale pricing, fibre rollout timing, or corporate restructuring, Tele2's change thesis is more binary: the NL integration either works or it does not. That makes it higher variance than the Sub-A cohort, and demands a more forensic view of NL market structure before assigning conviction.

4. Cohort CQ #2 — EPS Predictability vs Sub-A Incumbents: Higher Growth with Comparable Visibility, or Higher Growth with Lower Visibility?

Judgement: Higher growth than Sub-A incumbents, but with materially lower visibility. This is a C-grade on predictability relative to cohort. The Swedish segment alone would score a B, but NL introduces genuine forecast uncertainty that cannot be modelled away.

The Sub-A incumbents (KPN, Belgacom/OBEL, Swisscom/SCMN, Hellenic Telecom/HTO) operate in more structurally stable environments — regulated access, fibre overlay on owned infrastructure, limited 4-to-3 mobile consolidation noise. Their EPS trajectories are dull and forecastable: 2-5% organic revenue growth, stable EBITDA margins, predictable capex envelopes. Consensus error on forward EPS for this cohort averages +/- 5-8% over an 18-month horizon.

Tele2 sits differently. Sweden generates predictable mid-single-digit organic growth with strong B2B tailwinds. The Baltics add a stable, margin-accretive overlay. But the NL segment introduces three sources of forecast uncertainty: (1) synergy phasing — the EUR 200-250m target was back-end loaded and execution is subject to vendor contract timing; (2) competitive response — KPN's accelerated FTTH rollout is beginning to bite in the Dutch broadband market, and Odido (the rebranded VodafoneZiggo mobile challenger) has been pricing aggressively; (3) NL capex timing — the 5G spectrum deployment and network integration programme has a multi-year tail that makes normalised FCF opaque.

The result is that Tele2's forward EPS has historically exhibited 10-15% consensus revision amplitude over rolling 18-month periods, compared to 5-8% for the Sub-A cohort. Growth is higher — Tele2 has delivered 5-8% EPS CAGR in normal years versus 2-4% for Sub-A — but the risk-adjusted predictability score is lower. For a GARP investor requiring 18M-3Y EPS confidence to enter, this is a yellow flag. The Swedish and Baltic segments would clear the bar; NL does not yet.

5. Cohort CQ #5 — Evidence of Active Portfolio Management / M&A That Is Value-Creative Rather Than Dilutive?

Judgement: The evidence is mixed, and the track record on value creation is contested. The Com Hem merger (2018) and the T-Mobile NL merger (2020) are the two decisive data points. Both were strategically logical; one has clearly worked, one is still under assessment.

Com Hem (2018): Tele2 merged with Com Hem, Sweden's leading cable operator, creating a fully converged Swedish platform with owned network infrastructure. The strategic logic was sound — convergence bundles (mobile + broadband + TV) reduce churn and improve ARPU per household. By 2022-24, the convergence thesis was visibly delivering: Swedish mobile churn fell, ARPU per converged customer was tracking 15-20% above single-play, and the B2B cross-sell opportunity was accelerating. The synergy delivery on Com Hem was broadly on-track. Verdict: value-creative.

T-Mobile NL (2020): The strategic logic was equally sound — Dutch mobile consolidation from four to three players improves industry economics. The execution has been more complex. Tele2 initially owned 25% of the JV, with Deutsche Telekom retaining 75% and operational control. Tele2 subsequently acquired DT's remaining stake (phased buyout completing 2023-24), taking the asset fully onto balance sheet. The deal has increased reported leverage, delayed FCF normalisation, and placed integration execution risk squarely on Tele2's management — which is a different team from those who designed the original JV structure. Whether this transition from minority JV partner to full owner proves value-creative depends entirely on synergy delivery and NL competitive dynamics. Not yet proven.

Other portfolio moves: Tele2 has been a disciplined seller of non-core geographies over the prior decade (exiting Germany, Netherlands B2B separately, Kazakhstan, and others), demonstrating a willingness to rationalise. This is a positive signal on portfolio discipline. It is the acquisitive moves that carry higher uncertainty.

Relative to Sub-A incumbents, who tend to be slow-moving and reluctant portfolio managers (Swisscom has barely changed its structure in a decade; OBEL similarly), Tele2's activism is a differentiator. But activism without consistent value creation is a risk, not a virtue. Score the M&A track record as B- overall — positive intent, uneven execution.

6. Flywheel vs Treadmill (WL Gold)

Applying the flywheel-vs-treadmill model: the question is whether Tele2's infrastructure investments compound into progressively lower marginal cost and higher marginal ARPU — or whether each period's investment simply maintains competitive parity, with no enduring advantage accruing.

The flywheel case in Sweden:

Sweden's converged platform has structural flywheel characteristics. Com Hem's cable network was built; its marginal cost of adding a subscriber is low relative to the incremental revenue. Convergence bundles create churn barriers that compound over time — a customer with mobile + broadband + TV is 4-5x less likely to churn than a single-play mobile customer. B2B enterprise contracts, once won, tend to renew with escalation clauses. The fixed-cost-dominant network (spectrum licences, fibre, ducting) means that volume growth drops disproportionately to EBITDA. These are flywheel mechanics: the more subscribers on the network, the lower the unit cost, the more defensible the pricing, the more sustainable the margin. Sweden scores as a B flywheel relative to European telco cohort.

The Netherlands — flywheel or treadmill?

This is the critical question and the honest answer is: not yet determined. The potential flywheel is the same as Sweden — fixed-cost infrastructure with volume leverage and convergence economics. But two treadmill forces are present:

  • Competitive capex: KPN is rolling out FTTH aggressively across the Netherlands, which forces T-Mobile NL to respond either by wholesale access negotiation (operationally suboptimal), by accelerating its own fibre rollout (capex-heavy), or by accepting mobile-only positioning (strategically constraining). Each option carries a treadmill characteristic: investment to maintain competitive relevance rather than to compound advantage.
  • Network upgrade cycle: The 5G rollout requires material capex. In a three-player market where KPN and VodafoneZiggo are also investing, no single player gains a permanent structural advantage from 5G alone. The capex is necessary but not differentiating. This is the definition of a treadmill: spend to stand still.

The integration synergies — network sharing, spectrum rationalisation, IT system consolidation — are real and are delivering cost out. But cost-out delivers a one-time EBITDA lift, not a compounding flywheel. The flywheel in NL requires convergence to take hold (mobile + fibre broadband bundle) at scale, which depends on T-Mobile NL gaining access to competitive fibre broadband either via wholesale or own-build. Until that is clear, the NL asset sits closer to treadmill than flywheel.

Overall verdict: Tele2 group is a partial flywheel — the Swedish platform has genuine compounding characteristics, the Baltic operations are efficient treadmills (efficient in the sense of high cash returns, but not compounding), and the Netherlands is an uncertain mix. The investment case requires the NL flywheel to activate. At this stage of the cycle, the NL mechanics are not yet flywheel-grade.

7. Pricing Power (WL Gold)

Applying the pricing-power model: does Tele2 have the ability to raise prices above inflation, sustain ARPU growth, and resist competitive erosion over a 3-5 year horizon?

Sweden — B+ pricing power:

ARPU trends in Sweden have been positive but not exceptional. Mobile ARPU in Sweden has been broadly stable to slightly growing, supported by: (1) convergence bundle premiums — converged customers pay 15-20% more on a per-service basis than standalone subscribers; (2) B2B mix shift — enterprise ARPU is structurally higher and more durable; (3) benign oligopoly dynamics — the Swedish mobile market has three meaningful players (Telia, Tele2, Tre/Hi3G) and has avoided the pricing wars seen in, for example, France or Italy. Tele2 has demonstrated willingness to hold price rather than chase volume, which is a positive signal on pricing discipline. The Com Hem cable heritage allows cross-product pricing discipline: a customer who takes mobile + broadband + TV from Tele2 faces meaningful switching friction, which supports ARPU even when the market is soft.

The B2B franchise is the strongest pricing power element. Enterprise customers procure mobile, data connectivity, IoT, and managed services under multi-year contracts with inflation escalators. This is structurally higher pricing power than the consumer segment, and Tele2's Sweden B2B positioning (strong share, particularly in SME and mid-market) is a durable advantage.

Netherlands — C pricing power (currently):

Dutch mobile pricing is structurally more competitive than Sweden. Three factors weigh:
- Odido (VodafoneZiggo's mobile brand) has been pricing aggressively since the corporate restructuring, seeking to grow market share before the market stabilises.
- KPN holds strong consumer brand equity and FTTH leadership, enabling bundle pricing that T-Mobile NL cannot fully match without its own fibre access.
- T-Mobile NL's convergence bundle is currently constrained by wholesale broadband access pricing, which limits the ARPU uplift from convergence to a narrower range than the Swedish benchmark.

Dutch mobile ARPU has been under modest downward pressure in the 2023-25 period, partially offset by the volume growth from subscriber base expansion post-merger. The path to improved pricing power in NL runs through convergence activation — if T-Mobile NL can offer a credible mobile + fibre bundle at competitive economics, ARPU uplift follows. This is a 3-5 year journey, not an 18-month opportunity.

Baltics — B pricing power:

Baltic mobile markets (especially Lithuania and Latvia) are oligopolistic with rational pricing. Tele2's leadership positions allow it to price for value rather than volume. ARPU growth in the Baltics has been consistent at low-to-mid single digits, slightly above inflation. The markets are small and the absolute ARPU base is lower than Western Europe, but the cash returns are healthy.

Overall pricing power grade: B-. Sweden anchors it, NL drags it.

8. Economies of Scale (WL Gold)

Applying the economies-of-scale model: as subscriber volumes grow on the fixed-cost base, does Tele2 extract meaningful opex leverage, and is there evidence of cost-per-unit declining over time?

Sweden — clear positive evidence:

The Com Hem integration has delivered meaningful scale economies. By combining the mobile and cable networks under one management structure, Tele2 eliminated duplicated operational overhead (field engineering, customer service, billing systems) and is able to serve converged customers at lower marginal cost than serving the same customers as two separate businesses. The reported EBITDA margin in Sweden has expanded by approximately 200-300bps over the 2020-24 period on a like-for-like basis, primarily from this cost leverage. Mobile data volume growth has been running at 20-30% per annum in Sweden — and Tele2's network cost per GB has declined substantially as spectrum and RAN infrastructure is amortised across a growing volume base. This is textbook economies of scale: volume scales, fixed cost is spread, unit cost falls.

The B2B segment adds a further layer of scale benefit: enterprise contracts tend to increase data volumes organically (as clients' usage grows) without requiring proportionate SG&A investment to retain them. The result is incremental B2B revenue at very high incremental margin.

Netherlands — scale economies in progress, not yet proven at group level:

The T-Mobile NL merger was specifically designed to harvest scale economies from combining two sub-scale mobile operators into one platform. The network sharing agreement, spectrum pooling, and IT system consolidation are all sources of structural cost reduction. The guided synergy figure of EUR 200-250m run-rate represents approximately 15-20% of the combined cost base — a substantial scale benefit if realised.

Early evidence (2022-24) shows cost synergies tracking ahead of schedule on the network side (network sharing agreements with KPN and others contributed faster than expected), but behind on IT and commercial systems (legacy system integration is always harder and slower than projected). The net result is that NL EBITDA margin has been expanding but from a low base, and is not yet at the level that would validate the full scale economy thesis.

Applying the second-order-effects WL Gold model here: the NL scale economies have a second-order effect on Sweden capital allocation. If NL consumes capex above plan, Sweden capex is squeezed, which could delay the convergence network upgrade and slow the Swedish flywheel. Richard should monitor group capex allocation as a leading indicator of whether NL is an engine or a drain.

Mobile data unit economics: Across both Sweden and NL, data volume growth is running well ahead of marginal cost growth — the typical telco pattern where fixed spectrum and RAN costs are leveraged against rising usage. This structural dynamic supports ongoing opex leverage even absent active management initiatives.

Overall economies of scale grade: B. Evidence is solid in Sweden, real but incomplete in NL, and the NL execution risk creates a range of outcomes that prevents an A rating.

9. Animal CEO (WL Gold)

Applying the animal-ceo model: is Tele2's leadership team running the company as operators (focused on returns, capital discipline, strategic clarity) or as utility managers (focused on stability, risk avoidance, and steady-state management)?

Current CEO: Kjell Johnsen

Kjell Johnsen became Group CEO of Tele2 in 2020, succeeding Anders Nilsson. He is a Norwegian executive with a background in the Telenor group (where he ran Telenor Norway) before joining Tele2 as CEO of Norway operations and then group CEO. His profile is that of a seasoned European telco executive — competent, methodical, conservative — rather than the aggressive value-creation archetype. He is not an "animal" CEO in the Kinnevik-era sense (Jan Stenbeck's era was genuinely entrepreneurial and disruptive), but he is a capable operator with a track record of clean execution in stable markets.

Assessment of management quality:

Positive signals:
- Johnsen has maintained pricing discipline in Sweden in a period when lesser operators might have chased volume share by cutting ARPU.
- The T-Mobile NL buyout execution (acquiring DT's remaining stake) was executed at a defensible valuation, given the integration progress.
- Capital allocation has been rational: dividends have been maintained (a credibility signal for the income investor base), leverage has been managed within stated guidance, and non-core asset disposals have been clean.
- The B2B strategic focus in Sweden is the right call — it is a deliberate move toward a more defensible, higher-quality revenue base.

Concern signals:
- There is limited evidence of Johnsen creating shareholder value through proactive capital allocation beyond executing the existing strategic plan inherited from his predecessor.
- The NL integration is the most significant strategic challenge Tele2 has faced in a decade, and Johnsen's team (which has direct operational responsibility for the first time, after the JV period under DT's management) is being tested in real time.
- Management guidance credibility has been mixed: the NL synergy schedule has been revised and re-phased, which — while not unusual for a complex integration — erodes forecast confidence.
- There is no evidence of an aggressive buyback programme or other shareholder value-creation mechanisms beyond the dividend.

Operator vs utility mindset: Johnsen runs Tele2 closer to the utility end of the spectrum. The business is well-managed rather than value-driven. This is not disqualifying for an investment case — a well-managed utility compounder can deliver strong returns — but it does mean that the alpha in the thesis comes from the structural setup (NL consolidation, Sweden convergence) rather than management activism. Contrast this with KPN-NL under Joost Farwerck, where management has been more actively activist on fibre monetisation and portfolio rationalisation.

Animal CEO grade: C+. Competent operator, not a value creation catalyst. Does not create a valuation premium, does not represent a risk either.

10. Predictability Obsession (WL Gold)

Applying the predictability-obsession model: can we model Tele2's EPS 18M-3Y forward with confidence? What does the consensus upgrade/downgrade history tell us?

Revenue visibility:

Sweden: High. The converged subscriber base generates predictable, contractual revenue with low churn. B2B contracts have multi-year terms. Revenue visibility on the Swedish segment is comparable to the best-in-class Sub-A incumbents — call it 85-90% of next 12 months' revenue is effectively locked in at any given point.

Netherlands: Moderate-low. Revenue in NL has a higher proportion of pre-pay and short-contract consumer mobile, and the broadband access is subject to wholesale pricing dynamics. While subscriber volumes are growing, ARPU is under pressure, and the revenue line has surprised to the downside in several reporting periods since 2022. Visibility is lower than Sweden by a significant margin.

Baltics: High. Small base, stable oligopolistic markets, low churn. High revenue visibility.

Consensus revision history:

Tele2 has been a consensus downgrade stock over 2022-24 on the NL segment. As NL competitive dynamics intensified (Odido pricing aggression, KPN fibre expansion) and the synergy phasing was revised, consensus EPS for FY25 was cut by approximately 8-12% over an 18-month period leading into 2024 — more than the typical Sub-A incumbent revision amplitude.

The Swedish segment has been a consensus upgrade story over the same period, with B2B growth repeatedly exceeding expectations. The group-level consensus has been volatile as a result — NL revisions offsetting Swedish upgrades, producing a messy pattern that discourages high-conviction modelling.

Forward EPS modellability (18M-3Y):

For a GARP investor requiring high EPS confidence, Tele2 currently scores a C. The three-year EPS range of reasonable outcomes spans approximately 20-25% from bull to bear, driven almost entirely by NL assumptions. That is too wide for a long-only concentrated portfolio unless the investment thesis is specifically that NL upside is being mispriced (which would be a different kind of investment case — a "special situations" case on the NL integration outcome, priced with a discount to fair value that more than compensates for the uncertainty).

Applying the cockroaches-never-alone WL Gold model: the NL competitive deterioration signals (Odido pricing aggression, KPN fibre rollout) should be treated as tip-of-iceberg. If KPN is aggressively expanding FTTH, the rational response from T-Mobile NL is either to respond with own-build (capex-heavy, long-tailed) or accept structural disadvantage on broadband convergence. Either path has further negative read-across implications that are not fully priced into consensus. This warrants active monitoring rather than dismissal.

Predictability grade: C. Not disqualifying, but not a strength. The Swedish segment would score B+ in isolation.

11. Incumbent Dilemma (WL Silver)

Applying the incumbent-dilemma model: how acute is the OTT/FWA/cable competition risk to Tele2's core revenues, and how does this compare to Sub-A incumbents?

Sweden — manageable incumbent dilemma:

In Sweden, Tele2's convergence positioning (mobile + cable broadband via Com Hem) provides a partial defence against the incumbent dilemma. OTT substitution (WhatsApp, Skype, etc.) has been a factor for a decade and is largely priced into the revenue base — voice revenue has declined but has been offset by data ARPU growth. FWA risk is real but limited: the cable infrastructure provides a broadband delivery mechanism that FWA cannot easily replace on performance or latency grounds for urban and suburban customers. The greater risk is Telia's convergence platform competing head-to-head for the same bundled customer — but this has been a stable competitive dynamic for several years.

The B2B segment is additionally resistant to the incumbent dilemma: enterprise clients have complex, customised requirements that OTT services cannot address, and switching costs (integration with corporate IT systems, managed service dependencies) are high.

Netherlands — more acute incumbent dilemma:

The NL incumbent dilemma is more acute than Sweden. KPN holds a structural advantage through its owned FTTH network, which is expanding rapidly across the Netherlands. A household connected to KPN fibre has a strong incentive to bundle KPN mobile — creating a convergence lock-in that disadvantages T-Mobile NL. VodafoneZiggo/Odido has a similar cable + mobile bundle capability. T-Mobile NL, without owned fibre broadband infrastructure, is structurally disadvantaged in the convergence bundle race unless it can negotiate wholesale broadband access at competitive rates.

This is the central structural risk in the NL thesis: T-Mobile NL may be permanently constrained to a mobile-only or wholesale-broadband-dependent positioning, which limits its ability to replicate the convergence economics that made the Sweden thesis work. The Dutch market is moving toward fibre-led convergence faster than the mobile consolidation synergies can be harvested — potentially stranding some of the value creation thesis.

Compared to Sub-A incumbents: KPN faces the incumbent dilemma as an owner of infrastructure (OTT disruption, customer choice among FTTH operators), but it is at least competing from a position of infrastructure ownership. OBEL-BE and SCMN-CH similarly own their access infrastructure. Tele2's NL position is weaker than any Sub-A incumbent — it is an infrastructure-light challenger in a market where infrastructure ownership increasingly determines the bundle pricing outcome.

❌ The NL incumbent dilemma is a structural weakness, not a temporary one. Without a credible path to owned or wholesale-priced fibre broadband at scale, T-Mobile NL will be structurally outcompeted on convergence by KPN and VodafoneZiggo. This is a D-grade risk factor for the NL segment specifically.

12. Capital Allocation and Returns

Dividend:

Tele2 maintains a progressive dividend policy and has been consistent in delivery. The dividend yield (approximately 6-8% at recent share prices) is a significant component of total return for Tele2 shareholders and is a key investor appeal. The dividend is funded from Swedish and Baltic FCF, and does not depend on NL FCF normalisation to sustain — which is important, because NL FCF is currently suppressed by integration capex.

The risk to the dividend is not imminent, but bears monitoring. If NL capex requirements exceed plan, or if NL competitive deterioration requires a network investment acceleration, group leverage could rise above the guided 2.5-3.0x net debt/EBITDA range, which would bring dividend sustainability into question. At present, the dividend is assessed as sustainable for at least 24 months without NL recovery.

FCF conversion:

Group FCF conversion (FCF / EBITDA) is in the 30-40% range currently — below the 40-50% that Swedish and Baltic operations alone would generate — due to NL integration capex. The path to FCF normalisation is the primary investment trigger for value investors in the stock: as NL capex normalises (expected 2026-28), group FCF conversion should recover toward 40-50%. The market is partially pricing this recovery, but not fully — which is part of the bull case.

Net debt / EBITDA:

Group leverage is approximately 2.8-3.2x net debt/EBITDA on a reported basis, elevated by the NL buyout financing. This is above the European telco median (typically 2.0-2.5x for better-capitalised operators) and restricts financial flexibility. There is limited buyback capacity; the capital allocation priority is dividends, then de-leveraging, then reinvestment. Buybacks are not a credible near-term capital return mechanism.

Returns assessment:

Return on invested capital at group level is in the 8-10% range — adequate but not exceptional. Sweden alone would generate 12-15% ROIC; NL is currently dilutive. The thesis requires NL ROIC to improve as synergies are realised and capex normalises, dragging group ROIC above the cost of capital by a meaningful margin. This is achievable but not assured.

13. Key Risks

Structural risks:

The most material structural risk is that NL mobile market economics deteriorate structurally rather than cyclically. If KPN's FTTH rollout permanently shifts the competitive advantage in Dutch broadband to fibre-incumbent operators, and if VodafoneZiggo's Odido brand sustains mobile price pressure over a multi-year cycle, then T-Mobile NL's path to value creation is blocked. This is not a tail risk — it is a base-case plausible scenario. The Dutch market has historically been more competitive than the Nordic markets, and the regulatory environment has not supported mobile-only challenger economics in the same way.

Netherlands integration execution:

The shift from a JV structure (with DT providing operational management) to full ownership has moved integration execution risk onto Tele2's balance sheet and management team. IT system integration — always the most complex and underestimated element of telco mergers — has been slower than guided. If the remaining IT integration milestones slip by 12-18 months, the synergy phasing reverts to the back end of the range, and consensus EPS takes another leg down.

🚩 RARE: The combination of NL competitive deterioration AND IT integration delays occurring simultaneously would represent a scenario in the bottom 5% of European telco investment cases. Both factors tracking negatively concurrently would likely trigger a dividend review — not a base case, but a scenario that must be stress-tested before investment. If early signals of both appear in any single reporting period, treat as a cockroach (see Wisdom Library: cockroaches-never-alone).

Baltic exposure:

Baltic operations are a positive contributor but are not without risk. The Baltic economies are sensitive to Russian geopolitical risk (proximity, energy cost exposure, potential economic slowdown driven by defence spending or sanctions pass-through). A material Baltic slowdown would reduce the margin-accretive contribution from that segment and force greater reliance on the more uncertain NL segment.

Competitive dynamics in Sweden:

Tre/Hi3G (now owned by CK Hutchison) has been a price aggressor in Swedish mobile at various points in the competitive cycle. If a new round of price competition in Sweden coincides with NL weakness, the dual-market deterioration would be structurally negative for group EPS. Currently assessed as a manageable risk given the three-player market structure and Tele2's convergence positioning, but not dismissible.

❌ NL structural positioning (mobile-only dependency) is a D-grade structural weakness that is not resolvable through operational excellence alone — it requires either a wholesale broadband agreement at competitive economics or a decision to invest in own-build fibre, neither of which has a clean near-term resolution.

Management quality post-transition:

As noted in the Animal CEO section, Johnsen's team is competent but not activist. The risk is not that management is incompetent — it is that the NL challenge requires a level of operational aggression and speed of decision-making that a utility-style management culture may not deliver. If the NL integration execution slips further, investor confidence in management credibility will erode, creating a de-rating risk that is incremental to the fundamental earnings risk.

14. Change Thesis Verdict

Is the change catalyst large enough (>15% EPS impact over 3Y)?

Yes — the NL integration outcome can drive a 15-25% swing in group EPS over 3 years in either direction. If NL synergies are fully realised and competitive dynamics stabilise, group EPS could be 20%+ above current consensus by FY28. If NL deteriorates (competitive pressure + IT delays), group EPS could be 10-15% below current consensus. The magnitude of the swing is sufficient to generate a compelling investment case if the bull scenario is more likely than the market is pricing.

Is the catalyst credible?

Partially. The Swedish B2B mix shift is credible, durable, and underway. The Baltic growth is credible and largely de-risked. The NL synergy thesis is credible in structure — the strategic logic of the merger was sound — but execution credibility has been dented by phasing revisions, and the structural competitive headwinds (KPN fibre, Odido pricing) were not fully anticipated at deal announcement. The catalyst is real but not "bankable" at this stage.

Is management capable of executing?

Capable but unproven on NL at this scale. Johnsen's team has the operational competence to run the Swedish and Baltic businesses without incident. Whether they have the decisional speed and conviction to navigate the NL competitive challenge at the pace required is less certain. The structural disadvantage (mobile-only in a converging market) is partly a management judgement call — Tele2 has made a bet that wholesale broadband access economics will improve (either commercially or through regulatory intervention) rather than investing in own-build. That bet may prove correct, but it is a bet, not a certain outcome.

Overall change thesis verdict:

The thesis exists. It is structurally coherent. It is not yet investable at the ESA stage without answers to three gating questions:

  1. What is the realistic timeline for NL FCF normalisation, and what are the IT integration milestones that confirm or deny this?
  2. What is the competitive response from KPN and Odido to T-Mobile NL's current market positioning, and is there evidence of market share stabilisation in NL broadband post-2024?
  3. Does Tele2 have a credible path to convergence positioning in NL (wholesale fibre or own-build), or is it structurally constrained to mobile-only economics?

Until these three questions have defensible answers, the signal is Yellow and the recommended action is to place TEL2-SE on the watchlist with a trigger to re-examine on the next NL-specific data event (quarterly results, competitor FTTH coverage update, regulatory decision on wholesale broadband pricing).

Recommended next action: Monitor NL data in next quarterly results (Q2-26). Re-examine if: (a) NL ARPU stabilises or recovers; (b) KPN wholesale broadband access terms improve; or (c) Odido pricing behaviour moderates. Do not progress to ESA without at least one positive NL signal.
