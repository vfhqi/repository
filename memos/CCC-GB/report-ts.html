Section 3 – What the company does 15 findings

Section summary
  1. The business is a strategically favored global intermediary that extracts reliable transactional and contractual profits from enterprise IT complexity through scale and deep vendor alliances.

Core business segments and revenue generation

1.
Integrated service portfolio: Computacenter assists clients to source, transform, and manage their IT infrastructure, providing multiple strategic entry points to secure long-term relationships
2.
Dominant technology sourcing: Sourcing hardware remains the primary driver of gross invoiced income, generating £11,297.5m in FY2025 by delivering commercial structures and logistics
3.
Contractual professional services: Professional Services generated £847.2m in FY2025, deploying over 5,000 engineers and 750 project managers for complex network and cloud integration projects
4.
Stable managed services: Managed Services revenue of £843.6m in FY2025 provides recurring maintenance and support, showing improved pipeline visibility despite a slight constant-currency decline
5.
Thinner transactional margins: Technology Sourcing generates lower-margin transactional revenue with lower visibility, but creates excellent customer loyalty through supply-chain reliability and operational speed

Value chain positioning and competitive advantages

6.
Favoured intermediary position: The company sits between 3,000+ technology vendors and large enterprises, acting as a critical aggregator of purchasing power and simplifying procurement to capture high value
7.
Unprecedented customer concentration: Sales to the largest North American customer reached £2,731.2m in FY2025, highlighting an extremely concentrated revenue dependency for the US division
8.
Durable physical barriers: Massive Integration Centers in Hatfield, Kerpen, and Atlanta act as physical moats, enabling automated configuration and rack deployment at scale
9.
Significant switching costs: The deployment of proprietary tooling and deep integration into customer procurement systems creates substantial switching costs for large corporate accounts
10.
Sticky public contracts: Public sector agreements in Germany and the UK provide highly predictable multi-year revenues, backed by complex framework agreements

Market structure and peer comparison

11.
Consolidating enterprise market: The IT reseller industry is a consolidating market dominated by a few scale players, where Computacenter maintains a 7% target market share
12.
Favourable geographic positioning: Peer CDW dominates North America, while Bechtle leads Germany, leaving Computacenter uniquely exposed to the fast-growing transatlantic corridor
13.
SMEs versus corporates: Unlike UK peer Softcat which targets high-margin small-and-medium enterprises, Computacenter focuses primarily on complex, high-volume enterprise and hyperscaler accounts
14.
Other side of the story: Large hyperscale customers may eventually bypass resellers to buy directly from hardware manufacturers, completely eliminating the intermediary sourcing margin

Section 5 – The possible investment case 12 findings

Section summary
  1. The investment case strengthens significantly due to the company's unique positioning to capture structural hyperscale artificial intelligence capital expenditure without the asset-heavy risk of building datacenters.

Driver 1 – US data center and AI expansion

1.
Favourable US growth: High-volume demand from hyperscalers building GPU-dense AI clusters drove a near-doubling of North American adjusted operating profits in FY2025
2.
High plausibility rating: This driver carries high plausibility, as the company is helping build the world's largest AI cluster for a key US hyperscaler
3.
Potential failure mechanism: The driver would fail if US hyperscaler data center investments hit a cyclical peak or experience rapid technological obsolescence

Driver 2 – Strategic GAI and AgreeYa acquisitions

4.
Strategic federal entry: The May 2026 acquisition of GAI for up to $92m opens access to the stickier, non-overlapping US federal government IT segment
5.
Medium plausibility rating: This expansion carries medium plausibility, as federal regulatory frameworks are complex, but GAI's leadership will remain to manage the integration
6.
Potential failure mechanism: This catalyst would fail if integration bottlenecks or strict government security reviews stall GAI's historical $390m gross invoiced income run rate

Driver 3 – Services scalability and delivery synergy

7.
Favourable offshore delivery: The January 2026 acquisition of AgreeYa for $120m adds significant offshore delivery capabilities in Noida, India, boosting Professional Services margins
8.
High plausibility rating: Margin improvement carries high plausibility, as the company has successfully migrated historical acquisitions onto its core Group ERP platforms
9.
Potential failure mechanism: This catalyst would fail if severe consultant utilization declines or intense wage inflation in India offsets the expected cost efficiencies
10.
Independent catalyst probability: The forward investment case does not require all drivers to fire simultaneously, as the German public sector recovery is not reliant on the success of the US hyperscaler cycle
11.
Other side of the story: High hardware price increases for servers and RAM could cause enterprise customers to postpone non-essential IT migration projects, flattening the forward EPS curve

Section 7 – Key risks and the other side 6 findings

Section summary
  1. The bear case is credible but largely mitigated by the company's strong contractual relationships, conservative cash management, and the massive geographical diversification of its revenue streams.

Key risks and early warning signals

1.
Hyperscaler concentration risk: A sudden pullback or insourcing by the largest US hyperscale client represents the single failure mode that would most damage the group's forward EPS trajectory
2.
Hardware margin dilution: An accelerating mix of high-volume, low-margin hardware sourcing represents a "false friend" signal that masks severe operating margin compression
3.
Partner channel instability: Transitioning to the Cisco 360 Partner Program and changes to dealer registrations could compress volume rebates, squeezing the reseller margin profile
4.
Early warning indicator: Investors should closely monitor the committed product order backlog of £7.1bn, as any sharp contraction would warn of a demand slowdown
5.
Other side of the story: Sourcing contracts are primarily executed on a cost-plus basis, ensuring that inflation and component price spikes have minimal impact on gross margin dollars

Section 9 – Net read and what to check next 5 findings

Section summary
  1. Computacenter warrants immediate and deep investment work because its structural US AI data center tailwinds and massive net cash provide an incredibly predictable 18-month earnings trajectory.
1.
Favourable bull thesis: The strongest argument for investment is its unique position as a premier global "picks and shovels" player capturing high-margin services upside from recent acquisitions while remaining debt-free
2.
Other side of the story: The strongest argument against investment is the extreme concentration of its US revenue on a single hyperscale customer, exposing the division to severe volatility if that client pulls back
3.
Investigate US integration: Investors should immediately commission deep work on the integration of AgreeYa and GAI to verify whether the acquired services capability is successfully cross-selling into the US enterprise base
4.
Verify hyperscaler backlog: Analysts must closely verify the exact quarterly delivery schedule of the record £7.1bn product order backlog to confirm that the expected H1 2026 earnings momentum remains on track and supports a predictably visible earnings trajectory
