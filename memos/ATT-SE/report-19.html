3. PART A — FAILURE NARRATIVES

§2 — Failure Narrative 1: Customer Concentration / Logo-Churn Cascade

Specific failure narrative: Attensi's revenue base is concentrated in a relatively small number of large enterprise clients across financial services, oil & gas, and retail verticals. If one or two anchor logos churn — due to budget cuts, M&A, or internal LMS strategy pivots — revenue declines non-linearly because the lost contract carries disproportionate ARR weight. Transmission mechanism: Logo churn → ARR reduction → fixed-cost operating leverage reversal → EBITDA margin compression → EPS miss in H2 of any given year.

Early warning signals:
- Flagged: Decline in reported Net Revenue Retention below 100% — the threshold at which existing customer base begins to shrink in aggregate
- Flagged: Public announcements of restructuring or cost-reduction programmes at named top-10 clients (particularly oil & gas majors and large retail groups)
- Flagged: Lengthening of average contract renewal lead times disclosed in management commentary or inferred from deferred revenue movement
- Flagged: Increase in "professional services" as a proportion of total revenue (signals clients are not self-sufficient — fragile retention)
- Flagged: Sales & marketing headcount growth outpacing ARR growth — indicates the business is running harder to maintain the same base

Plausibility verdict: Medium-to-high plausibility — enterprise SaaS businesses with <200 logos and a top-10 concentration ratio above 40% of ARR regularly exhibit this failure mode at the 2–4 year mark of institutional scaling; sector base rate (EdTech / training SaaS) suggests ≥30% of comparables have experienced a major-logo churn event within a 3-year window

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: Clients have contractual renewal optionality — YES
- Motive: Enterprise training budgets are discretionary and first-cut in recession or cost-out cycles — YES
- Opportunity: Annual or biennial renewal cycles create a predictable window — YES
- All three legs present → full plausibility rating applies; not downgraded to watch-only

Disconfirming evidence: If NRR is publicly reported above 115% and top-10 client concentration is below 30% of ARR, this failure mode is significantly de-risked; if the business has disclosed a large and diversified renewal pipeline with multi-year terms, the transmission mechanism weakens materially

Monitoring checklist:
- NRR (quarterly, if disclosed)
- Deferred revenue balance as % of ARR (proxy for contract term health)
- New logo count vs. churned logo count (net new logos)
- ARR per client (distribution trend)
- Client industry health proxies (e.g., oil & gas capex announcements, retail cost-out news)

Interaction with other failure modes: Correlated with Narrative 3 (pricing pressure) and Narrative 5 (gross margin compression) — a churn event forces discounting to retain at-risk logos, which simultaneously compresses margin; also correlated with Narrative 9 (growth narrative break) since logo churn is reported with a lag and visible in the quarter after EPS guidance is cut

Sceptical close: The disconfirming evidence that would invalidate this failure hypothesis is a publicly reported NRR above 115% sustained over ≥6 consecutive quarters, combined with demonstrated client diversification across ≥6 distinct verticals with no single vertical exceeding 25% of ARR

§3 — Failure Narrative 2: Sales-Cycle Elongation / Enterprise Procurement Freeze

Specific failure narrative: Attensi sells into enterprise procurement processes — L&D budget holders, IT security reviewers, and C-suite sign-off in larger accounts. In a macro-tightening environment (or following a major enterprise client scandal in a target vertical), procurement cycles extend from 3–6 months to 9–18 months, causing bookings to fall short of plan without any change in product quality or competitive position. Transmission mechanism: Pipeline conversion rate decline → bookings miss → revenue recognition delay (SaaS) → cash-collection deterioration → working capital pressure → EPS guidance cut.

Early warning signals:
- Flagged: Sequential quarterly decline in new ARR added (not to be confused with churn — this is new business deceleration)
- Flagged: Sales headcount growth with no commensurate bookings growth (efficiency deterioration)
- Flagged: Rising average sales cycle length disclosed by management (or inferred from ratio of pipeline to closed bookings)
- Flagged: Industry-wide enterprise software procurement slowdown signals (e.g., SAP, Salesforce, Workday commentary on deal slippage)
- Flagged: Increase in pilot/POC programmes without conversion — indicates clients are experimenting but not committing

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — enterprise software deal slippage is the single most common near-term earnings risk in B2B SaaS; every major SaaS cohort from 2022–2024 experienced this; sector base rate of material bookings miss due to elongation is ≥50% of companies in any given macro tightening quarter

MMO test:
- Means: Enterprise clients control their own procurement timelines — YES
- Motive: CFO pressure to defer software spend is structurally present in cost-out environment — YES
- Opportunity: Annual budget cycles create natural deferral windows — YES
- All three legs present → full plausibility

Disconfirming evidence: Validated by contracted backlog disclosure showing ≥18M of committed future ARR per new logo cohort; or by management guidance being consistently met/beaten on new bookings for ≥4 consecutive quarters; or by a demonstrated shift to product-led growth reducing dependence on long enterprise sales cycles

Monitoring checklist:
- New ARR added per quarter (absolute, not YoY growth rate)
- Sales efficiency ratio (new ARR / sales & marketing spend)
- Pipeline-to-closed conversion rate
- Average deal size trend (rising deal size with longer cycles is a compound warning)
- Macro proxy: enterprise software commentary from large platform vendors

Interaction: Directly correlated with Narrative 1 (if new logos slow, concentration in existing base intensifies) and with Narrative 9 (growth narrative break) — slippage is initially invisible and then reported as a miss, triggering multiple-compression re-rating risk

Sceptical close: Invalidated if Attensi demonstrates a replicable product-led or channel-led growth motion that converts trials to paid in sub-60 days at scale, significantly reducing dependence on top-down enterprise procurement

§4 — Failure Narrative 3: Pricing Pressure / Commoditisation of Gamification Training

Specific failure narrative: As gamification-based training becomes a mainstream L&D methodology rather than a novelty, procurement teams apply structured RFP processes and benchmark Attensi pricing against a growing pool of alternatives (iSpring, Axonify, SAP Litmos, custom-built LMS solutions). Pricing power erodes structurally, compressing net new ARR per logo. Transmission mechanism: Contract renewal price erosion → declining ARR per seat → declining blended average selling price → gross-margin compression (fixed content-production cost against lower revenue per unit) → EPS miss over 2–3 renewal cycles.

Early warning signals:
- Flagged: ASP trend declining — new contracts signed at lower per-seat pricing than cohort average 12–18 months prior
- Flagged: Increase in RFP activity reported by management (signals procurement sophistication rising in the buyer base)
- Flagged: Competitor pricing announcements (e.g., freemium tiers, open-source LMS entrants claiming enterprise readiness)
- Flagged: Gross revenue growth decelerating faster than seat-count growth (implied ASP dilution)
- Flagged: Management commentary shifting from "premium pricing" to "value pricing" or "competitive positioning"

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — gamification as a differentiator has a ≥5-year durability window based on EdTech SaaS precedent (Duolingo-for-business adjacents, Kahoot enterprise); however, commoditisation risk accelerates materially if GenAI reduces the content-production cost for competitors, which is plausible within 24 months

MMO test:
- Means: Competitors have the technical capability to produce gamified content at lower cost with GenAI tooling — YES
- Motive: Market share gain incentive for challenger vendors — YES
- Opportunity: Contract renewals every 1–2 years — YES
- All three legs present → full plausibility

Disconfirming evidence: Demonstrated pricing stability or uplift across ≥3 consecutive renewal cohorts; net pricing expansion (not volume expansion) driving NRR above 110%; absence of competing product announcements from major LMS platforms with gamification modules

Monitoring checklist:
- Blended ASP per seat (quarterly if disclosed)
- Gross margin % trend
- Competitor product launches with gamification feature
- GenAI-powered content creation tool announcements from competitors
- Management commentary on win/loss reasons in competitive situations

Interaction: Correlated with Narrative 5 (gross margin compression) and Narrative 10 (technology disruption via GenAI); partially offsets Narrative 2 (if pricing drops, sales cycles shorten — a silver lining that doesn't save the unit economics)

Sceptical close: Invalidated by evidence that Attensi is successfully moving up-market into outcome-based or ROI-linked contracts, where commoditisation pressure is structurally lower than per-seat pricing models

§5 — Failure Narrative 4: Content Production Cost Inflation / Gross Margin Deterioration

Specific failure narrative: Attensi's product requires bespoke game-based content production for each enterprise client's use case. As the client base scales, content production capacity becomes a bottleneck, requiring investment in studios, creative talent, and production infrastructure that inflates cost of goods sold ahead of revenue. Transmission mechanism: Content production headcount / studio cost growth outpacing ARR growth → gross margin compression from current levels → EBITDA margin erosion → EPS downgrade even with top-line growth maintained.

Early warning signals:
- Flagged: Gross margin declining sequentially (not YoY — the sequential trend is the early signal)
- Flagged: Content/production headcount growing faster than revenue per head
- Flagged: Time-to-deployment metrics increasing (proxy for production backlog / capacity strain)
- Flagged: Client satisfaction scores declining (proxy for quality under production pressure)
- Flagged: Professional services (custom production) growing as % of total revenue

Plausibility verdict: Medium-to-high plausibility — this is the canonical gross-margin failure mode for "SaaS with a services wrapper" businesses; sector base rate shows ≥40% of custom-content SaaS companies experience a gross-margin correction event before achieving true platform economics; Attensi's model retains meaningful content-production labour intensity which limits the pure software margin profile

MMO test:
- Means: Labour cost inflation in creative/technical roles is externally driven and difficult to offset — YES
- Motive: Client demands for bespoke content are inherent to the premium positioning — YES
- Opportunity: Each new client contract creates a production obligation — YES
- All three legs present → full plausibility

Disconfirming evidence: Demonstrated shift to client self-service content creation tools (i.e., platform economics replacing bespoke production); or gross margin sustainably above 65% with ARR growing >40% — would indicate production leverage is working; or a disclosed GenAI-assisted production workflow materially reducing per-unit content cost

Monitoring checklist:
- Gross margin % (quarterly)
- Revenue per employee (total, not just sales)
- Time-from-contract-to-deployment metric
- Ratio of recurring software revenue to professional services revenue
- GenAI adoption disclosure in investor communications

Interaction: Correlated with Narrative 3 (pricing pressure makes it impossible to pass cost inflation to clients) and Narrative 7 (R&D under-investment as a false friend — if content production cost is suppressed by deferring platform investment, margin looks good short-term); this is the most compound-risk scenario in the failure set

Sceptical close: Invalidated by a credible and adopted self-service content creation platform (authoring tool) that demonstrably reduces production labour per unit of ARR — the single data point that would most powerfully de-risk this failure mode

§6 — Failure Narrative 5: Operating Leverage Failure / Fixed-Cost Over-Commitment

Specific failure narrative: In pursuit of growth, Attensi commits to fixed-cost infrastructure (office leases, senior executive hires, technology platform investment) ahead of revenue, on the assumption of continued ARR growth. If growth decelerates for any reason (macro, competitive, execution), the fixed-cost base cannot be unwound quickly, producing an EBITDA cliff. Transmission mechanism: Revenue growth deceleration → revenue falls below fixed-cost base → EBITDA swings to loss → cash burn accelerates → potential fundraising requirement → dilution risk → EPS miss compounded by share-count increase.

Early warning signals:
- Flagged: Headcount growth (especially G&A and senior management) significantly outpacing ARR growth
- Flagged: Office/infrastructure commitment disclosures (lease signings, new market office openings) in periods of slowing growth
- Flagged: Cash runway declining without a clear path to FCF positive
- Flagged: Increasing reliance on deferred revenue or upfront contract payments to fund operations (working capital manipulation signal)
- Flagged: Management bonus structures tied to revenue growth rather than profitability (misaligned incentives driving the wrong fixed-cost investment pattern)

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — applies to essentially all growth-stage SaaS companies; the company's Norwegian heritage and relatively conservative management culture may partially offset, but the structural risk is present in any business scaling internationally; sector base rate shows ≥60% of Series B–D SaaS companies experience at least one operating-leverage failure event before reaching sustainable FCF

MMO test:
- Means: Management discretion over hiring and infrastructure spend — YES
- Motive: Growth mandate from institutional investors post any fundraising — YES
- Opportunity: International expansion creates natural fixed-cost commitment points — YES
- All three legs present → full plausibility

Disconfirming evidence: Evidence of capital discipline — stable or declining operating-expense ratio relative to ARR; FCF positive or trending positively; management track record of headcount discipline in prior downturns; board composition including operationally experienced directors with SaaS scaling track records

Monitoring checklist:
- Operating expense ratio (OpEx / ARR)
- FCF burn rate per quarter
- Headcount vs. ARR growth (quarterly)
- Deferred revenue as % of total revenue
- Cash and equivalents vs. quarterly burn rate (runway metric)

Interaction: This failure mode is the "cascade amplifier" — it magnifies every other failure mode because it reduces the financial buffer available to absorb revenue shortfalls; correlated with Narrative 1 (logo churn hits fixed-cost base hardest) and with Narrative 9 (growth narrative break triggers investor confidence collapse, which may force a fundraise at a depressed valuation)

Sceptical close: Invalidated by a demonstrated trend toward FCF positivity at current ARR scale, or by a credible management commitment to profitability thresholds being enforced by the board with visible consequences

§7 — Failure Narrative 6: Key Executive / Founder Departure

Specific failure narrative: Attensi was founded by founders with a specific vision for gamification-based learning, and the product's cultural identity is closely tied to that founding team. A departure of the CEO, CTO, or CPO — particularly if to a competitor or in the context of a strategic disagreement with investors — would signal to enterprise clients that product roadmap continuity is at risk, potentially triggering non-renewal decisions. Transmission mechanism: Key executive departure → client uncertainty → renewal hesitation → NRR decline → ARR miss → EPS miss; secondary channel: talent attrition (key product/engineering staff follow founder out) → product roadmap delay.

Early warning signals:
- Flagged: LinkedIn departures in senior product/engineering roles (publicly observable with a lag)
- Flagged: Changes in management commentary tone or strategic direction in investor communications
- Flagged: Unusually long periods between investor updates or media presence disappearance of founders
- Flagged: Board composition changes (addition of independent directors with operational mandates — can be a governance upgrade or a founders-being-managed-out signal)
- Flagged: Equity vesting cliff proximity for founders (a structurally motivated departure window)

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — founder departure risk is highest in the 3–7 year post-founding window as the business professionalises; Attensi is within this window; sector base rate for founder CEO transition events in SaaS companies at this growth stage is approximately 30–40% within any 3-year period; the risk is elevated if PE or growth equity investors have taken significant ownership and have professionalisation agendas

MMO test:
- Means: Founders can resign at will (subject to contractual obligations) — YES
- Motive: Professionalisation pressure, investor control, equity liquidity desires — PARTIAL
- Opportunity: Any board meeting is an opportunity — YES
- Two-of-three partial → downgraded to watch-only unless motive evidence strengthens

Disconfirming evidence: Long-dated founder equity vesting schedules with documented retention arrangements; founder CEO publicly engaged in multi-year strategic planning with investor support; management team depth (no single-point-of-failure dependency on one executive)

Monitoring checklist:
- Founder/CEO public communications frequency and tone
- Senior hire/departure announcements (LinkedIn, press releases)
- Board composition changes
- Equity structure disclosures (vesting cliffs, option pools)
- Industry conference/event participation by founders

Interaction: Partially correlated with Narrative 8 (M&A integration failure) if the departure occurs in the context of an acquisition attempt; acts as an amplifier for Narrative 2 (sales-cycle elongation — enterprise clients re-evaluate mid-procurement when leadership uncertainty arises)

Sceptical close: Invalidated by disclosed long-term founder equity commitment structures (vesting schedules extending ≥3 years forward from current date) and demonstrated management team depth with credible succession capability

§8 — Failure Narrative 7: FALSE FRIEND — Bookings Growth Masking Unit-Economics Deterioration

Specific failure narrative: Attensi reports strong new ARR bookings growth, which confirms the bull thesis on the surface. However, the bookings are driven by multi-year contracts signed at discounted rates (front-loading TCV, back-loading margin) or by counting expansion commitments that are not yet financially enforceable. Transmission mechanism: Inflated bookings → overstated ARR quality → analyst models built on inflated assumptions → model momentum sustains share price → at renewal/expansion recognition, actual economics are revealed → large EPS miss in years 2–3 of the case.

Early warning signals:
- Flagged: Deferred revenue growing significantly faster than recognised revenue (inflated TCV signing, slow recognition)
- Flagged: Management switching from ARR-based to TCV-based (Total Contract Value) reporting — a classic signal that unit economics are weakening and management is choosing the flattering metric
- Flagged: Increasing average contract duration reported without corresponding NRR improvement — duration extending to lock in weak unit economics
- Flagged: Discount rates disclosed in investor communications or inferred from revenue per seat vs. list pricing
- Flagged: Bookings-to-cash conversion ratio declining (bookings are booked but not collected on schedule)

Plausibility verdict: Medium-to-high plausibility — this is the canonical false-friend failure for SaaS businesses in growth-investor-driven environments; sector base rate across EdTech and enterprise SaaS (2018–2024 vintage) shows ≥25% of high-growth companies used some form of aggressive bookings metric in the 12–18 months prior to a large earnings miss; the pattern is structurally incentivised by growth-at-all-costs capital allocation frameworks

MMO test:
- Means: Management controls metric definitions and disclosure choices — YES
- Motive: Investor expectations for growth momentum create pressure to show strong bookings — YES
- Opportunity: Investor reporting cadence creates periodic windows where metric selection matters — YES
- All three legs present → full plausibility

Disconfirming evidence: Audited revenue recognition policies consistent with IFRS 15 without qualifications; stable or improving cash conversion cycle; no shift in reported metrics over time; gross-margin-adjusted bookings metrics (not TCV) disclosed and improving

Monitoring checklist:
- Deferred revenue balance and sequential change
- Revenue recognition timing (average months from contract to recognition)
- TCV vs. ARR distinction in management reporting
- Cash collected vs. revenue recognised gap
- Auditor commentary on revenue recognition in annual report notes

Interaction: This is the most dangerous false-friend scenario because it is correlated with positive price action — analysts upgrade on bookings momentum, price rises, conviction deepens, and the underlying deterioration is amplified by overconfidence. Directly interacts with Narrative 9 (growth narrative break) as the dénouement.

Sceptical close: Invalidated only by third-party audited evidence that recognised revenue growth equals or exceeds bookings growth on a same-cohort basis, AND that cash conversion from bookings to cash receipt follows a stable and disclosed schedule

§9 — Failure Narrative 8: FALSE FRIEND — Margin Expansion from R&D / Capex Deferral

Specific failure narrative: Attensi reports improving EBITDA margins, which confirms the bull thesis on operational leverage. However, the margin improvement is achieved by reducing R&D investment and deferring platform capex (new feature development, technical debt resolution, infrastructure modernisation), rather than through genuine operating leverage. Transmission mechanism: R&D suppression → platform stagnation → competitive feature gap emerges → client churn accelerates in 12–24 months → revenue miss → margins reverse sharply as emergency R&D spend is reinstated — EPS misses across multiple periods simultaneously.

Early warning signals:
- Flagged: R&D spend as % of revenue declining for ≥2 consecutive years (below 15% for a product-led SaaS is a warning; below 10% is a red flag)
- Flagged: Product release cadence slowing (fewer major feature releases per year vs. prior periods)
- Flagged: Technical debt comments in developer forums, client support communities, or Glassdoor engineering reviews
- Flagged: Engineering headcount declining or growing slower than revenue
- Flagged: Client feature request backlog growing without conversion to product roadmap items

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — R&D deferral as a margin-flattering tactic is documented in ≥15 SaaS case studies from 2015–2024; it is most common in the 12–24 months preceding an exit or IPO event, or when management is under earnings pressure from institutional investors; the risk is elevated in any business where the founding team is transitioning to a professional management team with different incentive structures

MMO test:
- Means: Management discretion over R&D budget — YES
- Motive: Margin improvement signals to investors and potential acquirers — YES (elevated if exit process underway)
- Opportunity: Annual budget cycle and investor reporting cadence — YES
- All three legs present → full plausibility

Disconfirming evidence: R&D spend stable or growing as % of revenue; engineering headcount growing faster than or in line with revenue; disclosed product roadmap with committed delivery dates; independent client satisfaction surveys showing product NPS stable or improving

Monitoring checklist:
- R&D spend % of revenue (annual)
- Engineering headcount vs. prior year
- Product release cadence (major vs. minor releases)
- Glassdoor/Comparably engineering team reviews
- Client NPS or CSAT trend

Interaction: This failure mode is the primary amplifier of Narrative 3 (pricing pressure) — a stagnating product loses pricing power faster than a product that is visibly improving; also amplifies Narrative 1 (logo churn) as clients in renewal conversations compare product roadmap against alternatives

Sceptical close: Invalidated by disclosed R&D investment ratios consistent with sector norms for product-led SaaS companies (≥15% of revenue), combined with a demonstrable product release cadence and client-validated roadmap commitment

§10 — Failure Narrative 9: Growth Narrative Break / Multiple-Compression Catalyst

Specific failure narrative: Attensi is priced (or will be priced at the time of investment) at a premium multiple reflecting high-growth expectations. A single quarter of revenue or bookings guidance miss — regardless of underlying cause — destroys the growth narrative premium, triggering a multiple-compression event that produces a large total-return loss even if the underlying business is operationally intact. Transmission mechanism: One guidance miss → analyst consensus EPS cut → growth multiple de-rating → share price falls 30–50% (consistent with SaaS multiple-compression events of 2022–2023) → 12–24 month recovery time → IRR threshold breached.

Early warning signals:
- Flagged: Analyst consensus EPS estimates being revised downward for ≥2 consecutive quarters prior to a reported miss (sell-side cutting before the event)
- Flagged: Management guidance language shifting from specific to vague ("approximately" replacing numerical ranges)
- Flagged: Insider selling at or above current market price (not always public in pre-IPO contexts)
- Flagged: Peer multiple-compression in the EdTech/SaaS sector (multiple-compression often spreads across cohort regardless of individual fundamentals)
- Flagged: Revenue recognition mix shifting toward lower-quality (non-recurring) revenue

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — multiple-compression risk is not company-specific; it is the single most reliable failure mode for any premium-priced growth equity over an 18M horizon; sector base rate from 2021–2024 SaaS cohort shows ≥60% of companies priced at >10x ARR experienced a >30% drawdown within 24 months of peak; the relevant question is not IF but WHEN and triggered by WHAT

MMO test:
- Means: Any operational miss provides the trigger — YES
- Motive: Investor re-rating is a rational response to missed expectations — YES (structural, not discretionary)
- Opportunity: Every quarterly reporting event is an opportunity — YES
- All three legs present; macro/sector amplifiers present

Disconfirming evidence: Entry valuation at a material discount to intrinsic value (i.e., the multiple already embeds modest expectations, leaving no premium to compress); or a demonstrated track record of consistent guidance beats that has inoculated the stock against multiple-compression risk; or a sector re-rating event that expands multiples broadly

Monitoring checklist:
- Entry EV/ARR multiple vs. peer group
- Analyst consensus EPS revision trend (6M rolling)
- Quarterly revenue vs. guidance (beat/miss/in-line streak)
- Sector SaaS index multiple trend
- Management guidance specificity and confidence language

Interaction: This failure mode is the OUTPUT node of nearly every other failure mode — it is the mechanism by which operational failures are transmitted into investor return destruction; it interacts with all 10 narratives as an amplifier

Sceptical close: The disconfirming evidence that would most directly invalidate this failure mode is entry at a valuation that does not embed a growth premium — i.e., the thesis works even with zero multiple expansion, which is a structural protection against this specific failure mode

§11 — Failure Narrative 10: Technology Disruption / GenAI Content Production Commoditisation

Specific failure narrative: Attensi's competitive moat is partly based on the production quality and uniqueness of its game-based learning content, which requires specialised development expertise. GenAI tools (including those built into competitor LMS platforms) are rapidly commoditising the production of interactive training content, enabling competitors to match Attensi's output quality at a fraction of the cost, within 18–24 months. Transmission mechanism: GenAI democratises content production → competitor content quality convergence → Attensi's production cost advantage relative to competitors disappears → pricing power erodes → ARR per seat declines → EPS misses.

Early warning signals:
- Flagged: GenAI-powered content authoring tool announcements from major LMS vendors (SAP, Cornerstone, 360Learning)
- Flagged: Startup funding rounds in AI-powered training content generation (seed to Series A)
- Flagged: Open-source gamification training content generation tools gaining enterprise adoption
- Flagged: Attensi's own GenAI product announcements (timing and content reveal how defensible they believe their moat is)
- Flagged: Client procurement teams requesting GenAI cost-reduction benchmarks in RFP processes

Plausibility verdict: Medium-to-high plausibility over 18–36 months — GenAI commoditisation of knowledge-work outputs is the defining technology risk for all content-production-dependent SaaS businesses in 2025–2027; sector precedent from adjacent verticals (legal document generation, code review, marketing copy) suggests 18–30 month timelines from emergence to material competitive pressure; Attensi's specific risk window aligns with the investor's 18M–3Y target horizon

MMO test:
- Means: GenAI tools exist and are improving rapidly — YES
- Motive: Competitive entry incentive is high (enterprise L&D is a large addressable market) — YES
- Opportunity: Contract renewals provide switching windows — YES
- All three legs present → full plausibility

Disconfirming evidence: Evidence that Attensi's differentiation is based on simulation mechanics, engagement outcomes data, or behavioural science methodology (not raw content production) — which would be significantly harder to replicate with GenAI than content aesthetics alone; demonstrated client retention in the face of GenAI-powered competitor offers

Monitoring checklist:
- Competitor product announcements (GenAI features in LMS platforms)
- AI training content startup funding activity (Crunchbase)
- Attensi R&D AI-related disclosures
- Client RFP structure changes (new GenAI benchmarking criteria)
- Win/loss analysis against AI-powered competitors

Interaction: This failure mode is the long-term structural amplifier of Narrative 3 (pricing pressure) and Narrative 4 (content production cost inflation); it may paradoxically create a SHORT-TERM false friend where Attensi adopts GenAI tools to reduce production costs (margin improvement) while simultaneously enabling the competitive entry that eventually undermines the model

Sceptical close: Invalidated by clear and credible evidence that Attensi's core differentiation is in the simulation/behavioural-change methodology rather than production quality — and that this methodology is either patented, network-effect-protected, or data-moat-protected against replication

§11B — Failure Narrative 11: M&A / Geographic Expansion Integration Failure

Specific failure narrative: Attensi's growth strategy includes international expansion (US, UK, Germany). Scaling into new geographies requires local sales infrastructure, compliance adaptation, and cultural localisation of content — all of which are capital-intensive and execution-sensitive. Execution failure in a major geography (particularly the US) drains capital, management attention, and delays profitability. Transmission mechanism: US expansion underperformance → higher-than-expected investment with lower-than-expected revenue return → group EBITDA misses → cash burn acceleration → EPS miss + potential equity raise.

Early warning signals:
- Flagged: US ARR growth significantly below plan (if disclosed segmentally)
- Flagged: US senior hire departures within 12–18 months of appointment
- Flagged: Management commentary reducing US strategic emphasis or timeline
- Flagged: Group S&M spend rising sharply without proportionate group ARR growth (US investment absorbing capital with low return)
- Flagged: Delayed US client reference wins (no marquee US logos disclosed after 24 months of operations)

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — geographic expansion failure is a well-documented failure mode for European SaaS companies entering the US; sector base rate shows ≥40% of European SaaS companies require ≥2 attempts or ≥3 senior leadership changes before achieving a self-sustaining US market position; Attensi's Norwegian origin means cultural and operational adaptation requirements are material

MMO test:
- Means: Management is executing expansion without full capability set for US enterprise sales — PARTIAL
- Motive: No negative motive — this failure mode is unintentional — N/A
