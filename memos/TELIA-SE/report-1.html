§1 — General

Telia Company AB is a Stockholm-headquartered telecommunications group, listed on Nasdaq Stockholm (TELIA) and Nasdaq Helsinki, serving approximately 21 million customers across the Nordic and Baltic regions.

Description: Telia is an integrated telco — fixed broadband, mobile, TV/media, and B2B IT/cloud services — with operations in Sweden, Finland, Norway, Denmark, Estonia, Latvia, and Lithuania [#I]
- Sub: Sweden (~50% of revenue) is the home market; Finland (~20%), Norway (~12%), and Denmark (~8%) are the other major markets; Baltics (~10%) are the fastest-growing segment
- Sub: The company previously had Eurasian operations (Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, Azerbaijan, Georgia) and a majority stake in Turkcell (Turkey) — both fully exited by 2021-23, removing the most volatile earnings sources

Strategic objective: "Better Telia" programme (launched H2 2023) targets cost savings of SEK 2.5bn by end-2026 vs 2022 base, leveraging network automation, workforce reduction (~3,000 FTE target over 2023-25), and B2B digitalisation [#I]
- Sub: Secondary objective is selective fibre FTTH densification in Sweden and Baltics, monetising infrastructure through both retail and wholesale channels
- Sub: Capital discipline is explicitly stated — management targets net debt/EBITDA of 2.0-2.5x (currently approximately 2.3x FY24)

Organisation: Telia operates via five reporting segments — Sweden, Finland, Norway, Denmark, and Baltics — plus a central Telia Carrier unit (international wholesale) and a Media/TV unit (embedded within Sweden) [#I]
- Sub: Each country segment has its own P&L accountability; B2B and consumer sub-channels are embedded within country segments rather than reported as separate global lines
- Sub: Headquarters in Stockholm; listed workforce approximately 17,000 FTE as of FY24, down from ~22,000 in FY20 (post-Eurasia exits + Better Telia headcount reductions)

Listing: TELIA on Nasdaq Stockholm; reporting currency SEK; also cross-listed on Nasdaq Helsinki [#I]
- Sub: Current share price approximately SEK 31-33 (range Q1-Q2 2026); 52-week range approximately SEK 28-36
- Sub: Market cap approximately SEK 135-145bn (€12-13bn) at current price; EV approximately SEK 220-230bn including net debt of ~SEK 80-85bn

§2 — Company Details

Revenue source: Core revenue lines are mobile services (~45% of group), fixed broadband/FTTH (~20%), TV/media (~8%), B2B IT/cloud/UCaaS (~15%), and wholesale/carrier (~7%) [#I]
- Sub: Mobile ARPU trends have been flat-to-modestly-growing in Sweden (SEK ~210-220/month) and Finland, but declining in Norway (intense competition from Telenor and ice.net) and Denmark
- Sub: B2B IT/cloud (Telia Cygate, Telia ACE) is the highest-growth segment — organic revenue +5-8% in FY23-24 — and the primary margin-expansion narrative

Predictability: Subscription base + long-term contracts (B2B typically 2-3 year terms) make ~85% of revenue highly predictable quarter-to-quarter; consensus FY25 revenue variance ±1.5% reflects this [#A]

Driver count: Revenue is driven by: (1) mobile subscriber count, (2) ARPU per subscriber, (3) fixed broadband net adds, (4) B2B contract wins/churn, (5) FTTH wholesale take-up, (6) TV/media subscriber base — six primary drivers, which is typical for an integrated incumbent [#A]
- Sub: The most watch-worthy driver is B2B ARPU (enterprise digital transformation revenue) — this is the growth lever management has most optionality on
- Sub: FTTH wholesale (selling access to OTA/MVNO players on Telia's own FTTH network) is an emerging driver in Sweden — currently contributing ~2-3% of Sweden revenue but growing rapidly

Competitive advantage: Telia's primary moat is network infrastructure (spectrum + fibre assets) — high capex barriers to entry in any given national market [#A]
- Sub: Brand recognition in Sweden and Finland is strong (NPS scores ~45-50 per company disclosure, above Swedish telco median of ~35-40)
- Sub: Customer churn (consumer mobile) approximately 12-14% annually — slightly above Elisa Finland (~9%) but below the Norwegian market average (~18%), consistent with mid-tier moat

Switching cost: Mobile consumer switching costs are moderate (number portability commoditises) — stickiness derives more from bundle packaging (mobile + broadband + TV) than technology lock-in [#A]
- Sub: B2B IT/cloud services have higher switching costs — enterprise infrastructure migrations are 12-24 month processes; Telia Cygate (Sweden's largest B2B IT integrator) benefits from this stickiness
- Sub: Bundle penetration (customers with 2+ services from Telia) is approximately 35-40% of Swedish consumer base — a meaningful loyalty driver

Secular trend: 5G adoption (+smartphone upgrade cycle), fibre-to-the-home densification, cloud/hybrid-IT enterprise migration, and streaming-driven data consumption are the four primary secular demand tailwinds [#A]
- Sub: B2B digital transformation (cloud, SD-WAN, cybersecurity) is the most monetisable trend for Telia given Cygate/ACE capabilities — management estimates addressable B2B IT market in the Nordics at ~SEK 150bn, of which Telia holds <10% share
- Sub: Fibre-over-copper replacement is both capex burden (build cost) and revenue opportunity (higher ARPU for FTTH subscribers vs legacy copper/DSL)

Operating leverage: Network-based telcos have high fixed-cost structures — ~60% of costs are fixed (network operations, spectrum fees, D&A) — providing meaningful operating leverage once revenue grows above maintenance capex recovery threshold [#A]
- Sub: Management's "Better Telia" savings programme targets SEK 2.5bn of annualised savings by 2026 — approximately 7-8% of current opex base; if achieved, incremental EBITDA margin accretion of ~2.7pp
- Sub: Precedent: Elisa (Finland) achieved 5pp EBITDA margin improvement over 2010-2020 through similar efficiency programmes combined with ARPU growth — cited by Berenberg as the Nordic best-practice comps case [AS-Berenberg]

- Delivery: B2B IT segment revenue +6.1% organically in FY2023 and +7.3% in FY2024 — the only segment growing materially above group organic rate [#A]
- Delivery: Net debt/EBITDA reduced from 2.6x (FY2022) to 2.5x (FY2024) — modest deleveraging given capex investment cycle; management target of 2.0-2.5x is effectively at the top-end boundary [#A]

- Internal change: Telia Carrier (international wholesale backbone) sold to Polhem Infra for SEK 4.5bn in December 2021; Telia retained a 40% stake in the entity — retains strategic network access without full ownership burden [#I]

Sceptical counter-read (§2): The "Better Telia" narrative is a cost-efficiency story with low-single-digit revenue growth as the ceiling — not a revenue-inflection story. B2B IT growth of +7% on a small base (SEK ~14bn) adds ~SEK 1bn incremental revenue pa, which is easily offset by Norway/Denmark consumer ARPU compression. The bundle-penetration "moat" story is common to every European incumbent and has not historically translated into durable pricing power vs well-capitalised challengers (Telenor, Iliad, Hutchison); switch-cost depth in consumer mobile is low

§3 — Industry Context

Net signal: Nordic telco is a structurally mature 3-4 player oligopoly in each national market, with rational pricing in Sweden/Finland but intensifying competition in Norway/Denmark; FTTH capex cycle is a sector-wide burden compressing near-term FCF across all incumbents [#J]

Market structure: Each Nordic market operates with 3-4 licensed mobile network operators; consolidated fixed-broadband infrastructure is increasingly shared or wholesale [#A]
- Sub: Sweden: Telia (~34%), Telenor (~22%), Tele2 (~21%), Three/Hutchison (~10%) — top 4 = ~87% consumer mobile postpaid market
- Sub: Finland: Telia (~35%), DNA/Telenor (~27%), Elisa (~31%) — effectively a three-player duopoly-plus, highly rational pricing
- Sub: Norway: Telenor (~46%), Telia (~25%), ice.net (~15%), Others (~14%) — ice.net's rapid growth (from zero in 2016) is the key structural disruption; has gained ~2pp share pa for five consecutive years
- Sub: Denmark: TDC/Nuuday (~37%), Telia (~20%), Telenor (~18%), Norlys (~15%) — four-player with an aggressive low-price challenger (Norlys, cooperative structure, no capital market pressure)

Competitive intensity: Sweden and Finland are "disciplined oligopolies" — ARPU trends flat-to-modestly-positive, minimal irrational price wars; Norway is the exception with ice.net pursuing market-share-over-ROCE strategy ❌ [#A]
- Sub: Berenberg notes that Nordic telco pricing discipline is structurally better than Southern European markets (France, Italy, Spain) where Iliad has historically attacked; this is a genuine relative quality signal vs broader European telco sector [AS-Berenberg]
- Sub: Citi's Nordic telco framework (2024) categorises Sweden and Finland as "mature-rational" and Norway as "structurally competitive" — same language used to describe French market in 2013-17 before Iliad fatigue [AS-Citi]

New entrant risk: ice.net (Norway) is the most live new-entrant threat; no credible new greenfield MNO entrant in Sweden or Finland given spectrum costs and passive infrastructure sharing [#A]
- Sub: MVNO segment (Vimla, Hallon, Comviq — all Telia-owned MVNOs in Sweden) shows that Telia has proactively occupied the low-price tier with own sub-brands, limiting greenfield low-cost attacker opportunity
- Sub: Fiber network challengers (e.g. Öppen Fiber, local cooperative FTTHs in Sweden) are taking municipal share in rural/semi-rural Sweden — a persistent structural headwind on fixed broadband wholesale revenue

Disruption: OTT voice/messaging (WhatsApp, Teams) has largely disrupted voice ARPU already (priced into current low ARPU structure); next disruption wave is AI-driven network automation reducing opex, and private 5G networks potentially displacing enterprise mobile connectivity revenue [#A]
- Sub: Private 5G networks (Ericsson, Nokia direct enterprise sales) could bypass carrier services — the primary disruption risk to Telia's B2B wireless revenue; currently sub-5% of enterprise mobile, but growing
- Sub: Starlink/satellite broadband is a rural fixed-broadband threat; Sweden has significant rural broadband underserved markets where Telia currently has limited competition — this is a 3-5 year risk horizon

BtE (barriers to entry): Spectrum licences, passive infrastructure (towers, ducts, exchanges), FTTH civil works, regulatory licensing, and brand trust are all significant barriers; capital requirement to replicate Telia's Swedish network estimated at ~SEK 80-100bn [#A]
- Sub: Towerco separation trend (e.g. Vantage Towers, Cellnex) has modestly reduced BtE on the passive side — MNOs can now lease towers without building, lowering greenfield capex for new entrants; however, spectrum remains the binding constraint
- Sub: Regulatory BtE: BEREC (European Electronic Communications Code) mandates open-access wholesale obligations on dominant operators (Telia qualifies in Sweden, Finland, Baltics) — this partially neutralises Telia's infrastructure moat by forcing wholesale supply to competitors

Macro shift: Inflation (2022-24) created cost pressure on network energy costs (+25-30% in FY2022) and labour; energy costs have partially moderated but remain above pre-2022 levels [#A]
- Sub: SEK/EUR exchange rate matters for handset procurement (USD-denominated) and for translating Finnish/Baltic revenues back to SEK; SEK weakness in FY2022-23 was a headwind on cost; more stable in 2024-25
- Sub: Swedish housing market weakness (2022-24) temporarily slowed FTTH adoption in new-build developments — a modest headwind to Swedish fixed broadband net adds

Regulatory shift: EU Connectivity Toolbox (2023) and updated EECC implementation have mandated network access obligations but also provided partial regulatory relief on copper retirement timelines, allowing faster DSL/copper cutover to FTTH [#A]
- Sub: Swedish PTS (Post and Telecom Authority) annual review of regulated wholesale access prices — currently in a "stable" phase; risk of PTS imposing price cuts on Telia's wholesale copper/fibre access would be a direct margin headwind
- Sub: Baltic regulatory environment is more permissive — Estonian and Latvian regulators have allowed Telia to price FTTH wholesale above copper equivalency, supporting investment economics

FTTH sector capex cycle: All major Nordic incumbents are in parallel FTTH build cycles — Telia, Elisa, Telenor, TDC all investing >15% of revenue in network through 2026; this is suppressing sector-wide FCF simultaneously [#A]
- Sub: JP Morgan's 2024 European telco capex analysis estimates Nordic FTTH cycle peak in 2024-25, with capex normalisation expected from 2026; if correct, sector-wide FCF inflection is a 12-18 month horizon event [AS-JP Morgan]
- Sub: Risk: FTTH take-up rates in Sweden (~62% of passed homes activated, per company disclosure) lag UK (~80%) and Netherlands (~75%) — underperformance on activation rates could extend the capex-intensive phase and delay FCF normalisation

Value chain dynamics:
- Supplier returns: Primary suppliers are Ericsson and Nokia (network equipment) — both have pricing power given global oligopoly (Ericsson + Nokia + Huawei = ~80% global RAN market); Telia's multi-vendor strategy (primarily Ericsson in Sweden, Nokia in Baltics) provides modest negotiating leverage [#A]
- Distributor returns: B2C distribution is largely direct (Telia stores, online) or via retailer (Elgiganten, Mediamarkt) — retail distribution take ~8-12% gross margin on handsets; no material distributor margin dependency for service revenue [#A]
- Customer returns: Consumer customers have weak bargaining power individually; business customers have moderate-to-strong (enterprise >SEK 10m/year contracts have genuine competitive tension) [#A]

Sceptical counter-read (§3): The "Nordic rational oligopoly" framing is empirically valid today but was equally valid for Norwegian market in 2015-18 before ice.net's aggressive build; the cooperative-ownership structure of Norlys (Denmark) and ice.net removes capital-market discipline and creates structurally irrational pricing threats; a second-generation low-price attacker in Sweden or Finland cannot be ruled out — especially post 5G spectrum auction where new entrants received spectrum blocks; FTTH build by non-telco actors (Öppen Fiber, municipal networks) is gradually eroding Telia's last-mile infrastructure moat in fixed broadband

§4 — BB#2 Foundation Quality

Net signal: Telia's foundation quality is below the BB#2 ideal profile across most dimensions — operator is improving but not "great," moat is moderate (infrastructure BtE, network incumbency) rather than deep (pricing power, switching cost), and financial returns are sub-cost-of-capital; the improvement story is real but starting from a low base [#J]

§4.1 — Operator

Strategic ambition: Ambition is "best Nordic telco" (operational efficiency, #1 or #2 in each national market, B2B digital leader) — modest rather than expansionary; no articulated global or adjacency growth ambition [#I]
- Sub: Strategy is congruent with ambition: "Better Telia" cost-efficiency is coherent with a mature, low-growth market; B2B IT push is coherent with enterprise digital migration secular trend
- Sub: Concern: the ambition ceiling is structurally capped by market maturity — management has not articulated a revenue growth vector that could plausibly deliver 12-20% annual EPS growth; the strategy optimises the existing asset base rather than expanding the opportunity set [#J]

CEO — Patrik Hofbauer: Internal promotion from Telia Sweden CEO (2019-2022) to Group CEO (Jan 2023); knows the Swedish network and operating model deeply; credible operational manager [#I]
- Sub: Hofbauer's track record in Sweden: EBITDA margin expanded ~2pp at Telia Sweden under his tenure; NPS improved from 38 to 48; "Better Telia" programme concept originated in his Sweden P&L experience
- Sub: Not an "animal CEO" archetype — no history of transformational M&A, aggressive capital reallocation, or paradigm-shifting strategic pivots; compare to Allison Kirkby (BT Group CEO) who has a more activist strategic profile, or Elisa's CEO Veli-Matti Mattila who drove Elisa's Finnish margin leadership over 10 years [AS-Berenberg, AS-Goldman Sachs]

Leadership team: CFO Per Christian Mowinckel (appointed Q3 2023) has a cost-discipline and working capital background — appropriate for the current efficiency-programme phase; country heads are experienced local operators [#I]
- Sub: Resourcing: workforce reduction of ~2,500 FTE completed through FY2024 (out of 3,000 target); this is on-track and operationally executed without significant service disruption — a positive execution data point
- Sub: Culture: described by Goldman Sachs as "utility-operator culture" — competent, process-driven, not known for speed-of-innovation; this is consistent with Nordic telco archetype and not necessarily negative for a turnaround/efficiency story [AS-Goldman Sachs]

Guidance posture: Management has guided for SEK 2.5bn cost savings by 2026 (confirmed FY2024); EBITDA margin guidance of "above 37%" for FY2025; capex guidance of SEK 17-18bn for FY2025 — all within historical guidance accuracy ±5% [#I]
- Sub: Guidance record (FY2022-FY2024): all EBITDA guidance ranges met; capex guidance consistently in-range; no material guidance downgrades — this is a positive signal, contrasting with BT Group's repeated capex guidance changes or Vodafone's restructuring reversals
- Sub: Communication clarity: quarterly earnings calls are clear and structured; Hofbauer provides segment-by-segment commentary; does not over-promise; SS analysts (Berenberg, SEB Equities, Carnegie) consistently describe guidance as "conservative-to-realistic" [AS-Berenberg, AS-SEB Equities]

Track record — capital allocation: Prioritised deleveraging and network investment over shareholder returns; sold non-core assets (Eurasia, Telia Carrier backbone) at reasonable multiples; maintained SEK 2.00 dividend (no cuts) [#I]
- Sub: Share issuance: no material dilutive issuance since 2015; shares outstanding stable at ~4.32bn; no buyback programme active — Swedish state ownership (~37%) makes buybacks structurally constrained [#A]
- Sub: RONIC on capex: 5G and FTTH capex RONIC is opaque but management targets >8% IRR on fibre investments (stated CMD 2023); this is below the BB#2 25-30%+ threshold and even below Telia's WACC of ~7-8% on a risk-adjusted basis ❌ [#J]

Governance: Swedish state ownership at 37.3% creates a governance overhang — state shareholders have historically prioritised employment preservation and universal service over shareholder value optimisation [#A]
- Sub: Board composition is independent-majority with standard Nordic governance standards (Investor AB model); no activist pressure visible as of Q1 2026
- Sub: ESG: Telia has a credible net-zero 2040 commitment (Scope 1+2 by 2030), consistent with Nordic telco sector norms; no material governance controversies post the FCPA/anti-bribery settlement (Uzbekistan, 2017, SEK 14.5bn fine) which is now fully resolved

Sceptical counter-read (§4.1): ESA depth — operator quality assessment requires deeper analysis of CEO historical return on capital decisions, culture vs peer benchmarks, and governance alignment with minority shareholders at Q8 depth

§4.2 — Advantaged Business + SRCA

MOAT — customer moats: Network incumbency + bundle bundling + brand trust generate moderate customer stickiness; annual mobile churn ~12-14% is mid-range — not weak but not a top-quartile lock-in moat [#A]
- Sub: B2B IT (Cygate, ACE) has higher switching-cost moats — multi-year managed services contracts, embedded IT infrastructure; this is Telia's strongest customer moat sub-segment
- Sub: Consumer broadband has moderate lock-in — early termination fees (typically 3-month notice) and service quality dependency provide stickiness but not proprietary lock-in

MOAT — production moats: Physical network infrastructure (fibre ducts, spectrum, exchange infrastructure, tower sites) constitutes a genuine production moat — the civil works cost to replicate Telia's Swedish FTTH + mobile network is prohibitive for any new entrant [#A]
- Sub: Spectrum holdings (700MHz, 900MHz, 1800MHz, 3.5GHz bands in Sweden) are licenced through 2030-2034 — medium-term certainty; however, spectrum licences are periodically re-auctioned with no incumbency guarantee
- Sub: FTTH duct sharing agreements with municipalities reduce Telia's own build-cost but also slightly reduce the exclusivity of the production moat

MOAT — scale advantage: Telia is the largest telco by revenue and subscriber base in Sweden and Finland; scale enables lower per-unit network costs, preferred supplier pricing, and national advertising efficiency [#A]
- Sub: Scale economics in mobile: Telia's 5G network utilisation (customers per MHz of spectrum) is industry-leading in Sweden, driving lower cost-per-GB vs smaller competitors; this translates to modest but real cost advantage
- Sub: Scale matters less than in manufacturing sectors — the floor on network build cost is largely independent of scale once past a threshold utilisation point

MOAT — regulatory moat: Regulatory licences (spectrum, number ranges, broadcasting rights) create barriers; however, regulatory obligations (wholesale access mandates) partially offset the moat by forcing competitor access to infrastructure ❌ [#A]
- Sub: In Sweden and Finland, Telia is designated as having Significant Market Power — this triggers wholesale access obligations on both fixed and mobile networks, limiting the ability to use network advantage as a competitive weapon
- Sub: Baltic regulatory environment is relatively less burdensome on SMP obligations — Telia's Baltic moat is structurally stronger than its Swedish moat on a regulatory-net basis

MOAT — relative scale + market position: Sweden #1 (34% mobile postpaid share), Finland #1-tied with Elisa (~35%), Estonia #1, Latvia #1, Lithuania #1 — market leadership in four of seven operating countries [#A]
- Sub: Norway #2 (~25%) and Denmark #2 (~20%) — not #1 positions, with competitive pressure from Telenor (Norway) and TDC/Nuuday (Denmark) in those markets
- Sub: High relative scale vs Nordic peers: Telia is 2.5x the revenue of Elisa (nearest Finnish peer at ~€1.9bn) and comparable to Telenor (~SEK 90bn) at the group level

MOAT — TAM: Nordic addressable telecom market (~SEK 200bn total including B2B IT) is not large on a global scale; Telia's current ~45% share of pure telco but <10% of B2B IT services implies meaningful TAM expansion potential in the IT/cloud space [#A]
- Sub: B2B IT Nordic addressable market is approximately SEK 150bn — growing ~5-7% pa; Telia holds ~SEK 14-15bn (~10% share); organic expansion without M&A is realistic to SEK 20-25bn over 5 years
- Sub: This is not a "large realistic TAM" in the BB#2 sense — the 12-20% growth facilitation that a large TAM enables is structurally absent; total addressable telco+IT market is capped and competitive

OPERATE (high-level, ESA depth Q8): Service Cost per Unit trends are being reduced via "Better Telia" automation; Relative Service Cost per Unit vs Elisa is negative (Elisa is the Nordic cost-efficiency benchmark) — pricing power is limited in consumer markets but positive in B2B IT [#A]
- Sub: ESA depth required: detailed SCU decomposition, RSCU vs Elisa/Swisscom, and granular pricing-power analysis belong in Q8

GENERATE — EPS and revenue growth: Organic revenue growth 1-3% annually (FY22-24) — well below BB#2 12-20% threshold; adjusted EPS growth approximately 4-6% (benefiting from margin expansion and modest deleveraging) ❌ [#J]
- Sub: Consensus FY25-27E EPS CAGR approximately 5-7% (per SEB Equities, Carnegie, Berenberg estimates) — structurally below BB#2 ideal profile
- Sub: Revenue growth is organic-only (no M&A tailwind in pipeline as of Q1 2026); the only credible above-market growth lever is B2B IT acceleration

GENERATE — capex deployment at RONIC: SEK 17-18bn capex pa with management-stated IRR target of >8% on FTTH — this falls well short of BB#2 25-30%+ RONIC threshold ❌ [#J]
- Sub: Contrast with Elisa (Finland): new FTTH subscriber incremental RONIC estimated at 18-22% per Berenberg (2024 sector review) — Elisa's superior fibre economics reflect higher take-up rates and lower civil costs in urban Finland vs Sweden [AS-Berenberg]

GENERATE — FCF conversion and margins:
- FCF conversion: Reported net income to equity FCF conversion approximately 100% (net income ~SEK 5bn, equity FCF ~SEK 5-5.5bn in FY2024) — this appears strong but is flattered by working capital management; unlevered FCF to EBITDA conversion ~37%, below sector median ~45% due to elevated capex ❌ [#A]
- Gross margin: ~65% — consistent with European incumbent median; not a distinguishing signal either positive or negative
- EBIT margin: ~12% (FY2024) — below BB#2 "solid" threshold of 20% for highly asset-intensive businesses ❌
- EBITDA margin: ~37% — mid-range for European incumbents; below Swisscom (~42%), in-line with Orange, above Proximus

GENERATE — returns:
- ROCE: ~7-8% (FY2024) — below BB#2 15%+ threshold and approximately at WACC; returns are not value-destructive but are not value-creating either ❌
- ROTC: ~10-12% — below BB#2 30%+ threshold ❌
- ROIC: ~5-6% — below sector median of Elisa (~14%), Swisscom (~12%)

GENERATE — tax: Effective tax rate approximately 24-26% (FY22-24) — in-line with Nordic corporate tax rates; no material tax leakage or optimization; broadly neutral [#A]

Operating leverage: Fixed-cost heavy structure (network D&A, spectrum fees) means incremental revenue converts at ~55-60% EBITDA margin — positive operating leverage signal, but requires revenue growth to crystallise [#A]
- Sub: "Better Telia" savings are effectively an engineering of operating leverage without revenue growth — substituting cost efficiency for top-line leverage; valid strategy but lower multiple-expansion value than genuine revenue-driven leverage

Gravitational pull (high-level, ESA Q8): The mechanical force working for Telia is the FTTH capex cycle completion — as fibre overbuild concludes (~2026-27), capex naturally declines and FCF inflects upward without requiring heroic operational change; this is a genuine gravitational improvement [#A]
- Sub: ESA depth required — full SRCA improvement analysis belongs in Q8

Improvement scope (high-level, ESA Q8): Peer precedent exists: Elisa's EBITDA margin trajectory (30% in 2010 → 38% by 2020) and Swisscom's FCF improvement post FTTH cycle suggest 3-5pp margin headroom for Telia if execution holds; peer precedent supports the bull thesis [#A]
- Sub: ESA depth required — detailed improvement scope modelling belongs in Q8

Sceptical counter-read (§4.2): Telia fails every quantitative BB#2 GENERATE threshold except gross margin and tax rate; the moats are infrastructure-incumbency moats that are gradually being eroded by wholesale access regulation and FTTH build-by-others; the "gravitational" capex normalisation story requires regulatory stability and FTTH take-up assumptions that may not materialise; RONIC on new capex is sub-WACC, meaning every incremental investment destroys shareholder value in expectation — management's articulation of >8% IRR on fibre is itself below the cost of capital threshold

§4.3 — Value Chain

Customer demand: Demand for mobile and broadband services is near-inelastic in the short term (essential service); enterprise IT/cloud demand is structurally growing at 5-8% pa in the Nordics [#A]
- Sub: Route to market: direct (Telia-owned retail stores + online) for consumer ~60% of new acquisitions; indirect (retailers, MVNO channels) ~40%; B2B predominantly direct sales force (Cygate, ACE enterprise sales)
- Sub: Wholesale demand (access seekers: Bahnhof, Vimla, etc.) is growing as FTTH overbuild accelerates — Telia has a wholesale revenue stream growing ~5-10% pa from third-party access seekers on its Swedish fibre network

Existing competition: See §3 — 3-4 player national markets; competition is rational in Sweden/Finland, irrational in Norway/Denmark; B2B IT faces competition from Atea, Dustin, HP/Dell direct, and hyperscalers (AWS, Azure, GCP) [#A]
- Sub: Hyperscaler competition is the most underrated risk in B2B IT — AWS Direct Connect and Azure ExpressRoute are displacing traditional WAN/MPLS managed services that Telia Carrier and Cygate sell; this is a slow structural erosion

Adjacencies entering: Hyperscalers (AWS, Microsoft, Google) entering enterprise connectivity and networking; potential entry by tech companies into mobile MVNO (Amazon/Aldi/MVNO models) — both real but neither imminent in Nordic scale [#A]
- Sub: Tele2 (Swedish peer) has progressively taken B2B IT share from Telia over 2020-24 — an adjacency expansion from pure telco into IT services that mirrors Telia's own strategy; creates direct competitive tension in the Swedish B2B market [AS-Carnegie]

Disruption / regulatory / supplier (ESA depth Q8): Full Disruption, Political/Regulatory SWOT, and Supplier SWOT analysis belongs in Q8 — IG-depth noted in §3 [cross-ref §3] [#I]

§4.4 — Industry Structure

Industry rank: Telia is #1 in Sweden, #1-tied in Finland, #1 in Estonia/Latvia/Lithuania, #2 in Norway and Denmark — #1 or #2 in all seven operating markets [#A]
- Sub: Sweden and Finland (71% of group revenue) are the markets where Telia's market leadership is most durable and most profitable; #1 position in both is a genuine structural advantage

Top 5 share: In Sweden, top 4 operators = ~87% consumer mobile postpaid market; FTTH infrastructure market top 3 (Telia, Tele2, Ownit) = ~75%; concentrated markets by European standards [#A]
- Sub: BB#2 CQ55 (top 5 share >30%) — met comfortably in all national markets; Finnish market (Telia + DNA + Elisa = ~95%) is exceptionally concentrated

Distribution curve: Swedish and Finnish mobile markets exhibit a steep distribution curve — top 3 operators collectively hold ~5x the combined market share of operators ranked 4-10 (MVNO tier) [#A]
- Sub: Norwegian market is flattening — ice.net's growth has compressed the gap between #2 (Telia, ~25%) and #3 (ice.net, ~15%); this is a negative structural trend for Telia in Norway

Competitor attitudes (summary): Swedish and Finnish markets exhibit benign competitor behaviour — price increases (ARPU +2-3% in Sweden FY2024) without triggering retaliatory price wars; volume growth is not prioritised over ROCE in these markets [#A]
- Sub: Norway and Denmark are the exceptions — ice.net and Norlys behave as volume-over-ROCE operators; this is structurally corrosive to market-level MOLs in those two geographies
- Sub: ESA depth required — full competitor attitude analysis, MOL stability, shark attack risk, and competitiveness trend modelling belongs in Q8

Competitiveness trends: Swedish and Finnish markets have been stable-to-improving (pricing recovery post-COVID discounting); Norwegian and Danish markets have been deteriorating on a 2-year view ❌ [#J]
- Sub: Net structural trend: concentration is holding in core markets but is at risk of disruption in Norway/Denmark — these markets combined represent ~20% of Telia's revenue, limiting but non-trivial impact

§4.5 — LT Growth + Paradigm Fit

Volume secular: Underlying demand for data, broadband, and cloud/IT services is growing 5-8% pa in volume terms across the Nordic/Baltic region; however, telco ARPU compression (pricing per unit declining as data allowances expand) means revenue growth is structurally capped at 1-3% pa absent pricing power recovery [#A]
- Sub: B2B IT and cloud adjacencies offer a genuine volume secular growth lever; the question is whether Telia can capture enough market share to offset core telco revenue pressure
- Sub: FTTH-driven ARPU uplift (+15-20% premium vs copper) is a real but one-time structural revenue step-up; once copper migration is complete, the ARPU premium normalises

GDP+ historical growth: Telia's organic revenue CAGR (FY2022-24) of ~1.7% is approximately GDP-rate growth — below GDP+ and far below BB#2 ideal (12-20%); not a GDP+ compounder [#A]
- Sub: ESA depth + Q23 thematic context required for full LT growth and paradigm-fit analysis

Paradigm fit: Telia does not fit the "compounding high-ROIC growth" paradigm that BB#2 idealises; it fits a "yield + recovery" or "quality turnaround" paradigm — a lower-multiple, income-oriented investment with moderate capital appreciation potential [#J]
- Sub: Cross-ref Q23 thematic for paradigm-fit scoring — not a primary BB#2 compounder candidate at current trajectory

Sceptical counter-read (§4): Telia's IG-depth foundation quality analysis flags at least five BB#2 GENERATE threshold failures, a below-WACC RONIC on incremental capex, and no identifiable mechanism to reach 12-20% EPS growth — the business is a low-growth, capital-heavy telco incumbency undergoing a cost efficiency programme; the "improvement story" requires simultaneous execution of cost savings, FTTH capex normalisation, B2B IT share gains, and Nordic pricing recovery; the probability of all four occurring within a 3-year window is low; any ESA-depth investigation should prioritise Q8 to test whether the improvement scope precedents (Elisa, Swisscom) are genuinely applicable or flattered by different market structures

§5 — Future

Net signal: Forward outlook is moderately predictable on costs (Better Telia tracking), less predictable on revenue (Norway/Denmark competitive risk); consensus EPS revision direction is mildly positive but not inflecting; 12-18 month horizon has a FCF inflection catalyst (capex normalisation) which is the primary reason to look more carefully [#J]

Predictability: "Better Telia" cost savings are on-track and quantifiable — high predictability; revenue side is less predictable, particularly in Norway/Denmark where competitive dynamics can shift quarterly [#A]
- Sub: Swedish FTTH take-up rate is the single most watched leading indicator for FCF inflection timing — current 62% activation rate vs 75% target; each 5pp improvement adds ~SEK 500m EBITDA via higher ARPU and lower per-subscriber cost
- Sub: Consensus FY25-27E revenue CAGR of 1.5-2.5% (SEB, Berenberg, Carnegie) has low variance — this is a low-ambiguity forecast, which is itself a feature (predictable, not exciting)

Risk — capex cycle: If FTTH take-up underperforms (activation rate stalls below 65%), capex normalisation is pushed beyond FY2026, delaying the FCF inflection by 12-24 months [#A]
- Sub: Risk: Ericsson and Nokia supply chain normalisation post-2022 disruption has improved, but any re-tightening of 5G equipment supply could re-spike network capex
- Sub: Risk: Spectrum re-auction cycle (Swedish 3.5GHz reallocation, 2025-2026) could require SEK 3-5bn incremental spectrum investment — a one-off FCF headwind

Risk — competitive: Norway is the primary competitive risk — ice.net gaining 15%+ market share has been managed so far; any acceleration above 20% would force Telia Norway into a price response, pressuring group margins [#A]
- Sub: Denmark: TDC/Nuuday's infrastructure consolidation could either create a stronger #1 (pressure on Telia) or trigger state-backed wholesale access reform (a regulatory wildcard)
- Sub: Swedish B2B IT: Tele2 and Atea are both aggressively taking share in managed IT services — Telia Cygate's competitive position warrants closer monitoring at ESA depth

Technicals (approximate, L12M): TELIA-SE share price range approximately SEK 28-36 over the past 12 months; 200-day MA approximately SEK 32; 50-day MA approximately SEK 31-32; currently trading roughly in-line with both MAs — no strong technical momentum signal [#A]
