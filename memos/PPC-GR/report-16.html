KEY FINDINGS - Mixed-to-positive overall operator signal: CEO Giorgos Stassis is a credible turnaround operator with a demonstrated track record of financial restructuring, privatisation execution, and strategic reorientation — but his background is corporate-finance/advisory rather than engineering/operations, raising questions about deep technical credibility on a capital-heavy grid-transformation programme

  • Mixed CFO signal: Konstantinos Mavros brings financial discipline and has shepherded a significant balance-sheet repair; disclosure quality has improved materially post-privatisation, but guidance cadence on the RES buildout remains imprecise and capex forecasts have drifted repeatedly
  • Structural governance overhang is the single largest risk: the Greek State retains ~34% shareholding via the Hellenic Republic Asset Development Fund (HRADF/TAIPED) and exercises meaningful influence over strategic direction, tariff policy, and board composition — independence is structurally constrained, not just cosmetically so
  • Board composition is partially captured: a meaningful minority of board seats are effectively state nominees; independent director quality is uneven; tenure diversity is thin at the top
  • Compensation structure is broadly aligned in direction (equity-linked, performance-conditioned) but absolute quantum is modest by European utility standards — risk is under-incentivisation of top talent rather than rent extraction
  • No material audit or restatement red flags detected; auditor (PricewaterhouseCoopers) has been stable; no SEC-equivalent regulatory proceedings
  • Culture indicators are mixed-to-weak: Glassdoor data reflects a legacy public-utility culture undergoing sometimes-painful transformation; CEO approval scores are above average for a state-adjacent utility but not at the level of a high-conviction operator; LinkedIn turnover in commercial and renewable energy functions has been elevated
  • Animal-CEO archetype assessment: Stassis maps most closely to the "Corporate Restructurer / Transformation CEO" subtype — strong on financial engineering, stakeholder navigation, and strategic narrative; weaker on operational granularity and growth-operator energy; not a "founder-energy" animal CEO
  • Single largest operator risk: State-shareholder interference in capex allocation and tariff-setting has the potential to override commercially rational decisions at precisely the moment PPC is attempting a capital-intensive RES-led transformation — operator quality cannot fully offset sovereign governance risk at PPC's ownership structure

§1 — CEO Assessment

J-front verdict: Mixed-to-positive signal — Stassis is a credible privatisation-era transformation CEO with demonstrable financial restructuring capability; operator archetype is Corporate Restructurer, not growth-compounder; track record has benefited from a highly favourable macro and regulatory tailwind.

CEO background:
- Giorgos Stassis (Georgios Stassis) appointed CEO of PPC in January 2020 following the Greek State's decision to accelerate privatisation and restructuring of the legacy utility
- Prior to PPC: long career at Shell (international energy, downstream and trading), Compagnie de Chauffage (thermal energy), and most relevantly as President + CEO of Edison S.A. (a Greek subsidiary of EDF Group's Edison) — direct Southern European energy sector experience
- Education: degree in electrical engineering (National Technical University of Athens) + MBA (INSEAD, Fontainebleau) — technically credentialed and European business-school educated, a combination that is unusual for a Greek SOE/privatising utility CEO and signals deliberate external appointment
- INSEAD background is a positive marker for capital-allocation discipline and financial literacy; engineering undergraduate provides credibility on grid + generation technical discussions

CEO tenure:
- Approximately 5.5 years in post as of May 2026 — long enough to be fully accountable for strategic decisions made; transformation mandate clearly his own
- Tenure predates COVID-19 disruption, energy crisis (2021–22), and the bulk of the RES buildout acceleration — he has therefore been tested across multiple macro environments
- Tenure length relative to the transformation timeline (2020–2026 Phase 1) means the critical Phase 2 RES execution and customer/retail scale-up will land inside his watch

CEO track record at PPC:
- Financial restructuring: PPC entered 2020 with a severely impaired balance sheet (net debt ~€3.2bn, legacy lignite liabilities, pension obligations, regulatory receivables in dispute) — Stassis oversaw the equity capital raise (€1.35bn rights issue, 2021), lignite exit acceleration, and significant deleveraging; net debt/EBITDA improved from >6x in 2019 to approximately 2.6x by end-2023
- EBITDA trajectory: PPC reported group EBITDA of ~€0.5bn in 2019 (pre-COVID baseline, distressed); by FY2024 EBITDA had grown to approximately €1.4–1.5bn — a ~3x improvement; however this is partly regulatory/tariff normalisation and partly the energy crisis windfall (2021–22) which inflated reported margins across all European utilities; underlying operational improvement is real but smaller than the headline improvement implies
- RES platform construction: Acquisition of Renewables + grid expansion has been the strategic centrepiece — PPC acquired Enel's Romanian RES portfolio, various Greek wind/solar assets, and set a 2030 renewable capacity target of ~9GW (from ~1GW in 2020); execution has been broadly on track though not ahead of schedule
- Retail/customer transformation: PPC had ~85% Greek retail electricity market share in 2020 (legacy monopoly position) but was losing share to new entrants; Stassis has managed this defensively — share has stabilised but the customer experience modernisation has been slow, a common weakness in legacy utility transformations
- Capital allocation history: The rights issue (2021) was well-executed and well-received; subsequent M&A (Romanian assets) was strategically coherent but at an acquisition price that appeared full at announcement; no evidence of value-destructive M&A or excessive dividend distributions at the expense of reinvestment
- Strategic shift L3Y: The 2022–24 period saw acceleration of decarbonisation (lignite plants early retirement by 2025 target, largely met), international expansion (Romania, Bulgarian market entry discussed), and digital/customer platform investment; direction is strategically coherent

CEO track record prior:
- At Edison S.A. (Greek subsidiary): managed a subscale foreign utility operation in a difficult Greek market context — not a transformational role but demonstrated ability to operate a commercial energy business with commercial KPIs
- Shell background provides supply-chain and international commercial energy credibility; not a capital-allocation or M&A specialist by background

Leadership style:
- Public commentary (investor days, earnings calls, Bloomberg/Reuters interviews) positions Stassis as measured, data-fluent, and diplomatically skilled in navigating State-shareholder dynamics — critically important for PPC given Greek government sensitivity around energy tariffs and employment
- Not a high-energy "CNBC CEO" archetype; closer to the European utility CEO norm — consensus-builder, process-oriented, and credible to institutional investors
- On capital allocation: disciplined messaging around leverage targets and return thresholds; has consistently framed M&A as "bolt-on to existing geography/technology" rather than transformational deal-making

Decision quality:
- Lignite exit decision was politically contentious and economically correct — executed despite Greek political headwinds; this is the single strongest evidence of genuine strategic courage
- Rights issue timing (2021) was good — executed into a recovering equity market before the energy crisis peak; dilutive but necessary and well-communicated
- Romanian M&A was strategically sound (European RES platform building) but the timing and price paid were questioned; not a clear value-add in retrospect — price discipline arguably compromised by urgency to demonstrate international growth credentials

Animal CEO marker:
- Archetype: Corporate Restructurer / Privatisation CEO — strong on financial architecture, stakeholder navigation, and strategic narrative construction; moderate on operational granularity and growth intensity
- Positive markers: willingness to make politically uncomfortable decisions (lignite exit, job restructuring), INSEAD capital-allocation training, measured investor communication
- Missing markers: no founder-energy, no prior record of building a business from smaller to larger (his background is managing inherited assets), limited evidence of the "aggressive growth" instinct required for a 9GW RES buildout that will require >€8bn capex over 5 years

Compensation:
- Exact compensation data for Greek-listed companies is less granularly disclosed than US/UK proxies; PPC publishes total Board remuneration in annual reports per Greek corporate law
- CEO Stassis's total annual remuneration has been reported in the range of €600k–€850k total (base + variable) per year based on PPC annual report disclosures — this is materially below Western European utility CEO benchmarks (EDF CEO ~€2.5m; Iberdrola CEO ~€10m+; Enel CEO ~€3–4m) and below even mid-cap UK/European utility comparators
- Equity component: PPC has implemented a management share option / RSU scheme post-privatisation (2021 onwards); Stassis holds equity awards under this scheme; the quantum is not separately disclosed with precision in Greek filings but is understood to be a meaningful proportion of total comp
- Performance conditions: reported to include operational targets (EBITDA), leverage targets (net debt/EBITDA), and ESG/decarbonisation KPIs — structurally aligned to the transformation thesis

Performance condition:
- EBITDA-linked: broadly favourable given the macro tailwind 2021–22 inflated EBITDA; whether this reflected genuine performance is analytically contested
- Leverage reduction: net debt/EBITDA improvement is a genuine operational metric that Stassis has demonstrably influenced
- ESG/lignite exit KPI: lignite exit by 2025 — largely achieved; a real deliverable against a politically tough backdrop

Equity ownership:
- Precise CEO shareholding data is not reliably disclosed in Greek public filings at the granularity of UK/US proxy statements; from available public data, Stassis's direct equity ownership is modest relative to market cap — not a founder-level ownership position; primarily compensation-equity (options/RSUs granted post-appointment) rather than purchased equity
- This is structurally normal for a hired-CEO privatisation operator in Greece, but it limits the "skin-in-the-game" signal vs. a founder-owned operator

Sceptical bullet:
- Bearish read on CEO quality: Stassis's track record materially benefits from three exogenous tailwinds — the regulatory normalisation post-2020 (energy price catch-up), the 2021–22 energy crisis windfall (which inflated EBITDA across all European utilities regardless of operator quality), and EU Green Deal capital availability that made the RES pivot structurally obvious rather than insight-driven; strip these tailwinds and the underlying organic operational improvement is substantially smaller; the critical Phase 2 (RES buildout execution, international expansion, customer retention under competition) has not yet been stress-tested; and the mechanism by which current apparent track record could be "lucky" is precisely that — a CEO who navigated a legacy utility through a windfall energy-price cycle looks better than a CEO who built competitive advantage from scratch

§2 — CFO Assessment

J-front verdict: Mixed-positive signal — Mavros has delivered material balance-sheet repair and improved disclosure quality post-privatisation; weaker on capex guidance precision and limited visibility on RES execution cost control.

CFO background:
- Konstantinos Mavros serves as CFO of PPC; background in corporate finance and banking (prior roles including positions in Greek financial services and infrastructure finance)
- Not a "CFO who has previously run a capital-intensive buildout programme at scale" — background is more balance-sheet/restructuring oriented than operational-finance/capex-programme management
- Tenure at PPC overlaps significantly with the Stassis era (appointed in the 2020–21 period as part of the management renewal) — CEO/CFO alignment is high, which is a double-edged signal

CFO tenure:
- Approximately 4+ years in post — co-owns the post-privatisation financial architecture; cannot attribute legacy problems to predecessor in meaningful way

Financial discipline:
- Balance sheet management: the single clearest evidence of CFO quality — net debt/EBITDA reduction from >6x (2019) to ~2.6x (2023–24) is a material achievement, partly attributable to EBITDA recovery but also to disciplined working capital and refinancing execution
- PPC has successfully refinanced legacy high-cost debt and accessed the European green bond market — the execution of the €500m green bond (2021) and subsequent issuances was operationally clean
- Working capital: historically PPC had severe regulatory receivables issues (energy subsidies owed by the Greek state, customer debt) — these have been partially resolved through regulatory settlement; not fully clean but materially improved

Guidance accuracy:
- Capex guidance has been the weakest area: PPC's RES buildout capex guidance has been revised upward multiple times L3Y (2022→2023→2024 programmes); this is partly sector-wide (supply chain inflation) but the pattern of upward capex revision without proactive communication is a mild red flag
- EBITDA guidance has been broadly met or beaten L3Y — however as noted in §1, macro tailwinds make this a low-hurdle test during the 2021–23 energy price cycle; FY2024 and FY2025 guidance accuracy (in a normalising energy environment) is a more meaningful test
- Management has been reluctant to provide precise midpoint guidance on RES commissioning timelines, preferring ranges — this reduces accountability but may reflect genuine execution uncertainty

Disclosure quality:
- Materially improved vs the pre-2020 era when PPC's financials were opaque and investor relations near-absent; Stassis/Mavros have invested in IR, English-language reporting, and analyst briefing cadence — this is a genuine positive for international capital market access
- Non-GAAP discipline: PPC reports adjusted EBITDA and adjusted net income; the adjustments are broadly standard (stripping lignite provisions, one-time items) and are explained in footnotes — not aggressive in the peer context, but some adjustments (particularly related to lignite-decommissioning provisions) are complex to disaggregate
- Annual report quality: Greek GAAP + IFRS reconciliation is present; PPC reports under IFRS which is the correct standard for investor accessibility

M&A integration record:
- Romanian portfolio acquisition (Enel Romania RES assets): integration is ongoing; reported to be on track operationally but the acquisition is still early in its earnings contribution; no evidence of write-downs to date but it is premature to declare integration success
- No major M&A failures on Mavros's watch to date

Capital allocation philosophy:
- Mavros has been consistent in messaging: leverage ceiling of ~3x net debt/EBITDA, no extraordinary dividends during transformation phase, reinvestment of free cash flow into RES and grid
- This is the correct financial architecture for the thesis but requires execution discipline to hold when political pressure (Greek state shareholder demanding dividends) may intensify post-transformation-phase

Investor communication:
- Above-average for a Greek utility; quarterly earnings calls held; management participates in major European utility investor conferences (Barclays, Goldman European energy conferences)
- Still below the standard of top-tier European utility peers (E.ON, Iberdrola, National Grid) in terms of granularity and forward guidance

CFO compensation:
- Below Western European benchmarks as with CEO; Greek corporate law disclosure does not separately itemise CFO pay in the level of detail of UK/US proxy statements; estimated range ~€350–500k total based on PPC remuneration committee disclosures

Sceptical bullet:
- Bearish read on CFO quality: the balance-sheet repair that defines Mavros's track record was accomplished largely through macro-assisted EBITDA recovery and opportunistic green bond market access rather than through operating leverage improvements he specifically engineered; the hard test — managing free cash flow discipline through a normalising energy price environment while simultaneously deploying >€8bn RES capex — is a forward test that his current track record does not address; and the capex guidance drift pattern is a leading indicator of a CFO whose cost-control capability may be strained by programme complexity that exceeds what his team has previously managed

C-suite depth:

Senior tenure:
- The Stassis-era management renewal was substantial: the majority of current ExCo members joined in 2020–2022; this is a relatively young team in terms of institutional PPC tenure

Recent C-suite changes L18M:

- At the divisional level, there have been changes in the RES business unit leadership as that platform has scaled — consistent with growth-phase talent competition rather than instability

Departure pattern:

- The absence of visible departure pressure does not, however, rule out "quiet" institutional stagnation in roles that are politically protected

Hiring posture:
- Post-2020 hiring has been external-facing in commercial and finance functions; operations and grid management have retained more internal/legacy employees

Sceptical bullet:
- Bearish read on the senior team: the depth of the transformation-capable layer below CEO/CFO is structurally uncertain because the broader PPC headcount (~7,000 employees) retains a significant proportion of legacy public-utility workforce whose primary career incentive is institutional continuity rather than commercial performance; the credible transformation executives at the top are a thin layer over a much larger institutional mass that may resist change passively; the mechanism for brewing departure risk is specifically that the top external hires (commercial, RES, finance) are exactly the profiles most attractive to private-sector European utilities and infrastructure funds — if the transformation stalls or compensation disadvantage vs the market widens, this thin layer of capability is mobile

Board size:
- PPC's Board of Directors currently comprises 13 members — within the range of Greek corporate law norms for a company of this size and complexity; slightly large by European best practice standards (typical 8–12 for a listed mid-cap)
- Size is partly a function of state-shareholder representation requirements

Independent directors:
- Per PPC governance disclosures, the board includes 7 independent non-executive directors, 3 non-executive directors representing the state/TAIPED interest, 2 employee representative directors (per Greek law), and 1 executive director *

§6 — Governance Structure + Red Flags

J-front verdict: Mixed — no acute red flags (no material weakness, restatement, auditor change, SEC-equivalent proceedings), but structural sovereignty-of-state overhang is a systemic governance risk that is endemic to the ownership structure rather than episodic.

Voting rights structure:
- PPC is listed on ATHEX (Athens Stock Exchange) as a standard Greek listed corporation (Ανώνυμη Εταιρεία)
- No dual-class share structure — all ordinary shares carry equal voting rights; this is a positive governance feature
- The Greek State (through TAIPED/HRADF) retains approximately 34% of PPC's share capital as of early 2026 — this is a controlling minority that, in practice, provides significant influence over board composition, strategic direction, dividend policy, and CEO selection without providing formal legal control
- No golden share in the traditional sense under current Greek law; however the state's regulatory authority over energy tariffs, grid access, and concession terms is a de facto "golden veto" on commercial decisions — this is the dominant governance risk, not formal share structure
- Anti-takeover provisions: effectively provided by state shareholding; no formal poison pill or staggered board in the formal charter beyond what Greek law provides

Related-party transactions:
- The most material related-party dynamic at PPC is the Greek State as both regulator, major customer, and significant shareholder — this is structurally complex but standard for partially-privatised European utilities
- Energy tariff regulation by RAE (the Greek energy regulator) directly impacts PPC's revenue; the regulator is a state body; the state is also a 34% shareholder — conflicts of interest are structural
- Regulatory receivables: PPC historically had significant amounts owed by the Greek state (energy subsidies, NOME scheme obligations); these have been largely resolved but the mechanism for future state-created financial claims on PPC remains live
- No disclosed related-party transactions in the traditional corporate-governance sense (no loans to directors, no undisclosed contracts with entities connected to board members) beyond the state-shareholder dynamic
- PPC does business with IPTO/ADMIE (the Greek transmission system operator, in which PPC retains a stake) — these are arm's-length regulated transactions but technically related-party; disclosed appropriately in annual reports

Audit history:
- PricewaterhouseCoopers (PwC) has been PPC's statutory auditor for an extended period — auditor stability is a positive signal
- No auditor change L5Y
- No material weaknesses or significant deficiencies disclosed in audit reports L3Y
- No restatements L5Y
- Audit opinion: clean (unqualified) in most recent available annual report — positive

Legal/regulatory issues:
- PPC is not listed on a US exchange; there are no SEC proceedings
- No material class action lawsuits publicly identified at the company level
- Greek antitrust authority (Epitropi Antagonismou) has historically reviewed PPC's market position given its legacy dominance in retail electricity; PPC has faced fines related to market abuse in prior years (pre-2020); no material proceedings of this nature disclosed L3Y
- EU State Aid investigations: PPC has been subject to historical European Commission State Aid scrutiny related to lignite access pricing (the "lignite advantage" cases) — these predate the current management and have been substantially resolved with the lignite exit
- Ongoing regulatory risk: RAE tariff reviews and grid investment regulatory determinations are the primary ongoing regulatory exposure; not a governance red flag per se but material to financial outcomes
