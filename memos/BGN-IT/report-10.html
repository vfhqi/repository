Executive Summary

The bear case for Banca Generali S.p.A. (BGN IM) centers on the argument that its record-breaking 2025 financial performance is "lower quality" than it appears, driven by significant non-recurring items and structural dependencies that create a ceiling for valuation. While the company reported a record net profit of €445.80 million, a substantial portion—€39.00 million—was a one-time tax refund. Furthermore, recurring net profit growth of 6.80% was accompanied by an 8% rise in core operating expenses as the bank aggressively scales its IT and AI infrastructure, potentially indicating diminishing marginal returns on its operating leverage.

Governance remains a primary concern for skeptics. Banca Generali operates as a captive wealth management arm for Assicurazioni Generali, which holds 50.20% of the shares. This relationship is characterized by frequent related-party transactions, such as the private placement of a €100.00 million bond to the parent and the purchase of minority stakes in subsidiaries from other group companies. Bears argue this creates a conflict of interest where the bank may be used to support the broader group's liquidity or strategic needs at the expense of minority shareholders. The Mediobanca tender offer in late 2025 further highlighted this vulnerability, as it created a period of market uncertainty that hampered senior talent recruitment.

Operationally, the bank is pivoting toward "in-house" funds and financial wrappers to capture more margin, but this increases the risk of performance fee volatility. In 2025, performance fees dropped by over €50.00 million at its Luxembourg asset management hub, a trend that could worsen if market conditions deteriorate. Additionally, the integration of Intermonte Partners SIM S.p.A. brings execution risk; the Board of Statutory Auditors has explicitly flagged the need for organizational improvements to align the new subsidiary with the Group's risk control measures.

Finally, the bank faces significant litigation risk. The provision of €61.50 million for losses related to illiquid products indicates that previous distribution strategies may have structural flaws, leading to ongoing civil and criminal challenges. Combined with a deteriorating Italian macro outlook—characterized by rising BTP yields and widening spreads—Banca Generali's capital-light model may be tested by asset-side volatility and the potential for increased regulatory capital requirements. While no formal short-seller reports have targeted the bank to date, these internal red flags suggest a vulnerability to a "growth story" derailing if non-recurring tailwinds dissipate.
