Section 10 – Disruption Forces + Structural Evolution + LT Growth + Paradigm Fit 28 findings

Section summary
  1. Evolving global deplastics legislation accelerates the shift to fiber-based solutions, establishing sustainable material design as a critical industry success factor.
  2. Large-scale converters benefit from industry consolidation, utilizing centralized procurement and regional footprints to navigate non-integrated raw material exposure.
  3. The structural transition from plastic to paper fuels long-term growth, aligning with consumer preferences and ESG-compliant capital regimes.

Regulatory frameworks and deplastics legislation

1.
Strict regulatory push: the EU's Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation (PPWR) applies from August 12, 2026, forcing a shift to sustainable packaging
2.
Recyclability mandates: all packaging placed on the European Union market must be recyclable by 2030
3.
Strict plastic ban: the PPWR bans composite packaging containing 5% or more plastic from January 1, 2030
4.
Transatlantic policy alignment: California's Senate Bill 54 (SB 54) mirrors the PPWR, enforcing eco- modulated producer responsibility fees
5.
Rigid plastic targets: California law requires a 25% source reduction in single-use plastic packaging by 2032
6.
EPR fee shifts: Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) transforms packaging from a simple unit cost to a total ownership cost
7.
Emerging market mandates: India's Plastic Waste Management Rules enforce nationwide bans on single- use plastics to drive paper substitution
8.
Strong recycling targets: the rules require brand owners to meet mandatory recycling targets or face steep financial penalties

14.
