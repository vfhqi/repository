KEY FINDINGS

- Pure-play European power cable leader. NKT has completed a multi-decade transformation from diversified conglomerate to focused cable solutions provider. The 2024 sale of NKT Photonics (EUR 225m to Hamamatsu) marked the final divestiture. The business now operates across three segments: Solutions (high-voltage, ~60% of revenue), Applications (medium/low-voltage distribution, ~30%), and Service & Accessories (~10%).

- Unprecedented order backlog provides multi-year revenue visibility. High-voltage order backlog stood at EUR 10.2bn at end-2025. This represents ~3.8x annual Solutions revenue. Backlog is dominated by interconnectors (~55%) and offshore wind (~40%), with execution stretching well into the 2030s. The EUR 2.2bn Eastern Green Link 3 contract (signed Mar 2026) is the largest single project in company history.

- Record-high profitability with structural margin expansion. Operational EBITDA hit EUR 390m in 2025 (14.3% margin on std. metal prices), up from EUR 15m in 2019. Every segment contributed margin expansion in 2025. Service & Accessories is the standout margin story at 19.1% EBITDA margin, up from 9.2% in 2023.

- Heavy investment cycle creates near-term FCF drag but positions for step-change in capacity. EUR 2bn cumulative capex guided for 2025-2028 (~75% growth, ~25% maintenance). Karlskrona expansion adds a third extrusion tower, boosting HV output by 50% from 2027. The NKT Eleonora cable-laying vessel (operational 2027) doubles installation fleet capacity. FCF was negative EUR 244m in 2025.

  • Tight oligopoly with extreme barriers to entry. The HVDC submarine cable market is effectively a triopoly: Prysmian, Nexans, NKT. Combined they control the vast majority of European HV capacity. New entrants face 5-7 year lead times to build factories, qualification requirements, and track record barriers. Chinese competition is emerging but not yet credible in European HVDC.

- Demand runway is structural, not cyclical. EU targets require tripling offshore wind capacity by 2030. Grid interconnection plans across North Sea, Baltic, and UK markets underpin a decade-plus pipeline. Cable manufacturing capacity is booked out years in advance, giving incumbents pricing power and backlog certainty.

- Near-term revenue growth may decelerate as CHPE project ramps down. 2026 guidance implies flat-to-slightly-lower Solutions revenue (mid-single-digit organic decline) as the large Champlain Hudson Power Express project in the US completes. New Karlskrona capacity does not come online until 2027.

- Net cash balance sheet despite heavy investment. Net interest-bearing debt was EUR -963m (i.e., net cash) at end-2025. The company has significant financial headroom to fund its EUR 2bn investment programme without equity dilution.

- Valuation reflects embedded growth expectations. At DKK ~867/share (Apr 2026), market cap is ~DKK 46bn (~EUR 6.2bn). Consensus is neutral (4 buy, 4 hold, 3 sell) with average TP DKK 833. The stock has risen ~47% over the past year.

- Organisational restructuring underway. From 1 Jan 2026, NKT reports under new segment names: Transmission (was Solutions), Distribution (was Applications), and Grid Solutions & Accessories (was Service & Accessories). Two-year restated financials expected with Q1 2026 report in May 2026.

SECTION 1 — GENERAL

1. Company Description

NKT A/S is a Danish power cable manufacturer and solutions provider headquartered in Brøndby, Denmark. Founded in 1891 as Nordisk Elektrisk Ledningstraad og Kabel-Fabrik, the company spent a century as a diversified industrial conglomerate before systematically narrowing its focus to power cables from the 1990s onwards.

Key divestiture milestones: Lauritz Knudsen sold to Schneider (1999), GIGA sold to Intel (2000), NKT Flexibles sold (2012), NKT Photonics sold to Hamamatsu (2024, EUR 225m EV). Conversely, the 1999 acquisition of Felten & Guilleaume doubled production capacity and established NKT's German manufacturing base.

What the company does: NKT designs, manufactures, installs, and services power cable systems across the voltage spectrum. Its core competence lies in high-voltage direct current submarine and underground cable systems used for offshore wind farm connections, cross-border interconnectors, and power-from-shore applications. The company also manufactures medium- and low-voltage cables for power distribution grids and construction.

Strategic objectives (Charging Forward strategy, launched Nov 2025):
- Organic revenue growth >7% CAGR 2024-2030
- Adjusted EBITDA >EUR 900m by 2030
- ROCE >22% by 2030
- Become the world's leading power cable solutions provider

Organisational structure (from 1 Jan 2026):
- Transmission (formerly Solutions): High-voltage AC and DC cable systems, turnkey project delivery including installation. Factory: Karlskrona, Sweden. Vessel fleet: NKT Victoria, NKT Eleonora (from 2027).
- Distribution (formerly Applications): Medium- and low-voltage cables for distribution grids and construction. Factories across Europe incl. Germany, Czech Republic, Poland, Portugal (NKT Esposende, acquired via SolidAl 2024).
- Grid Solutions & Accessories (formerly Service & Accessories): Cable accessories (joints, terminations), repair preparedness, monitoring, maintenance. Factory: Cologne, Germany (accessories); Falsterbo, Sweden (new test hall).

Employees: ~6,200 across production facilities in 10 European countries.

CEO: Claes Westerlind (appointed May 2023, Swedish, joined NKT 2017 from ABB/Hitachi Energy, M.Sc. Mechanical Engineering). CFO: Line Andrea Fandrup.

Rating: B+ — NKT's transformation into a pure-play cable company is complete. The strategic focus is clear and well-articulated. The Charging Forward targets are ambitious but backed by a EUR 10.2bn backlog. Organisational quality appears solid under relatively new leadership.

Sceptical points:
- CEO tenure is short (under 3 years). The strategy is still his first full cycle.
- The Charging Forward 2030 targets (EBITDA >EUR 900m) require more than doubling current profitability. That demands flawless execution on both capacity expansion and project delivery simultaneously.
- NKT remains a single-product company in a concentrated industry. Any structural shift in demand drivers (e.g., hydrogen pipelines substituting for electricity interconnectors) would be existential.

5. Financials — Analysis

Operating leverage: The EBITDA bridge from EUR 15m (2019) to EUR 390m (2025) demonstrates extreme operating leverage. HV cable manufacturing has high fixed costs (factory, vessels, engineering teams) and significant variable margins on incremental projects. When utilisation rates are high and project execution is satisfactory, margins expand rapidly. The inverse is also true — 2019 proved that underutilisation crushes profitability.

Capital intensity: NKT is capital-intensive by any measure. The EUR 2bn capex programme for 2025-2028 represents ~50% of the current market cap. However, this investment is funded entirely from operating cash flow and the existing cash balance — no equity dilution is required. The EUR 743m capex in 2025 alone exceeds the total EBITDA of any single year prior to 2024.

Revenue predictability: The EUR 10.2bn HV order backlog provides exceptional revenue visibility. Assuming ~EUR 1.6bn annual Solutions revenue, the backlog represents ~6+ years of future work. However, backlog execution timing is subject to permitting delays, weather windows for offshore installation, and customer readiness. The gap between "backlog" and "annual revenue" is widening, suggesting an accelerating pipeline that will require the new Karlskrona capacity to service.

Capital allocation priorities (ranked):
1. Organic capacity expansion (Karlskrona third tower, NKT Eleonora vessel, Esposende MV/HV expansion)
2. Working capital investment (HV projects require significant upfront NWC)
3. Selective M&A (SolidAl in 2024 for EUR ~100m est.)
4. No dividends or buybacks currently

NWC dynamics: HV cable projects have complex cash flow profiles. Customers typically pay milestone-based progress payments, but NKT must fund raw material procurement (copper, aluminium, polyethylene) upfront. The 2024 improvement in working capital (contributing to EUR 400m FCF) reflected favourable milestone timing. The 2025 deterioration reflected the capex ramp and project mix.

Rating: A- — The financial analysis reveals a business with powerful operating leverage, structural demand tailwinds, and disciplined capital allocation. The investment programme is well-funded and clearly value-accretive if executed on time and budget.

Sceptical points:
- Capital intensity creates path dependency. Once the EUR 2bn investment is committed, NKT must win enough projects to fill the new capacity. Utilisation risk is asymmetric.
- Working capital swings can be violent in project-based businesses. A EUR 600m+ swing in FCF from 2024 to 2025 demonstrates this volatility.
- Zero shareholder returns during the investment phase means investors are entirely dependent on share price appreciation. No income cushion.

6. Revenue Model

Geographic split (approximate, 2025):
- Germany: ~25% (largest single market, driven by Distribution and Accessories)
- UK: ~15% (growing rapidly via EGL1, EGL2, EGL3 contracts with SSEN/National Grid)
- Netherlands: ~15% (TenneT offshore wind connections)
- Nordics (Denmark, Sweden, Norway): ~15% (domestic utility demand + Karlskrona production)
- Other Europe (Poland, Czech Republic, Portugal): ~15%
- North America : ~10% (Champlain Hudson Power Express — declining as project completes)
- Rest of World: ~5%

Business unit split (2025, std. metal prices):
- Solutions/Transmission: ~59% of revenue, ~66% of EBITDA
- Applications/Distribution: ~31% of revenue, ~23% of EBITDA
- Service & Accessories: ~12% of revenue, ~15% of EBITDA
- Group/Other: negative EBITDA contribution

Revenue characteristics:
- Solutions: Project-based, lumpy, long-cycle (2-5 years from order to delivery). Revenue is recognised over time using percentage-of-completion. Fixed-price contracts with escalation clauses for raw materials. High visibility from backlog but execution-dependent.
- Applications: Shorter-cycle (weeks to months). Mix of framework agreements with utilities and spot market. More cyclical — exposed to construction activity (low-voltage) and grid investment (medium-voltage).
- Service & Accessories: Recurring and growing. Installed cable base creates ongoing demand for joints, terminations, repair preparedness, and monitoring. Highest-margin segment with the most predictable revenue.

Macro sensitivity:
- Low correlation to GDP: HV cable demand is driven by energy transition policy, not economic cycles. The EU's REPowerEU plan, UK's GB Energy programme, and national offshore wind targets are regulatory commitments, not discretionary spend.
- Moderate cyclicality in Applications: Low-voltage construction cables track residential and commercial building activity. This segment was "largely subdued" in 2025 according to management.
- Copper/aluminium price exposure: Revenue at actual metal prices can diverge significantly from standard metal prices. NKT hedges metal exposure, but reported revenue volatility adds noise to the growth picture.

Rating: A — Revenue model is structurally attractive. The HV backlog provides multi-year visibility that is rare in industrial businesses. The growing Service & Accessories segment adds recurring, high-margin revenue that improves earnings quality over time. Geographic concentration in Northern Europe is a strength given the energy transition tailwinds in this region.

Sceptical points:
- Revenue concentration in a small number of very large projects creates lumpiness. Loss of one major contract (e.g., a TenneT cancellation) would be material.
- The US revenue contribution from CHPE is a one-off. NKT's US presence is negligible after this project completes. The company remains a European pure-play.
- Applications segment macro sensitivity means ~30% of revenue has normal industrial cyclicality.

7. Business Model Summary

How NKT makes money:
NKT designs, manufactures, and installs power cable systems. In its core HV business, it operates as a turnkey solutions provider — from initial engineering through factory production, cable-loading onto vessels, offshore/onshore installation, testing, and commissioning. This vertical integration captures the full project value chain.

Revenue is earned on fixed-price contracts (with commodity escalators) over multi-year execution periods. Margins are earned through:
1. Engineering and design expertise (proprietary cable system designs)
2. Manufacturing efficiency (utilisation of capital-intensive factory assets)
3. Installation capabilities (owned cable-laying vessels)
4. Risk management (project execution track record reduces contingency requirements)

Competitive advantages:
- Oligopoly position: One of only three companies globally capable of manufacturing and installing extra-high-voltage DC submarine cable systems. Barriers to entry are extreme.
- Vertical integration: Owning both manufacturing (Karlskrona) and installation (NKT Victoria, NKT Eleonora from 2027) assets reduces dependency on third-party subcontractors and improves project control.
- Technology: XLPE-insulated HVDC cables at 525 kV are technically demanding. NKT's R&D capabilities and type-test certifications represent decades of accumulated know-how.
- Track record: Successful execution of marquee projects (Viking Link, BorWin5, TenneT 2GW programme) builds customer trust and reduces competitive tendering risk. In a market where failure costs billions, track record is the ultimate competitive moat.
- Installation fleet: Cable-laying vessels are scarce and expensive assets. Owning two vessels (from 2027) gives NKT scheduling flexibility and avoids reliance on limited third-party vessel availability.

Strategic priorities:
1. Execute the Karlskrona capacity expansion on time and budget (50% output increase by 2027)
2. Fill new capacity with high-quality orders (target selective growth)
3. Expand the Service & Accessories business to capture aftermarket value from the growing installed cable base
4. Drive continuous innovation (next-generation cable technologies)
5. Selective geographic expansion while maintaining European leadership

Rating: A — The business model is structurally advantaged. Oligopoly + vertical integration + track record = durable competitive position. The transition from conglomerate to pure-play has been executed decisively. The strategic direction under Charging Forward is logical and well-resourced.

Sceptical points:
- Turnkey fixed-price contracts carry binary execution risk. A single botched project (cable failure, vessel damage, installation delay) can wipe out a year's profit.
- The competitive moat depends partly on scarcity of cable-laying vessels. If vessel availability increases (new builds by competitors or third parties), NKT's installation advantage diminishes.
- The Charging Forward 2030 EBITDA target of >EUR 900m implies the business more than doubles earnings in five years. History suggests cable companies struggle to sustain 15%+ EBITDA margins through full cycles.

8. Structural / Secular Demand Trends

Energy transition demand drivers:
- Offshore wind: EU targets require 120 GW of offshore wind by 2030 (from ~30 GW today). Every offshore wind farm needs export cables to connect to the onshore grid. Larger, deeper, more distant wind farms require longer, higher-voltage cable systems — exactly NKT's sweet spot.
- Interconnectors: Cross-border electricity interconnection is critical for grid stability in a renewables-heavy system. Projects like Viking Link (UK-Denmark), EGL1-3 (Scotland-England), and the North Sea Wind Power Hub consortium represent decades of demand.
- Grid reinforcement: Onshore grid infrastructure across Europe is aging and undersized for renewable integration. Utilities are spending record amounts on grid upgrades, driving demand for medium- and high-voltage onshore cables.
- Data centre power demand: The AI-driven surge in data centre construction is creating new demand for dedicated power cable connections. This is an emerging but rapidly growing demand driver.
- Undergrounding trend: Regulatory and public acceptance pressures are driving a shift from overhead lines to underground cables, particularly in densely populated European markets.

Supply-side constraints:
- HV cable manufacturing capacity is sold out globally for the next 3-5 years.
- Cable-laying vessel capacity is extremely tight. New vessel construction takes 3-4 years.
- Qualified engineering and project management talent is scarce.
- These supply constraints create pricing power for incumbents and extend the order backlog runway.

Quantified market opportunity:
- NKT estimates its addressable HV power cable market awarded ~EUR 2bn of projects in Q1 2025 alone.
- The total pipeline of European HV cable projects stretches into the 2030s, with estimated cumulative value in the hundreds of billions of euros.
- EU Grid Action Plan (Nov 2023) explicitly identifies cable manufacturing capacity as a strategic bottleneck requiring policy support.

Rating: A+ — The structural demand case is as strong as any industrial sub-sector in Europe. The combination of regulatory mandates, climate targets, energy security concerns, and grid modernisation needs creates a multi-decade demand runway. Supply constraints amplify the advantage for incumbents.

Sceptical points:
- Policy risk is non-trivial. Changes in government (e.g., a Eurosceptic shift), budget pressures, or geopolitical events could delay or cancel planned projects.
- Technology disruption is possible. Superconducting cables, gas-insulated lines, or hydrogen pipelines could theoretically reduce demand for conventional HVDC cables — though none of these are commercially viable at scale today.
- "Sold out for 3-5 years" is a statement about current capacity. Competitors are also expanding. Prysmian, Nexans, and LS Cable are all investing heavily. The supply-demand balance may shift by 2028-2030.

9. Business Model — Customer Side

Customer base:
- Transmission system operators (TSOs): The primary customers are state-owned or regulated TSOs like TenneT (Netherlands/Germany), SSEN Transmission , National Grid , Energinet (Denmark), and Statnett (Norway). These are essentially sovereign-backed counterparties with guaranteed cost recovery through regulated tariffs.
- Offshore wind developers: Major utilities and developers including Ørsted, RWE, Vattenfall, and Equinor contract cable systems for their wind farm export cables.
- Distribution network operators (DNOs): For the Applications segment, DNOs across Europe procure medium-voltage cables under framework agreements.

Competitive advantages on the customer side:
- Switching costs are extreme in HV. Once a cable system is designed, manufactured, and partially installed, switching suppliers mid-project is effectively impossible. The engineering integration between cable design and installation methodology creates deep lock-in.
- Qualification barriers: TSOs require extensive pre-qualification and type-testing before a supplier can bid on projects. This process takes years and creates a significant moat for established suppliers.
- Repeat business: NKT's relationship with TenneT illustrates the dynamic — initial project delivery leads to framework agreements for future projects. The TenneT 2GW Programme framework agreement for Nederwiek 3, Doordewind 1, and Doordewind 2 (~EUR 1.5bn) followed successful execution on earlier TenneT projects.
- Customer concentration: The top 5-10 customers likely account for 60-70%+ of HV revenue. This is a feature, not a bug — these customers have multi-decade investment programmes and deep institutional relationships with NKT.

Rating: A — The customer profile is exceptional. Sovereign-backed or regulated counterparties with long-duration investment programmes. Extreme switching costs and qualification barriers create deep lock-in. The risk of customer loss is low; the risk of project cancellation or delay is moderate.

Sceptical points:
- Customer concentration means a dispute with one major TSO could be material.
- TSOs are monopoly buyers in their jurisdictions. They have significant bargaining power in procurement processes.
- Payment terms on large projects can be unfavourable. TSOs are not known for generous upfront payments.

10. Business Model — Economies of Scale

Scale advantages:
- Factory utilisation: HV cable manufacturing has very high fixed costs (factory, extrusion towers, testing facilities). Marginal costs are primarily raw materials (copper, aluminium, polyethylene). Higher utilisation drives powerful operating leverage — as demonstrated by the EBITDA swing from EUR 15m (2019, low utilisation) to EUR 390m (2025, high utilisation).
- Vessel economics: A cable-laying vessel costs EUR 300-500m to build and operate. Once operational, the marginal cost of deploying it on an additional project is relatively low. Owning two vessels (from 2027) spreads this fixed cost across more projects.
- R&D leverage: Cable technology R&D is expensive and specialised. Larger players can spread R&D costs across more projects. NKT's R&D investment benefits all segments.
- Procurement: Larger cable volumes improve purchasing power for copper, aluminium, and insulation materials.

Scale disadvantages:
- NKT is significantly smaller than Prysmian (revenue ~EUR 20bn vs. ~EUR 3.6bn). Prysmian's scale advantage in procurement, R&D, and risk diversification is real.
- However, in the specific HV submarine cable niche, NKT's scale is competitive. The relevant market is narrow enough that NKT holds ~30% market share in HVDC submarine cables.

Rating: B+ — NKT has meaningful scale advantages within its niche but remains small relative to Prysmian overall. The Karlskrona expansion will improve scale economics in HV. The dual-vessel fleet will be a significant advantage from 2027.

Sceptical points:
- Prysmian's scale advantage is structural and growing. NKT cannot close this gap organically.
- The addition of new capacity at Karlskrona must be matched by order intake. If fill rates disappoint, the operating leverage works in reverse.
- Diseconomies of scale may emerge as NKT manages increasingly complex, simultaneous mega-projects across multiple geographies.

11. Delivery Over Last 3 Years (2023-2025)

2023: Breakout year. 36% organic growth — the fastest in company history. Solutions delivered 59% organic growth as major projects (TenneT, CHPE) ramped up. Operational EBITDA reached EUR 244m, shattering the previous record. HV order backlog surged to EUR 10.8bn. The NKT Victoria cable-laying vessel was deployed on multiple projects simultaneously.

2024: Consolidation and reinvestment. 26% organic growth continued the momentum. EBITDA hit EUR 344m (new record). SolidAl acquisition (June 2024) added MV/HV capacity in Portugal. NKT Photonics divestiture completed — the company became a pure-play cable business. FCF was strong at EUR 400m despite rising capex. The HV backlog held at EUR 10.6bn.

2025: Record EBITDA despite growth deceleration. 6% organic growth reflected the CHPE ramp-down and the constraint of existing HV capacity (new Karlskrona capacity not yet online). EBITDA reached EUR 390m (new record). Every segment contributed margin expansion. The EUR 2.2bn Eastern Green Link 3 contract was signed (largest single order ever). Capex peaked at EUR 743m. FCF was negative EUR 244m. Net cash position remained EUR 963m.

Guidance accuracy:
- 2023: NKT raised guidance multiple times during the year. Delivered above original guidance.
- 2024: Similar pattern — repeated guidance upgrades. Strong beat vs. original expectations.
- 2025: Guidance was raised at Q2 and Q3. Full-year EBITDA of EUR 390m was within the final guided range.
- Track record of under-promising and over-delivering — a positive signal for management credibility.

Rating: A — Three consecutive years of record results, repeated guidance upgrades, and strategic transformation milestones (SolidAl acquisition, Photonics divestiture, Charging Forward launch). Execution has been exemplary.

Sceptical points:
- The 2023-2025 period benefited from an unusually benign combination: massive order intake, high factory utilisation, favourable project mix, and rising metal prices. This will not persist indefinitely.
- The CHPE ramp-down in 2025/2026 creates a natural gap in HV revenue before new Karlskrona capacity fills. This gap is visible in the 2026 guidance (mid-single-digit Solutions decline).
- Management has not been tested in a downturn under the current leadership. The current team took over in 2023 — at the start of the greatest upcycle in cable industry history.

14. Industry Structure and Value Chain

Value chain in HV cable systems:

  1. Raw materials: Copper, aluminium, polyethylene (XLPE insulation), lead sheathing. Commodity inputs — no supplier lock-in. Copper typically represents 30-40% of cable cost at actual metal prices.
  2. Cable design and engineering: High-value, knowledge-intensive. Proprietary designs protected by trade secrets and type-test certifications. Significant barrier to entry.
  3. Manufacturing: Capital-intensive. Extrusion towers (200m+ tall for HV cables) require EUR 300m+ investment and 3-4 years to build. Global HV extrusion capacity is limited to ~10-15 towers worldwide.
  4. Transportation and logistics: HV cables are heavy, bulky, and must be loaded onto cable-laying vessels or large transport drums. Specialised logistics capabilities required.
  5. Installation: Submarine cable installation requires dedicated cable-laying vessels (EUR 300-500m each, 3-4 year build time). Onshore installation requires civil engineering (trenching, directional drilling). Weather and sea-state dependent.
  6. Testing and commissioning: Extensive pre-commissioning testing is required. Type-test certifications from independent bodies are mandatory.
  7. Service and maintenance: Ongoing repair preparedness, monitoring, and maintenance create a growing aftermarket. Average cable system lifetime is 40-50 years.

Industry concentration: The HV submarine cable market is a tight oligopoly. Three European players (Prysmian, Nexans, NKT) plus two Asian players (Sumitomo Electric, LS Cable) account for virtually all global capacity. Entry barriers include:
- Capital intensity (EUR 1bn+ for a greenfield factory + vessel)
- Qualification timelines (5-7 years for type-testing and TSO pre-qualification)
- Track record requirements (TSOs require demonstrated project execution capability)
- Vessel scarcity (global cable-laying vessel fleet is ~15-20 vessels)
- Technology (XLPE HVDC cable design at 525 kV is state-of-the-art)

Competitive dynamics: The industry is transitioning from cyclical price competition to strategic capacity allocation. With backlogs stretching 5-7 years, manufacturers can be selective about which projects to accept. This is shifting pricing power towards manufacturers and improving margin visibility.

Rating: A — Industry structure is highly favourable for incumbents. The combination of oligopoly, extreme barriers to entry, and structural demand growth creates a textbook case of durable competitive advantage.

Sceptical points:
- The benign competitive environment is partly a function of the current demand surge. In a downturn, the triopoly could revert to aggressive price competition.
- State-backed Chinese competitors (ZTT, Hengtong) have unlimited capital and could potentially build HV capacity within 5-7 years if they make a strategic commitment to the European market.
- Regulatory intervention is possible. If cable capacity becomes a true bottleneck for the energy transition, governments could subsidise new entrants or mandate capacity sharing.
