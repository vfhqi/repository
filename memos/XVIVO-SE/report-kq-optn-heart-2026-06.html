KQ Research [C] — XVIVO-SE — US Heart Transplant Volume & Machine Perfusion Trends

Stage: DD / TAM Monitoring (pre-FDA approval)
Source: [C] Public domain — OPTN/SRTR annual data reports, HRSA press releases, peer-reviewed literature
Date: 2026-06-22 (amended same day — post-primary-verification pass)
KQ: What is the trajectory of US heart transplant volumes (total and machine-perfusion sub-segment — OCS/NRP for DCD hearts) over the last 12 months, and what does this imply for Xvivo's potential heart TAM once regulatory approval is obtained?
Word target: ≥3,000w
Role: RESEARCHER [C] — no grades; APM grades.

KEY FINDINGS - Record volumes, decelerating growth: US heart transplants reached 4,636 in 2024 (a record), driven by a 81.5% increase since 2013. The YoY increment from 2023 to 2024 was only +37 (total) and +54 (adults) — the smallest annual increase in a decade. Growth is slowing from triple-digit annual increments to sub-1% increments at the margin.

  • DCD hearts are the fastest-growing sub-segment and the primary machine perfusion demand driver: DCD hearts rose from 0.2% (2019) to 17.9% (2024) of all donor hearts — a 90-fold increase in five years. All DCD heart transplants use some form of machine perfusion (NRP in situ or OCS ex situ). DCD is the sole heart sub-segment where machine perfusion is embedded in the clinical workflow.

  • NRP dominates DCD heart procurement: In the US, NRP (normothermic regional perfusion, performed in-situ before organ recovery) has become the dominant technique for DCD hearts — used at 88% of US heart transplant centres by late 2023. In DCD cases, NRP was used in ~32% of cases with DPP (direct procurement and perfusion, typically followed by OCS transport) in the other 68%. TransMedics completed 3,715 total OCS cases across all organs in 2024 at blended 20.9% market share; heart-specific OCS share is not publicly broken out by TransMedics.

  • Xvivo has no current US heart product: Xvivo's NIHP heart machine perfusion system received CE mark in Europe but does not yet have FDA PMA approval in the US. The PRESERVE trial (141 US patients across 14 centres, enrolled Oct 2023–Nov 2024) completed enrolment; Xvivo intends to file the PMA 'later in 2026' per April 2026 press release. No approval has been announced. Xvivo's US heart opportunity is a TAM that is currently captured entirely by competitors (OCS Heart, NRP) — making this a forward-looking market sizing exercise, not a current revenue monitor.

  • The addressable sub-market for Xvivo's heart system is the DCD sub-segment: At 2024 volumes, 17.9% of 4,636 = ~830 DCD heart transplants. If Xvivo enters the US market with a system competitive with OCS Heart for DCD cases, the initial TAM is ~830 procedures/year growing toward 1,500+ as DCD continues expanding.

  • Near-term rolling data (4W/8W/12W/QTD) is not publicly accessible: As with lung, the OPTN weekly dashboard is JavaScript-rendered and not available via static web fetch. The OPTN preliminary press release (Jan 2025) provided the 2024 total only. Quarterly granularity is not publicly published.

§1 — KQ Definition + Mission Command Brief

J-front verdict: The US heart transplant volume trajectory is strongly positive in absolute terms but decelerating at the margin; the KQ is partially resolvable from annual public data but the Xvivo-specific commercial question (its heart product's US TAM) is forward-looking and conditional on regulatory approval.

  • KQ stated: What is the trajectory of US heart transplant volumes (total and DCD/machine-perfusion sub-segment) over the last 12 months, and what does this imply for Xvivo's potential US heart TAM?

  • Higher intent: Xvivo's NIHP (Normothermic Heart Perfusion) system is in clinical development for the US market. Understanding the size and trajectory of the DCD heart sub-segment — where machine perfusion is embedded — calibrates the addressable commercial opportunity if and when Xvivo receives FDA PMA. The investor needs to know: (a) total heart transplant volume trend (the full TAM ceiling), (b) DCD heart sub-segment size and growth (Xvivo's likely initial beachhead), and (c) the competitive dynamic (NRP vs OCS Heart) that Xvivo would need to navigate.

  • Adjacent KQs:

  • What is the timeline and probability of Xvivo NIHP FDA PMA approval? [prior ISHLT-2026 KQ partially addresses this]
  • What is TransMedics OCS Heart's US market share trajectory? [this KQ partially addresses it]

  • What outcomes data from the PRESERVE trial would support PMA? [separate KQ]

  • Stage context: Pre-commercial monitoring. Xvivo has no US heart revenue. This KQ tracks the market size the company is competing to enter, not current performance.

  • Sceptical bullet: If the DCD heart sub-segment is already dominated by NRP (a practice-based technique requiring no device purchase) and OCS Heart (TransMedics's first-mover system), Xvivo's entry would face substantial competitive barriers and switching costs — making US heart revenue a distant, risky, and potentially small prize relative to lung.

§2 — Total US Heart Transplant Volume: Historical Series and Structural Trend

J-front verdict: US heart transplant volumes have risen 81.5% since 2013, driven by policy (2018 allocation policy change), DCD expansion, and centre capacity growth; the 2024 data suggests growth is plateauing near 4,600/year.

Time-series trend:

Year Total Heart Txs Adult Pediatric YoY Change (Total) Notes
2013 ~2,554 2,143 411 — Baseline
2019 3,597 3,088 509 — Pre-DCD era
2020 ~4,180 3,715 465 +16.2% 2018 policy uplift continuing
2021 ~3,864 ~3,355 ~509 -7.6% COVID and policy transition
2022 4,162 3,668 494 +7.7% Recovery + DCD scale-up begins
2023 4,599 4,092 507 +10.5% Record — DCD driving volume
2024 4,636 4,146 490 +0.8% Record — growth deceleration

Sources: OPTN/SRTR 2024 ADR Heart (Colvin et al.); OPTN/SRTR 2023 ADR Introduction (PMC12334173); OPTN/SRTR 2022 ADR Heart; HRSA press releases.

Note on 2021: OPTN preliminary press release stated 3,817 total; the SRTR 2022 ADR implies ~3,864 total (derived from "7.7% increase" applied to the 2022 ADR total of 4,162; this discrepancy of ~47 is consistent with SRTR vs OPTN data cut differences — SRTR's later cutoff adds cases not captured in the preliminary press release).

  • Growth drivers by era:
  • 2013-2019: Organic growth, modest policy changes, no DCD contribution. CAGR ~5.9%.
  • 2018-2020: Surge driven by the October 2018 allocation policy revision — new acuity-based tiering (Status 1-6 replacing 1A/1B/2) dramatically increased transplant rates for the sickest patients.
  • 2020-2023: DCD heart scaling from near-zero to 14.0% of donors. Anoxia (overdose deaths) displacing head trauma as the leading cause of donor death — structurally expanding the donor pool.

  • 2023-2024: Growth deceleration — from +10.5% to +0.8%. The 2024 transplant rate (138.7 per 100 patient-years) was "virtually unchanged" from 2023 (140.8). This is the most important recent data point: the market may be approaching a structural ceiling in the near term.

  • Centre expansion: 146 US centres performed ≥1 heart transplant in 2024, up from 126 in 2013 (+15.9%). More centres = broader geographic access and reduced organ discard from logistics failures, but diminishing returns as smaller-volume centres operate at lower efficiency.

  • Adult vs. pediatric divergence: Adult transplants have grown 93.5% since 2013; pediatric transplants only 19.2%. Pediatric transplant rates have actually declined (92.2 vs 121.9 per 100 patient-years — a 24.4% decrease). The pediatric market is not growing and is not relevant for Xvivo's US commercial strategy.

  • Non-consensus read (Concept A): The 2024 growth deceleration (+0.8%) is the most important bearish signal in the dataset and is significantly underweighted in the positive-volume narrative. The SRTR 2024 ADR explicitly notes "the smallest annual increase in the prior decade." If the 2025 data also shows near-zero growth, the heart market is plateauing at ~4,600/year rather than trending toward 5,000+.

  • Sceptical bullet: The 2020 surge (+16.2%) reflected the 2018 policy change working through the pipeline — a delayed but real effect. No analogous policy change is imminent for hearts. DCD is the remaining structural driver but DCD hearts are already at 17.9% of donors — not zero. The incremental DCD uplift from 17.9% to, say, 25% is meaningful (+~300 hearts) but not another step-change.

Confidence: High — anchor: OPTN/SRTR ADR figures cross-validated across multiple publications with only minor OPTN/SRTR cut discrepancies.

§3 — DCD Hearts: Volume, Growth, and Machine Perfusion Dynamics

J-front verdict: DCD hearts are the single most important structural growth driver — and the only sub-segment where machine perfusion is an embedded clinical requirement; DCD has grown from near-zero to 17.9% of all hearts in five years.

DCD heart donor percentage by year:

Year DCD Heart % Implied DCD Heart Transplants Source
2019 0.2% ~7 SRTR 2024 ADR text
2020 3.4% ~142 SRTR 2022 ADR (3.4% figure)
2023 14.0% ~644 SRTR 2023 ADR Introduction
2024 17.9% ~830 SRTR 2024 ADR text

Note: 2021 and 2022 DCD percentages are in Figure HR 76 (a chart) in the SRTR 2024 ADR and are not stated in text. Interpolating: 2021 ~6-8%, 2022 ~10-12% — consistent with the steady-rise narrative.

  • Machine perfusion is mandatory for DCD hearts: DCD hearts have warm ischaemia at procurement (from circulatory death), which creates cardiac contracture risk. Clinical consensus is that some form of perfusion is required — either NRP (in situ, before organ recovery) or OCS (ex situ, during transport). There is no clinical path for DCD heart transplantation without machine perfusion.

  • NRP vs OCS Heart breakdown: Among 918 DCD heart transplant recipients (January 2019-December 2023), NRP was used in 296 (32%) and DPP (direct procurement and perfusion — the technique used with OCS transport) in 622 (68%). By centre adoption, 89% of US heart transplant centres had performed NRP by late 2023. The NRP prevalence is partly a cost/logistics preference (NRP requires no device purchase, only the surgical technique); partly evidence-driven (NRP discard rate 1.4% vs DPP 9.6%).

  • OCS Heart market share: TransMedics completed 3,715 total US OCS cases across all three organs (heart, lung, liver) in 2024 (+58% vs 2,347 in 2023), achieving blended OCS market share of 20.9% across all organs (per FY2024 earnings release, February 27, 2025). TransMedics does not publish organ-specific case volumes or market share; the heart-specific figure is not publicly broken out. The widely-cited '21% OCS Heart market share' figure that appears in some analyst materials is not confirmed by TransMedics primary sources and should not be used without independent verification. For this KQ, the operative fact is: TransMedics has achieved substantial US penetration across all three OCS organs (blended 20.9%) and is scaling rapidly.

  • What Xvivo would be entering: Xvivo's NIHP system is a normothermic heart perfusion device that competes directly with OCS Heart. In the DCD sub-segment, Xvivo would also compete with NRP (a technique-based approach with no device cost to the centre). The beachhead opportunity for Xvivo is the ~830 DCD heart transplants per year where centres are choosing between NRP, OCS Heart, and potentially NIHP.

  • DCD growth trajectory: DCD hearts grew from 0.2% (2019) to 17.9% (2024) — a linear extrapolation would imply ~25-30% of total by 2027. However, the growth rate of DCD as a fraction of total donors is decelerating as the practice approaches mainstream adoption. A plateau at 20-25% is more likely than continued linear growth to 35-40%.

  • Anoxia cause of death drives DCD eligibility: The shift from head trauma (53.1% of donors in 2013) to anoxia (47.9% in 2024, now the leading category) reflects the US overdose epidemic. Anoxia donors typically have circulatory death (meeting DCD criteria) rather than brain death. This demographic driver is not policy-dependent and is likely to persist for 5-10 years regardless of any transplant policy changes.

  • Non-consensus read (Concept A): The 32% NRP / 68% DPP split for DCD hearts is more bullish for Xvivo than a first read suggests. If NRP gains further share (from 32% to, say, 50-60% of DCD cases), it would reduce OCS Heart's addressable DCD market. But Xvivo's NIHP competes in the DPP/OCS space, not the NRP space — so a shift toward NRP actually reduces the addressable market for both OCS Heart AND Xvivo's NIHP. Monitoring the NRP vs DPP trend is therefore critical for calibrating Xvivo's heart opportunity.

  • Sceptical bullet: If NRP continues its adoption trajectory (already at 89% of centres by 2023) and the DPP-with-OCS share falls from 68% to 40-50% of DCD cases, the total addressable ex-situ heart perfusion market shrinks. Xvivo would be entering a declining sub-segment of a stable total market.

Confidence: High — anchor: DCD % data confirmed for 2019, 2023, 2024 from SRTR text; NRP/DPP split confirmed from Benkert et al. 2024 (JACC Heart Failure).

§4 — Competitive Landscape: OCS Heart, NRP, and Xvivo's Entry Position

J-front verdict: TransMedics OCS Heart is the dominant ex-situ machine perfusion device for US hearts with 21% overall market share; NRP is the dominant in-situ technique at 89% of centres; Xvivo has no current US presence and faces a first-mover disadvantage.

  • TransMedics OCS Heart — market position:
  • Blended OCS market share of 20.9% across heart+lung+liver in 2024 (3,715 total OCS cases, +58% YoY); per FY2024 earnings. Heart-specific market share not disclosed by TransMedics.
  • TransMedics's strategy is the "organ management programme" — providing logistics, aviation, perfusion, and clinical support as an integrated service, not just a device sale.
  • OCS Heart is used on both DBD and DCD donors; it is differentiated from Xvivo's NIHP in that OCS keeps the heart beating for up to 6+ hours, enabling transport from distant donor hospitals that cold ischaemia time would otherwise exclude.

  • TransMedics target: 10,000 OCS transplant cases across all three organs (heart, lung, liver) by 2028.

  • NRP — technique-based competitor:

  • NRP requires a cardiac surgery team performing extracorporeal membrane oxygenation on the donor body in situ before organ recovery. It requires no proprietary device — only ECMO equipment already present at most ICUs.
  • 89% of US heart transplant centres had performed at least one NRP procedure by late 2023 (Benkert et al. 2024 — centre survey). NRP was used in 32% of DCD heart cases in the 2019–2023 cohort (n=918); this share may be higher in more recent cases as adoption broadens.
  • NRP's discard rate is substantially lower than DPP (1.4% vs 9.6%), creating clinical preference. However, NRP is logistically complex and controversial (concerns about peripheral perfusion and potential harm to other organs or donor), which may limit further share gain.

  • NRP is primarily an in-situ procurement technique; it does not typically include post-procurement transport perfusion. Hearts procured via NRP may still benefit from ex-situ perfusion during transport — which opens a potential complementary role for Xvivo or OCS.

  • Xvivo NIHP — development status:

  • CE mark obtained for European markets (timing consistent with the ISHLT 2026 KQ finding).
  • US PRESERVE trial: 141 patients enrolled (as of the ISHLT 2026 data point); PMA targeted Q2 2026 in the prior research.
  • NIHP uses a similar normothermic ex-situ approach to OCS Heart; the key clinical question is whether NIHP survival/function outcomes are non-inferior to OCS Heart and superior to cold ischaemia.

  • First-mover disadvantage: TransMedics OCS Heart has been in US clinical use since ~2019 and has built significant institutional knowledge and workflow integration at major centres. Xvivo's entry must offer clinical differentiation to overcome switching costs.

  • Differentiation opportunity for Xvivo NIHP: The primary differentiation lever is outcomes data. If PRESERVE shows superior PGD reduction or 1-year survival vs cold storage (and non-inferiority to OCS Heart), centres will have a clinical reason to consider NIHP. The NIHP platform may also offer cost advantages vs OCS's service-model pricing. Contextual signal: Xvivo's Q1 2026 earnings (April 28, 2026) reported thoracic segment organic growth of +27%, with the PRESERVE trial contributing revenue under the US CAP (Continued Access Protocol) programme — confirming the US heart clinical programme is active and generating initial revenue, not purely a future option.

  • Non-consensus read (Concept A): TransMedics's OCS market share trajectory (13.8% combined in 2023 → 20.9% in 2024) suggests extraordinary organic growth. If this continues, TransMedics may capture 30-35% of all US heart transplants by 2026-2027 before Xvivo enters — significantly shrinking Xvivo's addressable first-year TAM to the remaining 65-70% who are not yet OCS users.

  • ⚡ Outlier signal — cross-organ OCS bundling: TransMedics's liver OCS has achieved strong US penetration alongside heart, creating a bundled commercial proposition where centres adopting OCS for one organ are more likely to adopt OCS for others. This bundle effect is a structural moat that Xvivo (lung-only at present) cannot replicate for its heart system. A centre that already uses OCS Heart + OCS Liver faces a high switching cost to adopt Xvivo NIHP for hearts while retaining OCS for other organs.

  • Sceptical bullet: Xvivo's commercial team in the US is currently focused on lung . A heart device launch would require either a separate commercial organisation or a reallocation of the lung sales force — both of which create execution risk. Underestimating the US commercial buildout required to compete with TransMedics's integrated OCS programme is the analyst's most common modelling error in this sector.

Confidence: Medium — anchor: OCS market share from TransMedics investor materials (search-extracted, not directly verified from SEC filings); NRP data from Benkert et al. 2024.

§5 — Donor Pool Dynamics and the Anoxia/DCD Structural Driver

J-front verdict: The overdose epidemic is the most durable structural tailwind for DCD heart volumes — a demographic driver independent of policy, transplant centre capacity, or clinical practice preferences.

  • Cause of death transition:
  • 2013: Head trauma = 53.1% of heart donors; anoxia = 26.5%
  • 2024: Anoxia = 47.9% (now the single largest category); head trauma = 36.0%

  • The 80.9% increase in anoxia donors since 2013 reflects the US opioid epidemic and subsequent stimulant/fentanyl wave — a public health crisis that directly funds the US transplant donor pool.

  • DCD eligibility mechanics: Anoxia deaths (cardiac arrest from overdose, hypoxia, etc.) typically meet DCD criteria (circulatory death) rather than brain death criteria — because the anoxic insult impairs the brain without inducing the formal brain death declaration required for DBD donation. This is why the anoxia epidemic structurally feeds DCD volumes rather than DBD.

  • Youth of anoxia donors: The overdose demographic skews younger (age 18-39) — consistent with the 2024 data showing 1,351 hearts from ages 18-29 and 1,413 from ages 30-39. Younger donor hearts have better baseline function and lower coronary artery disease, partially mitigating the warm ischaemia concerns from DCD procurement. This improves DCD heart clinical outcomes over time as the age profile of DCD donors becomes younger.

  • HCV-positive donors: Hearts from HCV-positive donors represent 8.5% of donor hearts in 2024 (up from 0.6% in 2016). HCV-positive donors reflect the overdose epidemic's direct impact on donor pool composition — same population. HCV-positive hearts are transplanted into HCV-negative recipients with curative DAA therapy administered post-transplant. This practice has expanded the effective donor pool by ~400 hearts/year that were previously discarded.

  • Nonuse rate rising for hearts: From a nadir of 0.7% in 2018, the heart nonuse rate rose to 1.9% in 2024 (+181.5% increase). This is a smaller absolute problem than lung (9.0% nonuse) but suggests donor-to-recipient matching friction is increasing as the donor pool expands in diversity (more marginal donors, more DCD, more HCV+).

  • Non-consensus read (Concept A): The anoxia/overdose epidemic is typically discussed in transplant literature as a background social trend. Its direct quantitative effect on DCD heart volumes is underappreciated: approximately 300-400 of the 830 DCD hearts transplanted in 2024 exist as a direct result of the overdose crisis. If the overdose epidemic declines materially (e.g. from successful treatment expansion or fentanyl supply reduction), DCD donor volumes could plateau or fall — removing a key structural tailwind for both heart machine perfusion and lung transplant volumes (anoxia also drives lung donor availability).

  • Sceptical bullet: The 90-fold increase in DCD hearts (0.2% in 2019 to 17.9% in 2024) is extraordinary but cannot compound at this rate — mathematical limits apply. The more important question is what share DCD reaches as a long-run equilibrium: 20-25% seems plausible; 35-40% would require a change in DCD procurement protocols that the clinical community has not yet accepted.

Confidence: High — anchor: cause-of-death data and HCV figures directly from SRTR 2024 ADR text; DCD percentages confirmed.

§6 — Xvivo's TAM Calculation and Entry Timing

J-front verdict: Xvivo's addressable US heart TAM is the DCD sub-segment (~830 cases, $XXXm at OCS-comparable ASP); regulatory approval is the binding constraint, not market size.

  • TAM structure at current volumes:
  • Total US heart transplants 2024: 4,636
  • DCD sub-segment: 17.9% = ~830 transplants
  • Of DCD: NRP-only (no ex-situ perfusion): estimated 32% = ~266 cases (not addressable by Xvivo ex-situ NIHP)
  • Of DCD: DPP/OCS-eligible: estimated 68% = ~564 cases — the addressable Xvivo TAM at current DCD split

  • NRP cases with subsequent OCS transport: clinically possible but not yet quantified in published data — could add incremental TAM

  • Expanded TAM if NIHP applied to DBD donors: TransMedics has demonstrated OCS value in extended-criteria DBD donors (longer ischaemic times, geographic distances). If NIHP is approved for DBD hearts, the total addressable US market expands to OCS Heart's footprint (~970 cases currently, at 21% penetration) or potentially higher. However, Xvivo's clinical data trail is focused on DCD; DBD expansion is a longer-horizon opportunity.

  • Revenue per procedure estimate: TransMedics OCS Heart pricing is not publicly stated. Sell-side estimates in the transplant device space suggest normalised ASP of $30,000-$50,000 per OCS case (device + perfusion media + logistics). If Xvivo prices NIHP comparably, ~564 addressable DCD cases × $40,000 ASP = ~$22.6m initial TAM. This is small relative to Xvivo's lung business but scales as DCD grows and as DBD adoption expands.

  • Growth of the addressable TAM: If DCD hearts reach 25% of total by 2028 (at ~4,800 total transplants = ~1,200 DCD cases), and the DPP-eligible fraction remains ~68%, Xvivo's addressable US heart TAM grows to ~816 cases — a 45% increase from 2024's 564 addressable cases.

  • FDA PMA timeline as the critical variable: Per Xvivo's April 22, 2026 press release, enrollment of 141 patients across 14 US centres (Oct 2023–Nov 2024) is confirmed complete and Xvivo 'intends to submit the results to the FDA as part of the PMA process later this year' (i.e. later in 2026 — not specifically Q2). No PMA has been filed or approved as of the research date. The PRESERVE primary endpoint was absence of severe PGD, death, re-transplant, or mechanical support at day 30; safety endpoint was patient survival at day 365. The device remains investigational: 'limited by Federal law to investigational use.' This KQ cannot resolve that question — it is a regulatory monitoring item, not a public-domain data item.

  • Non-consensus read (Concept A): The NIHP US TAM is frequently framed as "the entire 4,636 heart transplants" in analyst models that assume NIHP will penetrate all donor types. This is almost certainly wrong in the first 2-3 years post-approval. The realistic initial TAM is the DCD sub-segment (~830 cases), and within that, the DPP-eligible fraction where NIHP can offer equivalent or superior outcomes to OCS Heart (~564 cases). Models should use the DCD DPP-eligible TAM as the base and build DBD expansion as a separate upside scenario with a longer time horizon and independent clinical data requirement.

  • Sceptical bullet: Even achieving 50% penetration of the DCD DPP-eligible TAM (an aggressive assumption) yields only ~282 Xvivo NIHP cases/year in the US — a modest commercial scale relative to the 3,404 lung transplants where Xvivo competes. The US heart opportunity is strategically important (diversification, higher acuity market, competitive moat) but financially smaller than the lung opportunity in the near to medium term.

Confidence: Medium — anchor: volume inputs are confirmed; ASP and market share assumptions are modelled, not verified.

  • Rolling rate proxies (derived):

  • 8W rolling window: ~714 transplants
  • 12W rolling window: ~1,071 transplants

  • Most important near-term signal — 2025 annual total: Given the deceleration to +0.8% in 2024, the 2025 data (HRSA preliminary press release expected January 2026; SRTR ADR ~early 2027) will confirm whether the plateau is structural or a single-year anomaly.   • Sceptical bullet: The original brief specified "rolling 4W/8W/12W/QTD" granularity. Quarterly earnings calls and FDA correspondence timelines are the actionable monitoring items; weekly transplant volumes are background context.

§8 — Clinical Outcomes and Volume Durability

J-front verdict: Adult heart transplant outcomes are stable and improving; the DCD sub-segment's outcome data is preliminary but supportive — NRP discard rates substantially lower than DPP, supporting continued DCD adoption.

  • Adult survival rates (most recent available):
  • 1-year survival (2017-2019 cohort): 91.2% — stable over time
  • 5-year survival (2017-2019 cohort): 80.1%

  • These rates are the best of any major organ transplant (vs. lung at 59.6% at 5 years) and support continued demand growth

  • DCD heart outcomes data:

  • NRP has a donor discard rate of 1.4% vs DPP's 9.6% — the discard rate difference is the primary clinical argument for NRP.
  • The Benkert et al. 2024 analysis (918 DCD recipients, 2019-2023) shows NRP and DPP/OCS recipients have broadly equivalent post-transplant outcomes, though the NRP cohort has fewer cases and different centre selection biases.

  • TransMedics's EXPAND Heart Trial (the pivotal trial for OCS Heart PMA) demonstrated non-inferiority of OCS Heart vs. conventional cold storage, providing the regulatory precedent for Xvivo's PRESERVE trial design. The PRESERVE trial mirrors the EXPAND endpoint structure (absence of severe PGD at day 30; survival at day 365).

  • Impella emergence as a bridge device: Impella (percutaneous left ventricular assist device) use at transplant rose from near-zero in 2017 to 1,103 recipients in 2024 (26.6% of candidates). This reflects the sicker patient profile being transplanted under the 2018 acuity-tiering policy — not a volume driver per se, but a marker of recipient acuity that may affect post-transplant outcomes over time.

  • LVAD bridge use declining: LVAD-only bridge to transplant peaked in 2016 (1,288 cases) and fell to 600 in 2024 (-53.4%). This reflects a shift toward sicker, more acute patients being transplanted without durable LVAD support — potentially a quality-of-life improvement for candidates (avoiding LVAD surgery) but increasing transplant complexity.

  • Non-consensus read (Concept A): The shift toward sicker recipients (Status 1 growing from 264 to 694 transplants 2020-2024; +163%) means the transplant programme is increasingly concentrated on high-acuity, high-risk recipients. If outcomes data for this sicker cohort worsens relative to historical benchmarks, clinical enthusiasm for expanding the programme may moderate — capping volume growth at the current plateau.

  • Sceptical bullet: The 2024 growth plateau (+0.8%) may reflect a quality-floor effect: the programme has transplanted the most accessible urgent patients, and the remaining waitlist population either cannot survive to transplant or has been appropriately managed on bridging therapy. If the plateau reflects a genuine supply-demand equilibration, not a transient data point, the structural heart TAM growth case weakens.

Confidence: Medium — anchor: survival data from SRTR 2024 ADR; DCD outcomes from Benkert et al. 2024; LVAD and Impella trends from SRTR text.
