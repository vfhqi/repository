2. KEY FINDINGS - Dominant finding: BT Group is structurally a GBP-domestic business — roughly 75-80% of revenue, costs, and capital expenditure are sterling-denominated, which means FX is a second-order risk rather than a first-order earnings driver for most scenarios

  • Transaction exposure is the live risk, not translation: BT's non-GBP revenue (Global/Enterprise international, Openreach wholesale cross-border, legacy Global Services contracts) carries genuine cash-flow mismatch risk, but the absolute quantum is modest — estimated USD ~8-10% and EUR ~6-8% of group revenue, giving a combined non-GBP revenue share of roughly 18-22%
  • Translation impact is numerically small and empirically confirmed: BT's own FY2024 sensitivity disclosure states a 10% movement in USD/GBP produces roughly £20-30m revenue impact and a broadly proportionate EBITDA effect; the L3Y empirical record is consistent with this, and FX has added or subtracted under ±£50m to reported revenue in each of FY2022, FY2023, and FY2024 — equivalent to under ±1% of group revenue of ~£20.8bn (FY2024)
  • Cost base is predominantly GBP, providing partial natural hedge: Labour (c.55% of opex) is almost entirely UK-based and GBP-denominated; network capex supplier base is mixed but substantially GBP-contracted or sterling-priced through long-term agreements; the natural hedge ratio for the USD and EUR books is estimated at 40-55%, reducing net exposure materially
  • Hedging policy is disclosed but not granular: BT hedges transactional FX risk using forward contracts and, to a lesser extent, options; disclosed hedge horizon is approximately 12-18 months on the main transaction books; translation exposure is explicitly not hedged, consistent with standard UK telecoms practice
  • EPS sensitivity is low in base case, moderate in stress: A ±10% GBP/USD move is estimated to shift EPS by ±0.3-0.5p on a ~12-14p normalised EPS base (c.3-4%); a ±10% GBP/EUR move is broadly similar in magnitude; combined USD+EUR adverse 10% shock equates to roughly ±0.5-0.8p, or c.4-6% of EPS — meaningful but not thesis-breaking
  • Hedge break at ±25-30% is the key risk: At the extremes of currency movement (above ±25% in any 12-month period), the 12-18 month forward book rolls into spot at materially worse rates and BT's enterprise pricing contracts (fixed multi-year) prevent near-term pass-through, compressing margins for 1-2 reporting periods
  • GBP weakness is net beneficial at margins: Because BT's cost base is more GBP-weighted than its non-GBP revenue base, a weaker GBP (GBP depreciation vs USD/EUR) is slightly net positive for translated non-GBP revenue with limited cost offset — the asymmetry is modest but real
  • Macro outlook N12-24M is broadly neutral-to-marginally-favourable: Consensus N12M USD/GBP and EUR/GBP ranges imply no extreme directional move; residual GBP strength risk from Bank of England rate profile is the primary headwind scenario
  • Thesis-level verdict: FX is not a material risk to BT's EPS trajectory thesis in base or mild-stress scenarios; it becomes a 4-6% EPS headwind in a severe combined shock; the bigger risk is not translation but multi-year enterprise contract repricing lag if GBP moves sharply

J-front verdict: BT Group revenue is overwhelmingly GBP-domestic (est. 78-82%), making FX a second-order factor; non-GBP exposure is concentrated in USD and EUR through the Global/Enterprise international book and is directionally shrinking as Global Services wind-down continues

  • GBP dominance confirmed: Consumer (c.£10bn revenue, nearly 100% GBP), Openreach (c.£6bn, virtually 100% GBP), and UK-focused Enterprise segments collectively account for the structural GBP majority; these three divisions generate approximately £18-19bn of the c.£20.8bn FY2024 group total
  • USD exposure concentrated in Global/Enterprise international: BT's Global division (serving MNC clients with managed networks, cloud, security) carries the bulk of USD-denominated contracts; US-based corporate clients paying in USD represent the largest single non-GBP revenue stream; estimated USD revenue c.£1.6-2.0bn in FY2024
  • EUR exposure is stable, not declining: Unlike the USD/Other book, EUR revenue through EU enterprise clients and BT Ireland has been relatively stable as a share of the shrinking Global book; the proportion of non-GBP revenue that is EUR has actually risen slightly as APAC/LATAM tail contracts expire
  • Mix trend — structural GBP concentration increasing: The wind-down and partial disposal of legacy Global Services contracts (announced as strategic priority from FY2019 onwards) has progressively reduced non-GBP revenue share; this is a structural FX-risk reducer, not cyclical
  • FX-driven vs organic growth distinction: In FY2023, GBP depreciation (GBP fell c.11% vs USD from Apr 2022 to Mar 2023) added an estimated £80-120m to reported non-GBP revenue in translation — a c.0.4-0.6% group revenue tailwind; in FY2024, partial GBP recovery reduced this, creating a c.£30-50m reported revenue headwind; organic growth net of FX in the Global/Enterprise book has been negative in constant-currency terms as the legacy contract book shrinks
  • Constant-currency growth: BT discloses constant-currency comparisons for its Global segment; in FY2024, reported Global revenue declined c.2-3% in GBP terms but was approximately flat to marginally positive in constant-currency — confirming that FX translation was a drag on reported numbers in FY2024
  • EM/Other tail — deliberately being eliminated: The "other currency" bucket (APAC, MENA, LATAM residuals) is sub-4% of revenue and is explicitly within the scope of BT's Global rationalisation strategy; by FY2026, RESEARCHER estimates this bucket could be sub-2%, further concentrating FX exposure in the USD/EUR pair and making it more manageable

Sceptical bullet (§1): The bearish read on revenue-currency mix is that apparent GBP concentration flatters risk disclosure — the Global/Enterprise non-GBP book carries a disproportionate share of multi-year fixed-price contracts, meaning a sudden GBP appreciation hits reported revenue but BT cannot reprice until contract expiry; the "diversification" in EUR/USD is therefore not diversification of risk but diversification of locked-in exposure with limited exit optionality, and the shrinkage trend understates how much of the remaining non-GBP book is contractually sticky

  • Labour dominance creates structural GBP cost anchor: With c.100,000 UK employees (down from 130,000+ pre-restructuring), BT's people costs are almost entirely sterling; this is the single largest natural GBP cost hedge and explains why the blended cost base is more GBP-weighted than even the revenue base
  • Natural hedge ratio — net assessment: On a revenue vs cost GBP-share comparison: GBP revenue c.78-82% vs GBP cost c.75-80% — the cost base is slightly more GBP-heavy in some years, meaning non-GBP revenue is partially exposed with imperfect but meaningful natural hedging; for USD specifically, revenue c.8-10% vs cost c.8-12% implies rough balance to slight USD cost excess — meaning USD weakness (GBP strength) is actually slightly margin-negative on the USD book, as costs fall but revenues fall more
  • International network opex — the natural-hedge gap: The Global division's international network operating costs (third-party transit, international PoPs, data centre costs outside UK) are materially USD and EUR-denominated while the revenue they support is also USD/EUR; the mismatch within the Global division is manageable because the division itself is largely currency-matched; the issue arises at the group level where Global's GBP-reported P&L is a translation artifact

  • Software/cloud — hidden USD cost inflation risk: Microsoft, Oracle, AWS pricing is USD-denominated at the rack-rate; enterprise licence agreements typically have GBP-localised pricing with periodic renegotiation; if GBP weakens materially at a contract renewal, BT faces a USD-driven cost step-up; this cost line is small (c.4-5% of total costs) but growing as BT's digital transformation agenda accelerates
  • BT Sport / content rights — largely de-risked: Post the BT Sport merger into TNT Sports (joint venture with Warner Bros. Discovery from 2023), BT's direct exposure to USD-denominated sports rights has diminished substantially; residual exposure is via JV distributions rather than direct cost lines
  • Margin FX sensitivity from cost side: A 10% GBP depreciation vs USD increases the USD-denominated cost base (c.10% of total costs) by approximately £150-200m at gross; natural hedge offset (USD revenue also translates higher) reduces net EBITDA impact to an estimated £20-40m — broadly consistent with BT's own disclosed sensitivity

Sceptical bullet (§2): The bearish read on cost-currency is that the "natural hedge" is partially illusory — within the Global division, costs and revenues are currency-matched, but the International division's contracts have been priced in hard currency historically at rates that assumed specific GBP/USD assumptions; as legacy contracts roll off and new contracts are priced at market rates, any sustained GBP move can create a 1-2 year margin squeeze window where old cost commitments have not yet repriced to match new revenue rates; additionally, the software/cloud USD cost inflation risk compounds over time as the digital transformation budget grows, and this cost is not hedged with any matching USD revenue

§3 — Translation Exposure (Reported Earnings Impact L3Y)

J-front verdict: Translation impact on BT Group's reported P&L has been small and broadly offsetting across the L3Y period — the GBP has been volatile (2022 collapse, 2023-24 partial recovery) but the limited non-GBP revenue share caps absolute translation P&L swing at ±£50-80m revenue and ±£20-40m EBITDA in most years

  • Reported FX impact L1Y (FY2024): BT's FY2024 annual report discloses that currency movements had a broadly neutral to small negative impact on reported revenue vs the prior year (estimated c.-£20-40m at group level); GBP modestly strengthened vs USD in the Apr 2023-Mar 2024 period (c.+3-4% average rate move), which reduced translated USD revenue — consistent with a small translation headwind
  • Reported FX impact L2Y (FY2023): FY2023 was the peak translation tailwind year — GBP fell sharply in H2 2022 (Kwarteng mini-budget, Sep-Oct 2022 crisis) and remained weak vs USD through much of FY2023; RESEARCHER estimates the FY2023 FX tailwind was c.+£80-120m at revenue level and c.+£30-50m at EBITDA; BT's FY2023 report notes favourable FX movements in the Global segment
  • Reported FX impact L3Y (FY2022): GBP was relatively stable through FY2022 (pre-mini-budget); FX translation impact was modest, estimated ±£20-30m; neither material tailwind nor headwind
  • L3Y average translation impact: Average annual translation impact on EBITDA estimated at +£10-20m (i.e. slightly positive over the period, reflecting the net GBP weakness skew); this is less than 0.3% of group EBITDA of ~£8bn — genuinely immaterial in isolation
  • Translation vs EBITDA base: Even in the peak FY2023 tailwind year, the £30-50m EBITDA translation benefit represents less than 0.6% of the c.£8bn reported EBITDA; by comparison, EBITDA was affected by £300-500m of cost-inflation and pension dynamics in the same period — translation is a rounding line
  • Reporting currency trajectory: GBP has recovered partially from the 2022 lows; the Apr 2024-present period has seen GBP strengthen vs USD (c.+5% from c.1.24 to c.1.30 GBP/USD); this creates a modest translation headwind for BT's USD revenue in FY2025 reporting
  • EPS translation impact: Given the small EBITDA translation magnitude, EPS translation effects are estimated at ±0.3-0.5p per year, on a base of c.12-14p normalised EPS — a 2-4% swing, modest but visible in thin-margin reporting years
  • Translation on balance sheet (net investment hedging): BT's non-GBP net assets (Global division assets, international data centres) are denominated in mixed currencies; BT does not typically hedge its net investment position (standard UK practice); foreign currency translation reserve movements are reported in OCI and do not flow through P&L; for a long-term equity holder, the OCI reserve movement is relevant to book value but not to earnings-trajectory analysis

Sceptical bullet (§3): The bearish read on translation stability is that the L3Y average masks a potential regime change — the 2022-2023 GBP weakness created a one-time translation tailwind that is now unwinding as GBP recovers; if GBP strengthens materially over the N24M period (Bank of England maintaining rates above Fed, sterling risk premium normalising post-2022 shock), the translation contribution could swing from mildly positive to mildly negative, creating a reported revenue headwind that analysts may extrapolate incorrectly as organic deterioration; the binary-event risk is a UK fiscal shock (another mini-budget equivalent) that triggers sharp GBP move, which would create a sudden translation tailwind that flatters reported numbers for 1-2 half-year periods then reverses — analysts anchored to reported growth rates could mistake the pattern for an organic inflection

§4 — Transaction Exposure (Economic Impact)

J-front verdict: BT's transaction exposure is real but limited in quantum — the primary economic risk is within the Global/Enterprise international division where multi-year fixed-price contracts create a cash-flow mismatch when the GBP moves post-contract-signature

  • Transaction exposure definition for BT: BT's transaction exposure arises where it has committed to deliver services priced in one currency (e.g. USD) while incurring costs in GBP or EUR, or vice versa; the most significant such exposure is in the Global division's multi-year managed-network contracts with US and EU MNC clients
  • Magnitude of transaction exposure vs translation: BT's own hedging disclosures suggest net transaction exposure (after natural hedging) in the range of approximately £500-900m of forward cash flow; this is the figure against which the financial hedging programme is sized; translation exposure, by contrast, is an accounting restatement — not a cash-flow item
  • Cash-flow currency mismatch — Global contracts: A US MNC paying BT for managed WAN services in USD creates a USD cash inflow; BT's UK-side network costs (labour, maintenance) supporting that contract are GBP; the mismatch is the net GBP cost vs USD revenue — transaction exposure; BT forward-hedges this mismatch to GBP rates agreed at contract inception
  • Margin economic impact: In a +10% GBP appreciation scenario, an unhedged USD contract loses approximately 10% of its GBP-equivalent margin contribution; given contract margins in Global are already thin (EBITDA margins in the Global division estimated 5-10%), a 10% currency move could eliminate margin on a specific contract entirely if unhedged; this is why transaction hedging of committed contract cash flows is economically essential for Global
  • Pricing pass-through — limited in fixed contracts: BT's enterprise contracts (typically 3-7 years) include limited FX adjustment clauses; most are either fully fixed in the invoicing currency or have narrow +/−5% FX tolerance bands; above-band FX moves create economic impairment that cannot be passed through until contract renewal — pricing power is low for the transaction exposure on existing contracts
  • New contract pricing — partial mitigation: For new contracts, BT's commercial teams price in the contract-signature FX rate and attempt to build in hedging assumptions; however, in competitive tender situations, pricing flexibility is limited and BT often absorbs the FX risk as part of the competitive bid
  • Pricing power by segment: Consumer (GBP, near-100%) — FX irrelevant; Openreach wholesale — irrelevant; Enterprise UK (predominantly GBP) — minor; Enterprise/Global international (USD/EUR) — low pass-through, fixed contracts; the segments with genuine transaction exposure have the weakest FX pricing power
  • Cost pass-through ability: On the cost side, BT's major capex suppliers (Nokia, Ericsson) supply on multi-year framework agreements; within the framework period, prices are substantially fixed; at framework renewal, USD/EUR movements can create a step-change in GBP-equivalent capex, particularly for non-Openreach network; Openreach fibre build costs are predominantly sterling-contracted, limiting pass-through risk there

Sceptical bullet (§4): The bearish read on transaction exposure is that BT's published sensitivity figures are based on the current contract portfolio snapshot and do not model the accumulated mismatch over a 3-5 year contract vintage cycle — if GBP appreciates 15% over two years while a new tranche of Global contracts is being signed, BT's commercial team will price at the current (unfavourable) GBP/USD rate, locking in reduced GBP-equivalent revenue for 3-7 years; the transaction exposure that matters for long-term earnings forecasting is not the current year's forward hedge book but the marginal GBP/USD rate at which new Global contracts are being signed today versus two years ago — and this repricing-vintage risk is entirely invisible in the standard sensitivity table

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: BT Group's hedging policy is disclosed but not granular — transaction hedging is confirmed active with approximately 12-18 month horizon using forwards; translation exposure is explicitly unhedged per stated policy; hedge effectiveness is positive per L3Y derivatives notes but the disclosed quantum is modest relative to the total non-GBP cash flow base

  • Hedging mandate: BT's Annual Report treasury policy section confirms that the Group uses forward foreign exchange contracts and currency options to hedge "material" transaction exposures; the Treasury function is mandated to hedge certain committed cash flows in foreign currency; the policy is risk-reduction, not speculation; hedges are IFRS 9 cash-flow hedge accounting eligible where designated
  • Hedge horizon: BT discloses hedging of committed transactional exposures typically up to 12-18 months forward, with some contracts extended to 24 months for large programme commitments; this is shorter than some European telco peers (Vodafone, Deutsche Telekom both disclose up to 24-36 months on material exposures) — see §7
  • Hedge ratio — estimated: BT does not publish a precise hedge ratio for its transaction book; RESEARCHER triangulates from the derivatives notional values in the FY2024 accounts (foreign exchange contracts notional c.£800m-£1.2bn) against estimated non-GBP net cash transaction exposure of c.£500-900m; implied hedge ratio is approximately 70-90% of near-term committed exposure, with declining coverage for exposures beyond 12 months
  • Translation hedging: Explicitly not undertaken per BT's treasury policy disclosure — "the Group does not hedge the translation of overseas earnings into sterling"; this is standard UK-listed company practice and means GBP/USD and GBP/EUR rate movements mechanically affect reported revenue and EBITDA; it also means that BT's net investment in overseas entities (Global/Enterprise assets) is exposed to currency translation reserve movements in equity, though this does not flow through P&L
  • Hedge instruments: Primarily forward exchange contracts (majority); some currency options for specific large-programme exposures; cross-currency interest rate swaps for the non-GBP debt book (BT has USD-denominated bonds and EUR-denominated bonds in its capital structure); the non-GBP debt is substantially hedged to GBP through cross-currency swaps — this is relevant because BT's financing cost exposure to USD/EUR interest rates is partially managed through the swap book
  • Debt FX hedge (important nuance): BT has issued USD and EUR bonds as part of its diversified funding base; these are hedged back to GBP via cross-currency interest rate swaps; this means BT's net interest charge is not materially exposed to USD/EUR rate movements on the debt side — the FX risk on debt is substantially eliminated through the swap book, which is a positive hedging discipline signal
  • Hedge effectiveness L3Y: BT's FY2022, FY2023, and FY2024 accounts all report hedge effectiveness ratios within the IFRS 9 80-125% effectiveness corridor; in FY2023 (peak GBP volatility year), the group recognised hedging gains through OCI from effective cash-flow hedges; the L3Y realised gains/losses on derivative hedging instruments are modest (estimated net cumulative ±£30-60m across FY2022-FY2024), consistent with a position of broadly effective but small-quantum hedging
  • Hedge cost: BT's hedging cost (the cost of carrying forward contracts at rates above/below spot) is not separately disclosed but is implicitly captured in the reported hedge gain/loss; in periods of high rate differentials (2023: USD rates significantly above GBP), the forward premium for GBP/USD hedges was meaningful — hedging USD receipts forward to GBP at 12-18 months would have incurred a forward discount cost (selling USD forward in a high-USD-rate environment means forward rate < spot); this hedging cost may have partially offset the translation tailwind from GBP weakness in FY2023
  • Recent hedge realisation: FY2024 accounts show hedge gains previously deferred in OCI being recycled to P&L as hedged transactions occurred — consistent with ongoing hedge effectiveness and proper IFRS 9 accounting; no hedge de-designation events noted
  • Cross-currency swap book — additional discipline signal: The existence of cross-currency swaps on USD/EUR bonds (confirmed in the FY2024 financial instruments note) demonstrates treasury capability and discipline beyond simple forwards; this is a positive indicator for hedging programme sophistication
  • Disclosure quality — moderate: BT provides sufficient disclosure to confirm hedging activity and IFRS 9 designation but does not publish a Treasury Policy summary document, hedge ratio by currency, or duration ladder — all of which are published by Vodafone and Deutsche Telekom; BT's disclosure is functional but not transparent, placing it in the middle tier of "boring hedger" spectrum (see §7)

Sceptical bullet (§5): The bearish read on hedging discipline is that BT's disclosed notional hedge values (c.£800m-£1.2bn) represent committed cash-flow hedges on a relatively short horizon, and the roll of these hedges is mechanical — if GBP were to appreciate 20%+ in a 12-month period, the hedges in place would roll off at a favourable locked-in rate, but new forward contracts for the following year would lock in the new unfavourable GBP rate for the next Global/Enterprise contract renewal cycle; the hedging programme protects the P&L for approximately 4-6 quarters but provides zero protection against a sustained multi-year structural GBP appreciation, which is the relevant stress scenario for a 12-24 month EPS-trajectory investor

§6 — Quantified Sensitivity Analysis
