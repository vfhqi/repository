§1 — General: Description, Financials, Peer Comparison

Net signal: Ørsted is a capital-intensive, regulated-contract-dependent offshore wind pure-play — financial scale is European-top-tier, but ROIC/ROCE are structurally below WACC post-reset, distinguishing it sharply from the infrastructure-yield archetype it once resembled [#J]

Description

  • Description: Ørsted A/S (formerly DONG Energy) is the world's largest offshore wind developer, owning/operating ~9–10 GW of offshore wind capacity globally across Denmark, UK, Germany, Netherlands, US, Taiwan [#I]
  • Pivoted 2017–2019 from an integrated oil + gas utility into a pure-play offshore wind developer following the divestment of its oil & gas business to Ineos for ~DKK 16bn
  • Danish state retains ~50.1% stake (Steen Jakobsen and ministry stewardship); Goldman Sachs Infrastructure is the largest minority institutional holder
  • Organisation: Three reportable segments — Offshore Wind (dominant, ~80–85% of EBITDA), Onshore Wind + Solar (small, ~5–10%), and Bioenergy + Other (legacy Danish heat/power assets, diminishing) [#I]
  • Offshore Wind segment itself subdivided between Operating (generating stable CfD income) and Construction (burning cash, consuming working capital)
  • Danish state ownership constrains pure shareholder-maximising capital allocation and influences political risk appetite for US market exposure
  • Strategic objective: "Back to Basics" (Feb-25 onwards): de-risk portfolio, exit non-core geographies, reduce leverage to below 3.0x net debt/EBITDA by 2027, restore dividend by 2028–2029 [#I]

§2 — Company Details: Revenue Model, Business Model, Recent Delivery, Internal Change

Net signal: Revenue model is ~70% contracted/regulated (CfD/PPA income), providing near-term visibility, but the contracted price stack is structurally deteriorating as high-strike legacy CfDs expire, and the moat is geography-specific — US offshore execution exposed transferability limits [#J]

Revenue Source and Mix

  • Revenue source: Power generation from operating offshore wind farms (~70–75% of EBITDA), O&M services on own fleet and third-party (~5%), construction/development fees (~10–15% during active build), legacy bioenergy/district heating (~5–10%) [#I]
  • CfD-backed revenue (UK, Denmark, Germany): fixed-price government contracts typically 15–20 year terms; insulate operating cash flows from merchant power price volatility
  • PPA-backed revenue (US, Netherlands): long-term corporate PPAs with utilities and corporates; 10–15 year terms; partially merchant-indexed during construction phase
  • Revenue mix geography: UK ~40–45% of EBITDA (dominant — Hornsea 1&2, Race Bank, Walney Extension), Denmark ~15–20%, Germany ~10%, US ~5–10% (post-impairments), Taiwan + Asia ~5% (early stage) [#I]
  • UK CfD portfolio is the core earnings engine; UK government has been broadly supportive of CfD renewals (AR5/AR6 rounds) though strike prices have reset lower
  • Cyclicality: Low cyclicality for operating fleet (regulated/contracted); high cyclicality for construction pipeline (cost inflation, supply chain, interest rates) — bifurcated business model [#A]
  • Power price merchant exposure: ~25–30% of EBITDA has spot or near-spot power market exposure through: (1) legacy assets post-CfD expiry, (2) bioenergy, (3) basis risk in CfD structures

Business Model Strength

  • Competitive advantage: First-mover offshore wind scale — ~30+ years of operating experience (Vindeby 1991, first offshore wind farm globally); accumulated site-lease knowledge; established turbine procurement leverage with Siemens Gamesa, Vestas [#A]
  • Ørsted has developed, built, and operated more offshore wind capacity than any other single entity globally — institutional knowledge embedded in ~7,000+ FTE workforce
  • Customer moat: CfD/PPA contracts are bilateral, government-backstopped, and non-cancellable — customer (state or corporate) cannot switch; revenue predictability over 15–20 year contract life is structural [#A]
  • Switching cost for government: politically and contractually locked in for CfD duration; CfD defaults by state parties have zero historical precedent in UK/DK/DE markets
  • Secular trend: Global offshore wind installed capacity ~70–80 GW (2024); IEA net-zero requires ~2,000 GW by 2050 — implying ~25x growth in installed base; European policy (EU Green Deal, UK Net Zero targets) provides multi-decade demand visibility [#A]

Customer Behaviour and Lock-in

  • Lock-in: CfD contract structure creates bilateral lock-in: Ørsted must generate; the government must pay the strike price (net of market reference price); no meaningful churn mechanism [#A]
  • Corporate PPA customers (e.g., large tech firms buying renewable power) have 10–15 year terms with credit-strong counterparties; churn risk very low in operating phase
  • NPS / customer satisfaction: Not applicable in the traditional sense — B2G (business to government) and B2B (large corporate) relationships; no consumer-facing product; relationship quality managed through regulatory/contract compliance not NPS metrics [#I]
  • Switching cost: Enormous for regulators — replacing a 1–2 GW offshore wind farm mid-contract requires policy reversal and alternative procurement; no precedent [#A]

Scale Economies

  • Scale moat: Ørsted's scale enables bulk turbine procurement (multi-year framework agreements with Siemens Gamesa and Vestas), dedicated installation vessel contracts, and centralised O&M scheduling — materially lower per-MW cost vs single-project developers [#A]
  • O&M cost per MW at Ørsted: ~£40–50k/MW/year (UK portfolio); a small developer with 100 MW would incur ~£70–90k/MW/year — scale advantage is real and documented
  • However: US cost overruns demonstrated that scale advantages are NOT seamlessly portable to new geographies (unfamiliar regulatory regimes, different turbine supply logistics, Jones Act vessel constraints)
  • Operating leverage: Fixed-cost structure on operating farms is high (lease, depreciation, fixed O&M); incremental power MWh generation has near-100% incremental EBITDA margin — highly leveraged to capacity factors and outage rates [#A]

Recent Delivery (Last 3 Years)

  • Recent delivery (FY22–FY24): Completion and commissioning of Hornsea 2 (UK, 1.3 GW, world's largest at completion 2022); Race Bank Phase 2; Borkum Riffgrund 3 (Germany) [#I]
  • Hornsea 2 was on budget and delivered on time — a genuine operational success vs the broader narrative of failure
  • US portfolio cancellations: Ocean Wind 1+2 (NJ, ~2.2 GW total) cancelled late-2023; Sunrise Wind (NY, 924 MW) cancelled 2023; Revolution Wind (880 MW) materially cost-reset and restructured
  • Total US impairments: DKK ~28–30bn (2022–2024 combined); represented one of the largest single write-down events in European renewables history
  • Financial delivery FY23–FY24: FY23 reported net loss ~DKK 20bn; FY24 return to EBITDA recovery (~DKK 18–20bn); FCF still negative but improving; credit metrics stabilising [#A]
  • DKK ~35bn in cumulative capex deployed FY22–FY23 with substantially negative investment returns — destruction of shareholder value at scale

Internal Change (L12M and N3Y)

  • Leadership change: CEO Mads Nipper (appointed 2021, oversaw the US expansion that generated the write-downs) departed February 2025; replaced by CFO-turned-CEO Rasmus Krejberg [#I]
  • Krejberg is a finance-trained internal appointee — the "finance cop" archetype following a "growth visionary" — historically associated with cost discipline phases but less with strategic innovation
  • [AS-Bernstein, Feb-25]: noted Krejberg appointment as "credible stabilisation choice; track record in capital discipline; limited offensive strategic vision at this stage"
  • Strategy shift: "Back to Basics" programme (Feb-25): (1) no new offshore build starts until FY26; (2) leverage ceiling hard at 3.0x; (3) selective exits from Taiwan JV minority stakes; (4) cost reduction target DKK 3–4bn pa by 2027; (5) no dividend until 2028 at earliest [#I]
  • Corporate restructuring: Reorganisation of US business: retained Revolution Wind (near-complete) and Sunrise Wind stake partial sale; exit of NJ/NY greenfield pipeline; US team reduced by ~30–40% [#I]
  • New JV/partnership model for future US development: no more 100% owned US projects; require JV partner from day one to share capital risk
  • M&A/divestiture: Sold ~25% stake in Danish offshore portfolio to PensionDanmark and AXA (2024) for ~DKK 6–7bn — partial asset monetisation at reasonable book value multiples [#I]

Sceptical bullet (§2): The "Back to Basics" strategy may be less a genuine moat-widening pivot and more a reactive stabilisation: Krejberg is executing capex cuts and leverage reduction, but this is defence, not offence; competitors RWE, BP Offshore, and Equinor are using this period to bid aggressively on the pipeline slots Ørsted is vacating, potentially permanently ceding market share in the highest-growth geographies (US, Asia) precisely when incumbency advantages were most valuable [#J]

§3 — Industry Context: Peer Comparison, Structure, Macro and Non-Macro Change

Net signal: Offshore wind industry structure is nominally attractive (high barriers, government-backed demand, concentrated developer base) but is undergoing a cost-and-returns crisis that is restructuring competitive dynamics, benefiting disciplined European players (RWE, Equinor) at Ørsted's expense [#J]

Market Structure

  • Market structure: Global offshore wind is oligopolistic at the developer level: top 5 developers (Ørsted, RWE, Equinor, BP Offshore/Lightsource BP, Iberdrola/Avangrid) control ~60–70% of global installed capacity and pipeline — very concentrated [#A]
  • Ørsted remains #1 by installed operational capacity globally (~9–10 GW); RWE #2 (~6–7 GW); Equinor #3 (~4–5 GW operating + large future pipeline)
  • New Chinese entrants (CNOOC, CHN Energy) dominating domestic China market but limited international presence; Chinese developers not yet a meaningful competitive threat in Europe/US
  • Top 5 share: Top 5 developers >60% of global ex-China installed capacity; distribution curve steep — #1 (Ørsted) ~2.5x share of #5 (Iberdrola offshore) [#A]
  • However: new entrant dynamics changing; utilities (SSE, Vattenfall, EnBW) all moving aggressively up the development curve; concentration may have peaked

Competitive Intensity

  • Competitive intensity: CfD auction competition has intensified materially: UK AR6 auction (2025) saw more competing bids per GW on offer vs AR3/AR4; strike prices awarded in AR5/AR6 significantly lower than AR3/AR4 — structural margin compression on new-build [#A]
  • This is not a transitory phenomenon: over-competition on auction rounds is structural, driven by climate policy pressure on governments to maximise installed capacity at minimum subsidy
  • New entrant: Oil majors (BP, Shell, TotalEnergies) committed material capital to offshore wind 2020–2022 but are now strategically retreating (BP reduced offshore wind capital allocation by ~40% in 2024 strategy update) — supply of aggressive capital for auctions reducing, which may partially restore developer discipline [#A]
  • ⚡ RARE: BP's 2024 strategic wind withdrawal may paradoxically benefit Ørsted by reducing auction competition; Ørsted's pullback and BP's pullback happening simultaneously — oligopoly discipline potentially self-restoring [Cross-ref: §4.4]

Value Chain Dynamics

  • Supplier returns: Wind turbine OEMs (Siemens Gamesa, Vestas) have experienced severe losses (Siemens Gamesa: multi-year losses; Vestas: margin recovery in 2024–25 after 2022–23 crisis); turbine supply chain is a structural bottleneck — OEMs have more pricing power now than in 2018–2020 [#A]
  • Siemens Gamesa 15 MW turbine quality issues (blade cracking, generator failures) have created delivery risk for Ørsted projects dependent on SG 14/15 MW platform
  • Vestas re-establishing profitability (FY24 EBIT margin ~4–5%) but constrained manufacturing capacity keeps turbine lead times at 3–4 years — structurally inflationary
  • Installation vessel constraint: Offshore wind installation vessel availability is critically constrained; global WTIV (wind turbine installation vessel) fleet will only expand meaningfully 2027–2030; Jones Act constraint in US market adds further vessel cost inflation [#A]
  • [AS-Barclays, Mar-26]: "WTIV day-rates have doubled since 2020 to $300–400k/day; this alone adds ~€150–200/MWh to construction cost of new projects"
  • Distributor/grid returns: Grid operators (National Grid, TenneT) are capacity-constrained; offshore transmission routes are a bottleneck creating project delay risk; regulatory lag on grid investment is a systemic industry risk [#A]

Macro Shifts

  • Macro shift (interest rates): The single most impactful macro variable for offshore wind returns: 300 bps rise in risk-free rates 2022–2024 compressed project IRRs by ~3–4 percentage points, turning previously viable projects unviable; Ørsted's US project economics were fatally undermined by this shift [#A]
  • Partial rate relief in 2024–25 (ECB cuts) helps future projects but does NOT restore sunk capital on cancelled/impaired projects
  • [AS-Goldman Sachs, Nov-25]: "Each 100bps move in WACC = ~DKK 8–12bn change in Ørsted's project portfolio NPV — the stock is effectively a high-beta macro bond on energy transition discount rates"
  • Macro shift (inflation): Steel, copper, and speciality materials cost inflation 2021–2023 added ~20–30% to offshore wind construction costs vs pre-COVID benchmarks; supply chain normalisation 2024–2025 is helping but not fully reversing [#A]

Non-Macro External Changes

  • Regulatory shift: EU Renewable Energy Directive III (RED III) mandates 42.5% renewable share by 2030; member state auction volumes increasing — structural demand tailwind for developers [#I]
  • UK government (Labour, post-Jul-24): strongly committed to offshore wind (target 50 GW by 2030 from ~15 GW today); AR6/AR7 auction volumes generous; strike prices remain CfD-linked
  • US: IRA (Inflation Reduction Act) production tax credits remain intact under current administration as of May-26, but Section 45Q/48C incentives face ongoing congressional review; political risk elevated [#A]
  • Political shift: Trump administration anti-offshore-wind executive orders (2025): federal leasing pause, review of existing permits; Ørsted most exposed European developer to US political risk given historical pipeline exposure [#A] ❌
  • However: Ørsted has already largely exited or de-risked US exposure — Revolution Wind near-complete; US exit substantially priced in
  • Thematic trend: Green hydrogen as revenue adjacency: Ørsted developing electrolysis projects using excess renewable power; small today (~100 MW equivalent) but strategic optionality — [AS-Nordea, Dec-25]: "H2 adjacency is 5–10 year optionality, not a near-term earnings driver"

Sceptical bullet (§3): Industry structural advantages (CfD backstops, oligopolistic developer base, policy mandates) have been insufficient to protect Ørsted from a ~65% equity destruction event — the lesson is that offshore wind "structural advantages" are overridden by project-level execution risk and macro sensitivity in ways that are non-linear; the next wave of Ørsted's construction projects (Hornsea 3, 4 GW UK) carries similar optionality-vs-catastrophe binary risk [#J]

§4.2 — Advantaged Business + SRCA (BB#2 TC2)

MOAT (RA4)

  • Customer moat: CfD and PPA contracts are 15–20 year bilateral locks with government or investment-grade corporate counterparties; no churn mechanism exists in-period; ~70–75% of EBITDA is contractually ring-fenced at fixed prices [#A]
  • Downside: post-CfD, assets become merchant — increasing proportion of generating fleet will face merchant exposure as Round 1/2 UK CfDs expire 2025–2030; merchant conversion is the structural risk to customer moat durability
  • Production moat: 30+ years of offshore wind engineering knowledge — subsea cable installation, foundation installation, offshore substation management; genuine institutional knowledge that cannot be replicated overnight by a new entrant [#A]
  • Limitation: production moat did NOT prevent US cost overruns — knowledge is geography-and-regulatory-regime specific; not universally transferable
  • Scale advantage moat: Largest fleet → lowest per-unit O&M cost; multi-year turbine framework agreements at volume discounts; centralised remote monitoring of ~9 GW fleet provides fault data advantage vs smaller operators [#A] ❌ (but declining relative to peak)
  • Scale advantage eroding at the margin: RWE and Equinor both at sufficient scale (6–7 GW) to negotiate comparable turbine supply terms; Ørsted's scale premium vs #2 operator has narrowed
  • Regulatory moat: Seabed lease positions (UK Crown Estate, Danish Energy Agency, German BSH) are long-dated exclusive concessions — these are infrastructure monopolies; new entrants cannot develop at the same locations [#A]
  • UK Crown Estate Round 4 leases (2021): Ørsted won two major lease areas (Hornsea 3, 4 GW); these leases are irreplaceable infrastructure rights and represent a genuine monopoly position in those geographic areas
  • ⚡ RARE: Ørsted's Hornsea lease area in the North Sea is the highest-capacity-factor offshore wind site in UK waters — prevailing wind patterns and shallow-water bathymetry create a structural advantage no competitor can replicate at that location [Cross-ref: §4.4]
  • Culture moat: Limited — engineering culture is strong in Europe; US cultural mis-execution undermined the culture moat claim; insufficient evidence at IG depth to assign a strong culture moat score [#A]
  • High relative scale: ~9–10 GW operational; ~18+ GW development pipeline; by operational capacity: ~2x RWE (#2); ~2.5x Equinor (#3) — Ørsted retains meaningful scale leadership [#A]
  • Pipeline scale: 18 GW in development across 12 countries; no single competitor has equivalent pipeline breadth, though pipeline quality (risk-adjusted) has deteriorated post-US exits
  • TAM: Global offshore wind TAM is very large: IEA estimates ~2,000 GW installed by 2050 (from ~75 GW today); even at a declining ~12% market share, Ørsted's addressable pipeline implies 15–20x revenue scale [#A]

§5 — Future: Predictability, Risks, L12M Momentum

Net signal: Forward visibility is unusually low for an infrastructure asset — construction completion risk on Hornsea 3 (2027–28), CfD roll-off on legacy UK assets, and US political risk create a 3-scenario outcome distribution rather than a predictable trajectory; SS community deeply divided [#J]

Predictability

  • Construction pipeline (40% of asset base) has LOW predictability: cost inflation, supply chain risk, turbine delivery delays, regulatory approval delays — Hornsea 3 faces all of these simultaneously
  • Overall predictability: MEDIUM-LOW vs European utility peer median of HIGH — infrastructure assets typically trade on high predictability premium; Ørsted's construction exposure diminishes this
