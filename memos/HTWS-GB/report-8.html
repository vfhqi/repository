§1 — Industry Overview + Value Chain Economics

The African independent tower company sector is a capital-intensive infrastructure utility business whose economics are driven by colocation density, contractual revenue protection, and operating leverage over a fixed-cost base.

  • Industry size: Global tower industry estimated at ~$110bn revenue in 2024 (GSMA/industry estimates); African sub-sector approximately $4-5bn in annual leasing revenue, with MENA adding a further $1-2bn addressable for Helios Towers's current footprint [#A]
  • Growth trajectory: African tower market growing at estimated 8-12% CAGR 2023-2027 driven by MNO network densification; significantly above global tower sector median of 4-6% CAGR (US/Europe mature markets drag the global figure) [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: 4G population coverage in sub-Saharan Africa c.50-55% vs global average c.75%; 5G rollout still nascent in most Helios markets — structural densification runway is long
  • Sub-bullet: GSMA forecasts sub-Saharan Africa to add 300mn+ mobile subscribers by 2030, most growth in existing Helios footprint markets
  • Structural driver — technology: MNO capex migration from 3G to 4G/5G requires more tower sites per square kilometre; tower sharing becomes economically essential as MNOs seek to manage their own balance sheets [#A]
  • Structural driver — MNO balance sheet: African MNOs consistently operate with tighter balance sheets than developed-market peers; sale-and-leaseback of tower portfolios has been the dominant industry formation mechanism since 2010 [#A]
  • Geographic split: Helios Towers operates in 11 markets — primary clusters in (1) West Africa (Ghana, Senegal, Côte d'Ivoire), (2) East/Southern Africa (Tanzania, DRC, Madagascar, Malawi), (3) MENA (Oman, South Africa entry via Wiocc partnership); revenue weighted roughly 60% East+Southern Africa, 40% West Africa/MENA [#A]
  • Fragmentation: African tower sector has consolidated rapidly from highly fragmented (MNO-captive towers) toward ITC dominance; IHS Towers + Helios Towers + American Tower Africa account for ~60-65% of third-party tower infrastructure across target markets [#A]
  • Consolidation: Consolidation trend is strongly directional — MNOs continue to divest captive tower assets; ITC sector is on a consolidation path with scale players absorbing smaller portfolios [#A]

§3 — Barriers to Entry + Exit + Disruption Forces

  • Verdict: Barriers to entry are structurally high (capital, anchor tenant, regulatory, scale); disruption forces from technology are real but longer-dated than consensus models; near-term threat is low [#J]

  • Capital requirement: A single tower greenfield build costs $50,000-$150,000 in sub-Saharan Africa depending on market; a viable ITC portfolio requires 1,000+ sites to achieve scale economics; minimum capital for credible market entry is $100-200mn [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Helios Towers's total asset base exceeds $3bn; replicating this at current replacement cost is not achievable for new entrants without institutional capital backing
  • Scale economy: Power and maintenance management costs per tower fall materially with scale; Helios Towers's energy-as-a-service infrastructure (hybrid power units, solar, centralised NOC) requires scale to be economical [#A]
  • Switching cost: Once an MNO's active equipment is installed on a specific tower, the cost of physical relocation is significant (logistics, network outage risk, re-permitting); MNO churn from ITC towers is structurally rare [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: MLA early termination penalties are typically 12-24 months of contracted revenue; this creates a de facto lock-in mechanism beyond physical switching costs
  • Regulatory barrier: Tower construction requires planning permissions, spectrum coordination approvals, and aviation/environmental clearances in each market; in several Helios markets (DRC, Tanzania), the permitting environment is opaque and relationship-dependent [#A]
  • Distribution barrier: Helios Towers has existing ground lease relationships covering its entire portfolio; entering a new market requires negotiating hundreds of individual ground leases before first MNO tenant can be signed — a multi-year process [#A]
  • Structural exit barriers: Once capital is deployed into tower assets, exit requires finding a buyer willing to pay close to replacement value; tower assets are illiquid and market-specific; exit barriers are effectively as high as entry barriers [#J]
  • Vanilla entrant threat: MNO-backed greenfield ITC entry is the most plausible vanilla entry mode; however, MNOs have consistently demonstrated preference for asset-light balance sheet strategies rather than reintegrating tower infrastructure [#A]
  • Innovative entrant — small cells: Dense small-cell networks (street furniture, in-building) are structurally complementary rather than substitutive to macro towers for African markets; small cells require higher GDP/urban density than most Helios markets currently exhibit [#A]
  • Innovative entrant — satellite (Starlink/LEO): Starlink and LEO broadband satellites are a credible substitute for rural fixed broadband but are NOT a substitute for mobile cellular coverage requiring terrestrial tower infrastructure; MNO core use cases remain tower-dependent [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: Starlink in Africa is expanding but serves unconnected rural use cases, not urban mobile traffic that drives ITC colocation demand; near-term competitive threat is minimal
  • Adjacency entry: Fibre operators (e.g. Liquid Telecom, WIOCC) are building out backhaul but are structurally complementary to tower operators rather than competitive; no documented case of a fibre operator meaningfully displacing ITC revenue in any Helios market [#A]
  • Substitutes: The core product (passive tower infrastructure for MNO antenna hosting) has no close substitute at scale in sub-Saharan Africa; drone-based coverage is still experimental; balloon/HAPS (High Altitude Pseudo-Satellites) are a very long-dated risk [#A]
  • Disruption force — Open RAN: Open RAN could reduce MNO capex per network element, potentially reducing densification capex and slowing new tower demand; but Open RAN deployment in Africa is nascent and the tower-demand impact is unclear [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: Open RAN disrupts the active equipment supply chain (Nokia/Ericsson/Huawei), not the passive tower infrastructure layer where Helios operates
  • Regulatory SWOT: Telecommunications regulation in Helios markets is a mixed picture — Rwanda, Tanzania, Oman have relatively stable regulatory environments; DRC, Ghana, Senegal carry higher regulatory change risk; tower sector regulation (including mandatory tower sharing mandates) has historically been supportive of ITCs [#A]
  • Political SWOT: Ghana's IMF programme (2023) and DRC's political instability are live political risks in Helios's portfolio; forced renegotiation of MLA terms or tower nationalisation is a tail risk that cannot be zero-rated in sovereign risk assessment [#A]
  • Supplier SWOT: Ground lease landlords and diesel suppliers are the primary supplier categories; both are highly fragmented and have no pricing power; energy cost inflation (2022-2023) demonstrated that diesel costs CAN move adversely even in a contractual framework where costs are nominally passed through [#A]
  • Sceptical bullet: Inversion case on barriers — Helios Towers's barriers are high in existing markets but NOT in new markets it is trying to enter; greenfield entry into a new African market (Helios's growth mode) faces exactly the same barriers that would deter new competitors; Helios must replicate its own entry process each time; the FX crisis in Ghana (2022-2023) demonstrated that MNO contractual commitments can be delayed even without formal breach, effectively creating a collection risk that barrier analysis understates [#J]
