EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (BLUF) 2 findings

Interparfums is led by co-founders Jean Madar and Philippe Benacin with unmatched 44-year industry tenures, having led the company for over 44 years since its inception in 1982, establishing a highly stable leadership footprint that delivered exceptional long-term growth through disciplined brand licensing, achieving a 9% sales CAGR between 2005 and 2025 . However, the complex parent-subsidiary architecture, persistent material weaknesses in internal control over financial reporting for fiscal 2024 and 2025, and an informal SEC Enforcement Division investigation create significant governance friction that materially offsets the founders' operational track record. This report synthesizes 15 sections of deep-dive research into CEO career history, track record, leadership style, sell-side perception, senior team quality, board composition, executive compensation, governance structure,

1.
GOVERNANCE ROBUSTNESS: Weak structural checks despite committee independence Interparfums SA maintains a highly concentrated board dominated by its co-founders, with the board reduced from 11 to 8 members in April 2025, though five of the eight board members are classified as independent, representing a 62.5% independence level exceeding the Middlenext Code recommendations . All three board committees boast 100% independent director membership, with Marie-Ange Verdickt chairing the Audit Committee, Constance Benqué chairing the GNRC, and Caroline Renoux chairing the CSR Committee . However, independent directors hold negligible equity stakes in the French listed entity, with Marie-Ange Verdickt holding only 15 shares directly and Constance Benqué holding no shares directly, which may limit their motivation to challenge the co-founders' joint voting control

The parent company Interparfums, Inc. owns 72% of the Euronext-listed subsidiary Interparfums SA, with Interparfums Holding SA controlling 72.4% of share capital but commanding 83.8% of theoretical voting rights amplified by double voting provisions created in September 1995, resulting in minority shareholders holding 27.3% of capital but only 16.0% of theoretical voting rights, effectively preventing public market blockholders from influencing strategic corporate decisions . Management identified material weaknesses in internal control over financial reporting for fiscal 2024 and 2025, lacking a structured process to identify and mitigate financial statement risks, with material weaknesses impacting IT general controls related to user access at Interparfums SA, including inappropriate privileged access rights including SAP-ALL master codes, directly delaying the filing of the fiscal 2025 Annual Report . The Audit Committee dismissed Forvis Mazars, LLP and appointed Grant Thornton, LLP in May 2026, while Interparfums faces an informal investigation by the SEC Enforcement Division focusing on alleged failures to disclose prior auditor deficiencies in the 2023 proxy .
