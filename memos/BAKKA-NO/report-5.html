  • New product/VAP: VAP segment expanding capacity; new processing investments in Faroes aimed at upgrading product mix toward higher-margin portioned and smoked salmon [#A]

§9 — Industry + Sector Context + Sceptical Synthesis

  • Sector trend — biology: Sea lice treatment costs have risen across the Norwegian farming sector over L5–7 years; no farming region has demonstrated a durable reversal to historical cost levels once lice pressure intensified [#J]

  • This is the most important sector context for Bakkafrost: if Norwegian peers have not resolved the biological cost problem despite years of investment, Faroese "normalisation" may be an optimistic framing

  • Sector trend — pricing: Atlantic salmon demand-supply dynamics remain constructive; global supply growth is constrained by biological and regulatory limitations; demand is growing faster than supply in key markets [#A]

  • This supports the long-term thesis: ASP support is structural, providing a margin floor

  • Supply chain / input costs: Feed costs (fishmeal, fish oil) have been elevated in 2023–2024; some moderation expected but no return to pre-2021 levels near-term [#A]

  • Bakkafrost's vertical integration in feed (Havsbrún) provides partial insulation but does not eliminate exposure

  • Competitive dynamic: Sector is oligopolistic at the premium quality end; Bakkafrost's Faroese positioning (cold water, long grow-out) is differentiated [#A]

  • No structural competitive threat to market position identified; threat is self-inflicted biological cost

  • Macro context: European consumer resilience on premium protein remained relatively solid in 2024; salmon consumption not a discretionary cut category [#A]

  • Foodservice recovery post-COVID is a slower-moving tailwind; retail demand steady

  • End-market health: Bakkafrost's key markets (Northern Europe, US premium segment) show stable-to-growing demand; no demand-side red flag [#A]

  • Reverse-engineered bear case: The bear case has three compounding legs [#J]

  • Leg 1: Faroese biological costs are structurally elevated, not cyclically elevated; the DKK 10–15/kg EBIT/kg that underpins the "normalisation" recovery thesis is historically achievable but not the new steady state
