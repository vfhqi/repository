SECTION 3 — COMPANY DESCRIPTION

The Gym Group is the UK's second-largest low-cost gym operator by club count, behind PureGym. It operates approximately 260 sites across the UK, offering 24/7 access at headline membership prices of approximately £25/month for a standard membership. Sites are staffed, differentiating GYM from the fully unmanned PureGym model.

Founded in 2007, listed on the London Stock Exchange in 2015, the company expanded rapidly to reach over 180 sites by 2019. COVID forced closures in 2020-2021 and the resulting financial damage — combined with high IFRS 16 lease liabilities — produced a multi-year period of impaired financial performance. The appointment of Will Orr in September 2023 initiated the turnaround.

The business model generates revenue almost entirely from monthly direct debit membership fees. Approximately 950,000 members paying an average of £21.60/month generates approximately £20.5m/month or £246m annualised revenue — close to the reported £244.9m, confirming the model integrity.

The capital structure is straightforward. £102m of bank facilities (£45m term loan + £57m RCF), drawn to £62m at year-end. Non-property net debt of £59.3m at 1.0x EBITDA leverage. IFRS 16 lease liabilities are large — the gross lease obligation across 260 sites with 10-15 year terms is substantial — but this is a matching of long-term obligations against long-term revenue streams.

SECTION 10 — SCEPTICAL / BEAR THESIS

Full Bear Case

The GYM bear case requires the following to materialise simultaneously or in sequence:

ARPMM Ceiling

The UK budget gym market has a natural price ceiling. At £25-30/month, the product competes with streaming services, local authority leisure centres (£20-25/month), and home fitness alternatives. Pushing prices above £28-30/month risks significant churn among price-sensitive members.

ARPMM growth has already decelerated from 7% to 4%. If inflation in general moderates but GYM continues to push membership prices above wage growth, net churn could increase. The average member tenure of 18 months, while improving, remains below premium gym levels and reflects ongoing price sensitivity.

Site Saturation in Urban Core

GYM's highest-returning sites are in high-density London and major urban locations. These locations are finite. PureGym opened 32 UK sites in 2025 and is also targeting the same retail park and urban residential locations. Competition for the best sites will intensify.

If GYM is forced to accept lower-density locations to meet its 20-site annual target, average site ROIC could fall from 27% toward 20%. At 20% ROIC, the FCF self-funding arithmetic deteriorates and the expansion narrative weakens.

Leverage Risk in an Adverse Scenario

Non-property net debt will increase in FY2026 as capex (£60-65m) exceeds FCF (approximately £40m). If EBITDA disappoints and capex is higher than expected, leverage could reach 1.5-2.0x. Still within covenant at 3.0x, but uncomfortable.

A prolonged UK recession causing member volumes to decline by 5-10% — while possible but unlikely given the budget price point — would produce a meaningful EBITDA decline and stress the balance sheet.

PureGym Competitive Threat

PureGym operates 680+ sites vs GYM's 260. The scale differential creates asymmetric marketing leverage (PureGym can afford higher customer acquisition costs), network density advantages (members can visit any site), and cost advantages on equipment purchasing and technology. If PureGym launches a significantly improved digital product, GYM's "staffed premium budget" positioning could be squeezed.

Downside Risk Rating: YELLOW — Bear case requires multiple things to go wrong simultaneously. Balance sheet headroom and track record reduce the probability. But the ARPMM deceleration trend needs to be watched carefully over the next 2-3 reporting periods.
