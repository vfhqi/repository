EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (BLUF) 2 findings

Prysmian operates as the undisputed global cable leader, leveraging unparalleled manufacturing scale and recent US acquisitions to capture highly profitable energy-transition and data-centre growth driven by global electrification, renewable energy integration, and digital infrastructure upgrades.

Business positioning and competitive advantage

Prysmian operates 107 production plants and 27 R&D centres across more than 50 countries with over 33,000 employees, dwarfing European rival Nexans which employs roughly 28,000 people globally, and generated €19.65 billion in revenues during 2025.

The company occupies the most profit-favoured stages of the cable value chain, leveraging deep vertical integration from raw copper rod milling to downstream subsea installation, with raw materials constituting over 80% of total cable manufacturing costs, creating a critical moat against supply chain disruptions and securing metal availability.

Prysmian dominates the top tier with revenue roughly double that of its closest European rival Nexans, while Nexans and NKT form the remaining tier-one challengers collectively controlling over 80% of the premium high-voltage subsea market.

The company controls a commanding 60-65% combined market share with Southwire in the highly profitable North American low-voltage segment, while South Korean LS Cable and Japanese Sumitomo target Western markets but lack localized installation track records.

Financial performance and capital efficiency

Prysmian's revenues surged to €19.65 billion in 2025, up from €17.02 billion in 2024, reflecting a 15.4% increase, with adjusted EBITDA expanding from 11.3% in 2024 to 12.2% in 2025 achieving €2.39 billion. Prysmian's margin structurally aligns with Nexans at 11.9% but trails NKT's project-heavy 14.3%, with margin uplift driven by integration of higher-margin US acquisitions like Encore Wire and Channell.

Management consistently upgrades guidance, most recently targeting €2.62 billion to €2.77 billion for 2026, while diluted EPS grew sharply to €4.30 in 2025, up 73% from €2.52 in 2024, demonstrating immense operational leverage.

Free cash flow reached €1.17 billion in 2025 with operating net working capital remaining highly efficient, benefiting from structural down-payments on long-term transmission contracts, while the company aims for a structural free cash

flow conversion rate above 50% of EBITDA through 2028. Return on Capital Employed expanded to 21.0% in 2025, up from 16.0% in 2024, with Nexans also posting a strong ROCE of 21.3% in 2025, validating robust industry-wide economics.

Industry structure and margin dynamics

The global cable market is projected to grow from €116bn in 2023 to €157bn by 2028, a solid ~6% CAGR, outpacing historical GDP growth driven fundamentally by grid modernisation and renewable energy integration. The submarine and extra-high-voltage manufacturing stages accrue the highest absolute margins, with operators in this tier capturing EBITDA margins approaching the 18-20% upper corridor.

Major transmission system operators like Amprion and 50 Hertz form a highly concentrated sticky customer base for high-voltage projects, with long-standing relationships with European grid operators securely anchoring the multi-year production backlog.

Capacity exhaustion across the high-voltage industry has handed absolute pricing power to tier-one manufacturers, with utilities forced into paying premium prices simply to secure production slots for grid-critical infrastructure.

Prysmian's operating net working capital fell to 6.3% of annualised revenues in early 2026 down from 7.8%, mechanically driven by massive customer prepayments on high-voltage transmission contracts to secure scarce manufacturing capacity, effectively shielding manufacturers from the immense capital burdens of executing multi-year submarine projects.

Strategic execution and operator quality

Prysmian has executed a highly aggressive US acquisition strategy, with the 2024 Encore Wire buyout creating a virtual duopoly in the US low-voltage building wire market, the Channell deal expanding its footprint in US telecommunications enclosures and connectivity, and the early 2026 ACSM purchase bringing critical submarine cable installation and surveying capabilities in-house.

The company has a proven track record consistently delivering cost synergies exceeding 4% of acquired sales on past deals, while a €1.2 billion investment plan is

underway to increase US optical fibre manufacturing capacity by 40-50%.

Prysmian exhibits exceptional foundation quality driven by an elite operator track record in M&A integration, with Massimo Battaini assuming leadership in 2024 after serving as COO ensuring deep strategic continuity and operational stability, while the operator consistently exceeds synergy targets extracting cost savings of nearly 6% of sales from the Encore Wire deal surpassing historical baselines.

Return on capital employed expanded to 21.0% in 2025 aligning closely with Nexans at 21.3% indicating highly rational industry profit pools, while the business consistently converts roughly 50% of its EBITDA into free cash flow achieving a record €1.19bn LTM FCF in early 2026. 16

Long-term growth positioning and disruption risks

Prysmian is structurally positioned to capture multidecade growth across AI data-centre and energy-transition mega-trends, fortified by impenetrable entry barriers, though Asian export penetration and capacity overbuild remain latent disruption risks.

Major technology companies are rapidly becoming a dominant concentrated customer cohort for Digital Solutions, with peer Corning recently securing a massive $6 billion framework agreement with Meta, while Prysmian is actively finalising long-term optical fibre contracts exceeding €5 billion with tier-one US hyperscalers. Hyperscaler fibre agreements are materially extending in length with typical two-year telecom contracts transitioning into massive 5-to-10-year capacity reservation agreements for AI data centres, while high-voltage customers now routinely provide structural down-payments to reserve scarce manufacturing capacity, dramatically reducing late-stage project cancellation risks.

However, Prysmian's cash conversion relies heavily on upfront customer down-payments for multi-year transmission projects, and a normalization of working capital dynamics combined with its high debt load from recent M&A could compress free cash flow generation and elevate balance sheet risk in a cyclical downturn. Additionally, massive parallel capacity expansions across Prysmian, NKT, and Nexans could outpace grid-connection bottlenecks by 2028, collapsing the oligopoly's pricing power. 20

1.
Forward-looking implications: Prysmian's dominant market position and vertically integrated business model position the company exceptionally well to capitalize on structural mega-trends driving cable demand through the late 2020s, particularly AI-driven data centre expansion and renewable energy grid integration

The company's ability to secure multi-year capacity reservation agreements with hyperscalers and utilities provides unprecedented revenue visibility and pricing power that fundamentally differentiates it from historical commodity- driven cable cycles.

The aggressive US market consolidation through Encore Wire and upstream copper rod integration creates a formidable competitive moat that regional players cannot easily replicate.

However, management must carefully navigate three critical risks that could materially impact the investment thesis: first, synchronized capacity expansions across all tier-one players could recreate oversupply conditions by 2028-2030, eroding the current exceptional pricing environment; second, the abnormally high margins in the US low-voltage segment acquired through Encore Wire remain vulnerable to macroeconomic downturns or regulatory changes that could restore buyer leverage; and third, the heavy reliance on customer down-payments to fund working capital creates balance sheet fragility if utility spending slows or project deferrals accelerate.

2.
Analytical limitations: This analysis is constrained by limited granular disclosure on segment-level returns, project-specific profitability, and the true sustainability of current pricing dynamics beyond the immediate capacity shortage

The cross-value-chain signal analysis relies heavily on broker estimates and expert interviews rather than direct primary sourcing from customers and suppliers, introducing potential confirmation bias.

Additionally, the 8-10 year financial benchmarking is incomplete due to limited historical data availability for newly formed segments post-2024 reorganization, making long-term trend identification challenging.

Future research should focus on developing more sophisticated models for forecasting the timing and magnitude of capacity normalization, conducting deeper primary research with utility procurement officers to validate switching cost assumptions, and building scenario frameworks that stress-test margin assumptions under different macroeconomic and competitive dynamics.
