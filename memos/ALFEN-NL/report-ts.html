Section 2 – The 80/20 (bottom line up front) 8 findings

Section summary
  1. Alfen presents a highly volatile, transitional recovery case under new leadership as its core markets reset from the uncurbed volume-driven growth of the past.
1.
Diversified product-market mix: Alfen designs, engineers, and builds secondary transformer substations for grid operators, stationary and mobile energy storage systems, and AC charging infrastructure
2.
Plausible recovery case: The long-term investment thesis rests on reigniting profitable growth driven by structural European grid expansion, rising electrification, and decentralized renewable generation
3.
Critical margin swing: Near-term forward earnings are highly sensitive to product-market mix dynamics as lower-margin smart grid and energy storage segments outgrow high-margin EV charging
4.
Fading revenue performance: Revenue declined by 10.70% to €435.60m in FY25 compared to €487.60m in FY24, primarily driven by a 21.20% drop in EV charging revenues
5.
Marginally stable profitability: Adjusted EBITDA margin remained stable at 5.80% in FY25 versus 5.80% in FY24 due to aggressive headcount reductions and cost alignment
6.
Severe grid bottlenecks: Ongoing delays in permitting and persistent labor shortages at Dutch grid operators remain the primary risks that could break the recovery
7.

Section 3 – What the company does 16 findings

Section summary
  1. Alfen operates a diversified, grid-critical assembly model that holds a market-leading position in Dutch substations but faces commoditization in retail EV charging.

Segment capabilities and market presence

1.
Smart grid core: Smart Grid Solutions produces secondary distribution transformer substations and medium-voltage transport distribution systems, representing 43.00% of Alfen's total FY25 revenues
2.
Energy storage focus: Energy Storage Systems delivers stationary multi-megawatt battery systems and mobile storage solutions under 'TheBattery' brand, accounting for 29.00% of total FY25 sales
3.
EV charging infrastructure: EV Charging Equipment provides smart AC charging stations for home, workplace, and public locations under the Eve brand, contributing 28.00% of FY25 revenues
4.
Concentrated customer base: The Smart Grid Solutions segment relies on a highly concentrated customer base, with the top three Dutch operators holding a 95.00% market share
5.
Fragmented consumer segment: The EV charging client landscape is highly fragmented, with Alfen operating as a pure B2B supplier selling to system integrators, utilities, and wholesalers
6.
Integrated energy solutions: Alfen's primary growth focus is offering integrated cross-selling packages that combine transformer substations, battery storage, and EV charging points
7.
Direct B2B sales: In EV charging, Alfen operates as a pure B2B player, avoiding direct installations or network operations to focus entirely on hardware

Competitive positioning and value chain

8.
Dominant local share: Alfen maintains a strong competitive advantage in the Netherlands, holding an estimated 70.00% market share in local secondary transformer substations
9.
Turnkey deployment edge: Turnkey execution, local grid expertise, and in-house engineering provide Alfen with a strong moat in Dutch substations compared to pure importers
10.
Interoperable software platform: Software platforms like TheBattery Connect and proprietary smart charging network protocols create switching costs in commercial storage and charger plazas
11.
Highly commoditized markets: Home EV charging AC hardware is experiencing severe commoditization, with barriers to entry remaining low for standard physical assembly
12.
Stretched European consolidation: The European energy storage sector is growing rapidly but remains crowded, with Alfen competing against large players like Fluence and Tesla
13.
Technical protocols edge: Early leadership in smart grid technology and OCPP software development gives Alfen a deep integration advantage over new physical assemblers
14.
High local grid moat: Grid operators prefer Alfen's substations due to a 90-year history of local grid compliance, creating massive barriers against new importers
15.
Other side of the story: Standardized physical charging hardware could face rapid pricing erosion as low-cost Chinese manufacturers expand in Europe, rendering domestic assembly unprofitable

Section 5 – The possible investment case 15 findings

Section summary
  1. The investment case for Alfen is moderately strengthening but highly conditional on a successful structural expansion of the grid and the stabilization of EV charging pricing.

Structural tailwinds and business optimization

1.
Direct grid play: Smart Grid Solutions acts as a direct play on European grid congestion, which requires massive infrastructure reinforcement across core countries
2.
Accelerating regulatory tailwinds: Passage of the Dutch Crisis Act and elevated regulatory spending provide a high-probability catalyst to accelerate permitting times for substation installations
3.
Scaled industrial storage: Energy Storage Systems benefits from stationary utility-scale utility demand, where installations are projected to grow by 33.00% annually until 2030
4.
Rationalized EV portfolio: Portfolio simplification in EV charging aims to reduce AC charger complexity from five to three platforms, driving down procurement costs
5.
Targeted geographic reach: Commercial refocusing targets high-volume Southern European EV charging markets, including Italy, Spain, and Portugal to recover lost European market share
6.
Elevated network investments: Dutch grid operators have announced record-high capital investment budgets for the 2026 to 2028 period to combat grid capacity limits
7.
Expanding European reach: Alfen targets private smart grid expansion in Germany, Sweden, Finland, and France by leveraging existing commercial relationships

Trajectory constraints and bull case alignment

8.
Grid capacity limitations: Grid operator expansion plans could fail if severe labor scarcity and nitrogen emission constraints persist longer than anticipated
9.
Storage backlog risks: Storage growth remains lumpy and could stumble if continuing battery price deflation delays customer final investment decisions
10.
Severe price pressure: EV charger margins could erode further if low-cost Asian competitors intensify aggressive pricing campaigns in the residential segment
11.
Compound probability warning: The bull case requires all three segments to recover simultaneously, which represents a highly complex coordination task
12.
Backlog margin dilution: Storage gross margin targets were cut to a 15.00%-25.00% range, diluting group profit mix as utility-scale storage outgrows other segments
13.
Inherent project execution: Storage turnaround remains highly sensitive to contract disputes and project execution delays due to its lumpy, project-driven nature
14.
Other side of the story: Delayed grid connections could stall substation installations, completely cancelling expected operational leverage and leaving capacity underutilized

2.
Excess inventory risk: Alfen holds €10.00m of excess inventory in charging that must be digested, depressing near-term cash conversion for the next two years

Subdued margin mix: A major mix shift towards lower-margin grid projects and battery storage could severely compress consolidated adjusted operating margins

Turnaround cost drag: Realization of a €4.50m restructuring provision in 2026 could stifle near-term free cash flow generation during the turnaround phase
5.
Rising input costs: Turnaround margins are highly vulnerable to localized price inflation in memory chips, components, and logistics
6.
Stretched building schedules: Grid projects are frequently delayed by severe labor shortages and physical land constraints, pushing back equipment deliveries

7.
Short seller activity: Stated short interest remains moderate at 5.90% of share capital, having fallen significantly from historical peaks of 14.00%
8.
Single failure mode: A failure in project execution of multi-megawatt storage deals represents the most destructive single failure mode for earnings and bank covenants

11.
