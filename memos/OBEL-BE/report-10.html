  • Revenue stagnation: Belgian mobile and fixed revenue has been broadly flat-to-declining in real terms for several years; the bull case requires service revenue inflection that has not yet materialised in a credible trend [#J]

§4 — Accounting + Governance Red Flags

J-front verdict: accounting quality is mixed-to-cautionary; the most material concern is governance (controlled-parent structure with minority squeeze-out risk), not outright fraud; specific accounting flags are moderate not severe [#J]

  • Controlled-shareholder governance: ❌ Telenet (Liberty Global subsidiary) ~52% + Orange SA ~22% = ~74% combined control; minority shareholders (~26% free float) have limited ability to block related-party transactions, dividend policy changes, or squeeze-outs [#J]
  • Sub: Belgian company law allows squeeze-out at 95% threshold; theoretically distant but the structural trajectory of parental consolidation cannot be excluded

  • Sub: comparable European listed subsidiaries (e.g. Sunrise under Liberty Global prior to delisting) have experienced squeeze-outs at prices below independently assessed fair value

  • Related-party transactions: intercompany transactions with Orange SA (roaming, brand licensing, network sharing agreements) are disclosed but terms are not always independently verified as arm's-length [#A]

  • Sub: brand licensing fee paid to Orange SA is a recurring cash outflow; quantum disclosed in annual report but economic fairness vs. standalone brand value is not independently tested

  • Sub: network sharing arrangements with Telenet (the Wyre JV / shared infrastructure) create value-transfer risk — if terms favour Telenet, OBEL-BE minority shareholders bear the cost

  • Non-GAAP gap: OBEL-BE reports Adjusted EBITDA with recurring add-backs including restructuring, network integration costs, and transaction-related items [#A]

  • Sub: restructuring charges have recurred for 3+ consecutive years, raising the question of whether they are truly one-off or structural operating costs

  • Sub: GAAP EBIT is materially below Adjusted EBITDA; investors focused on Adjusted EBITDA may be systematically overstating normalised earnings power

  • Capitalisation — aggressive signal: OBEL-BE capitalises a substantial portion of network investment and subscriber acquisition costs; capitalisation rates are within Belgian GAAP/IFRS norms but at the more aggressive end of disclosed peer practice [#A]

  • Sub: subscriber acquisition cost capitalisation is standard in telecom but creates an asset base that must be amortised; rising SAC capitalisation can mask deteriorating payback periods on new subscribers

  • Sub: if churn accelerates, capitalised SAC is written off faster than depreciation schedules assume — earnings hit is front-loaded

  • Depreciation policy: depreciation and amortisation charges are high as a % of revenue (Belgian telecom norms), but useful-life assumptions on network assets have not been visibly shortened despite technology transition risks (copper-to-fibre obsolescence) [#A]

  • Sub: bear case: copper and legacy fixed-network assets may be impaired ahead of accounting schedules if full-fibre overbuild by Proximus accelerates

  • Revenue recognition: no specific revenue recognition concern identified; IFRS 15 disclosures are standard; bundle/contract revenue recognition is consistent with sector peers; no obvious channel stuffing signal [#A]

  • Auditor: PwC Belgium has been statutory auditor for OBEL-BE; no auditor change in recent history; no material weakness disclosed; audit opinions are unqualified [#A]

  • Sub: PwC continuity is a mild positive; no rotation pressure or independence concern visible

  • Board independence: ❌ board composition is dominated by parent nominees; independent director count is at the minimum Belgian CG Code requirement; effective minority shareholder representation on the board is limited [#A]

  • Sub: audit committee independence is technically compliant but the "independent" directors were in several cases nominated with parent influence

  • Sub: peer median for European listed subsidiaries shows similar structures, but this does not reduce the risk — it indicates the sector is systemically weak on this dimension

  • Management comp structure: CEO and CFO compensation is not fully disclosed to a granular level in annual report; LTI structure is linked to group Orange SA metrics, not solely OBEL-BE standalone performance [#A]

  • Sub: incentive misalignment: management has a career trajectory within the Orange SA group; decisions favouring the parent over OBEL-BE minority shareholders are not necessarily punished in compensation terms

  • Legal / regulatory: OBEL-BE faces ongoing Belgian telecom regulatory proceedings (BIPT rate regulation, wholesale access pricing); no material outstanding litigation disclosed beyond normal regulatory disputes [#A]

  • Sub: BIPT has historically set wholesale access prices that constrained OBEL-BE's ability to extract high margins from MVNOs; regulatory risk is asymmetrically downward for margins

  • NWC liability — receivables: no significant receivables aging deterioration identified from public accounts; trade receivables are consistent with sector norms [#A]

  • NWC liability — payables stretching: trade payables as a % of COGS have drifted modestly higher in recent periods, potentially indicating mild cash pressure; not at alarm level but worth monitoring [#A]

  • FCF-to-net-income conversion: FCF conversion is below reported net income in multiple recent years when capex and spectrum payments are included; this is telecom-normal but the gap has widened vs. prior cycle [#A]

  • Bull rebuttal (§4): the accounting is telecom-standard IFRS; PwC provides unqualified audit; Belgian regulatory framework imposes adequate disclosure; the governance concerns are structural (ownership) not operational fraud; manageable for a long investor who prices the discount

§6 — Counter-Arguments to Bull Case

J-front verdict: the five main bull thesis pillars each have a credible structural bear rebuttal; the capex/FCF and governance rebuttals are the most analytically robust [#J]

  • Bull thesis #1: management is executing a convergent fixed-mobile strategy that will drive ARPU growth and margin expansion [#J]
  • Bear rebuttal #1: OBEL-BE's fixed-mobile convergence strategy is a follower, not a leader; Proximus launched a full-fibre-led convergence offer first; OBEL-BE's cable infrastructure (via the Telenet/Orange network share) is a depreciating asset in a fibre world
  • Sub: convergence penetration in Belgium has not demonstrated ARPU uplift at scale; churn reduction from bundling is offset by discounting required to win converged customers
  • Sub: competitive evidence: Proximus's VHCN investment (€3bn+ announced) structurally changes the access landscape; OBEL-BE cannot match this capex without impacting FCF severely

  • Sub: track record: OBEL-BE has guided for convergence-led margin improvement multiple times; EBITDA margin has been broadly flat, not expanding

  • Bull thesis #2: dividend yield is attractive and sustainable, providing a total return floor [#J]

  • Bear rebuttal #2: the dividend is not clearly covered by organic FCF after capex and spectrum payments; payout is partly a function of parent (Orange SA) need for distributions, not standalone FCF generation
  • Sub: evidence: in years of elevated capex (5G, spectrum auctions), FCF has fallen below declared dividend; gap was bridged by timing of capex payments or working capital; structurally fragile
  • Sub: if 5G capex continues at current pace + spectrum fees are elevated + EBITDA growth misses, dividend cut probability is non-trivial (subjective: 20–35% over 3Y horizon)

  • Sub: peer anchor: European telecom dividend coverage ratios (FCF/DPS) ≥1.2x considered safe; OBEL-BE has periodically been below 1.0x on a pure FCF basis

  • Bull thesis #3: squeeze-out premium provides a floor / upside option [#J]

  • Bear rebuttal #3: squeeze-out timing is entirely at the parent's discretion; minority shareholders cannot force or accelerate a bid; waiting for a premium that never comes is a value trap
  • Sub: Liberty Global has a documented history of delisting subsidiaries (Ziggo, Sunrise) at premiums that, while positive, were below independent fair value estimates
  • Sub: the squeeze-out "option" has no expiry or strike; it could take 5–10 years, during which the stock returns nothing if dividends are cut

  • Sub: governance evidence: Telenet/Liberty Global's track record at Belgian and Swiss subsidiaries suggests squeeze-out at 10–20% premium to prevailing market, not 30–50% premium to fair value

  • Bull thesis #4: 5G monetisation will drive a new revenue cycle in enterprise and IoT [#J]

  • Bear rebuttal #4: Belgian enterprise 5G market is small and highly contested; Proximus has the incumbent enterprise relationships; OBEL-BE's enterprise share is structurally below its consumer share
  • Sub: IoT monetisation in Belgian telco has been a perpetual "next cycle" promise across multiple management teams; no evidence of breakout IoT revenue at any Belgian MVNO or MNO at scale

  • Sub: 5G capex is front-loaded; revenue upside is 5–7 years out; in the interim, the capex/revenue mismatch suppresses FCF

  • Bull thesis #5: OBEL-BE trades at a discount to European telecom peers on EV/EBITDA — valuation is undemanding [#J]

  • Bear rebuttal #5: the apparent discount is a governance/liquidity/control discount, not a re-rating opportunity; it persists structurally for controlled subsidiaries
  • Sub: peer anchor: European controlled telecom subsidiaries (Iliad-listed entities, Swisscom with confederation control, others) trade at a structural 15–25% discount to independent-company EV/EBITDA multiples; OBEL-BE's discount may be correctly priced
  • Sub: if EBITDA declines, even a stable EV/EBITDA multiple produces a lower share price; discount doesn't protect if the denominator falls

  • Sub: on bear-case EBITDA (-10 to -15% from current consensus) at current or slightly compressed multiples, stock is fairly valued or overvalued, not cheap

  • Bull rebuttal (§6): bull thesis #3 (squeeze-out optionality) has the weakest bear rebuttal — a squeeze-out at even a modest premium is a certain positive return; the bear case cannot quantify timing risk with precision; the squeeze-out option value is non-zero and potentially significant if parents accelerate consolidation plans

  • Sub: EPS downside: if dividend cut is driven by EBITDA miss, EPS also misses; combined P/E and yield-basis de-rating

  • Sub: revenue impact: fixed broadband revenue -10 to -15%; margin impact: fixed is higher-margin; EBITDA impact -7 to -12%

  • Sub: capex impact: OBEL-BE forced to accelerate counter-investment or accept subscriber losses; neither outcome is FCF-positive

  • Downside scenario C (squeeze-out at low premium):

  • Sub: minority shareholders receive "fair price" legally but below independent DCF valuation; effectively value destruction for long-term holders who bought at higher prices

  • Margin compression: EBITDA margin could compress from ~30–33% (current) to 26–28% under combined competitive pressure + elevated cost base; at this level, FCF generation is insufficient to cover capex + dividend [#I]
