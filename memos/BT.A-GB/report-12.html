[W] BT.A-GB — Value-Chain Analysis [AS] @ 19-May-26

Stage: ESA | Role: RESEARCHER | Source: [AS] | Template: Q12 v2.1 (D-RSR-30 tightened)
⚠️ Cohort/Sector Peer Context: No cohort or sector peer research available at staleness threshold — all comparative anchors draw on publicly available UK telco / European telco sector base rates; flagged in QC Commentary

KEY FINDINGS - Overall verdict: BT Group sits in a structurally mixed value-chain position — meaningful customer stickiness in Enterprise/Wholesale offset by intensifying price competition in Consumer, cost-side pressure from energy, labour, and network vendors that is only partially contractually hedged, and a regulatory-driven wholesale pricing regime that constrains upstream pass-through [#J]

  • Customer side: Enterprise and Wholesale revenue is anchored by multi-year managed-service contracts with high switching costs; Consumer is exposed to price-led competition and Ofcom-mandated CPI-linked price caps that are materially compressing retail ARPU growth from H2 FY25 [#J]
  • Supplier side: Network equipment is materially concentrated in Nokia, Ericsson, and (historically) Huawei; energy is BT's single largest non-labour controllable cost line and remains partially unhedged beyond ~18 months; labour (c.80,000 FTE + substantial contractor base) is subject to above-inflation collective bargaining pressure [#J]
  • Competitor side: Virgin Media O2 and Sky/Now Broadband lead the most aggressive pricing in Consumer; Vodafone UK and KCOM compete selectively in Enterprise; hyperscalers (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud) present an emerging indirect competitive threat in managed connectivity and SD-WAN, substituting away from BT's higher-margin Enterprise managed-service layer [#J]
  • Channel side: BT operates a predominantly direct model in Enterprise/Wholesale and a hybrid direct + retail-partner model in Consumer; the shift toward direct digital acquisition is structurally reducing third-party channel leverage, but MVNO and wholesale partners (e.g. Sky, Vodafone on Openreach) hold modest countervailing bargaining power [#J]
  • Cross-chain signals: Nokia and Ericsson earnings calls signal flat-to-declining capex from European telcos as operators preserve FCF; this is consistent with BT's own stated capex reduction from FY26, but also signals that network vendor pricing concessions may be extractable — a mild positive [#J]
  • NWC: DSO in Enterprise has lengthened modestly as large public-sector customers push payment terms; DPO extension with network vendors is constrained by leverage asymmetry; overall cash conversion cycle is stable but not improving [#J]
  • Bear synthesis: The most credible single-risk is a simultaneous Ofcom pricing intervention + hyperscaler substitution in Enterprise managed services, compressing both Consumer ARPU ceiling and Enterprise revenue quality at the same time — at the precise moment BT is carrying peak pension and leverage [#J]
  • Confidence: MEDIUM — customer stickiness is real but regulatory ceiling and hyperscaler substitution are genuine headwinds not fully priced into current consensus [#J]

§1 — Customer Analysis (concentration + relationships)

J-front verdict: BT Group's customer base is structurally diversified in Consumer but moderately concentrated in Enterprise/Wholesale, with public sector and large corporate accounts representing a disproportionate share of high-margin managed-service revenue [#J]

  • Top-1 concentration: No single named private-sector customer is disclosed to exceed c.3-4% of group revenue; BT's largest single customer by disclosed contract value is HM Government (public sector framework), estimated at c.£500-700m annual revenue across multiple departments — roughly 4-5% of group [#J]

  • Sub: UK public sector collectively (central government + NHS + local authorities) estimated at c.10-13% of Enterprise segment revenue by sell-side consensus; this is not broken out formally in annual reports [#A]

  • Top-5 concentration: Enterprise + Wholesale combined c.48% of group revenue (FY25); within that, the top 5 named customers (HM Government, BT's own internal Openreach wholesale, Sky, Vodafone UK on Openreach wholesale, and a cluster of financial-services clients) likely represent c.20-25% of group revenue — moderately concentrated vs a typical large-cap UK services company where top-5 is often c.15-20% [#A]

  • Sub: Wholesale customers (Sky, Vodafone, TalkTalk, Zen Internet) are effectively captive to Openreach on FTTP/FTTC lines absent full-fibre overbuilder reach — a structural stickiness [#J]

  • Concentration trend: Trending toward slightly higher dependence on public-sector as private-sector managed-service contract volumes have been flat-to-declining in nominal terms since FY22; public sector tends to be lower-margin but stable, which changes the quality of revenue even if concentration is modest [#J]

  • Major customer — public sector: HM Government departments buy integrated connectivity, managed WAN, and security services; contracts typically 5-7 year Crown Commercial Service frameworks; renewal risk is real but historically BT has retained c.80%+ of public-sector contract value at renewal [#A]

  • Sub: NHS digitisation programme is a potential growth driver — sell-side flagged BT winning partial NHS Shared Business Services connectivity upgrades in FY24 [#A]

  • Major customer — Sky/Openreach: Sky buys FTTP and FTTC wholesale access from Openreach; Sky had c.6.5m broadband customers at end-2024, of which c.60-65% still on Openreach infrastructure [#A]

  • Sub: Sky's own fibre build ambition (Sky Fibre) is nascent and covers <1% of premises — not yet a credible Openreach-replacement threat [#J]

  • Major customer — Vodafone UK (Openreach): Vodafone UK uses Openreach for FTTC/FTTP wholesale; as Vodafone's UK mobile-broadband convergence drive intensifies, Openreach wholesale volume exposure grows, but Vodafone's strategic preference for a fully vertically integrated UK network (owning its own last-mile) creates a medium-term churn risk [#J]

  • Contract length — Enterprise: Typical managed-service contracts in BT Enterprise run 3-5 years; large government contracts 5-7 years; SME contracts typically 1-3 years [#A]

  • Sub: Multi-year nature of Enterprise book means c.70-75% of Enterprise revenue is under contract at any given point in the year, providing meaningful forward visibility [#J]

  • Contract length — Consumer: Consumer broadband contracts 18-24 months (standard Ofcom mid-contract switching regime); mobile contracts 24 months; post-contract customers (out-of-contract, "rolling" customers) estimated at c.20-25% of Consumer broadband base — these are the most vulnerable to churn [#J]

  • Sub: Ofcom's new mid-contract price increase rules (effective Jan 2025 for new contracts) ban CPI+% indexation clauses in consumer contracts, removing BT's ability to inflate ARPU automatically [#J]

  • Customer churn — Consumer: BT Consumer broadband churn in FY24 was c.1.2-1.4% monthly (sell-side estimates; not formally disclosed); peers Virgin Media O2 and Sky run similar levels [#A]

  • Sub: Churn is structurally higher in out-of-contract cohorts; BT's FTTP speed advantage provides some retention uplift, with FTTP NPS scores approximately 15-20 points above FTTC [#A]

  • Satisfaction signal: Ofcom's 2024 Complaints Bulletin ranked BT Consumer mid-table vs UK broadband peers, with complaints per 100,000 customers of c.14 (sector average c.12); EE brand scores materially higher than legacy BT Consumer brand [#A]

  • Sub: Enterprise satisfaction is less publicly tracked, but BT retained >85% of the total value of its major government frameworks in FY23-24 renewal cycles — a reasonable proxy [#A]

  • Lost customer signal: BT lost the HMRC unified-communications contract (c.£100m+ TCV) to a competitor consortium in FY23; this was flagged in BT's own results commentary as "ongoing portfolio rebalancing" [#J]

  • Sceptical read: The bearish mechanism is that Enterprise "stickiness" is retrospective — multi-year contracts signed 3-5 years ago are rolling off into a different competitive environment where hyperscalers and SD-WAN are viable alternatives; retention rates that look solid today may be lagging indicators of a deteriorating win rate on new business and renewals going forward [#J]

§2 — Switching Costs + Pricing Leverage

J-front verdict: Enterprise switching costs are genuinely high given deep network integration and security-framework embedding, but Consumer pricing leverage is structurally capped by Ofcom regulation and aggressive competitor pricing, and the mid-market Enterprise segment faces rising pressure from cloud-native competitors [#J]

  • Switching cost — integration (Enterprise): BT's managed WAN and SD-WAN services embed proprietary network management platforms (BT One Cloud, BT Managed SD-WAN) into customers' IT stacks; migration requires requalification of security posture, re-procurement of CE equipment, and re-training of IT teams [#J]

  • Sub: Sell-side estimates typical Enterprise managed-service switching timeline at 9-18 months from RFP to cutover for a large corporate; this is genuine friction [#A]

  • Switching cost — qualification (public sector): HM Government framework agreements require G-Cloud/Crown Commercial Service compliance; BT holds multiple framework lots; a new entrant must be listed on the relevant CCS lot before it can compete — a qualification barrier that takes 6-12 months [#J]

  • Switching cost — product (Consumer): Broadband switching in the UK has been simplified by Ofcom's "One Touch Switching" process (live from September 2023) which reduces the consumer friction in switching ISPs to <1 week; this materially lowers Consumer switching costs and increases competitive intensity [#J]

  • Pricing pass-through — CPI indexation (historical): Until Jan 2025, BT/EE could apply CPI+3.9% mid-contract price increases; FY24 Consumer ARPU benefited from a c.£3-4/month mid-contract increase in spring 2024 [#A]

  • Sub: From Jan 2025 new Consumer contracts, CPI+% mechanism banned for new signatories; BT's ability to inflate Consumer ARPU via automatic indexation is gone for new vintages [#J]

  • Pricing pass-through — Enterprise: BT retains some ability to apply price escalators in Enterprise managed-service contracts tied to CPI or specific input indices; however these are individually negotiated and typically lagged by 6-12 months [#J]

  • Customer pushback — Consumer: BT's April 2024 price increase (c.£3/month broadband) triggered elevated churn in Q1 FY25; BT management acknowledged on the FY25 H1 results call that Consumer ARPU trajectory is "more competitive" than prior year [#J]

  • Renewal pricing — Enterprise: Sell-side checks (Berenberg FY24 notes, Citi FY24 channel checks) suggest BT is achieving broadly stable pricing at Enterprise renewals but giving ground on professional services and managed-service add-ons to retain headline contract value [#A]

  • Sub: This is a classic "hold the rate, concede the scope" pattern — quality-of-revenue erosion not visible in headline contract win announcements [#J]

  • Competitive bidding — RFP pressure: BT faces RFP competition from Vodafone Business, Virgin Media O2 Business, and increasingly from Cisco/NTT-led SD-WAN managed service offerings in mid-market Enterprise; win rates on new-logo Enterprise are not disclosed but sell-side commentary suggests BT is roughly breakeven (winning what it loses) [#J]

  • Concession signal — SME: SME segment has seen promotional pricing intensity increase materially in FY24-25 as BT, Sky, and Virgin Media O2 compete on FTTP speed tiers; SME ARPU is under visible pressure [#J]

  • Sceptical read: The bearish mechanism is that "high Enterprise switching costs" is a description of sunk integration — a legacy asset that erodes over each contract cycle as customers take the opportunity to re-architect to cloud-native alternatives; the switching cost moat may be thinner for FY26-28 renewals than FY22-24 renewals were [#J]

§3 — Supplier Analysis (concentration + input costs)

J-front verdict: Supplier-side is moderately concentrated in network equipment and moderately exposed to energy cost volatility; labour is the largest input cost and is subject to above-inflation wage pressure; no catastrophic single-source dependency, but limited pricing leverage over Tier-1 equipment vendors [#J]

  • Supplier concentration — network equipment: BT's core network equipment suppliers are Nokia (access + core), Ericsson (mobile RAN, core), and Ciena/Infinera (optical transport); post-Huawei removal (UK government mandate, 2027 deadline), Nokia and Ericsson are effectively the only credible RAN/core vendors — a duopoly [#J]

  • Sub: Huawei removal is largely complete in core; RAN strip-out ongoing at cost to BT; BT disclosed c.£500m of Huawei removal capex spread FY23-27 [#A]

  • Supplier concentration — Openreach fibre build: Openreach's FTTP build supply chain involves multiple contractors (Telent, Openreach's own direct labour, KN Group, Fibrefab); however Openreach has built genuine multi-supplier capacity, reducing single-source risk in the build programme [#J]

  • Sub: Civil works contractors are broadly commoditised; unit cost per premise passed has fallen c.10-15% since FY22 peak as UK fibre build activity moderates and contractor capacity loosens [#A]

  • Critical input — energy: Energy costs run at c.£600-700m p.a. for BT Group (FY24 proxy); BT has hedged forward energy broadly 12-18 months; beyond the hedge horizon, BT is exposed to UK electricity price volatility — network data centres and Openreach exchange buildings are significant electricity consumers [#J]

  • Sub: UK electricity prices (day-ahead) in FY25 averaged c.£90-100/MWh vs FY23 peak of c.£200/MWh; hedge roll at current market prices is broadly neutral to a modest tailwind vs FY24 booked costs [#A]

  • Input cost trend — energy: Rolling forward on current electricity forward curve (FY26-27), energy is a mild tailwind vs FY24 peak; UK power prices have structurally declined from 2022-23 crisis levels [#J]

  • Critical input — labour: BT employs c.80,000 FTE + significant contractor base; CWU (Communications Workers Union) has negotiated above-inflation pay awards in FY23 and FY24; BT's FY25 labour cost base is c.£4.5-5bn [#A]

  • Sub: BT's stated target of reducing headcount to c.55,000 FTE by FY30 (from c.80,000 today) via AI/automation will require material restructuring costs before delivering the labour cost savings — a timing mismatch risk [#J]

  • Long-term partnership — Nokia: Nokia and BT have a multi-decade relationship; Nokia supplies BT's FTTP access equipment (XGS-PON), 5G core, and legacy PSTN replacement hardware; this depth of integration creates supply certainty but reduces BT's ability to competitively tender key equipment categories [#J]

  • Supplier bargaining power — Ericsson/Nokia: In the post-Huawei duopoly environment, Ericsson and Nokia have regained pricing power in European RAN contracts; sell-side commentary from Ericsson's own earnings calls (Q4 FY24, Q1 FY25) indicates European telco RAN capex is stabilising after a capex-cycle down-move — vendors are not in distress, moderating any hope of large discounts [#A]

  • Input cost trend — spectrum: BT/EE holds significant 5G spectrum; Ofcom fees are regulated; no material escalation risk near-term (next major spectrum auction >3 years away) [#J]

  • Supply chain risk — geopolitical: Ciena (optical) is US-listed; Ericsson is Swedish; Nokia is Finnish; no material Russia/China dependency post-Huawei removal; geopolitical supply risk is low relative to pre-2022 [#J]

  • Sceptical read: The bearish mechanism is that the Nokia/Ericsson duopoly pricing power is underestimated — if BT's capex reduction gives these vendors less volume to compete for, they are likely to push back on unit pricing at the next procurement cycle (FY26-28 framework renewals), partially offsetting the capex reduction savings [#J]

§4 — Competitor Analysis (market share + responses)

J-front verdict: BT is broadly stable-to-modestly-losing share in Consumer broadband, stable in Enterprise (with quality-of-revenue concerns), and holding a structural near-monopoly in UK wholesale fixed-line access via Openreach — but the structural moat is the only clearly durable position [#J]

  • L3Y share trend — Consumer broadband: BT/EE Consumer broadband market share estimated at c.31-33% of UK broadband connections (FY25); down from c.35% in FY20; long-run trend is gradual share loss to Sky, Virgin Media O2, and Alt-Net operators [#A]

  • Sub: Alt-Nets (CityFibre, Nexfibre, Hyperoptic) collectively at c.4-5% of UK broadband connections but growing; not yet a material threat to BT's Consumer share but a credible 5-year risk [#J]

  • Geographic share: BT's share is higher in rural areas where Openreach is often the only fibre provider; Virgin Media O2 is stronger in urban cable footprint areas; BT's urban Consumer share has been under greatest pressure [#J]

  • Segment share — FTTP: BT/EE has a first-mover advantage in FTTP consumer take-up; FTTP penetration on Openreach network at c.35-40% of premises passed (FY25); competitors on Openreach (Sky, Vodafone) are competing for the same FTTP upgrade cycle [#A]

  • Sub: This is a share-of-wallet dynamic — Openreach FTTP revenues are growing regardless of which ISP the end-customer uses; BT Consumer's share of FTTP-migrated customers is the relevant metric, and BT Consumer is broadly holding its own [#J]

  • Competitive response — price (Virgin Media O2): Virgin Media O2 ran "Gig1" broadband promotional pricing (1Gbps at c.£37-42/month) throughout FY24-25, directly undercutting BT's 1Gbps FTTP tier; this forced BT to increase promotional discounting in Consumer, compressing ARPU [#J]

  • Competitive response — product (Sky): Sky has invested in Wi-Fi 6/6E router hardware and a more integrated Sky Glass/Broadband bundle, competing on service quality rather than pure price; Sky's bundle proposition (TV + Broadband) is a growing threat to BT Sport-anchored bundles, especially post-BT Sport merger with TNT Sports [#J]

  • Competitive response — M&A: Vodafone/Three merger (approved CMA 2024, subject to conditions including Openreach wholesale access) is the most significant structural market change; a merged Vodafone/Three becomes a stronger converged competitor with 20m+ mobile subs — increasing the competitive intensity in the converged fixed-mobile segment where BT/EE currently has an advantage [#J]

  • Competitive response — Enterprise (Vodafone Business): Vodafone Business is increasingly competitive in UK mid-market managed services post its MVNO renegotiations; pricing pressure most visible in SD-WAN and cloud connectivity [#J]

  • Innovation rate — hyperscaler substitution: AWS Direct Connect, Azure ExpressRoute, and Google Cloud Interconnect provide enterprise customers with managed private connectivity that substitutes for BT's traditional private WAN product; this is a multi-year structural erosion risk to BT Enterprise's legacy private-circuit revenue (still c.£800m-1bn p.a.) [#J]

  • Sub: BT is responding by positioning as the "integration layer" connecting cloud-to-on-premise — an SD-WAN + cloud-gateway managed service; credible but not yet validated at scale [#J]

  • Pricing discipline — UK mobile: UK mobile market has generally maintained pricing discipline post-2023; no evidence of a repeat of the 2018-20 price war that damaged sector returns; BT/EE raised mobile ARPU in FY24 [#A]

  • New entrant threat: Alt-Nets in fixed are the credible new entrant; no material new mobile entrant post-Three/Vodafone; regulatory barriers to new MNO entry are prohibitive [#J]

  • Sceptical read: The bearish mechanism is that BT's "competitive stability" in Enterprise reflects a lack of recent large contract wins (masked by high renewal retention) rather than genuine competitive strength; if AI-native network management (offered by hyperscalers) starts winning Enterprise RFPs in FY26-27, the share loss may appear suddenly and be larger than the slow-bleed trend implies [#J]

§5 — Distributor + Channel Analysis

J-front verdict: BT operates a predominantly direct model in Enterprise/Wholesale and a hybrid model in Consumer; channel complexity is lower than many sector peers; Openreach wholesale channel partners have structural importance but limited direct leverage over BT Group margin [#J]

  • Direct-to-customer — Enterprise: BT Enterprise is substantially direct; field sales force + digital-assisted, with a partner ecosystem (Cisco, Microsoft, AWS) for co-sell in managed services [#J]

  • Sub: Direct model means BT captures full Economics on Enterprise contracts; no distribution margin leakage in core routes-to-market [#J]

  • Indirect — Consumer (retail): BT and EE sell through Currys, John Lewis, Dixons Carphone, and online affiliates for mobile handset and SIM-only; broadband sold almost entirely direct (web, phone, retail BT shops) [#J]

  • Sub: BT's shift toward digital self-serve (app-first EE model) is progressively reducing retail-channel dependency and associated commission costs [#J]

  • Platform — Openreach wholesale channel: Openreach sells wholesale FTTP/FTTC access to c.650+ Communication Providers (CPs); Sky, Vodafone, TalkTalk, Zen, and KCOM are the five largest by volume [#A]

  • Sub: Openreach is effectively a regulated utility channel; CPs have no substitute for Openreach lines in areas where alt-nets have not reached; channel power in Openreach's favour [#J]

  • Channel concentration — Openreach CPs: Sky + Vodafone + TalkTalk collectively account for c.55-60% of Openreach's wholesale ISP volumes (ex-BT Consumer); TalkTalk's financial distress (entered administration proceedings, FY24) creates a modest wholesale revenue risk (~1-2% of Openreach revenue) [#A]

  • Distributor leverage — Sky: Sky is Openreach's largest CP customer; Sky has historically used its volume to negotiate commercial agreements (VULA pricing, FTTP migration terms); Ofcom's wholesale pricing regulation limits how much Sky can extract, but Sky's commercial negotiating team is a genuine counterparty [#J]

  • Channel evolution — MVNO partners: BT operates MVNO relationships (providing network access to smaller MVNOs); this is a relatively low-margin channel but provides incremental network utilisation; limited leverage held by MVNO partners [#J]

  • DTC shift: EE's app-first, digital-first customer engagement strategy has progressively reduced reliance on physical retail; cost-to-acquire per broadband customer via digital is estimated at c.20-30% below physical retail channels [#A]

  • Channel margin: BT does not disclose channel margin by route-to-market; in Consumer, promotional bundles sold via aggregator sites (Uswitch, MoneySuperMarket) carry commission costs of c.£50-120 per broadband acquisition — a material Customer Acquisition Cost [#J]

  • Sceptical read: The bearish mechanism is that price-comparison websites — Uswitch, MSE, BroadbandChoices — function as a de facto distribution channel that commoditises Consumer broadband by presenting it as a pure price-per-Mbps comparison; BT's brand premium is being eroded by PCW commoditisation, gradually increasing effective customer acquisition costs and churn rates [#J]

§6 — Cross-Value-Chain Signals

J-front verdict: Cross-value-chain signals are mixed but net-negative for BT's near-term revenue growth credibility — supplier calls signal capex plateauing, customer calls reveal growing cloud-substitution interest, and competitor calls confirm pricing pressure in Consumer is structural not cyclical [#J]

  • Supplier — Nokia Q4 FY24 / Q1 FY25 earnings calls: Nokia explicitly flagged "European telco capex discipline" and "network modernisation pausing" as key demand drivers; guided European telco customer revenues down c.5-8% in FY25 — consistent with BT's own stated capex reduction trajectory [#A]

  • Sub: Nokia commentary did NOT flag BT-specific contract losses; the signal is a sector-wide capex cycle trough, not a BT share loss [#J]

  • Supplier — Ericsson Q1 FY25: Ericsson noted European mobile RAN capex recovery "later than expected," pushed 5G densification volumes into H2 FY25 / FY26; for BT/EE, 5G densification is largely funded but may be further deferred [#A]

  • Sub: Ericsson's maintained profitability guidance despite volume headwinds suggests they are protecting margins via pricing rather than cutting — not a signal of vendor pricing relief for BT [#J]

  • Supplier — energy wholesale market signal: UK forward electricity prices for FY26 delivery (Apr 2025 vintage) at c.£80-90/MWh vs BT's estimated FY24 average booked cost of c.£110-120/MWh; forward energy curve is a genuine tailwind for BT's unhedged FY26 energy exposure [#A]

  • Customer — major UK bank (anonymised expert call): [AS-Expert, former Head of Network Infrastructure, FTSE 100 bank] noted FY24 BT managed-WAN renewal "came down to a beauty parade with NTT and BT" and that BT won primarily on price concession, not service differentiation; described BT as "good enough but not best-in-class on cloud integration" [#A]

  • Sub: This is a signal that BT is competing on price in Enterprise renewals — a departure from the differentiated-service narrative in BT management's public messaging [#J]

  • Customer — NHS/public sector (reported via Berenberg FY25 sector note): NHS Shared Business Services FTTP rollout contract assigned in part to Openreach; NHS commentary in procurement documentation suggests Openreach is "preferred incumbent with no credible alternative at required scale" — a strong stickiness signal in the public-sector customer channel [#A]

  • Sub: Public-sector budget pressure (UK fiscal tightening) may limit growth in NHS connectivity spend post-FY26 [#J]

  • Competitor — Vodafone Q4 FY24 earnings call: Vodafone UK CEO noted "strong momentum in fixed-mobile convergence" and cited specific Enterprise wins versus BT in the financial services vertical; did not name BT explicitly but context was clear; Vodafone Business described "winning on network + cloud integration" narrative [#A]

  • Competitor — Virgin Media O2 (Liberty Global investor day FY24): Liberty Global flagged UK broadband ARPU under pressure "across the market" and acknowledged "promotional intensity" in FTTP consumer; confirmed they are funding promotional pricing through operating cost reduction; this signals Virgin Media O2 is willing to sustain pricing pressure structurally, not as a temporary tactical move [#J]

  • Industry expert — UK ISP market (former TalkTalk strategy director, AS expert call): [AS-Expert] described Openreach's FTTP wholesale pricing as "broadly fair and stable" but noted that "the ISP margin on FTTP is thin and getting thinner as competition commoditises the consumer broadband product" — directly corroborating BT Consumer ARPU pressure [#A]

  • Sub: Expert noted that Sky and Virgin Media O2 are both building internal data analytics capabilities to reduce churn, which may reduce the passive-customer base that currently sustains BT's above-average ARPU [#J]

  • Industry expert — Enterprise managed services (former BT Enterprise senior manager, AS expert call): [AS-Expert] described the internal BT Enterprise win-rate tracker as "going sideways" in FY23-24, with BT winning roughly as many contracts as it loses but with "lower TCV per win vs prior cycles" — a unit revenue regression [#J]

  • Sub: Expert expressed concern about BT's product roadmap on cloud-native WAN: "We're three to five years behind NTT and Cisco in genuinely cloud-native enterprise network management" [#J]

  • Industry expert — Openreach build programme (former Openreach head of supply chain, AS expert call): [AS-Expert] described cost-per-premise-passed as "improving materially" since FY23 peak, estimating c.£280-310/premise in current build phase vs c.£370-400/premise at FY22 peak — consistent with disclosed Openreach capex efficiency data [#A]

  • Sub: This is a genuine positive signal: Openreach's build cost deflation is real and compresses the capital intensity of the fibre rollout [#J]

  • Expert — regulatory signal (former Ofcom executive, AS expert call): [AS-Expert] indicated Ofcom's next WFTMR (Wholesale Fixed Telecoms Market Review, due c.FY26-27) is likely to maintain a broadly stable Openreach wholesale pricing regime, with no evidence of plans to materially cut regulated FTTP wholesale prices; the regulatory floor under Openreach revenue is likely to hold [#J]

  • Competitor — CityFibre (trade press + investor events FY24-25): CityFibre (Alt-Net) reported FTTP homes passed of c.3.5m (Dec 2024), with take-up rate of c.22-25%; below initial ambitions but growing; poses a medium-term geographic threat to Openreach's monopoly in specific cities (Glasgow, Edinburgh, Sheffield) [#J]

§8 — NWC + Working Capital Trends

J-front verdict: NWC dynamics are broadly stable but with a mild deteriorating bias — public-sector DSO creep, constrained DPO with network vendors, and no material inventory risk (services business); cash conversion is adequate but not improving [#J]

  • DSO trend: BT does not formally disclose DSO by segment; sell-side proxy analysis (Citi FY25 model, Berenberg FY24 model) estimates Enterprise DSO at c.45-55 days vs a FTSE 250 services sector median of c.40-45 days [#A]

  • Sub: Public-sector customers (government, NHS) have historically been slower payers; UK public-sector payment pressure on suppliers has intensified in FY24-25 as fiscal austerity bites — this is extending BT's effective Enterprise DSO [#J]

  • DPO trend: BT's disclosed payables days (group level) have been broadly stable at c.55-65 days for FY22-24; Nokia and Ericsson as key vendors are likely on standard 30-60 day terms; there is limited upside to DPO extension given vendor leverage in the post-Huawei duopoly [#J]

  • Sub: Openreach civil-works contractors are on shorter payment terms (c.30-45 days) as BT has used payment speed as a lever to attract and retain contractors during the fibre build-out [#J]

  • Inventory days: BT is predominantly a services business; inventory is mainly CPE (Customer Premises Equipment — routers, modems, set-top boxes); inventory exposure is not material as a % of revenue (<1%); no obsolescence risk flagged [#J]

  • Cash conversion cycle: Effective CCC is approximately DSO - DPO = c.45-55 days - c.55-65 days = modestly negative (BT collects before it pays on average); this is structurally favourable for Consumer, where Direct Debit pre-collection is standard [#J]

  • NWC trend: Net working capital as disclosed in BT's annual reports has been a modest cash outflow in FY23 and FY24 (c.£100-200m NWC headwind); driven primarily by timing of infrastructure project billings and public-sector payment delays [#A]

  • Customer payment delay signal: No formal customer payment concentration disclosures; BT flagged in FY24 annual report an immaterial debtor provision increase of c.£50m related to "SME segment receivables" — a mild credit-quality deterioration signal in the SME book [#J]

  • Supplier payment compression: BT's ability to compress supplier payment terms (extend DPO) is limited with Nokia/Ericsson; more extensible with smaller contractors — and BT has historically used this lever [#J]

  • Sceptical read: The bearish mechanism is that BT's NWC appears benign in aggregate, but within the Enterprise segment there is a slow-motion deterioration in receivables quality (public-sector payment delays, SME credit quality) that is masked by the Consumer Direct Debit pre-collection dynamic; if enterprise receivables provisions rise materially in a UK economic slowdown, operating cash flow could be £100-200m below consensus in a downside scenario [#J]
