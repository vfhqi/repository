3. THEME §1 — Revenue Drivers + Visibility

J-front verdict: Revenue-side uncertainty is HIGH — ServCo's standalone top-line is not yet well-documented in public filings; consumer broadband churn and B2B contract renewal cadence are the two live risk vectors

Q1 — Consumer broadband net adds trajectory

  • Question: "What is the current monthly net-add run-rate on consumer FTTH, and what's your 12-month forward assumption?"
  • Expected answer: Management guides to modest positive net adds, citing FTTH upsell momentum offsetting copper-to-fibre migration
  • Good answer: Specific number, trend-line improving, churn below 1.5% monthly, ARPU uplift from fibre conversion quantified
  • Bad answer: Directional only ("improving"), no ARPU delta cited, churn described qualitatively rather than numerically
  • Follow-up probe: "What share of the net-add pool is incremental new customers vs copper-base migration — and what's the ARPU differential?"
  • Diagnostic note: Fluent answer with specific numbers → bull case (volume + price momentum intact); vague directional answer → false-friend (headline stabilisation masking mix deterioration); quantified ARPU compression → bear case (price war)

Q2 — B2B contract renewal pipeline

  • Question: "What percentage of your B2B enterprise contract value renews in the next 18 months, and at what estimated pricing delta?"
  • Expected answer: Management acknowledges a moderate renewal pipeline, describes pricing as "broadly stable" with some competitive pressure at mid-market
  • Good answer: Specific TCV at renewal, explicit pricing delta assumption, named retention rate on top-20 accounts
  • Bad answer: No TCV figure, "we're confident in retention," competitive pressure dismissed without data
  • Follow-up probe: "Which verticals — public sector, financial services, manufacturing — are showing the most pricing compression at renewal?"
  • Diagnostic note: Detailed renewal schedule → management has visibility and it's genuinely manageable; vague confidence → renewal risk is live and undisclosed; sector-specific compression cited → bear case on B2B revenue quality

Q3 — Wholesale revenue post-NetCo

  • Question: "What's the contracted wholesale revenue flowing to ServCo from the NetCo entity, and what's the price-review mechanism and timing?"
  • Expected answer: Management describes a multi-year wholesale agreement with a defined floor and a periodic price-review linked to a regulatory benchmark
  • Good answer: Specific annual € volume, price-review interval named, floor protection quantified, no pending disputes
  • Bad answer: "We're working through the details," pending regulatory determination, no floor cited
  • Follow-up probe: "If AGCOM sets the wholesale reference price 10% below your current intra-group rate, what's the EBITDA impact on ServCo?"
  • Diagnostic note: Contractual certainty → revenue floor under ServCo; price-review ambiguity → wholesale pricing is the false-friend risk hiding in plain sight; explicit sensitivity quantified → management is running this scenario, which is both reassuring and concerning

Q4 — Volume vs price decomposition

  • Question: "In your last full-year consumer revenue, what share of growth came from volume (subscriber adds) versus price (ARPU increase)?"
  • Expected answer: Mix answer — modest subscriber losses offset partially by ARPU improvement from broadband-to-fibre migration
  • Good answer: Explicit % split, ARPU trend positive, pricing power narrative supported by mix shift data
  • Bad answer: Cannot decompose, "revenue was broadly stable," ARPU cited without volume context
  • Follow-up probe: "Is the ARPU improvement durable, or is it a one-time migration effect that normalises once the copper base is exhausted?"
  • Diagnostic note: Price-led growth with ARPU expansion → pricing power exists; volume-led with flat ARPU → growth is subscriber-acquisition dependent; ARPU improvement that exhausts with copper migration → false-friend growth story

Sceptical bullet: A fluent answer on consumer broadband metrics may indicate a well-rehearsed IR deck rather than genuine granular visibility — probe whether the numbers cited are management accounts or extrapolated from subscriber counts, and test whether they match what AGCOM publishes independently

4. THEME §2 — Margin Trajectory + Levers

J-front verdict: Margin trajectory is the crux question for 18-24M EPS — ServCo EBITDA margin post-separation is underdetermined in public filings; opex structure, labour costs, and wholesale cost allocation are the three live variables

Q5 — ServCo standalone EBITDA margin bridge

  • Question: "What's your ServCo EBITDA margin today, and what's the specific bridge to your medium-term margin target — line by line?"
  • Expected answer: Management describes a starting margin in the 25-30% range with a 3-5pp improvement driven by cost reduction, IT modernisation, and labour restructuring
  • Good answer: Quantified bridge (€m per lever), timeline per lever, risks to each lever acknowledged
  • Bad answer: "We're targeting improvement," directional only, no timeline, "we'll provide more detail at the CMD"
  • Follow-up probe: "What's the single largest risk to the margin bridge — which lever has the most execution uncertainty?"
  • Diagnostic note: Quantified bridge with acknowledged risks → management has done the work; directional only → the margin target is aspiration, not plan; single risk acknowledged honestly → credibility signal

Q6 — Labour cost and headcount restructuring

  • Question: "What is the total FTE reduction embedded in your cost plan, and what is the per-head cost and timing of the severance program?"
  • Expected answer: Management describes a multi-year headcount reduction program with defined annual cohorts and per-FTE severance costs drawn from existing Italian labour agreements
  • Good answer: Total FTE target named, annual cohort structure, € per head, P&L timing (restructuring charge vs cash flow lag), net run-rate saving quantified
  • Bad answer: "We're in discussions with unions," no FTE target, "we'll update at H1 results"
  • Follow-up probe: "Is the restructuring program fully provisioned on the balance sheet today, and what's the remaining cash outflow timeline?"
  • Diagnostic note: Fully provisioned with defined cohort → bear-case on cash drag is bounded; under-provisioned → restructuring drag extends and capex/dividend trade-off worsens; union negotiations unresolved → execution timeline is genuinely open

Q7 — IT modernisation and opex-to-capex conversion

  • Question: "How much of your IT transformation spend is being capitalised versus expensed, and is the capex/opex classification consistent year-over-year?"
  • Expected answer: Management describes a defined IT modernisation program with a mix of capitalised infrastructure and expensed software/services
  • Good answer: Explicit € split, stable classification methodology, no material year-on-year shift, external auditor sign-off cited
  • Bad answer: Classification has shifted meaningfully toward capitalisation, "consistent with peers," no explicit reconciliation offered
  • Follow-up probe: "If the capitalisation rate reverses to last year's level, what's the EBITDA impact — and can you walk through the treatment?"
  • Diagnostic note: Stable classification → EBITDA trend is clean; upward shift in capitalisation → reported EBITDA improvement is partly accounting; management deflects the classification question → this is the most important number to verify independently

Q8 — Pricing-cost spread and energy/input costs

  • Question: "What is your energy cost as a percentage of opex, and do you have pass-through or hedging mechanisms in consumer/B2B contracts?"
  • Expected answer: Energy is 5-8% of opex, partially hedged 12-18 months forward, no contractual pass-through in consumer but B2B has some indexed clauses
  • Good answer: Specific % cited, hedge book tenor named, sensitivity (€m per 10% energy price move) offered proactively
  • Bad answer: "We hedge appropriately," no % cited, no sensitivity, "it's not a material item"
  • Follow-up probe: "What happens to your EBITDA margin if energy stays at current levels for 24 months versus your planning assumption?"
  • Diagnostic note: Proactive sensitivity → management models this; no sensitivity → they don't run the scenario or won't disclose it; energy cited as immaterial → verify independently against network operator cost benchmarks

5. THEME §3 — Capital Allocation + Balance Sheet

J-front verdict: Capital allocation is the highest-consequence question post-NetCo — leverage trajectory, dividend restoration timing, and residual capex obligations are all live and the analyst's 18-24M IRR depends materially on getting this right

Q9 — Terminal leverage target and path

  • Question: "What is your net debt / EBITDA target, what is today's ratio, and what is the specific path — year by year — to close the gap?"
  • Expected answer: Management targets 2.5-3.0x net debt/EBITDA, currently above target, path driven by EBITDA growth and debt repayment from operating cash flow
  • Good answer: Explicit ratio today and target, annual EBITDA growth and FCF conversion assumptions, debt maturity schedule addressed, no refinancing risk flagged near-term
  • Bad answer: "We're committed to reducing leverage," no ratio cited, maturity schedule not addressed, "we'll update at CMD"
  • Follow-up probe: "What's the earliest year in which you expect to be within your target leverage range — and what single assumption most determines whether it's Year 2 or Year 4?"
  • Diagnostic note: Credible year-by-year path → leverage trajectory is manageable; directional commitment without numbers → leverage is the live bear-case; single assumption dominates the path → that assumption is the most-diagnostic variable to stress

Q10 — Dividend policy and FCF conversion

  • Question: "When do you expect to restore a dividend, at what FCF payout ratio, and what must be true on the balance sheet for the board to approve it?"
  • Expected answer: Management describes a conditional dividend — FCF positive on a sustained basis, leverage within target, board authorisation — targeting restoration in Year 2-3
  • Good answer: Explicit FCF threshold, leverage pre-condition, board governance process described, no equity dilution contemplated
  • Bad answer: "We're committed to returning capital," no threshold, "it depends on conditions," hybrid or dilutive instrument hinted
  • Follow-up probe: "If EBITDA comes in 10% below your plan in Year 1, does dividend restoration slide by 12 months or 24 months?"
  • Diagnostic note: Conditional but specific → dividend is a real option with bounded conditions; vague commitment → dividend is a narrative prop, not a financial plan; EBITDA sensitivity to dividend timing → FCF conversion is the binding constraint, not EBITDA growth

Q11 — M&A philosophy and residual portfolio

  • Question: "Are there remaining non-core assets for disposal, and what is the post-NetCo M&A envelope — acquisitive or purely organic?"
  • Expected answer: Management describes a primarily organic ServCo, with possible small B2B bolt-ons but no transformational M&A given leverage constraints
  • Good answer: Specific asset disposal pipeline named and valued, M&A explicitly constrained by leverage, no transformational deal contemplated, returns hurdle cited (ROIC > WACC in Year 3)
  • Bad answer: "We're always looking at opportunities," no disposal pipeline, M&A not explicitly ruled out, returns hurdle absent
  • Follow-up probe: "Is there any scenario in which you would consider a further consolidation in Italian mobile, and what's the regulatory read on that?"
  • Diagnostic note: Disposal pipeline confirmed → balance sheet cleanup is ahead; transformational M&A hinted → capital allocation discipline is weaker than it sounds; regulatory consolidation floated → this is a separate thesis driver that changes the IRR calculus

6. THEME §4 — Strategy + Competitive Position

J-front verdict: The strategic question is whether ServCo has a sustainable competitive position in a symmetrically-accessed fibre market — the bull case requires a service/brand/B2B capability moat; the bear case is regulated utility with pricing erosion

Q12 — Win condition in symmetric-access broadband

  • Question: "Once all operators access OPEN Fiber and TIM's network on equivalent wholesale terms, what is your win condition in consumer broadband — why does a customer choose TIM?"
  • Expected answer: Management articulates a service quality / brand / convergence (fixed-mobile bundle) differentiation story
  • Good answer: Specific NPS data cited, bundle attach rates quantified, brand premium vs price positioned, convergence share vs competitors named
  • Bad answer: "We have the best network," incumbency-as-moat, no NPS or bundle data, circular answer ("customers choose us because we're TIM")
  • Follow-up probe: "What is your fixed-mobile bundle penetration today versus your nearest competitor, and what's the churn differential between bundled and unbundled customers?"
  • Diagnostic note: Quantified bundle advantage → convergence moat is real; incumbency narrative without data → the moat is eroding; churn differential on bundled customers → this is the most durable competitive variable in Italian broadband

Q13 — B2B competitive positioning versus hyperscalers

  • Question: "In the enterprise segment, where are hyperscalers (AWS, Azure, Google) displacing TIM's managed services, and what is your response?"
  • Expected answer: Management acknowledges hyperscaler displacement in non-differentiated cloud/managed services but cites regulatory/data-residency requirements as a structural protection in public sector and financial services
  • Good answer: Specific verticals where TIM is winning vs losing named, data-residency / sovereign-cloud angle quantified, new B2B product pipeline described
  • Bad answer: "We partner with hyperscalers," hyperscaler displacement minimised, no vertical-specific win/loss data
  • Follow-up probe: "What percentage of your B2B revenue is in categories where you have no differentiation from a hyperscaler alternative?"
  • Diagnostic note: Honest displacement acknowledgement with defence → management is running this scenario; partnership narrative without loss data → competitive erosion is undisclosed; sovereign-cloud protection quantified → this is a genuine moat in Italian public sector B2B

Q14 — Regulatory positioning on wholesale pricing

  • Question: "What is AGCOM's current review timeline for wholesale access pricing, and what is your base-case and downside scenario for the outcome?"
  • Expected answer: Management describes an ongoing AGCOM review, base case is broadly flat or minor downward adjustment, downside is a 5-10% cut with phased implementation
  • Good answer: Specific review timeline, base case and downside quantified in € EBITDA terms, ServCo vs NetCo impact separated
  • Bad answer: "We're constructively engaged with AGCOM," no timeline, no sensitivity, outcome described as "manageable"
  • Follow-up probe: "Is the wholesale pricing outcome contractually passed through to ServCo, or does ServCo absorb it against a fixed wholesale cost?"
  • Diagnostic note: Quantified sensitivity → this risk is bounded; "manageable" without numbers → the sensitivity is material and management doesn't want to quantify it; ServCo/NetCo pass-through ambiguous → the false-friend scenario (regulatory cut absorbed at ServCo level) is live

7. THEME §5 — Management Quality + Operator Track Record

J-front verdict: Management credibility is load-bearing at this stage of the TIT-IT thesis — the ServCo rump is immature as a standalone entity and EPS trajectory depends almost entirely on execution against a plan that has limited public documentation

Q15 — Incentive structure and compensation alignment

  • Question: "What are the primary KPIs in your long-term incentive plan, and what is the weighting between EBITDA, FCF, leverage, and total shareholder return?"
  • Expected answer: Management describes a balanced LTI with EBITDA growth (40%), FCF (30%), and relative TSR (30%) with a 3-year vesting cliff
  • Good answer: Explicit KPI weights, vesting cliff named, relative TSR benchmarked against a named peer group, no single KPI that incentivises short-term EBITDA at the expense of balance sheet
  • Bad answer: Cannot articulate KPI weights, "we're paid in line with market," KPIs are all EBITDA-forward with no FCF or leverage element
  • Follow-up probe (Power of Incentives): "If you hit your EBITDA target but FCF conversion is 15% below plan, does that affect your LTI payout — and by how much?"
  • Diagnostic note: FCF in LTI → management is incentivised to convert, not just grow reported EBITDA; EBITDA-only LTI → opex capitalisation risk is structurally incentivised; TSR benchmarked vs peers → alignment with shareholder interests is genuine

Q16 — Sandbagging discipline and guidance track record

  • Question: "Looking back at your last three annual guidance sets, how often did you beat, meet, or miss — and what was the single most-frequent miss driver?"
  • Expected answer: Management acknowledges a period of significant misses during network separation but describes a reset guidance framework with more conservative assumptions
  • Good answer: Specific beat/miss history cited, dominant miss driver named (e.g. B2B revenue, labour costs), explicit methodology change described
  • Bad answer: "We've been through a period of transformation, which is behind us now," no specific miss analysis, guidance methodology not described
  • Follow-up probe: "What has changed in your planning methodology that gives you confidence the current guidance is achievable — not aspirational?"
  • Diagnostic note: Honest miss history with named driver → management has done a post-mortem; transformation excuse without specifics → sandbagging discipline is weak; methodology change described → credibility is incrementally higher, but test against the actual guidance range

Q17 — Crisis playbook and decision speed

  • Question: "In the last 24 months, what was the most significant operational or financial decision you had to accelerate — and what did the decision process look like?"
  • Expected answer: Management describes the NetCo separation governance — a complex decision with board, regulatory, and financial workstreams managed simultaneously
  • Good answer: Specific decision cited, timeline compressed, trade-off between stakeholders named, outcome assessed against original objective
  • Bad answer: Vague reference to "transformation," no specific decision, governance process described at high level only
  • Follow-up probe: "What would you do differently in hindsight — and has that changed how you make capital decisions today?"
  • Diagnostic note: Specific decision with honest hindsight → growth mindset and operational self-awareness; vague transformation narrative → management is in IR mode, not operational mode; "nothing differently" answer → credibility red flag

8. THEME §6 — Risk + Downside (Pre-Mortem Cross-Ref)

J-front verdict: Three live failure modes dominate — wholesale pricing reset, B2B revenue quality deterioration, and restructuring underprovision; all three are underdisclosed in public filings and a management meeting is the primary data-gathering opportunity

Q18 — Bear-case probe: wholesale pricing reset

  • Question: "If AGCOM resets wholesale pricing 15% below current levels in the next 18 months, walk me through the direct EBITDA impact on ServCo — step by step."
  • Expected answer: Management provides a quantified sensitivity, likely €150-250m EBITDA impact at 15% cut, with partial mitigation from retail price pass-through and cost reduction
  • Good answer: Specific € sensitivity, mitigation levers named and quantified, timeline for pass-through to retail pricing described
  • Bad answer: "We don't expect that outcome," no sensitivity, mitigation described as "we would take actions," retail pass-through assumed without evidence
  • Follow-up probe: "Do you have contractual ability to raise retail prices in response to a wholesale price decision, or is retail pricing constrained by competitive dynamics?"
  • Diagnostic note: Quantified sensitivity with mitigation → this scenario is managed; refusal to quantify → EBITDA sensitivity is material and management knows it; retail pass-through assumed without evidence → the bear case on ServCo EBITDA is wider than the bull-case deck implies

Q19 — False-friend probe: B2B customer stickiness

  • Question: "⚡ Of your top-20 B2B customers by revenue, how many have multi-year contracts expiring in the next 24 months — and what is the average tenure of those relationships?"
  • Expected answer: Management describes a mix of long-tenured public-sector relationships and more competitively-exposed mid-market contracts
  • Good answer: Specific contract expiry schedule, average tenure cited, competitive RFP activity quantified, win-rate on renewals named
  • Bad answer: "Our B2B relationships are long-standing," no expiry data, no RFP win-rate, "we're confident in retention"
  • Follow-up probe: "What was your B2B contract renewal win-rate in the last 12 months, and how does that compare to two years ago?"
  • Diagnostic note: Declining win-rate on renewals → B2B revenue quality is deteriorating faster than reported; stable win-rate with specific data → B2B revenue is sticky; refusal to name expiry schedule → concentration risk is higher than the filings imply

Q20 — Failure-mode test: restructuring underprovision

  • Question: "Is the current restructuring provision fully funded for all committed headcount reductions, or does additional provisioning remain ahead?"
  • Expected answer: Management describes the restructuring provision as substantially complete for the committed program, with possible incremental charges if additional FTE reductions are identified
  • Good answer: Specific € provision balance, committed vs uncommitted FTE reduction separated, no material incremental charge expected in the next 12 months
  • Bad answer: "We review our provisions regularly," no balance cited, incremental charges not ruled out, "it's part of the normal operating process"
  • Follow-up probe: "If you accelerate the restructuring program by 12 months to capture opex savings earlier, what's the incremental cash outflow and what does that do to FCF in Year 1?"
  • Diagnostic note: Fully provisioned → downside on restructuring charge is bounded; incremental provisioning likely → FCF in Year 1 is weaker than the bull case assumes; acceleration possible → management has optionality on cost savings but cash drag is front-loaded

Sceptical bullet: Q19 ACH matrix items NOT covered by any question in this memo: (1) Italian macroeconomic deterioration and its impact on consumer ARPU and B2B contract volume — not directly probed; (2) KKR / minority NetCo governance conflict and its potential to create regulatory litigation risk back to ServCo — only touched indirectly in Q14; these two items should be added if meeting time allows

9. §8 — CROSS-CUTTING META + NON-OBVIOUS QUESTIONS

J-front verdict: The most diagnostic questions are the ones management will instinctively deflect — wholesale pricing sensitivity, B2B renewal win-rate, and LTI structure are the three most-likely deflection targets and therefore the most-valuable to push on

Non-obvious angle (WH Smith probe): "What single internal metric, if it moved against you by 10%, would cause you to personally re-examine the plan — and what is that metric doing right now?"

  • Why unconventional: Forces management to reveal their private leading indicator — not what they disclose externally but what they actually watch
  • Good answer: Specific metric named (e.g. "B2B contract renewal win-rate in the public sector" or "monthly FTTH net adds vs plan"), current trend disclosed honestly
  • Bad answer: "We watch many metrics," generic, no current trend disclosed
  • Diagnostic note: The metric they name is more diagnostic than any metric in the filings

Cross-roads question: "If you could re-run the NetCo separation with full hindsight, would you structure ServCo's wholesale dependency differently — and what would you change?"

  • Why load-bearing: Invites honest post-mortem on the single most-consequential strategic decision; management's answer reveals whether they believe ServCo's cost structure is right or locked
  • Good answer: Specific structural change identified, mitigation in place, honest acknowledgement of the dependency risk
  • Bad answer: "The separation was the right decision and we're very happy with the outcome," no nuance

⚡ Outlier probe — incentive misalignment at the board level: "Does any member of the supervisory board hold debt instruments or credit instruments in Telecom Italia — and if so, is there a conflict committee process for capital structure decisions?"

  • Why non-obvious: Telecom Italia's debt overhang and KKR involvement create potential creditor-vs-equity alignment issues at the governance level; this question surfaces a structural agency risk that is almost never asked in consensus sell-side meetings
  • Diagnostic note: Conflict committee exists and is active → governance is functioning; no conflict committee → capital structure decisions may be influenced by creditor interests that are adverse to equity IRR

Multiple-truths probe (Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 3): "I want to acknowledge that the ServCo restructuring thesis is genuinely compelling AND that the regulatory dependency creates a tail risk the bull case understates — where do you think the market is getting it most wrong in BOTH directions?"

  • Why load-bearing: Frames the question to prevent either-or deflection; management must engage with both the bull and bear simultaneously
  • Good answer: Identifies a specific market misconception in each direction (e.g. "market overestimates wholesale pricing risk but underestimates B2B contract renewal risk")
  • Bad answer: Pivots to bull-case only, doesn't engage with the "AND" framing

Edge-detection probe: "In the last six months, what have you learned from a competitor's actions — DAZN partnership, Vodafone Italy, or WIND-Tre — that surprised you and changed something you're doing?"

  • Diagnostic note: Competitive learning signal — a management team that is genuinely tracking competitor moves in real time vs one that is heads-down on internal transformation; silence or vagueness → insularity risk

Stewing trigger: "If the equity story here requires 25%+ IRR over 18-24 months, what is the single thing that must go right — and what is the probability you would attach to it going right?"

  • Why load-bearing: Forces management to rank-order their own thesis; the item they name is the most diagnostic variable; the probability they attach is a calibration signal for sandbagging vs over-confidence

Incentive probe (Concept A, continued from §6): "Your LTI vests in Year 3 — if the company is acquired before vesting, does your LTI accelerate in full? And does that affect your openness to M&A conversations?"

  • Why uncomfortable: Acceleration clauses are a documented source of management M&A bias; asking this surfaces whether management's stated opposition to transformational M&A is genuine or contingent on their incentive structure

Diagnostic crystalliser (most-likely management deflection pattern): Management will likely deflect on: (1) wholesale pricing sensitivity — "we're constructively engaged with AGCOM"; (2) B2B contract expiry schedule — "relationships are long-standing"; (3) LTI structure — "we're paid in line with market." Each deflection is itself diagnostic — treat non-answer as a bear signal on that specific item.

Sceptical bullet: Questions NOT included because they feel uncomfortable — (1) "Is KKR's NetCo stake creating governance pressure on ServCo's wholesale cost — and have you had any conversation with KKR about the wholesale pricing schedule?" (direct — but surfaces the most structurally important conflict); (2) "What happens to your role and this management team if the equity story doesn't work in 24 months — who owns the strategic review?" (succession / accountability probe). The obstacle is the way — both questions should be asked if the meeting format allows it.

10. AGGREGATE WEAK SIGNALS / DOWNSIDE (❌)

  • ❌ Wholesale pricing risk is structurally underdisclosed — ServCo's EBITDA sensitivity to AGCOM decisions has not been quantified in any public filing reviewed; this is a live downside vector with no public upper bound
  • ❌ B2B revenue quality is the least-interrogated component of the ServCo thesis; contract renewal cadence and win-rate are not publicly disclosed and management may have incentive to obscure deteriorating trends
  • ❌ Restructuring provision adequacy is uncertain — Italian labour law restructuring programs are notoriously long-tailed; if additional FTE reductions are required beyond the current provision, Year 1-2 FCF is weaker than the bull case implies
  • ❌ NetCo / ServCo intra-group pricing governance is a structural conflict — KKR as a NetCo stakeholder has interests that may not align with ServCo equity value maximisation; this conflict has limited public documentation
  • ❌ IT capitalisation rate — if the year-on-year shift in opex/capex classification is material, reported EBITDA improvement is partially accounting-driven; this is a standard European telecom risk but requires explicit verification here
  • ❌ No cohort or sector peer data available — all downside assessments are based on TIT-IT's own facts; peer-anchored downside probability calibration is unavailable for this run (D-BATCH-25 gap)

11. AGGREGATE OUTLIERS (⚡)

  • ⚡ Q19 — B2B top-20 contract expiry schedule: No consensus sell-side question has ever publicly surfaced the specific contract expiry and renewal win-rate for TIT-IT's top B2B accounts; this is the highest-information question in the set and the most likely to produce a genuinely diagnostic non-answer
  • ⚡ Governance / debt-holder conflict probe (§8): Board-level debt instrument holdings and conflict committee existence for capital structure decisions is a structurally important but consensus-invisible question; the KKR/NetCo dynamic makes this unusually live at TIT-IT specifically
  • ⚡ LTI acceleration clause and M&A openness probe (§8): Asking whether LTI accelerates on acquisition and whether that biases management toward M&A conversations is almost never asked in European telecom management meetings; the answer would be uniquely diagnostic for a company that has been cited repeatedly in M&A speculation
