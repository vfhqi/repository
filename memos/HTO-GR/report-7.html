§1 — Executive Summary

HTO-GR's driver landscape is a recognisable "infrastructure-led compounding" setup common to mid-cap European incumbents post-network investment cycle. The fulcrum is fibre monetisation, and the capital return programme provides a durable total-return floor. [#J]

  • Driver list: Six drivers identified: (1) Fibre/broadband ARPU uplift, (2) Capital return (dividend + buyback), (3) Cost restructuring & margin expansion, (4) Romanian segment stabilisation, (5) Enterprise/B2B revenue growth, (6) Macro/regulatory environment [#J]

  • Impact ranking: Driver 1 carries the highest EPS leverage; Drivers 2-3 are compounding support; Drivers 4-5 are swing factors; Driver 6 is an enabler/constraint [#A]

§2 — Key Driver #1: Fibre/Broadband ARPU Uplift

Primary fulcrum — fibre monetisation is the clearest 24M earnings-growth mechanism, but ARPU migration pace is the unresolved critical variable [#J]

  • Driver definition: OTE has invested heavily in FTTH/VDSL network rollout across Greece; the revenue payoff requires customers to (a) migrate from copper/ADSL to fibre, and (b) upsell to higher-speed/bundled tiers at materially higher ARPU [#J]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Higher broadband ARPU × growing fibre subscriber base = service revenue growth; operating leverage on fixed network infrastructure means incremental revenue is high-margin, flowing through to EBITDA and EPS disproportionately [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Sustained ARPU growth signals successful infrastructure-cycle monetisation → re-rates EV/EBITDA toward Western European fibre-deployer peer multiples (~7-8× vs OTE's current ~5.5-6×) [#A]

  • Company guide: OTE management has guided for continued broadband revenue growth mid-single-digit percentages; fibre homes-passed target exceeding 3 million by 2025-26; subscriber mix shift toward fibre framed as multi-year structural trend [#J]

  • Management commentary: CEO/CFO commentary in FY24-25 results calls consistently emphasises fibre as the "growth engine," with specific reference to ARPU accretion as customers migrate to 100Mbps+ tiers; management has been careful NOT to give explicit numerical ARPU guidance — a mild yellow flag [#A]

  • Track record: OTE has delivered consistent broadband revenue growth in the 3-5% range over the prior 3-year period; fibre network buildout has been on-schedule; management has not missed fibre rollout milestones publicly [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Guidance on fibre ARPU uplift is deliberately vague — management tends to guide at the "service revenue" line rather than per-product ARPU; this creates upside optionality but also obscures the key driver [#J]

  • SS consensus: Public broker notes (Berenberg, JPMorgan) frame broadband as the core growth driver; Citi has highlighted the ARPU migration risk more explicitly; Barclays and HSBC broadly constructive but note Greek market maturation risk [#A]

  • Bullish analyst: Berenberg and JPMorgan frame OTE's fibre story as underappreciated relative to Western European peers — arguing Greek ARPU levels have structural catch-up potential as household incomes rise [#A]

  • Sceptical analyst: Citi has flagged that fibre penetration rates in Greece lag northern European markets and that competitive dynamics (Nova/Wind Hellas) could compress pricing during the migration phase [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: If broadband ARPU grows 4-6% annually over 24M (vs ~2-3% implied by consensus), EPS uplift is approximately 8-12%; if ARPU is flat or declining, EPS growth from this driver is negligible [#A]

  • Recent delivery: FY24 results showed Greece fixed-line broadband revenues up approximately 4-5% YoY; subscriber base growing modestly; ARPU data not cleanly disclosed — inferring from revenue / subscriber count [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: Greek broadband revenue CAGR approximately 4% over 2021-2024; fibre subscriber growth accelerating but from a low base [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: Western European fibre incumbents (BT, Deutsche Telekom, Orange) have demonstrated ARPU uplifts of 10-20% for customers migrating from copper to FTTH; OTE's implied uplift is lower, suggesting either more conservative pricing or weaker demand elasticity [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: Greek GDP growth 2.3% in 2024, household consumption recovering post-crisis decade; remote working and digitisation trends support demand for higher-speed broadband [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Greek household disposable income still ~20-25% below EU average — willingness to pay premium for faster tiers is constrained relative to Northern European comparators [#A]

  • Competitive dynamics: Nova (formerly Wind/HOL) and Vodafone Greece are also deploying fibre; price competition during the adoption phase is structurally likely; OTE's scale advantage provides some insulation [#A]

  • Visibility: Medium — fibre rollout is within management's control, but ARPU migration pace depends on consumer demand and competitive pricing [#J]

  • Bear case: Consumers take entry-tier 100Mbps fibre at prices only marginally above legacy ADSL; subscriber growth is strong but ARPU is flat or dilutive; OTE has built the network but cannot monetise it at the ARPU level required for the investment-case return [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Broadband revenue growth stays at 2-3% (consensus floor) rather than accelerating to 5-6%; 24M EPS impact is ~4-6% below the bullish case [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: If this driver misses, EPS is ~5-8% below the bull case over 24M; the re-rating thesis also stalls — multiple stays at current discount rather than compressing toward peers [#J]

  • Red flags: Absence of explicit ARPU disclosure in OTE's reporting is a mild red flag — management controls the narrative; if ARPU were clearly accelerating, disclosure incentive would be higher [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — fibre rollout is on track but ARPU migration pace is unproven at scale; delivery depends on Greek consumer willingness-to-pay exceeding current implied pricing [#J]

§3 — Key Driver #2: Capital Return (Dividend + Buyback)

High-conviction floor driver — dividend yield ~6-7% and active buyback programme provide durable total return base independent of revenue growth [#J]

  • Driver definition: OTE operates a progressive dividend policy with supplementary share buyback; capital return is both a direct shareholder return mechanism and a signal of management's confidence in free cash flow durability [#J]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Buyback reduces share count → accretive to EPS on a per-share basis; dividend does not directly affect EPS but signals FCF durability [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: High yield creates a floor on valuation multiple compression; buyback reduces float and supports technical price floor; combined yield + buyback represents total capital return of ~8-10% of market cap annually [#A]

  • Company guide: Management has consistently guided for dividend maintenance/growth; FY25 dividend guidance confirmed as progressive; buyback programme authorised and active [#J]

  • Management commentary: CFO commentary in recent results calls explicitly frames capital return as a "pillar" of the shareholder value proposition; DT's ownership stake (~48-49%) aligns incentives toward sustained dividend (DT relies on OTE dividends as upstream cash flow) [#A]

  • Track record: OTE has maintained or grown its dividend every year since 2019 restructuring completion; buyback programmes delivered as authorised; no capital return cuts during COVID period [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Capital return guidance is among the most conservative and reliable components of OTE's guidance suite; DT alignment and board composition create structural commitment [#A]

  • SS consensus: Unanimously cited by covering brokers as a core investment merit; Berenberg, JPMorgan, HSBC, Citi all highlight the yield as a key support to valuation [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Buyback running ~1-2% annual share count reduction; over 24M, approximately 2-4% EPS accretion from buyback alone [#A]

  • Estimated total return contribution: Dividend yield ~6-7% × 2 years = ~12-14% total return floor from income alone, independent of capital appreciation [#A]

  • Recent delivery: FY24 dividend confirmed; buyback executed as guided; no shortfall vs guidance on capital return in L3Y [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: OTE's ~6-7% dividend yield is above the Eurostoxx Telecoms sector median (~4-5%); buyback yield of ~1-2% is in line with sector; combined capital return yield is at the high end of European telecoms [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: Low-rate exit environment (ECB tightening cycle complete or near-complete) increases the relative attractiveness of high-yield equity vs fixed income; OTE's yield spread over Bund has widened attractively [#A]

  • Macro headwind: If FCF deteriorates (capex overrun on fibre, or revenue miss), dividend sustainability could be questioned; this is a tail risk given current FCF coverage [#A]

  • Visibility: High — capital return is the most visible and committed driver in OTE's guidance suite [#J]

  • Bear case: FCF deteriorates due to fibre capex overrun or revenue weakness; dividend sustainability becomes a question; DT strategic portfolio review triggers forced secondary, depressing OTE's price and potentially disrupting buyback [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Dividend is maintained but not grown; buyback is reduced due to FCF pressure; total capital return yield drops from ~8-10% to ~5-6%; still above sector median but below current expectation [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: Capital return miss is primarily a valuation de-rating event rather than an EPS miss; ~10-15% multiple compression if dividend is cut or buyback cancelled [#J]

  • DT overhang interaction: DT's ownership is a structural support for dividend (DT needs the upstream cash) AND a structural risk (secondary placement would depress price and reduce DT's commitment to OTE independence) [#J]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — structural DT alignment, strong FCF coverage, and consistent track record make capital return the most durable driver in the case [#J]

§4 — Key Driver #3: Cost Restructuring and Margin Expansion

Incrementally supportive — multi-year cost reduction programme has delivered well but easy wins are largely captured; incremental savings are real but diminishing [#J]

  • Driver definition: OTE has executed a sustained cost reduction programme since ~2013-2015, including workforce restructuring, network consolidation, and procurement optimisation; the question is whether further meaningful margin expansion is achievable [#J]

  • Mechanism to EPS: OpEx reduction increases EBITDA margin → flows through to EBIT → EPS; operating leverage amplifies revenue growth into EPS [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: EBITDA margin expansion signals operational efficiency and re-rates the EV/EBITDA multiple upward [#A]

  • Company guide: Management targets "stable to improving" EBITDA margins; specific guidance on cost savings is given in terms of FTEs reduced and IT consolidation milestones rather than hard margin targets [#J]

  • Management commentary: Recent results calls note ongoing digital transformation (self-service platforms, AI-assisted customer service) as cost levers; language has shifted from "restructuring" to "efficiency" — signalling the heavy-lift phase is largely complete [#A]

  • Track record: OTE's EBITDA margin has improved from mid-30s% to approximately 42-44% over 2015-2024 — a substantial multi-year achievement; trajectory is genuine [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Management is not guiding for dramatic incremental improvement; incremental margin guidance is modest (50-100bps/year) — appropriately calibrated given where margins already are [#A]

  • SS consensus: Brokers broadly agree the restructuring phase is largely complete; incremental savings from digital transformation are real but smaller magnitude than prior workforce restructuring cycles [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: If EBITDA margins expand 100-150bps over 24M, EPS uplift is approximately 2-4%; below the magnitude of prior restructuring cycles [#A]

  • Recent delivery: FY24 EBITDA margins reported in the 41-43% range for Greek operations; broadly stable YoY with modest improvement; consistent with "efficiency" phase vs "restructuring" phase [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: EBITDA margins have plateaued in the low-40s% range since approximately 2021-22; incremental improvement is real but the gradient has flattened [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: OTE's ~42-44% EBITDA margin is broadly in line with mid-tier European telco incumbents; above some Southern European peers (Telecom Italia ~35%); below Northern European leaders (Swisscom ~40%+ but different scale) [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Greek wage inflation running ~5-7% in 2023-24 — partially offsets efficiency gains; energy cost volatility is a second-order input cost pressure [#A]

  • Competitive dynamics: Network sharing agreements and passive infrastructure co-investment (common in European telecoms) could provide additional cost benefits; OTE has explored tower monetisation [#A]

  • Visibility: Medium-high — cost programme is largely within management's control; wage inflation is the primary external variable [#J]

  • Bear case: Wage inflation and regulatory compliance costs (cybersecurity, data, universal service) offset incremental efficiency gains; EBITDA margin is flat or slightly declining rather than improving; EPS impact from this driver is effectively zero [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Margins flat at ~42% rather than improving to ~43-44%; EPS neutral rather than slightly accretive from this driver [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: If this driver delivers zero vs expectation, ~2-3% EPS miss vs bull case over 24M — manageable, not case-breaking [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — directionally correct but magnitude is limited; management has earned credibility on cost discipline, but the incremental opportunity is smaller than the prior cycle [#J]

§5 — Key Driver #4: Romanian Segment Stabilisation (Telekom Romania)

Swing factor with asymmetric downside bias — Romanian operations have been a persistent drag; stabilisation is a genuine upside scenario but execution track record is mixed [#J]

  • Driver definition: OTE owns 54% of Telekom Romania; the segment has faced structural challenges (market share loss, pricing pressure, capex requirements) and has historically dragged on group EBITDA and free cash flow [#J]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Romania contributes approximately 15-20% of group revenue but a smaller share of EBITDA due to lower margins; stabilisation/improvement flows through to group EBITDA at modest magnitude [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Romania has acted as a discount-to-peers valuation factor — investors applying a conglomerate discount to OTE partly due to Romanian uncertainty; resolution/stabilisation would be a de-rating catalyst for the discount [#J]

  • Company guide: Management has guided for "continued improvement" in Romania but specific guidance is deliberately cautious; no hard EBITDA turnaround timeline has been provided publicly [#J]

  • Management commentary: Recent CMD/results commentary frames Romania as a "self-funding transformation" — implying management does not expect to inject significant additional capital; tone is cautiously optimistic but not bullish [#A]

  • Track record: Romanian segment has missed improvement milestones repeatedly over 2019-2023; competitive environment (Digi RCS&RDS) remains structurally challenging; OTE has explored partial/full disposal without consummating a transaction [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Management guidance on Romania errs toward optimism relative to demonstrated delivery; this is a segment where sandbagging has been the pattern [#J]

  • SS consensus: Most brokers apply a partial-to-full write-down of Romania's contribution in SOTP analyses; the segment is broadly valued at minimal or negative enterprise value in some broker models [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact (bull): If Romania reaches EBITDA breakeven or low-single-digit margin improvement, group EPS benefit is ~3-5% [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact (bear): If Romania requires capital injection or is disposed of at below-book value, EPS headwind is ~2-4%; disposal at a discount to carrying value is a one-time charge risk [#A]

  • Recent delivery: FY24 Romania reported modest improvement in operational metrics (subscriber stabilisation, ARPU support); not a clear inflection — improvement is marginal rather than structural [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: Romania EBITDA has been in the €150-200m range annually (estimate), trending flat to slightly down; margin recovery has been elusive [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: Digi RCS&RDS (the primary Romanian competitor) is structurally lower-cost and has taken consistent market share; OTE's Romanian legacy infrastructure is at a cost disadvantage [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: Romanian GDP growth has been solid (~2-3% in 2024); consumer broadband demand is healthy; enterprise/B2B has growth potential as Romanian economy digitises [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Digi's competitive position is entrenched; mobile market is highly competitive; structural cost disadvantage of legacy copper network in Romania is not easily remediated [#A]

  • Competitive dynamics: Digi + Orange Romania + Vodafone Romania = three well-capitalised competitors; OTE is #4 or lower in some segments; market position is structurally weak [#A]

  • Visibility: Low — Romanian segment performance is among the least visible and most externally dependent in OTE's portfolio [#J]

  • Disposal optionality: A full disposal of Telekom Romania at any price above zero would remove the discount and be EPS-accretive to the extent dividend repatriation to Greek OpCo exceeds current Romania profit contribution [#J]

  • Bear case: Romania requires additional capital investment to remain competitive; EBITDA stays depressed; OTE is forced to impair the asset; investor re-rating toward a pure Greek telco story is delayed [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Romania EBITDA margin stays flat at current levels; no disposal progress; conglomerate discount on OTE persists at ~10-15% vs pure-play Greek telco implied valuation [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: If Romania actively deteriorates (EBITDA -10-15% vs current), ~2-4% group EPS headwind; more importantly, the valuation discount widens further [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Low-medium plausibility — improvement is possible but structural competitive challenges and track record of missed milestones warrant scepticism; disposal at acceptable valuation remains the most plausible positive resolution [#J]

§6 — Key Driver #5: Enterprise / B2B Revenue Growth

Medium-conviction supporting driver — ICT/cloud/managed services represent the clearest incremental growth vector in B2B, but scale relative to group revenue limits EPS impact [#J]

  • Driver definition: OTE's enterprise/B2B segment includes traditional fixed/mobile connectivity for business customers plus growing ICT services (cloud, managed services, cybersecurity, IoT); the driver thesis is that higher-margin ICT/cloud products displace lower-margin legacy connectivity [#J]

  • Mechanism to EPS: ICT revenue grows at higher rates than legacy B2B connectivity; mix shift toward higher-margin products improves blended B2B EBITDA margin; B2B is approximately 25-30% of Greek service revenue [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: B2B growth validates OTE's strategic positioning beyond a pure infrastructure/consumer story; enterprise segment re-rating is more modest than consumer fibre but additive [#A]

  • Company guide: Management has highlighted B2B digital transformation as a "growth pillar"; guidance is directional rather than quantified — mid-single-digit B2B revenue growth target [#J]

  • Management commentary: CMD presentations emphasise OTE as a "digital infrastructure and services" company rather than a legacy telco; B2B ICT is cited as the segment with the highest growth potential [#A]

  • Track record: B2B revenue growth has been positive but modest (~3-5% CAGR over 2020-2024); ICT/cloud is growing faster within the mix but from a small base [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Management's B2B narrative is constructive but the numbers delivered have been at the lower end of the 3-5% range; not dramatically missing guidance but not beating it either [#A]

  • SS consensus: Brokers treat B2B as a "nice-to-have" rather than a load-bearing driver; Berenberg and JPMorgan flag it as an upside optionality item rather than a base-case assumption [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: If B2B revenue grows 5-7% annually (vs 3-5% baseline) over 24M, incremental EPS benefit is approximately 2-4% [#A]

  • Recent delivery: B2B segment showing gradual improvement; specific ICT revenue disclosure is limited; public data points to Greek enterprise sector digitisation spending increasing [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: European telco ICT segments (Orange Business, Deutsche Telekom T-Systems) have faced headwinds from hyperscaler competition; OTE's Greek market position is stronger due to limited hyperscaler direct presence in SME segment [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: Greek enterprise digitalisation is structurally underpenetrated vs EU average; EU structural funds channelled through Greek enterprise sector support IT investment demand [#A]

  • Competitive dynamics: IBM, SAP, and local IT services firms compete in Greek ICT market; OTE's connectivity bundling provides a structural advantage for integrated offerings [#A]

  • Visibility: Medium — B2B growth is partially within management's control but depends on enterprise spending cycles and competitive pricing [#J]

  • Bear case: Legacy B2B connectivity revenue declines faster than ICT/cloud can compensate; net B2B revenue growth is 1-2% rather than 4-6%; EPS contribution is minimal [#J]

  • Miss scenario: B2B stays at historical 3% growth trajectory; ICT fails to accelerate meaningfully; EPS contribution is ~1-2% vs bull case ~3-4% [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: B2B miss is not case-breaking — the segment is important but not the primary EPS driver; ~1-2% EPS miss vs expectation if this driver underperforms [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — structural tailwinds exist in Greek enterprise digitisation, but scale limitations and historical delivery at the low end of guidance range constrain the upside case [#J]

§7 — Key Driver #6: Macro and Regulatory Environment

Enabler/constraint driver — Greek macro recovery is genuinely supportive; regulatory risk is the primary external threat that is not within management's control [#J]

  • Driver definition: The macro and regulatory environment is not a standalone EPS driver but conditions the achievability of Drivers 1-5; Greek GDP growth, household income trends, and EETT regulatory decisions on wholesale access and pricing are the key variables [#J]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Positive macro → consumer willingness to pay higher broadband ARPU → supports Driver 1; negative regulatory decision → forced price reduction or network sharing → compresses margins → impacts Drivers 1 and 3 [#A]

  • Company guide: Management does not guide explicitly on macro; regulatory submissions are non-public; management frames regulation as "constructive" in public communication [#A]

  • Recent macro context: Greece GDP growth approximately 2.3% in 2024, outperforming EU average; unemployment declining from crisis-era peaks; household consumption recovering [#A]

  • Regulatory context: EETT (Hellenic Telecommunications and Post Commission) has historically intervened on OTE's wholesale access pricing; current regulatory cycle is supportive of fibre investment incentives but this can change [#J]

  • Macro tailwind: Greece is in a multi-year recovery cycle post-financial crisis; consumer confidence improving; tourism economy driving broadband demand in hospitality sector [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Greek household disposable income is ~20-25% below EU average; limits ARPU expansion pace; sovereign debt overhang (though dramatically reduced from 2015 peak) creates latent macro risk [#A]

  • Regulatory headwind: EU Digital Markets Act and telecom regulatory framework evolution could impose open-access requirements that limit OTE's pricing power on fibre network [#A]

  • EU structural funds: Greece is a significant beneficiary of EU cohesion and recovery funds (~€30bn+ from NRRP); enterprise and public sector digital investment is partly EU-funded — supportive of B2B growth [#A]

  • Interest rate context: ECB cutting cycle in 2024-25 is modestly supportive of consumer credit and business investment; OTE's debt structure benefits modestly from lower rates [#A]

  • Visibility: Low — macro is external; regulatory decisions are partly predictable but have surprised to the downside historically [#J]

  • Bear case: EETT mandates lower wholesale access prices on OTE's fibre network — reducing the revenue uplift from fibre investment and potentially triggering a competitor free-ride scenario; this is a tail risk but historically has occurred in European telecoms markets [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Macro growth slows to 1-1.5% (EU-wide slowdown); consumer broadband ARPU pressure intensifies; combined effect suppresses revenue growth to 1-2% range [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: If macro deteriorates and regulation tightens simultaneously, the combined effect on OTE's revenue growth could be ~3-5% below current consensus over 24M [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility as an enabler — Greek macro recovery is structurally credible, but regulatory risk is a genuine and not fully-priced threat; management cannot control this driver [#J]

§10 — Driver Synthesis + Sceptical Synthesis

Bearish synthesis: The OTE investment case rests on three simultaneous deliveries (fibre ARPU migration, cost discipline, capital return); if the primary fulcrum (fibre ARPU) stalls and regulatory headwinds emerge, the entire thesis deflates despite robust capital return [#J]

  • Driver dependency: Drivers 1 and 3 are positively correlated — fibre ARPU growth drives revenue operating leverage which amplifies margin expansion; if Driver 1 misses, Driver 3's contribution is also compressed [#J]

  • Driver correlation — adverse scenario: A Greek macro slowdown (Driver 6 negative) would simultaneously suppress fibre ARPU migration (Driver 1), B2B growth (Driver 5), and potentially challenge dividend sustainability (Driver 2) — three drivers impacted by a single negative macro event [#J]

  • Correlation across drivers: Drivers 1, 3, and 5 are moderately positively correlated on the upside AND on the downside; Driver 2 (capital return) is partially insulated but not fully independent of FCF generation [#J]

  • Cumulative plausibility: If Drivers 1-3 each have 60-70% independent probability of delivery, joint probability of all three delivering is approximately 22-34%; the 25%+ IRR case requires all three to deliver — joint probability of the full bull case is uncomfortably low [#J]

  • Setup archetype: Pattern-matches "infrastructure-cycle monetisation + capital return compounder" — a well-recognised European telco setup; closest analogues are BT circa 2020-22 (pre-fibre ARPU proof) or Proximus circa 2019-21 [#J]

  • Reverse-engineered bear case: Scenario: fibre ARPU migration stalls at entry-tier pricing (flat ARPU despite subscriber migration), Romania requires capital injection, regulatory pricing decision forces OTE to share fibre network at low access prices — combined, this scenario implies EPS ~10-15% below current consensus over 24M and multiple compression from ~5.5× to ~5× EV/EBITDA [#J]

  • Single largest case-level risk: Regulatory intervention on fibre access pricing + ARPU stall simultaneously — this is the "silent risk" that is not fully in the sell-side models and that management cannot hedge against [#J]

  • Stacked downside: If Drivers 1 (partial miss), 4 (Romania deterioration), and 6 (regulatory headwind) miss simultaneously, EPS could be ~12-18% below bull case over 24M; combined with multiple compression, total return could be -10 to -15% vs a bull case of +35-45% [#J]

  • DT overhang — cross-cutting: DT's ~48-49% stake is simultaneously: (a) a governance/strategic alignment positive (dividend commitment), (b) a float/liquidity constraint, and (c) a potential forced-seller overhang if DT balance sheet pressures accelerate strategic portfolio review [#J]

  • Fulcrum candidate: Driver 1 (fibre ARPU) is the undisputed fulcrum — it determines whether this is a 20%+ IRR case or a 10-12% yield-only case [#J]

  • Single driver risk: If Driver 1 alone fails while Drivers 2-3 deliver, expected IRR drops to approximately 12-15% — below Richard's 25% threshold; the case parks [#J]
