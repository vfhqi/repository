[W] HTWS-GB — Short-Sellers + Bear Case [C] @ 19-May-26

Tags: #ESA #Risk #BearCase #ShortSellers
Source: [C] | Stage: ESA | Company: Helios Towers plc (HTWS-GB)

KEY FINDINGS - Bear case verdict: No major published short-seller reports targeting HTWS-GB have been identified; the bear case is constructed primarily from structural/fundamental concerns rather than activist attack [#A]

  • Leverage risk dominates: Net debt/EBITDA of ~5x at FY2023 following the Oman/Madagascar expansion wave is the single most-cited bearish concern; peers in the EM tower sector typically carry 3-4x at equivalent maturity [#J]
  • Currency structural problem: Revenue earned in local African/ME currencies (TZS, GHS, CDF, XAF, ETB, OMR) against USD-denominated debt creates a structural FX mismatch that bears argue is systematically under-disclosed in adjusted metrics [#J]
  • Tenancy ratio stagnation: HTWS-GB's average tenancy ratio of ~2.1x (FY2023) has improved only modestly from ~1.8x at IPO (2020); the bull thesis of rapid coloading is proceeding slower than implied targets [#A]
  • Ghana macro deterioration: Ghana entered IMF programme in 2023 with cedi depreciation exceeding 50% vs USD in 2022; HTWS-GB's Ghana segment generates ~15-17% of consolidated revenues, creating outsized exposure to a single-country macro shock [#J]
  • Free cash flow negative at group level: HTWS-GB reported negative reported FCF through most of the 2021-2023 period as capex for new tower builds and acquisitions absorbed operating cash; bear case argues FCF positivity is perpetually deferred [#J]
  • Governance structure: Millicom (Kinnevik/Nasdaq-listed) remains a significant shareholder and anchor tenant simultaneously; bears flag the conflict between landlord and tenant interests in lease renegotiations [#A]
  • Accounting complexity: HTWS-GB's heavy reliance on EBITDA and "Adjusted EBITDA" metrics — with meaningful gaps to reported IFRS profit — has attracted scepticism about earnings quality; leasehold capitalisation under IFRS 16 inflates asset base [#A]
  • Competitive entry risk: IHS Towers, American Tower, and Eaton Towers (now part of American Tower) all compete in overlapping African markets; bears argue market consolidation has reduced HTWS-GB's pricing power in anchor markets [#A]
  • Refinancing wall approaching: A meaningful portion of HTWS-GB's debt stack was structured with 2025-2027 maturities; in a higher-for-longer rate environment, refinancing at materially higher rates would compress equity returns sharply [#J]

§4 — Accounting + Governance Red Flags

J-front verdict: HTWS-GB's accounting is complex but not evidently fraudulent; the primary concerns are IFRS 16 lease capitalisation inflating assets, the gap between Adjusted EBITDA and IFRS metrics, and a governance structure with inherent anchor-tenant conflicts [#J]

  • Non-GAAP gap: HTWS-GB heavily promotes "Adjusted EBITDA" which excludes share-based compensation, restructuring costs, and certain one-off items; the gap between Adjusted EBITDA and IFRS operating profit is substantial — IFRS operating profit is depressed by D&A on right-of-use assets and tower assets [#A]

  • Sub-note: The Adjusted EBITDA/IFRS gap is structurally similar to all tower companies globally (AMT, CCI, SBA all face the same critique), but HTWS-GB's gap is proportionally wider due to higher leverage and D&A load

  • Adjustments increasing: The absolute quantum of "non-recurring" adjustments in HTWS-GB's accounts has grown as the company has expanded; acquisition-related costs, integration charges, and currency translation losses have persistently appeared in the adjustment bridge [#A]

  • Revenue recognition: HTWS-GB's revenue recognition is relatively transparent: tower lease revenues are contractual, with escalation clauses linked to CPI or fixed escalators; revenue is recognised on an accruals basis and there is no evidence of channel-stuffing or accelerated recognition [#A]

  • Sub-note: The bear case on revenue recognition is NOT that revenues are fraudulently overstated but that FX translation understates the deterioration in real economic value of revenues

  • Capitalisation aggressive: HTWS-GB capitalises tower construction costs, ground lease prepayments, and certain maintenance capex; the line between maintenance capex (should be expensed) and growth capex (appropriately capitalised) is a source of accounting discretion [#A]

  • Sub-note: Peer IHS Towers applies broadly similar capitalisation policies; this is an industry-wide accounting judgement area rather than HTWS-GB-specific red flag

  • IFRS 16 impact: IFRS 16 lease accounting creates a substantial right-of-use asset on HTWS-GB's balance sheet (ground leases for tower sites); this inflates assets and creates a corresponding lease liability that bears argue obscures the true debt load [#A]

  • Sub-note: HTWS-GB's "reported" net debt excludes lease liabilities in management metrics but includes them in covenant calculations under some facilities; the definitional gap is material given ~3,000+ tower sites each with individual ground leases

  • Related-party: Millicom International Cellular (Tigo) is simultaneously a major shareholder and HTWS-GB's largest single tenant in multiple markets; the terms of master lease agreements with Millicom affiliates are related-party transactions subject to audit committee approval [#A]

  • Sub-note: Bears argue that Millicom's influence over HTWS-GB's board creates downward pressure on lease rates in renegotiations; management contends lease rates are contracted and independently benchmarked

  • Sub-note: Millicom's own financial difficulties in certain LatAm/African markets create a secondary risk that HTWS-GB's anchor tenant faces credit stress

  • Customer concentration: HTWS-GB's top 3 tenants (MNOs including Millicom/Tigo, Airtel, and Vodacom/Vodafone affiliates) likely account for the majority of revenues across markets; customer concentration is higher than mature US tower operators due to limited MNO market depth in each country [#A]

  • Sub-note: In Tanzania (HTWS-GB's largest original market), the MNO market has historically had 4-5 operators but market consolidation reduces coloading opportunity

  • Board independence: HTWS-GB's board includes representatives associated with major shareholders including Millicom and CDC Group/British International Investment; full independence of non-executive oversight is questioned by bears [#A]

  • Auditor change: No material auditor change or audit qualification identified; HTWS-GB's accounts have been audited by a Big Four firm without identified material weaknesses [#A]

  • Material weakness: No material weakness disclosure identified in HTWS-GB's public filings as of available data [#A]

  • Pending lawsuit: Routine commercial litigation exists in individual African jurisdictions; no material class action, securities fraud litigation, or regulatory enforcement action identified [#A]

  • NWC liability: Working capital in HTWS-GB's model is relatively simple (tower lease receivables, payables to ground landowners/MNOs for shared services); no evidence of systematic receivables aging or payables stretching beyond industry norms [#A]

  • Receivables aging: MNO customers in some African markets have historically been slow payers; HTWS-GB has disclosed MNO payment arrears in selected markets (most notably DRC and Ethiopia) in prior reporting periods [#A]

  • Sub-note: Bears argue that accounts receivable balances may contain aging MNO receivables that require provisioning; this has been flagged in prior year notes but not quantified as a material impairment risk by management

  • Mgmt comp structure: HTWS-GB's management compensation is heavily oriented toward Adjusted EBITDA and revenue growth metrics rather than IFRS profit or GAAP FCF; bears argue this incentivises tower count growth over returns discipline [#A]

  • Sub-note: This incentive structure is common across the global tower industry but is particularly concerning at HTWS-GB given leverage levels

  • Bull rebuttal (sceptical): IFRS 16 and EBITDA complexity are industry-standard issues, not HTWS-GB-specific red flags; tower companies globally are valued on EV/EBITDA multiples with the FX/leverage concerns largely transparent in filings; no auditor qualification suggests the complexity is within normal bounds [#I]

§6 — Counter-Arguments to Bull Case

J-front verdict: The bear rebuttals to HTWS-GB's bull case are strongest on FCF conversion, leverage paydown trajectory, and FX structural mismatch; the tower growth story is credible but the financial architecture is less resilient than bulls imply [#J]

  • Bull thesis #1: Tower tenancy ratio will expand from ~2.1x to 2.5-3.0x, driving operating leverage and margin expansion to 50%+ EBITDA margins [#I]
  • Bear rebuttal #1: Tenancy ratio improvement has been materially slower than management guidance since IPO; improvement from 1.8x (2020) to 2.1x (2023) over 3 years implies the 2.5-3.0x target is a decade+ away at current pace
  • Evidence supporting rebuttal: MNO capex constraints in Africa, network sharing agreements between MNOs reducing coloading incentive, and government-mandated tower sharing in certain jurisdictions all compress organic coloading demand below IPO-era assumptions

  • Competitive evidence: IHS Towers (IHST-US) operates at ~1.6x average tenancy in some markets suggesting sector-wide tenancy ratio uplift is structurally constrained in African EM

  • Bull thesis #2: HTWS-GB will reach FCF positive in 2024/2025, unlocking deleveraging and eventually a dividend [#I]

  • Bear rebuttal #2: FCF positivity has been a "next year" story since 2020; repeated deferrals of FCF break-even suggest structural rather than timing reasons; the capex requirement for new tower builds to maintain tenancy pipeline continuously absorbs cash
  • Evidence supporting rebuttal: Published accounts show persistent negative reported FCF; management's FCF definition typically excludes lease payments and certain site-build capex that bears argue should be included as maintenance

  • Track record evidence: Every year from 2020-2023 has seen capex guidance revised upward as geographic expansion (Oman, Ethiopia, Madagascar) exceeded initial scope

  • Bull thesis #3: Long-term contracted revenues (10-15 year master lease agreements) provide high revenue visibility and reduce cyclical risk [#I]

  • Bear rebuttal #3: Long-term contracts do not eliminate FX risk; revenues contracted in local currencies (TZS, GHS) translate to USD/GBP at spot rates; a 50% currency devaluation halves reported revenue even with full contractual compliance
  • Evidence supporting rebuttal: Ghana cedi: -54% vs USD in 2022; Ethiopian birr: -60%+ vs USD over 2020-2023; these are not tail-risk events but observed outcomes in HTWS-GB's actual markets

  • Macro evidence: IMF programme countries (Ghana 2023, Ethiopia IMF discussions) are specifically likely to face managed devaluation; HTWS-GB has outsized exposure to fragile-state currencies

  • Bull thesis #4: Management is executing a clear capital allocation strategy — expand portfolio, improve tenancy, deleverage, return capital [#I]

  • Bear rebuttal #4: The sequencing of capital allocation has been acquisition-first, deleveraging-later; each acquisition (Oman 2021, Madagascar 2021, Ethiopia 2020, DRC/Congo) added leverage before the underlying markets matured
  • Track record evidence: Net debt/EBITDA increased from ~4x at IPO to ~5x+ post-acquisition wave; the promised "leverage reduction" pathway has been delayed by successive new market entries

  • Mgmt response pattern: Management has consistently refuted concerns with medium-term guidance; the question is whether medium-term guidance has been materially met

  • Bull thesis #5: HTWS-GB is an inflation hedge — CPI-escalation clauses in leases protect real revenues [#I]

  • Bear rebuttal #5: CPI escalation clauses are denominated in local currency CPI, not USD/GBP CPI; in a high-inflation / high-devaluation EM environment, local CPI escalation does NOT compensate for FX translation losses
  • Evidence supporting rebuttal: Ghana experienced ~50% CPI inflation in 2022-2023 while the cedi fell ~54% vs USD; CPI escalation protected local-currency revenue but USD-translated revenues still fell sharply

  • Competitive evidence: US tower operators with international EM exposure have structurally lower EBITDA margins in EM segments specifically because of FX translation drag, despite CPI clauses

  • Bull thesis #6: HTWS-GB's stock is undervalued relative to US tower peers on EV/EBITDA basis [#I]

  • Bear rebuttal #6: The discount to US tower peers is rational given structural differences: higher leverage, lower tenancy ratios, EM FX risk, smaller market caps, less liquid equity, sovereign/political risk — all justify a structurally lower multiple
  • Evidence supporting rebuttal: IHS Towers (IHST-US) trades at a similar or larger discount to AMT/CCI/SBA despite NYSE listing; the discount is sector-wide for EM tower companies, not HTWS-GB-specific, suggesting it reflects genuine risk

  • Valuation evidence: AMT/CCI/SBA trade at 20-25x EV/EBITDA in US market; EM tower peers at 8-12x; the 40-50% structural discount has been persistent, not a temporary mispricing

  • Bull thesis #7: Rising African smartphone penetration and data demand are structural growth drivers that will drive MNO tower demand for decades [#I]

  • Bear rebuttal #7: Secular growth in data demand does not automatically translate to tower company revenue growth; MNOs may densify networks via small cell / DAS rather than macro towers; 5G densification architectures reduce macro tower dependence relative to 4G

  • Competitive evidence: In more mature EM markets (South Africa, Kenya), urban network densification has been more complex than simple macro tower coloading; HTWS-GB's markets (Tanzania, DRC, Ethiopia) are earlier stage but may face the same dynamic

  • Bull rebuttal (sceptical): The bear rebuttals are strongest on FX (demonstrably true outcome in 2022-2023) and FCF deferral (track-record verifiable); they are weakest on tenancy ratio (2.1x is still meaningful and improving, just slowly) and on secular growth drivers (data demand tailwinds are real) [#I]

§8 — Quality of Bear Evidence (rate the bear case strength)

J-front verdict: The HTWS-GB bear case is moderately well-reasoned on leverage and FX grounds, with fact-based evidence supporting both concerns; it is weaker on governance/fraud dimensions where allegations are opinion-based; overall credibility is medium [#J]

  • Fact-based allegation — leverage: Net debt/EBITDA of ~5x is a verifiable, audited metric; this is not speculation — it is disclosed in annual accounts and is measurably elevated relative to US tower peers at equivalent maturity [#A]

  • Sub-note: Substantiated: peers AMT/CCI/SBA at similar network maturity phases operated at 4-5x but with USD-denominated revenues (no FX translation risk); HTWS-GB's leverage is structurally riskier than an equivalent US leverage ratio

  • Fact-based allegation — FX translation: Ghana cedi depreciation of 54% in 2022, Ethiopian birr depreciation of 60%+ over 2020-2023 are observable macroeconomic facts that demonstrably impacted reported results; this is not speculation [#A]

  • Validated: subsequent results confirmed the FX headwinds bears flagged; management's post-2022 communications specifically acknowledged FX translation as a significant factor

  • Fact-based allegation — FCF negative: Reported FCF has been negative in multiple annual periods; this is disclosed in cash flow statements; the bear allegation that FCF positivity has been deferred is supported by audited accounts [#A]

  • Sub-note: Management's counter (that "operational FCF" adjusting for growth capex is positive) is partially valid; the bear is correct that total reported FCF has been persistently negative, management is correct that this reflects growth investment not operational failure

  • Opinion-based allegation — governance conflict: The Millicom shareholder/tenant conflict concern is opinion-based; there is no documented evidence that lease rates have been set at below-market rates for Millicom affiliates; this is a structural concern not a proven abuse [#A]

  • Sub-note: Not substantiated by documented evidence; plausible structural risk, but bears would need disclosed lease rate benchmarking data to substantiate

  • Opinion-based allegation — Adjusted EBITDA manipulation: The allegation that EBITDA adjustments are being used to overstate economic performance is opinion-based; the adjustments are disclosed in detail and audited; the gap between Adjusted EBITDA and IFRS profit is a legitimate concern but not evidence of manipulation [#A]

  • Sub-note: Partially refuted: HTWS-GB's EBITDA adjustments are consistent with industry peers and the specific items adjusted are disclosed; this is aggressive accounting, not fraudulent accounting
