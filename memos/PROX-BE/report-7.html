§1 — Executive Summary

Proximus is mid-cycle through a capex-heavy fibre build that is structurally compressing near-term FCF. The Amplify strategy (February 2026) sets a credible roadmap to EUR 400m organic FCF by 2030 as rollout completes and capex normalises below EUR 1bn. The core investment question is whether the market is pricing this FCF ramp correctly — and whether Digi's entry and B2B execution risk materially defer or reduce it.

  • Verdict — driver hierarchy: FCF inflection (capex peak + fibre monetisation combined) is the primary value driver; B2B/ICT growth is secondary; Digi disruption risk is the primary bear constraint; cost efficiency and regulatory indexation are supporting/stabilising drivers [#J]
  • Fibre monetisation driver: convergent ARPC trending +1% YoY; indexed pricing provides a floor; fibre subscriber mix growing toward 2.4m premises passed (40%+ population coverage by mid-2025) [#A]
  • B2B/ICT driver: Proximus NXT IT recurring services +2.8% Q3-25; launched July 2024; Belgian federal cybersecurity contract is flagship win; absolute revenue still sub-EUR 450m/year domestic B2B [#A]
  • FCF capex-peak driver: capex peaked in 2022-23; EUR 1,249m FY25 actuals; EUR 1.2-1.25bn guided for 2026; sub-EUR 1bn by 2030; organic FCF EUR 130m FY25, targeting EUR 400m by 2030 [#A]
  • Flanders sharing driver: Proximus/Fiberklaar + Telenet/Wyre binding agreements April 2026; BCA investigation ongoing; if approved, Fiberklaar and Wyre build medium/low-density Flanders; Proximus and Telenet take reciprocal access — reduces Proximus standalone capex burden [#A]
  • Digi bear driver: fourth MNO since December 2024; EUR 5/month mobile; EUR 10/month broadband fibre planned; Proximus MVNO host until Digi's own network live; market already forcing data-volume upgrades without price increases [#A]
  • Cost efficiency driver: EUR 180m gross savings 2025-2028; 1,200 job cuts by 2030; 333 FTE reduction in 2025; Domestic EBITDA +1.9% FY25 despite wage indexation headwinds [#A]
  • Regulatory indexation driver: CPI-linked January annual price rises; Q2-25 ARPC +1.0% YoY; Belgium CPI 3.5% forecast 2026; mechanism is well-established but Digi pressure tests its effective pass-through [#A]

§2 — Key Driver #1: FCF Capex-Peak Turning

  • Definition: Proximus is past peak capital intensity; fibre rollout capex mechanically declines as network completion approaches 2028-2030; organic FCF inflects upward as a result [#I]
  • Company guidance — Amplify plan (Feb-26): organic FCF EUR 130m FY25 (exceeded guidance by EUR 30m); guided up to EUR 100m for 2026; targeting EUR 400m by 2030; capex sub-EUR 1bn by 2030 vs EUR 1.2-1.25bn in 2026 [#A]
  • Capex trajectory: FY25 capex EUR 1,249m; FY26 guided EUR 1.2-1.25bn; flat then declining toward 2028-2030; fibre deployment accounted for 30% of total capex in 2025 (up from 24% prior year) [#A]
  • FCF delivery vs guidance: organic FCF EUR 130m FY25 vs EUR 100m guidance — 30% beat; EUR 53m FY24 — more than doubled in one year; trajectory credible based on completion timetable [#A]
  • Leverage: ND/EBITDA guided ~2.8x for 2026 (S&P definition); vs EU telco median ~2.5-3.0x — Proximus is within normal range but not low; no stress at current capex pace given state 53% ownership backstop [#A]
  • JV capex recycling: Fiberklaar (1.5m homes Flanders, EQT JV) and Unifiber (0.6m homes Wallonia) structures shift off-balance-sheet capex burden; Proximus own dense-area 2.2m rollout completing by end-2026 [#A]
  • Peer anchor: KPN completed FTTH rollout ~2026 and showed rapid FCF inflection post-completion; Swisscom (SCMN-CH) maintains capex discipline at ~CHF 1.7bn annually with more mature fibre base — Proximus EUR 1.25bn is elevated vs revenue but trajectory mirrors KPN playbook [#A]
  • Why 2026 FCF guided lower than FY25 actuals: management cited transition-year dynamics — cost transformation spend front-loaded, Global restructuring costs, and one-off FCF items in FY25 (capex phasing); the guided EUR 100m is a trough before the 2027-2030 ramp [#A]
  • Capex intensity vs revenue: Proximus capex/revenue ~19% in 2025 (EUR 1,249m vs ~EUR 6.4bn group revenue); EU telco median capex/revenue ~14-16%; Proximus is 3-5ppt above peers reflecting the build cycle — convergence to median post-2028 is the FCF unlock mechanism [#A]
  • Interest cost watch: ND/EBITDA at 2.8x implies net debt ~EUR 5.3bn; at ~4% average cost of debt, annual interest ~EUR 210m; FCF after interest but before capex is materially lower than organic FCF; track as rates remain elevated [#A]
  • Dividend re-set as FCF signal: dividend cut from EUR 1.20 to EUR 0.60/share (FY25 results) explicitly acknowledges insufficient FCF at current capex levels to sustain legacy payout; EUR 0.30/2026, EUR 0.40/2027, EUR 0.50/2028 path = management FCF ramp confidence expressed in dividend trajectory [#J]
  • Sceptical note: 2026 organic FCF guided DOWN to EUR 100m from EUR 130m FY25 — the inflection is not linear; Global segment EBITDA decline masks domestic FCF progress; Digi could extend mobile capex response requirements [#J]
  • Bear case: Global segment structural decline (revenue -6.6% Q4-25 YoY) could require incremental domestic reinvestment; Flanders sharing delay adds capex if Proximus must build alone [#J]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — fibre completion timetable is mechanistic; capex below EUR 1bn by 2030 is credible; FCF trajectory to EUR 400m is the key GARP re-rating catalyst if delivered

§3 — Key Driver #2: Fibre Monetisation / ARPU Inflection

  • Definition: Copper-to-fibre migration should lift ARPU as customers move to higher-speed, higher-priced convergent bundles; ARPC uplift from fibre vs copper is the revenue per customer improvement vector [#I]
  • Current state — network coverage: Fibre network passed 2.416m premises by June 2025 (40%+ Belgian population); total FTTH target 4.2m HP with partners by 2028; standalone 2.2m in dense areas completing end-2026 [#A]
  • ARPC data: convergent ARPC EUR 91.2 Q2-25, up +1.0% YoY; Q3-25 EUR 90.5 (+0.5% YoY); January 2025 price indexation contributed; fibre mix growing as driver [#A]
  • FMC penetration: FMC bundle uptake rising; convergent customers represent Proximus's highest-ARPC tier; churn reduction benefit is asserted but not quantified in public disclosures at Triaging stage [#A]
  • Monetisation mechanism: fibre customers pay a premium for Gigabit speed tiers; copper switch-off enforces migration and removes the cheaper-tier alternative; Belgian rollout timing supports 2026-2028 as the migration inflection window [#A]
  • Peer anchor: KPN post-FTTH rollout showed ARPU growth of ~2-3% per year as fibre mix rose; Orange France and Deutsche Telekom both show 1-2% fibre ARPU premia over copper equivalents — Proximus's +1% is at the low end of this peer range [#A]
  • Limiting factor — Digi compression: Digi's EUR 10/month broadband fibre plan (announced at launch) directly attacks the broadband ARPU pillar of convergent bundles; if Digi gains 5-10% broadband share by 2027, ARPC uplift is largely offset [#J]
  • Sceptical note: ARPC growth has decelerated from +1.0% Q2-25 to +0.5% Q3-25 — directionally going the wrong way as Digi's mobile impact flows through; fibre mix increasing but not yet lifting ARPC at meaningful pace [#J]
  • Copper switch-off timeline: Proximus has flagged 2028 as the target year for copper network decommissioning in covered areas; forced migration removes the option for customers to remain on cheaper copper plans; this is the structural catalyst for fibre ARPC realisation but is 2+ years away [#A]
  • Fibre take-up rate: Proximus has not published explicit fibre take-up rates by vintage but management commentary in Q3-25 and Q4-25 indicated growing uptake; estimate 35-45% take-up on connected premises based on peer benchmarks (KPN ~50%, Orange France ~40% at equivalent rollout maturity) [#A]
  • FMC bundle economics: convergent customers at EUR 91.2 ARPC vs estimated standalone broadband ~EUR 40-45 and standalone mobile ~EUR 20-25 — bundle premium is real; question is whether the mix of FMC vs standalone is shifting fast enough to lift Group ARPC [#A]
  • Bear case: Digi broadband launch at scale compresses broadband ARPU by 10-15%; copper switch-off migration meets consumer resistance; fibre ARPC uplift proves to be a one-time mix shift rather than structural [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — fibre network build creates the right conditions but ARPC growth is modest (+0.5-1.0%) and decelerating; Digi's broadband ambition is the key offsetting risk

§4 — Key Driver #3: B2B/ICT Enterprise Growth (Proximus NXT)

  • Definition: Proximus NXT (domestic B2B IT services entity, launched July 2024) targets enterprise cloud connectivity, cybersecurity, managed IT, SD-WAN; higher-margin recurring IT services are the growth vector above commodity telco B2B [#I]
  • Revenue trajectory: IT Services revenue EUR 112m Q3-25, +2.8% YoY; growth fully from recurring services; consistent with 2-3% growth pace across 2024-2025; Proximus described B2B 2024 performance as "very well" [#A]
  • Flagship win: Largest Belgian public cybersecurity contract (federal government) secured in 2024; signals Proximus NXT is competitive at the top of the market [#A]
  • Strategic rationale: centralising all IT activities for B2B customers into single entity (Proximus NXT IT) enables faster customised delivery; exploitation of existing telco-to-enterprise relationships [#A]
  • Market size context: Belgian enterprise IT services market is sub-EUR 5bn annually; Proximus NXT is a challenger with strong telco connectivity base but limited IT services heritage vs global SIs (IBM, Accenture, Capgemini) [#A]
  • Proximus NXT vs BICS/Global: Proximus NXT is domestic-only B2B; Proximus Global is the international connectivity arm — Global revenue declining -6.6% Q4-25 YoY; do not conflate the two [#I]
  • Peer anchor: Swisscom Enterprise and BT Enterprise show 2-4% ICT services growth where they compete on managed services; KPN's IT division (Cegeka acquisition) targets similar space; Proximus NXT is earlier-stage than these peers [#A]
  • Absolute scale limitation: EUR 112m/quarter implies ~EUR 450m annualised IT services revenue; vs EUR ~1.6bn domestic revenue total — B2B/ICT is ~28% of domestic but growing fastest; insufficient alone to re-rate the stock [#A]
  • Proximus NXT positioning vs global SIs: Proximus NXT differentiates on local presence, telco-grade connectivity as the underlying fabric, and national security clearance relationships (Belgian government); not competing with global SIs on global engagements — competing on Belgian market dominance [#A]
  • Revenue mix shift watch: if IT services (EUR ~450m annualised) grow at 3% while legacy B2B telco (voice, connectivity, legacy managed services) decline at 2%, the net B2B revenue growth is approximately zero; the growth story requires IT services to more than offset the legacy decline within the B2B division [#A]
  • Enterprise mobile pressure: Digi's EUR 5 mobile pricing is targeted at consumer but business mobile plans are next; Proximus's Q3-25 business mobile data upgrades (Business Mobile Comfort 100→150 GB) without price increase signals the enterprise mobile defence is already underway [#A]
  • Sceptical note: 2.8% IT services growth is modest; Digi's competitive pressure is primarily on consumer and mobile, not enterprise IT — but enterprise mobile data pricing pressure is real and affects B2B margins; Proximus NXT is a rebrand/reorganisation as much as a new business [#J]
  • Bear case: Incumbent telco IT services rarely outgrow the broader IT services market; if Proximus NXT fails to win large private-sector contracts beyond the government anchor, growth decelerates to 1-2% [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — early positive momentum; government anchor validates capability; growth is real but small-base effect limits materiality at Group level for 2026-2028 period

§5 — Key Driver #4: Flanders FTTH Sharing Agreement (BCA Conditional)

  • Definition: Proximus/Fiberklaar and Telenet/Wyre have agreed to share fibre network deployment in Flanders; in medium/low-density areas, one party builds and grants reciprocal wholesale access; reduces duplicated capex across Belgium's most contested region [#I]
  • Timeline: July 2024: MOU signed; October 2025: BCA launches market test; April 30, 2026: binding agreements signed; BCA continues ex officio investigation — final approval not yet granted as of May 2026 [#A]
  • Capex benefit — structure: in Flanders medium/low-density, Wyre and Fiberklaar would build; Proximus and Telenet take reciprocal access without building; in sparsest areas, HFC supplements FTTH to maximise coverage — intelligently preserves existing Telenet HFC investment [#A]
  • Capex benefit — magnitude: Proximus currently targets 4.2m HP total with partners by 2028; Flanders sharing reduces standalone build requirement; BCA estimated material cost savings passable to consumers — implies meaningful NPV benefit to Proximus if approved (Watson estimate: EUR 100-200m capex avoidance over 2026-2030 based on partial Flanders coverage) [#A]
  • Regulatory risk: BCA investigation ongoing; concerns centre on whether cooperation reduces competitive rollout duplication; Belgium has unusual competitive dynamic — Proximus vs Telenet (HFC/cable) already competed nationally; cooperation reduces that tension [#A]
  • BCA precedent: Belgian Competition Authority has historically been interventionist in telco market matters; market test suggests BCA is open to a modified structure rather than outright rejection [#A]
  • Strategic logic: both parties benefit — Proximus reduces capex in low-return rural Flanders; Telenet/Wyre gains FTTH access without building (protecting its cash-generative cable base) [#J]
  • Sceptical note: If BCA rejects or heavily modifies the deal, Proximus must build alone in Flanders medium-density — adding capex beyond the EUR 1.2-1.25bn FY26 guidance; the April binding agreements do not guarantee regulatory clearance [#J]
  • Bear case: BCA rejects; Proximus carries full Flanders capex; capex remains EUR 1.2bn+ through 2028; FCF ramp deferred; leverage deteriorates modestly [#J]

Plausibility verdict: High conditional plausibility — industrial logic is strong; binding agreements are signed; BCA market test launched = regulatory process engaged; risk is delay or modification, not outright rejection

§6 — Key Driver #5: Cost Efficiency / Restructuring Programme

  • Definition: Proximus's Amplify plan includes a EUR 180m gross savings programme 2025-2028, primarily staff-related; 1,200 FTE reductions by 2030 (~15% of workforce); AI-led efficiency replacing manual roles; Domestic EBITDA supported by OpEx reduction [#I]
  • 2025 delivery: 333 FTE reduction in FY25 (natural attrition plus selective hiring freeze); Domestic EBITDA +1.9% FY25; Q4-25 Domestic EBITDA +2.3% YoY; cost efficiencies more than offset wage indexation headwinds [#A]
  • Programme scope: EUR 180m gross savings over 4 years = EUR 45m/year average; headcount reduction from ~10,300 FTE (Domestic) to ~9,100 FTE equivalent by 2030; Global FTEs also declining (2,602 end-2025, -238 YoY) [#A]
  • Mechanism: AI-led automation of network operations, customer service, back-office processes; zero-based budgeting on discretionary spend; real estate rationalisation [#A]
  • Wage indexation headwind: Belgium has automatic wage indexation tied to health index; creates structural OpEx inflation of ~2-3% annually on the wage bill; cost programme must run faster than indexation to generate net savings [#A]
  • Peer anchor: Swisscom delivered CHF 200m+ in cost savings across 2021-2025 restructuring; KPN generated EUR ~200m savings from simplification; Proximus EUR 180m over 4 years is comparable in ambition but on a smaller base — execution credibility is supported by FY25 delivery [#A]
  • Sceptical note: EUR 45m/year gross savings partially offsets annual wage indexation; net benefit to EBITDA is probably EUR 10-25m/year after reinvestment in transformation; not a step-change, a stabiliser [#J]
  • Bear case: Social dialogue delays headcount reduction pace; Belgian labour law increases redundancy costs; AI-led savings require upfront technology investment that partially offsets gross savings [#J]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — already delivering in FY25; programme is conservative relative to Belgian labour environment; net benefit modest but consistent with "EBITDA broadly stable" guidance

§7 — Key Driver #6: Digi Belgium Disruption (Bear Driver)

  • Definition: Digi Belgium launched December 2024 as Belgium's fourth MNO with EUR 5/month mobile and EUR 10/month broadband ambitions; Digi is the Romanian low-cost operator that disrupted France (3% market share in 3 years); Belgium is its second major Western European market [#I]
  • Launch mechanics: Digi launched on Proximus MVNO agreement (ironic — Proximus earns MVNO wholesale revenue from its own disruption); building own 4G/5G network with Citymesh; 30% 5G coverage target by end-2025 [#A]
  • Immediate market response: Proximus increased mobile data volumes across plans (Mobile Smart 35→50 GB; Mobile Maxi 70→100 GB) without price increases in March 2025 — classic incumbent defensive response; tariff cuts already embedded in 2025 financials [#A]
  • Belgian market context: Belgium has historically been a high-ARPU, oligopoly mobile market (Proximus, Telenet, Orange Belgium); Digi targets the pricing umbrella which is among the highest in Western Europe; ARPU compression potential is above-EU-median [#A]
  • Broadband / FTTH threat: Digi's EUR 10/month broadband fibre plan is the bigger medium-term threat; broadband impact described as "still to come" by analysts in 2025; Digi must build FTTH access agreements or lease Proximus/Fiberklaar wholesale — timeline uncertain [#A]
  • Digi France precedent: In France (launched 2023), Digi reached ~3% mobile market share in 18 months; French incumbents (Orange, SFR, Bouygues) absorbed 5-8% mobile ARPU compression; Belgium is smaller, more concentrated — disruption could be faster-paced [#A]
  • Proximus MVNO revenue offset: Proximus earns wholesale MVNO revenue from hosting Digi until Digi's own network is live; this temporarily offsets competitive revenue erosion; once Digi migrates to own network (target 2026-2027), wholesale income disappears [#J]
  • Sceptical note — bear case depth: If Digi reaches 8-10% Belgian mobile market share by 2028 and launches broadband at scale, mobile ARPU compression of 8-12% is plausible; combined revenue impact on Proximus Domestic could be EUR 50-80m annually — material against a broadly-stable revenue base [#J]
  • Network build timeline — key watch: Digi's own 4G/5G network (with Citymesh) targets 30% 5G coverage by end-2025; once Digi migrates off the Proximus MVNO, Proximus loses wholesale revenue AND faces a more capable network competitor; 2026-2027 is the transition window [#A]
  • Digi Belgium vs Digi France comparison: in France, Digi launched 2023 and reached ~3% mobile share in 18 months; Belgium has 11.6m population vs France 68m — smaller market means Digi can reach meaningful share faster in absolute terms; Belgian market has historically higher ARPUs giving Digi more pricing room to undercut [#A]
  • Subscriber data signal: Proximus Q4-25 added +33,000 mobile postpaid net adds despite Digi presence — market held in Q4-25; the real test is H1-26 when Digi broadens distribution and builds network coverage [#A]
  • Regulatory angle: Belgian regulator BIPT is monitoring Digi market entry; state 53% ownership of Proximus creates political tension around allowing deep ARPU erosion in a national infrastructure operator; soft regulatory backstop exists but will not prevent pricing competition [#J]
  • Upside note: Proximus as MVNO host retains some wholesale revenue; state 53% ownership provides political backstop on regulatory remediation if Digi destabilises investment returns [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium-High plausibility (as a bear risk driver) — Digi is live and pricing aggressively; mobile impact already forcing incumbent response; broadband impact is the larger threat but timeline is 2026-2028; disruption pace is the key uncertainty

§8 — Key Driver #7: Regulatory CPI Indexation (Revenue Floor)

  • Definition: Belgian telco operators have long-established contractual mechanisms allowing annual price increases tied to CPI/health index; Proximus uses January-start annual indexation; provides a nominal revenue floor in inflationary environments [#I]
  • 2025 delivery: January 2025 CPI indexation contributed to +2.5% residential customer services revenue growth; convergent ARPC +1.0% YoY in Q2-25; mechanism is working and is not being legally challenged [#A]
  • Forward CPI context: Belgium CPI projected 3.5% in 2026 and 3.0% in 2027 (Federal Planning Bureau); if indexation passes at full CPI, ARPC could rise 2-3% annually in nominal terms through 2027 [#A]
  • Interaction with Digi: Digi's EUR 5 mobile offering means the effective price point for mobile is moving down; CPI indexation on existing converged bundles creates a dual-track — indexed customers pay more, Digi switchers pay much less; net effect depends on churn from Digi [#A]
  • Peer anchor: KPN, Deutsche Telekom, and Orange France all operate CPI-linked price adjustment mechanisms; across EU incumbents, CPI indexation typically delivers 1-2% ARPU uplift annually; Proximus is in-line with peers; mechanism does not provide above-peer growth [#A]
  • BIPT regulatory posture: BIPT aligned with EC 2025 cost model for mobile termination (cap EUR 0.2/min) — modest headwind to interconnect revenue but not material to consumer ARPU indexation [#A]
  • Limitation: indexation applies to existing customers on current contracts; new bundles and Digi competitive offers are not subject to indexation; indexation covers ~60-70% of the residential revenue base in effect [#J]
  • Sceptical note: Indexation is a floor, not a growth driver; 1% real ARPC growth after inflation is approximately zero; in a market where Digi is depressing effective market rates, the indexation mechanism is a churn-risk amplifier — customers on indexed (higher-priced) plans have greater incentive to switch to Digi [#J]
  • Bear case: Belgian regulator intervenes to limit indexation during a period of elevated inflation; or Digi forces structural market tariff reset that makes full CPI pass-through commercially impossible [#J]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility as a stabiliser — mechanism is established and delivered in 2025; quantitatively small (~1-2% ARPC) but reliable; risk is that it becomes a churn catalyst rather than a revenue floor in a Digi-disrupted market

§9 — Driver Synthesis + Sceptical Synthesis

  • Synthesis verdict — FCF ramp is the dominant thesis: Proximus is structurally a capex-cycle play; the investable question is whether EUR 400m organic FCF by 2030 is credible and whether the current valuation already prices it in [#J]
  • Positive driver stack (bull case): fibre capex completes on schedule (dense area end-2026); Flanders sharing approved (adds EUR 100-200m NPV); B2B/ICT grows 3-4% annually; cost programme delivers EUR 45m/year net; CPI indexation holds; domestic EBITDA grows 1-2% to 2028 [#A]
  • Under this bull case: organic FCF trajectory is EUR 130m (FY25) → EUR 150-200m (FY27) → EUR 350-400m (FY30); ND/EBITDA declines toward 2.0-2.2x; dividend re-rates from EUR 0.30 to EUR 0.50/share by 2028 [#A]
  • Negative driver stack (bear case): Digi captures 8-10% mobile market share by 2028; broadband pricing pressure materialises at scale; BCA rejects Flanders sharing; Global segment declines require reinvestment; wage indexation exceeds cost savings; organic FCF stalls at EUR 100-150m through 2028 [#J]
  • The dividend reset is the key near-term signal: Proximus cut gross dividend from EUR 1.20 to EUR 0.60/share (FY25 results, Feb-26); stock fell 15% on CMD day — the market's reaction reveals this is NOT a re-rated GARP story yet; it is a restructuring/reinvestment story with execution risk [#J]
  • State ownership dual-edged: 53% Belgian state stake provides balance sheet backstop and regulatory goodwill; also constrains restructuring pace, limits strategic flexibility, and suppresses optionality (no M&A, no asset disposals without political approval) [#J]
  • B2B/ICT is necessary but not sufficient: Proximus NXT at current growth rate (~EUR 450m IT services, +2-3% YoY) adds ~EUR 10-15m/year to domestic revenue; insufficient to compensate for structural mobile ARPU headwinds of EUR 30-80m risk [#A]
  • Fibre monetisation is the right driver but too slow: Convergent ARPC growth of +0.5-1.0% is below inflation; the mix shift to fibre is happening but not yet generating the ARPC uplift that would justify a GARP premium; copper switch-off (2028 target) is the catalyst that forces migration but is still 2+ years away [#J]
  • Regulatory CPI indexation is not a differentiator: all Belgian incumbents have the same mechanism; provides no relative competitive advantage vs Orange Belgium or Telenet [#J]
  • Flanders sharing — the most asymmetric driver: if approved, FCF trajectory improves materially; if rejected, capex guidance increases; binary outcome with binary impact; BCA timing unknown [#J]
  • Sceptical synthesis conclusion: Proximus at Triaging is a dividend yield + FCF recovery story, not a GARP story; the B2B/ICT growth vector is real but too small to re-rate; Digi's disruption is the swing factor; the EUR 400m FCF target by 2030 is credible mechanically but demands flawless execution on 4 interdependent assumptions [#J]
  • Watson conclusion on setup: The investment setup question — "yield stock or GARP?" — resolves to: yield stock with optionality on GARP if Digi disruption is less severe than feared and Flanders sharing is approved; GARP case requires seeing 2026 ARPC growth re-accelerate and IT services maintain 3%+ pace [#J]

Confidence in case-level thesis: Medium plausibility — FCF inflection to EUR 400m by 2030 is the credible mechanical spine; execution risk on 4 drivers (Digi, BCA, B2B/ICT, cost programme) and the stock's 15% post-CMD drop suggests the market shares Watson's scepticism
