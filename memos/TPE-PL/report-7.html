§2 — Key Driver #1: Regulated Electricity Distribution Tariff Reset

  • J-front verdict: Distribution tariff revision is the highest-leverage near-term binary event; cost-reflective outcome would add 20-35% to distribution EBIT, but URE track record introduces real regulatory risk [#J]

  • Driver definition: TPE-PL's distribution subsidiary (TAURON Dystrybucja) operates under URE-regulated tariffs; periodic reset determines the allowed return on the regulated asset base , capex recognition, and OPEX cost pass-through [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Higher approved RAB value + higher WACC allowance = higher allowed revenue; maps directly to distribution EBIT and, after interest and tax, to EPS; distribution is est. 50-60% of group EBIT [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Tariff outcome re-prices the earnings stream in the market's DCF; a positive reset is a re-rating catalyst for the regulated stub and reduces the discount applied to coal-exit uncertainty [#J]

  • Company guide: Management has guided for cost-reflective tariff recognition in capex-heavy distribution investment cycle; specific IRR guidance of ~6-7% real on RAB expansion disclosed in investor presentations [#A]

  • Management commentary: TPE-PL management publicly noted URE's improving methodology on recognising network-upgrade investments; cautiously optimistic tone in H2 2024 presentations [#A]

  • Track record: Historically URE has applied conservative WACC allowances (3.5-5% real vs company-requested ~6%); partial cost recognition has been the norm; management credibility on tariff optimism is moderate at best [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Company guidance leans optimistic relative to URE historical award levels; sandbagging not evident — management has consistently argued for higher RAB recognition [#A]

  • SS consensus: Polish sell-side models distribution on ~5% real regulated return; upside scenario models 6%+ return; downside models 4% or URE cap on capex recognition [#A]

  • Consensus changes: L6M estimates on distribution segment revised slightly lower following URE commentary suggesting cost-efficiency targets before full capex pass-through; sentiment cautious [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Full cost-reflective tariff (6%+ return) vs current scenario = est. PLN 200-350m incremental EBITDA annually; translates to 15-25% group EPS uplift (no peer anchor available — flagged as thin) [#A]

  • Recent delivery: Distribution EBIT grew modestly in 2023-24 despite capex acceleration; allowed revenue increases were partial; no major negative tariff surprise but no step-change uplift either [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: Distribution EBIT has been the most stable segment; CAGR est. +4-6% over 2021-24 vs flat-to-declining generation EBIT [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: European regulated network utilities (e.g., Innogy/E.ON Netz, ENEA Operator in Poland) typically trade on 5-7% real regulated returns; TPE-PL distribution currently at lower end — upside exists if URE aligns to European norms [#A] (Note: no cohort peer data available; flagged)

  • Macro tailwind: Polish grid modernisation is a structural necessity — EU grid-investment obligations, EV charging infrastructure, distributed-generation integration all require network capex; regulatory environment likely to gradually accommodate higher returns [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Polish government's sensitivity to retail electricity prices creates political pressure on URE to limit tariff increases; inflationary environment amplified this tension 2022-24 [#A]

  • Visibility: Moderate — URE tariff decisions are public and scheduled; timing and quantum are uncertain but process is transparent [#A]

  • Bear case: URE imposes efficiency cap, limits capex recognition to maintenance only, and holds WACC at 4% real; distribution EBIT growth stalls; market re-rates distribution at lower multiple [#J]

  • Miss scenario: URE tariff decision delayed or interim award below cost of capital; management forced to cut distribution capex guidance, triggering earnings and strategic-plan downgrades [#J]

  • Miss magnitude: 15-25% EBIT downside to distribution segment; combined with loss of re-rating catalyst, 20-30% potential SP downside in isolation [#A]

  • Red flags: URE published cost-efficiency benchmark targets in 2024 that imply it expects TAURON Dystrybucja to reduce OPEX before full capex recognition — signals partial rather than full cost-reflective award [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: What if URE's new regulatory methodology structurally caps returns below the cost of capital across all Polish DSOs, not just TPE-PL? This would invalidate the bullish distribution thesis for all Polish utilities simultaneously and remove the re-rating catalyst entirely [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — cost-reflective tariff partly delivered but full uplift dependent on URE methodology shift not yet confirmed in practice

§3 — Key Driver #2: Capacity Market Revenues

  • J-front verdict: Polish capacity market is a structurally supportive revenue stream providing near-certain 10-15% EBIT contribution for next 3-5 years; the tail risk is political capacity-mechanism redesign [#J]

  • Driver definition: Poland operates a capacity remuneration mechanism (CRM/capacity market) under which generation units (including coal and gas) receive capacity payments in exchange for availability commitments; TPE-PL is a significant beneficiary as a large fleet operator [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Capacity payments are largely fixed and contracted 1-5 years out; they provide a floor revenue stream that insulates generation EBIT from spot-price volatility; directly additive to EPS with high visibility [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Capacity revenues improve earnings quality (recurring, contracted) and reduce the cyclicality discount applied to the generation segment [#J]

  • Company guide: TPE-PL has disclosed contracted capacity revenues in the PLN 500-700m annual range across disclosed auction results; management treats these as a stable earnings base in guidance [#A]

  • Management commentary: Management has emphasised capacity market participation in strategic plan to 2030; explicit bridging mechanism as coal exits — capacity payments compensate declining generation margins [#A]

  • Track record: Polish capacity market launched 2021; TPE-PL has successfully participated in all auctions to date; no revenue shortfall vs contracted amounts disclosed [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Contracted capacity revenues are well-evidenced; management guidance here is credible and supported by auction results [#A]

  • SS consensus: Named brokers (PKO BP Equity, Pekao IB) model capacity revenues as a 'ballast' item — high confidence, low upside surprise potential; used as an anchor in bear-case modelling [#A]

  • Consensus changes: Capacity revenue assumptions broadly stable over L12M; modest upside from 2025 auction clearing prices reported slightly above prior-year assumptions [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: PLN 500-700m capacity revenues at high EBITDA margin (est. 60-70%) = PLN 300-490m EBITDA contribution; approximately 10-15% of group EBITDA [#A]

  • Recent delivery: 2024 capacity revenues in line with contracted levels; no shortfall; new 2025-26 auction results confirm revenue continuity [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: Capacity revenues have been the most predictable earnings contributor since CRM launch; growing modestly as auction prices increased in 2023-24 [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: European capacity markets (France, GB, Belgium) provide 5-15% of integrated utility EBITDA for generators; Poland's mechanism is newer but clearing prices have been competitive [#A] (no direct peer data; flagged)

  • Macro tailwind: Polish grid security concerns (coal-exit timeline, limited interconnection with EU grid) create political pressure to maintain and extend the capacity mechanism; structural tailwind for near-term continuity [#A]

  • Macro headwind: EU state aid review of capacity mechanisms creates medium-term risk; European Commission has challenged similar mechanisms in France and Belgium on competition grounds [#A]

  • Visibility: High for contracted periods (1-3 year delivery obligations already auctioned); medium for 4-5 year horizon depending on mechanism redesign [#A]

  • Bear case: EU state aid investigation forces material redesign of Polish capacity market; clearing prices decline sharply as renewables reduce scarcity; TPE-PL's coal units disqualified from future auctions [#J]

  • Miss scenario: New auction clearing prices fall 20-30% from current levels; contracted revenue renewal at lower rates; 8-12% EBITDA headwind [#J]

  • Miss magnitude: 8-12% group EBITDA decline; moderate multiple compression on generation segment but partially offset by ongoing contracted payments [#A]

  • Red flags: EU is actively reviewing Polish capacity market design; 2024 European Commission enquiry into capacity mechanism compatibility with clean-energy transition is a live process [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: What if the EU forces Poland to exclude coal units from capacity auctions before 2030? This would remove the bridging revenue stream that underpins TPE-PL's ability to fund renewables capex without distress-level dilution [#J]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — contracted revenues are secured for near term, but EU regulatory risk introduces medium-term uncertainty beyond 2027

§4 — Key Driver #3: Coal-Exit Timing and Liability Management

  • J-front verdict: Coal-exit represents the largest binary liability in the investment case; optionality value is real but timing opacity and state-support uncertainty make this the most difficult driver to underwrite [#J]

  • Driver definition: TPE-PL operates coal-fired generation assets (primarily Jaworzno III, Łagisza, Łaziska, Siersza plants); regulatory and commercial pressure to exit coal by latest 2035-2040 creates stranded-asset risk, EU ETS cost escalation, and decommissioning liability exposure [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Clean exit (state-supported, structured decommissioning) removes coal losses and ETS costs from P&L; unstructured exit crystallises write-downs and decommissioning provisions; EPS impact ranges from +20% (structured) to -30%+ (unstructured) [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Market currently applies a discount to TPE-PL for coal exposure; successful exit removes the discount and allows re-rating toward cleaner utility peers [#J]

  • Company guide: TPE-PL is a signatory to the Polish government's social contract for coal-sector transformation; management guidance assumes state support for structured exit from 2025 onward; timeline: phased closure 2026-2036 [#A]

  • Management commentary: Management acknowledges EU ETS cost headwinds; emphasises Polish government commitment to compensating coal operators for early closure; cautiously optimistic on finalisation of compensation framework [#A]

  • Track record: Polish government has repeatedly delayed finalisation of coal-exit compensation legislation; EU state aid approvals for Polish coal programmes have faced Brussels scrutiny; execution credibility is low [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Management guidance on structured exit is highly optimistic relative to political and regulatory track record; multiple deadlines have slipped [#A]

  • SS consensus: Named brokers diverge — PKO BP Equity takes a constructive view assuming compensation framework by end-2025; Erste Group and BDM Maklerski apply heavier coal-discount provisions; consensus models partial compensation only [#A]

  • Consensus changes: L12M: consensus has been reducing assumed state compensation — each quarter of political delay is eroding the bull case; EPS estimates net of coal write-downs have drifted lower [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Full structured exit with compensation removes est. PLN 300-500m annual ETS + operational losses; translates to EPS uplift of 15-25% on a clean basis; stranded write-down risk est. PLN 1-3bn worst case (book value of coal assets) [#A]

  • Recent delivery: Jaworzno III unit returned to operation after extended outage in 2023; continued operating losses in coal generation in 2024-25; EU ETS cost escalation materially worsening coal EBIT [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: Coal generation EBIT has been negative or near-zero since 2022 as wholesale prices normalised and ETS costs rose; segment has been a net drag, partially offset by capacity market payments [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: German coal-exit (RWE, Uniper) required ~€10-15bn state support across the sector; Polish GDP-adjusted equivalent suggests TPE-PL alone could need PLN 5-10bn; current provisions are a fraction of this [#A] (flagged as rough analogy; different regulatory context)

  • Macro tailwind: EU climate policy (Fit for 55, Carbon Border Adjustment) accelerates cost of holding coal; strong structural incentive for government to finalise framework [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Polish government energy security concerns post-Ukraine war are slowing coal exit; domestic political resistance to mine closure (employment); EU state aid approval process adds 18-24 month delays [#A]

  • Visibility: Low — compensation framework not finalised; timeline has slipped twice; legal and political complexity is high [#J]

  • Bear case: Compensation legislation fails to pass EU state aid review; TPE-PL forced to book large stranded-asset write-downs; equity issuance required to maintain credit metrics; coal losses continue through 2027+ [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Delayed exit by 2-3 years beyond current timeline; ETS costs compound; rating agency downgrades increase debt servicing costs [#J]

  • Miss magnitude: PLN 1-3bn write-down scenario = 30-50% book-value impairment; potential credit-rating downgrade; 25-40% SP downside in adverse scenario [#A]

  • Red flags: Jaworzno III extended outage and subsequent technical issues signal aging coal fleet reliability problems — accelerating optionality destruction even before policy resolution [#J]

  • ❌ Coal segment drag: Coal EBIT has been negative for 3+ consecutive years with no clear inflection point; state compensation framework unresolved despite multiple announced deadlines [#J] [Cross-ref: §4]

  • Sceptical bullet: What if EU state aid rules effectively prohibit meaningful compensation, and Polish government lacks fiscal capacity to fund an unsubsidised coal exit at the scale required? TPE-PL's coal book would become a chronic earnings drain rather than a solvable liability [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Low-medium plausibility — structured exit optionality is real but dependent on political and EU state-aid processes that have consistently underdelivered on timeline

§5 — Key Driver #4: Renewables Build-Out and Green Capex Returns

  • J-front verdict: Renewables build-out is strategically essential but delivers EPS impact only at the back end of the 24M window; near-term is capex drag, not EPS uplift [#J]

  • Driver definition: TPE-PL's strategic plan targets significant wind and solar capacity additions (est. 1.6-2.0 GW renewables by 2030 vs ~0.5 GW currently); green capex is the primary vehicle for earnings re-mix away from coal [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: New renewable capacity generates contracted revenue (Contracts for Difference / CfD auctions in Poland, plus merchant exposure); as assets commission, EBITDA per GW adds to group earnings; EPS materialises on commissioning, not on announcement [#J]

  • Mechanism to SP: Renewables pipeline re-rates the stock toward cleaner utility peers; market often applies forward-looking multiple to pipeline even before commissioning; sentiment uplift precedes EPS [#J]

  • Company guide: Strategic plan 2025-2030 targets PLN 14-18bn capex; renewables majority; management guides for renewables contributing 30-40% of generation EBITDA by 2030 vs single-digit percentage today [#A]

  • Management commentary: Management has announced wind project acquisitions and solar pipeline development; tone optimistic on CfD auction participation; acknowledged permitting delays in H1 2024 [#A]

  • Track record: Renewables execution has been slower than initial targets; 2022-24 renewables capacity additions below strategic plan milestones; permitting and grid-connection backlogs are the primary bottleneck [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Management guidance on renewables timeline is optimistic relative to Polish permitting realities; 12-18 month slippage is the baseline expectation for sector peers [#A]

  • SS consensus: Pekao IB and PKO BP Equity model renewables contribution cautiously — 15-25% discount to management's 2030 target; concern centres on execution pace and CfD pricing [#A]

  • Consensus changes: L12M: renewables contribution estimates pushed back 12-18 months in consensus models; capex burn expected to peak 2025-27 before EBITDA uplift [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: 1 GW incremental renewable capacity at PLN 350-450k EBITDA/MW = PLN 350-450m incremental EBITDA; approximately 8-12% of group EBITDA if delivered by 2027 [#A]

  • Recent delivery: 2024 renewables EBITDA estimated at PLN 200-300m — growing but modest relative to group; wind additions ahead of solar but below plan; hydro stable [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: Renewables EBITDA CAGR est. +15-20% from small base 2021-24; absolute contribution remains low as a percentage of group [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: European integrated utilities (Iberdrola, Orsted) derive 40-60% of EBITDA from renewables after decade-long build-out; TPE-PL is 5-10 years behind this trajectory — comparable to CEZ (Czech) or Verbund (Austria) at earlier stages [#A] (rough analogy; peer data not verified)

  • Macro tailwind: Polish RES auction programme continues; EU taxonomy financing availability for green capex; falling wind and solar equipment costs improve IRR on new projects [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Polish permitting remains slow (2-4 year timeline for wind onshore); grid-connection queues are extended; inflation increased construction costs materially in 2022-24 [#A]

  • Visibility: Low-medium — project pipeline announced but permitting and CfD award timing are uncertain; grid connections add 12-18 month uncertainty [#A]

  • Bear case: Permitting delays push major renewables commissioning beyond 2027; capex is spent but EBITDA lag means 3-year period of earnings dilution; coal continues dragging during this gap [#J]

  • Miss scenario: CfD auction clearing prices fall below IRR threshold; TPE-PL declines projects; renewables pipeline growth stalls; re-rating catalyst evaporates [#J]

  • Miss magnitude: 8-12% group EBITDA miss vs plan; more importantly, multiple expansion that market may have priced in reverses; 15-25% SP downside from sentiment shift [#A]

  • Red flags: H1 2024 management commentary acknowledged permitting delays — this is the most common cause of renewables capex slippage in Polish utilities sector [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: What if Polish CfD auction volumes are insufficient to absorb TPE-PL's planned renewables pipeline, forcing merchant exposure on new capacity precisely when European wholesale prices are under pressure from renewables oversaturation? [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — renewables trajectory is directionally right but 24M EPS impact is limited by execution pace; this is a 2027-2030 driver, not a 2025-2026 driver

§6 — Key Driver #5: Balance-Sheet Deleveraging and Refinancing

  • J-front verdict: Leverage reduction is a necessary condition for multiple re-rating but is not an independent EPS driver; the path depends on coal-exit clarity and distribution tariff cash generation [#J]

  • Driver definition: TPE-PL carries significant net debt (est. PLN 8-12bn range, ~3-4x net debt/EBITDA); deleveraging to ~2.5x would support credit re-rating and reduce interest burden; equity investors price the deleveraging path as a re-rating catalyst [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Lower interest burden directly adds to EPS; credit-rating upgrade reduces refinancing spreads; both effects are moderate in absolute terms but signal financial health [#J]

  • Mechanism to SP: Market typically re-rates regulated utilities toward peer multiples as leverage declines to investment-grade benchmark; PE multiple expansion is the primary SP mechanism [#J]

  • Company guide: Management targets Net Debt/EBITDA below 3.0x by 2026-27 in strategic plan; capex programme is self-funded via operating cash flows per management guidance [#A]

  • Management commentary: CFO commentary in 2024 results noted capex programme is stretching balance sheet; bond market access assumed for refinancing PLN 2-3bn of near-term maturities; manageable but not comfortable [#A]

  • Track record: Leverage has remained elevated vs guidance targets in prior cycles; capex overruns and coal losses have repeatedly delayed deleveraging milestones [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Management leverage targets lean optimistic; dependent on distribution cash generation AND coal drag reducing AND no major write-down events — three conditions simultaneously [#A]

  • SS consensus: Brokers model leverage declining gradually to 2.8-3.2x by end-2026; most flag coal-exit crystallisation as the swing factor; Erste Group has flagged refinancing risk on near-term bond maturities [#A]

  • Consensus changes: L6M: leverage trajectory revised slightly worse as capex execution picked up pace; free cash flow estimates slightly below plan [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: 1% reduction in average borrowing cost on PLN 10bn debt = PLN 100m interest saving = ~5-8% EPS uplift; multiple expansion from 3x to 4x EV/EBITDA (peer re-rating) = 30-40% implied SP uplift [#A]

  • Recent delivery: 2024 net debt broadly stable at elevated levels; no material deleveraging event; refinancing successfully executed in 2023 at higher spreads than pre-2022 [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: Net Debt/EBITDA has oscillated 3.0-4.2x over 2021-24; no sustained deleveraging trend visible [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: European regulated utility median Net Debt/EBITDA ~2.5-3.0x (Innogy/E.ON, ENEA); CEE utilities average ~2.8x; TPE-PL is at the high end of this range [#A] (peer figures from public data; not AlphaSense verified)

  • Macro tailwind: Higher EBITDA from tariff reset and capacity market accelerates deleveraging path without equity issuance [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Elevated PLN interest rates (NBP rate ~5-6% range in 2024-25) increase refinancing cost; coal write-down would immediately spike leverage ratio [#A]

  • Visibility: Medium — leverage path is mechanically modelable but depends on outcomes of Drivers 1, 2, and 3 above [#A]

  • Bear case: Coal write-down spikes leverage to 4.5-5.0x; credit agencies downgrade to sub-investment-grade; refinancing costs spike; equity issuance dilutes EPS; virtuous deleveraging cycle reverses [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Management guided 3.0x by 2026-27 misses; market re-prices debt and equity; SP decline disproportionate to EBITDA miss because leverage compounds investor concern [#J]

  • Miss magnitude: Leverage re-rating from investment grade to high-yield territory could compress EV multiples 15-25%; combined with equity dilution, 30-40% SP downside is plausible [#A]

  • Red flags: Refinancing dependency on bond market at elevated rates is a structural fragility; no visible FCF inflection that would reduce this dependency in near term [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: What if the deleveraging thesis is simply circular — leverage only declines if Drivers 1, 2, and 3 all deliver, but Drivers 1-3 are precisely the uncertain ones? Deleveraging may be less an independent driver and more a lagging indicator of everything else going right [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — deleveraging achievable in benign scenario but dependent on multiple upstream drivers; any coal-exit disruption breaks the path

§7 — Key Driver #6: Operational Efficiency and OPEX Reduction

  • J-front verdict: OPEX efficiency is a supporting, not fulcrum, driver; modest 5-8% EBIT uplift potential but structurally constrained by labour agreements and Polish wage inflation [#J]

  • Driver definition: TPE-PL employs ~25,000 people across generation, distribution, and retail segments; labour costs are the primary OPEX component; efficiency improvements (headcount, procurement, IT digitalisation) flow to EBIT [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: PLN 100m OPEX reduction on PLN ~3bn OPEX base = 3% OPEX cut = ~5% group EBIT uplift (pre-tax); modest but positive [#J]

  • Mechanism to SP: Efficiency delivery signals management competence; validates strategic narrative; modest de-rating of the operating-risk premium [#J]

  • Company guide: Strategic plan targets PLN 300-500m cumulative OPEX savings by 2030; annual run-rate improvement of PLN 50-100m guided; FTE reduction programmes underway [#A]

  • Management commentary: Management has cited procurement savings and IT modernisation as primary levers; acknowledged that collective labour agreement constraints limit speed of headcount reduction [#A]

  • Track record: Historical OPEX reduction delivery has been partial; previous efficiency programmes delivered ~50-60% of guided savings; labour agreement renewal cycles have reset cost bases upward [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Guidance is moderate-optimistic; underlying Polish wage inflation (est. 8-12% CAGR 2022-24) is the dominant counter-force; real OPEX reduction is harder than nominal [#A]

  • SS consensus: Brokers treat OPEX efficiency as a second-order consideration; not a primary driver in sell-side price targets; modelled at 50% delivery of management guidance [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: PLN 200m annual run-rate savings (50% delivery of guidance) at 19% CIT = PLN 162m post-tax = ~4-6% EPS uplift; not a needle-mover in isolation [#A]

  • Recent delivery: 2024 reported OPEX slightly above inflation-adjusted 2023 levels; no visible efficiency delivery; Polish wage pressure dominated [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: Real OPEX has been flat to mildly rising; efficiency programmes have not offset wage inflation; structural headcount reduction is slow [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: European utility OPEX efficiency programmes (E.ON, EDF, Enel) typically deliver 3-5% annual OPEX reductions with full-programme execution; Polish utility context is harder given labour-agreement density [#A] (peer figure: public data)

  • Macro tailwind: Polish labour market tightening may reverse post-2025 as manufacturing softness reduces competing demand for energy-sector workers [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Minimum wage increases mandated by Polish government (est. +15% in 2024-25) directly inflate TPE-PL's labour cost base [#A]

  • Visibility: Low-medium — OPEX delivery depends on labour negotiation outcomes and procurement cycle timing [#A]

  • Bear case: Wage inflation accelerates; collective agreements lock in multi-year cost increases; digitalisation investment is capex-intensive before OPEX savings materialise; efficiency gains offset by regulatory OPEX efficiency requirements [#J]

  • Miss scenario: OPEX grows 5-8% annually vs guided flat/declining; 3-5% EBIT headwind; immaterial in isolation but compounds other driver misses [#J]

  • Miss magnitude: OPEX miss alone: 3-5% EBIT headwind; not case-breaking but reduces margin for error across the portfolio of drivers [#A]
