  • Who uses this output: APM uses the ACH matrix to inform the pillar-level grade; the grade drives position sizing
  • What "done" looks like: a verbal verdict ("high / medium / low plausibility") on the leading hypothesis, ≥3 monitoring triggers, and a stewing recommendation — not a balanced overview

Competing Hypotheses — Enumerated

Per Q22-HE gate: ≥3, mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive, each stated as a falsifiable claim.

Structural note: the three hypotheses below are the canonical archetypes for a durability-of-performance KQ — the most common load-bearing Q22 type in concentrated equity DD. Analyst to restate each with the specific company and metric substituted in.

Hypothesis A — Structural / Durable:
- Claim: The performance metric under examination (margin, revenue growth, FCF conversion, ROIC) reflects durable structural advantages that will persist through the next business cycle
- Falsifiable condition: if the metric deteriorates by more than [X]pp in the next downturn relative to the prior cycle, Hypothesis A is falsified
- Causal mechanism preview: proprietary position / switching costs / network effects → pricing power → margin persistence regardless of volume

Hypothesis B — Cyclical / Mix-Artefact:
- Claim: The performance metric reflects a favourable cyclical position, mix shift, or one-time cost benefit that will mean-revert as conditions normalise
- Falsifiable condition: if the metric holds through the next volume softening or input-cost cycle, Hypothesis B is falsified
- Causal mechanism preview: volume leverage → cost absorption → margin expansion that does not survive volume reversal

Hypothesis C — Execution / Management-Driven (Transient):
- Claim: The performance metric reflects identifiable management actions (restructuring, pricing discipline, cost programme) that are time-limited and will not be replicated once the one-off driver exhausts
- Falsifiable condition: if margin holds flat or improves two years after the restructuring programme closes, Hypothesis C is falsified
- Causal mechanism preview: management action → one-cycle improvement → regression to sector mean once programme exhausted

Hypothesis D — Null / Neither (Measurement Artefact):
- Claim: The performance metric is not reflecting genuine economics — it is an accounting / reporting artefact (capitalisation policy, pension smoothing, FX translation, revenue recognition timing) with no durable cash analogue
- Falsifiable condition: if FCF conversion tracks reported margin improvement closely over ≥3 years, Hypothesis D is falsified
- Causal mechanism preview: accounting policy choice → reported improvement → cash does not follow → KQ is about the wrong metric

Sceptical bullet: The runner-up hypothesis in most equity DD cases is Hypothesis B (cyclical artefact) — it accumulates disconfirming evidence when: (a) the metric holds through a period of volume decline, (b) competitors without the same reported improvement show margin compression, and (c) management guidance is forward-based not backward-looking; the analyst should pre-commit to testing all three before declaring Hypothesis A the winner

§2 — Evidence Catalogue (Organised by Source Incentive)

Source-Incentive Distribution Assessment

  • Source category: management / IR-sourced disclosures — earnings calls, investor day transcripts, annual reports
  • Incentive lens: management is incentivised to amplify structural narratives, suppress cyclical-artefact interpretations, and omit measurement-artefact flags; treat as aligned-source, high-volume, low-adversarial weight

  • Confidence modifier: high factual accuracy on disclosed numbers; low reliability on causal attribution

  • Source category: audited financial statements

  • Incentive lens: auditors are incentivised to sign off on conforming statements; accounting policy choices that benefit reported metrics are within GAAP and will not be flagged; cash-flow statement cross-check is the adversarial tool here

  • Confidence modifier: high reliability on reported figures; medium reliability on economic-reality translation

  • Source category: sell-side (bull notes)

  • Incentive lens: sell-side analysts with buy ratings are incentivised to find structural narratives; their models often build in management guidance on margin trajectory; treat as aligned-source, useful for bull-case mechanism articulation, not for disconfirmation

  • Confidence modifier: medium on mechanism; low on disconfirmation

  • Source category: sell-side (bear notes / downgrades) — LOAD-BEARING per Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 1

  • Incentive lens: sell-side analysts downgrading are incentivised (by reputational risk) to be specific about what will go wrong; bear notes are the highest-quality adversarial source in the public domain short of short-seller reports

  • Confidence modifier: high on disconfirmation mechanism; medium on timing

  • Source category: competitor disclosures

  • Incentive lens: competitors are incentivised to disclose their own performance without reference to Stock X; cross-referencing sector margin trends tests Hypothesis B (cyclical artefact)

  • Confidence modifier: high for sector-trend triangulation; not directly applicable to Stock X causal attribution

  • Source category: short-seller reports (where available)

  • Incentive lens: short-sellers are incentivised to identify accounting artefacts and cyclical vulnerabilities; the highest-quality adversarial source in the public domain for Hypothesis D (measurement artefact) and Hypothesis C (execution transience)
  • Confidence modifier: high on mechanism specificity; must be cross-checked for motivated reasoning on timing

Evidence Items — Structural Catalogue

Per Q22-IL gate: source-incentive lens applied per item. ≥15 items required. Items 1–15 are the structural placeholders at full operational depth — analyst populates specifics.

E1 — Reported margin trend [3-5 years]
- Evidence: margin trajectory over the observable window
- Source: audited financial statements
- Incentive lens: factual; no directional incentive on reported figures
- Confidence: high
- Diagnostic relevance: tests whether improvement is real and consistent (discriminates A vs B vs C)

E2 — FCF conversion vs reported margin
- Evidence: free cash flow as % of reported earnings or EBIT
- Source: cash flow statement (audited)
- Incentive lens: cash flow statement is harder to manage than P&L; divergence between reported margin improvement and FCF conversion is the primary Hypothesis D signal
- Confidence: high
- Diagnostic relevance: primary Hypothesis D test — if FCF tracks, D weakens; if FCF lags, D strengthens ⚡

E3 — Revenue and volume decomposition
- Evidence: price vs volume vs mix contribution to revenue growth
- Source: segmental disclosure / management commentary
- Incentive lens: management may be incentivised to emphasise mix/price (structural narrative) over volume; cross-check with units shipped / capacity utilisation where available
- Confidence: medium (dependent on disclosure granularity)
- Diagnostic relevance: tests Hypothesis B (volume leverage artefact)

E4 — Input cost and gross margin bridge
- Evidence: gross margin change decomposed into input cost tailwind vs pricing vs efficiency
- Source: earnings call commentary / broker margin bridge models
- Incentive lens: management will present the bridge favourably; broker bridges are more neutral
- Confidence: medium
- Diagnostic relevance: tests Hypothesis B directly — if gross margin improvement is substantially input-cost driven, B is strengthened

E5 — Restructuring / cost programme timeline and annualised savings
- Evidence: announced programme, savings quantum, and completion timeline
- Source: management / regulatory filings
- Incentive lens: management will disclose savings claims; independent audit of run-rate realisation is rarely available publicly
- Confidence: medium
- Diagnostic relevance: tests Hypothesis C — if programme is substantially complete and margin is holding, C weakens

E6 — ROIC vs WACC trend
- Evidence: return on invested capital trajectory relative to cost of capital
- Source: financial statements (derived)
- Incentive lens: neutral — calculated metric
- Confidence: high (given correct WACC assumption)
- Diagnostic relevance: if ROIC is structurally above WACC and widening, Hypothesis A is supported; if ROIC improvement tracks restructuring timeline, C is supported

E7 — Competitive position data — market share proxies
- Evidence: revenue growth relative to addressable market; customer win/loss data (if disclosed)
- Source: management commentary / industry reports / competitor disclosures
- Incentive lens: management will claim share gains; cross-check with competitor disclosures
- Confidence: low-medium (depends on disclosure availability)
- Diagnostic relevance: structural competitive position vs cyclical share is a key discriminator

E8 — Pricing power evidence
- Evidence: realised price increases vs input inflation over ≥2 cycles; price-mix contribution in revenue bridge
- Source: management disclosure / earnings call
- Incentive lens: management will emphasise pricing power; base-rate check — what % of sector peers claim similar pricing power? [PEER-GAP — no cohort data]
- Confidence: medium
- Diagnostic relevance: if pricing power is demonstrated through a volume softening period, Hypothesis A is strengthened

E9 — Customer concentration and contract structure
- Evidence: top customer % of revenue; contract length and price reset mechanism
- Source: annual report / segmental disclosure
- Incentive lens: neutral — disclosed in filings
- Confidence: high
- Diagnostic relevance: long-term contracts with inflation linkage support A; spot-priced, concentrated customers support B or C

E10 — Capex intensity and maintenance vs growth capex split
- Evidence: capex as % of revenue; disclosed split between maintenance and growth
- Source: financial statements / management commentary
- Incentive lens: management may under-disclose maintenance capex to inflate FCF optics
- Confidence: medium
- Diagnostic relevance: if capex intensity is rising while margins improve, sustainable A is more plausible; if capex is suppressed, Hypothesis C or D becomes more plausible (harvesting)

E11 — Balance sheet capacity and leverage trend
- Evidence: net debt / EBITDA trajectory; interest cover; refinancing schedule
- Source: audited financial statements
- Incentive lens: neutral — disclosed
- Confidence: high
- Diagnostic relevance: rising leverage during reported improvement is a Hypothesis D / C signal (financial engineering rather than operational durability)

E12 — Accounting policy changes over the observation window
- Evidence: changes to revenue recognition, capitalisation policies, pension assumptions, or depreciation lives coinciding with reported improvement
- Source: audit notes / accounting policy disclosures (annual report)
- Incentive lens: auditors do not flag permitted policy changes as problems; analyst must read the notes independently
- Confidence: high if policies have been stable; low if changes coincide with improvement
- Diagnostic relevance: primary Hypothesis D discriminator — policy changes that mechanically improve reported metrics without cash benefit are the canonical D signal ⚡

E13 — Management tenure and incentive structure
- Evidence: CEO/CFO tenure; long-term incentive plan metrics (what performance measures drive LTI vesting?)
- Source: remuneration report / annual report
- Incentive lens: management is incentivised to deliver LTI metrics; if LTI is EPS-based rather than FCF/ROIC-based, Hypothesis C (managed EPS) or D (accounting artefact) is more plausible
- Confidence: high
- Diagnostic relevance: tests M/M/O for Hypothesis C and D

E14 — Analyst consensus revision trend
- Evidence: direction of EPS revision over the past 12 months; revision dispersion (bull/bear spread)
- Source: sell-side consensus (Bloomberg / Visible Alpha proxy)
- Incentive lens: consensus revisions are an aggregated aligned-source signal; they reflect the bull-case narrative; high revision dispersion is a diagnostic signal of hypothesis uncertainty
- Confidence: medium
- Diagnostic relevance: a narrowing bull/bear spread combined with consistent upward revision supports A; high dispersion is a composite-truth signal for §5

E15 — Historical through-cycle margin performance (prior downturn)
- Evidence: how margin behaved in the prior volume downturn (e.g., 2015-16, 2020, or sector-specific cycle)
- Source: historical financial statements
- Incentive lens: neutral — historical data
- Confidence: high (if data available)
- Diagnostic relevance: most powerful single test of Hypothesis A vs B — a company that held margin through the prior downturn has demonstrated the A mechanism empirically; one that did not has not

Sceptical bullet: The evidence catalogue as constructed is [C]-dominated and public-filing-driven; the most diagnostic evidence items — expert calls on customer relationships (E9 depth), independent cost programme verification (E5 depth), and accounting-policy analysis by forensic accounting specialists (E12 depth) — are not available in this run; any ACH winner declared without [AS] sourcing is provisional and should not be used for position sizing

ACH Matrix — Interpretation Principles

  • Diagnostic item priority: E2, E12, E15 are the three highest-priority diagnostic items; if the analyst can only populate three items before a stewing checkpoint, populate these three first
  • Counting rule: the ACH winner is the hypothesis with the LOWEST count of − entries; ties go to §5 (multiple-truths synthesis)
  • Hypothesis D treatment: D is structurally the hardest to rule out because the evidence items that disconfirm it (E2, E12) require accounting-level forensics; a positive (non-artefact) verdict on D is not achievable without detailed note inspection
  • Hypothesis A confirmation trap: the most common ACH error in equity DD is prematurely accepting Hypothesis A because the reported-metric evidence all looks positive; E15 (through-cycle test) and E2 (FCF conversion) are the specific gates that Hypothesis A must pass through — not just reported trends
  • Close-call signal: if N_A and N_B are within 2 disconfirming-evidence counts of each other, the answer is likely composite (§5 applicable); the analyst should not force a clean A or B verdict
  • Diagnostic items not populated = monitoring triggers: any diagnostic item that cannot be populated with current evidence automatically becomes a monitoring trigger in §6; this is not a failure — it is the output of the ACH discipline
  • Incentive lens effect on matrix: evidence items from aligned sources (E1 management commentary, E3 management bridge) carry lower weight when they produce + for Hypothesis A; they carry equal weight when they produce −, because aligned sources have no incentive to disconfirm their own narrative
  • Bear-note check: before finalising the matrix, the analyst should apply Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 1 — take the most recent bear note or short-seller report and ask which cells it would change; if it changes ≥3 cells, the matrix is not stable and a stewing period applies

§4 — Per-Hypothesis Assessment (incl. M/M/O Test)

J-front verdict bullet: the hypothesis with the strongest causal architecture — where Means, Motive, and Opportunity are all aligned for the posited actor — is determined by stock-specific evidence; structural M/M/O templates delivered here for all four hypotheses

Hypothesis A — Structural / Durable

Causal mechanism: proprietary competitive position (brand, switching costs, network effects, regulatory moat) → sustainable pricing power → margin floor that survives volume and input-cost cycles → predictable earnings trajectory

Means / Motive / Opportunity test:
- Means: company possesses a structural differentiation asset that competitors cannot easily replicate (proprietary technology, regulatory licence, installed base, brand premium) — analyst must verify this claim specifically; it is not sufficient to assert it from management commentary
- Motive: not applicable in the A/B/C/D framework — Hypothesis A is about structural conditions, not actor intent; M/M/O is most relevant for C and D
- Opportunity: the competitive structure of the market must permit margin sustainability (oligopoly / duopoly / protected niche); in a fragmented market with low barriers to entry, A is inherently weak regardless of current metrics
- Verdict gate: A requires all three: confirmed differentiation asset (Means), market structure permitting pricing power (Opportunity), and through-cycle evidence (E15 empirical test); missing any one of these three should lower A's plausibility

Strongest supporting evidence (structural):
- Long-term contracts with inflation linkage and high customer switching costs would directly support A
- ROIC structurally above WACC over ≥5 years without deterioration is the cleanest A signal
- Margin holding or expanding in the prior volume downturn (E15 positive) is the empirical proof point

Strongest disconfirming evidence:
- FCF conversion lagging reported margin improvement (E2 negative) undermines the cash reality of A
- Revenue decomposition showing the improvement is mix/price in a favourable cycle (E3/E4 negative) shifts weight to B
- New competitor entry or customer renegotiation post-contract expiry would structurally disconfirm A

Verbal verdict [structure-dependent]: high plausibility IF through-cycle evidence (E15) and FCF conversion (E2) both support; medium plausibility if E15 is absent (company untested); low plausibility if E2 and E3 both point to cyclical artefact

Hypothesis B — Cyclical / Mix-Artefact

Causal mechanism: volume cycle enters favourable phase → operating leverage drives margin expansion → input cost cycle (commodity, labour) turns favourable → gross margin improvement → management correctly identifies the tailwind but misattributes it as structural → when cycle turns, margin reverts

Means / Motive / Opportunity test:
- Means: the cycle mechanism requires that the company has meaningful operating leverage (fixed cost base as % of total cost); high variable-cost businesses have less B exposure
- Motive: not the primary M/M/O axis for B (cycle doesn't have intent); the relevant actor is management — they have motive to describe cyclical improvement as structural (supports multiple — LTI, strategic narrative, capital raising)
- Opportunity: B is most powerful when the observable improvement coincides with a sector-wide tailwind; if competitors without the company's specific attributes show similar improvement, B is strongly supported

Strongest supporting evidence:
- Revenue decomposition showing volume leverage as the primary driver (E3 B-positive)
- Gross margin improvement tracking input cost tailwind rather than pricing (E4 B-positive)
- Sector peers showing similar margin improvement in the same period [PEER-GAP — no cohort data available to test this item]

Strongest disconfirming evidence:
- Margin holding through a volume softening period post-improvement (E15 B-negative)
- Price-mix contribution dominating volume leverage in revenue bridge (E3 A-positive / B-negative)
- ROIC improvement preceded the favourable cycle or outlasts it (E6 B-negative)

Verbal verdict [structure-dependent]: medium plausibility as the standing runner-up; becomes high plausibility if E4 (input cost tailwind) and sector-wide margin improvement [PEER-GAP] are both confirmed; low plausibility if through-cycle evidence (E15) is present and positive for A

Hypothesis C — Execution / Management-Driven (Transient)

Causal mechanism: new management team or activist-prompted cost programme → restructuring charge → identified savings (headcount, procurement, footprint) → one-time improvement in operating margin → programme exhausts → incremental improvement stops → without volume growth or pricing power, margin plateaus or reverts as reinvestment requirements emerge

Means / Motive / Opportunity test:
- Means: management has the authority and organisational capability to execute the cost programme; in practice, C is weakest when the initial savings quantum has been delivered and there is no announced second phase
- Motive: management has strong LTI motive to deliver margin improvement; if LTI is EPS-based (E13 data), the incentive to execute C is explicit; management will also have motive to describe C as A (strategic narrative)
- Opportunity: C requires that costs were structurally bloated pre-programme; in a business that was already lean, the programme savings will be small and C will be a partial hypothesis at best

Strongest supporting evidence:
- Margin improvement timing coincides precisely with announced restructuring programme
- Savings quantum disclosed vs the observed margin improvement match closely (E5 C-positive)
- No evidence of structural competitive position improvement concurrent with the programme (E7 neutral / negative for A)

Strongest disconfirming evidence:
- Margin improving beyond the announced savings quantum (improvement exceeds programme — A or B must also be present)
- FCF conversion tracking reported improvement (E2 C-negative — genuine cash improvement reduces D overlap but does not eliminate C)
- Management announcing a second-phase programme before the first is exhausted (signals structural improvement, not C)

Verbal verdict [structure-dependent]: medium plausibility in the early post-programme period; shifts to low plausibility once the programme is confirmed complete and margin has held ≥2 subsequent years; holds at medium in the liminal period where programme is complete but hold-through is unconfirmed

Hypothesis D — Null / Measurement Artefact

Causal mechanism: accounting policy choice (revenue recognition, capitalisation of previously expensed items, pension assumption change, depreciation life extension) → reported metric improves → no cash equivalent → FCF diverges from reported earnings → analyst models on reported numbers → margin forecast appears durable but will normalise at the next audit cycle or policy reversal

Means / Motive / Opportunity test:
- Means: management has the means to make accounting policy changes within GAAP; auditors permit changes that do not violate standards; the means are structural within the reporting framework
- Motive: management motive is explicit where LTI is EPS-based (E13) or where the company is preparing for a capital raise, refinancing, or M&A transaction
- Opportunity: the opportunity is highest when there is a change in external auditor, a change in CFO, or a period of rapid expansion / acquisition; these events correlate with accounting policy changes in the academic literature

Strongest supporting evidence:
- FCF conversion lagging reported margin improvement (E2 D-positive — primary signal)
- Accounting policy changes coinciding with the improvement period (E12 D-positive — primary signal)
- Rising capitalisation rates or extending depreciation lives in the annual report notes

Strongest disconfirming evidence:
- FCF tracking reported improvement closely over ≥3 years (E2 D-negative)
- No accounting policy changes in the improvement period (E12 D-negative)
- Clean short-seller / forensic accounting opinion on the reporting quality [adversarial source — not available in [C] run]

Verbal verdict [structure-dependent]: low plausibility as the base case (most reported margin improvements are not accounting artefacts); elevated to medium plausibility if E2 and E12 both point to it; high plausibility only if a forensic accounting analysis has confirmed it — a [C]-only run cannot reach high plausibility on D

Sceptical bullet: the hypothesis the analyst is most likely flinching from at DD stage is Hypothesis D — it is uncomfortable because it implies the entire analytical framework has been applied to the wrong metric; per the obstacle is the way (Concept B / Marcus Aurelius), the analyst must explicitly read the accounting policy notes and cross-check FCF before declaring D low plausibility; a single analyst reading of the notes is not sufficient — this is the specific item where an expert call with a forensic accountant is load-bearing

§5 — Multiple-Truths-Coexist Synthesis

J-front verdict bullet: most real equity cases at DD stage resolve as composite truths between Hypothesis A and B, or A and C; the synthesis question is which conditions favour which partial truth

Composite Architecture — A + B (Most Common Case)

Elephant view: the most likely composite truth in a well-run industrial or consumer company is that structural advantages are real but their magnitude is currently inflated by a cyclical tailwind ; the structural advantage is worth paying for, but not at the price implied by the cyclical peak

Conditional truth:
- Hypothesis A partial truth: pricing power, switching costs, and ROIC above WACC are real and durable; the floor margin (through-cycle floor, not peak margin) is structurally higher than the pre-improvement period
- Hypothesis B partial truth: the current margin level reflects a cyclical increment above the structural floor; mean-reversion risk is on the cyclical component, not the structural component
- Practical implication: the analyst should model on the through-cycle structural floor margin , not the current reported margin (A+B); the difference is the cyclical overhang that will compress in the next downturn

Time-frame split: over 3-5 years, A dominates if the structural advantages are confirmed; over 12-18 months, B may dominate as the cycle turns; the holding period of 12-24 months means the analyst must price both

Segment split: in multi-segment businesses, A may dominate in some segments (high switching costs, long contracts) and B in others (commodity-adjacent, spot-priced); blended reported margin conceals this; segment-level decomposition is the correct analytical unit

Scenario split: the composite truth becomes a single truth under two scenarios:
- Scenario 1 (cycle turns hard): B dominates in the near term, margin compresses, but structural floor holds; A is confirmed at the trough
- Scenario 2 (structural erosion from new entrant): A loses, B fully explains the current level, margin reverts to sector mean or below

Composite Architecture — A + C (Second Most Common)

Six-blind-men synthesis: in post-restructuring businesses, management-driven improvement and structural advantage coexist; the restructuring has removed structural bloat that was obscuring the underlying structural advantage; C is the mechanism, A is the destination — C gets you to the A state, but only if the underlying structural advantages were always there

Conditional truth:
