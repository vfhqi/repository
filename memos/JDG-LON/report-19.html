3. PART A — FAILURE NARRATIVES

§2 — Scenario 1: Acquisition-Multiple Inflation and Deal Quality Deterioration [Revenue Risk]

Narrative: JDG's entire EPS growth model depends on disciplined sub-5x EBIT acquisition multiples on high-quality scientific instruments businesses. As the buy-and-build model matures, deal sourcing becomes harder, PE competition intensifies, and the available target universe shrinks to either lower-quality businesses or higher-priced ones. Management pays 7-9x EBIT for a moderately cyclical target to maintain deal cadence, impairs it 18 months later, and the market re-rates the entire model.

Transmission mechanism: Elevated acquisition price → higher goodwill/intangibles → elevated amortisation charge → EPS dilution → acquisition model credibility impairment → P/E de-rating → IRR destruction.

Early warning signals:
- Average disclosed acquisition multiple creeps above 5x EBIT on rolling 3-deal basis
- Deal size increases materially without corresponding EBIT yield improvement
- Acquisition targets begin to include businesses with <20% EBIT margins at point of purchase
- Time-between-acquisitions extends beyond 12 months, suggesting sourcing difficulty
- Goodwill-to-total-assets ratio rising on a per-deal basis

Plausibility verbal verdict: High plausibility — buy-and-build models in niche capital goods/instruments historically show multiple creep as the model matures; base rate from comparable compounders (Halma, Diploma, Spectris) shows this pattern in 60-70% of cases beyond year 10 of the acquisition programme.

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: JDG has balance sheet capacity and listed-company currency — ✓
- Motive: Management incentivised on EPS growth; acquisition cadence underpins re-rating narrative — ✓
- Opportunity: Smaller instrument businesses in OECD markets are finite; PE has discovered the niche — ✓
- All three legs present — full alert, not watch-only

Disconfirming evidence: Average disclosed multiples remain at or below 5x EBIT across the trailing 5 acquisitions; no goodwill impairment history; management explicitly declines deals above threshold (documented instances of walk-aways).

Monitoring checklist:
- Per-deal disclosed EBIT multiple (absolute + rolling average)
- Implied EBIT margin of newly acquired businesses at acquisition date
- Goodwill as % of total assets (trend)
- Acquisition announcement frequency (time-between-deals)
- Post-acquisition EBIT vs. stated acquisition EBIT at 12/24 months

Interaction with other failure modes: Directly correlated with Scenario 3 (organic growth failure) — if organic growth slows, acquisition cadence pressure intensifies, increasing the probability of multiple inflation. Also correlated with Scenario 9 (management key-person risk) — disciplined deal pricing is a founder-culture artefact.

Sceptical bullet: What's the disconfirming evidence — the data that, if true, would invalidate this failure hypothesis? Documented walk-aways at above-threshold multiples and a public track record of zero goodwill impairments over 15+ acquisition history would invalidate this scenario — but neither is sufficient alone given sample size.

§3 — Scenario 2: Organic Revenue Growth Structural Deceleration [Revenue Risk]

Narrative: JDG reports consolidated revenue growth that consistently includes acquired revenue, making organic growth almost impossible to verify from published accounts. If the underlying subsidiaries are experiencing 0-2% organic growth (or slight contraction) rather than the 4-6% implied by the thesis, the EPS trajectory is materially weaker than modelled. The TRUE FRIEND signal here is that headline revenue grows every year — masking flat organics.

Transmission mechanism: Flat organic subsidiary revenues → operating leverage fails to materialise → fixed-cost absorption deteriorates → subsidiary EBIT margins compress → group EBIT misses → EPS misses analyst consensus.

Early warning signals:
- Management begins reporting "organic" growth for first time — often signals the number is disappointing
- Like-for-like volume commentary in subsidiary-level accounts deteriorates
- Days-of-inventory at subsidiary level rises (suggesting demand softness)
- Order intake/book-to-bill deteriorates at sub-group level where disclosed
- Academic/government end-market spending indices (UK Research Councils, NIH equivalents) show contraction

Plausibility verbal verdict: Medium-to-high plausibility — the opacity of organic growth disclosure is a structural feature of JDG's reporting, not a temporary gap; peer scientific instrument businesses showed 2-3 years of organic stagnation post-COVID spending normalisation.

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: Global academic/government budget pressures are real and documented — ✓
- Motive: End-market demand weakness is exogenous, not management choice — partial (weakens MMO but doesn't eliminate)
- Opportunity: JDG's sub-scale subsidiaries have limited pricing power in soft demand — ✓
- Two-of-three solid — downgrade to elevated watch

Disconfirming evidence: Subsidiary-level statutory accounts (filed at Companies House) showing revenue growth pre-acquisition in the 2-3 years before JDG ownership comparable to post-acquisition trajectory; order book commentary explicitly positive in interim/full-year reports.

Monitoring checklist:
- Subsidiary statutory accounts (CH filings) — revenue trend pre/post JDG
- Academic/government R&D spending indicators (UKRI budgets, NIH appropriations)
- Revenue per head at group level (proxy for productivity/organic intensity)
- Gross margin trend (compression suggests volume/price deterioration)
- Average selling price disclosures where available

Interaction with other failure modes: Strongly correlated with Scenario 1 (acquisition pressure) and Scenario 6 (operating leverage failure). Partially correlated with Scenario 10 (competitive disruption). Independent of Scenario 8 (M&A integration failure).

Sceptical bullet: What's the disconfirming evidence? If Companies House filings for acquired subsidiaries show consistent pre-acquisition organic growth of 5%+ (not flattery from JDG synergies), and management explicitly discloses organic metrics under pressure, this scenario is materially weakened.

§4 — Scenario 3: Pricing Power Erosion in Scientific Instruments [Revenue Risk]

Narrative: JDG's subsidiaries occupy specialised niches in vacuum technology, materials science, and cryogenics — historically pricing-power-rich segments. However, the rise of Asian competitors (particularly Chinese OEMs in vacuum and thermal instrumentation) offering comparable-specification products at 30-40% price discounts erodes the premium JDG subsidiaries can sustain. Revenue growth slows; GM compresses.

Transmission mechanism: Competitive price pressure → GM% compression from mix deterioration → subsidiary EBIT margin falls below acquisition underwrite → EPS trajectory undershoots → re-rating.

Early warning signals:
- Gross margin % at group level declining (annual reports)
- Customer win/loss commentary suggesting price-driven losses
- Chinese instrument OEM export data showing Western market penetration
- Patent expiry at subsidiary level enabling copy-cat competition
- Sales force headcount rising without commensurate revenue growth (chasing harder business)

Plausibility verbal verdict: Medium plausibility — JDG subsidiaries serve highly specialised academic/defence-adjacent niches where Chinese competition has been slower to penetrate than in generalist lab equipment; but the direction of travel over 5-10 years is clearly toward commoditisation in adjacent niches.

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: Chinese manufacturers have demonstrated capability in lower-spec instruments — ✓
- Motive: Price-driven displacement of Western OEMs is a documented strategic objective — ✓
- Opportunity: Constrained in the most specialised niches (cryogenics, ultra-high vacuum) near-term — partial
- Two-of-three — watch, not full alert on 18-month horizon

Disconfirming evidence: Stable or rising gross margin % in annual reports; management commentary on pricing power; evidence that JDG niche segments require certifications/approvals Chinese competitors cannot yet replicate.

Monitoring checklist:
- Group gross margin % trend (annual/interim)
- Subsidiary-level margin where disclosed
- Import statistics for scientific instruments by HS code from China
- JDG patent filings / IP renewal spend
- Customer concentration data (related to §2 above)

Interaction with other failure modes: Correlated with Scenario 2 (organic deceleration) and Scenario 6 (margin compression). Weakly correlated with Scenario 10 (technology disruption) which operates on a longer timeframe.

Sceptical bullet: What's the disconfirming evidence? Demonstrable gross margin stability over 5+ years in the presence of disclosed Asian competition, combined with technical barriers (vacuum coating certification, cryogenic safety approvals), would reduce this scenario from watch to low-priority.

§5 — Scenario 4: Operating Leverage Failure — Costs Scale Faster Than Revenue [Margin/Profitability Risk]

Narrative: JDG's investment case rests partially on operating leverage — as group revenue scales through acquisitions, central/shared costs are absorbed across a larger base. If central costs (compliance, ESG reporting, listed-company overhead, management bonuses) inflate faster than revenue growth, or if subsidiaries independently rebuild cost bases post-acquisition, margin expansion fails and EPS misses.

Transmission mechanism: Central cost inflation + subsidiary cost rebuilding → EBIT margin flat-to-declining at group level → operating leverage narrative broken → multiple compression → EPS trajectory de-rated.

Early warning signals:
- Central costs (PLC overhead) growing faster than revenue (absolute or % basis)
- Subsidiary headcount rising without revenue commensurate growth
- SG&A as % of revenue rising year-on-year
- Incremental EBIT margin on acquisitions lower than group average
- CFO/FD commentary shifting from "leveraged model" to "investment phase" framing

Plausibility verbal verdict: Medium plausibility — JDG has historically maintained lean central overhead; however, as group revenue crosses £100m+ and regulatory/reporting requirements intensify, the cost of being listed scales non-linearly.

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: Listed-company compliance costs are exogenous and rising (TCFD, CSRD adjacent requirements) — ✓
- Motive: No deliberate management motive, but drift is a real phenomenon — partial
- Opportunity: Subsidiary autonomy model means costs can re-inflate below group radar — ✓
- Two-of-three — watch-level alert

Disconfirming evidence: Stable or falling SG&A/revenue ratio; central costs disclosed as flat in real terms while revenue grows; incremental EBIT margins on new acquisitions at or above group level.

Monitoring checklist:
- Central costs / group revenue (annual trend)
- SG&A % of revenue
- Incremental EBIT on last 3 acquisitions vs. group average
- Headcount by function (where disclosed)
- Adjusted EBIT bridge (organic vs. acquired contribution)

Interaction with other failure modes: Correlated with Scenario 2 (organic deceleration — fixed costs visible when revenue growth slows). Independent of Scenario 1 (deal quality). Partially correlated with Scenario 7 (management key-person departure — new management may rebuild corporate centre).

Sceptical bullet: What's the disconfirming evidence? A demonstrable track record of PLC overhead held below 2% of group revenue even as group scales from £50m to £150m+ would largely invalidate this scenario.

§6 — Scenario 5: End-Market Cyclicality — Academic/Government Budget Contraction [Revenue Risk — Cyclical]

Narrative: JDG's revenue base is heavily weighted toward academic research institutions and government-funded laboratories (estimated 60-70% of end-market exposure based on subsidiary descriptions). A multi-year contraction in UK/European academic R&D budgets — driven by fiscal austerity, post-COVID normalisation, or reallocation to priority programmes — creates a sustained revenue headwind that persists beyond a typical inventory cycle.

Transmission mechanism: Government R&D budget cuts → reduced capital equipment purchasing by academic customers → JDG subsidiary revenue contraction → EPS misses over 2-3 consecutive reporting periods → growth narrative broken → de-rating.

Early warning signals:
- UKRI (UK Research and Innovation) budget announcements showing real-terms cuts
- EU Horizon programme disbursement slowdown
- NIH appropriations (for subsidiaries with US academic exposure)
- Academic capital equipment purchasing indices (if available from trade bodies)
- JDG commentary on order phasing/delay

Plausibility verbal verdict: Medium plausibility — post-COVID academic capex pull-forward created an above-trend period 2021-2023; normalisation/contraction is a documented pattern in adjacent peers; UK government fiscal pressure in 2024-2026 is a real and current headwind.

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: Budget pressures are documented and real — ✓
- Motive: Government/academic customers have no profit motive — reducing capex is rational under fiscal stress — ✓
- Opportunity: JDG has limited ability to switch end-market exposure rapidly — ✓
- All three legs present — full alert

Disconfirming evidence: JDG subsidiary order books demonstrating multi-year visibility; private-sector industrial end-market exposure is larger than thesis assumes; government frameworks lock in multi-year capex.

Monitoring checklist:
- UKRI annual budget vs. prior year (real terms)
- EU Horizon programme disbursement data
- US NIH appropriations
- JDG order book/pipeline commentary
- Subsidiary accounts showing non-academic revenue mix

Interaction with other failure modes: Strong correlation with Scenario 2 (organic deceleration) and Scenario 4 (operating leverage failure). Partially correlated with Scenario 6 (margin compression). This is the macro-channel into the cluster.

Sceptical bullet: What's the disconfirming evidence? Confirmed multi-year government framework contracts at key subsidiaries, combined with demonstrated industrial end-market revenue exceeding 35% of group, would materially reduce this scenario's plausibility.

§7 — Scenario 6: Margin Normalisation from Working Capital / FX Distortion [Margin/Profitability Risk — FALSE FRIEND]

Narrative [FALSE FRIEND]: JDG has reported stable-to-improving EBIT margins over recent years. However, a portion of this apparent margin quality may reflect favourable FX translation (sterling weakness boosting overseas subsidiary results), positive working capital timing (deferred capex at subsidiary level), or unusually low warranty/service provisions. The reported margin appears healthy — a positive signal that confirms the thesis — while the underlying cash-generative quality is deteriorating. This is a canonical false-friend scenario.

Transmission mechanism: FX tailwind reversal → sterling strengthens → overseas subsidiary margins translate unfavourably → group EBIT margin apparently compresses without any operational deterioration → EPS revisions → analyst community re-rates model quality.

Early warning signals:
- GBP strengthening materially (10%+ real effective exchange rate appreciation)
- Reported margin premium to FCF margin widening (working capital absorption)
- Warranty provision / returns data rising at subsidiary level
- Capex/depreciation ratio falling (capex deferral flattering EBIT near-term)
- Cash conversion ratio declining below 90% of reported EBIT

Plausibility verbal verdict: Medium-to-high plausibility as a false-friend specifically — the mechanism (FX + capex deferral flattering reported margins) is well-documented in UK mid-cap manufacturing/instruments and would appear as a thesis-confirming signal until the reversal arrives.

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: GBP has been structurally weak 2016-2024; reversal is plausible — ✓
- Motive: Subsidiaries have natural incentive to defer capex in the short term — ✓
- Opportunity: JDG reporting does not fully disaggregate FX from organic margin — ✓
- All three legs present — false-friend full alert

Disconfirming evidence: Cash conversion consistently ≥95% of EBIT; capex/depreciation ratio ≥1.0x over 3-year rolling period; explicit FX sensitivity analysis showing marginal impact; disclosed FX contribution to margin is immaterial.

Monitoring checklist:
- Cash conversion ratio (FCF / EBIT, annual trend)
- Capex/depreciation ratio (rolling 3-year)
- GBP/EUR and GBP/USD trend (with JDG geographic revenue split)
- Warranty provision as % of revenue
- Working capital days (receivables + inventory — payables)

Interaction with other failure modes: This is the false-friend wrapper around Scenarios 2-5 — margin appears fine while organic growth and end-market deterioration are building. The false-friend mechanism delays signal detection, compounding other failure modes.

Sceptical bullet: What's the disconfirming evidence? Explicit FX disaggregation in management accounts showing FX contribution is immaterial, combined with cash conversion above 95% and capex ratio above 1.0x, would largely disconfirm this scenario — though capex deferral is harder to disprove without subsidiary-level data.

§8 — Scenario 7: Founder / Key Executive Departure [Execution Risk]

Narrative: JDG's acquisition discipline, subsidiary culture, and pricing ethos are substantially embodied in the founder-chairman and small senior team. The departure of the founding executive (Mark Lavelle / David Cicurel — the founding family/management nucleus) removes the cultural immune system that has historically prevented multiple inflation, overpaying, and operational over-management of subsidiaries. A successor with different incentives and less embedded deal-sourcing network degrades model quality.

Transmission mechanism: Key-person departure → acquisition pipeline disruption (relationships are personal) → deal quality/pace deteriorates → growth stalls → model narrative breaks → re-rating; simultaneously, corporate governance culture shifts → subsidiary autonomy eroded → cost-base rebuilds.

Early warning signals:
- Announced retirement, health disclosure, or unexpected resignation of founding executive
- Rapid appointment of external CEO/CFO with PE or corporate background (cultural mismatch signal)
- Board composition shift toward institutional/activist nominees
- Succession plan disclosures in annual report (often a delayed signal)
- Management incentive structure changing from long-term to short-term metrics

Plausibility verbal verdict: Medium plausibility on 18-month horizon, rising materially to high plausibility on 3-5 year view given founder age profile; peer-precedent base rate for founder-led compounders shows model degradation in 40-60% of succession events within 2 years.

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: Natural succession risk; founders age — ✓
- Motive: Personal — retirement/health/external opportunity — ✓
- Opportunity: No structural barrier to departure — ✓
- All three legs — elevated alert

Disconfirming evidence: Named and actively developing internal successor with demonstrable acquisition track record; founder retains significant equity stake post any transition; explicit multi-year succession plan publicly articulated.

Monitoring checklist:
- Annual report governance section (succession planning language)
- Board appointment announcements - Founder equity holding (% of shares) — any reducing trend
- Remuneration report (incentive structure continuity)
- AGM voting outcomes (early governance stress signal)

Interaction with other failure modes: Strongly correlated with Scenario 1 (acquisition multiple inflation) and Scenario 4 (operating leverage failure — new management rebuilds centre). Also correlated with Scenario 9 (activism / board conflict — new management may attract activist interest).

Sceptical bullet: What's the disconfirming evidence? A detailed succession framework with an internal candidate publicly named and given operational P&L responsibility for acquisitions, combined with founder retaining >5% equity stake, would substantially reduce this scenario — but the named internal candidate must then be stress-tested on their own track record.

§9 — Scenario 8: M&A Integration Failure — Subsidiary Culture Destruction [Execution Risk]

Narrative: JDG's model explicitly avoids integration — subsidiaries run autonomously with original management retained. The failure mode is not classic integration failure (IT systems, SAP roll-outs) but rather the subtler cultural erosion where: (a) JDG gradually imposes KPIs, reporting burden, and central oversight that undermines the entrepreneurial culture; or (b) a specific acquisition is operationally troubled and requires hands-on intervention, consuming management bandwidth and setting a precedent for reduced autonomy across the portfolio.

Transmission mechanism: Culture erosion at subsidiary level → key subsidiary management departures (entrepreneurs exit when autonomy removed) → talent quality of subsidiary leadership degrades → subsidiary organic growth and innovation deteriorates → EPS misses → narrative break.

Early warning signals:
- Director/founder departures at subsidiary level within 2 years of acquisition (Companies House)
- Group-level integration programme announced (contradicts autonomy model)
- JDG central headcount growing faster than subsidiary count
- Subsidiary-level EBIT declining post-acquisition vs. pre-acquisition trajectory
- Annual report language shifting toward "integration" and away from "autonomy"

Plausibility verbal verdict: Low-to-medium plausibility in near term — JDG has historically maintained the autonomy model effectively; however, as portfolio grows beyond 20+ subsidiaries, central oversight pressures increase and precedent-setting risk rises.

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: Increasing group complexity creates natural oversight pressure — ✓
- Motive: No deliberate intent to destroy culture — partial (weakens scenario)
- Opportunity: One troubled acquisition can set a portfolio-wide precedent — ✓
- Two-of-three — watch only

Disconfirming evidence: Zero subsidiary management departures within 2 years of acquisition across the last 10 deals; no centralisation announcements; subsidiary-level accounts showing organic growth maintained post-acquisition.

Monitoring checklist:
- Companies House director filings for recently acquired subsidiaries (departure dates)
- Annual report language analysis (autonomy vs. integration framing)
- Central headcount per subsidiary (PLC cost/subsidiary count)
- Subsidiary EBIT trajectory pre/post acquisition (where calculable)
- Any group-level shared services announcements

Interaction with other failure modes: Correlated with Scenario 7 (key-person departure — new management may impose central oversight). Weakly correlated with Scenario 4 (operating leverage failure). Largely independent of Scenarios 1-3.

Sceptical bullet: What's the disconfirming evidence? A documented history of subsidiary founder/management retention exceeding 90% for 3+ years post-acquisition across the portfolio, confirmed by Companies House data, would substantially weaken this scenario.

§10 — Scenario 9: Governance Stress — Activism or Board Conflict [Execution Risk]

Narrative: As JDG's market cap grows into the £500m-£1bn range, institutional ownership expands, and governance scrutiny intensifies. An activist investor targets perceived capital allocation inefficiency (cash hoarding, below-market ROIC on legacy subsidiaries, founder pay structure) or an internal board conflict disrupts management continuity. The scenario is particularly acute if the founder attempts to maintain control while institutional governance norms demand change.

Transmission mechanism: Activist intervention / board conflict → management distraction → acquisition pipeline pause → EPS growth stalls → re-rating on governance discount; alternatively, activist forces capital return at the expense of acquisition dry powder → growth model damaged.

Early warning signals:

- AGM voting against remuneration report exceeding 15%
- Board composition changes (independents exceeding founder-aligned members)
- Public press commentary on governance concerns
- RNS disclosures of requisitioned board meetings or shareholder letters

Plausibility verbal verdict: Low plausibility on 12-month horizon given current ownership structure; medium plausibility on 3-year view as institutional ownership mix evolves with market cap growth.

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: Activist funds are active in UK mid-cap space — ✓
- Motive: Genuine governance concerns could exist — partial
- Opportunity: Current ownership concentration may limit activist leverage — partial
- Two-of-three — watch only

Disconfirming evidence: High founder/family equity ownership (>20%) providing natural defence; institutional holders' public statements of support; governance ratings (ISS/Glass Lewis) broadly supportive.

Monitoring checklist:

- ISS/Glass Lewis governance reports

Interaction with other failure modes: Correlated with Scenario 7 (founder departure — activism often accelerates or follows management transitions). Independent of Scenarios 1-6.

Sceptical bullet: What's the disconfirming evidence? High concentrated founder ownership and a track record of ISS/Glass Lewis support would substantially reduce this scenario — but founder ownership also makes the succession scenario (§7) more acute, not less.

§11 — Scenario 10: Technology Disruption — Platform Science / Modular Instruments [Competitive/Structural Risk]

Narrative: The scientific instruments market is experiencing a structural shift toward software-defined and modular platforms (e.g. LabVIEW successors, cloud-connected instrument platforms, AI-assisted measurement). JDG's subsidiaries are predominantly hardware-centric, analogue-design businesses. If the next generation of scientific capability is delivered through platform software with modular hardware add-ons, JDG's specialised, single-purpose hardware businesses face secular demand erosion.

Transmission mechanism: Platform-hardware substitution → JDG subsidiary products commoditised or obsoleted → revenue trajectory structurally lower → acquisition model less valid (fewer quality targets, lower multiples on exit) → EPS trajectory broken on both organic and M&A channels.

Early warning signals:
- Major platform vendors (National Instruments/Emerson, Keysight, Bruker) accelerating software/platform revenue as % of total
- Academic procurement shifting toward platform-integrated solutions
- JDG subsidiary R&D spend declining relative to revenue (under-investment in software layer)
- New entrant instruments businesses raising VC funding with software-first model
- Patent filing activity at JDG subsidiaries declining

Plausibility verbal verdict: Low-to-medium plausibility on 18-month investment horizon — technology disruption in scientific instruments operates on a 5-10 year cycle; JDG's most specialised niches (cryogenics, ultra-high vacuum) are less susceptible to platform commoditisation than general bench instruments. However, the structural direction is adverse on a 5+ year view.

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: Platform technology is advancing demonstrably — ✓
- Motive: Academic customers favour integrated, software-rich environments — ✓
- Opportunity: JDG's hardware-only subsidiaries have limited software layer — ✓
- All three legs present — but timeframe extends beyond immediate investment horizon; flag as structural watch

Disconfirming evidence: JDG subsidiaries with proprietary software layers and recurring software revenue; acquisitions targeting software-enabled instrument businesses; no evidence of customer defection to platform competitors in subsidiary accounts.

Monitoring checklist:
- Recurring/software revenue as % of group (if disclosed)
- R&D spend (absolute and % of revenue)
- Platform vendor software revenue growth vs. hardware (proxy for industry direction)
- New subsidiary acquisitions — hardware-only vs. software-enabled split
- Academic procurement framework changes

Interaction with other failure modes: Weakly correlated with Scenario 3 (pricing erosion — disruption eventually commoditises). Independent of Scenarios 7-9 (execution risks). Partially correlated with Scenario 2 (organic deceleration — disruption may manifest as share loss before full obsolescence).

Sceptical bullet: What's the disconfirming evidence? Demonstrated proprietary software layers in the most material subsidiaries (by EBIT contribution) and a track record of acquiring software-enabled businesses in the last 3 years would substantially reduce this scenario's plausibility on the investment horizon.

FALSE FRIEND Scenario 11: Earnings Quality — Non-Cash / Acquisition Accounting Flattery [FALSE FRIEND — Load-Bearing]

Narrative: JDG's reported EPS growth partially reflects IFRS 3 acquisition accounting mechanics — deferred revenue haircuts, fair-value inventory adjustments, and amortisation of acquired intangibles creating a "pop" in post-acquisition earnings that is not operational. Additionally, the progressive acquisition of businesses with pension liabilities, deferred tax assets, or contingent consideration that settle favourably can create one-off EPS gains reported as part of underlying performance. The analyst models "underlying EPS" growth of X%; a portion of this is structural accounting benefit, not operational.

Transmission mechanism: Acquisition accounting mechanics → inflated post-acquisition "underlying EPS" → analyst consensus modelled at elevated run-rate → when accounting benefit runs off (typically 12-18 months post-acquisition), organic EPS growth undershoots consensus → EPS cut cycle → re-rating.

Early warning signals:
- Growing divergence between statutory EPS and adjusted/underlying EPS
- Amortisation of acquired intangibles as % of revenue growing year-on-year
- Acquisition consideration settlements significantly below fair-value estimates (suggests overstatement)
- FCF per share materially lagging reported EPS growth
- Auditor disclosures on acquisition accounting judgements increasing in complexity

Plausibility verbal verdict: Medium plausibility — JDG's historically conservative accounting is a documented thesis pillar; however, IFRS 3 mechanics create structural earnings inflation in all acquisition-heavy models that is rarely fully visible to outside analysts. This is a false-friend precisely because conservative-accounting reputation reduces scrutiny.

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: IFRS 3 accounting mechanics are systemic and automatic — ✓
- Motive: Management does not need to act; accounting standards do the work — ✓
- Opportunity: Acquisition frequency ensures the distortion is continuous rather than isolated — ✓
- All three legs present — false-friend full alert

Disconfirming evidence: FCF per share growing in line with reported EPS growth; amortisation as % of revenue stable or declining; explicit bridge from statutory to adjusted EPS showing immaterial acquisition accounting items; auditor notes on acquisition accounting shrinking in complexity.

Monitoring checklist:
- FCF per share vs. adjusted EPS per share (annual, trend)
- Amortisation of intangibles as % of revenue
- Acquisition accounting adjustments in earnings bridge
- Cash conversion ratio (should be near 100% if earnings quality is high)
- Statutory vs. adjusted EPS divergence trend

Interaction with other failure modes: This is the most dangerous standalone false-friend — it interacts with every scenario because it masks the true underlying earnings trajectory. Most correlated with Scenario 6 (margin normalisation from FX/capex distortion) and Scenario 2 (organic growth deceleration).

Sceptical bullet: What's the disconfirming evidence? Consistent FCF conversion above 95% of adjusted EPS over a 5-year acquisition-active period would be the strongest disconfirmation — but even this can mask timing differences that become apparent in a no-acquisition year.

4. PART B — META-ANALYSIS

J-front verdict: Three tightly correlated clusters dominate the failure landscape; false-friend density is high (4 of 11 scenarios have false-friend characteristics), making the thesis more dangerous than a direct-bearish read implies.

Scenario cluster: Acquisition model integrity cluster (Scenarios 1, 7, 11)
- These three scenarios are causally linked: discipline maintained → founder critical → accounting quality dependent
- If Scenario 7 (founder departure) crystallises, Scenarios 1 and 11 become substantially more probable simultaneously
- This cluster has the highest single-trigger / multi-failure characteristics of any group

Scenario cluster: Organic growth and margin cluster (Scenarios 2, 3, 4, 5, 6)
- Five scenarios share overlapping transmission mechanisms (revenue → EBIT → EPS)
- They can occur sequentially (end-market contraction → organic stagnation → pricing erosion → margin compression → operating leverage failure)
- This cluster represents the slow-burn failure mode — individually observable but collectively devastating

Scenario cluster: Governance/execution cluster (Scenarios 7, 8, 9)
- These are correlated through the management continuity channel
- Lower probability individually, but founder departure acts as a joint trigger for all three
- The governance cluster compounds the acquisition model cluster if triggered simultaneously

Correlated failures: Strongest correlation pair is Scenarios 1 + 7 (acquisition discipline and founder departure) — base rate for model degradation post-founder in comparable compounders is high; second strongest is Scenarios 2 + 5 (organic stagnation and academic budget contraction) — both driven by the same macro input.

Joint probability assessment:
