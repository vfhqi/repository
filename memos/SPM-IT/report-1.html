§1 — General Description, Financials, Peer Comparison

[C]: Saipem is a mid-cycle EPCI turnaround: post-2022 balance sheet repair complete, revenue growing 23% in 2024 to €14.5bn, EBITDA margins expanding from crisis lows — but still 5-7pp below Subsea 7's 16% in 2024, implying significant catch-up opportunity if execution holds. Pending Saipem7 merger could transform competitive standing.

Business Overview and Organisational Structure

  • [AS·ARS]: Saipem S.p.A. is a global leader in the engineering, procurement, construction, and installation of major projects for the energy and infrastructure sectors. The company operates a highly specialized fleet of offshore construction vessels, pipelay assets, and drilling rigs, allowing it to execute complex projects in both shallow and deepwater environments globally. Following recent organizational restructuring, Saipem operates as a "One Company" model designed to foster synergies across its operations.
  • [AS·ARS]: Operations divided into three primary reporting segments for financial purposes: (1) Asset Based Services — comprising Offshore Engineering & Construction (deepwater pipelines, subsea structures, platform installation) and Offshore Wind business lines; (2) Energy Carriers — comprising the Onshore E&C and Sustainable Infrastructures business lines; (3) Offshore Drilling — managing a fleet of drillships, semi-submersibles, and jack-ups.
  • [AS·ARS/Investor Presentation]: Saipem's current strategic objectives, outlined in its 2025-2028 plan, focus heavily on execution excellence, maintaining a capital-light vessel strategy, and capitalizing on the prolonged up-cycle in upstream oil and gas investments. The company is also broadening its energy transition offerings, targeting carbon capture, blue/green molecules, and offshore wind developments.
  • [C]: Scale and market cap — share price €4.23 (22-May-26), market cap approximately €8.1bn; EV approximately €7.4bn (pre-IFRS 16 net cash €683m end-2024); trailing revenue €15.5bn (FY2025). Average daily trading volume approximately 6-8 million shares; institutional float circa 53% (ENI ~31% stake, CDP Equity ~12.5%).

EBITDA and Margin Expansion

  • [C]: Adjusted EBITDA: €926m (2023, 7.8% margin) → €1,329m (2024, 9.1% margin, +44% YoY) → €1,717m (2025, 11.1% margin, +29% YoY) — 330bp expansion over two years.
  • [C]: ❌ Margin gap vs peers: Subsea 7 EBITDA margin 16% FY2024; TechnipFMC adjusted EBITDA margin approximately 13-14% FY2024; Technip Energies 8.3% FY2024 — Saipem at 9.1% in 2024 was below the peer median for dedicated offshore players.
  • [AS·Jefferies]: Saipem's group EBITDA margin of 11.1% in 2025 trails the sector median (~15%), though its Asset Based Services division has rapidly improved, generating a 16.5% margin in Q1 2026. Subsea 7, which operates as a pure-play SURF and offshore wind contractor, achieved a 21.5% EBITDA margin in Q1 2026 — the gap versus Saipem's blended 12.3% reflects the drag from Saipem's diversified Energy Carriers segment with its approximately 2.0% margin.
  • [AS·Rothschild & Co Redburn/ING]: Versus Technip Energies, Saipem's business mix is fundamentally different, as Technip operates an asset-light model heavily levered to LNG engineering and technology. Technip Energies commands lower absolute margins in its core Project Delivery segment (typically 6.5% to 8.0%), but generates exceptionally high returns on invested capital due to large project-specific cash prepayments. Saipem requires significantly more capital expenditure to maintain its vessel fleet, resulting in structurally different ROIC profiles. SBM Offshore's unique Lease & Operate FPSO model yields group EBITDA margins above 33%, driven by 15-20 year guaranteed lease contracts — not a direct comparison to Saipem's project-based revenues.
  • [AS·Bernstein]: SBM Offshore's highly levered balance sheet carried $5.6 billion in directional net debt at end-2025 due to heavy ongoing FPSO construction financing — Saipem's recent achievement of a net cash position (pre-IFRS 16) contrasts favourably.
  • [C]: Strategic Plan 2025-2028 targets EBITDA margin of 12% in 2027 and EBITDA approximately €2.0bn in 2028 — implies further 100bp+ expansion from current 11.1%.

Net Income and EPS

  • [C]: Net income €180m (2023) → €306m (2024, +70% YoY) → €310m (2025, approximately flat) — earnings recovery ongoing but modest absolute level relative to revenue scale; net margin approximately 2% reflects capital-intensive contractor model. EPS trajectory: approximate FY2025 EPS €0.16-0.17 based on €310m net income and approximately 1.9bn shares.

This divergence is structural: fleet expansion via operating leases continues to build D&A; as long as Saipem grows its chartered fleet, D&A scales proportionally.

  • [C]: Pre-IFRS 16 net cash €683m (end-2024) vs net cash €216m (end-2023) — delivered net cash position through operating improvement alone; commitment to reduce gross debt by €650m (2025-2027 maturities); investment grade credit rating target medium-term.
  • [C]: ❌ Post-IFRS 16 net cash only €23m end-2024 — lease liabilities of approximately €706m remain a financing obligation; fleet charter strategy adds off-balance-sheet commitments. New leases €1.2bn signed in 2025 alone — growing IFRS 16 balance sheet liability.

  • [C]: ROIC approximately 12.3% (trailing, from public financial aggregator); peer context: Subsea 7 ROIC approximately 14-16% post-recovery; TechnipFMC guided ROIC above 15% by 2026 — Saipem's improving but not yet best-in-class. FCF conversion: FCF €792m (2025) / EBITDA €1,717m = 46% — mid-range for capital-intensive EPCI; lease repayments (~€400m annual) are the primary drag.

Capital Allocation History

  • [C]: 2022 rights issue: 95-new-for-1-existing ratio at €1.013/share, 30% discount — one of the most dilutive rights issues in European industrial history; raised approximately €2bn to repair balance sheet after contract losses and COVID impairment. Total shares multiplied approximately 95x from pre-crisis count, rendering pre-2022 per-share history economically irrelevant.
  • [AS·Morgan Stanley/Jefferies]: The €2.0 billion capital increase was heavily supported by core shareholders Eni and CDP (who underwrote 43% of the offering) and was highly dilutive, radically expanding the share count but successfully resolving the company's leverage crisis.
  • [AS·Jefferies/INTRM]: Disposals and M&A — Saipem divested its Onshore Drilling business to KCA Deutag for $550 million in cash plus a 10% equity stake in the combined entity. The company finalised the sale of the 10% interest to Helmerich & Payne in early 2026 for approximately $89.5 million, converting the strategic residual into cash.
  • [AS·ARS/Jefferies]: Dividends and Share Repurchases — after a decade of suspended payouts, Saipem's operational turnaround allowed it to resume dividends in 2025. Based on the strong free cash flow generated in 2025, the Board proposed a dividend of €0.17 per share. Saipem's updated distribution policy commits to paying out at least 40% of its Free Cash Flow (post-repayment of lease liabilities) to shareholders beginning in 2027. Historically, Saipem's share repurchases have been limited to covering employee Long-Term Incentive Plans.
  • [C]: Post-recovery dividend: FY2025 proposed €0.17/share (approximately €330m total, ~43% of net income); no buybacks announced; management prioritising gross debt reduction and investment grade rating over aggressive returns. Eni and CDP Equity together hold approximately 43.5% of Saipem's equity — minority shareholders have limited blocking power.

Backlog (⚡ RARE)

  • [C]: Order intake acceleration: 2023 backlog was €29.8bn; 2024 grew by €4.3bn net of execution — indicates pricing power and demand strength in current upcycle. 2025 backlog maintained above €31bn despite €15.5bn of revenue burn — intake continues to more than replace execution.

  • [C]: ❌ Sceptical: Margin recovery story is compelling on trajectory but Saipem's 9.1% EBITDA margin in 2024 remained materially below Subsea 7's 16% despite similar work types — the gap may reflect structural issues (legacy lump-sum fixed-price contracts, onshore EPC drag, higher cost base) rather than purely a temporary catch-up opportunity.

§2 — Revenue Model, Segments, Delivery, Internal Change

[C]: Saipem's three-segment structure concentrates 65% of backlog in Asset Based Services (offshore EPCI), with a high-conviction repositioning underway: exiting low-margin lump-sum onshore EPC, growing offshore wind and NOC-driven deepwater, and completing divestiture of Onshore Drilling. Contract mix is improving but execution risk on fixed-price offshore contracts remains the primary earnings volatility driver.

Revenue Segments

  • [AS·Earnings Presentation/ARS]: Asset Based Services (Offshore E&C and Offshore Wind) — €9.04 billion in 2025 revenue, representing a 12.2% year-over-year growth rate. Profitability has improved significantly, with the segment achieving a 14.4% EBITDA margin in 2025, up from 11.8% in the prior year. By Q1 2026, this segment's EBITDA margin expanded further to an impressive 16.5%. Backlog €22.5bn (66% of total).

  • [C]: Flagship projects: Bonga North (Nigeria, SNEPCO), GranMorgu (Suriname, TotalEnergies, $1.9bn), Qatar North Field Compression ($4bn, QatarEnergy LNG), Marjan field offshore Saudi Arabia ($2bn, Aramco) — signed Sep-Dec 2024.

  • [AS·Bernstein/ARS]: Energy Carriers (Onshore E&C and Sustainable Infrastructures) — €5.62 billion in 2025 revenue, representing negligible year-over-year growth as the company deliberately prioritises value over volume. The division operates with structurally lower profitability, delivering an EBITDA margin of roughly 2.0% in 2025. Backlog €10.3bn (30% of total). Qatar NFS compression offshore platform work and onshore pipeline/processing plant EPC sit within this segment — lower-margin and carries legacy lump-sum exposure.

  • [AS·Earnings Presentation]: Offshore Drilling — €829 million in 2025 revenue, with a 10% year-over-year decline due to the reduction of its leased jack-up fleet and scheduled maintenance. However, it remains Saipem's highest-margin business, delivering a 38.2% EBITDA margin in 2025. Backlog €1.3bn (4% of total). Revenues are generated through standard daily rate charter contracts, currently benefiting from a favourable pricing environment due to tight global rig supply.

Rest of Europe contributing €1.43 billion, Italy generating €728 million, the CIS (Commonwealth of Independent States) adding €93 million. Active commercial pipelines across the Americas, West Africa, and Asia Pacific.
  • [C]: West Africa / Sub-Saharan Africa: Nigeria (SNEPCO Bonga North), Mozambique, Angola — deepwater complex EPCI work; Saipem has permanent local infrastructure in the region. Europe / North Sea: offshore wind (Dogger Bank), North Sea hook-up and commissioning, Italian market maintenance. Americas: Suriname (TotalEnergies GranMorgu $1.9bn), Brazil (Petrobras relationships), US Gulf — growing contribution.
  • [C]: September 2024 alone: Saipem announced approximately $7bn of contract wins — dominated by Middle East NOC activity; reflects the NOC capex supercycle and the strength of Saipem's embedded regional position with Saudi Aramco and QatarEnergy.
  • [C]: Customer concentration — Saudi Aramco and QatarEnergy together likely account for 25-35% of annual revenue based on disclosed contract values; ENI (~31% shareholder) historically a significant client but relationship is arm's-length post-independence push; TotalEnergies, Petrobras, Shell round out top-10 customers. NOC dominance exceeds 60% of revenue estimated.

Contract Types and Risk Profile

  • [AS·ARS/Transcript]: Following severe financial difficulties in 2021 and 2022 stemming from fixed-price contracts, Saipem has fundamentally overhauled its commercial risk profile. Management has adopted a selective bidding strategy that favours hybrid EPC contracts with balanced risk profiles, ensuring that cost-plus and reimbursable components protect against raw material and logistics inflation. Saipem is actively expanding its Project Management Consulting and Operation & Maintenance (O&M) services, which offer higher margins and a recurring, predictable revenue stream without the capital intensity of lump-sum construction.
  • [C]: Offshore EPCI contracts remain predominantly lump-sum or fixed-price — margin upside is capped but downside unlimited on execution failures (the root cause of 2021-2022 crisis); management's "value over volume" doctrine post-2022 involves refusing low-margin tenders and adding contractual protections. However, the post-2022 pricing discipline is not independently verifiable and relies on management self-reporting.
  • [C]: Onshore EPC historically dominated by lump-sum EPC; management has explicitly stated intent to shift Energy Carriers segment toward reimbursable / cost-plus to reduce risk. Offshore Drilling: pure day-rate contracts; more predictable earnings but exposed to utilisation and day-rate cycles.

Customer Concentration

  • [AS·ARS/Earnings Presentation]: Top three clients each accounted for more than 10% of total revenue in 2025 — Customer A €2.49 billion, Customer B €1.92 billion, Customer C €1.92 billion, meaning the top three clients collectively represented 41% of Saipem's total consolidated revenue. National Oil Companies (NOCs) account for 46% of the backlog; International Oil Companies (IOCs) and independent operators represent 47%; other non-oil energy clients 7%.

Competitive Advantages and Vessel Fleet

  • [AS·Jefferies]: Vessel Fleet — Saipem operates a highly specialised offshore fleet consisting of 17 owned construction vessels and 9 owned drilling rigs, complemented by 5 fabrication yards globally. The company possesses distinct advantages in specific pipelay methodologies, utilising flagship vessels like the Castorone and JSD 6000 to execute complex J-lay and S-lay operations in deepwater environments.
  • [C]: ⚡ RARE — Post-merger combined fleet: Saipem7 will own 12 pipelayers, creating the largest pipelay fleet globally by a significant margin — competitive moat at scale not replicable by new entrants without multi-decade capital commitment. Fleet diversity (S-lay, J-lay, and reel-lay vessels configured for specific depth ranges) allows project wins that single-format competitors cannot service.
  • [AS·Sustainability/ARS]: Technology and IP — Saipem holds more than 2,200 patents and actively invests in energy transition technologies, filing 27 new patent applications in 2025 alone. Key technological differentiators include Star1 floating wind technology, proprietary subsea robotics (Hydrone-W and FlatFish), and advanced capabilities in Carbon Capture, Utilisation, and Storage . In-house EPIC chain control (engineering, fabrication yards in Italy and Indonesia, installation vessels) avoids subcontracting margin leakage.
  • [AS·ARS/Bernstein]: Regional Dominance — Saipem benefits from highly integrated regional operations, particularly in the Middle East. The company's Saudi fabrication yard (Saipem Taqa Al-Rushaid Fabricators) fulfils local content requirements and enables complex offshore installations without relying on external fabrication bases. Bernstein Research notes that Saipem's historical positioning gives it a unique "Mattei goodwill" advantage, securing a disproportionate share of complex infrastructure contracts in developing nations across Africa and the Middle East.

Recent Delivery and Backlog

  • [AS·ARS/Transcript]: Under CEO Alessandro Puliti, Saipem has established a track record of consistent execution, beating its initial financial guidance for three consecutive years ending in 2024. The company successfully transitioned from burning cash to generating robust free cash flow, delivering €792 million in FCF (post-leases) during 2025, comfortably supporting the resumption of dividend payments. Management has materially de-risked legacy contracts, such as the Courseulles-sur-Mer offshore wind project, while maintaining stringent commercial discipline on new awards.
  • [C]: Backlog and book-to-bill: Record backlog €34bn (end-2024); book-to-bill 1.3x in 2024 (order intake €18.8bn vs revenue €14.5bn); Q4 2024 order intake €5.3bn, book-to-bill 1.2x; 2.3x revenue coverage provides 27-month forward visibility. The company still manages significant revenues from legacy zero-margin onshore projects; as these tail off, realised margins are expected to structurally improve.

Internal Change (Leadership / Strategy L12-36M)

  • [C]: CEO Alessandro Puliti — appointed August 2022 (concurrent with rights issue); ENI lifer (30+ years, Chief Upstream Officer 2019-2021); orchestrated the entire post-crisis recovery including segment restructuring, value-over-volume pricing doctrine, Onshore Drilling divestiture, and Subsea 7 merger negotiation. Board re-confirmed mandate May 2024 through to AGM approving 2026 accounts.
  • [C]: Strategic Plan 2025-2028 published February 2025 — four pillars: (1) Execution Excellence; (2) Capitalising on Oil and Gas Upcycle; (3) New Paradigm for Onshore E&C; (4) Broaden Energy Transition offering. Investment-grade credit rating target is an explicit commitment.
  • [C]: Subsea 7 merger announcement February 2025: shareholders approved September 2025; merger agreement signed July 2025; completion anticipated H2 2026 — would create Saipem7 with approximately €21bn revenue, EBITDA exceeding €2bn and €300m synergies from Year 3. Onshore Drilling divestiture completed June 2024 — portfolio simplification removes lower-return assets.
  • [C]: ❌ Sceptical: Saipem's lump-sum offshore contract book remains the primary execution risk: a single large project failure (as in 2021) could consume 2-3 quarters of EBITDA. The post-2022 pricing discipline is not independently verifiable and relies on management self-reporting — the record 2024 order intake may have included competitively priced awards to build backlog coverage.

§3 — Industry Context

[C]: The offshore EPCI industry is in a sustained multi-year upcycle driven by NOC deepwater expansion (Saudi Aramco, QatarEnergy, Petrobras) and energy security spending post-Ukraine; supply-side constraint — vessel availability and skilled workforce — supports pricing discipline and margin expansion for established players. Saipem is a direct beneficiary as one of three global-scale offshore EPCI contractors.

Pricing Discipline and Capacity Dynamics

  • [AS·Jefferies]: Competitive intensity in the offshore services sector has shifted from fierce price-cutting during the 2014-2020 downturn to a period of sustained pricing discipline. The primary driver is structural constraint on supply — contractors have severely limited their capital expenditures, resulting in virtually no new-build capacity entering the market for high-end, deepwater installation vessels. Deepwater contractors are currently booked into 2027 and 2028.
  • [C]: Heavy pipelay and deepwater construction vessel availability is constrained: the global fleet of DP3 pipelay vessels is approximately 20-25 units across all major players combined; new vessel construction requires 3-5 years and $500m-plus capital — no meaningful new supply imminent. Vessel day-rates for high-spec assets increased approximately 20-30% from trough (2020) to 2024.
  • [AS·DNB Carnegie]: Floater utilisation rates are projected to approach 100% by the end of 2027 amid accelerating tendering activity and longer-duration awards. Subsea 7 is currently trading at a 2026 free cash flow yield of approximately 6-8%, demonstrating the strong cash conversion inherent in the pure-play subsea model when vessel utilisation is high.
  • [AS·Expert Call — Supply Chain Consultancy Advisor]: An industry expert confirms this dynamic, noting that IOCs are heavily focused on capital discipline, which has forced the supply chain toward performance-based commercial models rather than a race to the bottom on price.

Offshore vs Onshore Capex Trends

  • [C]: Total offshore energy investments expected to exceed $300bn in 2026 for second consecutive year; deepwater upstream spending set to increase 15% in 2026; deepwater E&P capex growth trajectory toward $142bn in 2028 from $87bn in 2023 — a sustained structural increase. Deepwater activity concentrated in Brazil (Petrobras), Guyana (ExxonMobil/Hess), Namibia (TotalEnergies/Shell), US Gulf, West Africa — all Saipem target markets.
  • [AS·Morgan Stanley]: Offshore investments have nearly doubled since 2020, with South America and the Middle East serving as the primary engines of this growth. Morgan Stanley forecasts a 2026-2030 offshore well and exploration capex CAGR of 2.2%, led by a robust 4.0% CAGR in the deepwater segment. Due to the rapid decline rates of onshore shale reservoirs and decreasing productivity from onshore non-OPEC fields, offshore deepwater production is expected to be the main contributor to global non-OPEC oil supply beyond 2027.
  • [AS·Jefferies]: Conversely, onshore capital expenditures, particularly in North America, are moderating as the sector matures. U.S. land operators are prioritising capital efficiency and maintaining flat to modest lateral footage growth, which limits the upside for onshore-focused service providers compared to their offshore peers.
  • [C]: Near-term risk: Clarkson Research (May 2026) projects 24% decline in offshore oil and gas capex FDI decisions in 2026 vs 2025 ($111.9bn → $85bn) — driven by oil price uncertainty and IOC capital discipline. NOC spending more resilient but a pullback would extend backlog execution timeframes and reduce future order intake.

NOC Spending Trends and LNG Capex Cycle

  • [AS·Bernstein/Expert Call]: NOCs represent the most resilient customer segment in the current market because their capital programmes are driven by long-term government growth mandates rather than short-term oil price volatility. The Middle East Oil Services market is currently valued at approximately $111 billion. Within this region, spending is heavily directed toward capacity expansion and major infrastructure overhauls, providing a massive pipeline of fixed-price and reimbursable contracts for entrenched players like Saipem.
  • [C]: NOC dominance: Saudi Aramco, QatarEnergy, Petrobras, ADNOC collectively represent approximately 40%+ of Saipem's total order intake; NOCs have longer-term capex commitments and are less sensitive to short-cycle oil price moves vs IOCs. QatarEnergy's North Field Expansion programme alone represents a multi-year multi-billion dollar pipeline.
  • [C]: Qatar North Field Expansion (NFS and NFE phases) represents the largest single LNG capex programme globally (estimated $100bn+ across all phases); Saipem's $4bn offshore compression award in September 2024 cements it as a primary delivery partner alongside TechnipFMC. Additional NFS contracts awarded December 2025 ($3.1bn EPCI) extending the Qatar runway.
  • [AS·Morgan Stanley]: Approximately 200 million tonnes per annum (mtpa) of LNG export capacity is currently under construction globally, which is expected to drive total global supply above 600 mtpa by 2030. This LNG wave generates substantial onshore E&C demand, directly benefiting contractors capable of executing complex liquefaction and regasification terminals.

Energy Transition

  • [AS·ARS]: The global energy transition is acting as a structural opportunity rather than a headwind for top-tier EPIC contractors. Saipem's energy transition strategy is heavily focused on CCUS, blue and green fertilisers, and offshore wind installations. The CCUS segment is a particularly strong fit, as it utilises legacy pipeline and subsea injection capabilities.

  • [AS·ARS]: The transition also introduces stringent regulatory and environmental compliance risks.
