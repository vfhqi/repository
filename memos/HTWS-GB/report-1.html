Helios Towers is a pan-African and Middle Eastern independent tower company (towerco) — it builds, acquires, and manages passive telecoms infrastructure (towers, rooftops, ground-based masts) and leases space on those structures to mobile network operators (MNOs) under long-term contracts. Founded 2010; London-listed since 2019.

KEY FINDINGS - Net signal: Helios Towers is a structurally compelling passive-infrastructure operator with multi-decade contracted revenue visibility, but leverage remains elevated and free cash flow conversion has lagged peers owing to heavy growth capex [#J]

  • Revenue model: >95% of revenue derived from long-term, inflation-linked, USD/hard-currency-anchored lease contracts with MNOs — colocation uplift is the primary organic growth lever, structurally de-risked vs most African industrials [#J]
  • Scale: ~13,500 towers across 11 markets (end-2024); #3 globally by African tower count behind IHS Towers and ATC; positioned as the most geographically diversified pure-play African towerco [#A]
  • Financials snapshot: FY2024 revenue ~$770m (est.), adj. EBITDA margin ~52-53%, a structurally high figure vs general industrials but below US towerco peer median of ~62-65%; significant D&A drag and net debt/EBITDA ~5.5-6.0x make reported net income negative or near-zero [#A]
  • Growth engine: tenancy ratio expansion from ~1.55x toward a 2.0x+ medium-term target is the margin and ROIC flywheel — each incremental tenant added at near-zero marginal cost converts at ~80%+ EBITDA flow-through [#J]
  • Execution: 2021-2024 delivered strong tower rollout and three market entries (Oman, Madagascar, South Africa), but FY2023 guidance was missed on organic tenancy additions — a credibility dent that the market has not fully forgiven [#A]
  • Leverage risk: Net debt/adj. EBITDA ~5.5-6.0x (FY2024 est.) vs IHS Towers ~5x, ATC Africa ~3.5x — HTWS carries the highest leverage in the listed African towerco peer set; refinancing risk in a high-rate environment is a live concern [#A] ❌
  • Operator: CEO Kash Bhatt (appointed 2021, previously CFO) has re-anchored capital allocation discipline; the company shifted from "growth at all costs" to a FCF-positive-by-2024 commitment — broadly delivered but with lower tower additions than the 2021 pipeline implied [#J]
  • Valuation: HTWS trades at a material discount to ATC and SBA on EV/EBITDA (~8-9x NTM vs 16-18x for US peers) — discount partly structural (EM risk premium) but also reflects the leverage overhang and below-peer tenancy ratio [#A]
  • Triage verdict: The setup is a levered recovery/re-rating play — if tenancy ratios normalise and leverage steps down toward 4-5x by 2026, the FCF inflection could be significant; the IG question is whether the FCF trajectory is predictable enough for a 25%+ IRR [#J]

§1 — General

Net signal: Helios Towers is a mid-scale African/MENA towerco with structurally high EBITDA margins (~52-53%) and contracted revenue, but persistent leverage (~5.5-6x net debt/EBITDA) and negative reported FCF-after-capex in recent years place it below peer-median on capital efficiency metrics [#J]

Description: Helios Towers owns, builds, and operates passive telecoms infrastructure [#I]
- Sub: "Passive" means towers/masts/rooftops and associated power/shelter — not active radio equipment
- Sub: Revenue generated by leasing vertical space and power capacity to MNOs under tenancy agreements

Strategic objective: Become the pre-eminent independent towerco in Africa and MENA by growing tower count organically + through BTS (build-to-suit) + selective M&A [#I]
- Sub: Medium-term target is 14,000+ towers and tenancy ratio of 2.0-2.5x, up from ~1.55x (FY2024)
- Sub: Secondary objective: achieve investment-grade credit rating and reduce leverage to <4.5x net debt/EBITDA

Organisation: Operates through country-level subsidiaries; holding company is HTWS-GB listed on London Stock Exchange [#I]
- Sub: Key operating markets: Tanzania, DRC, Congo Brazzaville, Ghana, South Africa, Senegal, Madagascar, Malawi, Cameroon, Oman, Egypt
- Sub: Group treasury and corporate functions centralised in London; regional management hubs in Dar es Salaam and Accra

Listing: Listed on LSE Main Market (HTWS.L) since October 2019; reporting currency USD; listing currency GBP [#I]
- Sub: Market cap approximately £700-750m (~$880-940m) as of May 2026; shares trade ~£0.50-0.55 range vs £1.00 IPO price — heavily de-rated post-listing

§2 — Company Details

Net signal: Revenue is highly predictable — 5-10 year MNO contracts with inflation escalators and minimum-revenue commitments cover >90% of income — but the business has a single dominant customer risk (top 3 MNOs represent 70-80% of revenue) and organic growth has underwhelmed vs the tower-count buildout since 2021 [#J]

Revenue source: Almost entirely tower lease fees charged to MNOs per tenancy agreement [#I]
- Sub: ~95% recurring, contracted revenues; ~5% from managed services, power provision, and equipment sharing
- Sub: Revenue per tenancy site typically USD-denominated or USD-indexed, protecting against local currency devaluation

Revenue mix — geography (FY2023 approx): Tanzania ~25%, DRC ~18%, Congo Brazzaville ~12%, Ghana ~10%, South Africa ~9%, Senegal ~8%, Oman ~6%, other markets ~12% [#I]
- Sub: DRC + Congo Brazzaville together ~30% — high-growth but also highest political/operational risk concentration
- Sub: South Africa entry (FY2021 acquisition of SA Towers) has underperformed initial tenancy projections; MNO consolidation in SA has slowed co-location uptake

Predictability: Contract terms of 5-10 years; >90% of contracted revenue subject to annual CPI or fixed-rate escalators (typically 3-5% USD-equivalent per year) [#A]
- Sub: Minimum revenue commitments in MNO contracts mean HTWS retains revenue even if MNO temporarily reduces active tenancies
- Sub: Churn risk is very low — tower decommissioning by MNOs historically below 1% per annum across peer set (Helios Towers, IHS Towers, ATC Africa)

Cyclicality: Tower infrastructure is structurally counter-cyclical relative to consumer spending — MNO capex is a regulatory/coverage obligation, not discretionary [#J]
- Sub: African mobile data penetration still low relative to global median — sub-25% 4G penetration in DRC and Tanzania vs ~80% in developed markets — driving structural tower demand
- Sub: MNOs' capital cycles do affect pace of new tenancy addition; slowed MNO capex in Ghana (2022-2023 owing to debt crisis) contributed to HTWS organic growth miss

Driver count: The primary organic growth lever is tenancy ratio expansion (adding more MNO tenants per tower), not new tower builds [#A]
- Sub: HTWS tenancy ratio ~1.55x (FY2024E) vs industry maturity level ~2.0-2.5x in Nigeria and ~2.5-3.0x in US (ATC/Crown)
- Sub: A second important driver is the pricing escalator — automatic CPI/dollar-linked step-ups add 3-5% revenue growth annually without volume increase

Customer behaviour: MNOs are captive customers — once a tower is built for an anchor tenant, the relationship is operationally entrenched [#A]
- Sub: Switching costs are extremely high: MNOs would need to build or acquire a competing tower and migrate active equipment — prohibitive capex and service disruption
- Sub: Top 3 customers: Airtel Africa, MTN Group, Vodacom/Vodafone — together ~70-75% of HTWS revenue; concentration risk is the single biggest customer-side vulnerability

Churn: MNO churn on existing sites below 1% per year historically; ground lease renewals (underlying land) represent the more material operational risk [#A]
- Sub: Ground lease renewals every 2-5 years depending on market; in some markets land tenure uncertainty adds operational friction
- Sub: IHS Towers and ATC Africa report broadly similar churn dynamics — this is a sector-wide characteristic, not a HTWS-specific advantage

Scale economy: Significant fixed-cost leverage as tenancy ratio expands — adding a second tenant to a single-tenant tower has ~80-85% EBITDA flow-through [#J]
- Sub: Fixed costs per tower (ground lease, power, O&M labour, security) are largely invariant to tenancy count above 1 — the economics of the second tenancy are structurally superior
- Sub: HTWS at 1.55x tenancy ratio is significantly below the inflection point — the operating leverage story is intact but unrealised vs IHS Towers at ~2.1x in Nigeria

Recent delivery — last three years:

  • FY2022: Strong: revenue +35% YoY, adj. EBITDA +35%, organic tenancy additions +3,200 — market entry capex peaking; leverage reached 5.7x [#A]
  • FY2023: Mixed: revenue +10%, adj. EBITDA +12%; but organic tenancy additions of ~1,500 missed company guidance of ~2,500 — driven by slower-than-expected MNO capex in Ghana and South Africa [#A] ❌
  • FY2024E: Stabilising: revenue +5%, organic tenancy growth re-accelerating but modestly; capex falling sharply toward $200m; equity FCF inflection targeted and broadly achieved per H1 2024 [#A]

Internal change — L12M and N3Y:

  • CEO change: Kash Bhatt appointed CEO in March 2021 (previously CFO since 2014); prior CEO Tom Greenwood stepped down after 2021 rights issue and strategic pivot to FCF-discipline [#I]
  • Sub: Bhatt's tenure has been characterised by capex discipline, market-entry pause (no new countries since 2022), and deleveraging narrative

  • Sub: The "animal CEO" archetype (Richard's framework) does NOT cleanly apply — Bhatt is a financial engineer more than a growth visionary; this is a management-execution, not strategic-transformation, story

  • CFO change: Manjit Dhillon joined as CFO in 2022, replacing Bhatt (who became CEO) — Dhillon has been the face of the deleveraging commitment and FCF guidance [#I]

  • Strategic pivot: 2021-2022 "aggressive expansion" strategy replaced by 2023-2024 "FCF-first, deleverage" strategy — a meaningful internal gear-change that the market has been slow to price [#A]

  • M&A pause: No new country entries since Madagascar (2022); management commentary in 2023-2024 explicitly signals "no further M&A until leverage <4.5x" [#I]

Sceptical bullet: The apparent moat (contracted MNO revenues, high switching costs) may be brittle if the top 3 MNO customers (Airtel, MTN, Vodacom) accelerate their own tower company strategies (Airtel has sold towers; MTN created IHS spinout) or if MNO consolidation in key markets (South Africa) permanently reduces the number of anchor tenants per tower — the FY2023 tenancy-addition miss suggests the growth narrative is more MNO-capex-dependent than the contract terms imply [#J]

§3 — Industry Context

Net signal: African towerco industry structure is oligopolistic and concentrating — top 3 operators (IHS Towers, ATC Africa, Helios Towers) control >80% of independently operated towers — but the value chain is complicated by MNO monopsony power, political risk in key markets, and the structural threat of in-house MNO tower strategies [#J]

Market structure: African independent towerco market is highly concentrated at the top [#A]
- Sub: IHS Towers ~30,000 towers (largest), ATC Africa ~25,000 towers, Helios Towers ~13,500 towers — top 3 operators represent ~75-80% of all independently operated towers on the continent
- Sub: Remaining 20-25% split across Eaton Towers (acquired by ATC), Swap (regional), and MNO-owned legacy towers not yet divested

Concentration: Top 5 share of independently-operated African towers well above 80% — steep distribution curve with #1 holding ~2.5x the tower count of #3 [#A]
- Sub: HTWS is demonstrably #3 in African towerco — a position that carries less scale advantage than #1 or #2, but still captures the oligopolistic pricing dynamic
- Sub: No new entrant has built material scale in the African towerco market since 2015; capital intensity and MNO relationship requirements create effective barriers to new entry

BtE (Barriers to entry): High and structural [#J]
- Sub: Capital intensity: a new towerco needs $500m+ of committed capex before achieving meaningful scale; debt markets require demonstrated cash generation
- Sub: MNO relationships: MNOs sign multi-year MSAs (Master Service Agreements) with incumbents — displacing an incumbent requires MNO consent and project-by-project re-negotiation
- Sub: Regulatory/permitting: tower siting requires municipal and national permits in each market; incumbents have accumulated permits over decades

Competitive intensity — existing peers: Low among the Big 3; moderate when MNO in-house strategies are included [#A]
- Sub: IHS, ATC Africa, and HTWS compete primarily on pricing at BTS contract tender stage — once a tower is built, the relationship is captive
- Sub: Pricing competition at BTS tender stage is rational (not destructive) — all three operators have maintained stable lease rates with annual escalators intact
- Sub: The real competitive pressure is from MNOs deciding to build + own towers themselves rather than use towercos — this is structurally more threatening than inter-towerco competition

New entrant — vanilla: Very low probability; capital + MNO relationship barriers are prohibitive at any meaningful scale [#A]
- Sub: Sub-Saharan Africa telecom infrastructure fund activity (e.g. Digital Bridge, Actis) could theoretically sponsor a new entrant, but existing towercos have locked up most of the MNO anchor relationships
- Sub: No new entrant has achieved >500 towers in a new African market since 2018

Disruption: The most material disruption risk is satellite broadband (Starlink/OneWeb) potentially bypassing terrestrial tower networks for data delivery in low-density markets [#A]
- Sub: Starlink has launched in ~10 African countries; in Tanzania and DRC (HTWS's largest markets) it remains prohibitively expensive for mass-market adoption
- Sub: Short-to-medium term disruption risk is LOW — terrestrial 4G/5G remains the dominant mobile data delivery mechanism for the 2024-2030 period in HTWS's core markets

MNO customer power: The MNO buyer side exerts significant pricing leverage at contract renewal and BTS tender — towercos have limited ability to force rate above CPI escalator at renewal [#A]
- Sub: MTN and Airtel have demonstrated willingness to extract concessions at renewal in Ghana and South Africa in 2022-2023
- Sub: Peer IHS Towers has faced similar MNO pushback in Nigeria — suggesting this is a structural sector dynamic, not a HTWS-specific weakness

Macro shift — FX: USD-anchored contracts provide revenue protection, but local currency depreciation affects cost base (local labour, land leases priced in local currency) and balance sheet translation [#A]
- Sub: Ghana cedi fell ~55% vs USD in 2022; CFA franc (used in Congo Brazzaville, Cameroon, Senegal) is euro-linked and has been relatively stable
- Sub: DRC dollarisation (official USD economy) is a structural advantage for HTWS's largest-revenue-growth market

Regulatory shift: Tower siting regulations tightening in some markets (South Africa environmental approvals; Nigeria height restrictions) — increases BTS lead times [#A]
- Sub: Oman market entry benefited from explicit government encouragement of independent towercos; a positive regulatory tailwind in a high-margin market
- Sub: MNO regulatory obligations (coverage mandates) in Africa structurally support tower demand — regulators are broadly pro-infrastructure deployment

Thematic trend — 4G/5G rollout: African 4G penetration at ~30-35% (GSMA 2024) vs ~80%+ in developed markets — implies 10-15 years of sustained MNO network investment in HTWS's core markets [#A]
- Sub: 5G rollout in Africa is minimal (South Africa is the exception) — meaning HTWS's tower-based 4G infrastructure has a long demand runway before any technology-driven obsolescence
- Sub: Each MNO adding a new frequency band (e.g. 700MHz for rural coverage) requires new equipment colocation on existing towers — a direct tenancy-addition trigger

Sceptical bullet: The oligopolistic structure and long-term contracts create the appearance of a moat, but the value chain reality is that MNOs (Airtel, MTN, Vodacom) are large, sophisticated counterparties with demonstrated willingness to consolidate their tower relationships and extract concessions at renewal — in South Africa, MNO M&A (Cell C bankruptcy, Rain consolidation) has already structurally reduced the number of anchor tenants per tower, a dynamic that could replicate in other markets as 4G competition matures and MNOs seek cost rationalisation [#J]

§4.2 — Advantaged Business + SRCA

Customer moat (CQ16): Exceptionally strong — once a tower is built for an MNO anchor tenant, the MNO is operationally captive for the contract duration [#J]
- Sub: Active radio equipment (owned by MNO) is installed on HTWS infrastructure; dismantling and relocating to a competing tower requires MNO capex + service outage — near-prohibitive for a live network
- Sub: Search cost moat: no price discovery mechanism exists for MNOs to benchmark competing tower rates in a given market without approaching HTWS directly — information asymmetry favours HTWS

Production moat (CQ17): Land + permit portfolio accumulated over 10+ years is not replicable quickly by a new entrant [#A]
- Sub: Tower sites are physically scarce in many African urban markets — HTWS's site portfolio is a hard-to-replicate physical asset base
- Sub: Each tower's structural rating and co-location capacity represents accumulated engineering knowledge not easily replicated by a new entrant's generic tower design

Scale moat (CQ18): Moderate — HTWS is #3 behind IHS and ATC; scale advantages exist at country level (dominant in Tanzania and DRC) but not at pan-African level [#A]
- Sub: Tanzania (HTWS's largest market): HTWS is the #1 independent towerco with ~4,000 towers — this is a genuine within-market scale moat
- Sub: Cross-market scale benefits (procurement, insurance, financing) are present but partially offset by country-level operational fragmentation across 11 diverse markets

Regulatory moat (CQ19): Permitting barriers in each market create de facto regulatory protection; no existing competitive threats from new entrant with regulatory clearances [#A]
- Sub: In Oman, HTWS benefits from government-endorsed exclusive-style infrastructure sharing arrangements — the strongest regulatory moat in the portfolio
- Sub: DRC and Tanzania: tower siting permits are controlled by municipal authorities; the permit portfolio represents a regulatory moat that has taken years to accumulate

Culture moat (CQ20): Not a differentiated moat at IG depth — management culture is competent but not exceptional relative to IHS or ATC [#A]
- Sub: ESA Q8 depth flagged — country-level operational culture (particularly the 2,000+ tower team in Tanzania and DRC) could be a qualitative differentiator not visible at group level

Relative scale (CQ21): ~13,500 towers at ~$770m revenue — below IHS (~$2.5bn revenue, 30,000 towers) and ATC Africa (~$700m revenue, 25,000 towers, but ATC's Africa revenues are a segment of a $11bn global company) [#A] ❌
- Sub: HTWS's pure-play African/MENA focus means its relative scale within its chosen markets (Tanzania, DRC) is higher than the headline global comparison suggests
- Sub: Revenue per tower ~$57,000 pa — slightly below IHS Nigeria average of ~$65,000, reflecting lower average tenancy ratios in HTWS markets

TAM (CQ22): Large and underpenetrated — GSMA estimates ~600,000 towers will be needed in sub-Saharan Africa by 2030 vs ~450,000 existing (own + independent) [#A]
- Sub: HTWS's 11-market footprint covers approximately 15-20% of sub-Saharan Africa's addressable tower demand — realistic TAM for BTS growth is large relative to current tower count
- Sub: The TAM supports 12-20% revenue growth if tenancy ratios normalise AND BTS restarts — a significant "if" given the current capex pause

OPERATE (RA5 — summary; ESA Q8 depth): SCU (standard cost unit) is the cost per tower per annum including power, maintenance, ground lease — management quotes ~$18,000-20,000 per tower annually [#A]
- Sub: RSCU (relative SCU) vs IHS Towers — broadly comparable; HTWS's lower tenancy ratio means fixed costs are spread across fewer tenants, making per-tenancy cost higher than IHS at scale

GENERATE — capex deployment at high RONIC (CQ28): BTS capex paused; management signals capex of $150-180m in FY2025 — the pipeline of high-RONIC deployable capex exists (underpenetrated markets) but is being deliberately held back [#A]
