Section 3 – What the company does 15 findings

Section summary
  1. The business is structured around a highly disciplined, de-risked regional contracting model that prioritizes high-margin infrastructure and sustainable residential projects.

Divisional Operations and Regional Markets

1.
High-volume residential construction: The Dutch residential housing segment operates under the division Netherlands, delivering sustainable and affordable homes for private buyers. Home sales have accelerated significantly in the domestic market, reaching 2,354 sales in FY25 compared to 1,854 in FY24. This represents a solid volume expansion of 27.00% year-on-year, demonstrating strong market demand. These homes are primarily purchased by private buyers and long-term institutional investors who seek high-quality, energy-efficient housing assets
2.
Large-scale civil engineering: The civil engineering operations within the Netherlands division focus heavily on transport systems, rail networks, and critical utilities construction. This segment is increasingly driven by public sector capital projects and infrastructure upgrades. Profitability is supported by sustained demand for projects related to the energy transition
3.
Comprehensive non-residential building: Non-residential construction and property development are managed under the division Netherlands, which delivers office complexes, healthcare facilities, and educational institutions. This business segment has shown robust recovery, with revenue supported by high production levels on major commercial properties. However, non-residential projects can be subject to delays and supply chain friction
4.
Dispersed UK and Ireland operations: This segment manages civil engineering and building services throughout the UK and Ireland, which includes major rail and infrastructure projects. The UK civil engineering division sustained a strong performance, particularly in rail and energy transition contracts. Visibility is secured by a robust local order book of €6.9 billion at the end of FY25
5.
Strategic asset divestments: BAM has aggressively simplified its regional footprint, exiting high-risk regions and non-core business activities. The group completed the divestment of its 50.00% equity stake in the Invesis joint venture to PGGM in Q1 2025. This transaction follows the previous sale of German and Belgian contracting units, representing a material de-risking of the operational profile

Value Chain Dynamics and Customer Concentration

6.
Profit-favored positioning: BAM occupies a lucrative position in the construction value chain by focusing on highly specialized, complex civil engineering projects. The company acts as a primary contractor, managing subcontractor networks while retaining structural engineering and design capabilities. This allows BAM to capture higher margins than standard commercial builders
7.
Low customer concentration: The group's customer base is highly diversified across public-sector agencies, corporate entities, and private residential buyers. Key public relationships include long-term framework agreements with transport authorities such as ProRail and grid operators like Tennet. No single client represents a concentration risk that could threaten the group's ongoing revenue stream
8.
Long-term contractual revenue: The company's order book of €13.0 billion provides excellent visibility, with a substantial portion under long-term maintenance and framework agreements
9.
Approximately 85.00% of the next 12 months of revenue is typically covered by the existing order book, ensuring predictable cash inflows. This contractual backlog reduces transaction-risk compared to purely transactional competitors

Competitive Advantages and Barriers to Entry

10.
Scale and regional brand: With over 150 years of experience, BAM possesses significant brand equity and deep regional relationships in its home markets. Its scale allows it to secure bonding capacity and banking facilities that smaller contractors cannot access. This acts as a robust barrier to entry for large-scale infrastructure tenders
11.
Proprietary sustainable technologies: The company has developed a strong competitive edge in low-emission building and circular construction methods. The proprietary Flow modular wooden housing concept allows industrialized home production in factories. This enables rapid construction with reduced carbon emissions, giving BAM an advantage under strict environmental laws
12.
Concentrated industry structure: The primary regional markets are highly concentrated, leaving a small group of top-tier contractors capable of bidding on major civil projects
13.
The limited number of large operators prevents destructive price wars on complex civil engineering tenders
14.
Other side of the story: Highly cyclical end-markets such as private non-residential building are susceptible to rapid economic downturns, exposing BAM to margin contraction if public infrastructure spending fails to offset private declines

Section 4 – Financial profile and performance 12 findings

Section summary
  1. BAM's financial trajectory shows a powerful margin recovery and a robust cash-rich balance sheet, easily surpassing peer benchmarks after a multi-year restructuring.
[ A data table from this section could not be extracted cleanly from the source and is omitted; the figures are carried in the surrounding findings. ]

Revenue and Profitability Trajectory

1.
Solid top-line expansion: Group revenue increased by 9.00% year-on-year to €7,040 million in FY25, following €6,455 million in FY24. This growth was driven by high production volumes in the Dutch non-residential and civil engineering segments. Revenue has successfully stabilized above the €6.0 billion level targeted in the group's strategic plans
2.
Accelerating EBITDA margins: The adjusted EBITDA margin expanded to 5.7% in FY25, compared to 5.2% in FY24 and 4.9% in FY23. This steady margin trajectory demonstrates the success of the group's self-help program and disciplined project selection. BAM's margin performance is increasingly aligned with top-performing European peers
3.
Earnings quality improvements: Net profit rose to €211.0 million in FY25, representing a significant increase from €82.2 million in FY24. Earnings quality has improved as the negative drag from legacy onerous projects continues to fade. Operational results are increasingly clean with fewer restructuring or impairment charges
4.
Fluctuating capital returns: Return on capital employed (ROCE) landed at 5.8% in FY24, down from 13.7% in FY23, primarily due to non-cash impairments. These impairments were linked to the held-for-sale classification and subsequent divestment of the Invesis joint venture. Operational returns are expected to recover as the group focuses on higher-margin contracting activities

Cash Flow and Balance Sheet Strength

5.
Negative working capital advantage: Trade working capital efficiency was recorded at -11.9% in FY25, compared to -11.7% in FY24. This represents a highly efficient negative working capital structure, which acts as a powerful source of liquidity during expansion phases. Working capital has remained highly favorable, consistently beating the strategic target of less than -10%
6.
Net cash balance sheet: BAM has maintained a robust net cash position since 2015, ensuring exceptional balance sheet security. Cash and cash equivalents stood at €763.42 million at the end of FY24, leaving the group with €440.21 million in net cash after accounting for bank borrowings and lease liabilities. Net cash was approximately €500 million by the end of FY25, highlighting excellent cash generation capabilities
7.
Robust solvency ratio: The solvency ratio remained stable at 23.0% in both FY24 and FY25, meeting the company's regulatory and operational needs. This provides a solid capital base that allows BAM to easily absorb potential project cost overruns. Solvency has improved significantly from 13.4% in FY20, illustrating a remarkable balance sheet rehabilitation

Recent Results and Guidance Policy

8.
Earnings beat in FY25: The FY25 preliminary results surpassed consensus expectations, with revenue and adjusted EBITDA beating consensus by 4.00% and 5.00% respectively. Earnings per share came in at €0.80, representing a strong absolute improvement. The order book remained stable at €13.0 billion, providing excellent visibility into FY26 and beyond
9.
Conservative guidance policy: Management operates with a conservative guidance strategy, typically under-promising and over-delivering. For example, in mid-FY25, the company guided for an adjusted EBITDA margin of "at least 5%", which they comfortably exceeded by delivering 5.7% for the full year
10.
Increased shareholder returns: Based on the strong FY25 results, the board proposed a 20.00% increase in the dividend to €0.30 per share. This was supplemented by a €40 million share buyback program launched on May 11, 2026, demonstrating strong alignment with shareholder interests
11.
Other side of the story: Onerous contracts from legacy bids have historically inflicted massive cash drains, as demonstrated by the Co-op Live arena project in Manchester and Danish school projects that together depressed previous margins

Section 5 – The possible investment case 14 findings

Section summary
  1. The investment case is powerfully strengthened by a major operational turnaround, massive infrastructure backlogs, and aggressive share buybacks.

De-risking and Strategic Focus

1.
Trajectory driver one: BAM's forward earnings will be driven by the complete elimination of legacy, low-margin project drags. This driver primarily touches the construction and property segment margins in both the Netherlands and UK divisions. The operational handover of the final school project in Denmark and the finalization of the Co-op Live arena in Manchester in late 2025 mark the end of these legacy headwinds
2.
Plausibility of driver one: Plausibility of this driver is extremely high, as these projects are now operationally complete. The transition toward medium-sized projects with key clients under strict risk-reward criteria should support a stable margin profile
3.
Failure mode of driver one: This driver would fail if management relaxed its risk criteria, chasing high-volume, fixed-price contracts that leave the group vulnerable to subcontractor bankruptcies

Sustainable Construction and Environmental Tailwinds

4.
Trajectory driver two: Structural demand for energy transition infrastructure and grid upgrades will drive civil engineering volumes. This driver directly impacts the revenue and adjusted EBITDA of the Dutch civil engineering division, which has captured substantial contracts from grid operators
5.
Notable wins include high-voltage substations for Tennet and infrastructure maintenance for ProRail
6.
Plausibility of driver two: Plausibility is extremely high given the immense, government-mandated investment cycles in European power grids. BAM's established relationships and technical expertise make it a preferred partner for these complex works
7.
Failure mode of driver two: This driver would fail if public grid funding was delayed by bureaucratic backlogs or if grid operators faced capital constraints
8.
Trajectory driver three: The structural housing shortage in the Netherlands supports persistent residential development revenue. BAM's acquisition of Gebroeders Blokland in early 2026 adds high-quality development sites, expanding its pipeline to c.30,000 homes. This provides strong multi-year support to the domestic residential construction segment
9.
Plausibility of driver three: Plausibility is medium, as housing demand remains robust, though near-term volumes can be affected by high mortgage rates
10.
Failure mode of driver three: This driver would fail if mortgage interest rates surged further, pricing out private buyers and reducing home sale velocity

Capital Return and Shareholder Yield

11.
Independent driver interaction: The investment case is highly resilient because these core drivers are largely independent. A slowdown in private residential housing would not derail public-sector grid upgrades or defense infrastructure projects, meaning the investment case does not require a perfect alignment of all macro forces
12.
Aggressive capital return program: The group's robust cash generation supports an exceptionally high shareholder yield. Management plans to return €357.00 million to shareholders over the 2023-2026 period via rising dividends and share buybacks. This program has already reduced the outstanding share count by c.7.00% by late 2025, providing a powerful tailwind to forward EPS
13.
Other side of the story: Severe supply chain inflation and material shortages could erode the profitability of newly won contracts, particularly if inflation escalates faster than contractual indexation clauses can adjust

Section 7 – Key risks and the other side 9 findings

Section summary
  1. The risk profile is significantly improved but remains subject to legislative hurdles in the Netherlands and operational execution risks on complex infrastructure projects.

Regulatory and Environmental Hurdles

1.
Severe nitrogen restrictions: The ongoing Dutch nitrogen crisis represents a persistent headwind for building permits in the Netherlands. Strict nitrogen emission limits have slowed down residential construction starts across the country, creating significant uncertainty for project timelines. This could lead to delayed home sales in the Netherlands division
2.
Housing market volume pressure: The total Dutch new residential housing market experienced a volume decline of approximately 8.00% year-to-date in early 2026. While BAM has maintained a robust domestic performance, persistent industry-wide volume pressure could eventually constrain organic revenue growth if the market downturn worsens
3.
Geographic concentration risks: Following the divestment of contracting operations in Germany and Belgium, BAM's geographic exposure is highly concentrated in the Netherlands and the UK. This less diversified geographical footprint makes the group vulnerable to localized economic shocks in these core regions

Project Execution and Customer Liquidity

4.
Technical cost overruns: Large, complex civil engineering projects carry high execution risks, where design failures or material price spikes can lead to onerous contract provisions. These operational overruns can severely depress EBIT margins, as demonstrated by the previous loss-making Danish school projects
5.
False friend backlog: A rapidly growing backlog can act as a false friend if it includes low-margin or fixed-price contracts signed under high-inflation conditions. Bidding for volume over value remains a key risk that could break the long-term profitability target of 5-7% EBITDA margins
6.
Customer interest rate exposure: High interest rates affect BAM's clients, including municipal governments, private developers, and retail homebuyers, by raising their financing costs. This can alter customer behavior, leading to delayed project tenders or slower execution timelines
7.
Receivables quality signal: The primary early-warning indicator to monitor is the duration of trade receivables and gross impairments. Any shift toward longer durations or higher gross impairments would signal deteriorating customer liquidity and weakening cash generation capabilities
8.
Other side of the story: The negative impact of strict Dutch nitrogen regulations is heavily mitigated because BAM's industrialized, modular Flow wooden housing concept generates very low emissions, allowing BAM to win permits where conventional builders fail
