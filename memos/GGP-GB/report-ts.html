Section 7 – Key risks and the other side 10 findings

Section summary
  1. The overall risk picture is credible but manageable, with geological aquifer penetrations and secondary environmental permitting representing the primary hurdles to the development timeline.

Geological and Technical RisksPenetrating the sedimentary and water-bearing Permian cover sequences represents the primary geological risk to Havieron's underground decline schedule. The decline must safely traverse the Lower Confined Aquifer, which is a Tillite unit characterized by moderate permeability water-bearing structures. Any uncontrolled water inflows could saturate the decline, slowing mining productivity and escalating operating costs.

1.
Findings:
2.
Complex aquifer risk: The primary decline must penetrate the Lower Confined Aquifer, where unmanaged water inflows or unstable ground conditions could severely delay development and escalate capex
3.
Specialized contractor dependence: Re-commencing decline development through the sediments requires specialized raise boring contractors to build two critical ventilation shafts, creating elevated execution risk
4.
Stockpile false friend: Large low-grade stockpiles of 23.00Mt at 0.36g/t gold are a "false friend" because processing them reduces immediate mining costs but suppresses average mill grades, potentially hurting short-term revenues

Regulatory and Infrastructure RisksWhile primary Federal and State environmental permits have been successfully granted, Greatland remains dependent on secondary regulatory approvals to commence full-scale underground mining development. Delays in constructing critical surface infrastructure, such as additional water evaporation ponds and tailings lifts, present ongoing schedule risks.

5.
Findings:
6.
Secondary permitting dependencies: Substantive development following FID cannot commence until Greatland receives several outstanding secondary environmental approvals, posing an immediate schedule risk
7.
Critical path approvals: Delays in permitting and constructing three additional surface evaporation ponds would immediately disrupt the Havieron development schedule by preventing the restart of underground mining
8.
Early warning signal: The key metric to monitor is the daily decline advance rate, which is budgeted at a conservative 2.75m/day compared to the historical average of 3.30m/day
9.
Other side of the story: Other side of the story: Hydrological risks are highly mitigated by the completed boxcut tunnel project and the installation of a high-capacity dewatering pump station to handle extreme water volumes
