Section A — Company and financial snapshot

Orientation context for the Investment Analyst; this is not the investment-case analysis. Section A is excluded from the memo word-count floor.

The business. Elekta AB is a Swedish medical-technology company that designs, manufactures and services precision radiation-therapy and radiosurgery systems for cancer treatment. Founded 1972 by neurosurgeon Lars Leksell; headquartered in Stockholm; roughly 4,700 employees; sells into 120-plus countries. It is the global number-two in external-beam radiation therapy behind Siemens Healthineers' Varian. The business runs a "razor-and-blade" model: capital-equipment sales (linear accelerators, MRI-guided systems, radiosurgery units) generate a multi-decade installed base of over 7,500 devices, which in turn drives recurring service contracts, software licences and upgrades over 10-to-15-year equipment lifecycles.

Product portfolio.

  • Linear accelerators (the core revenue engine): the legacy Versa HD workhorse platform, and the new Elekta Evo CT-linac — an AI-enhanced CT-guided linac that received US FDA 510(k) clearance in January 2026 (the first new Elekta linac to gain US clearance in over a decade) and carries a roughly 30% price premium over Versa HD with broadly unchanged cost of goods sold.
  • Elekta Unity: the world's only commercialised high-field 1.5-Tesla MRI-guided linac; Elekta's principal technological differentiator; roughly 150-plus units installed; sole-provider position after competitor ViewRay's 2023 bankruptcy.
  • Leksell Gamma Knife: stereotactic radiosurgery for brain disorders; an estimated ~97% global market share in this niche; ~360 systems installed.
  • Brachytherapy: Flexitron afterloaders and associated software; an estimated ~65% global market share.
  • Elekta ONE software suite: unifies the MOSAIQ oncology information system, Monaco treatment planning, and AI-driven planning (auto-contouring via a GE HealthCare MIM Software partnership). MOSAIQ was named top performer in the 2025 Best in KLAS report. Software carries roughly 80% gross margins and is the principal mix lever for margin recovery.

Financial snapshot (FY2024/25, year ended April 2025; figures from the Triaging Earnings Delivery research report, the most current full-year source on disk).

  • Net sales: SEK 18,016M — essentially flat year-on-year against SEK 18,119M, a roughly -0.6% organic decline.
  • Adjusted EBIT margin: 11.6% (down from 11.8% the prior year).
  • Adjusted gross margin: 37.8% (up from 37.5%); Q4 FY2024/25 adjusted gross margin reached 40.3%, the best in five years.
  • Reported net income: SEK 237M, down 82% from SEK 1,302M, driven by a SEK 1,064M non-cash impairment in Q4 (discontinued R&D / abandoned internally-developed cloud software platform).
  • Reported EPS: SEK 0.62 (from SEK 3.41 the prior year); consensus FY2025/26 EPS approximately SEK 3.23.
  • Order book: SEK 34.15bn, after a stricter order-criteria review in FY2025/26 that cancelled aged or non-accretive orders (a SEK 4.9bn tranche in Q1 FY2025/26 plus a SEK 2.2bn tranche in Q2 FY2025/26 — roughly SEK 7.1bn cumulative, equivalent to ~39% of annual revenue removed from the backlog).
  • Book-to-bill: 1.09x rolling twelve months for FY2024/25; 1.17x in Q3 FY2025/26.
  • Net debt: roughly SEK 3.5bn; net debt / adjusted EBITDA roughly 3.0x on the FY2024/25 [C] research report basis (other sources put leverage nearer 1.3-1.5x; the discrepancy is flagged in Section F).
  • Dividend: SEK 2.40 per share declared for FY2024/25, held flat.

Most recent quarter (Q3 FY2025/26, November 2025-January 2026). Net sales +2% in constant currency (-10% reported, a 12-point FX wedge — the Swedish krona strengthened and the US dollar weakened); adjusted gross margin 38.3% (from 37.1% a year earlier) despite a combined 230 basis points of FX (130bps) and US-tariff (100bps) headwind; adjusted EBIT margin 11.9%; reported EBIT margin dragged 150bps by the R&D capitalisation / amortisation shift; Q3 order intake SEK 5,418M, a roughly 10% beat versus consensus and +3% year-on-year; China returned to positive order and revenue growth for the first time in two years.

Corporate and governance context. New CEO Jakob Just-Bomholt took office in September 2025, succeeding Gustaf Salford. Just-Bomholt's prior tenure was at 3Shape (a Danish dental 3D-scanning and CAD/CAM software company); he also has prior operating experience associated with Getinge. On arrival he replaced the CFO, Chief Commercial Officer and Chief People Officer, launched a four "Must-Win Battle" operational reset, and announced a roughly 450-role reduction (~10% of headcount, over 80% complete by Q3 FY2025/26) targeting at least SEK 500M in annualised cost savings with full run-rate from Q1 FY2026/27. Share structure is dual-class: A-shares (one vote) and B-shares (one-tenth vote); the Leksell family, via A-share dominance, controls roughly 30% of votes from a low single-digit percentage of economic capital, and Laurent Leksell chairs the board. The B-share (EKTA.B) is the investable, liquid line. A Capital Markets Day is scheduled for 17 June 2026; the Q4 FY2025/26 full-year results are due 28 May 2026, with a silent period in effect from 1 May 2026.

Competitive context. The global radiation-oncology market operates as a duopoly: Varian (Siemens Healthineers, which acquired it in 2021) holds roughly 60% of the global installed base and a stronger position again in the United States; Elekta holds the low-to-mid 30s globally. United Imaging Healthcare (UIH) is an aggressive domestic Chinese competitor expanding overseas. RaySearch Laboratories is a vendor-agnostic treatment-planning software pure-play. Accuray is a distant niche player in robotic radiosurgery.

Page-level Bottom Line Up Front

  • [C-conditional] Watson verdict: a narrow, conditional Triaging pass. Elekta clears the Triaging test — there are large, concrete change forces — but it clears it narrowly, and the recommendation is CONDITIONAL PROGRESS to Early-Stage Assessment, gated on the Q4 FY2025/26 result (28 May 2026) clearing a defined three-part test. This is a real case with a real "but," and the memo has not skirted the "but."
  • [B-] The change forces are genuine and unusually concrete for a Triaging-stage case. A decisive new CEO (Jakob Just-Bomholt, eight months in seat); a SEK 500M structural cost programme that is over 80% executed on the headcount action and worth roughly 280 basis points of EBIT margin; a real new product cycle in the FDA-cleared Evo linac (a ~30% price premium on unchanged cost of goods); and a China normalisation already visible in the order line. This is the strongest argument for the case and the reason it is "progress," not "park."
  • [C-/D+] But the base those change forces are landing on is not yet robust — and this is the heart of the case. Management has missed its own margin targets for roughly three consecutive fiscal years; the order book has been written down twice in six months (cumulatively ~SEK 7.1bn, ~39% of annual revenue); there is a structural foreign-exchange drag and a mechanical R&D-accounting headwind to fight through; and the fundamental-delivery record is incongruent with the thesis. The new CEO breaking the pattern is, at Triaging, a hypothesis — not a demonstrated fact.
  • [D+] The case is structurally complex and fails the case-simplicity guardrails on the two that matter most. It is not a focused bet — four interdependent drivers (margin execution, US recovery via Evo, China normalisation, order-book credibility) on a critical path, where the failure of any two cascades — and it does not have zero headwinds: a live battery of competitive, order-book, foreign-exchange and tariff headwinds is operating on the revenue line.
  • [C-] The value-chain position is defensible but slowly squeezed. Elekta has real structural advantages — installed-base switching costs, a hard-to-replicate service network, an improving supplier-side picture — but the Varian/Siemens bundle moat is a structural competitive disadvantage that compounds at one-to-two points of share per year regardless of Evo's point-product quality, and the multi-year share trend is down (US new-sales share from above 20% to below 15%).
  • [Active CAUTION] The single most important risk flag is the false-friend caution. Multiple features of the case — the standout Q4 FY2024/25 margin quarter, the order-book "quality improvement" framing, the MRI-linac "monopoly" narrative, service-revenue resilience as a lagging indicator — look like recovery confirmation but may be 12-18 months ahead of genuine delivery. The protection is to gate the case on the actual prints, which is exactly what the Section E recommendation does.

Section C.1 — Pillar I: Case change elements

E1 — Required input change forces (×2 double-weight)C
E1 is the only Pillar I Element in scope at Triaging, and it carries a x2 double-weight, so the bullet floor doubles to six minimum per Required Attribute and per Core Question. E1 tests two things in sequence. Required Attribute 1 asks whether a genuine, strong body of external and internal change forces is operating on the company. Required Attribute 2 asks whether the company has a robust enough base — absence of disqualifying headwinds, a well-invested platform, a delivery record congruent with the thesis, a large enough change-from-current, and a low enough trading multiple — for those change forces to actually convert into shareholder return.

E1 element rating: [C] — the change forces are real and unusually concrete for a Triaging-stage case, but Required Attribute 2 is where the case is genuinely strained: a three-year record of margin under-delivery, two backlog write-downs in six months, a structural foreign-exchange drag, and a mechanical R&D-accounting headwind together mean the base is not yet robust, and the change-from-current is bounded rather than large.

The honest read at Triaging is that E1 splits cleanly: Required Attribute 1 is a [B-] — there is a genuine, identifiable, partly-quantified body of change forces, which is more than many Triaging candidates can show. Required Attribute 2 is a [C-/D+] — the disqualifying-headwind battery is load-bearing, not marginal, and the "robust base" test is the part of E1 that the bull case most wants to skip past. Blending the two, with the analytical weight that the x2 multiplier demands placed on not skirting Required Attribute 2, the Element resolves to [C]. This is a case with real change but a base that has to be proven, not assumed.

E1 — RA1: Are strong external and internal change forces present?B
Required Attribute 1 summary — is there a genuine body of change forces, external and internal, that could plausibly drive a material improvement in earnings?

[B-] RA1 summary: yes — there is a real, identifiable, partly-quantified body of change forces, and it is more concrete than the typical Triaging candidate. The internal forces (a decisive new CEO, a substantially-executed structural cost programme, a genuine new product cycle in the Evo linac) are the stronger leg; the external forces (secular oncology demand, the clinical shift to adaptive radiotherapy, China normalising) are real but slower-acting and partly offset by an intensifying competitive set.

  • The internal change forces are the part of the case that has actually moved. A new CEO arrived in September 2025 and within two quarters replaced three of his direct reports, delayered the organisation from nine management levels to six, launched a roughly 450-role reduction, and committed to at least SEK 500M of annualised cost savings — of which over 80% of the headcount action was complete by Q3 FY2025/26. This is not a slideware programme; it is largely in the P&L already.
  • The cost programme is quantifiable and material against the base. SEK 500M on a roughly SEK 18bn revenue base is approximately 280 basis points of potential EBIT-margin improvement, split roughly 70% operating expenses and 30% cost of goods sold per management disclosure. Sell-side estimates (DNB Carnegie) put the net effect at close to 3 percentage points of EBIT margin "all else equal." The full run-rate lands in Q1 FY2026/27.
  • The Evo product cycle is a specific, time-bounded internal change force rather than a vague "innovation" claim. FDA 510(k) clearance came in January 2026 — the first new Elekta linac cleared in the US in over a decade. Evo carries a roughly 30% price premium over Versa HD on broadly unchanged cost of goods sold, so each Evo placement is structurally gross-margin-accretive; the AI-imaging software content (the "Iris" capability) is the premium, and software gross margins sit near 80%.
  • The external change forces are real but slower and more diffuse. Global cancer incidence is rising at roughly 1.5-2% per annum; radiation therapy is standard of care in around half of cancer cases; the global radiation-oncology market is growing at a high-single-digit rate. This is genuine secular demand — but it is the backdrop the company has had for years, not a new force.
  • The clinical shift to adaptive radiotherapy — re-optimising the treatment plan in real time as the patient's anatomy changes — is the most genuine external "technology cycle" force, and Elekta is positioned on it through both Unity (MRI-guided) and Evo (CT-guided). This is a real upgrade-cycle driver. But adoption is gradual and reimbursement-gated, and the competitor (Varian/Siemens) is positioned on the same trend with more scale.
  • China normalising is a real but qualified external force. After roughly two years of disruption from the government anti-corruption campaign, China returned to positive order and revenue growth in Q3 FY2025/26, with a book-to-bill above 1.1x and management guiding to roughly 10% order and revenue growth in the second half; sell-side estimates have total Chinese linac unit sales recovering from roughly 180 units at the trough to 240-260 in 2026. The qualification: two of the largest backlog write-down tranches were concentrated in China and emerging-market orders, so the prior "China backlog" was partly illusory, and UIH is taking share aggressively.

[B] RA1-CQ1 — Are there strong external change forces / tailwinds? (six-bullet floor)

Is there a genuine body of external tailwinds that could drive material EPS improvement?
  • The secular demand driver is genuine and durable. Cancer incidence rising at 1.5-2% per annum, radiation therapy used in roughly half of cancer treatments, and a global radiation-oncology market growing at a high-single-digit rate (the research reports variously cite ~7-11% for the broad market including software and services, with the hardware sub-segment growing slower at low-single-digits). For a Triaging "are there change forces" test this clears the bar — but it is a tailwind the company has always had, so it is context more than catalyst.
  • The clinical shift to adaptive radiotherapy is the strongest single external force, because it drives an equipment-upgrade cycle rather than just capacity expansion. Adaptive and image-guided radiotherapy is cited as growing at a low-double-digit rate against a low-single-digit base linac market. Elekta is positioned on this with two products. This is a real "change force" in the sense the Element means it — a reason the installed base turns over faster than pure demographics would imply.
  • China normalising post anti-corruption campaign is a real external force with observable confirmation: positive Chinese orders and revenue in Q3 FY2025/26 for the first time in two years, book-to-bill above 1.1x, and an industry forecast of 240-260 Chinese linac units in 2026 against roughly 180 at the trough. The structural under-penetration argument (China at roughly 1.8 linacs per million inhabitants against roughly 12 in the US) gives the recovery a long runway.
  • US installed-base age is an external tailwind specific to Elekta's position: over 60% of the existing US linac base is more than eight years old and replacement-eligible. With Evo now FDA-cleared, this is a pent-up-demand pool that can be addressed — the early evidence is that US order intake "turned sharply positive" after the January 2026 clearance.
  • Government healthcare-infrastructure spending is a periodic external tailwind in Europe — UK, France and Poland tender activity is cited as supporting EMEA orders, with EMEA the strongest-growing region at +8% constant-currency in Q3 FY2025/26. This is real but lumpy and budget-cycle-dependent.
  • The honest counter-weight: the external forces are partly offset by external headwinds operating on the same value chain — an intensifying competitor set (Varian's scale, UIH's aggression), US tariffs (a 100bps gross-margin drag in Q3 FY2025/26), and government-budget displacement risk. The external picture is net-positive but not clean. Rated [B] because the demand and adaptive-therapy forces are genuine and durable, but a clean [A] would require the external set to be one-directional, which it is not.

[B] RA1-CQ2 — Are there strong internal change forces? (six-bullet floor)

Is there a genuine body of company-controlled change forces that could drive material EPS improvement?
  • The CEO change is the keystone internal force. Jakob Just-Bomholt took office September 2025 and acted with unusual speed: three of his direct reports replaced within the first two months, a nine-to-six management delayering, a roughly 450-role reduction, and a full zero-based review of the order book. Decisiveness of this kind in the first six months is itself a change force — it shifts the question from "will this management do anything" to "will what they have already done deliver."
  • The SEK 500M-plus cost programme is the most quantifiable internal force. Over 80% of the headcount action complete by Q3 FY2025/26; full run-rate from Q1 FY2026/27; roughly 280 basis points of potential EBIT-margin improvement on the current revenue base; sell-side estimates put the net effect near 3 percentage points. The programme is described by Jefferies as flowing from a structural redesign of the operating model rather than arbitrary cost-cutting — which, if accurate, makes it more durable.
  • The shift to a decentralised, regional profit-and-loss operating model is a genuine structural internal change, not just a cost line. It pushes accountability to regional leaders and is intended to raise commercial velocity — directly relevant to the US "must-win battle." It is also the highest-execution-risk piece, because reorganisations of this kind can disrupt as much as they unlock.
  • The Evo product cycle is an internal change force the company controls the timing of: FDA clearance achieved January 2026, European CE mark since September 2024 (giving Europe a head-start — roughly two-thirds of new European systems are now Evo-related), a structural ~30% price premium, and an upgrade path into the aging installed base. This is a concrete, time-bounded earnings lever.
  • The R&D-accounting normalisation is an internal change force that is positive for earnings quality but negative for reported earnings in the near term: management is intentionally cutting the R&D capitalisation rate from roughly 60% toward 40% to align capitalisation with amortisation. It improves the underlying quality of the P&L and forces convergence between reported and cash EBIT — a genuine internal change — but it is a mechanical reported-margin drag for two-to-three years (analysed in RA2-CQ2 and the Wisdom Library "R&D capitalisation policy unwinding" model).
  • The software mix shift — Elekta ONE, the move toward software-as-a-service, ~80% software gross margins, rising attachment rates on Evo placements — is a genuine internal change force on margin mix. It is real and directionally right, but it is slow-acting and Elekta is behind peers on recurring-revenue penetration. Rated [B] overall for RA1-CQ2 because the internal forces are concrete, quantified and substantially in motion — stronger than the external set — but a clean [A] would require the delivery record to corroborate them, and at Triaging it does not yet.

RA1 rating: [B-] — a genuine, concrete, partly-quantified body of change forces, internal more than external. The reason it is [B-] and not [B] or higher: at Triaging the change forces are necessary but the Element also asks whether they are strong — and the honest answer is that their transmission to earnings is unconfirmed, the company has argued versions of this recovery before, and the external set is partly offset by an intensifying competitive environment. Strong views, weakly held: this is a real change-force body, rated one notch below the middle of the top quartile because it has not yet been corroborated by delivery.

E1 — RA2: Is the base robust?C
Required Attribute 2 summary — is there a robust enough base for the change forces to convert into shareholder return? This Required Attribute tests five things: absence of disqualifying external headwinds, a well-invested base, congruence between past and present fundamental delivery and the thesis, a large enough change-from-current, and a low enough trading multiple.

[C-] RA2 summary: this is where the Elekta case is genuinely strained. The base is not yet robust. There is a load-bearing battery of headwinds — a structural foreign-exchange mismatch, a mechanical R&D-accounting drag, and a backlog whose quality has had to be written down twice in six months — and the fundamental-delivery record is the single most damaging fact in the case: management has missed its own margin targets for roughly three consecutive fiscal years. The change-from-current is real but bounded, and the trading multiple already reflects a meaningful part of the recovery. None of this disqualifies the case, but it means the "robust base" precondition is not met at Triaging, and the case must be sized and gated accordingly.

  • The disqualifying-headwind question is the heart of RA2, and the headwind battery here is material rather than marginal: foreign-exchange structural mismatch, the R&D capitalisation/amortisation drag, and backlog-quality deterioration are each individually capable of absorbing a meaningful share of the cost-programme benefit. They are analysed in CQ1 below.
  • The "well-invested base" question is genuinely mixed. On one hand, Elekta has a real, hard-to-replicate asset base: over 7,500 installed devices, a sole-provider position in high-field MRI-guided radiotherapy, roughly 97% share in Gamma Knife radiosurgery, roughly 65% in brachytherapy, and a service network with deep clinical relationships. On the other, the FY2024/25 SEK 1,064M impairment of an abandoned internally-developed cloud software platform is direct evidence that part of the recent investment was misallocated, and management's own decision to move to a third-party platform is a tacit admission. The base is well-invested in hardware and installed-base terms, under-invested and partly mis-invested in software-platform terms.
  • The fundamental-delivery congruency question produces the most damaging finding in E1: the past and present record is not congruent with the thesis. The thesis is "this management will deliver 14%-plus EBIT margins." The record is roughly three consecutive fiscal years of missed margin targets, a FY2024/25 adjusted EBIT margin of 11.6% that actually declined 20 basis points year-on-year, and a reported net income down 82% on an impairment. The new CEO is the variable that is meant to break the pattern — but he is the new variable, by definition unproven in this seat.
  • The change-from-current question: there is a real change-from-current — a credible path from 11.6% toward 14% EBIT margin is roughly 240 basis points, which on an SEK 18bn base is a material absolute number and would drive a 20%-plus uplift in operating profit. But it is bounded: it is a margin-recovery story to a previously-held level, not a step-change to a new structural high, and the revenue line underneath it is roughly flat. A truly large change-from-current would have both a margin and a revenue leg; here the revenue leg is, at Triaging, a recovery-to-flat-to-modest-growth story.
  • The trading-multiple question: the stock has already re-rated. It rallied roughly 35% from its November 2025 lows as the market began to price the cost programme and the Evo clearance. On the research reports' figures it trades at roughly 10.8x enterprise value to next-twelve-months EBIT (Jefferies) and roughly 11.5x EV/EBITA on 2026 estimates adjusting for capitalised R&D (DNB Carnegie), or roughly 15-17x calendar-2026 price-to-earnings. That is not expensive against confirmed-delivery medtech peers, but it is not the deep-value entry point that would make the "robust base" question less important — a meaningful slice of the recovery is in the price.
  • Net: RA2 is a [C-]. The base has genuine strengths (installed base, niche monopolies, service annuity) but the disqualifying-headwind battery is real, the delivery record is incongruent with the thesis, and the multiple is not cheap enough to compensate. The honest Triaging read is that the change forces from RA1 are landing on a base that has to be proven robust, not assumed robust — and that proof is exactly what the imminent Q4 FY2025/26 result will start to provide.

[D+] RA2-CQ1 — Is there an absence of external headwinds? (six-bullet floor)

Are the disqualifying external and structural headwinds absent — or are they load-bearing?
  • They are load-bearing, not absent. The foreign-exchange structural mismatch is the largest. Elekta reports in Swedish krona but earns the large majority of revenue in other currencies (US dollar, euro, Chinese yuan, sterling) while carrying a cost base that is partly sterling-heavy (UK manufacturing) and euro-heavy (Netherlands). In Q3 FY2025/26 alone, foreign-exchange translation stripped roughly SEK 548M from reported net sales — a 12-percentage-point wedge between reported (-10%) and constant-currency (+2%) revenue — and contributed 130 basis points of the 230-basis-point combined FX-and-tariff gross-margin drag that quarter. This is structural and not fully hedgeable without moving manufacturing.
  • US tariffs are a second, active external headwind. Elekta manufactures hardware in Europe (UK and Netherlands) and ships into the US, so US import tariffs land directly on cross-border hardware sales — a 100-basis-point gross-margin drag in Q3 FY2025/26, and the research notes the exposure is rising as the US sales mix rises. This is not a tail risk; it is in the run-rate.
  • The competitive environment is an intensifying external headwind, not a benign one. Varian/Siemens has out-grown Elekta roughly 10% to 3% organically over three years and is reported to have captured roughly 10 percentage points of share since the 2021 acquisition; a new Varian platform (a TrueBeam replacement) is scheduled for roughly September/October 2026. UIH is taking Chinese share aggressively and expanding overseas. The competitive set is getting harder, not easier.
  • Government healthcare-budget pressure is a real external headwind in the EMEA region, Elekta's largest at roughly 35-40% of sales. The research is mixed — EMEA is currently the strongest-growing region on tender activity — but the structural risk (NATO defence-spending commitments competing with healthcare capital budgets, chronic public-hospital capital shortfalls) is identified in the thematic overlay as a genuine second-order drag.
  • The one external headwind that is genuinely absent is a demand-destruction or terminal-decline risk: the end market is growing, radiation therapy is not being disrupted as a modality, and Elekta's niche monopolies (Gamma Knife, brachytherapy, high-field MRI-linac) are not under near-term structural threat. So this is not a "the whole market is collapsing" situation.
  • Net: CQ1 is a [D+]. The disqualifying-headwind question is meant to test whether the change forces can land cleanly, and here they cannot — FX, tariffs and an intensifying competitor set are each a real drag, and they are concurrent. It is not an [F] because the headwinds are identifiable, partly quantified and partly manageable (the cost programme is explicitly sized to fight through them), and because the worst external headwind — end-market decline — is absent. But "absence of headwinds" is not a description that fits Elekta, and the memo should not pretend otherwise.

[C] RA2-CQ2 — Is the base well-invested? (six-bullet floor)

Is the company operating from a well-invested platform that the change forces can build on?
  • The hardware and installed-base platform is genuinely well-invested and hard to replicate: over 7,500 installed devices generating a multi-decade service annuity, with service revenue (roughly 42-43% of net sales) renewing at high rates. An installed base of this scale is the single most durable asset in the case and is not in question.
  • The niche-monopoly positions are well-invested and defensible: roughly 97% share in Gamma Knife radiosurgery (an installed base of ~360 systems, the gold-standard platform, far more published clinical evidence than competitors), roughly 65% in brachytherapy, and the sole commercialised high-field MRI-linac after ViewRay's exit. These are real, paid-for competitive positions.
  • The Evo platform is freshly invested and is the clearest evidence the company can still produce a competitive core product: FDA-cleared, CE-marked, AI-imaging content, a structural price premium. The investment in Evo is recent and is converting (two-thirds of new European systems are Evo-related).
  • Against that, the FY2024/25 SEK 1,064M impairment is hard evidence of mis-investment: an internally-developed cloud software platform was abandoned and written off, and management chose to move to a third-party platform. This is the single clearest signal that part of the recent investment programme did not earn its keep, and it raises a fair question about forward R&D capital allocation.
  • The software platform more broadly is under-invested relative to the competitor: the research reports note Varian's substantially larger software R&D budget and larger software installed base, and the "Windows 95" characterisation of legacy MOSAIQ from expert calls (referenced in the forward-stage research) points to a platform that needed the Elekta ONE refresh precisely because it had fallen behind. Elekta ONE is the catch-up investment; it is early.
  • Net: CQ2 is a [C]. The base is well-invested where it is hard to replace (hardware, installed base, niche monopolies, service network) and under-invested or mis-invested where the competitive frontier is now moving fastest (software platform). For a Triaging "can the change forces build on this base" test, the answer is a genuine mixed middle — the platform is solid enough to build on but is not uniformly well-invested, and the impairment is a real mark against management's prior capital allocation.

[D] RA2-CQ3 — Is past and present fundamental delivery congruent with the thesis? (six-bullet floor)

Does the company's actual delivery record support the thesis, or contradict it?
  • This is the most damaging Core Question in E1, and the honest answer is that the record is incongruent with the thesis. The thesis is "this management delivers a margin recovery to 14%-plus." The record is roughly three consecutive fiscal years of missed margin targets — the research reports are explicit that the equity market has been "reluctant to price in Elekta's savings targets due to past execution inconsistencies."
  • The most recent full-year datapoint is itself incongruent: FY2024/25 adjusted EBIT margin was 11.6%, which declined 20 basis points year-on-year against management's own implicit guidance of stable-to-improving. A company on a credible margin-recovery path would normally show the inflection in the full-year number; instead the inflection is being asserted off one strong quarter (Q4 FY2024/25 at 16.3% adjusted EBIT, Q3 FY2025/26 at 11.9%).
  • The reported net income collapse — down 82% to SEK 237M on the SEK 1,064M impairment — is a delivery fact that, while one-off and non-cash, is incongruent with a "quality compounder recovering" narrative. Quality compounders that get temporarily disrupted do not usually have to impair abandoned internally-built platforms.
  • The order book is a second incongruent delivery fact: a company with a credible forward order book does not normally have to write it down twice in six months (SEK 4.9bn then SEK 2.2bn). The management framing — "firmer interpretation of order criteria" — is internally coherent, but two consecutive write-downs is a pattern, and patterns are delivery evidence.
  • The genuinely congruent parts of the record: the cost programme is being delivered ahead of pace (over 80% of headcount action complete, prior cost programme delivered SEK 279M against a SEK 250M target — a beat), and Q3 FY2025/26 produced a real order beat (SEK 5,418M, ~10% above consensus) and a real gross-margin expansion (38.3% from 37.1%) despite the FX-and-tariff drag. So the record is not uniformly bad — the cost line and the order line are tracking.
  • Net: CQ3 is a [D]. The cost-execution sub-record is congruent and that matters — but the margin delivery record, which is the actual thesis, is three years incongruent, and the new CEO breaking that pattern is a hypothesis at Triaging, not a demonstrated fact. The Wisdom Library "operator validation horizon" model is directly on point: cost execution is visible at roughly six months, but the full operator-quality verdict needs 12-18 months, and Just-Bomholt is roughly eight months in. The delivery record does not yet support the thesis; it is the single biggest reason the Element does not rate above [C].

[C] RA2-CQ4 — Is there a large change-from-current / mispricing? (six-bullet floor)

Is the gap between the current situation and the thesis case large enough to be worth the risk?
  • There is a real change-from-current, and it is quantifiable: a credible path from 11.6% to 14% adjusted EBIT margin is roughly 240 basis points, which on a roughly SEK 18bn revenue base is approximately SEK 430M of incremental operating profit and a 20%-plus uplift in EBIT — before any revenue growth. That is a material change, not a trivial one.
  • The change-from-current has more than one lever, which strengthens it: the SEK 500M cost programme (~280bps), the Evo price-premium mix benefit, the software-mix shift toward ~80%-margin recurring revenue, and operating leverage on any revenue recovery. A multi-lever change-from-current is more robust than a single-lever one — though, as Section C.2 notes under E7, it is also more complex.
  • Against that, the change-from-current is bounded rather than large in the fullest sense. It is a recovery to a previously-held margin level (Elekta ran low-to-mid teens EBIT margins historically), not a step-change to a new structural high. And the revenue line underneath is roughly flat — FY2024/25 organic revenue was -0.6%, and consensus for FY2025/26 is only low-single-digit growth. A genuinely large change-from-current would have a margin leg and a revenue-acceleration leg; here the revenue leg is, at Triaging, modest.
  • The mispricing question is the weaker half of CQ4. The stock has already re-rated roughly 35% off its November 2025 lows, and on the research figures trades at roughly 10.8-11.5x forward EV/EBIT(A) and roughly 15-17x calendar-2026 earnings. That is not expensive — but it is the valuation of a stock where the market has already started to price the recovery, not one where the recovery is free.
  • The bull framing of the change-from-current, fairly stated: the sell-side (DNB Carnegie, Jefferies) argues the FY2025/26 consensus EPS of roughly SEK 3.23 sits well below a normalised earnings level, that the investor debate has shifted from "can Elekta survive" to "what is the right entry point," and that a synchronised US-and-China recovery could push organic growth above consensus. If that framing is right, the change-from-current is larger than the bounded version above.
  • Net: CQ4 is a [C]. There is a real, quantified, multi-lever change-from-current — that earns the middle of the scale rather than the bottom. But it is bounded by a roughly flat revenue line and partly pre-priced by the re-rating that has already happened, which is why it does not rate into the top quartile. The size of the prize is adequate, not compelling.

[C+] RA2-CQ5 — Is the trading multiple low? (six-bullet floor)

Is the entry multiple low enough to provide a margin of safety and asymmetric upside?
  • The headline multiples are moderate, not stretched. On the research reports' figures Elekta trades at roughly 10.8x next-twelve-months EV/EBIT (Jefferies) and roughly 11.5x EV/EBITA on 2026 estimates adjusting for capitalised R&D (DNB Carnegie), and roughly 15-17x calendar-2026 price-to-earnings. For a number-two player in a structurally attractive duopoly, these are not demanding multiples.
  • Against confirmed-delivery medtech peers the multiple is a discount: the research notes Siemens Healthineers trades materially higher on price-to-earnings, and the broader medtech peer set sits higher than Elekta on most measures. Elekta's discount is "appropriate for a low-growth, execution-uncertain medtech" — but a discount it is, and if the execution uncertainty resolves favourably the discount is the upside.
  • The multiple has, however, already compressed the easy part of the opportunity. The stock re-rated roughly 35% from its November 2025 lows. The deep-value entry point — when the market was pricing failure — has passed. What is left is a "fair multiple on uncertain delivery," not a "cheap multiple on a sure thing."
  • The bear anchor on the multiple is real and should be stated: Morgan Stanley's downside case applies a low-double-digit EV/EBIT multiple to a stagnant low-teens margin and gets to a share price materially below spot. That scenario is arithmetically coherent — it is what the multiple does if the margin recovery does not come.
  • The R&D-accounting point cuts in the company's favour on the multiple: because reported EBIT is being mechanically depressed by the capitalisation-to-amortisation shift, the reported multiple overstates how expensive the stock is on a cash-EBIT basis. DNB Carnegie's adjusted ~11.5x EV/EBITA is the more honest number, and it is genuinely undemanding.
  • Net: CQ5 is a [C+]. The multiple is the most supportive single fact in RA2 — it is moderate, it is a discount to confirmed-delivery peers, and it is undemanding on a cash-EBIT basis. It is not a [B] because the easy re-rating has already happened and the multiple is "fair," not "cheap" — there is no large margin-of-safety cushion if the delivery disappoints. It earns a notch above the middle because, of the five RA2 tests, valuation is the one that does not actively argue against the case.

RA2 rating: [C-] — the base is not yet robust. The headwind battery (CQ1) is load-bearing and concurrent; the well-invested-base test (CQ2) is a genuine mixed middle with a real mis-investment mark against it; the fundamental-delivery record (CQ3) is incongruent with the thesis and is the single most damaging fact in E1; the change-from-current (CQ4) is real but bounded; only the trading multiple (CQ5) actively supports the case, and even there the easy re-rating has passed. RA2 resolves to [C-]: the change forces from RA1 are real, but they are landing on a base that has to be proven, not assumed.

E1 overall element rating: [C] — blending a [B-] Required Attribute 1 with a [C-] Required Attribute 2. The x2 double-weight means the analytical instruction is to not skirt the harder Required Attribute, and RA2 is the harder one: the honest Triaging finding is that Elekta has a genuine, concrete body of change forces operating on a base that is not yet robust enough to convert them with confidence. That is a [C] — a real case with a real "but," where the "but" is exactly what the imminent Q4 FY2025/26 result begins to test.

[C] E5 — RA2: Suitability for AI disruption (six-bullet floor)

The shift to adaptive radiotherapy — Elekta's core commercial story — is fundamentally AI-enabled: deep-learning auto-contouring collapses physician planning time from tens of minutes to seconds, and the Evo's "Iris" imaging capability is explicitly AI-enhanced. AI is embedded in the product, and that is real.
  • AI is also a genuine service-margin lever: Elekta's predictive-maintenance capability is connected to a large share of the installed base, and the Elekta ONE planning suite's AI auto-contouring (via the GE HealthCare MIM Software partnership) drives software attach rates and average-revenue-per-user. This is AI as a monetised feature, not just a buzzword.
  • But Elekta is decisively not an AI enabler in the thematic's sense. The thematic's enabler profile requires explicit, quantifiable AI-specific revenue, pricing power tied to AI-infrastructure criticality, and multi-year hyperscaler-style contract visibility. Elekta has none of these. Its AI content is table-stakes for a 2026 medtech company — necessary to stay competitive, not a source of differentiated incremental revenue. It does not match the enabler attributes at all.

The research reports note Varian's substantially larger software R&D budget and larger software installed base — and in an AI-intensive environment, a larger installed base means more training data, which compounds into better auto-contouring and plan-quality models over a multi-year horizon. Elekta's smaller software base is a structural AI disadvantage, not an advantage. This is mild at-risk exposure.

Its core product (a physical linac with embedded software and a service annuity) is not the kind of business AI agents disintermediate. The downside AI exposure is "slow competitive erosion on the software layer," not "business-model disruption."
  • Net: RA2 is a [C]. [D] E7 — RA1: Focused bet? — summary (six-bullet floor)

E8 assesses Elekta's fit against each of the six acceptable investment-case setups and the two negative guardrails. At Triaging, each setup gets a three-bullet-minimum carve-out summary; the one or two setups identified here as the best fit will be the ones deepened at Early-Stage Assessment, while the others carry forward at three-bullet depth. The setup names below are Richard's framework setups; the analytical task is to test, honestly, which patterns Elekta actually fits.

E8 element rating: [C] — Elekta's best fit is a "transformation play / operational recovery in a structurally solid business" pattern, rated at the middle of the scale because the transformation structure is genuinely present (defined plan, credible-on-paper CEO, verifiable milestones) but the "quality business" precondition is only partially met and the recovery is one quarter into being evidenced. The negative guardrails are the more important E8 finding at Triaging: there is no unacceptable setup (no fraud, no terminal decline), but there is a real "false friend" caution — multiple features of the case look like recovery confirmation but are 12-18 months ahead of genuine delivery, and that caution is the single most important risk-management flag E8 produces.

RA1 — Setup 1 fit: Mis-modelled, high-quality EPS upgrader (three-bullet minimum)

  • Elekta is not a clean fit for the mis-modelled high-quality EPS upgrader. That setup requires a genuinely high-quality business whose earnings power the market has simply mis-modelled — and Elekta's issue is not that the market has mis-modelled a high-quality business, it is that the business has under-delivered for three years and the market has reasonably priced that.
  • There is a partial mis-modelling angle, fairly stated: the research reports note that consensus FY2025/26 EPS of roughly SEK 3.23 sits well below a normalised level, and that the R&D-amortisation drag is depressing reported earnings in a way some consensus models may not fully separate from underlying performance. So there is a "reported earnings understate cash earnings" sub-argument.
  • But that is an earnings-quality nuance, not a "high-quality business mis-modelled" setup. Rated [D] for RA1 — the high-quality precondition is not met (three years of margin under-delivery, a structural FX drag, a software platform that had to be impaired), so even though there is a minor mis-modelling angle on reported-versus-cash EBIT, this is not the setup that fits.

RA2 — Setup 2 fit: Big demand-change-driven EPS upgrade or earnings-power transformation (three-bullet minimum)

  • Elekta is a weak fit for the big-demand-change setup. That setup requires a large, identifiable demand inflection driving the earnings change — and Elekta's demand line is, at Triaging, roughly flat: FY2024/25 organic revenue was -0.6%, and consensus FY2025/26 growth is only low-single-digit.
  • There are demand-recovery components — China returning to growth, the US installed-base replacement pool unlocked by Evo clearance, EMEA tender strength — but these are recoveries toward normal, not a step-change in the demand level. A big-demand-change setup looks like a new structural demand wave; Elekta looks like a cyclical normalisation.
  • The earnings change in the Elekta case is driven primarily by cost and margin action, not by a demand surge. Rated [D] for RA2 — the demand-change setup does not fit, because the earnings story is a margin story sitting on a roughly flat revenue line, not a demand-inflection story. The five demand-related Core Questions under this setup (the scale, durability, visibility, breadth and pricing of the demand change) would each resolve weak; the setup is noted as not-fit and carried forward.

RA3 — Setup 3 fit: Strong corporate-change-driven EPS upgrade or earnings-power transformation (three-bullet minimum)

  • This is one of the two best-fitting setups for Elekta. The corporate-change-driven setup requires a defined change programme, a credible operator, a realistic timeline, and verifiable milestones — and Elekta has the structure of all four: the four "Must-Win Battles" and the SEK 500M cost programme (defined plan), Jakob Just-Bomholt (the operator), a roughly two-year path to the 14%-plus margin (the timeline), and concrete milestones (headcount action, FDA clearance, cost run-rate dates) that are verifiable.
  • The corporate change is genuinely the engine of the earnings story here — the EBIT-margin bridge from 11.6% toward 14% is driven by the cost programme, the operating-model redesign, and the mix shift, all of which are corporate-change actions. This matches the setup's core logic: the earnings change is largely within management control, which makes it more forecastable than a demand-driven change.
  • The qualification — and it is the reason this is a [C+] fit rather than an [A] fit — is the operator. The setup's strength depends heavily on the operator being genuinely credible, and Just-Bomholt is roughly eight months into the seat with a relevant-but-not-identical prior record (3Shape, a software/dental scale-up; prior Getinge association) and zero direct radiation-oncology experience. The Wisdom Library "operator validation horizon" model says the operator-quality verdict cannot be reliably reached before 12-18 months. Rated [C+] for RA3 — the corporate-change structure is a strong fit, but the operator leg is unproven, so the setup fits well as a pattern but not yet at high conviction. This is the primary fit setup.

RA4 — Setup 4 fit: Change-from-current clearing in a high-quality compounder (three-bullet minimum)

  • Elekta is a partial fit for the change-from-current-clearing-in-a-compounder setup. That setup requires a high-quality compounder that has a specific change-from-current item to clear, after which the compounding resumes — and Elekta has a real change-from-current list to clear (the cost programme, the order-book quality, the US share loss).
  • But the "high-quality compounder" precondition is the weak leg, the same weakness as RA1. Elekta was closer to a quality compounder pre-2019 (low-to-mid-teens EBIT margins, mid-40s gross margins, stronger FCF), but the last five years have been a margin and delivery deterioration, not a temporary disruption to a compounding base. The compounding history is not in the recent record.
  • There is a genuine forward argument that the post-recovery Elekta could resemble a compounder — software mix at ~80% gross margin growing faster than hardware, a recovered cost base, a service annuity. But that is the bull case for what Elekta becomes, not a description of what it is. Rated [D+] for RA4 — the change-from-current is real but the compounder precondition is not met in the recent record, so this setup fits only as a forward aspiration, not a present pattern. Carried forward at three-bullet depth.

  • Elekta is a moderate fit for this setup, and it is the closest of the "change-from-current clearing" family. The setup requires a solid-quality (not necessarily top-quality) business with a large, identifiable change-from-current event to clear — and Elekta's profile is genuinely "solid quality" (a real number-two duopoly position, niche monopolies, a durable installed base and service annuity) with a large change-from-current to clear (the operational reset).

Elekta is not a top-decile quality business — but it is a structurally solid one: it is not going away, it has defensible niches, and its problems are operational-execution problems rather than structural-demand or balance-sheet problems.

Rated [C] for RA5 — a genuine moderate fit: the "solid quality plus large change-from-current" shape is right, but the change-from-current is a programme rather than a single clearing event.   • Elekta is a partial fit for the trough-on-trough cyclical turn. The setup requires a solid-quality cyclical business turning up from a genuine trough — and parts of Elekta's business are cyclical (the capital-equipment line) and parts are arguably at or near a trough (China after the anti-corruption disruption, the US after the pre-Evo-clearance air-pocket, the margin after three years of decline).

The research describes "first evidence" of a trough — one strong order quarter (Q3 FY2025/26's SEK 5,418M beat), China's first positive quarter in two years, Q3 gross margin at 38.3% — but "first evidence" at one quarter is not a confirmed trough. A trough-on-trough turn setup is most reliable once the second up-quarter confirms the first.

A pure trough-on-trough cyclical turn applies to a more wholly-cyclical business. RA7 — Negative guardrail: Is there an unacceptable setup present (fraud, governance failure, terminal structural decline)? (three-bullet minimum)

RA8 — Negative guardrail: Is there a false-friend setup present (apparent recovery that masks structural deterioration)? (three-bullet minimum)

  • This is the more important E8 finding, and the honest answer is: yes, there is a real false-friend caution, and it should be the single most prominent risk flag E8 produces. The Wisdom Library "false friends" model defines the danger as a setup where the transmission mechanism from company action to EPS is unclear — and several features of the Elekta case have exactly that "looks like recovery, but is it really converting?" quality.
  • The specific false-friend features the research surfaces: (1) the Q4 FY2024/25 adjusted EBIT margin of 16.3% looks like margin recovery confirmed, but it was a single quarter, cost-action-driven, and the full-year margin actually declined; (2) the order-book write-downs are framed as "quality improvement," but two write-downs in six months can equally be read as demand destruction being relabelled as housekeeping; (3) the post-ViewRay "monopoly in high-field MRI-linac" narrative sounds powerful but obscures how small the MRI-linac total addressable market actually is; (4) service-revenue resilience looks like stability but is a lagging indicator — it stays stable for years even while the hardware installed base that feeds it is being eroded.
  • The false friends do not disqualify the case — they are not evidence the case is fake. What they do is define the monitoring discipline and the sizing discipline: if Elekta is bought on a narrative of "recovery confirmed," the buyer may be 12-18 months ahead of the genuine delivery, and the protection against that is to gate the case on the actual Q4 FY2025/26 and subsequent prints rather than on the narrative. RA8 negative guardrail: CAUTION — multiple false-friend features are present and active. This is the most important risk-management flag in E8 and it carries directly into the Section E recommendation.

E11 — Value-chain dynamicsC
E11 tests whether Elekta sits in a structurally advantaged or disadvantaged position within its value chain — relative to suppliers, customers, competitors, disruption forces, and the political/regulatory environment. The four-bullet floor applies per Required Attribute and per Core Question; no multiplier.

E11 element rating: [C-] — Elekta occupies a defensible-but-squeezed position in the value chain. [C+] E11 — RA1: Demand side — strong or fragile? (four-bullet floor)

  • The demand side is structurally durable but cyclically fragile, which nets to a genuine middle. This is a "strong customer, patient buyer" dynamic that limits Elekta's demand-side control.
  • E11 — RA1-CQ1: Is demand growing and durable? (four-bullet floor) — Yes on durability and growth at the end-demand level (cancer incidence, radiation-therapy penetration, high-single-digit market growth), but with a real cyclical-timing overlay at the equipment-order level. Rated [C+] — durable underlying demand, cyclical order timing.

(four-bullet floor) — Diversified geographically (three roughly comparable regions) but concentrated by customer type (large hospital systems and academic centres) and by modality (radiation oncology only — no diversification into adjacent treatment modalities). Net: moderately diversified, rated [C]. RA1 overall is a [C+].

[C] E11 — RA2: Competitive supportiveness or aggression? (four-bullet floor)

  • This is the most damaging Required Attribute in E11. The competitive environment is aggressive, not supportive, and it is getting more so. Varian/Siemens out-grew Elekta roughly 10% to 3% organically over three years and is reported to have captured roughly 10 points of share since the 2021 acquisition.
  • The structural problem is the bundle moat. The Wisdom Library "bundle moats in fragmented med-tech" model — itself derived partly from prior Elekta work — describes exactly this: in a two-to-three-player med-tech sub-sector, the player with multi-product bundling capability (Varian = linac plus diagnostic imaging plus the ARIA oncology information system, all inside the Siemens Healthineers franchise) progressively wins share against the point-product specialist, regardless of point-product quality, at one-to-two points per year. Elekta's Evo can be technically competitive and still lose the tender, because Evo competes at the point-product level while the Varian equivalent plugs into the Siemens imaging-and-informatics bundle.
  • The second competitive aggressor is UIH in China — taking share in lower-tier hospital segments on price, spending over 20% of revenue on R&D, and explicitly targeting overseas expansion. Elekta's defence (the "local-for-local" model) is holding its Chinese share in the high-30s for now, but the competitive pressure is structural.

(four-bullet floor) — Concentrated (a duopoly controlling roughly 90% of new linac installations), which would normally be supportive — but the concentration is asymmetric: the larger player is structurally advantaged by the bundle. Rated [C].
  • E11 — RA2-CQ2: Are competitors behaving rationally or aggressively? (four-bullet floor) — Aggressively. Varian is bundling and out-growing; UIH is price-competing; a new Varian platform is due autumn 2026. Rated [D+].

(four-bullet floor) — Deteriorating on the multi-year trend (US share from above 20% to below 15%; three-year organic growth gap to Varian), with a possible near-term stabilisation from Evo. The trend is down; the inflection is unproven. Rated [D+].

(four-bullet floor) — Yes, real ones: massive capital requirements, long clinical-validation cycles, deeply embedded software ecosystems, and a large installed base. These barriers protect Elekta's existence and its niche monopolies — they just do not protect its share against a bundling competitor. Rated [C+].

(four-bullet floor) — Yes, on the evidence: price increases have been a genuine, confirmed margin lever across recent quarters, and the Evo premium is holding. Pricing discipline is one of the more supportive competitive facts. Rated [C+]. RA2 overall is a [C] — concentrated structure and real barriers and holding pricing, offset by an aggressive competitor set and a deteriorating share trend.

[D+] E11 — RA3: Disruption forces? (four-bullet floor)

  • The disruption picture is mixed-to-mildly-negative. The biggest structural disruption question is the long-term competitive one already covered — the bundle moat — which is less "disruption" than "structural competitive disadvantage." Beyond that, there are two genuine disruption vectors.
  • Proton therapy is a long-horizon modality-disruption risk: it grows faster than photon linac therapy (a low-double-digit versus low-single-digit rate in the research) and is clinically superior for certain indications. Elekta's proton presence is small and non-core. This is a five-to-ten-year structural risk, not a Triaging-horizon one, but it is real.
  • AI is a disruption force that cuts both ways for Elekta — it enables adaptive radiotherapy (a positive, covered under E5) but it also compounds the competitor's software advantage (a negative, because Varian's larger software installed base means more training data).
