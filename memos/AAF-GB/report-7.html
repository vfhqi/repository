§2 — Key Driver #1: African FX Stabilisation / Naira Recovery

J-front verdict: FX is the dominant reported-EPS driver; even partial naira/shilling stabilisation would produce outsized translation uplift on a low base — but timing is fully exogenous [#J]

Driver definition: AAF derives ~75-80% of revenue in local African currencies (Nigerian naira, Kenyan shilling, Tanzanian shilling, Ugandan shilling, Zambian kwacha, others); USD reporting creates a translation drag when currencies depreciate [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Every 10% depreciation in the Nigerian naira reduces group reported USD revenue by ~2.5%, amplified through fixed-cost leverage into a larger EBITDA/EPS impact
  • Mechanism to SP: Market applies a "discount for currency uncertainty"; stabilisation removes this discount and re-rates the stock toward CC-implied intrinsic value

Company guide: Management does not provide explicit FX forecasts; they guide on CC revenue growth (mid-single to low-double digits) and EBITDA margin (guided ~48-50% on CC basis) [#I]

  • Management commentary: CEO Sunil Talwar has repeatedly framed FX as a "temporary headwind" and emphasised that operational KPIs (subscriber net adds, data ARPU, Airtel Money transactions) are on track
  • Conservatism vs optimism: Management framing is structurally optimistic — CC guidance is consistently met but does not help investors who book returns in GBP/USD

Track record: Nigerian naira was devalued ~35% in June 2023 following CBN float; further gradual weakness through 2024; Kenyan shilling weakened significantly before partial recovery in early 2024 [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: Group reported USD revenue declined from ~$5.0bn (FY22) to ~$4.3bn (FY23) to ~$3.8bn (FY24) to estimated ~$3.4-3.6bn (FY25), despite CC growth each year

SS consensus: Publicly available broker commentary suggests most analysts model a partial naira recovery to ~1,200-1,400/USD over a 12-24M horizon — but dispersion is wide [#I]

  • Bullish analyst view: Bulls (Berenberg, Citi noted in public sources) argue FX-adjusted EV/EBITDA is 3.5-4.0x vs EM peers at 5-6x, implying significant undervaluation
  • Sceptical analyst view: Bears argue Nigerian CBN policy credibility remains fragile and further devaluation is possible if oil revenues disappoint

Peer benchmark: MTN Group (South Africa, listed JSE) faces similar naira/FX exposure in Nigeria; MTN's Nigeria subsidiary share price has also been heavily depressed — provides some cross-validation that this is sector-wide, not AAF-specific [#A]

Macro tailwind: Nigeria's oil sector reform potential and IMF support framework provide a structural case for eventual FX stabilisation; Kenyan shilling has already partially recovered [#A]

Macro headwind: Nigerian external reserves remain under pressure; CBN credibility is incomplete; oil price sensitivity is high — any oil price decline below $70/bbl materially increases devaluation risk [#A]

Visibility: Low — this driver is entirely in the hands of CBN/Nigerian government and broader commodity markets

Estimated EPS impact: A 20% naira appreciation vs current levels would add ~$80-100m to group EBITDA on a translation basis, representing ~15-20% uplift to consensus reported EBITDA [#I]

Bear case: Naira depreciates a further 20-30% from current levels; USD EPS consensus needs to be cut again; sentiment deteriorates further; stock re-tests multi-year lows

Miss scenario: Oil price collapse + Nigerian fiscal deterioration triggers another managed devaluation; CBN FX intervention proves unsustainable

  • Miss magnitude: Further 20% naira devaluation would compress reported EBITDA by ~$60-80m, requiring ~10-15% consensus EPS cut

❌ FX baseline drag: Three consecutive years of reported revenue decline in USD terms despite consistent CC growth — no comparable EM telecom peer has sustained this translation drag duration without balance sheet stress [Cross-ref: §6]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — FX stabilisation is economically motivated but timing is exogenous and dependent on Nigerian fiscal/monetary policy variables outside management's control

§3 — Key Driver #2: Mobile Data Volume + ARPU Growth

J-front verdict: Data growth is the most reliable and execution-visible driver — structurally underpenetrated market, consistent delivery, and management's clearest operational lever [#J]

Driver definition: Subscriber base expansion, 4G network rollout, and data ARPU growth driven by rising smartphone penetration and data consumption across 14 African markets [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Data revenue ~40-45% of total service revenue; ARPU growth + volume growth = revenue compounding; EBITDA margin on incremental data is high due to network operating leverage
  • Mechanism to SP: Re-rates stock toward a "growth telecom" multiple vs a "distressed EM operator" multiple

Company guide: Management targets mid-single-digit CC data revenue growth per quarter; has guided 4G network coverage expansion toward 65-70% of sites within 12-18 months [#I]

Track record: Data revenue has grown consistently at 20%+ in CC terms over L3Y on a cumulative basis; 4G subscriber mix has risen from ~28% (FY22) to ~40%+ (FY25 estimate) [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: Data ARPU growth has been 5-10% per year in CC terms across the network; Nigeria and East Africa (Kenya, Uganda, Tanzania) are the highest-ARPU markets

SS consensus: Broadly aligned — this is the consensus "must deliver" driver; analysts model 8-12% CC data revenue CAGR over FY25-27 based on public summaries [#I]

  • Estimated revenue impact: Data revenue growing from ~$1.4bn (FY24) to ~$1.7-1.9bn (FY27E) on CC basis represents the core revenue bridge

Peer benchmark: MTN Group and Safaricom both show comparable data ARPU growth trajectories in East/West Africa; industry benchmark for 4G data ARPU in Sub-Saharan Africa is ~$2.50-3.50/month vs AAF's current ~$2.00-2.50 range, implying genuine ARPU headroom [#A]

Macro tailwind: Smartphone prices falling; device financing schemes proliferating; content consumption (video, social) driving data traffic; young demographic driving organic subscriber growth [#A]

Macro headwind: USD device costs remain elevated for sub-$100 smartphone tier; local currency depreciation raises effective handset prices; network quality concerns in some markets constrain premium ARPU migration [#A]

  • Competitive dynamics: MTN competes directly in Nigeria, Uganda, Zambia, Rwanda; Safaricom dominates Kenya; market shares appear relatively stable but MTN has been investing heavily in network quality

Visibility: High — this is the most controllable driver; network investment + tariff management are within management's operational domain

Estimated EPS impact: Assuming 10% CC data revenue CAGR over 2 years on ~$1.4bn base = ~$0.28bn incremental revenue; at 48% EBITDA margin = ~$135m incremental EBITDA; ~20% EPS uplift (before FX) [#I]

Bear case: Tariff regulation tightens (multiple African regulators have history of imposing price controls); subscriber growth slows as penetration reaches saturation in urban markets; competitive intensity increases mobile termination rate compression

Miss scenario: Nigeria regulator imposes data tariff floor rollback (there was a 2025 controversy around NCC tariff increase); data ARPU growth decelerates from 7% to 3%

  • Miss magnitude: 4% deceleration in CC data ARPU growth over 2 years = ~$80-100m EBITDA shortfall vs consensus

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — structural demand tailwinds are robust, management track record on this driver is strong, and peer benchmarks confirm the ARPU headroom thesis is real

§4 — Key Driver #3: Airtel Money Monetisation + Float Income

J-front verdict: Airtel Money is the highest-optionality driver but is also the most regulatory-sensitive; float income from interest rate environment is a near-term tailwind that may not persist [#J]

Driver definition: Airtel Money (mobile wallet and payments platform) generates revenue from transaction fees, merchant payments, float/e-money interest income, remittances, and micro-lending across 14 markets [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Airtel Money contributed ~25-28% of total service revenue in FY24; float income (interest on e-money balances) has become a material income line as African interest rates have risen
  • Mechanism to SP: Market assigns a fintech premium to Airtel Money; management has previously flagged partial monetisation / listing; structural re-rating if separated or if fintech-comparable multiples applied

Company guide: Management targets Airtel Money revenue growing faster than voice/data (double-digit CC growth); float income is highlighted as a structural positive in high-interest-rate environment [#I]

  • Management commentary: CEO has stated intent to "optimise" Airtel Money structure — partial listing discussed but no firm timeline; regulatory approvals in key markets (Nigeria, Kenya) are prerequisites

Track record: Airtel Money revenue grew ~22% in CC in FY23, ~18% in CC in FY24; transaction value growth has been strong; active wallet users approaching 40 million across the network [#A]

  • Float income: With African central bank rates at 15-25% in key markets (Nigeria, Kenya), float on ~$300-400m in e-money balances generates ~$50-80m in interest income — this is a recent structural positive

SS consensus: Analysts are broadly positive on Airtel Money trajectory; some assign a segment multiple implying $1.5-2.5bn in standalone value (vs group market cap of ~$2.5-3bn currently) [#I]

⚡ RARE: Airtel Money embedded value anomaly — public broker notes suggest Airtel Money standalone could be worth 60-90% of the current group market cap, implying the remaining telecoms business is being valued at near-zero or negative; this is a structurally unusual valuation setup that warrants scrutiny [Cross-ref: §10]

Peer benchmark: MTN MoMo (MTN's mobile money unit, partially listed on JSE) trades at a significant premium to the MTN group multiple; M-Pesa (Safaricom/Vodacom) is the regional benchmark at ~8-10x revenue — Airtel Money at 3-4x revenue (implied) appears cheap on a comparable basis [#A]

Macro tailwind: High African interest rates support float income; financial inclusion agenda supported by African governments; merchant payment digitisation accelerating post-COVID [#A]

Macro headwind: Regulatory risk is the primary constraint — Nigeria has imposed e-money issuer restrictions; Kenya is reviewing mobile money interoperability rules; Tanzania and Uganda have various wallet cap/taxation regimes [#A]

  • Regulatory risk: Forced separation of mobile money from telecom (as seen in Nigeria's NCC/CBN discussions) could impair the bundled ARPU model that currently subsidises wallet growth

Visibility: Medium — commercial trajectory is visible; regulatory timeline is opaque

Estimated EPS impact: Airtel Money growing from ~$1.0bn revenue (FY24E) to ~$1.3bn (FY27E) in CC, at ~45% EBITDA margin = ~$135m incremental EBITDA; plus float income uplift of ~$20-30m if rates stay elevated [#I]

Bear case: Nigerian CBN forces Airtel Money Nigeria into a separate entity with capital requirements; wallet revenue disrupted; float income declines as African central banks begin rate-cutting cycle

Miss scenario: Airtel Money ARPU growth decelerates from 15% CC to 6-8% due to competitive pressure from M-Pesa and bank-owned mobile wallets

  • Miss magnitude: 6% deceleration in Airtel Money CC revenue growth = ~$70-90m EBITDA shortfall over 2 years vs consensus

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — commercial trajectory is strong and structural demand is real, but regulatory uncertainty in Nigeria and Kenya introduces binary event risk that makes high-plausibility designation inappropriate

§5 — Key Driver #4: EBITDA Margin Recovery

J-front verdict: Margins have been compressed by energy costs, currency translation, and network investment; recovery toward 48%+ requires cost normalisation and FX tailwind operating together [#J]

Driver definition: Group EBITDA margin recovery from reported ~44-46% (FY24-25) toward 48-50% CC target — driven by energy cost reduction, operating leverage on revenue growth, and efficiency programmes [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Every 1pp of EBITDA margin improvement on ~$3.5bn revenue base = ~$35m EBITDA = ~5-7% EPS uplift; margin recovery is high-flow-through to earnings
  • Mechanism to SP: Re-rates stock from "EM distressed" to "EM compounder" multiple; typically 0.5-1.0 turn P/E expansion

Company guide: Management guides 47-50% EBITDA margin on CC basis; FY25 reported margin was ~44-45%, implying a 2-5pp gap to target [#I]

  • Management commentary: Energy cost inflation (diesel for towers, grid instability in Nigeria/DRC) cited as key headwind; efficiency programme targeting $150m in network cost savings over FY25-27

Track record: EBITDA margin peaked at ~~49-50% (FY21-22); compressed to ~44-46% through FY23-25 due to energy inflation and FX transaction costs; recovery has been gradual [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: Margin has been in a 44-47% CC range for 3 years; management has consistently guided toward the upper end and consistently landed in the middle

SS consensus: Consensus models ~46-47% reported EBITDA margin for FY26E, suggesting partial recovery; full recovery to 50%+ not in most base cases [#I]

Peer benchmark: MTN Group EBITDA margins have faced similar energy cost pressure in West Africa; sector median for Sub-Saharan Africa telecom operators (from public data) is 42-46% — AAF is already at or slightly above sector median, limiting magnitude of upside surprise [#A]

Macro tailwind: Fuel prices partially declining; solar/hybrid tower solutions reducing diesel dependency (management has cited 40%+ of towers now on solar hybrid) [#A]

Macro headwind: Persistent Nigerian grid instability; energy prices remain volatile; currency depreciation raises USD cost of local energy procurement [#A]

Visibility: Medium — cost efficiency is within management control; energy costs are partially exogenous

Estimated EPS impact: 2pp margin recovery on $3.5bn revenue = $70m EBITDA; at 30% tax/minority rate = ~$49m net; ~10-12% EPS uplift [#I]

Bear case: Energy costs re-inflate (oil price spike / grid deterioration); operating leverage thesis fails as revenue growth is absorbed by cost base; margin stuck at 44-45%

Miss scenario: Energy inflation returns; network expansion capex requires opex commitments that offset efficiency savings

  • Miss magnitude: Failure to recover margins keeps EPS ~10-12% below consensus FY27E; limits multiple re-rating

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — management has a credible efficiency programme but energy cost volatility and FX transaction costs are persistent headwinds; full recovery to 50% requires both execution and macro cooperation

§6 — Key Driver #5: Leverage Reduction / Balance Sheet Repair

J-front verdict: Net debt has risen in USD terms due to FX compression of EBITDA; leverage reduction is a necessary condition for dividend sustainability and re-rating — but execution depends heavily on FX [#J]

Driver definition: Net debt / EBITDA ratio reduction from ~2.2-2.5x (FY24-25 reported) toward management target of ~2.0x, achieved through FCF generation and/or asset monetisation [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Delevering reduces interest costs; lower leverage multiple re-rates equity; reduces refinancing risk premium embedded in current valuation
  • Mechanism to SP: EM telco market typically re-rates by 1-2 turns P/E for every 0.5x reduction in net leverage when starting from 2.0-2.5x

Company guide: Management has guided toward ~2.0x net debt/EBITDA as medium-term target; FY24 reported leverage was ~2.3-2.4x; no explicit timeline provided [#I]

  • Management commentary: Capex envelope guided at $750-800m for FY25-26; FCF generation targeted at $400-500m per annum after capex (CC basis)

Track record: Leverage has been rising in reported terms as USD EBITDA fell faster than USD debt; debt is a mix of USD and local-currency instruments — USD debt creates a currency mismatch risk [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: Net debt/EBITDA approximately: FY22 ~1.8x → FY23 ~2.0x → FY24 ~2.3x → FY25E ~2.3-2.5x — a consistent upward drift

❌ Leverage drift: Net debt/EBITDA has risen in 3 of last 3 years in reported terms despite management's deleveraging guidance — suggests the target is aspirational absent FX stabilisation [Cross-ref: §2]

SS consensus: Consensus models leverage at ~2.2x FY26E, with only modest reduction; full deleveraging to 2.0x requires either FX recovery or asset sale proceeds [#I]

Peer benchmark: MTN Group leverage at ~2.0x (FY24); African-focused EM telco sector median approximately 1.8-2.2x; AAF is at the upper end of peer range on a reported basis [#A]

Macro tailwind: If FX stabilises, EBITDA increases in USD terms without any operational change, mechanically reducing the reported leverage ratio [#A]

Macro headwind: Any further FX deterioration increases reported leverage; USD debt service costs are fixed while EBITDA in USD declines [#A]

  • Refinancing risk: $550m bond due in FY27; refinancing at higher rates in a risk-off EM environment would increase interest costs and compress FCF

Visibility: Low-medium — FCF generation is visible on CC basis; reported leverage path is FX-dependent

Estimated EPS impact: Deleveraging from 2.3x to 2.0x reduces interest costs by ~$40-50m; at 30% tax rate = ~$28-35m net EPS benefit; ~6-8% EPS uplift [#I]

Bear case: FX deterioration pushes reported leverage to 2.7-3.0x; refinancing risk materialises; dividend cut required; sentiment collapse

Miss scenario: FY27 bond refinancing at 150-200bps higher spread; interest cost increases by ~$25-30m; dividend cover weakens further

  • Miss magnitude: Leverage re-rating compression could wipe 20-30% off equity value if market prices in dividend risk

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — the deleveraging pathway is achievable on CC basis but requires FX stabilisation to manifest in reported numbers; bond maturity in FY27 creates a hard timing constraint

§7 — Key Driver #6: Capex Optimisation + FCF Generation

J-front verdict: Management is executing a capex reduction programme post-peak network investment; FCF conversion should improve as 4G rollout matures — but infrastructure sharing agreements are the swing factor [#J]

Driver definition: Transition from high-intensity capex cycle (4G rollout phase) to a maintenance + incremental expansion phase, improving FCF conversion from EBITDA and reducing cash drain [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Lower capex → higher FCF → stronger dividend cover → multiple re-rating; capex/revenue ratio target ~15-17% from current ~18-20%
  • Mechanism to SP: FCF yield re-rating — if FCF improves to $400m+ on a ~$2.5bn market cap, the stock trades at a 16%+ FCF yield, creating re-rating catalyst

Company guide: Management has guided capex at $750-800m for FY25-26, down from $900m+ in FY23; tower sharing agreements (Helios Towers, ATC, etc.) cited as key to reducing incremental network capex [#I]

  • Management commentary: CEO has highlighted tower monetisation and infrastructure sharing as structural capex reducers; managed services agreements reduce operational burden

Track record: Capex/revenue ratio has been elevated at ~18-22% for L3Y; FCF generation has been partially offset by spectrum payments and tax obligations [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: FCF after capex (CC basis) has been ~$350-450m per year; reported FCF lower due to FX impacts on cash translation

SS consensus: Analysts model FCF improvement to ~$400-450m by FY26-27 as capex moderates; FCF yield of 14-18% at current share price is cited as a value catalyst in bull case [#I]

Peer benchmark: MTN Group capex/revenue is ~17-18%; Safaricom capex/revenue ~15-17%; AAF's current ratio is at the higher end of African telecom peers, suggesting room for improvement as the network matures [#A]

Macro tailwind: Tower sharing market is deepening in Africa (Helios Towers, IHS Towers, ATC Africa all active); spectrum auctions are becoming more predictable; 5G is not a near-term capex obligation in most AAF markets [#A]

Macro headwind: USD equipment costs for network hardware remain elevated; local currency weakness raises effective local cost of imported equipment [#A]

  • Spectrum risk: Unexpected spectrum auction demands in Nigeria or Kenya could spike capex requirements

Visibility: Medium-high — capex trajectory is largely within management's control absent regulatory spectrum demands

Estimated EPS impact: Capex reduction from $800m to $700m = $100m FCF improvement; at current multiples = ~$0.03-0.04 EPS uplift; more important as a FCF yield / dividend support signal than a direct EPS impact [#I]

Bear case: African governments demand spectrum payments or new build requirements as conditions of licence renewal; tower sale-and-leaseback deals are renegotiated at less favourable terms; 5G spectrum auction timeline accelerated in Nigeria

Miss scenario: One large spectrum auction ($150-200m) in Nigeria or Kenya absorbs planned FCF improvement; FCF fails to improve vs consensus

  • Miss magnitude: $150m unexpected spectrum spend = ~35-40% of annual FCF; dividend cover collapses to <1x; forces guidance reduction

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — the capex reduction trajectory is credible and peer-supported, but spectrum and regulatory wildcards could absorb FCF improvement; infrastructure sharing deals are positive but slow to materialise

§8 — Key Driver #7: Nigeria Operational Recovery

J-front verdict: Nigeria is the single most volatile and high-stakes market in the portfolio — tariff reform, network quality, and subscriber recovery are all in flux post-2023-25 disruption [#J]

Driver definition: Recovery of Nigeria (AAF's largest single market, ~24-26% of group revenue) from a prolonged period of network investment catch-up, subscriber churn from quality issues, and tariff regulation uncertainty [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Nigeria recovery from 10% CC revenue decline (FY24) to flat/positive growth would add ~$80-120m to group revenue; operating leverage amplifies into EBITDA
  • Mechanism to SP: Nigeria is the most scrutinised market — any recovery would be a significant sentiment catalyst given deep investor scepticism

Company guide: Management guided Nigeria toward a "path to stabilisation" in H2 FY25; data volume growth positive; voice ARPU recovery dependent on NCC tariff framework [#I]

  • Management commentary: CEO acknowledged Nigeria as "a work in progress" in FY25 results; cited NCC's January 2025 approval of tariff increases (first in 11 years) as a structural positive
  • Tariff reform: NCC approved up to 50% tariff increase for mobile operators in Nigeria in January 2025 — this is a significant revenue recovery catalyst if operationally executed

Track record: Nigeria service revenue declined ~15-20% in USD terms through FY23-24; CC performance was flat to slightly positive but masked by naira devaluation; subscriber market share has been under pressure from MTN Nigeria [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: AAF Nigeria market share approximately 27-29% (vs MTN Nigeria ~37-38%); AAF has been a market share loser over L3Y in Nigeria's mobile market

SS consensus: Sell-side is broadly cautious on Nigeria — it is the highest-uncertainty market in the group; some analysts assign a negative "Nigeria risk premium" to AAF's valuation [#I]

  • Bullish analyst view: Bulls argue NCC tariff reform is transformative — 50% tariff increase directly feeds through to ARPU recovery at ~45% EBITDA margins
  • Sceptical analyst view: Bears argue Nigeria subscriber quality is deteriorating, competitive intensity vs MTN Nigeria is structurally unfavourable, and tariff increases may accelerate sim-swapping to MTN

Peer benchmark: MTN Nigeria (separate listed entity) provides a direct benchmark — MTN Nigeria has also suffered FX compression but has maintained stronger subscriber market share and ARPU, suggesting AAF Nigeria has a competitive disadvantage beyond the macro [#A]

⚡ RARE: Nigeria tariff reform timing — the January 2025 NCC approval of first tariff increases in 11 years is an industry-specific event with no precedent in the L11Y period; if operators capture 30-40% of the permitted increase vs full 50%, the ARPU uplift is still the single largest discrete upside event in the near-term; market may not have fully modelled the EBITDA sensitivity [Cross-ref: §2]

Macro tailwind: Nigerian macro stabilisation (Tinubu government reform programme); fuel subsidy removal long-term positive for fiscal health; CBN rate normalisation [#A]

Macro headwind: Inflation at 30%+; consumer purchasing power severely compressed; naira weakness persists; banking sector USD liquidity constraints [#A]

Visibility: Low — Nigeria is the least predictable market; regulatory, competitive, and macro variables all uncertain

Estimated EPS impact: Nigeria CC revenue recovery from flat to +8-10% growth = ~$100m incremental revenue; at 44% EBITDA margin = ~$44m EBITDA; ~8-10% group EPS uplift [#I]

Bear case: MTN Nigeria aggressively takes market share post-tariff reform; Nigerian government reverses or limits tariff implementation; naira depreciates further 20%; Nigeria becomes a value-destructive market

Miss scenario: NCC tariff implementation is delayed or partially rolled back; subscriber churn accelerates as consumers resist price increases; Nigeria revenue flat vs consensus +5-8% recovery assumption

  • Miss magnitude: Nigeria miss of $80-100m revenue vs consensus = ~$35-45m EBITDA miss; ~8-10% group EPS downside; significant sentiment impact given Nigeria's optionality framing

Plausibility verdict: Low-medium plausibility — the tariff reform catalyst is real and significant, but Nigeria-specific execution risks (competitive intensity, consumer affordability, regulatory consistency) are unusually high; this driver requires multiple variables to align simultaneously

§10 — Driver Synthesis + Sceptical Synthesis

J-front verdict: The strongest bearish case for AAF-GB is that all 7 drivers are positively correlated with the same macro variable (African FX/Nigeria stability) — meaning the investment thesis is less diversified than the 7-driver count implies [#J]

Driver dependency: D1 is not independent of D4 (margins), D5 (leverage), D6 , or D7 (Nigeria) — FX deterioration simultaneously impairs reported margins, increases reported leverage, reduces FCF translation, and exacerbates Nigeria's competitive position [#A]

Correlation across drivers: Only D2 (data volume growth) and D3 (Airtel Money CC trajectory) are partially insulated from the FX driver — they have independent operational catalysts (smartphone penetration, fintech adoption) that persist even in a weak-FX environment [#J]

Multi-driver risk: If FX deteriorates, 4-5 of 7 drivers simultaneously disappoint; this is not a "multi-variable thesis with diverse risk factors" — it is a concentrated bet on African macro stabilisation with operational execution as a modifier [#A]

  • Joint probability: If D2 (data) = 80% plausibility independently, but D1/D4/D5/D6/D7 are all ~50-60% and correlated at 70%+, the effective joint probability of thesis delivery is materially below the arithmetic product of individual probabilities

Setup archetype: "FX-optionality compounder with fintech overlay" — the setup pattern-matches to deep-value EM recovery plays where the stock is priced for continued deterioration and any stabilisation produces outsized returns [#J]
