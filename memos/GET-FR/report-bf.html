EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (BLUF)

Getlink operates a unique, long-duration cross-Channel infrastructure concession paired with rail freight and energy interconnection divisions, leveraging its monopolistic tunnel asset to generate highly cash-generative, inflation-linked returns despite exceptional resilience through near-term volume cyclicality and electricity market normalization. The group delivers infrastructure-level EBITDA margins and strong free cash flow, though significant deleveraging provides balance-sheet optionality while peer benchmarking remains constrained by data limitations. The concentrated but fiercely competitive cross-Channel market shows signs of rationalisation, with regulatory interventions structurally favouring Getlink's low-carbon fixed-link model over maritime peers.

Getlink operates the Channel Tunnel concession until 2086, providing cross-Channel rail transport, rail freight logistics, and electricity interconnection between France and the UK through its Eurotunnel division managing the fixed link infrastructure, Europorte offering end-to-end rail freight traction across France, Belgium, and Germany, and ElecLink operating a 1GW high-voltage direct current interconnector running through the tunnel. The group generated €1.595 billion in 2025, with Eurotunnel contributing 75% of revenue, ElecLink contributing 14% reflecting normalisation in energy markets and a temporary outage, and Europorte contributing 11% driven by

Eurotunnel's direct EBITDA margin approaching 55%, driven by fixed employee benefits and regulatory costs, while operating profit reached €609 million representing a 38.2% margin with stable depreciation charges of €229 million. The fixed link enjoys insurmountable barriers to entry, offering substantial operational advantages over alternative transport modes by providing faster, more frequent, and highly reliable crossings completely immune to adverse maritime weather conditions, boasting a massive environmental advantage generating up to 73 times fewer carbon emissions for passenger transport than ferries. Passengers and freight hauliers prioritize speed and reliability, enabling Getlink to command a pricing premium over ferries, with LeShuttle capturing a 56.1% car market share in 2025 demonstrating strong brand loyalty and willingness-to-pay for premium service, while freight customers value schedule frequency and predictability allowing the Truck Shuttle to maintain a 35.4% market share despite overcapacity. The infrastructure operates with significant unutilized capacity, with current capacity utilization sitting below 50% providing massive headroom to accommodate new entrants or increased frequencies, designed to handle over 20 million high-speed passengers annually, roughly double its recent peak. 5 The Short Straits transport corridor operates as a tight oligopoly dominated by four major infrastructure and maritime operators, with Getlink's fixed link competing directly with three main ferry operators–P&O Ferries, DFDS Seaways, and Irish Ferries–deploying fleets out of Dover and Calais, commanding essentially the entire Roll-

On/Roll-Off accompanied transport market across the vital Short Straits corridor as the shortest, quickest, and most heavily trafficked path for trade. Ferry operators have historically engaged in intense price wars to capture market share in an oversupplied maritime market, with the entry of Irish Ferries in late 2021 exacerbating existing capacity gluts and sparking aggressive tariff reductions, as 13 ferries currently operate between Dover and Calais representing an unsustainable peak in deployed vessel capacity. The inclusion of the maritime sector in the European Union Emissions Trading System acts as a structural penalty expected to escalate operating costs to between €6 and €8 per ferry ticket in 2025, with the United Kingdom also considering expanding its own domestic carbon trading scheme to include cross-Channel maritime activities, forcing maritime competitors to adjust base pricing upwards while Getlink's electrified infrastructure model completely insulates it from these specific fossil-fuel and seafarer wage shocks, allowing it to either regain lost market share or execute selective, margin-enhancing tariff increases. 9

Getlink occupies a monopolistic, high-margin infrastructure bottleneck within the cross-Channel value chain, commanding superior pricing power and approximately 54% EBITDA margins versus structurally-challenged maritime peers, primarily occupying the infrastructure operation and tolling stage where the Fixed Link acts as an irreplaceable physical bottleneck for high-speed passenger rail and carbon-efficient freight transport, capturing the highest margin pool yielding roughly 54% group EBITDA margins. The tolling and capacity-allocation stage captures the vast majority of the value chain's economic rent, as massive sunk costs and geographical constraints completely insulate the Channel Tunnel from direct physical replication by competitors, enabling a roughly 38% operating profit margin vastly outperforming downstream logistics operators. Environmental regulations are structurally shifting the value chain's margin pool away from maritime operators and toward electrified rail, with the inclusion of maritime shipping in the EU Emissions Trading System imposing significant new carbon taxes directly on ferry operators while the FuelEU Maritime directive mandates clean fuel usage requiring massive downstream capital expenditure for competing maritime fleets, as French anti-social dumping laws impose strict minimum wages and resting times on cross-Channel maritime crews neutralising the labour arbitrage previously exploited by low-cost ferry operators, forcing ferry cost bases structurally upward and mechanically improving Getlink's relative margin position and long-term pricing headroom.

Getlink commands exceptional pricing leverage and structural lock-in with major railway clients alongside strong retail satisfaction mitigating cyclical volume risks, with national railways and Eurostar collectively representing the largest customer bloc generating 26% of total group revenue in 2025, structurally secured by the mandatory usage of the tunnel by high-speed rail operators, as Eurostar alone carried 11.8 million passengers in 2025 reaching an all-time high and cementing its critical revenue contribution. The Railway Usage Contract binds national railways and operators until 2052 providing exceptional long-term revenue visibility, guaranteeing fixed annual charges and variable tolls per passenger for high-speed trains with tolls escalating mechanically via a defined formula tied to inflation minus 1.1% securing real returns. Getlink captures roughly 90% of passenger shuttle bookings directly online disintermediating third-party travel agents, yielding profound data insights enabling granular targeted yield management while preserving gross margins by eliminating external distributor commissions and booking fees.

Exceptional direct-to-customer channel dominance mitigates downstream risks, but acute upstream dependency on bespoke rail suppliers creates material execution vulnerabilities during asset renewals, as the group maintains a fragmented overall base of approximately 1,900 active suppliers with the top 180 suppliers accounting for roughly 80% of total purchasing expenditure and over 99% of Tier 1 suppliers concentrated in Europe. Manufacturers of bespoke railway equipment hold exceptional leverage over Getlink due to the hyper- consolidated nature of the European rail industry, as rolling stock and fixed link installations are supplied in very small volumes by a highly restricted number of specialist manufacturers creating an acute dependency limiting the group's ability to drive aggressive commercial procurement terms for capital equipment. The concentration of specialist suppliers creates material execution risks for major infrastructure and rolling stock renewal programmes, materialising when a key supplier unexpectedly terminated its contract for the Passenger Shuttle Mid-Life modernisation programme forcing Getlink into arbitration proceedings in April 2025 and necessitating a complex reorganisation of the renovation schedule.

The cross-Channel market remains fiercely competitive with excess maritime capacity, though regulatory cost pressures are forcing ferry operators toward rationalised pricing and capacity sharing, as Getlink operates as the structural leader across the Short Straits transport market maintaining absolute dominance in high-speed passenger rail while commanding a premium position in the roll-on/roll-off freight segment. Maritime competitors are now rationalising capacity to protect deteriorating operating margins, with P&O Ferries and Irish Ferries implementing a space charter agreement in 2024 allowing freight and passenger space sharing on the Dover-Calais route, while ferry operators are being forced to raise base tariffs to cover regulatory inflation as the EU Emissions Trading System significantly increases carbon costs for maritime shipping and anti-social dumping laws now mandate minimum wages for cross-Channel seafarers. New entrants represent a highly credible threat to Eurostar's historic monopoly, as Virgin Group and FS Italiane are preparing to launch competing cross- Channel rail services, with this downstream fragmentation being highly beneficial for Getlink as the infrastructure provider. Management's constructive pricing narrative strongly aligns with competitor rationalisation and downstream rail expansion, though upstream supplier concentration creates isolated infrastructure execution frictions, as downstream high-speed rail operators are actively expanding capacity to meet surging cross-border leisure demand with Eurostar's passenger volumes growing by 6% in 2025 driven by structural shifts toward low-carbon transport and confirming a major fleet expansion order for up to 50 new high-speed trainsets. Maritime competitors acknowledge that aggressive discounting and overcapacity on the Short Straits heavily eroded their recent profitability, with DFDS reporting a 7% year-over-year drop in revenue per lane meter as of mid-2024 as rival ferry operators were forced to slash tariffs to defend market share following the disruptive entry of Irish Ferries. Competing ferry operators are executing double-digit tariff hikes to offset rising regulatory burdens, with brokers noting that DFDS and P&O are pushing 24% to 29% tariff hikes for passenger cars in early 2025, fundamentally rebuilding Getlink's relative pricing power across the Channel.

Getlink represents a high-quality infrastructure monopoly governed by disciplined capital allocation and widening pricing power, though requiring heroic management navigation of near-term cyclical and regulatory headwinds, as management demonstrates strong execution through macroeconomic shocks with the group targeting €1 billion

in consolidated EBITDA by 2030, pivoting from pandemic recovery to a structural growth and yield-optimisation phase that management views as structurally de-risked, relying heavily on optimising the existing asset base rather than depending on transformational acquisitions. The Channel Tunnel is protected by a bi-national concession agreement running until 2086 effectively barring any direct fixed-link physical replication, benefiting from massive deterring sunk costs as an infrastructure bottleneck capturing roughly 25% of all UK-EU trade value, while high-speed rail operators are entirely dependent on the fixed link possessing zero alternative physical routes for their cross-Channel services, with the 35-minute transit time providing a profound structural advantage over 110-minute ferry crossings cementing high-yield customer loyalty. With roughly 46% of Eurotunnel costs tied to employee benefits incremental revenue flows almost entirely to EBITDA, as the structural lack of fuel-intensive maritime operations allows Getlink to maintain infrastructure-like margins vastly superior to standard transport peers, with consolidated EBITDA margin reaching a robust 53.9% in 2025 driven by the highly profitable Eurotunnel and ElecLink segments, while the asset generated €374 million in free cash flow in 2025 demonstrating excellent conversion capabilities despite normalising European energy markets. 28

Structural decarbonisation tailwinds and High-Speed Rail capacity expansions fundamentally outweigh near-term biometric border disruptions securing a durable long-term growth trajectory, as the Channel Tunnel's original £10 billion construction cost entirely shields the core infrastructure from direct physical replication by any prospective fixed-link competitor with subsequent indirect taxpayer investments of approximately £15 billion in surrounding rail infrastructure further elevating the replacement cost barrier, while Getlink holds an exclusive bi-national concession to operate the fixed link until 2086 legally prohibiting alternative tunnel developments. The EU's Entry/Exit System introduces complex facial and fingerprint checks threatening near-term passenger fluidity at constricted maritime and rail bottlenecks with concurrent rollouts of the UK's Electronic Travel Authorisation and the EU's ETIAS compounding the friction risk for cross-border leisure traffic, though Getlink mitigates EES friction through an €80 million investment in pre-registration zones, ANPR technology, and AI-driven digital terminal twins, with new Coquelles and Folkestone biometric installations capable of processing up to 2,000 passengers and 700 cars per hour preserving the speed premium over space-constrained ferry ports. Three bankable new entrants–Evolyn, Virgin Group, and Heuro–are actively raising capital and ordering rolling stock to challenge Eurostar's cross-Channel monopoly with FS Italiane Group and Certares backing a €400 million investment in 10 new high-speed trains for a 2029 launch, as the combination of Eurostar's 50-train fleet expansion and new entrant capacity could realistically double the High-Speed Rail TAM by the early 2030s while Getlink's base infrastructure can handle up to 1,000 trains per day accommodating massive growth without requiring proportional capital expenditure.
