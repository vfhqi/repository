Hellenic Telecommunications Organisation SA (OTE Group)
Date: 04-May-26 | Stage: IG | Source: Claude (knowledge base to May 2026) | Query: BD

1. BLUF

OTE Group is the dominant fixed and mobile telecommunications operator in Greece, with secondary mobile operations in Romania, majority-owned by Deutsche Telekom (~48%). The primary investment case is a quality incumbent telco at an inflection point: near-zero leverage (a rare outlier in European telecoms), an accelerating FTTH rollout underwritten by EU RRP capital flows, a 5G network that ranks among the most technically advanced in Europe on a standalone-architecture basis, and a capital returns programme that can sustain a 5-7% dividend yield while funding the fibre buildout. This warrants progression to Triaging. Signal: Green / Yellow — the business quality and balance sheet are clearly above-average for a European telco; the key variable is whether the change forces (network upgrade cycle + ICT/public sector revenue inflection) are large and durable enough to generate earnings growth, given Greece's structurally thin ARPU base and competitive intensity in Romania.

Group structure. OTE SA is the holding company. Its principal operating subsidiaries are:

  • Cosmote Mobile Telecommunications SA — the Greek mobile operator, 100% owned by OTE. Cosmote is the leading mobile brand in Greece by revenue share and network quality. This is the highest-quality and highest-margin segment.
  • OTE Romania / Telekom Romania Mobile — OTE's retained Romanian asset post the fixed-line exit. OTE Group sold the Telekom Romania fixed-line business to Orange Romania in a transaction that completed in 2023-2024, retaining the mobile-only entity. The mobile business trades as Digi rival in a price-competitive market.
  • OTE Group ICT — the group's IT managed services, cloud hosting, and digital transformation arm for Greek enterprise and public sector clients. The fastest-growing segment, benefiting from EU Recovery and Resilience Plan digitisation spending flowing into Greece.
  • OTE Estate / OTENET — property and legacy internet subsidiaries; not strategically significant at group level.

Deutsche Telekom's majority stake is operationally significant: it provides network technology transfer (notably the 5G standalone architecture rollout, modelled on DT Germany's approach), executive-level alignment, and a credible governance anchor. DT has been a gradual buyer of OTE shares over the years, and there is periodic market speculation about a full buyout, though no formal process has been announced.

4. Market Position

Greece — Fixed Broadband (incumbent dominant).
OTE is the regulated wholesale access provider for the Greek fixed network. Its retail fixed broadband market share is approximately 45-50%, declining only slowly as alternative operators (primarily Wind Hellas/Nova and Vodafone Greece, now merged into United Group / Nova Broadband) take share. The critical strategic development is the FTTH rollout: OTE is building fibre-to-the-home across Greece under a combination of proprietary investment and EU co-funding. Approximately 2-3 million homes passed by the FTTH network as of early 2026, targeting 3-4 million over the medium term. This is a genuine structural asset: once built, FTTH locks in customer relationships and compresses churn, and OTE's head start as incumbent means it will pass the infrastructure finish line well ahead of challengers.

PSTN/voice revenues continue to structurally decline but are now a small enough proportion of fixed revenues that the drag is manageable. Fixed B2B (enterprise connectivity, leased lines, managed WAN) is a stable and reasonably profitable segment.

Greece — Mobile (Cosmote): market leader.
Cosmote is the number one mobile operator in Greece by revenue share (~40%) and by network quality. Its two main rivals are Vodafone Greece and Wind Hellas/Nova; the market has consolidated to a stable three-player structure. Cosmote's premium positioning supports above-average ARPU. 5G rollout is the current network investment focus, and Cosmote's deployment is a genuine standout in the European context:

  • 🚩 RARE: Standalone 5G architecture, 70%+ population coverage. Standalone 5G means the network does not rely on the 4G core for signalling — it is a fully cloud-native, software-defined mobile core. This is rare among European operators; most large Western European incumbents are still running Non-Standalone 5G. The SA architecture enables lower latency, network slicing for B2B, and significantly lower long-run unit costs. By May 2026, Cosmote's 5G population coverage at 70%+ is top-5% among European mobile operators in terms of deployment progress. The DT technology relationship is the enabler — Cosmote followed DT Germany's SA architecture migration path.

Romania — Mobile (Telekom Romania Mobile): secondary position in competitive market.
Following the disposal of the fixed-line business to Orange Romania (transaction completing in the 2023-2024 period), OTE's Romanian exposure is now mobile-only. Telekom Romania Mobile holds approximately 15-20% revenue share in the Romanian mobile market, competing against Digi (price disruptor), Orange Romania (market leader), and Vodafone Romania. Romania is a lower-ARPU, higher-competition market than Greece. OTE's strategic posture here is profitability management and operational simplification, not growth investment. The fixed-line exit was the right strategic call — it removed a structurally challenged asset and simplified the group structure considerably. There is a reasonable question about whether the Romanian mobile asset is a long-term hold or whether OTE ultimately exits this position as well.

ICT / Cloud — OTE Group ICT:
The B2B digital services arm is the group's growth option. OTE Group ICT provides IT managed services, cloud hosting (including on the COSMOTE Cloud platform), cybersecurity, and digital transformation consulting to Greek corporates and the public sector. The EU Recovery and Resilience Plan is the near-term catalyst: Greece has ~EUR 36bn in RRP funds, of which a material portion is targeted at digital infrastructure and public administration digitisation. OTE Group ICT is positioned as a primary delivery partner for this spend. The Q4 2025 revenue growth of +57.8% year-on-year signals that this is moving from project-level awards to sustained revenues.

5. Business Model

Fixed (Greece).
Revenue model combines: (i) regulated wholesale access fees from alternative operators using OTE's copper/fibre network; (ii) retail broadband subscriptions (copper-based VDSL and FTTH); (iii) PSTN voice (declining); (iv) enterprise and B2B connectivity (leased lines, SD-WAN, managed connectivity). The FTTH upgrade cycle is capital-intensive now but structurally positive: fibre customers generate higher ARPU, lower churn, and lower maintenance cost than copper customers over time. The wholesale access regime is set by EETT (the Greek telecoms regulator), and OTE's regulated access charges are periodically reviewed — this is a source of regulatory risk but historically has been managed constructively.

Mobile (Cosmote — Greece).
Revenue model: mobile service revenues (voice, data subscriptions), handset sales (lower margin), roaming. The convergence opportunity — bundling Cosmote mobile with OTE fixed broadband — is the key ARPU and churn management lever. As FTTH rollout accelerates, OTE can offer a genuinely superior convergent product (high-speed fibre broadband + premium 5G mobile) versus cable-based or fixed-wireless competitors. 5G upsell to premium data tiers is an incremental revenue driver over the medium term, though Greek consumers are ARPU-constrained relative to Western European markets.

Romania (Mobile).
Revenue model: straightforward mobile service revenues in a competitive, price-driven market. Operating model has been restructured post fixed-line exit — leaner cost base, focused on profitable customer retention rather than market share acquisition. Contribution to group FCF is positive but modest.

ICT / Digital Services.
Revenue model: project-based fees (implementation), recurring managed service fees, and cloud subscription revenue. The public sector pipeline (RRP-funded digital transformation) is project-by-project in nature — this creates some lumpiness. The strategic goal is to convert initial project revenues into multi-year managed service contracts, which would build a recurring revenue base. This is still early-stage as a share of group revenue.

Balance sheet. OTE is one of the very few European telcos operating at or close to a net cash / near-zero-leverage position. Most European telco peers carry 2.0-3.5x net debt/EBITDA. This is a genuine structural differentiator. The consequence is threefold: (i) the company can fund the FTTH/5G capex cycle entirely from internal FCF without balance sheet strain; (ii) it can sustain a 100% FCF payout ratio on dividends without leverage creeping higher; (iii) it provides downside protection — in a sector where leverage has been a recurring source of equity risk (look at Altice, Telecom Italia), OTE has essentially no financial distress risk.

FCF generation. The FCF conversion is strong — EBITDA-to-FCF conversion is in the range of 35-45%, which is above average for a capex-heavy incumbent telco. The FTTH capex peak is likely to be 2024-2026 as rollout intensity increases; beyond this peak, maintenance capex should drop and FCF conversion should improve further.

Dividend. OTE has maintained a progressive dividend through the Greek economic recovery period. The current yield at market prices is in the 5-7% range, which is competitive with or superior to European telco peers — and in OTE's case, it is funded without leverage, which is the key distinction.

Comparison to Sub-cohort A peers:
- OBEL-BE (Proximus): carries ~2.0x net debt, facing copper-to-fibre transition with elevated capex, regulatory pressures on Belgian business. Lower FCF quality.
- KPN-NL: ~2.5x net debt, ahead of OTE on fibre rollout completion but lower growth optionality. Similar dividend yield but balance sheet is more stretched.
- SCMN-CH (Swisscom): investment-grade balance sheet but carries more leverage than OTE; Swiss ARPU is much higher but growth optionality limited given already-saturated market.
- OTE's combination of low leverage + FTTH optionality + 5G SA architecture + ICT growth arm puts it in a distinctively positive position relative to this sub-cohort.

7. Strategic Priorities

Priority 1 — FTTH rollout acceleration.
OTE is midway through the most important strategic investment of its generation: building a fibre-to-the-home network across Greece. Approximately 2-3 million homes passed as of early 2026, against a medium-term target of 3-4 million. The rollout is co-funded in part by EU structural and cohesion funds (including RRP), which reduces OTE's net investment burden and accelerates the timeline. The strategic logic is straightforward: a completed FTTH network significantly raises the infrastructure barrier to entry for challengers, locks in customer relationships through higher switching costs and superior product quality, and enables ARPU accretion versus the legacy copper product.

Priority 2 — Cosmote 5G: standalone architecture leadership.
Cosmote has taken the deliberate and technically demanding path of deploying standalone 5G rather than the cheaper and quicker non-standalone approach. By May 2026, 70%+ population coverage on SA 5G is a top-5% achievement in European mobile. The business case has two components: (i) near-term — 5G premium data tiers and improved spectral efficiency (lower cost per gigabit vs 4G); (ii) medium-term — network slicing for B2B/enterprise use cases, particularly relevant for the ICT growth strategy.

Priority 3 — ICT/RRP revenue inflection.
The Q4 2025 ICT revenue growth of +57.8% year-on-year is the most important change signal in recent results. EU RRP digitisation spending is the immediate catalyst, but the long-term prize is establishing OTE Group ICT as the dominant managed services and cloud provider for the Greek public sector and large enterprise. This mirrors what DT has built with T-Systems in Germany — a telco-adjacent B2B services franchise with recurring revenues and high switching costs. At present, ICT is ~5% of group revenue; a medium-term scenario where this doubles to 10%+ would be a material earnings driver.

Priority 4 — Romania simplification.
The disposal of the fixed-line Romanian business was the right call. The residual mobile-only asset is being managed for profitability. The question is whether OTE will eventually exit the Romanian mobile position entirely — the strategic case for retaining it is not obvious if margin improvement stalls. A full disposal would be simplifying and would release capital for additional buybacks or further Greek investment.

Priority 5 — Capital returns.
Near-zero leverage enables a 100% FCF payout strategy. The current dividend is progressive and the yield at recent prices is competitive. If the FTTH capex cycle peaks in 2025-2026 and FCF begins to accelerate post-peak, the optionality for a buyback programme supplements the dividend return. This is a genuinely unusual capital allocation position for a European telco incumbent.

8. Ownership and Governance

Deutsche Telekom (~48% stake).
The DT relationship is the single most important governance variable. DT provides:
- Technology and operational transfer: SA 5G architecture is the clearest example. Cosmote is effectively an implementation testbed for DT's own network engineering approaches in a less complex market. This gives OTE access to network technology that would otherwise require larger scale or higher investment.
- Strategic discipline: DT has a well-documented track record of running subsidiaries efficiently (T-Mobile US being the flagship example). Its influence on OTE's capital allocation and network strategy has been constructive.
- Stake trajectory: DT has been a gradual net buyer of OTE shares. There is a floor on the stock from the optionality of DT potentially moving toward a full buyout or squeeze-out if they cross 50% and eventually move to 90%+. This optionality is not priced into consensus intrinsic value frameworks.

CEO Kostas Nebis.
Appointed as CEO in 2023-2024, replacing long-serving CEO Michalis Tsamaz who built the post-crisis recovery franchise. Nebis has an operator background and has signalled strategic acceleration on two fronts: FTTH pace and ICT commercialisation. Early indicators from results commentary suggest he is executing rather than re-strategising. No material governance red flags identified.

Greek state (~5% stake).
Residual state ownership is small enough not to be an active governance issue. Historically, the Greek state's role in OTE has generated political noise around regulation, employment, and pricing — these risks are structurally reduced since DT took majority control, but they do not disappear entirely. Regulatory risk around wholesale access pricing and universal service obligations remains.

Free float (~47%).
Well-diversified institutional investor base. OTE is owned by European telco and emerging market equity funds, as well as yield-focused investors attracted by the dividend. No material activist or concentrated minority shareholder risk.

#A — Key analytical observations.

Five observations stand out versus the European telco universe:

  1. Balance sheet is the single biggest outlier. 🚩 RARE Net debt/EBITDA of 0.3-0.5x is genuinely anomalous for a fixed-mobile incumbent of this scale. European telco average is 2.5-3.0x. The consequence is that OTE's equity is not leveraged equity with all the associated downside risk — it is a clean claim on a cash-generative infrastructure business. This is a structural quality premium that screens well.

  2. 5G SA architecture leadership is real, not marketing. 🚩 RARE Standalone architecture is technically demanding and requires meaningful capex and engineering commitment. Most European operators are deferring this migration. Cosmote's completion to 70%+ coverage positions it to extract B2B commercial value (network slicing, private 5G) faster than peers. DT's influence is the causal factor.

  3. ICT/RRP is the change force to track at Triaging. The EU RRP spending cycle in Greece is a multi-year event, not a one-quarter spike. If OTE Group ICT is genuinely building a pipeline of recurring managed service contracts (not just project-by-project implementation fees), this becomes a compounding growth driver. The +57.8% Q4 2025 number needs to be interrogated for quality — is this contracted recurring revenue or lumpy project completion revenue?

  4. Greece macro risk is lower than the headline suggests. The primary Greece risk — sovereign/macro instability — is substantially reduced post the 2010-2018 crisis and subsequent IMF/EU support. GDP growth has recovered, consumer confidence is above pre-2020 levels, and the EU RRP is effectively a structural transfer mechanism keeping Greek public investment elevated for 2024-2026. This matters because OTE's revenues are predominantly domestic Greece — a stable macro environment supports stable-to-growing ARPU.

  5. Romanian mobile is a managed drag, not a crisis. The fixed-line disposal was the correct strategic move. The residual mobile asset is manageable. The risk is that competitive dynamics in Romanian mobile continue to deteriorate (Digi is a genuine price disruptor) and that margins compress further. If Romanian mobile becomes a value-destructive division, the correct move is disposal. At present, it is subscale but cash-contributing.
