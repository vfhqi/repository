KEY FINDINGS - DIAGNOSTIC EDGE: Three questions above all others discriminate bull/bear/false-friend — (1) the mechanism by which ITULAZAX/ACARIZAX gross margin inflects after FY26 Phase 3 investments complete; (2) whether SLIT tablet volume growth in the US is genuinely share-taking from SCIT or is cannibalising ALK's own OTC/legacy revenue; (3) whether management's 2025 royalty/out-licensing revenue guidance is structurally recurring or a pull-forward — these three answers alone reset the EPS trajectory with high confidence

  • BULL CASE PRIOR: Bull thesis rests on US SLIT tablet penetration expanding a structurally underpenetrated market (≈5% AIT compliance rate in US), ALK capturing disproportionate share as first/only dual SLIT tablet player, and Europe royalty streams providing a floor — management fluency on US payer access and physician education spend is the single best confirmatory signal

  • BEAR CASE PRIOR: Bear thesis is that US SG&A investment absorbs all gross margin improvement through at least FY27, EPS inflection is perpetually 18 months away, and the real optionality (house dust mite/grass pollen dual-therapy) is priced in before it materialises — management avoidance on SG&A breakeven per-rep metric is the single best confirmatory signal for the bear

  • FALSE-FRIEND RISK: False-friend profile is strongest around the royalty/licensing line — if European partner royalties are front-loaded and step down post-2026, the "recurring high-margin revenue" framing conceals a revenue cliff that suppresses EBIT margins precisely when US losses are peaking

  • INCENTIVE ALERT: CEO Hellmann's LTI vesting is understood to be tied in part to EPS milestones and revenue growth — this creates systematic incentive to emphasise near-term revenue visibility and downplay SG&A build; questions that force a margin-per-cohort disclosure are harder to deflect with top-line framing

  • NON-OBVIOUS PRIORITY: The most diagnostic unconventional question is: "What is the average number of physician offices your US reps visited in Q1 2026 that had ZERO prior SLIT tablet prescriptions — and what was the conversion rate?" — this surfaces US market development depth vs. penetration concentration risk in ways that revenue/rep metrics do not

  • SCEPTICAL NOTE: Management has delivered multi-year US growth messaging at every capital markets event since 2019; fluent, confident answers on US trajectory should be treated as potentially rehearsed rather than confirmatory — ask for the metric that disappoints them, not the one that excites them

3. THEME §1 — Revenue Drivers + Visibility

J-front verdict: Revenue uncertainty is HIGHEST on US SLIT volume trajectory and lowest on European royalty floor — questions should force disaggregation by geography/product rather than accepting blended top-line guidance

Q1 — Volume vs. price split, US SLIT tablets

  • Question: "In the US, what proportion of FY25 SLIT tablet revenue growth came from volume (new prescriptions) vs. average net price per prescription — and how do you expect that ratio to evolve in FY26?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely cite predominantly volume-driven growth, reference payer coverage wins, and decline to give precise price/volume disaggregation
  • Good answer signature: Precise split (e.g. 70/30 volume/price), commentary on gross-to-net evolution, reference to specific payer tier wins in CY25 formularies
  • Bad answer signature: Deflects to blended US revenue growth %; cannot distinguish volume from price; references only "improving payer access" without formulary specifics
  • Follow-up probe: "Which of the five largest PBMs have you achieved Tier 2 or better formulary status with — and what is your current weighted coverage rate?"
  • Diagnostic note: A precise volume/price split that shows >60% volume growth is BULL-confirmatory (market development, not just price leverage); a price-dominated answer or a deflection supports the BEAR hypothesis that US penetration is stalling and growth is being sustained by net price management — ACH matrix highest-uncertainty item

Q2 — Churn/retention, European prescription base

  • Question: "What is the annual patient discontinuation rate on ACARIZAX and GRAZAX in your core European markets — and is that rate stable, improving, or deteriorating?"
  • Expected answer: Management will reference 3-year treatment compliance as a structural challenge; likely cite 30-50% completion rates consistent with published AIT literature; may not volunteer market-specific data
  • Good answer signature: Quotes specific retention cohort data by market; links retention to clinical outcomes programmes; shows improving trend YoY
  • Bad answer signature: Deflects to clinical trial completion rates rather than real-world prescription-fill persistence; cites 3-year course completion without year-1 and year-2 dropoff rates
  • Follow-up probe: "What is your year-1 prescription refill rate in Germany and Denmark specifically — and how does that compare to two years ago?"
  • Diagnostic note: Deteriorating European retention is a FALSE-FRIEND signal — stable-looking revenue can mask replacement-treadmill dynamics where new patient acquisition is running faster than it appears just to offset dropout; cross-refs Q19 ACH item on European base sustainability

Q3 — Forward order book / payer pipeline

  • Question: "How many US commercial payer contracts covering SLIT tablets are up for formulary renegotiation in 2026 — and what is your net coverage position if all pending negotiations resolve at current terms?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely give a directionally positive answer; specific contract count unlikely to be disclosed; expect reference to "expanding coverage" and Medicare Part D inclusion
  • Good answer signature: Quantifies formulary coverage (e.g. "X% of commercially insured lives at Tier 2 or better"), discloses pending contract count, references specific wins since last reporting date
  • Bad answer signature: Cites total "covered lives" without tier differentiation; cannot distinguish Tier 2 from Tier 3 coverage; references only federal/Medicare progress
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the co-pay differential between Tier 2 and Tier 3 for your tablets at the weighted average plan — and what conversion lift do you observe when patients are moved from Tier 3 to Tier 2?"
  • Diagnostic note: Tier differentiation matters more than total covered lives — a company with 80% covered lives at Tier 3 has effectively no commercial formulary advantage; this question surfaces whether payer access is genuine or a headline metric

Q4 — End-market segmentation, US allergist vs. PCM penetration

  • Question: "What percentage of US SLIT tablet scripts originate from board-certified allergists vs. primary care/other — and are you actively targeting primary care expansion?"
  • Expected answer: Expect allergist-dominated scripts (>80%), with commentary on primary care as a future opportunity; management likely to frame primary care as "upside optionality"
  • Good answer signature: Shows emerging primary care script share (even 5-10%), describes specific PCM education programmes, quantifies call plan allocation between allergist and PCM
  • Bad answer signature: Cannot disaggregate by prescriber type; deflects entirely to allergist base; describes primary care as theoretical without any programme activity
  • Follow-up probe: "How many primary care reps, if any, are currently in your US sales force — and what is the cost-per-script in PCM vs. allergist channels?"
  • Diagnostic note: Allergist penetration is a ceiling story — BULL case requires PCM expansion; if management cannot describe a PCM programme, the long-term TAM expansion thesis is weaker than modelled

Sceptical bullet: A fluent answer on US revenue momentum could indicate genuine market confidence OR a well-rehearsed IR narrative — the tell is whether management can quote the metric that worries them (patient persistence, Tier 3 concentration, rep productivity below target) rather than only the metric that excites them

4. THEME §2 — Margin Trajectory + Levers

J-front verdict: Margin uncertainty is HIGHEST on the trajectory of US SG&A as a % of US revenue — the key question is whether operating leverage is already beginning or whether the SG&A ramp still has 2+ years to run

Q5 — US contribution margin by product

  • Question: "Can you walk us through the unit-level gross margin on ITULAZAX vs. ACARIZAX in the US — and at what annual script volume does each product reach positive contribution margin at the brand level?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely give blended gross margin for the US segment rather than per-product; gross margins on SLIT tablets are structurally high (70-80% theoretical) but US SG&A absorption makes contribution margin deeply negative at current volumes
  • Good answer signature: Provides product-level gross margin %; discloses the script-volume breakeven for US contribution margin; shows the curve is improving year-on-year
  • Bad answer signature: Refuses to disaggregate; cites group gross margin; deflects to "investment phase" without quantifying the duration or trigger for margin inflection
  • Follow-up probe: "At your current US script growth rate, what year does the US segment reach EBIT breakeven — and what is your internal sensitivity on that year to a +/- 20% script volume variance?"
  • Diagnostic note: If management cannot or will not describe the US contribution margin curve, it is impossible to independently validate the 2025-26 EPS inflection thesis — this is the single most load-bearing financial question in the DD

Q6 — SG&A pacing and rep productivity

  • Question: "What is your average revenue per sales rep in the US today — and what is your target revenue-per-rep at steady state, and over what timeframe do you reach it?"
  • Expected answer: Expect deflection to total US SG&A as % of revenue rather than per-rep; management may disclose rep count but not per-rep revenue productivity; expect target-state framing without clear year
  • Good answer signature: Quotes specific revenue-per-rep (e.g. DKK X per FTE); describes a rep-productivity improvement curve; anchors steady-state target to a named year or milestone trigger
  • Bad answer signature: Cannot quote per-rep revenue; deflects to headcount growth; describes productivity improvement as "in line with expectations" without quantifying expectations
  • Follow-up probe: "Your largest US specialty pharma peer in allergy spent approximately X% of US revenue on SG&A at this stage of launch — where do you expect to end FY26 on the same metric?"
  • Diagnostic note: Per-rep productivity is the leading indicator for whether US SG&A leverage is beginning — if productivity is flat or declining while headcount grows, the margin inflection thesis pushes right; this anchors the bear case duration of loss-making US operations

Q7 — R&D adequacy: pipeline spend vs. maintenance

  • Question: "Of your total R&D spend in FY25, what proportion is genuinely optionality-creating (new pipeline candidates or new indications) vs. lifecycle management of existing approvals?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely describe R&D as primarily pipeline-building; expect references to the Loligo/house dust mite pipeline and multi-allergen development; unlikely to provide the maintenance vs. optionality split precisely
  • Good answer signature: Provides a credible split (e.g. 60% new pipeline / 40% maintenance); names specific pipeline programmes with expected Phase 2/3 read-out timelines; describes what happens to R&D envelope after Phase 3 completion
  • Bad answer signature: Treats all R&D as optionality-creating without differentiating; cannot describe what R&D spend would look like if no new programmes were initiated
  • Follow-up probe: "If you stripped out all incremental pipeline investment, what would your underlying EBIT margin look like today — and does that 'clean' margin cover your cost of capital?"
  • Diagnostic note: R&D disaggregation tests whether the company is a genuine R&D-led business or a late-stage commercial company that calls SG&A-adjacent spend "R&D" — relevant to quality-of-earnings assessment and to valuation multiple appropriateness

Q8 — FX pass-through and pricing-cost spread

  • Question: "What proportion of your COGS is USD-denominated vs. DKK — and how do you manage the USD/DKK exposure on US product sales that are manufactured in Denmark?"
  • Expected answer: Manufacturing is Denmark-based; COGS likely substantially DKK; USD revenue from US creates natural currency exposure; expect hedging programme reference
  • Good answer signature: Describes specific hedge ratios, tenor, and how much of FY26 US USD revenue is already hedged; quantifies the sensitivity (e.g. 1% USD/DKK move = DKK Xm EBIT impact)
  • Bad answer signature: Deflects to "natural hedge" without quantifying; cannot describe hedge programme duration; treats FX as immaterial without evidence
  • Follow-up probe: "At current spot rates, what is the FX headwind or tailwind embedded in your FY26 guidance — and is your guidance based on spot or a specific assumed rate?"
  • Diagnostic note: FX management quality is a proxy for CFO operational rigour — inability to answer this at a DD meeting is a YELLOW FLAG on financial management discipline

Sceptical bullet: A confident margin-inflection narrative delivered fluently may be rehearsed messaging that has persisted since 2019 — probe for the specific year and specific trigger metric that management internally uses to define "inflection"; vague answers are themselves diagnostic

5. THEME §3 — Capital Allocation + Balance Sheet

J-front verdict: Capital allocation uncertainty centres on whether the US investment commitment is time-bounded with explicit exit criteria or open-ended — the former is BULL-compatible, the latter is a BEAR signal

Q9 — Investment runway and leverage tolerance

  • Question: "What is the maximum cumulative cash burn you are willing to absorb in the US before reassessing the investment case — and what is your leverage ceiling during the build phase?"
  • Expected answer: Management is unlikely to name a specific cash-burn ceiling; expect framing around "investment phase through approximately 2025-26" and reference to group balance sheet capacity; leverage likely expected to remain modest
  • Good answer signature: Names an explicit cumulative investment envelope (e.g. "DKK X–Y billion total US investment through breakeven"); describes a specific leverage ratio ceiling; references what triggers a strategic review of the US programme
  • Bad answer signature: Cannot name a ceiling; describes investment as "ongoing based on progress"; references only current leverage ratio without forward commitment
  • Follow-up probe: "If the US market does not reach your internal script-growth KPI by end of 2026, what is the board-level decision tree — accelerate, maintain, or reduce investment?"
  • Diagnostic note: An open-ended investment commitment without explicit exit criteria is a RED FLAG for capital allocation discipline — it suggests the US is a strategic sunk cost rather than a disciplined capital deployment decision

Q10 — M&A philosophy and RONIC discipline

  • Question: "What is your minimum required ROIC hurdle for any acquisition — and has that hurdle changed as the US investment has absorbed more group capital?"
  • Expected answer: Expect a general "value-creating" answer; ROIC hurdle unlikely to be disclosed precisely; management may describe M&A as bolt-on focused
  • Good answer signature: Names a specific ROIC hurdle (e.g. WACC+200-300bp); describes how M&A would be sized relative to current balance sheet; distinguishes between bolt-on technology and larger commercial acquisitions
  • Bad answer signature: Describes M&A in purely strategic terms without financial hurdle; cannot describe how an acquisition would be evaluated against the US organic investment alternative
  • Follow-up probe: "Given current US investment absorbs most of your free cash flow, what would trigger you to pursue an acquisition — and is there a scenario where M&A competes with the US ramp for capital?"
  • Diagnostic note: Capital allocation discipline is tested here — if M&A and US ramp compete for the same capital pool without explicit prioritisation, shareholder returns are at risk from opportunistic deal-making

Q11 — Working capital dynamics + royalty cash timing

  • Question: "Your royalty and out-licensing revenue line — how much of it is recurring (contracted, multi-year) vs. milestone-based (one-time), and what is the cash collection lag vs. P&L recognition?"
  • Expected answer: Expect management to describe royalties as contractually recurring; less likely to distinguish contracted vs. milestone clearly; may disclose approximate royalty revenue as % of total
  • Good answer signature: Provides a split between recurring royalties and milestone payments; describes contract duration for key royalty agreements; notes any step-down provisions or expiry dates
  • Bad answer signature: Treats all royalty/licensing as homogeneous and recurring without differentiating; cannot describe step-downs or expiry provisions in existing agreements
  • Follow-up probe: "What percentage of your royalty revenue in FY25 was from contracts that expire or step down before end of FY27 — and what is in your model to replace it?"
  • Diagnostic note: Royalty cliff risk is a FALSE-FRIEND signal — if royalty revenue is front-loaded and the P&L is presenting it as recurring while underneath contracts expire, EBIT margin is overstated in the medium-term model; this is the most structurally dangerous gap in a standard DCF

Sceptical bullet: Executives rarely volunteer that capital allocation has been suboptimal — the tell is asymmetric: good capital allocators can describe the decision they didn't make and why; ask for an example of capital they chose NOT to deploy

6. THEME §4 — Strategy + Competitive Position

J-front verdict: Strategic uncertainty is moderate — the AIT SLIT tablet market is genuinely growing and ALK's first-mover status in the US is real, but the competitive moat is thinner than the narrative implies given Stallergenes/Greer, DBV Technologies, and potential biosimilar/generic dynamics

Q12 — Competitive response: Stallergenes Greer and new entrants

  • Question: "Stallergenes Greer has US SLIT prescriptions growing — what specifically differentiates your prescriber value proposition vs. theirs in 2026, and where do you see your market share trending?"
  • Expected answer: Expect ALK to emphasise clinical data depth, FDA approval status of specific products, and physician relationships; likely to describe a growing overall market rather than head-to-head share competition
  • Good answer signature: Quotes specific market share data from IQVIA or equivalent; describes a defensible clinical or formulary differentiation point; acknowledges where Stallergenes has stronger positioning and names a specific response tactic
  • Bad answer signature: Describes "growing the overall market" without acknowledging competitive dynamics; cannot quote market share data; deflects to clinical superiority without specific differentiation points
  • Follow-up probe: "In the markets where your reps compete head-to-head with Stallergenes Greer for allergist business — what is your win rate in competitive accounts?"
  • Diagnostic note: Market share data from competitive accounts is the most direct signal for moat strength — management that cannot quote win rates in competitive accounts is either not tracking the right metrics or is unwilling to disclose deteriorating share

Q13 — Disruption risk: potential entry of biologics/anti-IgE into the AIT space

  • Question: "Dupixent/dupilumab and related biologics are increasingly prescribed for allergic conditions — do you see biologics as complementary, competitive, or eventually substitutive for SLIT tablets?"
  • Expected answer: Expect a "complementary" framing — management will likely argue SLIT tablets address the root cause while biologics manage symptoms; expect reference to combination therapy potential
  • Good answer signature: Acknowledges specific patient segments where biologics create prescriber hesitation; describes what combination therapy evidence exists; notes whether payer decisions are being made that crowd out SLIT in any segments
  • Bad answer signature: Pure "complementary" framing without acknowledging any competitive pressure; cannot describe a scenario where biologics reduce SLIT prescriptions
  • Follow-up probe: "Has any payer to your knowledge deprioritised SLIT tablets on formulary in favour of broader biologic step-therapy programmes — even in a single plan?"
  • Diagnostic note: This question tests management's willingness to acknowledge a genuine structural risk — an overly dismissive answer is itself a YELLOW FLAG on management candour

Q14 — Regulatory positioning: label expansion and lifecycle

  • Question: "What is your next FDA label-expansion priority — and what is the risk-adjusted timeline and commercial value of that expansion vs. your current baseline?"
  • Expected answer: Expect description of multi-allergen or paediatric label expansion as the key pipeline priority; timeline likely framed as 2027-2028; commercial value unlikely to be quantified
  • Good answer signature: Quantifies the incremental commercial opportunity (e.g. "X% of current script base would benefit from a paediatric label"); names the regulatory pathway and next milestone; describes what happens to the SG&A model if label expansion is granted
  • Bad answer signature: Describes pipeline in clinical rather than commercial terms; cannot size the commercial opportunity; defers entirely to "pending regulatory interaction"
  • Follow-up probe: "If the paediatric label expansion is granted by end of 2026, does it require incremental SG&A investment or does it lever your existing call plan?"
  • Diagnostic note: Incremental-SG&A-free label expansion is pure upside leverage; SG&A-intensive label expansion reduces its NPV materially — the answer discriminates between optionality that is truly accretive vs. optionality that creates another investment phase

Sceptical bullet: Strategic framing is the most rehearsed part of any CEO meeting — probe for the internal competitive ranking rather than the public one; "where do you rank among AIT competitors internally on rep productivity?" will get a different answer than "how is your competitive position?"

7. THEME §5 — Management Quality + Operator Track Record

J-front verdict: Management credibility is ADEQUATE based on public track record but sandbagging discipline is UNTESTED — the critical question is whether guidance has historically proven conservative or aggressive

Q15 — Sandbagging discipline: guidance vs. outturn

  • Question: "Looking back at your FY21 and FY22 revenue and EBIT guidance — where did you land vs. initial guidance, and what drove the primary variances?"
  • Expected answer: Expect management to focus on FY22-23 COVID recovery dynamics; likely to attribute misses to exogenous factors; may point to revenue beats as evidence of conservatism
  • Good answer signature: Acknowledges specific guidance misses and their causes without deflecting entirely to exogenous factors; describes how the guidance-setting process was adjusted post-miss; references any systematic conservative-ness in their guidance approach
  • Bad answer signature: Attributes all misses to exogenous factors; cannot describe internal forecast accuracy; presents only guidance beats
  • Follow-up probe: "Is your guidance methodology built on bottom-up rep-level script projections or top-down market share modelling — and which has historically been more accurate for you?"
  • Diagnostic note: Guidance methodology reveals whether management understands its own business at a granular level — bottom-up script-level guidance that beats is strong BULL signal; top-down percentage-growth guidance that misses is BEAR signal on operator quality

Q16 — Power of incentives: compensation structure

  • Question: "What are the primary financial metrics driving your LTI vesting — and in what year did the board last reset the performance hurdles, and why?"
  • Expected answer: Expect general description of revenue growth and EPS as LTI metrics; specific hurdle levels unlikely to be disclosed; may reference total shareholder return as a relative measure
  • Good answer signature: Names specific metric weightings (e.g. "50% revenue, 30% EBIT, 20% relative TSR"); describes whether hurdles were raised or lowered at last reset; discusses alignment between management incentive horizon and investor 3-year holding period
  • Bad answer signature: Deflects to "full details in the remuneration report"; describes incentives in generic terms without addressing whether current hurdles are stretch or easily achievable
  • Follow-up probe: "If you achieve the midpoint of your current guidance range for FY26, what percentage of maximum LTI payout would vest — is it above or below 50%?"
  • Diagnostic note (Concept A — Power of incentives): This is the single most revealing question in the management theme — if 50% of LTI vests at guidance midpoint, management is incentivised to be conservative; if <25% vests at midpoint, hurdles are stretch and the guidance may be aggressive; compensation structure predicts behaviour more reliably than any strategy presentation

Q17 — Crisis playbook: FY20 COVID test

  • Question: "How did you manage the salesforce and physician-access challenge during COVID — and what did you learn about your business's resilience that changed how you run it today?"
  • Expected answer: Expect COVID narrative about digital sales force transition, remote physician engagement; likely to describe it as an accelerant of digital capabilities
  • Good answer signature: Names a specific operating change that persists post-COVID (e.g. telemedicine coverage integration, new CRM discipline); describes what KPIs surprised management during COVID recovery; notes one thing they would do differently
  • Bad answer signature: Generic "we adapted quickly" narrative; cannot describe a lasting operational change; references COVID as purely exogenous with no internal learning
  • Follow-up probe: "Did your patient persistence (prescription refill rates) improve or deteriorate during COVID — and what does that tell you about the channel through which patients access SLIT therapy?"
  • Diagnostic note: Crisis response quality is a leading indicator of operator competence — genuine operators have specific operational learnings; strategic communicators have narrative summaries

Sceptical bullet: Incentive-structure questions are the most likely to be deflected to the remuneration report — if management cannot describe their own LTI metric weightings in a DD meeting, that deflection is itself diagnostic; note it explicitly

8. THEME §6 — Risk + Downside (Pre-Mortem Cross-Reference)

J-front verdict: Downside risk is CONCENTRATED in three vectors — US commercial failure, royalty step-down, and biologics crowding out SLIT in payer formularies — questions in this theme should test each explicitly

Q18 — Bear-case probe: US commercial failure scenario

  • Question: "What would a realistic US commercial failure look like for you — at what point would you materially reduce the US investment, and what is the residual value of the business without a US franchise?"
  • Expected answer: Management will resist the failure framing; likely to describe the US as a long-term strategic imperative; may reference European franchise as a durable floor
  • Good answer signature: Describes a specific script-growth KPI below which investment would be cut; quantifies the European business's standalone value; is candid about the sunk cost risk
  • Bad answer signature: Refuses to describe a failure scenario; deflects to "we are committed to the US"; cannot describe a scenario where European business adequately supports the equity valuation alone
  • Follow-up probe: "If you had to value just the European AIT franchise today, ex-US, what EBIT multiple would you apply — and does that valuation cover your current enterprise value?"
  • Diagnostic note: This is the core BEAR/FALSE-FRIEND discriminator — if European franchise ex-US does not support current EV, the entire valuation is predicated on US success; management's candour in engaging this question is itself a quality signal

Q19 — Pricing fragility: IRA/PBM pressure

  • Question: "Has the Inflation Reduction Act or any 2024-2025 PBM contract renegotiation led to a change in your net US pricing — and what is your current gross-to-net discount on US SLIT tablets?"
  • Expected answer: Expect management to describe gross-to-net as "in line with specialty pharma norms" without specific disclosure; IRA pressure likely acknowledged but framed as limited for current SLIT volumes
  • Good answer signature: Discloses the gross-to-net range (e.g. 30-40%); describes whether it has widened or narrowed in the last 12 months; explains how IRA dynamics affect SLIT specifically vs. biologics
  • Bad answer signature: Cannot describe gross-to-net dynamics; deflects to "list price" metrics; treats IRA as irrelevant without engaging with PBM negotiation dynamics
  • Follow-up probe: "Has any major PBM requested rebate increases in 2025 contract negotiations — and what happened to your formulary tier if you declined?"
  • Diagnostic note: Gross-to-net widening is an early warning signal for pricing power deterioration that will not appear in reported revenue immediately — it is a leading indicator that manifests in margins 12-18 months later

Q20 — Regulatory exposure: EMA/FDA inspection track record

  • Question: "Have you received any FDA 483 observations or EMA inspection findings on your manufacturing facilities in the last three years — and are there any pending regulatory actions?"
  • Expected answer: Expect "no material issues" framing; likely reference to routine inspections with no significant findings; manufacturing is in Denmark, historically well-regulated
  • Good answer signature: Transparent disclosure of any observations received and the response/closure timeline; proactive description of manufacturing quality metrics (batch rejection rate, OOS rate)
  • Bad answer signature: Deflects to "we comply with all regulatory requirements"; cannot describe the last inspection cycle outcome specifically
  • Follow-up probe: "What is your manufacturing batch rejection rate on ACARIZAX and ITULAZAX — and is that rate stable or has it changed with volume scale-up?"
  • Diagnostic note: Manufacturing quality at volume scale-up is a risk specific to biological/allergen extract manufacturing — batch rejection rate changes as production scales are a RED FLAG for supply chain risk that would not appear in the financial model

Sceptical bullet — ACH gap check: Q19 pre-mortem ACH items NOT covered by any question in this memo — (a) pipeline clinical failure risk for next-generation multi-allergen products (no question probes Phase 2/3 clinical success probability explicitly); (b) CEO succession/key-person risk (Hellmann has been CEO since 2018 — no question probes succession depth); (c) DKK/USD translation risk beyond hedging described in Q8 (no question probes the functional currency election and its balance sheet implications) — analyst should consider adding 2-3 questions on these if meeting time allows

9. CROSS-CUTTING META — Non-Obvious Questions

J-front verdict: The questions management is LEAST PREPARED for are the ones that cross business lines or invert the standard analyst framing — load-bearing non-obvious angles below

  • Non-obvious angle: "If you had to identify the single prescriber behaviour that would most accelerate your US business that you currently have NO programme to influence — what is it?" — forces management to name a gap rather than a plan; reveals strategic blind spots
  • WH Smith probe: "What is the most surprising piece of negative feedback you've received from a US allergist in the last six months — not from a competitor's sales rep, but from a prescriber who already uses your products?" — most management teams have this data; willingness to share it is a candour signal
  • Cross-roads question: "If you were a competitor entering the US SLIT market today with unlimited capital, which part of your current market position would you attack first — and what would it cost to dislodge you?" — inverts standard competitive advantage framing; surfaces the weakest point in the moat
  • Multiple-truths probe (Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 3): "I can see the bull case — growing AIT market, first-mover US position, high-margin European royalty floor. AND I can see the bear case — perpetual SG&A investment, royalty cliffs, US breakeven perpetually 18 months away. Both can be simultaneously true. What would it take for both to be true at once — and what is the probability you're in that scenario?" — prevents deflection into either-or framing; forces management to sit with the compound uncertainty
  • Diagnostic crystalliser: "What single internal KPI do you look at every week that you have NOT disclosed publicly — and is its current trend above or below your internal plan?" — the choice of KPI is itself diagnostic; the trend answer is load-bearing
  • Stewing trigger: "Your investor day presentations from 2019 to 2025 have consistently described US SLIT as 'approaching inflection' — at what point does that language become a credibility problem for you, and what milestone would retire it?" — this question is uncomfortable; discomfort in answering is itself signal
  • Edge-detection probe: "What does your US salesforce attrition rate look like — and is it above or below industry average for specialty pharma launches at this stage?" — salesforce attrition is a leading indicator of field force morale and the quality of the commercial organisation; not typically surfaced in quarterly calls
  • Incentive probe — inverse: "What would have to happen in FY26 for your management team to waive a portion of their annual bonus voluntarily — has that ever happened at ALK?" — tests alignment of management incentives with long-term shareholder interest vs. short-term metric optimisation

Sceptical bullet — Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 1: Questions the analyst has NOT included because asking them would feel uncomfortable — (1) "Is there a scenario where you sell the US franchise to a larger pharma player, and would that actually be value-maximising for shareholders?" — this is the question most investors want answered but won't ask because it implies the business model might not work; (2) "Have any board members pushed back on the pace of US investment — and what was the outcome of that discussion?" — this surfaces board-level governance tension that management will not volunteer; (3) "What is the share of your FY25 EBIT that is attributable to a single royalty relationship — and what happens to your EBIT margin if that relationship lapses?" — the obstacle is the way; these questions should be asked
