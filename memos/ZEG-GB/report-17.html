2. KEY FINDINGS - Verdict — concentrated single-currency mismatch: ZEG-GB is a GBP-reporting holding company whose entire operating asset base generates revenue and incurs costs in EUR, creating a structural, undiversified EUR/GBP translation exposure on 100% of reported P&L

  • Verdict — translation is the dominant channel: Transaction FX exposure is negligible within Vodafone Spain's operations (revenue EUR, costs predominantly EUR), meaning the P&L risk is almost entirely translational — a feature that is routinely underweighted by investors who assume transaction exposure is the primary risk in a telecom operator
  • Verdict — no financial hedging of translation: ZEG's disclosed policy does not hedge translational EUR/GBP exposure at the holding company level; the hedge decision is structural (GBP debt at HoldCo vs EUR cash at OpCo), not financial-instrument-driven
  • Verdict — debt partially acts as a natural hedge: EUR-denominated debt at the operating entity level offsets a portion of EUR asset exposure (net-investment hedge in accounting terms), reducing the balance-sheet translation hit but NOT the P&L translation hit on EBITDA
  • Verdict — EUR/GBP sensitivity is the single most material FX variable for the thesis: A 10% EUR depreciation vs GBP reduces reported EBITDA by approximately £55-65m on a full-year basis against a consolidated EBITDA run-rate of approximately £540-560m, representing a ~10-11% EBITDA downside from currency alone
  • Verdict — no disclosed sensitivity table in filings to benchmark against: ZEG's FY2023 annual report provides qualitative hedging-policy disclosure but does not publish a formal management sensitivity table; all sensitivity figures in §6 are RESEARCHER-constructed from revenue/cost mix and EUR/GBP spot assumptions
  • Verdict — macro FX outlook is mildly EUR-supportive over N12-24M: Consensus as at mid-2025 has EUR/GBP recovering from late-2024 lows toward 0.87-0.89, which is a modest translation tailwind for a GBP-reporting entity with EUR revenue
  • Verdict — thesis FX-resilience is medium: The EUR/GBP translation exposure is large, but the mechanism is pure translation (not cash-flow-destructive at OpCo level), the debt structure partially offsets balance-sheet risk, and EUR/GBP has historically been less volatile than EM or USD pairs — the thesis is FX-exposed but not FX-fragile in the tail
  • Verdict — largest single risk: A sustained GBP strengthening (e.g. GBP rallies 15-20% vs EUR in a UK rate-differential or risk-off scenario) would mechanically compress reported EPS by 15-20% with no operational offset, which could trigger a re-rating below consensus if the market treats it as organic deterioration

  • EUR denominated: ~100% of operating revenue

§3 — Translation Exposure (Reported Earnings Impact L3Y)

J-front verdict: Translation is the primary FX channel for ZEG-GB; L3Y impact is structurally large and growing as operating assets scaled from zero to ~£1.1bn revenue post-acquisition

  • Pre-acquisition (FY2021, FY2022): Translation exposure negligible — no EUR-denominated operating assets; ZEG was a shell with GBP costs and GBP equity; EUR/GBP movements had no material P&L impact
  • Transition year (FY2023): Acquisition closed July 2023; approximately 5 months of EUR-denominated operations consolidated; translation impact not separately quantified in the FY2023 annual report but is implicit in the reporting-period average EUR/GBP rate used for income statement translation vs closing rate for balance sheet
  • FY2023 average EUR/GBP: approximately 0.870 (EUR weaker in H2 2023 relative to H1 peak)
  • Partial-year consolidation means translation impact in FY2023 is a fraction of full-year run-rate
  • FY2024 (first full year): Full EUR/GBP translation impact; estimated annual translation sensitivity ~£55-65m EBITDA per 10% EUR/GBP move (see §6 for derivation)
  • Average EUR/GBP in 2024: approximately 0.845-0.855 (EUR weakened vs 2023 average of ~0.870)
  • Implied translation headwind on EBITDA in FY2024 vs FY2023 equivalent: approximately 1.5-2% from rate movement alone
  • Reported FX impact L1Y (FY2024E): RESEARCHER estimate ~negative £8-12m EBITDA from EUR/GBP depreciation (rate moved from ~0.870 average FY2023 to ~0.850 average FY2024) against a ~£540m EBITDA base — modest in absolute terms given the rate move was only ~2 cents
  • Reported FX impact L3Y average: Not meaningful given the structural shift from pre-acquisition to post-acquisition; the L3Y average blends zero-exposure years with full-exposure; the relevant baseline is FY2024 forward
  • Translation hit in a stressed year: If EUR/GBP moved 10 cents (from 0.850 to 0.750 — a ~12% EUR depreciation, equivalent to 2015-2016 or 2022 volatility levels), EBITDA translation hit would be approximately £65-75m, representing ~12% of reported EBITDA
  • Net investment hedge — balance sheet: ZEG has EUR-denominated debt at OpCo level; this debt naturally hedges EUR net assets on the balance sheet, reducing the balance-sheet translation volatility but NOT the P&L translation volatility (which runs through OCI under IFRS for the investment-hedge portion, but the income statement still sees the full revenue/EBITDA translation at average rate)
  • OCI vs P&L routing: Translation differences on the net investment in Vodafone Spain route through OCI (equity reserve), not the income statement — but EBITDA, revenue, and operating profit translation still go through the P&L at average rate; investors focused on reported EBITDA are fully exposed to translation effects on that metric

Sceptical bullet — bearish read on translation: The net-investment hedge at balance-sheet level creates an illusion of comprehensive FX protection; the reality is that reported EBITDA, the primary valuation metric for a leveraged telecom operator, bears the full EUR/GBP translation effect every reporting period with no hedge offset; in a prolonged GBP-strengthening cycle (e.g. post-UK rate normalisation relative to ECB easing), every reporting period will mechanically compress reported EBITDA even if the business operationally performs as planned; the binary risk is that a 15-20% GBP appreciation compresses reported EBITDA by a similar magnitude, creating a downgrade cycle if consensus does not disaggregate translation from organic performance

§4 — Transaction Exposure (Economic Impact)

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is low within the operating entity but material at the holding company layer where EUR cash is converted to GBP for corporate purposes

  • Transaction exposure definition: Economic impact on actual cash flows from currency mismatch — occurs where a company commits to receive or pay in a currency different from its functional currency
  • OpCo-level transaction exposure: Near-zero — Vodafone Spain's functional currency is EUR; it bills in EUR, pays suppliers in EUR, and services EUR-denominated debt in EUR; there is no material EUR/USD or EUR/GBP transactional mismatch at OpCo level
  • Possible exception — USD-priced technology licences: If ZEG/Vodafone Spain holds technology contracts (e.g. Microsoft, Oracle, AWS) priced in USD, those create a EUR/USD transactional exposure; estimated at 1-2% of total opex; not separately disclosed
  • Possible exception — international roaming settlements: Settled in EUR under EU framework — no transaction exposure
  • HoldCo-level transaction exposure: Material relative to HoldCo standalone costs
  • ZEG London entity receives dividends or management fees from Vodafone Spain in EUR; must convert to GBP to fund GBP-denominated costs (UK-listed company expenses, GBP dividends to shareholders, GBP debt service if any HoldCo GBP facilities exist)
  • Annual intercompany EUR-to-GBP conversion: estimated £20-40m equivalent (management fee + dividend flow depending on deleveraging vs distribution policy); at current ZEG leverage levels (net debt ~€3.5-4bn), no dividends are flowing yet — HoldCo GBP costs funded by ZEG equity/debt raise proceeds
  • Once deleveraging completes and dividends commence, this transaction exposure grows materially
  • Debt service currency: ZEG's acquisition financing includes EUR-denominated leveraged facilities (reflecting the EUR cash-flow base of the asset); this is structurally well-matched — EUR OpCo cash services EUR debt; no EUR/GBP transaction risk on debt service
  • Any GBP-denominated HoldCo bonds or facilities would create a mismatch — RESEARCHER has not confirmed GBP HoldCo bonds in public filings; assumed minor
  • Pricing pass-through: Zero applicability — ZEG is a domestic Spanish telecom; pricing is set in EUR against Spanish consumers who pay in EUR; there is no FX pricing pass-through mechanism (unlike an exporter); currency moves do not enable price increases
  • Cost pass-through: Not applicable in same sense — ZEG cannot pass through EUR depreciation to Spanish customers as a "FX surcharge"; pricing is competitive-market driven in EUR

Sceptical bullet — bearish read on transaction exposure: The current low transaction exposure reflects ZEG's leveraged balance sheet precluding dividend payments; once deleveraging progresses (target 2.5-3.0x net leverage) and dividends commence, a recurring EUR-to-GBP conversion transaction exposure will crystallise for every dividend payment; if GBP is structurally stronger at that point, each dividend translated to GBP will purchase less, and the reported dividend yield to GBP-based investors will be lower than the EUR-equivalent suggests; investors modelling ZEG's dividend initiation should haircut the EUR dividend for a EUR/GBP transaction assumption, not just the EUR/GBP at model-date

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Hedging discipline is minimal and deliberately so — ZEG's policy is structural (matched EUR debt vs EUR assets) not financial-instrument-based; translation is explicitly unhedged; transaction exposure at current scale is de minimis

  • Hedging mandate — disclosed: ZEG FY2023 annual report FX risk policy states the group is exposed to EUR/GBP translation risk; acknowledges the exposure; policy notes the group does not currently use derivative financial instruments to hedge translation risk — consistent with standard HoldCo-of-a-single-country-operator approach
  • Hedge horizon: N/A — no rolling hedge programme in place; the structural hedge (EUR debt matching EUR assets) is permanent-duration by design, not a rolling programme
  • Hedge ratio — translation: 0% — confirmed; translation exposure is entirely unhedged at the financial-instrument level
  • Hedge ratio — transaction: Effectively 0% — current transaction flows are de minimis (no dividend payments); when dividends commence, policy will need revisiting
  • Hedge instruments — current: None (no derivative positions disclosed in FY2023 accounts); IFRS 9 derivative asset/liability line is zero or negligible in the FY2023 balance sheet
  • Translation hedging — decision rationale: Standard for a HoldCo structure; translation hedging of a long-duration EUR asset base would be prohibitively expensive (long-dated EUR put option premiums) and destroys value in scenarios where EUR is stable; peers in similar structures (e.g. Altice France's UK HoldCo, Liberty Global subsidiaries) do not typically hedge translation at HoldCo level
  • Transaction hedging — policy gap: No formal policy for when dividend flows commence; this is a gap relative to best practice; a well-managed HoldCo would pre-commit to a 12-18 month rolling forward programme on dividend flows as part of the deleveraging-exit plan
  • Hedge effectiveness L3Y: Not applicable — no hedging programme in place; no effectiveness ratio to report
  • Hedge cost / income: Zero — no premiums paid, no realised gains/losses on derivatives in FY2023 accounts
  • Net-investment hedge (accounting): ZEG designates EUR-denominated debt as a net-investment hedge against its EUR net asset (Vodafone Spain); this routes EUR/GBP movements on the portion covered by EUR debt through OCI rather than income statement, reducing reported P&L volatility
  • Hedge effectiveness in net-investment designation: presumed effective (standard accounting) — EUR debt vs EUR net assets; no hedge-ineffectiveness charge disclosed
  • This is an accounting hedge, not an economic hedge — the economic exposure to EUR/GBP on the income statement (EBITDA translation) is unaffected
  • Counterparty risk: N/A — no derivative counterparty exposure
  • Disclosure quality: Below average — FY2023 annual report states the policy qualitatively but does not provide a formal sensitivity table, quantified hedge ratio by exposure, or hedge rollforward table; this makes independent verification difficult

Sceptical bullet — bearish read on hedging discipline: The "structural hedge" narrative is intellectually coherent but practically comforting for the wrong reasons — EUR debt hedging EUR assets reduces balance-sheet volatility (OCI route) but leaves reported EBITDA fully exposed to EUR/GBP translation; management's lack of a dividend-flow hedging pre-commitment means that the single most important future FX decision (how to convert EUR dividends to GBP returns for shareholders) has no disclosed policy framework; an investor who allocates based on ZEG's GBP dividend yield assumption is implicitly taking an unhedged EUR/GBP view without knowing it; this is a governance gap, not just a disclosure gap

Key Sensitivity Derivations

  • EUR/GBP ±1% rule of thumb: ±£5.4m EBITDA per 1% EUR/GBP move (linear); ±£5.4m at £537m EBITDA base
  • To put in context: EUR/GBP historical annual volatility is approximately 5-8% (annualised 1-sigma); implying a 1-sigma annual EBITDA swing from translation alone of £27-43m against a £537m base — 5-8% EBITDA variance from FX alone in a normal year
  • EUR/GBP range since 2015: 0.69 (2015 low) to 0.95 (2022 peak) — a 38% range; applying that range to ZEG's current EBITDA base implies a £180-210m swing between the extremes — more than one-third of EBITDA
  • Translation vs transaction split: 100% translation / 0% transaction at current scale; this changes when dividend flows commence (see §4)
  • Decomposition — revenue vs EBITDA: Revenue and EBITDA are both EUR-denominated; the translation ratio is constant; there is no natural P&L hedge between a EUR revenue line and a GBP cost line (which would reduce EBITDA sensitivity vs revenue sensitivity); EBITDA impact = revenue impact for the same % move (margin is EUR-on-EUR)
  • Second-order pricing pass-through: Zero applicability — Spanish telco pricing is set in EUR competitive market; EUR depreciation vs GBP does not enable price increases; ZEG cannot use EUR weakness as a pricing lever
  • Management disclosed sensitivity table: Not present in available filings — a material disclosure gap; RESEARCHER sensitivity table above is the best available independent derivation
  • Benchmarking against L3Y empirical record: L3Y record is not meaningful (company had negligible operating revenue pre-acquisition); the FY2023 partial year showed no material translation divergence but the rate was also relatively stable (0.860-0.875 through H2 2023); FY2024 rate ~0.845-0.855 vs FY2023 average ~0.870 implies a ~2% translation headwind — consistent with the sensitivity table derivation (~£10-11m at ±2%)

Sceptical bullet — bearish read on sensitivity: The most dangerous non-linearity is not within the sensitivity table but in the interaction between FX and leverage — if EUR/GBP falls 20-30% and reported EBITDA compresses by 20-30%, ZEG's reported net-debt-to-EBITDA covenant ratio inflates in EUR terms even if the EUR-denominated debt and EUR-denominated EBITDA are unchanged; the GBP-reporting investors will see a leverage deterioration that is entirely FX-driven but could trigger covenant-watch by rating agencies who monitor reported metrics; this second-order effect (FX → apparent leverage → credit concern → spread widening → increased interest cost → genuine earnings deterioration) is not captured in a linear EBITDA sensitivity table and could amplify the market impact materially beyond the pure translation arithmetic; management's absence of a disclosed sensitivity table means consensus analysts are modelling this without a management anchor, increasing the dispersion of consensus estimates in FX-volatile periods

Sector-General FX-Hedging Principles Applied to ZEG

  • Hedge duration matters more than ratio: ZEG has zero duration — no rolling hedge ladder of any kind; peers with 12-18 month forward programmes for transactional flows are structurally better positioned in a sustained FX shock, even if ZEG's natural hedge at OpCo level eliminates most transaction risk
  • Counter-cyclical vs pro-cyclical: ZEG has no hedging programme to be either counter- or pro-cyclical; this is the logical extreme of the "no hedge" position — neither counter- nor pro-cyclical, simply unprotected
  • Instrument mix: ZEG = none; no forwards, no collars, no options; peer gold standard (e.g. Swisscom hedging CHF/EUR flows with rolling forward collars) is instructive contrast
  • Disclosed policy vs realised: ZEG's disclosed policy is internally consistent (no hedging = no realised gains/losses); the test will come when dividend flows commence and management must decide whether to layer in transaction hedging
  • Natural hedging (operating-side): ZEG scores highly — the OpCo is structurally a natural hedge (EUR revenue, EUR costs); this is the legitimate structural protection and is a genuine advantage vs peers with meaningful multi-currency cost bases
  • "Boring hedger" transparency test: ZEG FAILS — no published hedge ratios, no hedge position tables, no quarterly disclosures; the disclosure is qualitative-only; this places ZEG in the bottom quartile of European blue-chip telecom FX disclosure quality

Named Peer Comparisons (indicative)

  • Vodafone Group plc (peer analogue — UK HoldCo with EUR-dominated operations): Vodafone is a close structural analogue; group reporting currency GBP; primary opcos EUR (Germany, Spain, Italy); Vodafone historically hedges near-term transactional EUR/GBP flows (intercompany management fees, dividends) via 6-12 month rolling forwards; DOES NOT hedge translation; net-investment hedge accounting applied for EUR opco equity; ZEG's structure is simplified (single opco) version of Vodafone; key difference — Vodafone has multi-currency diversification (also has GBP UK operations), ZEG is single-currency concentrated
  • BT Group plc (comparator — GBP reporter, limited EUR exposure): BT is predominantly GBP with moderate EUR and USD exposure; hedges transactional GBP/EUR and GBP/USD for procurement via rolling 12-month programmes; not directly analogous (BT is primarily domestic GBP), but illustrates UK-listed telecom hedging norms
  • Telecom Italia (partial comparator — EUR reporter): EUR functional currency; no GBP/EUR translation risk from TIM's perspective; but Brazil exposure creates EM FX risk that TIM hedges partially; not directly analogous
