EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (BLUF)

Management deliberately contracted the fleet by 3.2% in 2025 through strategic defleeting of unprofitable brokered business, prioritizing margin protection over volume growth in a disciplined capital allocation framework. The December 2025 CEO transition to Philippe de Rovira shifted corporate priorities toward customer satisfaction and operational improvements, bringing 27 years of automotive industry experience from senior Stellantis roles. Procurement advantages are reinforced through deep volume discounts and framework agreements, including an unprecedented Stellantis agreement for up to 500,000 vehicles over three years. Downstream remarketing capabilities are anchored by Ayvens Carmarket, operating as a B2B digital auction platform across 39 countries with AI predictive pricing models active in 20 countries to optimize resale margins. Barriers to entry remain formidable, requiring minimum fleet scale of 30,000 to 35,000 vehicles and extremely high capital intensity to finance balance sheet vehicle assets, protecting the industry from new entrants.

The single largest strategic risk is severe battery electric vehicle residual value volatility, with lessors facing significant write-downs on returned EVs as realized resale values fall below initial contractual estimates due to rapid technology cycles and OEM price cuts. Vertical integration threats are intensifying as captive finance arms like Volkswagen Financial Services manage over 20 million contracts globally and Renault's Mobilize targets a 1 million vehicle fleet, potentially bypassing independent multi-brand lessors and compressing third-party market share. Integration execution risks persist with final platform migrations yet to occur in the UK, Germany, and the Netherlands, creating temporary customer service bottlenecks that could elevate contract non-renewal risks and damage historically strong retention rates. Procurement power is eroding as traditional dealer discounts and volume bonuses are declining compared to the pre-merger era, with OEMs managing production capacity more tightly and reducing manufacturer rebate generosity. Return on average earning assets has declined from 7.4% pre-Covid to around 5.5% post-merger, with standalone competitor Arval achieving materially better cost-to-income ratios and higher ROTE, highlighting persistent operational efficiency gaps.
