§1 — CEO Assessment

J-front: Mixed-to-weak — Palermo is a credible utility technocrat, not an animal CEO; political appointment mechanism structurally caps operator quality

CEO background:
- Fabrizio Palermo appointed CEO Dec-2022, replacing Gianni Vittorio Armani (who had been CEO since 2017)
- Prior role: CEO of Cassa Depositi e Prestiti — Italy's state development bank — from 2018 to 2021; resigned from CDP following reported tensions with the Italian government/MEF over strategic direction
- Prior to CDP: CFO-level roles at Telecom Italia and other Italian large-cap infrastructure plays; engineer by training (University of Rome La Sapienza, electronic engineering)
- Pattern: career built inside Italian state-adjacent or regulated infrastructure — CDP, telecoms infrastructure, now utilities; no private-sector or international operator experience of note
- Appointment mechanism: Comune di Roma nominated Palermo to the board and then supported his election as CEO — consistent with the controlling-shareholder playbook at Italian municipal utilities

CEO tenure:
- ~2.5 years at time of this report (Dec-2022 to May-26); short by utility standards but long enough to assess early strategic posture
- Predecessor Armani served ~5 years; Acea has seen three CEOs in ~12 years — a rotation frequency higher than Terna or Snam (both of which have had more continuity), suggesting municipal influence produces periodic political turnover

CEO track record at Acea:
- Strategic shift L3Y: Palermo has continued Armani-era infrastructure investment plan (Piano Industriale) with incremental uplift; no major strategic discontinuity; EBITDA trajectory positive but largely tariff-regulation-driven rather than management-initiative-driven
- Capital allocation: capex guidance maintained at ~€600m/year range; no significant M&A under Palermo; disposal of some non-core assets (minor water/waste subsidiaries outside Rome); capex discipline appears adequate but externally constrained
- Revenue / EBITDA: Group revenues and EBITDA have grown under Palermo's tenure, but WACC-regulated tariff resets and inflation pass-through account for most of the uplift; management contribution to EBITDA growth is difficult to isolate from regulatory tailwinds
- M&A track record at Acea: limited — Acea has not made a meaningful acquisition under Palermo; bolt-on disposals rather than value-adding acquisitions
- Communication: Palermo presents credibly at capital markets days; language is measured and technocratic; no obvious communication missteps but also no standout moments of shareholder-value conviction

CEO track record prior:
- CDP: Palermo ran CDP during a complex period (2018-2021); CDP's AUM grew, strategic mandate expanded into green infrastructure; however, his departure was non-voluntary (political friction with Cassa management board), which is an important signal — he operates in political ecosystems but is not immune to displacement

Leadership style:

§6 — Governance Structure + Red Flags

J-front: Mixed — no acute red flags; structurally fragile due to municipal ownership; governance risks are structural and political rather than fraudulent or reckless

Voting rights structure:
- Standard one-share-one-vote; no dual-class structure; no golden shares in the formal sense
- However, Comune di Roma's ~51% stake is the functional equivalent of permanent control; no activist investor, institutional investor bloc, or minority shareholder coalition can realistically challenge management or strategy
- Anti-takeover: the 51% municipal stake is a permanent anti-takeover shield; this is "governance by shareholder concentration" rather than formal anti-takeover provisions

Founder control:
- Not applicable; Acea has municipal not founder roots; control dynamic is state-ownership rather than founder-ownership

Related-party transactions:
- Primary related party: Comune di Roma itself — Acea provides water, electricity, and waste services to Rome municipality under service contracts; these contracts are Acea's core revenue base and are regulated/tariff-governed
- Related-party receivables: Roma Capitale has historically been slow to pay Acea for services; this has contributed to elevated receivables on Acea's balance sheet (reported in annual reports' related-party disclosure section); the NFD elevation is partly a related-party receivables problem
- Quantification: as of 2023 annual report, related-party receivables from Roma Capitale and associated entities were in the range of €200-300m (recurring balance); this is a structural related-party risk, not fraud, but it represents a meaningful capital efficiency drag
- No other material related-party transactions identified; no officer-family transactions or obvious tunnelling

Audit history:
- Statutory auditor: PricewaterhouseCoopers (PwC); long-standing relationship; Italian mandatory auditor rotation applies (6-year rotation cycle for listed companies); no recent auditor change indicating stress
- No material weaknesses disclosed in PwC audit reports L3Y
- No restatements of reported financials identified
- Audit committee has reviewed and approved related-party transaction disclosures including the Roma Capitale service contracts

Regulatory/legal issues:
- No material ongoing class action identified against Acea management
- Acea has faced regulatory disputes with ARERA (Italian energy and water regulator) over tariff calculations — this is routine for utility operators and has not resulted in material adverse rulings
- Antitrust: no material antitrust proceedings identified
- Environmental: Acea has faced minor environmental enforcement actions related to waste operations; none material at group level

Whistleblower/governance complaint signals:
- No public whistleblower events identified
- Italian companies with state ownership are periodically subject to Corte dei Conti (Court of Auditors) oversight; no material adverse findings on Acea management reported publicly in L3Y

Anti-takeover provisions:
- Beyond the 51% stake concentration, Acea's statutes do not include additional poison pills; the municipal ownership is the structural defence

Structural governance fragility:
- The primary governance risk is not fraud or misconduct — it is political capture; strategic decisions that serve municipal interests (maintaining Rome's water/electricity services at regulated prices, employing politically-connected labour) may diverge from decisions that maximise shareholder value
- A change in Rome's municipal government (next mayoral election cycle 2026) creates a credible management disruption risk, as has historically been the pattern

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on governance — the clean audit record and absence of restatements are the floor, not the ceiling; the related-party receivables dynamic means Acea is in effect extending credit to its controlling shareholder, who has no incentive to accelerate repayment; this is not expropriation but it is capital inefficiency by governance design; the mechanism by which apparent governance cleanliness masks structural concern is precisely the Roma Capitale relationship — it is disclosed, it is "routine", and it is systematically value-leaking
