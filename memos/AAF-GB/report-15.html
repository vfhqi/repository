§7 — Narrative vs Reality Check + Sceptical Synthesis

J-front verdict: The strongest narrative-vs-reality disconnect is management's persistent USD-framing of ARPU and revenue growth, which obscures a local-currency purchasing power deterioration that directly undermines the subscriber monetisation thesis

Narrative claim — USD ARPU growth: Management reports ARPU in USD and frames ARPU growth positively across most markets; this is technically accurate for USD translation but misleading about real user monetisation trajectory
- Reality signal: In Nigeria and Zambia (two of the top-five markets by EBITDA contribution), local-currency ARPU in real terms (inflation-adjusted) declined in FY24-FY25; the USD number improved primarily due to partial naira stabilisation, not genuine monetisation improvement
- Disconnect identified: investors anchoring to USD ARPU growth as a proxy for competitive health are being given a misleading signal; the relevant question is whether users are paying more in local-currency real terms, and in the two largest challenged markets the answer is no

Narrative claim — MoMo competitive moat is widening: Management consistently describes Airtel Money's agent network and transaction value growth as evidence of a widening competitive moat
- Reality signal: MTN MoMo commentary (§6) and the former MoMo product manager (§2) both indicate the moat is narrowing in East and Central Africa; the moat may be widening in francophone West Africa and Southern Africa, where MTN is less present
- Disconnect identified: the management narrative is geographically undifferentiated — it papers over a market-by-market picture where MoMo strength is genuinely varied

Narrative claim — leverage reduction on track: CMD and earnings calls characterise leverage reduction as "progressing" toward the sub-2.0x target
- Reality signal: the absence of a specific timeline reinstatement at the most recent CMD and the conditional dividend language (§1 unusual quote) suggest the deleverage trajectory is slower than the prior guidance implied
- Disconnect identified: the walk-back is real but subtle; management has avoided explicitly lowering the target while softening the timeline language sufficiently to prepare the market for slower delivery

False-friend pattern — management confidence vs insider transaction magnitude: CEO and CFO language remains constructive; but neither has made a material discretionary open-market purchase (§3); the constructive language is not backed by large insider capital commitment
- Insider-vs-narrative gap: the gap is not alarming (no insider selling) but the absence of conviction-scale purchasing given the share price weakness is a soft false-friend signal

Expert-vs-narrative gap — operational depth: Management's "strong execution culture" narrative is not supported by multiple expert-network sources who flagged below-C-suite management quality and operational fragmentation (§2)
- Single largest credibility risk: if the execution culture gap materialises as operational delivery misses in FY26, the market will discount management credibility sharply — this is the highest-impact credibility risk in the current set

Customer-vs-narrative gap: Management describes enterprise as a growth vertical; the only named enterprise customer perspective in the research set is a switcher who cited reliability and pricing inflexibility as reasons to move to MTN Enterprise (§5 via §2)
- Note: single data point, cannot be generalised; but it is the only customer signal available and it is negative

Reverse-engineered bear: The bear case reconstructed from §1-§6 is: (1) FX drag compresses USD financials further than management's stabilisation narrative implies; (2) MoMo moat narrows in key East/Central African markets under MTN MoMo pressure; (3) deleverage slips, dividend sustainability becomes questioned; (4) below-C-suite execution fails to deliver the operational efficiency gains needed to offset revenue headwinds; (5) expert concerns about market-level EBITDA quality prove accurate, triggering a restatement or rebase
- Probability weighting: this is a tail-risk scenario, not a base case, but items (1) through (3) are individually above 20% probability in a 12-18M horizon based on available signal quality

Narrative claim — regulatory risk is manageable: Management consistently describes regulatory relationships as "constructive" and regulatory risk as "manageable"
- Reality signal: the former regulatory affairs expert (§2) described regulatory relationships in Tanzania and Uganda as "functional but fragile" with increasing revenue-sharing demands; this is a qualitatively different characterisation than "constructive"
- Disconnect identified: the gap between "constructive" (management) and "functional but fragile" (former insider) is directionally concerning even if neither characterisation is provably wrong at the current moment

Narrative claim — Airtel Money IPO / carve-out optionality is live: Management and CMD language keeps carve-out optionality "on the table"
- Reality signal: no named financial adviser engagement, no regulatory pre-filing in any market, and market conditions for African fintech listings have deteriorated since the initial carve-out commentary in FY22-FY23
- Disconnect identified: the carve-out optionality is increasingly theoretical; it functions as a valuation-support narrative rather than an actionable near-term catalyst

Highest-conviction concern: The combination of USD-framing distortion + MoMo moat narrowing signal + deleverage timeline walk-back constitutes the most coherent cluster of credibility challenges; each individually is manageable, but the three together reduce the reliability of management's 3-year EPS trajectory guidance below what would be required for high-conviction DD passage

Confidence in management narrative: medium plausibility — management is credible on market opportunity and structural thesis but shows observable hedging on near-term execution, FX, and deleverage trajectory that undermines high-conviction EPS predictability
