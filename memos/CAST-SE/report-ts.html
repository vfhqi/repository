Section 2 – The 80/20 (bottom line up front) 8 findings

Section summary
  1. A transitional commercial real estate player wrestling with high office vacancy and elevated leverage, pivoting toward aggressive capital recycling and share buybacks to enforce a strict return-on-equity mandate.
1.
Heavy urban portfolio: The business generates recurring rental income by leasing commercial space, predominantly city-centre offices and public sector buildings, to corporate and government tenants across the Nordic region
2.
Transformational investment case: The core argument for investing relies on management successfully executing an aggressive asset rotation strategy, where selling mature assets at a premium to buy back heavily discounted shares mechanically drives up net asset value per share
3.
Persistent leasing headwinds: The single most important swing factor for future earnings is the trajectory of net leasing, which must turn consistently positive to reverse a damaging trend of rising economic vacancy in core urban markets
4.
Deteriorating occupancy metrics: Operational performance has struggled recently, anchored by a record-high vacancy rate that climbed to twelve percent in the first quarter of 2026, up two full percentage points year-over-year
5.
Aggressive self-help pivot: The company has decisively shifted its capital distribution framework, suspending its traditional cash dividend in favour of rolling share repurchase programmes totalling over SEK 3.4 billion to exploit the persistent market discount
6.
Looming credit risks: The most critical risk threatening the bull case is the elevated loan-to-value ratio of over fifty-one percent, which has already prompted major rating agencies to revise their credit outlooks to negative
7.
Mixed first-look verdict: A closer look is warranted for deep-value investors comfortable with real estate cycles, primarily because the aggressive share buyback programme provides a hard floor under the stock despite undeniably soft underlying tenant demand

Section 3 – What the company does 13 findings

Section summary
  1. Castellum operates a massive, predominantly Swedish property portfolio anchored by central offices and public sector tenants, leveraging a decentralized local management model to drive tenant retention.

Portfolio composition and segments

1.
Dominant office weighting: The asset base is heavily concentrated in the traditional office sector, which accounts for roughly sixty-two percent of the total portfolio value, exposing the company directly to post-pandemic shifts in workplace utilization
2.
Defensive public sector: A secondary stabilizing segment comprises public sector properties, representing about seventeen percent of asset value, which provides highly secure, long-term contractual cash flows backed by government entities
3.
Logistics and retail mix: The remainder of the physical portfolio includes warehouse and light industry assets at fifteen percent, capturing e-commerce tailwinds, alongside a minimal five percent exposure to traditional retail spaces
4.
Norwegian strategic footprint: Beyond its directly owned assets, the company exercises influence in Norway through a thirty-eight percent equity stake in Entra, though management has acknowledged this low-float structure is not an optimal long-term solution

Tenant profile and lease structure

5.
Highly diversified customer base: The company operates with minimal individual tenant concentration risk, maintaining approximately seven thousand four hundred commercial leases where the single largest contract accounts for barely over one percent of total rental income
6.
Government tenant anchor: The Swedish Police stands as the single largest individual tenant across the portfolio, anchoring the public sector segment and providing exceptional credit quality during periods of macroeconomic turbulence
7.
Standardized lease durations: The weighted average unexpired lease term sits at approximately 3.6 years, which is standard for the Swedish commercial property market but requires constant, active renewal management by local leasing teams
8.
Decentralized operational model: The company relies on a boots-on-the-ground management structure, empowering local regional directors to handle tenant relationships, leasing negotiations, and property upgrades directly rather than routing decisions through a central bureaucracy

Industry positioning and competitive landscape

9.
Market scale advantage: Sitting at a valuation of roughly SEK 138 billion in gross property assets, the company enjoys unmatched scale advantages in procurement and financing compared to smaller regional peers like Platzer or Wihlborgs
10.
Fierce metropolitan competition: In the highly concentrated Stockholm market, the company faces intense competition for premium office tenants against specialized listed peers such as Fabege and Atrium Ljungberg
11.
Sustainability premium positioning: The company utilizes green certifications as a core competitive differentiator, with over seventy-two percent of the portfolio certified for sustainability, securing premium rental rates and cheaper green-bond financing
12.
Other side of the story: While the decentralized local management model theoretically improves tenant intimacy, it has historically bloated central administrative costs, forcing the new executive team to slash sixty head-office roles to restore basic operational efficiency

Section summary
  1. Multi-year financial performance shows robust historical margins now masking a deteriorating organic growth profile, exacerbated by rising vacancies and painful downward property revaluations.

Top-line growth and rental trajectory

Stagnating organic rents: The top-line trajectory has flattened considerably, with like-for-like rental income falling by two percent in the first quarter of 2026, a sharp deceleration from the mild positive growth seen in early 2024

Margin, earnings, and valuation adjustments

5.
Declining earnings generation: The core profitability metric, Income from Property Management, contracted by 4.4 percent year-over-year in 2025, driving EPRA EPS down to SEK 8.95 from SEK 9.32 the prior year
6.
Significant portfolio write-downs: Asset values have taken a severe hit during the high-interest-rate regime, with the company writing down property values by approximately SEK 14 billion, or nearly ten percent, since the market peak in 2022
7.
One-off earnings noise: Recent profitability metrics are heavily clouded by one-off items, including a SEK 30 million cost for bond consent solicitations and a SEK 40 million restructuring charge for head-office layoffs in late 2025

Balance sheet, leverage, and liquidity

8.
Elevated gearing levels: The loan-to-value ratio has crept up to 51.4 percent in early 2026, breaching the psychological fifty percent threshold and significantly exceeding the company's long-term internal target of forty percent
9.
Deteriorating debt multiples: Net debt to EBITDA has expanded uncomfortably to 9.5x in the trailing twelve months to March 2026, up from 8.2x two years prior, reflecting both declining earnings and debt-funded capital expenditures
10.
Robust liquidity buffer: Despite elevated leverage, the immediate refinancing risk is thoroughly mitigated by a massive liquidity reserve, featuring over SEK 22 billion in unutilized credit facilities and cash equivalents available to cover impending maturities
11.
Honest guidance history: Management's communication historically reads as honest and unvarnished, perfectly exemplified by the new CEO stating plainly on the first-quarter 2026 earnings call that the tenant market is tough and they must adapt
12.
Other side of the story: While the headline loan-to-value ratio looks dangerously high at 51.4 percent, the company successfully executed a massive SEK 5.6 billion property sale to AP7 at a premium to book value, proving the underlying asset valuations hold up in actual market transactions

Section 5 – The possible investment case 11 findings

Section summary
  1. The investment case strengthens modestly for deep-value buyers, driven by a deliberate strategy to shrink the asset base and aggressively repurchase shares at a steep discount to net asset value.

Capital reallocation and buyback yield

1.
Aggressive share repurchases: The primary driver for per-share earnings growth is the newly authorized share buyback programme, which fundamentally alters the capital return mechanism by retiring millions of shares below net asset value
2.
Dividend suspension catalyst: By entirely suspending the traditional cash dividend in 2025, management freed up SEK 1.2 billion annually, creating a highly probable mechanism to generate an immediate accretive yield through continuous stock repurchases
3.
Plausibility of execution: The probability of this buyback strategy succeeding is high, supported by the fact that the company has already repurchased over 24 million shares by early 2026, proving management is fully committed to the pivot

Interest rate relief and cost of debt

4.
Falling borrowing costs: A critical margin driver is the stabilization of European interest rates, which directly impacts the company's average cost of debt that currently hovers around 3.1 percent across a relatively short 3.3-year fixed-term profile
5.
Mitigated refinancing headwinds: Analysts at Morgan Stanley point out that if the Swedish central bank initiates regular rate cuts, the company's high interest coverage ratio of 3.2x will naturally expand, easing pressure on the bottom line
6.
Plausibility of rate relief: The plausibility of meaningful interest rate relief is medium, as persistent inflation shocks could unexpectedly reverse the current easing cycle, forcing the company to refinance upcoming bond maturities at punishingly high market spreads

Portfolio high-grading and operational efficiency

7.
Strategic asset disposals: The final earnings driver relies on high-grading the portfolio by shedding non-core assets, exactly as demonstrated by the recent SEK 5.6 billion sale of nine mature public sector properties to the state pension fund AP7
8.
Administrative cost slashing: Operational margins are set to expand purely through internal self-help, with the recent cull of central administrative staff explicitly designed to generate SEK 50 million in hard, recurring annual cost savings
9.
Independent bull case drivers: The beauty of this investment case is that the drivers are largely independent; the company can successfully execute accretive share buybacks using disposal proceeds even if macroeconomic interest rate relief fails to materialize immediately
10.
Other side of the story: The aggressive disposal strategy mechanically shrinks the total rental base, meaning that unless the remaining portfolio generates spectacular organic rental growth, absolute top-line revenue will continue to contract, entirely offsetting the per-share benefits of the buybacks

Section 6 – Valuation 8 findings

Section summary
  1. The shares look objectively cheap against historical averages and current net asset values, pricing in significant ongoing pessimism regarding the Nordic office sector's ability to retain tenants.
1.
Discount to asset value: The stock trades at a severe twenty-four to thirty percent discount to its reported Net Reinstatement Value of SEK 159 per share, a dramatic widening from its historical ten-year average discount of just seven percent
2.
Earnings multiple compression: The shares currently command a price-to-EPRA earnings multiple of roughly 13.0x to 13.5x on forward estimates, placing it at the lower end of the valuation spectrum compared to direct peers like Atrium Ljungberg
3.
Dividend yield elimination: Traditional income investors must look elsewhere, as the historical forward dividend yield of four to five percent has been deliberately crushed to zero percent to explicitly fund the ongoing share repurchase mandates
4.
Sell-side price targets: The median broker price target hovers around SEK 115 to SEK 120, implying negligible upside from current trading levels, as analysts struggle to underwrite near-term growth amidst deteriorating leasing volumes
5.
Broker divergence: DNB analysts maintain a cautious Hold rating with a SEK 110 target, citing the company's lack of organic growth relative to peers, while Morgan Stanley holds an Underweight rating at SEK 115, primarily driven by weak underlying tenant demand
6.
Priced-in expectations: The current market valuation appears to be entirely pricing in a prolonged period of stagnant rental growth and permanently higher structural vacancy rates across the core Stockholm office portfolio
7.
Other side of the story: Valuation models based heavily on current net asset value assume the underlying property appraisals are perfectly accurate, but if private-market price discovery forces further write-downs, the perceived thirty percent discount could vanish overnight as the denominator shrinks

Section 7 – Key risks and the other side 9 findings

Section summary
  1. The bear case is highly credible and centres on the toxic combination of soaring office vacancies and a heavy debt load that leaves virtually no margin for operational error.

Vacancy deterioration and office exposure

1.
Structural office headwinds: The single failure mode that would most damage the investment case is a permanent structural shift in Swedish workplace habits, rendering the company's sixty-two percent office weighting obsolete and forcing massive rental discounts
2.
Accelerating tenant departures: The immediate transmission mechanism to earnings is already visible, as tenant terminations vastly outpaced new signings throughout 2025, directly bleeding net operating income and expanding the vacancy rate to twelve percent
3.
False friend caution: The recent headline-grabbing 24,000 square metre lease with Ericsson acts as a dangerous false friend, potentially masking the broader, highly fragmented erosion of small-to-medium enterprise tenants vacating secondary B-location office spaces

Leverage threshold and credit rating pressure

4.
Credit rating downgrade risk: S&P recently revised the company's BBB credit rating outlook to negative, explicitly citing the material share buyback programme and elevated investment spending that keeps debt-to-capital metrics hovering dangerously near downside thresholds
5.
Debt cost transmission: If the rating agencies follow through with a formal downgrade to high-yield status, the transmission to earnings would be brutal and immediate, instantly locking the company out of the cheap commercial paper market and spiking interest expenses

Macro-economic headwinds and tenant bankruptcies

6.
Rising corporate defaults: The broader Nordic macroeconomic slowdown directly threatens cash collections, highlighted vividly in early 2025 when the bankruptcy of major tenant Northvolt instantly wiped out SEK 68 million in expected rental income
7.
Crucial early-warning signal: The single most vital metric to monitor quarterly is the net leasing figure excluding major build-to-suit projects; if the granular, day-to-day leasing of existing vacant space remains negative, the core business is still shrinking
8.
Other side of the story: The bear case fundamentally ignores the defensive fortress provided by the seventeen percent public sector portfolio, where sovereign-backed tenants like the Swedish Police guarantee a massive, unshakeable bedrock of cash flow regardless of broader economic recessions

Section 8 – Management: what is changing 5 findings

Section summary
  1. A ruthless executive overhaul orchestrated by an activist core shareholder is aggressively dismantling the legacy growth culture in favour of hyper-focused capital discipline.
1.
Activist-driven CEO transition: The appointment of Pål Ahlsén as CEO in late 2025 signals a massive strategic pivot, directly reflecting the desires of dominant twenty-five percent shareholder Roger Akelius to enforce strict profitability standards over mere portfolio expansion
2.
Wholesale executive clear-out: The new leadership mandate triggered an immediate and total restructuring of the C-suite, resulting in the abrupt departures of the CFO, Chief Investment Officer, General Counsel, and Chief Operating Officer to streamline the organizational hierarchy
3.
Incentive structure elimination: In a highly unusual move for a listed Nordic real estate firm, the board has entirely discontinued the bonus scheme for all employees, including executive management, starting in 2026, aligning perfectly with the new cost-slashing austerity mandate
4.
Insider alignment shift: The dramatic reduction of executive roles from thirteen to ten, coupled with the installation of Christoffer Strömback as a dual-hatted CFO and Head of Transactions, proves leadership is actively cutting its own overhead before asking shareholders for patience

Section 9 – Net read and what to check next 5 findings

Section summary
  1. Castellum warrants a mixed verdict, offering a deeply discounted entry point supported by ruthless self-help measures, though investors must stomach severely deteriorating leasing fundamentals in the near term.
1.
Strongest argument for: The management team is executing exactly the right playbook for a mature, over-leveraged real estate company, selling premium assets like the AP7 portfolio at book value to aggressively retire heavily discounted equity
2.
Other side of the story: Financial engineering through share buybacks cannot permanently mask a broken underlying product; with vacancies climbing relentlessly to twelve percent, the core office portfolio is clearly failing to attract organic market demand
3.
Next action – Validate leasing pipeline: Commission a targeted deep-dive into the Stockholm and Gothenburg local commercial brokerage markets to verify if Castellum's asking rents are still competitive, or if they are losing market share to newer, higher-spec developments by peers like Fabege
4.
Next action – Stress-test covenants: Calculate a granular downside scenario on the balance sheet to determine exactly how much further property values must fall before the 51.4 percent EPRA LTV breaches strict bond-market covenants or triggers an automatic S&P credit downgrade
