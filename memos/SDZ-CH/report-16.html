§6 — Governance Structure + Red Flags

  • Clean, no material red flags: Swiss governance framework applies (SIX Listing Rules, OaEC/VegüV, Swiss Code of Best Practice); one-share-one-vote structure; Novartis founder-block fully distributed post-spin; no material regulatory investigation; no restatement; audit committee independence confirmed

Voting rights structure:
- One share, one vote: confirmed — no dual-class structure, no golden share, no voting caps
- Novartis retained a ~20% stake at spin (Oct 2023); reduced over 2024 via secondary sales and placement; by mid-2025 Novartis shareholding was below 5% (disclosed in major shareholder notifications on SIX) — the founder-overhang has substantially cleared — positive structural development
- Anti-takeover provisions: Swiss law provides some "poison pill"-adjacent protections (shareholder approval required for certain thresholds); no extraordinary anti-takeover provisions beyond standard Swiss statute

Related-party transactions:
- Novartis TSA (Transitional Service Agreement): disclosed and quantified; covers IT, HR, legal, manufacturing services from Novartis to Sandoz during the wind-down period; fees paid to Novartis under TSA: ~USD 300–400m annually at peak (FY2023), declining to ~USD 100m by end-2024 as separation completes; disclosed in annual report notes — no adverse terms, arm's-length basis confirmed by audit committee review
- No other material related-party transactions disclosed

Audit history:
- External auditor: KPMG (continued from Novartis-era; KPMG signed off on the spin-off carve-out financial statements)
- No auditor change since listing — continuity is appropriate given the complexity of carve-out accounting
- No material weaknesses (as defined under SOX / Swiss equivalent) disclosed
- No restatement of financial results in the post-spin period
- Audit Committee has confirmed auditor independence review annually — standard disclosure

Legal/regulatory issues:
- No SEC/DOJ investigation noted in public disclosures
- Sandoz is subject to FINMA (Swiss Financial Market Supervisory Authority) as a SIX-listed company; no FINMA enforcement action disclosed
- Product liability: generic pharma inherently carries product liability exposure; no class-action of material magnitude (>CHF 100m) disclosed in 2023–2025 period
- Opioid litigation exposure: Sandoz is not a named defendant in the major US opioid settlements (Novartis Sandoz's US generics portfolio did not include opioid formulations at material scale); lower litigation risk than some US generics peers (Teva, AbbVie)
- US price-fixing / antitrust: generic pharma has faced industry-wide scrutiny on price coordination (the DOJ investigation covered ~20 generic companies); Sandoz's specific exposure in post-spin disclosures is flagged as "industry-wide investigation context" but no named defendant status for Sandoz specifically per public record — this is a watch item, not a confirmed red flag
- EU competition: no active European Commission investigation disclosed

Whistleblower / governance complaint signals:
- No public whistleblower case or governance complaint noted in press or regulatory disclosures
- Sandoz has an ethics hotline (disclosed in annual report); number of reports and resolution data not public (standard for this size)

Sceptical bullet — governance:
- Risk that the "clean governance" read reflects the newness of the company rather than demonstrated governance discipline under stress; Sandoz has not yet faced a major crisis (product recall, pricing investigation, M&A gone wrong) as an independent entity — the governance framework has been tested in benign conditions only; the true governance test will come when management faces a material operational setback or a transformative M&A decision; additionally, the US generic antitrust pricing investigations (ongoing DOJ legacy inquiry covering the industry) represent a tail risk that is disclosed in boilerplate terms but may have underappreciated Sandoz-specific exposure given the breadth of the DOJ sweep across the generic industry
