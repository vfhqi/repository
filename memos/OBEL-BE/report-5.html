§3 — Revenue + Organic Growth Analysis

J-front verdict: Revenue trajectory has stabilised at the margin but is not yet in genuine organic growth; fixed broadband is the growth pocket; mobile is a structural drag [#J]

  • Reported growth: FY 2024 total revenue approximately -0.5% to flat YoY (FY 2023: ~-2%), representing a meaningful deceleration of decline but not yet positive organic growth [#A]

  • Sub: The inflection from -2% to ~0% took two years (FY 2023 to FY 2024); accelerating into positive territory in FY 2025 requires mobile stabilisation

  • Organic growth: OBEL-BE does not operate with material FX or M&A distortion (Belgium-focused, EUR-reported); reported growth is a reasonable proxy for organic growth [#A]

  • Segment trajectory: Fixed/broadband segment is accelerating; mobile segment is decelerating / mildly declining [#A]

  • Sub: Fixed broadband net adds driven by fibre rollout via Fiberklaar (Wallonia) and Wyre (formerly Nexfibre, Flanders) partnerships — OBEL-BE's access-layer strategy

  • Sub: Mobile postpaid subscribers broadly stable but ARPU under pressure; prepaid continues structural decline industry-wide

  • Geographic mix: OBEL-BE is effectively mono-market (Belgium only); geographic diversification is not a revenue lever; all trends are Belgian-market-specific [#A]

  • Price contribution: Low-single-digit price increases were pushed through in mobile and fixed in FY 2024 (indexed to Belgian CPI-like mechanism), consistent with regulatory and market norms; these partially offset volume/mix headwinds [#A]

  • Sub: Belgian telco price indexation is relatively automatic; it protects ARPU in inflationary periods but does not constitute genuine pricing power above inflation

  • Volume contribution: Net volume contribution was marginally negative in mobile (subscriber count broadly flat, ARPU slight pressure) and modestly positive in fixed (fibre net adds adding revenue-bearing subscribers) [#A]

  • Pricing power: Pricing power is moderate but not exceptional; the Belgian market has three credible MNOs (Proximus, Telenet/BASE, Orange Belgium) plus growing MVNO segment; price discipline has held better than feared in 2024 but remains vulnerable to competitive resets [#A]

  • Market share: Mobile market share broadly stable at approximately 25–27% of Belgian mobile subscribers — consistent with FY 2022 and FY 2023; OBEL-BE is the number-three MNO [#A]

  • Sub: Fixed broadband market share is significantly lower (~10–12% nationally) and growing from a low base via the fibre access partnerships

  • End-market demand: Belgian consumer telecoms demand is resilient but not buoyant; mobile penetration is saturated (>100% SIM penetration); fixed broadband demand growth driven by upgrade from copper/cable to fibre rather than new subscribers [#A]

  • Forward revenue guidance: Management guided FY 2025 for low-single-digit revenue growth (+1–3%), implying mobile stabilisation is assumed and fixed broadband growth continues [#A]

  • Sub: This guidance is achievable if mobile ARPU floor is near; it is at risk if Proximus re-prices aggressively following completion of its own fibre programme

  • Sell-side revision: Post-FY 2024, public consensus moved FY 2025 revenue estimates modestly up by ~1–2%, reflecting increased confidence in fixed broadband trajectory rather than mobile improvement [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The fixed broadband growth story depends critically on Fiberklaar/Wyre access footprint expansion progressing on schedule; if fibre rollout is delayed or wholesale access terms deteriorate, the primary offset to mobile erosion disappears; mobile stabilisation assumed in guidance has not yet been demonstrated — H1 2025 is the proof point [#J]

§4 — Margin Profile + Quality

J-front verdict: EBITDA-AL margin is stable at ~29–30% but quality is mixed; cost discipline is real but partly driven by one-off savings; D&A intensity keeps net margins structurally thin [#J]

  • Gross margin: OBEL-BE does not separately disclose a "gross margin" in the traditional sense; the relevant operating metric is EBITDA-AL margin (~29–30%) and EBITDAaL (before leases, ~32–34%) [#A]
  • Sub: These levels are broadly consistent with L3Y trend (FY 2022: ~30–31%, FY 2023: ~29–30%, FY 2024: ~29–30%)

  • Sub: Peer European Tier-2 telcos (comparably sized) typically operate EBITDA margins in the 28–35% range; OBEL-BE sits towards the lower-middle of this band

  • Margin direction: Margin is flat to very marginally contracting over L3Y; the company has not delivered operating leverage in the traditional sense — revenue stabilisation has allowed margin defence, not expansion [#A]

  • Decomposition: Margin stability in FY 2024 reflects approximately: +1–2 ppts from cost savings (network opex, lower commercial subsidies), offset by -1–2 ppts from revenue mix shift (lower-margin mobile increasingly offset by lower-margin fixed access) [#A]

  • Operating leverage: ❌ Negative operating leverage in FY 2022–2023 (revenues fell faster than costs); FY 2024 showed marginal improvement but operating leverage has not yet turned positive [#A]

  • Sub: Operating leverage inflection requires +2–3% organic revenue growth sustained over 2–3 years; current trajectory is insufficient

  • D&A trend: ❌ D&A is elevated and rising due to fibre network capitalisation, 5G spectrum amortisation, and lease right-of-use asset depreciation; estimated D&A of ~€350–380m absorbs a significant portion of EBITDA-AL [#A]

  • Sub: D&A as % of revenues is approximately 24–26% — high for a sub-scale European telco; peer telcos with mature networks are in the 20–22% range

  • OpEx ratio: Total operating expenses (ex-D&A) as % of revenues have been broadly stable at ~70–71%; targeted cost efficiency programmes (workforce restructuring, network sharing agreements) are contributing incrementally [#A]

  • One-off costs: FY 2024 included approximately €15–25m of restructuring charges (workforce transformation programme); these are recurring in character though presented as non-recurring, which is a mild quality flag [#A]

  • Sub: OBEL-BE has carried restructuring charges in most years since FY 2020; treating them as truly non-recurring would be analytically misleading

  • Adjusted vs reported margins: The gap between adjusted EBITDA-AL and reported net income is very wide (~€200m+), principally D&A; investors who anchor to EBITDA-AL without scrutinising D&A and finance costs will overstate earning power [#A]

  • Non-GAAP gap: Adjustments are primarily: restructuring charges, stock-based compensation (minimal), and one-off integration costs; adjustments are declining slightly in absolute terms but have not reached zero [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: Cost savings in FY 2024 may be partly structural (headcount reduction is permanent) but partly cyclical (reduced commercial spend in a softening competitive market); if competition intensifies in 2025–2026, commercial cost reflation could reverse a portion of the margin defence; the mechanism is straightforward — promotional pricing wars require higher acquisition and retention spend [#J]

J-front verdict: Cash generation is real but thin after elevated capex; the company is at the peak of its investment cycle and FCF will improve materially only if capex moderates from 2025–2026 onwards [#J]

  • Operating cash flow: Operating cash flow (pre-capex) was approximately €380–420m in FY 2024, broadly consistent with EBITDA-AL adjusted for working capital and lease payments [#A]

  • Cash conversion: EBITDA-to-operating cash flow conversion of approximately 85–92% is reasonable for a capital-intensive telco; no major working capital distortions observed [#A]

  • Sub: European telco peers typically operate at 80–90% cash conversion; OBEL-BE is within normal range

  • NWC trend: Working capital was approximately neutral in FY 2024; no unusual build or release of receivables/payables visible in public filings [#A]

  • Capex: ❌ Total capex approximately €300–330m, representing ~21–23% of revenues — elevated vs European telco sector norm of ~15–18%; driven by 5G network rollout and fibre access co-investment commitments [#A]

  • Sub: Co-investment in fibre networks (Fiberklaar/Wyre) means a portion of capex is technically off-balance-sheet (equity method), but access-fee obligations are ongoing opex equivalents

  • Free cash flow: FCF (operating cash flow less capex) of approximately €70–100m in FY 2024 — thin relative to market cap and EBITDA; FCF yield is low-single-digit [#A]

  • Sub: FCF yield of approximately 2–4% at current market cap (~€1.1–1.3bn) is below the sector median for European telcos of ~5–7%

  • Net debt: Net debt approximately €800–900m (including lease liabilities: ~€1.2–1.3bn); net debt/EBITDA-AL approximately 2.0–2.1x (excluding leases) or ~2.8–3.0x (including leases) [#A]

  • Sub: Management's stated leverage target is approximately 2.0–2.5x net debt/EBITDA-AL (excluding leases); current positioning is at the upper end but within target range

  • Dividend coverage: Ordinary dividend confirmed at approximately €0.50 per share (~€40–50m total cost); covered by FCF but with limited headroom; coverage ratio approximately 1.5–2.0x [#A]

  • Sub: The dividend is affordable but not generous; yield of approximately 3.5–4.5% at current share price is modest for a telco

  • Buyback pace: Buyback of ~€20–30m announced; modest signal value, limited economic impact; total shareholder returns (dividends + buyback) approximately €60–80m in FY 2024 [#A]

  • Capital return sustainability: Sustainable at current FCF levels only if capex declines as guided from 2025; any capex overrun or deterioration in operating cash flow would pressure the dividend [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: FCF of €70–100m is extremely thin and highly sensitive to capex phasing; if fibre build costs escalate (as they have for peers including Proximus), or if 5G rollout obligations bring incremental spectrum capex, FCF could compress to near zero; the mechanism through which capital returns become unsustainable is a 10–15% capex overrun combined with flat revenue — a scenario that is not implausible given Belgian infrastructure complexity [#J]

§7 — Strategic Execution + Operational Progress

J-front verdict: Strategic execution on the fibre access transformation is on track; mobile network quality improvement is progressing; the pace is adequate but not differentiated [#J]

  • Fibre access strategy: The Fiberklaar (Wallonia) and Wyre (Flanders) co-investment partnerships are progressing broadly on schedule; OBEL-BE is gaining access to fibre infrastructure without owning the network outright, preserving balance sheet capacity [#A]

  • Sub: This asset-light access model is strategically sensible but creates dependency on partner execution timelines; any Fiberklaar or Wyre build delay directly impairs OBEL-BE's growth capacity

  • Network sharing: The MORAN (Mobile Open Radio Access Network) sharing agreement with Proximus continues to deliver incremental network cost savings (estimated €20–30m per annum in opex benefit); a differentiating structural cost advantage for OBEL-BE vs a standalone network build [#A]

  • Cost programme: The workforce transformation programme (targeting ~200–300 FTE reductions over 2022–2025) is on track; cumulative savings estimated at €15–25m annualised by end of FY 2024 [#A]

  • Sub: Programme completion expected by FY 2025, after which incremental cost savings will require new initiatives

  • 5G rollout: 5G population coverage has expanded to approximately 75–80% of Belgian population by end-FY 2024, broadly on schedule with BIPT regulatory obligations; no coverage-obligation penalty risk visible [#A]

  • New product: Orange Belgium launched or expanded convergent "Love" bundle propositions (mobile + fibre) in FY 2024 as fibre footprint expanded; early churn metrics on convergent customers are reportedly favourable (lower churn vs mobile-only) [#A]

  • Sub: Convergent customer traction is a medium-term ARPU defence mechanism; the penetration base is still small but growing

  • Competitive wins/losses: No material B2B contract wins or losses publicly announced; B2B segment (approximately 20–25% of revenues) performed broadly in line, with modest growth from IT services bundling [#A]

  • Portfolio change: No material divestitures or acquisitions in FY 2024; OBEL-BE remains Belgium-only with no announced inorganic expansion plans [#A]

  • Leadership change: No C-suite changes announced in FY 2024; CEO Xavier Pichon remains in post (appointed 2021); management team stability is a mild positive given the multi-year network transformation in progress [#A]

  • Parent company dynamics: Orange S.A. (France) owns ~52% of OBEL-BE; parent company strategy and any potential squeeze-out or privatisation scenario remains a background optionality factor not directly addressed in FY 2024 results [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The fibre access strategy's elegance (asset-light, balance-sheet-preserving) also means OBEL-BE has limited control over the rollout pace and wholesale access terms; if Fiberklaar or Wyre renegotiate access pricing upwards (as infrastructure owners are incentivised to do once the build is complete), OBEL-BE's fixed broadband unit economics deteriorate without operational recourse; execution risk is therefore partly outsourced to third parties [#J]
