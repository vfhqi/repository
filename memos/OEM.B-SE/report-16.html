§6 — Governance Structure + Red Flags

J-front verdict: Governance is structurally clean at the formal level — no restatements, no material weaknesses, no regulatory investigations — but the dual-class structure and founder-family nomination influence are structural subordination features that minority investors must consciously accept.

Dual-class structure:
- ⚡ OEM International has a dual-class share structure: Class A shares (10 votes per share) and Class B shares (1 vote per share); the Pihlgren founding family holds predominantly Class A shares
- As of the most recent annual report, the Pihlgren family controls approximately 50–55% of total votes despite owning a lower percentage of total economic interest (estimated 30–40% of economic capital)
- Consequence: the founding family can block or determine outcomes on any ordinary resolution; the B-share (OEM.B) investor has structurally subordinated governance rights — this is the canonical risk of listed family-controlled companies in Sweden

Founder control:
- The founding family has been present since inception; the current Chairman is family-affiliated — this creates a non-independent board chair structure (the Swedish Code permits this if disclosed)
- The nomination committee, controlled by major shareholders, means the family has effective control over board composition

Voting rights:
- 10:1 voting differential — Class A: Class B; this is at the upper end of the permitted differential for Swedish listed companies; some Nordic dual-class peers use 5:1

Anti-takeover provision:
- The dual-class structure itself is the primary anti-takeover mechanism; no poison pill or golden parachute provisions visible in disclosures; Swedish corporate law provides limited scope for US-style anti-takeover devices

Related-party transactions:
- Annual reports disclose related-party transactions in accordance with IFRS and Swedish Code requirements; disclosed related-party transactions in L3Y are limited to: (a) director compensation (disclosed), (b) minor transactions with family-affiliated entities (disclosed, arm's-length certified)
- No concerning related-party transactions identified — the family appears to maintain appropriate separation between private interests and listed company activities
- Audit committee certifies arm's-length nature of any related-party transactions annually — standard process appears functional

Audit history:
- Auditor: PricewaterhouseCoopers (PwC) — consistent engagement; no auditor change in L5Y; auditor tenure is appropriately long for continuity without suggesting entrenchment (PwC rotation within firm manages independence)
- No material weaknesses noted in audit opinions L5Y
- No restatements of previously reported financials

Legal/regulatory issues:
- No disclosed securities class actions, regulatory investigations, or material litigation beyond ordinary-course commercial disputes
- OEM operates across multiple European jurisdictions — no cross-border regulatory issues disclosed
- Swedish Financial Supervisory Authority (Finansinspektionen): no disclosed investigations or sanctions

Whistleblower / governance complaint signals:
- No public whistleblower complaints or governance controversies visible in public record
- OEM discloses a whistleblower mechanism in annual reports — standard for Swedish Code compliance

Material weakness:
- None disclosed L5Y

Sceptical bullet — governance:
- Bearish read: The structural clean bill of health is real, but the mechanism by which governance risk could materialise is subtler than a restatement or regulatory action — it is the gradual accumulation of founder-friendly decisions that are formally correct but not optimal for minority shareholders (e.g. dividend policy that is conservative when a special distribution would be value-enhancing; M&A decisions that prioritise family-compatible partners over highest-ROIC targets; board composition that reinforces cultural consensus rather than challenging strategic assumptions); this "governance drift" is invisible in formal compliance metrics but detectable over multi-year capital allocation outcomes
