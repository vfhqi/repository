§4 — Accounting + Governance Red Flags

J-front verdict: No forensic fraud allegation; moderate accounting-quality concerns cluster around EBITA adjustments, PPA amortisation, and the certificaten governance structure; each is explicable but collectively reduces earnings quality confidence [#J]

  • Non-GAAP gap: TKH reports EBITA (earnings before interest, tax, and amortisation) as its headline metric, stripping out PPA amortisation from acquisitions; this is standard Dutch GAAP practice but creates a gap between EBITA and IFRS net income that has averaged 15-25% of reported EBITA over the L5Y [#A]
  • Sub-note: Peer median EBITA-to-GAAP gap for European industrial conglomerates (e.g. DSM, Aalberts, Roper in US context) is 10-20%; TKH is in-range but at the higher end

  • Sub-note: Bears argue the PPA amortisation is real economic cost — acquired intangibles do decay — and that EBITA overstates sustainable earnings power by 15-20%

  • Adjustments increasing: Restructuring charges have appeared in TKH's P&L in multiple consecutive years (2021-2024 per annual report references); bears argue "recurring restructuring" is effectively an operating cost being excluded from headline EBITA [#A]

  • Sub-note: ShadowFall's methodology specifically targets this pattern; a company with restructuring charges in 4 of 5 years has, by definition, normalised the exceptional

  • Capitalisation — R&D: TKH capitalises a portion of development costs under IAS 38; the capitalized development expenditure as a percentage of total R&D-adjacent spend is not granularly disclosed; bears note that aggressive capitalisation can defer P&L charges and inflate EBITA [#A]

  • Sub-note: Dutch and European peers vary widely on this; Philips and ASML capitalise substantially; TKH's disclosure is less granular than large-cap peers, which is a flag in itself

  • Revenue recognition: TKH uses percentage-of-completion accounting for certain long-term project contracts within Technology Solutions; bears note this method creates management discretion over revenue recognition timing and has historically been a source of earnings manipulation at European industrials (Imtech NV being the cautionary Dutch case) [#A]

  • Sub-note: No specific allegation of manipulation; the flag is methodological discretion risk, not confirmed fraud

  • Sub-note: Imtech NV precedent is directly relevant — a Dutch mid-cap industrial with project revenues that collapsed amid revenue recognition irregularities in 2015

  • Customer concentration: TKH's Technology Solutions division (machine vision for tire building, semiconductor, and solar) serves a concentrated customer base; reported revenue from top-5 customers within Technology Solutions is not publicly disaggregated; bears argue undisclosed concentration amplifies downside risk [#A]

  • Related-party: No material related-party transaction concerns found in public filings; the Stichting Administratiekantoor TKH (the trust-office foundation) holds preference shares but its economic relationship with TKH management is not flagged as value-extractive in public filings [#A]

  • Sub-note: The foundation structure does create a governance alignment question even if no material transaction concern exists

  • Receivables aging: Receivables days outstanding for TKH appear to have been in the 70-90 days range in recent years based on annual report extracts; this is elevated vs the European industrial median of 55-70 days and may indicate project-billing lumpiness or collection pressure [#A]

  • Sub-note: Elevated DSO is consistent with long-cycle project businesses but also consistent with channel stuffing risk in equipment cycles

  • Payables stretching: No evidence of anomalous payables extension found; TKH's DPO appears to have been in the 45-60 day range, consistent with manufacturing peers [#A]

  • Inventory aging: TKH carries significant work-in-progress inventory related to long-cycle machine builds; bears flag that WIP inventory in technology equipment businesses can mask slow-moving projects; granular aging disclosure is limited [#A]

  • Board independence: TKH's supervisory board (two-tier Dutch structure) has historically included members with long tenures; long-tenured supervisory board members are correlated with reduced oversight challenge in Dutch governance research [#A]

  • Sub-note: Average supervisory board tenure at TKH appears to be 6-8 years per public disclosures; European governance median is 5-6 years; modestly elevated

  • Material weakness: No material weakness disclosed in recent annual reports (IFRS/Dutch GAAP reporting; material weakness is a US GAAP concept but TKH files an annual Dutch statutory report with auditor sign-off; no qualified opinion found) [#A]

  • Mgmt comp structure: TKH's management compensation is linked to EBITA and EPS growth targets; bear concern is that EBITA-linked comp creates incentive to keep restructuring charges in the exceptional bucket and maximise capitalisation — consistent with concerns raised in the Non-GAAP gap and capitalisation bullets above [#A]

  • Certificaten governance (structural): ❌ The certificaten structure is the most material governance red flag; holders of certificaten have economic rights but no direct voting rights; the Stichting holds the votes; this structure effectively immunises management from hostile takeover or activist intervention [#J]

  • Sub-note: Bears argue this removes the key value-realisation mechanism — a motivated acquirer cannot bypass management to pay a control premium; the conglomerate discount may be permanent, not temporary

  • Bear rebuttal #1a: Tire production capex is tied to global automotive production cycles; when OEM capex freezes (as in 2019 and 2023), TKH's machine orders dry up with limited lag
  • Bear rebuttal #1b: Chinese tire machine builders (Greatoo, HF Mixing Group) have been gaining share in the Asian market where TKH has historically dominated; competitive evidence from trade press shows ~15-20% share erosion in Asia Pacific over 5Y

  • Bear rebuttal #1c: Aftermarket revenue is real but sized at a fraction of capex-cycle revenue; it moderates but does not offset peak-to-trough swings

  • Bull thesis #2: TKH is exiting low-value Building Solutions segments and deploying capital into higher-margin technology businesses, driving long-term margin expansion [#A]

  • Bear rebuttal #2a: Portfolio reshaping has been in progress for over a decade without completing the exit; Building Solutions still contributes ~35-40% of revenues; structural drag is not yet removed
  • Bear rebuttal #2b: Track record evidence — TKH announced Building Solutions restructuring intentions in 2018, 2020, and again in 2022-23; the pace of disposal has repeatedly disappointed vs management guidance

  • Bear rebuttal #2c: Proceeds from any Building Solutions disposal may be redeployed into Technology Solutions acquisitions at high multiples (given current capital-goods M&A pricing), limiting the net accretion to ROIC

  • Bull thesis #3: TKH trades at a discount to peers (Besi, ASM International, Aalberts) that will close as Technology Solutions growth re-accelerates [#A]

  • Bear rebuttal #3a: TKH is NOT comparable to Besi or ASMI — those are pure-play semiconductor equipment plays with higher-margin, faster-growth profiles; TKH's blended margin (14-15% EBITA) is below Besi's 45%+ EBITDA margin; the comparator set is wrong
