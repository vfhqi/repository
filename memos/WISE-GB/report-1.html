§1 — Business Model and Revenue Sources

Core Business Mechanics and Pricing Strategy

Wise bypasses the traditional, inefficient correspondent banking network by building direct integrations into domestic payment systems globally. By connecting directly to systems like Brazil's PIX, Japan's Zengin, and the Philippines' InstaPay, the company fundamentally alters the underlying economics of cross-border transfers and lowers its unit costs. This proprietary infrastructure allows the platform to power high-speed transfers, with roughly 75% of all payments arriving instantly in under 20 seconds.

The company's commercial strategy operates on a "Scale Economies Shared" model, where operational efficiencies and cost savings are deliberately reinvested into lower transaction prices. As processing costs decline, management intentionally compresses the cross-border take rate to incentivize volume growth and deepen competitive moats. Driven by this long-term adoption strategy, the overall cross-border take rate dropped to just 0.51% by the fourth quarter of fiscal 2026.

Wise's Scale Economies Shared model demonstrating volume growth offsetting intentional take rate compression. Jefferies highlights that this multi-product approach enables the business to monetize underlying consumer activity on a highly favorable, scalable cost structure. [Jefferies Research, 10 Apr]

Rothschild & Co Redburn adds that Wise's relentless focus on reducing prices represents a clear and effective strategy to continuously entrench its competitive advantage against banking incumbents. [Rothschild & Co Redburn, 24 Jun 24]

Personal and Business Segments

Revenue generation is primarily diversified across the Personal and Business customer bases, both of which utilize the ecosystem to send, convert, spend, and hold over 40 distinct currencies. The Personal segment acts as the foundation of the network's volume, surpassing 14.9 million active users and generating £106.4 billion in cross-border volumes during the 2025 fiscal year. Within this retail segment, the cross-border take rate averaged a slightly higher 0.56% at the end of fiscal 2026.

The Wise Business offering serves as a high-growth counterpart tailored for small and medium-sized businesses (SMBs) that rely on advanced features like batch payments, invoicing, and complex accounting integrations. Business active customers reached 572,000 by the fourth quarter of fiscal 2026, bringing their respective cross-border take rate down to an efficient 0.39%.

Beyond core cross-border transaction fees, the company effectively monetizes both segments through card and interchange revenue streams. This supplementary revenue continues to accelerate as users increasingly adopt the multi-currency Wise Card for everyday domestic and international spending.

The NII Framework and Capital Allocation

As customers increasingly embrace Wise as an everyday financial hub, total customer account holdings swelled to £29.4 billion by the end of fiscal 2026. In a higher-rate macroeconomic environment, these substantial deposits generate significant Net Interest Income for the company.

Wise retains the first 1% of gross interest yield entirely to cover the operational costs associated with maintaining customer accounts. For any interest income generated above this 1% baseline threshold, the company targets returning 80% directly back to customers, while keeping the remaining 20% as corporate profit. Because of regulatory constraints in certain geographies, unpaid interest allocations frequently flow back into income before tax, providing additional capital for reinvestment.

Jefferies argues that bearish concerns regarding future NII deceleration are largely overdone, noting that the company systematically leverages these interest windfalls to aggressively fund marketing and platform expansion efforts. [Jefferies Research, 18 Mar]

Deutsche Bank observes that retaining elevated interest profitability allows Wise to aggressively reinvest in product operations while simultaneously keeping core transaction take rates stable for the end user. [Deutsche Bank Research, 6 Nov 25]

Wise Platform and B2B White-Label Solutions

The Wise Platform represents a highly strategic B2B embedded finance solution that allows traditional banks, digital challengers, and major enterprises to route their international payments entirely through Wise's global rails. Notable live integrations span from agile digital players like Monzo and Nubank to global institutional giants such as Morgan Stanley and Standard Chartered.

Pricing for these enterprise platform partners typically operates on a customized cost-plus model. Because the partner institution handles all end-user KYC, AML compliance, and customer acquisition costs, Wise can offer highly competitive wholesale pricing while maintaining its standard contribution margins. This B2B segment currently accounts for approximately 5% of total cross-border volume, but management targets reaching 10% in the medium term, with a long-term goal of having the platform process over 50% of the network's overall volume.

Jefferies emphasizes that implementation timelines for these Platform deals range between three and six months, offering a highly scalable avenue to capture massive enterprise-level transaction volumes. [Jefferies Research, 10 Mar 25]

Rothschild & Co Redburn views the Platform product as the ultimate key to unlocking an estimated £18 trillion global cross-border payments market, providing an immense duration of growth for the firm. [Rothschild & Co Redburn, 13 Apr]

§2 — Industry Structure, Competitive Positioning, and Moat

The Cross-Border and B2B Payments Landscape

The global cross-border payments industry is a massive, fragmented market moving over $22 trillion annually in consumer and SME flows, with projections to reach $34 trillion by 2032. Within this ecosystem, the business-to-business (B2B) segment represents an exceptionally lucrative and historically underserved $161 billion annual revenue total addressable market. Despite the rapid digitization of domestic finance, international B2B payments remain heavily reliant on legacy Tier 1 banks.

Traditional banking incumbents still process over 70% of all cross-border transactions, utilizing the decades-old correspondent banking network which suffers from high operational costs, slow settlement times, and opaque fee structures. [Jefferies Research, 13 Jan 25]

While the consumer-to-consumer (C2C) remittance space is highly commoditized and intensely competitive, the broader cross-border market is experiencing a structural shift toward digital-first alternative providers that capitalize on the inefficiencies of legacy banks. [Expert Call, 9 Feb]

Wise's Competitive Positioning and Moat

Wise has established a formidable economic moat by systematically replacing the correspondent banking network with a proprietary grid of direct integrations into domestic payment schemes (such as the UK's Faster Payments and Brazil's PIX). [Expert Call, 22 May]

Rothschild & Co Redburn emphasizes that Wise's ultimate competitive advantage stems from a powerful network effect built around internal netting. As the platform scales, Wise can match opposing currency flows internally, minimizing the need to access open foreign exchange markets and dramatically reducing external liquidity costs. This operational efficiency directly fuels the firm's Scale Economies Shared model, enabling management to perpetually lower transaction fees and starve potential new entrants of market share. [Rothschild & Co Redburn, 20 Oct 25]

Rothschild & Co Redburn notes that while the broader cross-border payments industry suffers from exceptionally low switching costs—with surveys indicating only 16% of consumers maintain loyalty to a single provider—Wise's Personal and Business cohorts exhibit highly anomalous, sticky retention metrics. [Rothschild & Co Redburn, 20 Oct 25]

Jefferies Research highlights that this stickiness translates to exceptional customer acquisition efficiency, with approximately 66% of new users joining the platform via organic word-of-mouth recommendations. [Jefferies Research, 1 Apr]

Comprehensive Peer Comparison

Revolut — Operates as a comprehensive financial super-app, competing fiercely with Wise in Europe by offering aggressive subscription tiers that waive cross-border fees for power users. Commands an impressive 90% retention rate over a two-year cohort period. Jefferies Research observes that while Revolut drives higher revenue per employee, its customer acquisition costs have climbed to £33 per user vs Wise's £26 per user, reflecting heavier reliance on paid marketing. [Jefferies Research, 1 Apr]

Adyen — Not a direct C2C remittance competitor, but rather an enterprise-focused payments giant that sets the industry benchmark for unified commerce technology. Bernstein Research points out that Adyen's primary differentiator is its single-stack global platform, which grants large enterprise merchants superior authorization rates and frictionless cross-channel integrations. [Bernstein Research, 11 Jun 25]

Remitly — A digital-first monoline remittance provider focused specifically on outbound C2C transfers from developed nations to emerging markets. Frequently deploys tactical, loss-leading promotional pricing. Relies heavily on third-party aggregators and partners for payout, lacking the deep, capital-intensive direct integrations that insulate Wise's cost structure. [Expert Calls, 3 Apr / 9 Feb / 20 Jan]

Western Union — Quintessential legacy operator defending market position through an unmatched global network of physical agent locations. Struggles with bloated technological architecture and high pricing, routinely charging blended take rates of 4% to 6%. Deutsche Bank notes continued top-line pressure and margin contraction. [Deutsche Bank Research, 24 Apr]

PayPal — Possesses unparalleled global distribution and brand awareness, but cross-border operations are frequently criticized for high, opaque FX markups. Xoom (remittance arm) has historically underperformed its potential. [Expert Calls, 3 Apr / 24 Dec 25]

OFX — Aggressively pivoting toward the B2B sector through its "OFX 2.0" digital platform strategy, targeting mid-market corporate clients. [ARS, 19 May]

Emerging Threats: Stablecoins and Incumbent Banks

Stablecoins facilitate near-instantaneous global value transfer by bypassing traditional fiat clearing houses entirely. Rothschild & Co Redburn points out that the primary threat materializes through a "stablecoin sandwich" structure, where fiat is converted to stablecoins, moved across borders instantly, and then off-ramped back into local fiat.

Bernstein Research tempers this risk, arguing that retail stablecoin adoption in developed markets currently functions as a solution looking for a problem, as consumer demand is stifled by lack of trust, high off-ramp conversion fees, and absence of broad merchant acceptance. [Bernstein Research, 25 May]

Deutsche Bank Research corroborates this, viewing stablecoin infrastructure as largely additive for B2B back-end settlement rather than an immediate threat to consumer flows. [Deutsche Bank Research, 13 May]

§3 — Financial Profile and Management Quality

Revenue and Underlying Income Trajectory

For the fiscal year 2026, the company generated £1,609.2 million in underlying income, representing an 18% reported year-over-year increase and a 19% increase on a constant currency basis. Active customer growth accelerated 21% to reach 18.9 million users, which drove total cross-border volumes up 25% to £181.7 billion. Management targets a medium-term CAGR of 15% to 20% for underlying income.

Jefferies Research models fiscal 2026 underlying income perfectly in line with management's guidance, projecting 18% reported growth to £1,608 million. [Jefferies Research, 1 Apr]

Profitability, Margins, and FCF Conversion

In fiscal 2025, Wise achieved an underlying profit before tax (uPBT) margin of 21%, generating £282.1 million. Management actively targets a lower medium-term uPBT margin range of 13% to 16%. For fiscal 2026, the company guided to the top end of this 13% to 16% range, heavily reinvesting excess margin into infrastructure build-outs, marketing, and further price reductions.

Free cash flow conversion: 108.9% in FY2025 (£615.4 million FCF generated); 96.1% in H1 FY2026.

Deutsche Bank Research notes that the fiscal 2026 margin outlook includes approximately £35 million in one-off costs related to the company's dual-listing project, bringing total expected administrative costs for the year to roughly £1.0 billion. [Deutsche Bank Research, 6 Nov 25]

Rothschild & Co Redburn argues that Wise's artificially suppressed margins mask significant long-term operating leverage, projecting a terminal operating margin of 35%, still well below the broader payments sector average incremental margin of 55%. [Rothschild & Co Redburn, 13 Apr]

Capital Allocation Strategy

Wise maintains a highly disciplined return-led capital allocation framework prioritizing organic growth and balance sheet resilience. The company has never paid a cash dividend and does not intend to initiate one. Primary mechanism for returning capital: strategic share repurchases to offset dilutive stock-based compensation.

In fiscal 2026, the company expanded its Employee Benefit Trust purchase program with an incremental mandate to acquire 25 million shares (~2.5% of issued capital). By end of H1 FY2026, approximately 50% of this specific repurchase program had been completed.

Liquidity position: £1.3 billion in net cash at end of FY2025; investment-grade BBB ratings from both S&P and Fitch.

Macro Headwinds and FX Sensitivities

As a global payments provider, Wise is inherently exposed to foreign exchange fluctuations and broader macroeconomic volatility. Short-term volume spikes driven by FX volatility generate temporary revenue windfalls but normalize over the medium term. Management proactively hedges non-sterling currency exposures.

Internal severe stress tests — simultaneous 5% depreciation of Euro and US Dollar — indicate a potential one-day realized loss of approximately $4.1 million. A strong US Dollar historically creates top-line conversion headwinds in GBP reporting. [Jefferies Research, 24 Mar]

Management Quality and Execution Track Record

Led by co-founder and CEO Kristo Käärmann, the executive team boasts a rare track record of delivering over ten consecutive years of profitability alongside more than 30x cross-border volume growth. Organizational culture strongly emphasizes decentralized decision-making, autonomy, and extreme ownership. Product and engineering pods are granted the authority to actively challenge top-down mandates.

Challenges: Company historically struggled to match aggressive US-based fintech compensation packages, periodically impacting retention of sales and commercial talent. CEO's publicized regulatory issues regarding unpaid personal taxes previously caused temporary reputational damage.

Nevertheless, management's strategic execution is highly regarded, particularly in successfully navigating complex regulatory environments to secure direct central bank integrations and operationalizing robust, scalable compliance frameworks. [Expert Calls, 20 Jan / 22 Jan]
