§1 — Industry Overview + Value Chain Economics

BT Group operates primarily in the UK telecommunications sector, a mature regulated market undergoing structural transformation driven by full-fibre infrastructure investment, mobile convergence, and enterprise digital connectivity demand.

  • Industry size: UK telecom market c.£38-40bn total revenues (2024E, Ofcom + GSMA estimates); BT's ~£20.8bn revenue implies ~50% market share by revenue including wholesale, substantially lower in pure retail [#I]
  • Sub: mobile market c.£14bn; fixed broadband c.£7bn; enterprise/govt IT c.£8bn; wholesale/infrastructure c.£6bn (approximate split)

  • Sub: European telecom sector revenues ~€290bn; UK is mid-sized market, smaller than Germany (~€65bn) and France (~€48bn) but comparable to Italy (~€35bn) (GSMA 2024)

  • Growth trajectory: low-single-digit nominal, structurally bifurcated — legacy voice/copper declining 8-12% pa; fibre broadband, enterprise IT, and 5G growing 4-8% pa; net industry revenue growth c.1-3% CAGR through 2027 [#A]

  • Sub: Openreach wholesale fibre connections growing ~40% YoY (from low base) but monetisation per connection rising slowly as ARPU uplift from fibre-vs-copper is modest (~£2-3/month)

  • Sub: enterprise/government IT services growing mid-single-digit driven by hybrid cloud, security, and SD-WAN demand

  • Structural driver: FTTP infrastructure cycle — mandatory copper retirement (Ofcom switch-off target 2030), enterprise digital transformation, and 5G spectrum economics are the three co-occurring structural forces shaping a 5-10 year transition period [#A]

  • Sub: copper switch-off by 2030 eliminates ~£1-2bn of Openreach legacy cost but also ~£1.5bn of legacy revenue; net effect is margin accretive if migration to fibre proceeds on schedule

  • Sub: enterprise demand for converged connectivity (fixed + mobile + cloud) growing faster than pure connectivity, but BT's position in higher-margin managed services is contested by global SIs (Accenture, Atos spin-offs)

  • Geographic split: overwhelmingly UK domestic — >90% of revenues from UK; small international enterprise footprint (BT Global Services restructured/sold down post-2019); residual international exposure in managed enterprise networks [#I]

  • Sub: international revenues ~£1.5-2bn (declining); BT's strategic exit from global consumer + non-core B2B markets over 2018-2022 has sharpened domestic focus

  • Fragmentation: oligopolistic with fragmentation at the edges — fixed broadband: BT/Openreach ~35% retail share, VMO2 ~24%, Sky ~23%; mobile: EE ~29%, O2 (VMO2) ~24%, Vodafone ~19%, Three ~11% (Ofcom 2024); concentrated at top but alt-nets fragmenting fixed market [#A] ❌

  • Sub: alt-net entrants (CityFibre, Hyperoptic, Gigaclear, Trooli, Nexfibre) now passing >8mn premises, targeting ~20mn over build period; collectively represent structural new-entrant pressure on Openreach's wholesale monopoly

  • Consolidation: mobile consolidating, fixed fragmenting — VMO2+Three merger (approved 2024, conditional on remedies) creates a three-player mobile market; fixed market is simultaneously fragmenting at the infrastructure layer [#A]

  • Sub: Three-VMO2 merger creates a larger rival to EE; network remedy commitments (pricing caps, neutral host access) reduce near-term pricing upside but long-term consolidation is positive for mobile ARPU stability

  • Value chain map: Openreach sits at the highest-margin, highest-barrier stage [#J]

§2 — Competitive Landscape + Industry Structure

  • Verdict: oligopolistic core, but structurally weakening — BT is #1 by revenue and network scale in UK fixed; mobile is a four-player (soon three-player) market; industry structure favours incumbents at infrastructure layer but competition is intensifying in retail and enterprise [#J]

  • Competitor tier: fixed broadband [#A]

  • Sub: Tier 1 (national scale): BT/Openreach (~35% broadband retail share, 100% fixed physical infrastructure coverage), VMO2 (~24% share, cable + FTTP hybrid), Sky (~23% share, reseller on Openreach + FTTP upgrade)
  • Sub: Tier 2 (alt-net challengers): CityFibre (>4mn premises passed, targeting 8mn), Hyperoptic (dense urban), Nexfibre (VMO2-backed), Gigaclear (rural/semi-rural)

  • Sub: Tier 3 (niche/regional): Grain, Trooli, Airband, KCOM (Hull only); individually sub-scale

  • Competitor tier: mobile [#A]

  • Sub: EE ~29% subscriber share; O2 (VMO2) ~24%; Vodafone ~19%; Three ~11% pre-merger (Ofcom 2024 data); post-VMO2+Three merger, market becomes EE / VMO2+Three / Vodafone

  • Sub: EE consistently ranked #1 network quality (RootMetrics, OpenSignal); premium network position supports slightly above-market ARPU but gap is narrowing as O2/Vodafone invest in 5G rollout

  • Intensity: HIGH and increasing at retail, MEDIUM at wholesale — broadband retail churn elevated (~14-16% pa industry average); price comparison aggregators (MoneySuperMarket, uSwitch) drive commoditisation; wholesale access pricing constrained by Ofcom regulation [#A] ❌

  • Sub: alt-net build is the single most material competitive intensity driver; 8mn premises passed today, 20mn+ targeted by 2028; once alt-net FTTP penetrates >15-20% of premises in a given geography, Openreach's local pricing power weakens structurally

  • Industry rank: BT is clearly #1 by revenues (c.£20.8bn vs VMO2 ~£12bn, Vodafone UK ~£6bn, Sky ~£5bn consumer telecom revenues); however, revenue rank overstates competitive advantage — Openreach's infrastructure #1 position is durable, BT Consumer's retail #1 is contested [#A]

  • Top 5 share: concentrated at ~85% — top 5 operators (BT/EE, VMO2, Sky, Vodafone, Three) account for ~85% of retail broadband + mobile revenues; Ofcom 2024 [#I]

  • Sub: steep distribution curve — BT alone accounts for ~35% of fixed broadband subs; #2 (VMO2) c.24%; #6-10 collectively <8% of subs; curve is steep but alt-net fragmentation is flattening it at the tail

  • Concentrated existing structure (TC4.RA14): market qualifies as top-5 >30% concentrated; BT is #1; distribution curve is steep at present but structurally vulnerable to alt-net share gain at the margins over 2025-2030 [#J]

  • Share stability (TC4.RA15): BT Consumer broadband retail share has declined from ~38% (2019) to ~35% (2024) — slow but consistent erosion as Sky + alt-net FTTP take share; EE mobile share stable at ~29%; Openreach wholesale share remains ~95%+ of total fixed access lines (alt-net PIA partial offset) [#A]

  • Sub: share consolidation does NOT flow to BT at retail layer; at infrastructure layer (Openreach), share is structurally stable because alternatives require massive capital commitment

  • Sub: new entrant share in fixed broadband growing ~1-1.5ppt pa from alt-net build; at current pace, alt-nets reach ~12-15% of total UK broadband subs by 2028

  • Concentrating gravitational pull (TC4.RA15): Moderate — infrastructure yes, retail no — Openreach's wholesale share is gravitationally sticky (high capital barrier for replication); retail share gravitates toward price/broadband speed comparators, not BT's brand; scale advantage does not drive customer re-consolidation in retail [#J]

  • Competitor attitudes (TC4.RA16): mixed benign-ness [#J]

  • Sub: Openreach wholesale pricing is regulatory-constrained — not a pricing shark market; operators negotiate charge controls with Ofcom, reducing bilateral aggression
  • Sub: BT Consumer vs Sky/VMO2 is price-competitive with periodic promotional wars; Sky historically price-rational (margin-focused); VMO2 more volume-focused since Liberty Global ownership
  • Sub: alt-nets (CityFibre especially) are pricing aggressively on ARPU (£20-25/month vs Openreach wholesale ~£13-14/month) to take share during build phase; this is funded by infrastructure investment not operating cash, creating temporary MOL pressure

  • Sub: mobile market has historically exhibited benign pricing dynamics (5G investment phase); Three+VMO2 merger remedies include pricing commitments, reducing post-merger shark attack risk near-term

  • MOL stability: EBITDA margins broadly stable at group level (~34-36% EBITDA margin FY22-24); Openreach margins expanding; consumer/business margins under modest pressure [#A]

  • Shark attack risk: MEDIUM — most acute risk is a well-capitalised alt-net (CityFibre, backed by Mubadala, Goldman, QIA) pricing for share in dense urban markets where Openreach FTTP coverage overlaps; full national-scale shark attack requires more capital than alt-nets currently have but cannot be dismissed post-2027 [#J]

  • Volume vs ROCE (TC4.RA16.CQ64): Openreach prioritises ROCE on regulated wholesale; BT Consumer has historically prioritised volume/share; shift underway post-Kirkby to deprioritise low-margin volume — June 2024 CMD signalled exit from unprofitable B2B contracts [#J]

  • Competitiveness trends (TC4.RA17): Net negative at retail; neutral to modestly positive at infrastructure — alt-net build and mobile data commoditisation are structural headwinds; FTTP completion + copper switch-off will reduce competitive surface area at infrastructure layer post-2028 [#J] ❌

  • Sceptical bullet: Bearish read on competitive landscape — the concentrated structure at infrastructure layer is real but it is a regulatory artefact (Ofcom-mandated open access); any regulatory shift toward structural separation (Openreach as separate listed entity) or further PIA loosening could erode Openreach's structural rent; at retail, the benign competitor attitude reading rests on Sky's historically rational pricing — Sky's ownership transition (NBCU/Comcast) or a more aggressive alt-net pricing posture post-build-phase could destabilise current MOL stability [#J]

§3 — Barriers to Entry + Exit + Disruption Forces

  • Verdict: high barriers at infrastructure layer, LOW barriers at retail and enterprise — the asymmetry between Openreach's structural protection and the competitive vulnerability of BT Consumer/Business is the defining tension in the BB#2 assessment [#J]

  • Capital requirement: Enormous at infrastructure, moderate at retail — building a competing national FTTP network requires £10-15bn+ capex over 5-7 years (Openreach's own build is c.£15bn total by completion); alt-nets with sub-national scope spend £500mn-£3bn [#A]

  • Sub: Openreach's duct + pole network (replacement cost estimated £50-80bn) creates an effectively irreproducible infrastructure barrier for national-scale entrants

  • Sub: retail ISP entry requires no infrastructure if accessing Openreach wholesale; barrier is commercial (margin squeeze from low wholesale/retail spread) not capital

  • Scale economy: Strong at infrastructure (network density, NOC/engineering efficiency), weak at retail — Openreach's engineering workforce (~35,000 field engineers) creates a learning-curve and geographic density advantage that an alt-net at 8mn premises cannot replicate [#A]

  • Sub: NOC (Network Operations Centre) and fault management at national scale requires c.£500mn+ pa of fixed cost; BT's scale spreads this over ~10mn Openreach active connections vs alt-net peers at <1mn connections

  • Brand: MEDIUM — EE brand strong in mobile quality; BT Consumer brand declining in NPS vs Sky, VMO2 — brand is a modest barrier in mobile (EE quality premium) but not a meaningful barrier in fixed broadband where price + speed dominate switching decisions [#A]

  • Switching cost: LOW in consumer fixed/mobile; MEDIUM-HIGH in enterprise IT — consumer switching is 1-2 day process; enterprise managed network contracts (3-5 year terms) create meaningful switching costs; government contracts (PSTN, public sector networks) have quasi-regulatory stickiness [#A]

  • Sub: BT's government/defence contracts (Atlas, MOD, HMRC) create multi-year entrenched relationships worth c.£1.5-2bn pa; switching costs here are contractual + security-clearance based, not purely economic

  • Regulatory barrier: OPENREACH IS A REGULATED MONOPOLY — Ofcom SMP (Significant Market Power) determinations create a regulated moat — but same regulation is the primary source of contestability risk (Ofcom can tighten access terms) [#A]

  • Sub: WFTMR 2021-2026 (Wholesale Fixed Telecoms Market Review) governs Openreach pricing for current regulatory cycle; next review 2026 is a major risk event

  • Sub: SMP determinations require Openreach to provide non-discriminatory access, eliminating most foreclosure strategies; moat is "regulated open access" not "genuine competitive exclusion"

  • Technology barrier: MEDIUM — spectrum management + FTTP engineering expertise creates barriers to new entrants [#A]

  • Sub: 5G spectrum licences (auction-allocated) create a hard entry barrier for new mobile operators; UK spectrum already fully allocated across EE, O2, Vodafone, Three

  • Sub: FTTP engineering talent supply is a short-term constraint on alt-net build pace; Openreach's workforce + training pipeline is a temporary competitive advantage

  • Lock-in mechanism: Government + enterprise contracts (multi-year), spectrum (20-year licences), duct + pole infrastructure (physical irreversibility), legacy system dependencies in enterprise IT [#A]

  • Disruption force: alt-net FTTP overbuild — most material near-term disruption force; CityFibre, Nexfibre collectively targeting 20mn+ premises; if they achieve 60-70% penetration in covered areas, Openreach's wholesale volume projections become unachievable [#A]

  • Sub: alt-net financial sustainability is the key uncertainty; many alt-nets are pre-FCF positive and reliant on infrastructure fund capital; a funding gap event (rising interest rates squeezing infrastructure fund IRR hurdles) could trigger alt-net consolidation or retreat, which would be benign for Openreach

  • Disruption force: mobile broadband substitution — 5G FWA (Fixed Wireless Access) is a credible partial substitute for fixed broadband in lower-density markets [#A]

  • Sub: Three UK most aggressive in FWA; post-VMO2 merger, combined entity has both mobile and fixed infrastructure to credibly offer FWA + cable convergence; threat to Openreach wholesale volume in semi-rural geographies

  • Disruption force: over-the-top + cloud bypass — enterprise SD-WAN and cloud connectivity (AWS Direct Connect, Azure ExpressRoute) partially bypass BT's managed network layer; MPLS revenue declining c.5-8% pa [#A] ❌

  • Sub: BT has pivoted toward SD-WAN resale and cloud connectivity management, but margin on these products is structurally lower than legacy MPLS; transition represents revenue mix deterioration, not pure substitution

  • Adjacency entry: Global SIs (Accenture, Capgemini, Computacenter) encroaching into enterprise IT/managed network space from application/consulting layer; cloud hyperscalers (AWS, Azure, Google) competing for enterprise connectivity spend directly [#A]

  • Sub: Microsoft (Teams Phone) has displaced significant BT enterprise voice revenues; VoIP substitution of ISDN/PSTN is complete; remaining enterprise voice at risk from MS Teams + Webex

  • Substitutes: Consumer: mobile broadband (partial, especially rural); Enterprise: cloud-native networking (SD-WAN, SASE) replacing MPLS; Voice: OTT (WhatsApp, Teams) replacing PSTN [#A]

  • Innovative entrant: Starlink and LEO satellite broadband are a nascent substitute for rural FTTP; limited threat to urban Openreach economics but relevant for BT's rural Universal Service Obligation commitments and government contract performance [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: Inversion case on barriers + disruption — the capital barrier argument for Openreach assumes that the regulatory bottleneck remains intact and that alt-nets remain financially constrained; if Ofcom accelerates structural separation of Openreach (forcing legal separation into a separately listed entity as a remedy), the moat changes character from "BT-owned monopoly" to "independently regulated utility" — Ofcom has debated this in every review cycle; the 2026 WFTMR renewal is the trigger event to watch; simultaneously, the global SIs + cloud hyperscaler disruption of BT's enterprise IT layer is already well underway and further to run [#J]

§5 — Advantaged Business + SRCA Depth

MOAT (RA4) — Full ESA Depth

  • Sub: Openreach's "customer" is ISPs accessing wholesale — these are multi-year regulated access contracts; switching cost for alt-net wholesale ISPs from Openreach to alt-net infrastructure is high (requires new exchange infrastructure), creating durable wholesale volume floor

§7 — Sceptical Synthesis (Cross-Cutting)

  • Verdict: BB#2 Foundation Quality is structurally bifurcated — Openreach carries most of the BB#2 strength; BT Consumer and Business are structural drags that repeatedly impair group-level financial outputs; the investment case reduces to "can Openreach's infrastructure quality + cost savings trajectory overcome the group's financial leverage and below-median return profile?" [#J]

  • Reverse-engineered bear (TC fragility): TC2 (Advantaged business + SRCA) is the most fragile TC at group level — Openreach's MOAT qualifies but GENERATE comprehensively fails (RONIC 8-12% vs 25%+ benchmark; FCF negative; ROCE 5-7% vs 15%+ benchmark); TC1 (Operator) is transitional and unproven; the two strongest TCs (TC3 and TC4) are structurally real but don't overcome TC2's financial output weakness [#J]

  • Single largest BB#2 risk: CQ29 — post-tax RONIC 25-30%+ is comprehensively not met and structurally cannot be met within a regulated utility model — this single CQ failure is a signal that BT Group is a financial restructuring + capex-cycle story, not a compounding quality business [#J]

  • Industry vs company: The majority of Openreach's BB#2 strength is INDUSTRY-STRUCTURAL (regulated monopoly, capital barriers) — not company-specific advantage — if the regulatory moat weakens (Ofcom), the company-specific advantage layer (engineering culture, IT systems, brand) is thin; company-specific factors would likely grade materially lower if the regulatory framework shifted [#J]

  • Early warning signals: (1) Openreach FTTP penetration below 32% by end-FY26 (volume shortfall signals monetisation delay); (2) Ofcom CPI+ mechanism removal (pricing power event); (3) CWU industrial action at scale (execution risk materialising); (4) Alt-net funding failures (bifurcated — could be bullish for Openreach if competitive threat recedes or bearish if it signals demand disappointment); (5) BT guidance cut on EBITDA FY26 (recurring behaviour pattern) [#A]

  • Through-cycle test: In a recession scenario: BT Consumer broadband is relatively resilient (utility-like); BT Business is more vulnerable (enterprise IT discretionary spend); Openreach wholesale volumes are very resilient (ISPs cannot switch infrastructure quickly); pension deficit funding increases if gilt yields fall; leverage becomes more constraining [#A]

  • Sub: BT's through-cycle performance is mixed: resilient volumes but financially constrained by leverage + pension in downturns; the 2020-2022 COVID period demonstrated BT's operational resilience but financial fragility

  • BB#2 TC by TC fragility ranking: TC2 (SRCA/Generate) most fragile; TC1 (Operator — transitional) second most fragile; TC5 (LT growth — low TAM headroom) third; TC3 (Industry value chain) and TC4 (Competitive structure) are the strongest TCs [#J]

  • Operator failure mechanism: The most plausible operator failure mode is NOT strategic misdirection (Kirkby's strategy is correct) but EXECUTION PACE FAILURE — headcount reduction slower than planned (CWU delays), legacy IT decommissioning costs higher than guided, copper switch-off delayed by Ofcom or political intervention (rural constituency pressure) [#A]

  • Moat erosion mechanism: Openreach's regulatory moat erodes through Ofcom structural separation enforcement (probability: LOW, but non-trivial in 2026 WFTMR) or through alt-net achieving >25% national premise coverage with sustainable economics (probability: MEDIUM over 5-7 years) [#A]

  • Paradigm misfit risk: If AI infrastructure re-rating drives investors to prefer pure-play data centre / fibre operators (Cellnex, Digital Bridge, IHS Towers) over integrated telcos, BT Group may suffer relative multiple compression even as Openreach improves fundamentally — a structural de-rating of the vehicle while the asset improves [#J]

  • Breaking points: (1) Openreach FTTP penetration <28% by FY27 (monetisation miss); (2) Net debt rising above 4× EBITDA (credit event risk, dividend re-cut); (3) Ofcom regulatory crackdown on CPI+ + charge controls simultaneously (double pricing headwind); (4) Kirkby departure (governance re-disruption) [#A]

  • Peer-relative downside: In a scenario where KPN is the structural endpoint, BT has ~40% upside from multiple + margin expansion; in a scenario where Proximus (lower-quality FTTP outcome, higher leverage persistence) is the endpoint, BT's current valuation already prices in most of the upside [#J]

Confidence in BB#2 strength: MEDIUM — Openreach's infrastructure quality and cost savings pathway are real and mechanically grounded; group-level financial outputs fail multiple BB#2 GENERATE thresholds; the investment case is a value recovery + financial restructuring story with an infrastructure quality core, not a BB#2-strength compounder [#J]
