§2 — Key Driver #1: CNMC Regulatory Rate-Base Reset (2026-2031)

J-front verdict: Highest-visibility, most mechanically predictable driver; CNMC's 2026-2031 gas and electricity network tariff review is the anchor for 40–50% of NTGY regulated EBIT [#J]

Driver definition: Spain's CNMC sets allowed revenues for gas distribution and electricity networks on 6-year regulatory cycles; the 2026-2031 cycle covers Naturgy's Spanish gas distribution network (one of the largest in Spain) [#A]

Mechanism to EPS: Higher allowed returns or rate-base growth → higher regulated EBIT → EPS uplift; even a 50bps improvement in allowed WACC vs prior cycle = meaningful multi-year EPS compounding [#I]

Mechanism to SP: Regulated utilities re-rate when investors gain confidence in the earnings floor; a favourable CNMC outcome removes the key regulated-earnings uncertainty premium [#J]

Company guide: Naturgy guided for continued rate-base investment in its 2023-2025 Strategic Plan; management has flagged the 2026 regulatory review as a key milestone without providing explicit uplift quantification in public presentations [#A]

Management commentary: CEO Francisco Reynés has consistently characterised the Spanish regulatory framework as "constructive and predictable"; company has invested to grow rate-base ahead of the review cycle [#A]

Track record: Naturgy navigated the prior 2020-2025 CNMC gas distribution cycle with broadly in-line allowed revenue outcomes; no material shortfall vs regulatory expectations on record [#A]

Conservatism vs optimism: Management guidance is directionally constructive but avoids specific WACC or rate-base quantum; the absence of explicit guidance limits sandbagging assessment [#I]

SS consensus: Sell-side broadly models ~2-3% regulated asset base CAGR through 2027; allowed return assumptions cluster around 5.5-6.5% real WACC for gas networks, consistent with CNMC precedent [#A]

Estimated EPS impact: A 2% RAB CAGR plus stable allowed returns = approximately +€0.12–0.18 EPS by FY27 vs FY24 base, roughly 7-10% of consensus EPS [#A]

Recent delivery: NTGY's regulated network EBIT has been stable to growing in L3 reporting periods; no material regulatory clawback events; H1 2025 regulated revenues in line with prior year [#A]

L3Y baseline: Gas distribution EBIT broadly flat 2022-2024 in nominal terms as tariff inflation offsets were matched by higher capex amortisation; real growth slightly negative [#A]

Peer benchmark: No cohort peer data available (D-BATCH-25 gap); Spanish utility peers (Endesa, Red Eléctrica/REE) have historically received CNMC allowed returns in 5-7% range — NTGY's gas distribution sits at the lower end of allowed return ranges vs electricity networks [#A]

Macro tailwind: Spanish energy infrastructure investment is structurally supported by EU taxonomy capital flows and decarbonisation capex requirements; the CNMC is politically incentivised to maintain investment attractiveness [#J]

Macro headwind: Spanish government has shown interventionist instincts in energy (windfall taxes 2023-2025); any extension of extraordinary levies to network operators would directly cut allowed-revenue flow-through [#J]

Visibility: High for rate-base quantum (capex plans are public); medium for allowed WACC (CNMC has not yet published 2026-2031 consultation) [#I]

Plausibility — H: Rate-base growth is contracted-style; regulatory precedent strongly supports continuity; Spain has never cut allowed returns mid-cycle for gas distribution [#J]

Bear case: CNMC compresses allowed WACC by 75-100bps vs prior cycle (consistent with falling eurozone risk-free rate) without offsetting RAB growth acceleration, netting to flat or negative real EPS growth from this segment [#J]

Miss scenario: CNMC review delayed to H2 2026 (regulatory consultation backlog) + allowed WACC reduction of 80bps = ~€0.08 EPS headwind vs base case, roughly 5% of group EPS [#A]

Miss magnitude: Moderate — regulatory miss would not be catastrophic but removes the most reliable bull case pillar [#J]

Per-driver sceptical bullet: CNMC is often characterised as "predictable" but the 2023-2025 period included extraordinary windfall tax impositions on utilities not anticipated in regulatory models; the next CNMC cycle could embed a social-cost mechanism that structurally reduces allowed returns

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — Spanish gas network regulatory precedent is stable; key assumption is CNMC maintains allowed WACC broadly consistent with prior cycle [#J]

§3 — Key Driver #2: LatAm Portfolio Restructuring (Argentina, Chile, Panama)

J-front verdict: Largest asymmetry in the case — LatAm carries both the highest upside (asset monetisation at premium) and highest downside (impairment + political disruption); medium plausibility on net [#J]

Driver definition: Naturgy holds distribution and generation assets in Argentina (Metrogas, Edesur stake), Chile (gas distribution, Chilquinta stake), Panama (gas distribution, electricity), and Colombia; these contribute ~20-25% of group EBIT [#A]

Mechanism to EPS: Asset disposals at book/premium → gain + capital return; political/FX deterioration → impairment + EBIT reduction; regulatory tariff improvements (Argentina pesification/tariff reset) → earnings normalisation [#I]

Company guide: Naturgy management has repeatedly described the LatAm portfolio as "under strategic review"; no explicit disposal timeline or valuation floor given publicly [#A]

Management commentary: CEO Reynés described LatAm as "value-generating assets in complex environments" in the most recent CMD; the language suggests retention preference but acknowledgement of optionality [#A]

Track record: Naturgy took a significant LatAm impairment in 2022 (Argentina-related); the prior 2020 strategic plan's LatAm divestment program was only partially executed; execution credibility on LatAm exits is limited [#J]

Conservatism vs optimism: Management language on LatAm skews optimistic relative to observed delivery; partial exits have been completed but full-portfolio strategic clarity remains absent [#A]

SS consensus: High divergence — some analysts value LatAm at book (~€2.5-3bn enterprise contribution), others apply a 20-30% political risk discount; no consensus on disposal timing [#A]

Estimated EPS impact (upside): Clean exit of LatAm at book = potential capital return of €1-1.5bn → buyback of 3-4% of shares = ~+€0.05-0.08 EPS accretion plus rerating from de-risking [#A]

Estimated EPS impact (downside): Argentina tariff freeze continuation + peso devaluation = further impairment risk; in extreme scenario ~€400-600m write-down = 15-20% EPS dilution in impairment year [#A]

Recent delivery: Argentina has had partial tariff recovery under Milei liberalisation (2024-2025); Metrogas tariff review yielded partial relief but not full normalisation; net LatAm earnings remain suppressed vs 2019 peak [#A]

L3Y baseline: LatAm EBIT contribution has been volatile: 2022 negative (impairments), 2023 recovering, 2024-2025 modestly positive; no compound growth trend visible [#A]

Peer benchmark: No cohort peers available; LatAm utility operators (Enel Américas, Sempra comparator) have historically traded at 20-35% conglomerate discount to pure-play peers — consistent with the drag NTGY LatAm places on group multiple [#A]

Macro tailwind: Argentina's Milei reform programme has accelerated energy tariff liberalisation; if sustained, Metrogas earnings recovery could add €0.05-0.08 EPS by FY27 [#J]

Macro headwind: Argentina FX volatility (blue-chip spread, parallel rate risk) means any tariff nominal gains are partially eroded in EUR translation; Chile and Panama face lower but non-zero political risk [#J]

Visibility: Low — LatAm outcome depends on host-country politics, currency regimes, and bilateral regulatory decisions largely outside NTGY management control [#I]

Bear case: Argentina reform stalls, peso depreciates 40% vs EUR, no tariff relief; Chile disposal falls through due to buyer financing constraints; Panama regulatory review adverse; combined EBIT hit of €200-300m vs consensus [#J]

Miss scenario: No disposals completed in 24M, LatAm EBIT flat/declining, markets reprice LatAm stake at 35% political discount — multiple compression of 0.5-1.0x EV/EBITDA on group level [#A]

Miss magnitude: High — if LatAm deteriorates, it simultaneously hits EPS, balance sheet, and sentiment; the correlation with gas price (see Driver #4) amplifies downside [#J]

⚡ RARE: Argentina optionality — Milei's energy liberalisation is one of the fastest utility deregulation cycles in EM history; if Metrogas achieves full tariff normalisation, NTGY could realise a disposal at 2-3× the current market-implied carrying value [Cross-ref: §3, §10]

Per-driver sceptical bullet: The LatAm "under review" language has appeared in every Naturgy CMD since 2018 without resolution; each year of inaction compounds the conglomerate discount; management's track record on executing LatAm strategy is a legitimate credibility concern

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — asymmetric driver dependent on Argentina macro reform sustainability and management execution on disposal; key assumption is Milei programme holds for 12+ months [#J]

§4 — Key Driver #3: Shareholder Structure Resolution

J-front verdict: The wildcard driver — a takeover or delisting at premium would be the single largest share price catalyst, but probability is low-medium and timing is entirely unpredictable [#J]

Driver definition: Naturgy's register is dominated by three strategic shareholders: CriteriaCaixa (~27%), IFM Investors (~20%), TAQA/Mubadala (~22%); the float is thin and the company has been the subject of partial takeover attempts (IFM's 2021 OPA at €23/share) [#A]

Mechanism to EPS: A full takeover at premium to market → immediate rerating; a partial delisting or compulsory purchase → rerating + exit crystallisation; a shareholder dispute → sentiment overhang worsens [#I]

Mechanism to SP: Corporate action premium is the most direct route to 25%+ IRR given the regulated utility earnings trajectory alone struggles to deliver it on a 24M horizon [#J]

Company guide: No formal guidance on ownership changes; company has consistently maintained independence as the default positioning [#A]

Management commentary: Management has discouraged speculation about corporate action; CEO framing has emphasised strategic plan execution over ownership structure [#A]

Track record: IFM's 2021 OPA (€23/share) was partially successful but did not result in delisting; TAQA/Mubadala entry in 2022-2023 was framed as strategic long-term investment, not a precursor to buyout [#A]

Conservatism vs optimism: Management's "business as usual" framing is not credible given the known strategic interests of both IFM and TAQA; the driver is real, management just won't confirm it [#J]

SS consensus: Spanish sell-side largely assign a 15-20% corporate action premium to target prices; international banks less willing to embed takeover probability explicitly; no consensus on timing [#A]

Estimated EPS impact: A full takeover at 20-25% premium to current price = IRR target achieved in single event; no incremental EPS — this is a pure rerating event [#A]

Recent delivery: No corporate action progress visible in L12M; TAQA/Mubadala has not increased its stake; IFM has not launched new OPA; the overhang has been a neutral-to-negative factor in L12M [#J]

L3Y baseline: IFM OPA (Feb 2021) → partial acceptance → TAQA entry (2022-2023) → strategic stalemate (2024-2025); corporate action probability has declined from 2021 peak [#A]

Peer benchmark: No cohort peers available; European utility takeouts (EDP, Centrica partial, Suez) suggest full-premium bids arrive at 25-35% premium to undisturbed price when strategic buyers are motivated [#A]

Macro tailwind: Gulf sovereign capital (TAQA, Mubadala) has demonstrated appetite for European energy infrastructure assets; regulatory softening on foreign ownership in Spain post-2023 reduces political blockage risk [#J]

Macro headwind: Spanish government retains golden share / national interest veto on utility control changes; any TAQA-led bid faces CNMC and Ministry of Energy screening; political resistance from a left-leaning government is a realistic blocker [#J]

Visibility: Very low — corporate action is binary and event-driven; no public signals confirm imminent transaction [#I]

Bear case: Shareholder stalemate persists indefinitely; float remains thin; minority investors are trapped in a quasi-controlled company with no catalytic mechanism; re-rating requires operational delivery alone [#J]

Miss scenario: TAQA/Mubadala sells stake (profit-taking) → market interprets as signal that acquisition interest has faded → overhang converts from neutral to negative → 5-8% de-rating [#A]

Miss magnitude: High if overhang persists; the opportunity cost of monitoring a non-event over 24M is the core risk [#J]

Per-driver sceptical bullet: The concentration of strategic shareholders does not automatically create a takeover catalyst; it may instead create a governance vacuum where no single shareholder has incentive to pay full value, resulting in indefinite discount to intrinsic value

Plausibility verdict: Low-medium plausibility — corporate action would deliver the IRR target in isolation, but requires Gulf sovereign strategic decision that is opaque; key assumption is TAQA/Mubadala views Spain as long-term strategic anchor [#J]

§5 — Key Driver #4: Gas Supply Margin Normalisation

J-front verdict: A recovery driver with real substance but gas price volatility makes this unpredictable; consensus models normalisation by 2026 but European LNG dynamics remain structurally elevated vs pre-2021 [#J]

Driver definition: Naturgy operates one of Spain's largest gas supply businesses, including LNG terminal capacity (Reganosa, GATE) and long-term gas supply contracts; the supply margin is the spread between wholesale gas procurement and retail/industrial customer prices [#A]

Mechanism to EPS: Compression in gas supply margins (as happened 2022-2023 on high spot prices) → EBIT headwind; normalisation of wholesale-to-retail spread → EBIT recovery; LNG optimisation gains from contractual flexibility → incremental upside [#I]

Company guide: Naturgy has guided for improved supply margins in FY25-26 as legacy high-cost supply contracts roll off and new hub-linked contracts replace them; management targets "normalised supply EBIT" without quantifying a specific margin floor [#A]

Management commentary: Management has highlighted the "optionality value" of its LNG portfolio in multiple presentations, framing it as a competitive advantage in a structurally tighter European gas market [#A]

Track record: Supply EBIT was significantly below management expectations in FY22-23 during the gas price spike; FY24 showed recovery but not full normalisation; management's track record of forecasting supply margins is mixed [#A]

SS consensus: Sell-side models suggest supply EBIT recovery of €200-300m above FY23 trough by FY26; this is a significant assumption embedded in consensus EPS of ~€1.75-1.80 for FY26 [#A]

Estimated EPS impact: Full supply margin normalisation = +€0.10-0.15 EPS vs FY23 trough; partial recovery (already visible in FY24) already partially priced [#A]

Recent delivery: FY24 supply margins improved vs FY23; H1 2025 supply business showing continued recovery; trend is positive but pace is below consensus recovery path [#A]

L3Y baseline: Supply EBIT: FY22 depressed → FY23 trough → FY24 recovery begun → FY25 partial normalisation; L3Y trend is recovery, not yet normalised [#A]

Peer benchmark: No cohort peers available; European gas supply operators (Engie, E.ON) have noted similar post-2022 margin recovery patterns; full normalisation typically takes 4-6 years post-price-shock as rolling contract structures reprice [#A]

Macro tailwind: European gas demand recovery post-winter 2024-2025 and structural LNG import dependency support elevated but stabilising TTF pricing; Naturgy's contractual flexibility allows opportunistic LNG cargo optimisation [#J]

Macro headwind: Russian gas alternative supply has not fully resolved; TTF prices remain 2-3× pre-2020 levels; any winter demand spike or LNG supply disruption compresses retail margins if pass-through lags [#J]

Visibility: Medium — supply margin recovery is trending in the right direction but depends on TTF forward curve, which is itself a macro variable [#I]

Bear case: TTF spikes again in winter 2025-2026 (cold weather + LNG supply disruption); Spanish gas retail price controls prevent immediate pass-through; supply EBIT re-compresses to 2023 levels = €0.10-0.12 EPS reversal [#J]

Miss scenario: Gas supply EBIT recovery of only €100-150m vs consensus €200-300m by FY26 = consensus EPS miss of €0.06-0.08, roughly 4-5% [#A]

Miss magnitude: Moderate — painful but not thesis-destroying; supply is one of four EBIT pillars [#J]

Per-driver sceptical bullet: European gas supply businesses are not "normalising to the old normal" — the structural shift post-Nordstream means LNG contract economics and hub pricing dynamics have permanently changed; Naturgy's pre-2021 supply EBIT template may not be the correct recovery benchmark

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — directional recovery is underway; full consensus recovery by FY26 requires gas prices to remain below 2022-2023 peaks and retail pass-through to function; key risk is another price spike [#J]

§6 — Key Driver #5: Renewable Capacity Build (Spain + International)

J-front verdict: Real but incremental — Naturgy's renewable build is smaller in scale relative to pure-play renewables peers; it adds EPS but is unlikely to be a re-rating catalyst on its own [#J]

Driver definition: Naturgy's 2023-2025 Strategic Plan included 3-4 GW of new renewable capacity additions (wind, solar) in Spain and internationally; renewables now represent a growing share of generation EBIT [#A]

Mechanism to EPS: Commissioned renewable capacity → contracted EBIT under Spain's RECORE regime (regulated returns) or PPAs → incremental EPS; scale matters — each GW at €50-70/MWh capture → ~€150-200m revenue [#I]

Company guide: Naturgy guided 3-4 GW additions over 2023-2025 strategic plan; progress has been below plan with supply chain delays, permitting bottlenecks, and grid connection queues [#A]

Management commentary: Management acknowledged permitting delays in H2 2024 earnings call; revised timeline for full plan delivery extended by 12-18 months without quantifying revised capacity addition target [#A]

Track record: Renewable build has lagged plan in every year since 2021; permitting in Spain has been a sector-wide constraint, not unique to Naturgy, but the company has been consistently over-optimistic on delivery pace [#J]

Conservatism vs optimism: Guidance has proven optimistic; each CMD resets the timeline without reducing the total ambition [#A]

SS consensus: Sell-side models 2-3 GW of net additions by FY27 (downward revised from company's 3-4 GW); EPS contribution from new renewables estimated at +€0.05-0.10 by FY27 [#A]

Estimated EPS impact: 2 GW additions at 25% equity return → ~+€0.06-0.09 EPS by FY27; smaller than Drivers #1 and #2 in magnitude [#A]

Recent delivery: H1 2025 renewables EBIT broadly in line with prior year; no step-change acceleration visible; pipeline of projects under development remains large but conversion rate to commissioned capacity is slow [#A]

L3Y baseline: Renewables EBIT has grown from near-zero (pre-2020) to a meaningful but minority share (~10-12%) of group EBIT; absolute growth is real but % contribution remains modest [#A]

Peer benchmark: No cohort peers available; European integrated utilities (Enel, Iberdrola, Endesa) typically target 5-8 GW annual additions; Naturgy at 1-1.5 GW/year is a smaller-scale participant [#A]

Macro tailwind: Spain's renewable energy targets (74% by 2030) require massive grid build; RECORE-regulated returns provide earnings floor on new capacity [#J]

Macro headwind: Spanish permitting for new wind/solar has been administratively slow; grid connection queue is 5-7 years for new projects in some regions; supply chain cost inflation (2022-2024) has increased project IRR hurdles [#J]

Visibility: Medium-low — project timelines are within management's control in theory, but permitting and grid connection are exogenous [#I]

Bear case: 1 GW rather than 2-3 GW commissioned by FY27 due to permitting failure; EPS contribution from renewables falls €0.04-0.06 short of consensus [#J]

Miss scenario: Persistent permitting delays → renewable capacity build contributes effectively zero incremental EPS vs base case by FY26 [#A]

Per-driver sceptical bullet: Naturgy's renewable ambition is real but its execution infrastructure (permitting resource, grid connection agreements, EPC contractor relationships) has not been demonstrated to be differentiated vs Spanish peers who face the same constraints

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — structural demand for Spanish renewables is strong; execution risk on permitting and delivery pace is persistent; key assumption is permitting environment improves in FY25-26 [#J]

§7 — Key Driver #6: Cost Efficiency Programme

J-front verdict: Most reliable and least controversial driver — cost efficiency programmes in regulated utilities are historically high-delivery; Naturgy has a credible track record here [#J]

Driver definition: Naturgy has committed to ongoing opex reduction and digitisation savings as part of its Strategic Plan; targets include network digitisation, workforce rationalisation, and procurement efficiency [#A]

Mechanism to EPS: Opex savings → direct EBIT margin expansion → EPS uplift; leverage is modest given regulated revenue floors that partially cap margin upside [#I]

Company guide: Naturgy guided €150-200m cumulative savings over 2023-2025 Strategic Plan; management has reaffirmed this target in each quarterly update [#A]

Management commentary: CFO has highlighted digitisation of gas network as the primary lever; workforce reduction through natural attrition (not announced redundancies) is the dominant cost mechanism [#A]

Track record: Cost efficiency delivery is the one area where Naturgy has consistently met or exceeded guidance; L3Y opex has been flat to declining in real terms despite inflation [#J]

Conservatism vs optimism: Guidance appears conservative here — actual delivery has tracked at the top of the guidance range in each year reviewed [#A]

SS consensus: Consensus models opex flat in nominal terms through FY27; implied ~3-4% real decline per year; consistent with company guidance [#A]

Estimated EPS impact: €150-200m cumulative savings = ~€0.04-0.07 EPS accretion over 3 years, roughly 2-4% of EPS base [#A]

Recent delivery: H1 2025 opex in line with trajectory; no unexpected cost inflation events; digitisation programme milestones being hit per management disclosures [#A]

L3Y baseline: Opex growth CAGR ~0-1% nominal (2022-2024) vs CPI ~3-4%; real cost reduction is real and compounding [#A]

Peer benchmark: No cohort peers available; European regulated utilities typically target 1-3% real opex reduction per year; Naturgy's delivery is consistent with sector norms [#A]

Macro tailwind: Spanish wage inflation has moderated (2025 outlook vs 2023 peak); energy prices (key utility input) have partially normalised [#J]

Macro headwind: Any re-acceleration in Spanish CPI or labour market tightening compresses achievable real opex savings [#J]

Visibility: High — cost programmes are largely within management control; execution risk is low [#I]

Bear case: Inflation re-acceleration in Spain + labour market tightness → opex savings offset → net opex flat in real terms rather than declining; EPS impact from this driver near zero [#J]

Miss scenario: Opex savings of €80-100m rather than €150-200m cumulative = €0.02-0.03 EPS shortfall vs base case [#A]

Miss magnitude: Low — this driver is EPS-supportive but not transformative; a miss here does not change the investment case [#J]

Per-driver sceptical bullet: Cost efficiency in a regulated utility is bounded — the CNMC will partially capture operating efficiency gains in future tariff reviews (efficiency pass-through mechanism), meaning long-term EPS benefit from cost programmes is smaller than the gross saving suggests

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — consistent delivery track record and management control over primary levers; key risk is macro inflation reversal; bounded upside magnitude [#J]

§8 — Key Driver #7: Capital Allocation Discipline (Dividend, Buyback, Leverage)

J-front verdict: A support driver not a growth driver — Naturgy's dividend yield (~4-5% at current prices) provides a floor, but the structural leverage and ownership dynamics limit transformative capital returns [#J]

Driver definition: Naturgy targets 80-100% dividend payout on attributable net income; share buybacks are episodic and modest; net debt/EBITDA maintained at ~3.5-4.0x [#A]

Mechanism to EPS: Buybacks reduce share count → EPS accretion; dividend payout signals earnings confidence; leverage reduction → financial risk premium reduction → re-rating [#I]

Company guide: 2023-2025 Plan targeted €1.20-1.30 DPS with progressive growth; management has maintained the dividend floor but not demonstrated meaningful upside surprise on capital returns [#A]

Management commentary: CFO framing on leverage: "comfortable at current levels with capacity to invest in regulated growth"; no explicit buyback programme announced as of most recent reporting [#A]

Track record: Dividend has been paid consistently; payout ratio maintained near the guided floor; no dividend cut in recent history despite LatAm turbulence [#A]

Conservatism vs optimism: Dividend guidance appears sustainable but not progressive in the sense that EPS growth is only partially flowing through to DPS growth [#A]

SS consensus: Consensus models DPS of €1.25-1.35 for FY26; yield at ~€24-26 share price = 4.8-5.4%; in line with Spanish utility sector average [#A]

Estimated EPS impact: No buyback programme → zero EPS accretion from this driver; dividend yield provides income return but not capital appreciation [#A]

Recent delivery: FY24 dividend paid in line with guidance; no special dividend; no buyback programme launched; capital returns have been the minimum of the guided range [#A]

L3Y baseline: DPS flat to marginally growing 2022-2025; payout ratio stable; no transformative capital return event [#A]

Peer benchmark: No cohort peers available; European utility dividend yields at 4-6% (Endesa, Enel, Iberdrola range); Naturgy's yield is in line with peer median [#A]

Macro tailwind: ECB rate normalisation (rate cuts 2024-2025) supports utility re-rating as bond proxies; lower risk-free rate expands the multiple for a stable yield [#J]

Macro headwind: If ECB cuts less than expected (persistent core inflation), the utility/bond yield spread remains compressed and the sector re-rating thesis weakens [#J]

Visibility: High for dividend (payout ratio is the floor); low for buyback (no programme announced) [#I]

Bear case: LatAm impairment or gas supply EBIT miss → dividend cover falls below 1.2× → progressive dividend guidance cut → yield de-ratings → multiple compression [#J]

Miss scenario: Dividend cut of 10-15% driven by EBITDA miss = yield collapses to 3.5-4%, triggering yield-investor selling = 10-15% share price impact [#A]

Miss magnitude: Moderate — dividend is not the growth driver but is the floor; a cut would be a significant negative sentiment event [#J]

Per-driver sceptical bullet: Naturgy's capital allocation is constrained by its shareholder structure — CriteriaCaixa as a major holder relies on NTGY dividends for its own financial needs, creating a dividend floor that may also cap reinvestment ambition; this is a structural constraint, not optionality

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — dividend floor is credible and well-covered; upside capital returns (buyback, special) require disposal proceeds or earnings acceleration not yet in evidence; key risk is dividend cover erosion from multi-driver miss [#J]
