B.1 Business Description 7 findings
Business Description -- A plain-English description of what the company does, how it makes money, and its primary market.
1.
Leading international secondary protein packer. Hilton designs, constructs, and operates highly automated processing facilities in 19 countries (21 sites), handling 523,379 tonnes in FY25. Revenue £4,214.6m; adjusted PBT £73.2m; EBIT margin 2.3%
2.
Cost-plus open-book pricing model. Over 90% of raw material costs pass directly to retail customers in real-time, deliberately capping margins at 2.3% in exchange for predictable ROCE (20.1% FY25). This is a structural choice, not a cyclical trough
3.
Extreme customer concentration. Five principal customers generate nearly all revenue: Tesco (~85–90% of UK), Woolworths (sole APAC partner), Ahold Delhaize, Coop Danmark, ICA Gruppen. Contracts 5–15 years with physical co-location barriers to switching
4.
Non-core drag defines the near-term problem. SeaChill (UK seafood, marginally loss-making), Foppen (Dutch/Greek smoked salmon, £27.6m exceptional charge FY25, US FDA export ban H1 2026), Dalco (vegan, loss-making). These three sit outside the new regional COO structure under standalone leadership; medium-term targets explicitly exclude their contribution
5.
CEO transition in progress. Mark Allen OBE (ex-Dairy Crest CEO 2007–2019) appointed Executive Chair November 2025 following sudden exit of Steve Murrells; confirmed permanent Group CEO March 31, 2026. Combines Chair and CEO roles (UK Governance Code Provision 9 non-compliance; temporary until NEC appointed)
6.
International expansion as the bull case. Walmart Canada (£80m total capex, early 2027 launch), Poland fresh prepared food (£30m, 2026–27), Saudi JV with NADEC (£6.5m, H2 2026). Total FY26 capex guided ~£100m, double the normalised core range of £45–55m
7.
Balance sheet comfortable despite elevated capex. Net bank debt £126.7m (0.9x EBITDA); £450m RCF refinanced February 2026. Dividend maintained at 35p (6.6% yield at 531p)

Value-Chain Dynamics -- We are looking for a supportive value chain on five dimensions: demand-side strength, competitive supportiveness, low disruption threat, benign political/regulatory backdrop, and supplier bargaining-power advantage.
Judgement summary
Hilton occupies a structurally squeezed midstream position between powerful primary processors and dominant retailer oligopolies. The cost-plus model provides downside protection against raw material cycles but deliberately sacrifices upside. Customer switching costs via physical co-location are genuine and material. The existential risk is Tesco concentration (85–90% UK revenue) and long-term disintermediation pressure from vertically integrated competitors. Graded C: defensible but not advantaged.
1.
E11 RA1: Structural position in the value chain: midstream packer. Hilton sits between primary processors (livestock ownership, slaughter, primary cuts) and retailers (shelf space, promotion, consumer pricing). It performs secondary processing (portioning, packing, marinating, labelling) under open-book cost-plus agreements. This is a deliberate business model: it avoids agricultural commodity risk upstream and branded consumer product risk downstream. The trade-off is permanently thin margins (2.3%) and zero independent pricing power. The business generates returns on capital through volume throughput, operational efficiency, and the processing fee, not through pricing power or product differentiation. This is a structurally weaker value-chain position than CWK (50%+ pig self-sufficiency, own genetics, premium brand exposure) or GNC (scale in convenience with proprietary recipe relationships with retailers). The structural position is understood and accepted as a starting point; the investment thesis does not rely on it improving
2.
E11 RA2: Customer switching costs: physical and digital lock-in are real. Co-located production facilities are embedded directly in retail distribution hubs, requiring joint capital commitments of millions of pounds from both HFG and its retail customers. Moving to an alternative supplier would require the retailer to invest in competing infrastructure, suffer service disruption during transition, and lose the operational data integration provided by HFG's Evolve4 ERP system. Expert call confirms: "Tesco would incur massive operational disruption costs to move to an integrated competitor from HFG's co-located facilities." In Australia, the sole supply arrangement with Woolworths creates a further bilateral dependency. JV structures in Europe (Ahold Delhaize, ICA Gruppen, Coop Danmark) further lock in partners through shared equity and property ownership, creating legal and financial barriers to switching beyond the operational barriers. These switching costs are real and material: they are the primary defensive moat in the business model
3.
E11 RA3: Cost-plus mechanics and their specific limits. The 90%+ raw material pass-through model protects HFG from the inflationary margin compression that a fixed-price manufacturer would experience. In Australia the fee-per-kilogram model provides particularly stable per-unit economics. The model's three specific limits are: first, retailer procurement teams delay pass-through during rapid inflation, creating temporary cash flow pressure (evidenced in FY25 by £30.7m working capital outflow); second, when consumer price elasticity breaks (as in H2 2025 on beef), volume declines directly reduce factory utilisation and overhead absorption, hitting margins even when pass-through is maintained; and third, open-book transparency gives retailers full visibility to claw back margin during deflation periods at contract renewal. Expert confirms: "retailers are highly skilled at avoiding real-time price pass-throughs." The model protects in normal operating conditions but fails at the extremes: deep inflation or consumer demand collapse
4.
E11 RA4: Tesco concentration: the existential risk. Tesco represents 85–90% of UK revenues based on expert network interviews (not explicitly disclosed in public filings). The UK and Ireland segment generated £1,344m of group revenue in FY25 (32% of total). At 90% Tesco concentration within the UK segment, Tesco represents approximately 28–29% of total group revenue. Expert call confirms: Tesco routinely shifts 5–7% of category volumes as pricing leverage during contract renewals. A 7% volume shift from Tesco to ABP or another integrated competitor would represent approximately £94m of revenue at risk. At 2.3% EBIT margin, this translates to approximately £2.2m of EBIT impact from a 7% volume shift: manageable. But a 20% volume shift (if Tesco genuinely diversified its supply base toward integrated processors) would represent £13.4m EBIT impact: equivalent to 20% of FY25 adjusted EBIT. This concentration risk is real and must be tracked. The mitigant is the switching cost (physical co-location) and the long-term contract protections. HFG also recently extended its critical Tesco partnership, which is a positive near-term signal
5.
E11 RA5: Disintermediation threat from vertical integration. Edeka (Germany's largest food retailer) owns complete processing networks and bypasses independent packers entirely. Post-pandemic supply disruptions accelerated retailer interest in suppliers with captive primary raw material supplies. ABP and Kepak (Irish integrated processors) are gaining UK market share. The medium-term structural risk is that UK supermarkets will increasingly prefer suppliers who can demonstrate raw material security: which HFG's pure secondary packing model cannot offer. CWK's 50%+ pig self-sufficiency and own-feed mill network is specifically designed to address this retailer preference. The disintermediation risk is real but the co-location moat provides a meaningful delay (years, not months). The risk is classified as a long-term structural concern rather than a near-term operational threat
6.
E11 RA6: Capital allocation and ROCE discipline. Return on average capital employed was 20.1% in FY25, modestly exceeding CWK's consistent 18.5% ROCE. The new capital framework mandates post-tax unlevered returns exceeding 20% on new investments. FY26 will see ROCE dilution below the 20% target as £100m of pre-productive capex (Canada, Poland) earns zero return during construction. This is understood and accepted at the current stage; the thesis requires the Canada facility to earn above the 20% hurdle from FY27 onwards to justify the capital deployment. If Canada earns at 20% ROCE on £80m deployed, the incremental EBIT contribution is approximately £16m (pre-tax) once the facility is fully operational: a material uplift from the current group EBIT base of approximately £95–99m in FY25
7.
E11 RA7: APAC value-chain position: Woolworths sole-supply model. In Australia, HFG supplies Woolworths as a sole-source partner from its purpose-built Brisbane and Melbourne facilities. This is an even more concentrated customer relationship than the Tesco UK arrangement: 100% of APAC revenue through a single customer. The APAC operating model is structurally distinct: the fee-per-kilogram pricing in Australia provides particularly stable per-unit economics without the open-book retailer transparency of the UK model. APAC delivered 5.5% operating profit growth in FY25 at constant currency, demonstrating resilience despite AUD/GBP translation headwinds. The risk-return of the APAC value chain is asymmetric: extremely high concentration on Woolworths creates single-point-of-failure risk, but the facility specificity (built for Woolworths at Woolworths' co-investment) makes this more a permanent bilateral arrangement than a switchable supply relationship. The APAC segment is the single most stable earnings contributor in the group portfolio and should be considered the quality anchor of the value-chain assessment
8.
E11 RA8: Working capital dynamics and the supply chain finance risk. HFG's value-chain position is evidenced in its working capital structure. Customer-provided supply chain finance (SCF) of approximately £150m (cost £9.6m per annum, approximately 6.4% effective rate) funds a substantial portion of HFG's receivables: the equivalent of a retailer paying HFG approximately 3 weeks early in exchange for a financing charge. A new supplier finance programme of £24.5m (new in FY25, no prior year) provides a mirror structure on the payables side. The combined SCF and supplier finance structures are evidence of HFG's intermediate value-chain position: it is downstream enough from primary processors to require working capital support from them, and upstream enough from retailers to receive working capital support in return. If Tesco or another major customer withdrew SCF facilities: for example in a credit stress event or contract renegotiation: HFG would face a £150m working capital outflow that would require £450m RCF drawdown. This is a tail risk, not a base case, but it is relevant to understanding the fragility of the apparent balance sheet strength at 0.9x net bank debt
9.
Counter-view considered (E11). The strongest counter on value-chain dynamics is that the midstream packing position is inherently unstable over the medium-to-long term as retailers internalise more processing capability or favour vertically integrated processors. Edeka's model (complete ownership of processing) represents the endpoint of this structural evolution. If UK retailers follow the German template, HFG's co-location moat would be dismantled at contract renewal points. This is a legitimate long-term structural concern but is partially rejected at Triaging because: (a) UK retailers have been moving in the opposite direction for 15+ years, outsourcing processing to specialists like HFG rather than internalising; (b) the physical capital embedded in co-located facilities creates bilateral switching costs that make change slow and expensive; (c) HFG's Tesco relationship recently extended, providing near-term evidence that the disintermediation threat is not materialising in the short term; and (d) the APAC Woolworths arrangement demonstrates that the value-chain model can be successfully replicated in markets outside the UK at even higher stability levels.
