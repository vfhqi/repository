[W] ORSTED-DK — Short-Sellers + Bear Case [C] @ 19-May-26

KEY FINDINGS - Overall bear verdict: Orsted presents one of the most substantively-damaged investment theses in European utilities — the bear case is fact-dense, multi-vector, and partially validated by catastrophic 2023 impairments [#J]

  • Impairment scale: DKK 28.4bn of offshore wind asset write-downs recognised in H2 2023 alone — representing roughly 35% of prior equity book value; no European utility peer has recorded comparable single-year impairment quantum in the renewables build cycle [#A]
  • Leverage trajectory: Net debt/EBITDA breached 7× at end-2023, forcing emergency equity issuance and dividend cancellation — peer median for investment-grade European utilities is approximately 3.5-4.5× [#A]
  • US portfolio abandonment: Full exit from US offshore wind contracted projects (Ocean Wind 1 + 2, Sunrise Wind, Revolution Wind reconfiguration) destroyed ~DKK 13bn of sunk capital and raised structural questions about management's project-selection discipline [#A]
  • No published short reports: No Muddy Waters, Hindenburg, Citron, or equivalent named-firm short-seller report on Orsted identified — the bear case has been constructed via sell-side downgrades and event-driven impairment rather than activist short campaigns [#I]
  • Short interest signal: Short interest has been elevated relative to European utility peers post-2023, consistent with a stock in structural re-rating rather than short-squeeze territory [#I]
  • Sell-side bear conviction: Multiple named brokers carry Sell or equivalent ratings; consensus PT implies meaningful downside from recent levels; estimate revision trend has been persistently negative since mid-2022 [#I]
  • Governance concern: CEO Mads Nipper and CFO Daniel Lerup both departed 2023-2024 following the impairment crisis — dual C-suite turnover mid-crisis is atypical and heightens execution risk under new leadership [#A]
  • Structural bear thesis: Rising WACC for offshore wind has structurally impaired project economics globally; Orsted's remaining pipeline carries embedded capital cost assumptions that were set in a 2020-2021 rate environment now 200-300bps below current [#A]
  • Invalidation risk is real: If CfD/PPA pricing does not recover materially by 2026-2027 auction rounds, or if construction inflation persists, the remaining ~35GW pipeline carries further impairment risk that consensus has not fully priced [#I]

§4 — Accounting + Governance Red Flags

Mixed signal with material concerns: Orsted's accounting raises substantive concerns around impairment recognition timing, capitalisation of project costs, and non-GAAP EBITDA adjustments; governance has been significantly disrupted by dual C-suite exits mid-crisis [#J]

  • Impairment recognition timing: The DKK 28.4bn impairment charge in H2 2023 was preceded by multiple quarters of management guidance that did NOT flag the magnitude of the pending write-down — raising questions about whether impairment testing was delayed [#A]
  • Sub: IFRS IAS 36 requires impairment testing when triggering indicators exist; rising WACC and energy price movements in H1 2023 should have constituted triggering indicators

  • Sub: The gap between the first public guidance downgrade (August 2023) and the final impairment quantum (November 2023) was DKK 10bn+ larger than initially indicated

  • Capitalisation aggressive: Orsted capitalises development costs and project overhead extensively during offshore wind project development phases; this is industry-standard but aggressive relative to peers who expense more development costs [#A]

  • Sub: Capitalised development costs that are subsequently abandoned (as with US projects) generate impairment charges with zero revenue offset — the accounting gain during capitalisation is reversed as a loss

  • Sub: Peer comparison: SSE, RWE, and Iberdrola use broadly similar capitalisation policies, but none has had as large a proportion of development spend subsequently abandoned in a single cycle

  • Non-GAAP gap: Orsted reports "EBITDA excl. new partnerships" as its primary earnings metric; this excludes gains and losses from farm-down transactions, which are material and recurring [#I]

  • Sub: Farm-down gains (selling minority stakes in operating wind farms) have historically been a significant component of reported profit — excluding them flatters the "underlying" EBITDA picture

  • Sub: In 2022-2023, farm-down activity slowed sharply as buyer appetite for offshore wind equity stakes declined; the underlying EBITDA ex-farm-downs deteriorated more than headline figures suggested

  • Adjustments increasing: The number of line items excluded from Orsted's "EBITDA excl. new partnerships" metric has expanded over 2021-2023, including "tax equity and PTC adjustments" for US projects and "strategic project costs" — typical creep pattern [#I]

  • Sub: Base-rate: Orsted's non-GAAP adjustment volume is in the top quartile of European utility peers by number of adjustment categories

  • Revenue recognition — farm-downs: Farm-down revenue recognition requires estimating the fair value of retained construction obligations — a material judgement that is not independently verifiable from public filings [#I]

  • Sub: With US project exits, some farm-down revenue previously recognised may need reassessment if the underlying asset is impaired

  • Inventory aging / WIP: Orsted carries significant "construction in progress" (CiP) assets on the balance sheet for projects under development; CiP is effectively unaudited project-level NPV [#I]

  • Sub: If construction cost inflation continues to erode project IRRs, CiP values carry embedded impairment risk not yet recognised

  • Related-party: No material related-party transaction concerns identified; Danish government as 50.1% shareholder creates a potential conflict-of-interest in capital structure decisions (e.g. reluctance to dilute government below 50%), but no evidence of financial related-party transactions that distort reported results [#I]

  • Mgmt comp structure: CEO and CFO departed in 2023-2024 under circumstances that included impairment announcements; compensation clawback provisions in Danish listed-company standards are weaker than UK/US equivalents [#I]

  • Sub: Mads Nipper (outgoing CEO) had received substantial LTIP awards benchmarked to TSR — TSR-based incentives may have contributed to optimistic project commitment in 2021-2022 during period of stock outperformance

  • Board independence: Danish government appoints multiple board members as 50.1% shareholder; independent-director proportion is below UK Corporate Governance Code standards [#I]

  • Sub: State-influenced boards are typically more conservative on strategic pivot but less willing to impose financial discipline on project pipelines with political/energy-security dimensions

  • Auditor change: No recent auditor change identified; PricewaterhouseCoopers has been Orsted's auditor for an extended period; no going-concern qualifications [#I]

  • Material weakness: No material weakness in internal controls disclosed in recent annual reports; internal controls are not the primary accounting concern [#I]

  • Pending lawsuit — US projects: Orsted faces potential legal claims from US state counterparties (New Jersey, New York, Connecticut) related to contract cancellations; New Jersey sought approximately $1bn in compensatory damages from the cancellation of Ocean Wind 1 and 2 [#A]

  • Sub: New Jersey's $300m penalty claim was settled; additional US state claims remain uncertain

  • Sub: The full quantum of US legal exposure is not fully provisioned in current balance sheet

  • NWC liability: Orsted's working capital position deteriorated in 2023 as project payables accelerated and receivables on farm-down transactions slowed [#I]

  • Sub: Days payable outstanding increased in 2023, consistent with cash pressure during the impairment crisis

  • Receivables aging: Farm-down receivables have longer collection cycles than utility operating receivables — creating timing mismatches between EBITDA recognition and cash receipt [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet (BULL rebuttal): Several apparent red flags are sector-structural rather than Orsted-specific — capitalisation of development costs, farm-down revenue recognition, and CiP accounting are standard across European offshore wind developers (SSE, RWE, Vattenfall); the impairment, while large, was ultimately disclosed transparently; the absence of auditor concerns or material weaknesses suggests the accounting is aggressive-but-standard rather than fraudulent

  • Sub: Track record evidence: Orsted under Nipper missed EBITDA guidance in 2021, 2022, and 2023 — three consecutive years; mean reversion toward guidance credibility requires demonstrated delivery, not mere replacement

  • Bear rebuttal #2: Net debt/EBITDA was still above 5× at end-2024 — above the 4× threshold that rating agencies have signalled for investment-grade comfort; further EBITDA recovery is required before leverage is genuinely normalised
  • Sub: Competitive evidence: Iberdrola and Enel operate at 3.5-4× net debt/EBITDA with diversified asset bases; Orsted's concentrated offshore wind exposure means a single sector downturn disproportionately affects FCF

  • Sub: Macro evidence: If interest rates remain structurally higher than 2019-2021, Orsted's refinancing of project-level debt will increase the weighted average cost of the operating portfolio — compressing operating-level FCF even on existing assets

  • Bull thesis #4: Offshore wind economics will recover as supply chains normalise and energy transition accelerates [#I]

  • Bull thesis #7: ESG premium and institutional ownership provide a valuation floor [#I]
