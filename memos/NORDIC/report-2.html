Nordic Semiconductor, Barco, and Shelly Group: Strategic Transformation Amid Semiconductor & IoT Hardware Sector Evolution
Key Takeaways

Nordic Semiconductor is executing a fundamental strategic pivot from a pure hardware component supplier to a comprehensive "chip-to-cloud" solutions provider, anchored by the $111.9 million Memfault acquisition and the launch of the 22-nanometer nRF54 platform to expand into Edge AI applications.

Leadership transformation is most dramatic at Nordic, where 60% of senior executives are new to their roles following CEO Vegard Wollan's appointment, driving a cultural shift from engineering-centric R&D toward commercial accountability and customer-focused execution under the "Nordic 2.0" initiative.

Barco is transitioning its cinema business model through "HDR by Barco," converting episodic projector hardware sales into a 15-year recurring "Cinema-as-a-Service" licensing model, targeting 15% recurring revenues by 2028 to smooth historically lumpy capital expenditure cycles.

Shelly Group demonstrates explosive hyper-growth momentum, sustaining 40%+ year-over-year revenue expansion while onboarding 3,000+ new households daily and rapidly scaling its professional installer network to over 5,300 members, successfully pivoting from DIY consumer sales to scalable B2B enterprise distribution.

Geopolitical and tariff risks are creating severe asymmetric impacts, with Nordic facing potential 30% EPS headwinds from US-China trade tensions due to 50%+ Taiwan wafer sourcing concentration, while Barco successfully mitigated margin compression by rapidly relocating Chinese mirror production back to European facilities.

Competitive dynamics are intensifying across all three companies, with Nordic confronting the Texas Instruments acquisition of Silicon Labs, Barco defending against Logitech's disruption of legacy ClickShare systems, and Shelly aggressively disrupting closed smart home ecosystems through hub-free, open-architecture interoperability at value-pricing.

Revenue predictability is undergoing structural transformation, as Nordic battles erratic customer "pull-in" behaviors masking true demand, Barco shifts toward book-and-turn ordering with reduced visibility, while Shelly engineers exceptional cyclical resilience by diversifying away from Q4 seasonality into continuous B2B professional installation projects.

Brief company descriptions
Nordic Semiconductor

Nordic Semiconductor operates as a fabless semiconductor company that designs, markets, and sells integrated circuits and complete solutions for wireless IoT connectivity.

Historically recognized primarily as a hardware provider of Bluetooth Low Energy chips, the company is actively transitioning over the next three years into a complete "chip-to-cloud" solutions provider.

This strategic evolution integrates its highly energy-efficient hardware with software development kits and post-deployment lifecycle management services, largely accelerated by the recent acquisition of Memfault.

The company's revenue is fundamentally segmented into three business units: Short-range, Long-range, and Other.

Short-range remains the dominant revenue engine, accounting for approximately 93% of the group's total revenue in late 2025, driven by multiprotocol products including BLE, Thread, Zigbee, and Matter.

Long-range technologies represent a vital growth vector, comprising cellular IoT and non-terrestrial network satellite connectivity, which doubled its revenue contribution in 2025 to $33.6 million.

The "Other" category captures early-stage adjacent technologies such as Power Management ICs and low-power Wi-Fi, which are expected to scale meaningfully over the next three years.

From an end-market perspective, Nordic targets the Consumer, Industrial and Healthcare, and Other verticals.

The Consumer segment historically provided the bulk of revenue through PC accessories and wearables, but the Industrial and Healthcare segment is driving current growth, reaching 35% of the total mix by the third quarter of 2025.

Geographically, the company maintains a massive global footprint with over 1,400 employees spanning 24 countries, while utilizing a localized manufacturing approach outsourced primarily to foundries in Taiwan and the Philippines.

Barco

Barco is a global technology leader specializing in advanced visualization, video networking, and collaboration solutions designed for mission-critical professional environments.

Over the past three years, the company has increasingly shifted its strategic focus from purely selling hardware displays and projectors to providing intelligent, connected, software and AI-driven workflow solutions.

The company organizes its operations across three primary divisions: Entertainment, Healthcare, and Enterprise.

The Entertainment division is the largest revenue contributor, generating 49% of group sales in 2025 through its Cinema and Immersive Experience business units.

Within cinema, Barco is driving a major business model transformation via its "HDR by Barco" rollout, which transitions the segment from one-time projector hardware sales to a recurring "Cinema as a Service" software and licensing model.

The Healthcare division accounts for 27% of revenues and provides advanced diagnostic imaging displays and surgical modality solutions designed to improve clinical workflows.

The Enterprise division, representing 24% of sales, focuses on corporate meeting experiences (notably the ClickShare ecosystem) and control room software platforms like Barco CTRL.

Barco operates a highly diversified geographic footprint with a "focused factory" manufacturing strategy in Europe and China to mitigate geopolitical risks and tariff uncertainties.

In 2025, the Americas accounted for the largest share of regional sales at 45%, followed by EMEA at 35% and APAC at 20%.

Over the next three years, management intends to leverage its installed base to grow in mid-segment markets and adjacent applications, while using AI to automate user workflows.

The company is also actively optimizing its capital allocation strategy, generating robust free cash flow to support strategic M&A, share buybacks, and steady dividend payouts.

Shelly Group (Allterco)

Shelly Group is a fast-growing European provider of Internet of Things (IoT) and smart building solutions for both do-it-yourself consumers and professional installers.

The company's core business revolves around developing and distributing smart relays, plugs, sensors, and meters that enable remote control, automation, and energy management of electrical appliances.

Shelly differentiates itself from competitors by offering devices with embedded web interfaces and multi-protocol wireless capabilities (Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, Zigbee, Matter) that do not require a proprietary centralized hub to function.

Smart home products under the Shelly brand account for over 95% of the group's total revenues, serving as the central pillar of its equity story.

The company operates a highly scalable, asset-light business model by outsourcing manufacturing to contract suppliers primarily located in China.

Over the last three years, Shelly has aggressively expanded its geographic presence; while the DACH region historically dominated sales, the "Rest of Europe" segment surpassed it in 2025, accounting for 50% of total revenue.

This international scaling is being driven by the establishment of localized sales teams in markets such as Italy, Spain, the Nordics, Benelux, and the UK.

Looking toward the next three years, Shelly is expanding its product portfolio into new high-value categories, including smart security cameras, advanced access control locks, and the Shelly X integration platform.

The company is also successfully transitioning its revenue mix to include higher-margin recurring software streams, evidenced by the rollout of its premium app subscription model.

A key strategic priority is capturing a larger share of the professional installer market, with its registered installer network growing nearly sixfold to over 5,300 members by the end of 2025.

Montega AG highlights that Shelly's broad product offensive and regional expansion perfectly position the company to sustain revenue growth rates near 40% year-over-year into 2026.

Change in leadership
Nordic Semiconductor

Nordic Semiconductor has undergone a massive leadership transformation over the past 18 months, concluding a long-planned succession process when Vegard Wollan took over as CEO in January 2024.

Following the CEO transition, the executive ranks experienced significant churn, leaving six out of ten senior executive leaders new to their roles during this period.

To strengthen execution discipline and accountability, the new leadership team reorganized the historical R&D department into four distinct business units: Short-Range, Long-Range, Wi-Fi, and Power Management.

Morgan Stanley reports that the new CEO is fundamentally shifting the corporate culture away from a purely R&D-driven model toward one that prioritizes delivering the performance customers need at the right commercial cost.

Jefferies notes that this restructuring highlights the CEO's ambition to sharpen the operating model, reduce unnecessary costs, and better utilize the company's world-class engineering assets.

The Board of Directors has also seen significant turnover, losing several industry veterans between 2022 and 2024 before appointing new members like Dieter May, who became Chair in 2025, and Helmut Gassel.

Following a challenging 2023 where downsizing caused employee engagement to drop to 6.9 out of 10, Nordic revamped its feedback mechanisms in 2025, moving from annual to quarterly surveys to gauge sentiment more frequently.

This approach yielded positive cultural momentum, with the 2025 baseline survey achieving an 80% response rate and recording high scores for peer relations (8.9) and psychological safety (8.9).

Employee retention metrics reflect this stabilizing environment, as voluntary turnover decreased from 7.24% in 2024 to 6% in 2025.

Change in strategy/structure
Nordic Semiconductor

Nordic Semiconductor recently implemented a major structural reorganization, dismantling its legacy R&D-centric model to establish four dedicated business units: Short-Range, Long-Range, Wi-Fi, and Power Management .

Management explicitly communicated to investors that this new structure is designed to sharpen strategic focus, enhance engineering execution, and improve accountability across the enterprise.

The overarching corporate strategy over the next 36 months centers on transitioning Nordic from a traditional hardware component supplier into a comprehensive "chip-to-cloud" solutions provider.

This strategic evolution aims to offer customers a seamlessly integrated platform combining world-class hardware, embedded software development kits, and cloud-based device lifecycle management services.

To accelerate this transition, the company aggressively utilized M&A, completing the acquisition of Memfault Inc. in July 2025 for a total consideration of $111.9 million.

The Memfault integration directly supports the chip-to-cloud strategy by adding advanced device observability, diagnostics, and Firmware Over-the-Air update capabilities to the nRF Cloud platform.

Nordic also executed a strategic bolt-on acquisition of Neuton.AI, significantly strengthening the company's position in machine learning and edge AI technologies.

These AI capabilities are slated for deep integration with the newly launched nRF54 Series, enabling highly scalable and accessible intelligence directly at the edge.

From a financial objective standpoint, management firmly reiterated its long-term ambition to deliver an average annual revenue growth rate above 20% through the end of the decade.

Alongside this top-line goal, the company has set a structural profitability target to achieve an operating model of approximately 25% EBITDA margin within five years.

The strategic rollout of the nRF54 product family is considered the primary engine to expand the company's addressable market and sustain momentum in the core short-range business.

In the Long-Range segment, the company is shifting its strategy toward cellular IoT cost-competitiveness through the ramp-up of the nRF9151 and the impending 2026 launch of the 22-nanometer nRF92 SoC.

Nordic is also heavily prioritizing satellite-enabled Non-Terrestrial Network connectivity, aiming to offer true global IoT coverage across both land and sea.

To ensure regulatory compliance and create a strategic moat, the company's new cloud services are designed to help customers automatically meet the stringent requirements of the upcoming 2027 EU Cyber Resilience Act.

Jefferies observes that Nordic's recent M&A activity clearly demonstrates management's focus on positioning the company as a strong standalone entity capable of competing in the broader market.

Jefferies further notes that integrating software bolt-ons like Memfault is a necessary strategic step to maintain the company's premium BLE positioning against intensifying industry competition.

Morgan Stanley highlights that while the company's 20% growth and 25% margin targets are achievable, they are highly dependent on the successful adoption of the nRF54 platform and disciplined operational expenditure control.

Morgan Stanley also points out that the company's heavy ongoing investments in operating expenses are currently limiting the structural operating leverage that investors might otherwise expect during a revenue recovery.
