§3 — Revenue + Organic Growth Analysis

J-front verdict: Revenue growth is decelerating from 2022-2023 energy-price peaks but underlying volume/network growth remains solid at c. 6-8% organic, with Networks as the durable compounder

  • Reported growth: FY2024 revenues €41.4bn, +6.5% YoY vs FY2023 €38.9bn; FY2023 was itself down from FY2022's €56.3bn — the FY2022 spike reflected peak European gas/power prices which inflated Supply revenues materially [#A]

  • The L3Y revenue CAGR (FY2022-FY2024) appears negative on a reported basis due to this peak-normalisation effect; underlying organic growth in regulated and contracted segments has been consistently positive

  • Organic growth: Stripping Supply/merchant revenue volatility, the Networks + contracted Renewables revenue base grew at c. 8-10% CAGR FY2022-FY2024 — this is the relevant "quality" growth rate for an 18M EPS thesis [#A]

  • IBE does not formally report an organic revenue growth figure; this is a Watson estimate from segment EBITDA trajectory and RAB growth disclosures

  • Segment trajectory: Networks segment revenue/EBITDA growing 12-15% PA, driven by regulated asset base expansion in UK (National Grid ESO adjacency), US (AVANGRID NY/Maine), Spain, and Brazil [#A]

  • Renewables segment: EBITDA growing 8-10% PA from capacity additions (c. 3GW net per year), partially offset by lower realised prices vs 2022-2023

  • Generation & Supply: structural drag; merchant Iberian exposure is declining as a % of group mix

  • Geographic mix: Spain and UK together c. 40% of EBITDA; US c. 25%; Brazil c. 15%; rest of world c. 20% — geographic diversification limits single-market regulatory risk but creates currency translation exposure [#A]

  • US EBITDA has been the highest-growth geography FY2022-FY2024 (AVANGRID rate cases, IRA credits); Brazil has been resilient; UK has been modest grower

  • Price contribution: In Networks, "price" is effectively regulatory tariff increases (RAB × WACC); UK CPI-linked tariffs added c. 3-4% organic growth in FY2023-FY2024 above volume growth [#A]

  • In Renewables, Power Purchase Agreement pricing has been the key variable; new PPAs signed in 2023-2025 at higher rates than legacy contracts, providing positive price contribution going forward

  • Volume contribution: Renewables volume growth c. 5-7% PA from new capacity additions; Networks volume (electricity distributed) broadly stable in mature markets [#A]

  • Iberian hydrology in Q1 2025 was notably above average — this is a one-quarter volume tailwind in hydroelectric generation, not structural

  • Pricing power: In regulated Networks, pricing power is administratively determined; IBE's regulatory relationships in Spain, UK, and US are generally constructive, with recent rate cases in New York and Maine settling at the high end of historical ranges [#A]

  • In Renewables, IBE's move toward long-term PPAs (c. 70% of output under fixed contracts as of FY2024) limits both upside and downside from spot power price moves — positive for predictability

  • Market share: Iberdrola is the largest renewable energy company globally by installed capacity (44.4 GW); organic capacity additions of 2.5-3.5 GW per year have outpaced most European peers [#A]

  • No evidence of material market share loss in any core geography; the competitive dynamic in renewables is absolute capacity growth rather than share redistribution

  • Forward revenue guidance: IBE does not give explicit revenue guidance; it guides on net profit and EBITDA; the implied revenue trajectory from the ">€6bn net profit" FY2025 guidance and historical margins suggests EBITDA of c. €18-19bn [#I]

  • This would imply c. 6-12% EBITDA growth from FY2024's €17.0bn — achievable if Networks continues at current trajectory

  • Sell-side revisions: Post-Q1 2025, public commentary indicates FY2025 EBITDA consensus revised up c. 3-5%; FY2026 revised up c. 2-4% — modest but directionally positive [#I]

  • Sceptical counter-read: The headline "organic growth" story masks a composition shift — Networks is growing fast but is also more capital-intensive and rate-case-dependent; Renewables growth is real but capacity additions are subject to supply chain, permitting, and grid connection delays that have materialised for peers; the sustainable organic growth rate may be 6-8% rather than the 10-12% implied by Q1 2025's run-rate

  • The FY2023-FY2024 margin expansion from 37% to 41% is more meaningful: Networks mix-shift and capacity additions growing EBITDA faster than reported revenue [#A]

  • Margin direction: EBITDA margins are in a structural expansion phase as Networks (highest-margin segment, c. 50%+ EBITDA margin on an asset-value basis) grows as a share of group — this is a genuine quality improvement [#A]

  • Net margin: FY2024 net profit €5.54bn on revenue €41.4bn = 13.4% net margin, vs FY2023 c. 11.5% — expansion driven by lower financing costs relative to EBITDA growth

  • Operating leverage: Revenue grew +6.5% in FY2024 while EBITDA grew +17% — strong positive operating leverage; the incremental EBITDA margin on Networks investment is structurally above the group average [#A]

  • D&A trend: elevated and rising as the €17bn+ annual capex programme builds the asset base; D&A FY2024 c. €5.5-6.0bn, growing c. 5-7% PA — this is the main headwind to EBIT-level margin expansion

  • EBIT margin: EBIT margins are lower than EBITDA margins would suggest due to heavy D&A; FY2024 EBIT c. €11bn on revenue €41.4bn ≈ 26.6% — comparable to regulated utility peers in Europe [#A]

  • D&A growth will continue as capex executes; EBIT margin expansion is therefore more constrained than EBITDA margin expansion

  • OpEx ratio: IBE's SG&A and corporate costs as % of EBITDA have been declining, reflecting operational scale; no evidence of cost creep in FY2024 results [#I]

  • Staff costs c. €3.5bn (estimate); procurement efficiencies in renewables construction are partially offsetting supply chain inflation in turbines and cables

  • One-off costs/benefits: FY2024 results included c. €400-600m of asset disposal gains (various wind/solar farm partial disposals, infrastructure asset monetisations) — these are recurring in character for IBE given its "build, operate, recycle" capital model but lumpy in timing [#A]

  • Q1 2025 net profit of €2.08bn almost certainly includes a material disposal gain component; the structural run-rate EBITDA of the Q1 number is the critical data point not yet publicly separated

  • Adjusted vs reported: IBE reports "net profit attributable to the parent" as the primary metric; it does not publish a separately-defined "adjusted" P&L in the same way as Anglo-Saxon reporters [#I]

  • This creates interpretive risk: disposal gains, fair value movements on financial instruments, and minority interest swings are all embedded in the headline net profit figure

  • ❌ Non-GAAP transparency: IBE's single-line "net profit" headline makes quality-adjustment analysis harder than peers (Enel separates "ordinary net income"; EDP publishes "recurring EBITDA") — this is a mild negative for investment analysis discipline

  • Sceptical counter-read: The EBITDA margin expansion story is partly a denominator illusion (merchant revenue falling out), partly a real mix-shift to regulated Networks, and partly a D&A lag effect (capex not yet fully reflected in depreciation); as the €17bn/year capex programme matures, D&A will grow to close the EBIT-EBITDA gap and reported net margins may compress even if EBITDA margins hold

§5 — Cash Flow + Capital Allocation

J-front verdict: Cash flow generation is strong and growing; leverage is managed conservatively relative to the capex programme; but the €17bn capex commitment leaves limited free cash flow for equity upside beyond the dividend

  • Operating cash flow: FY2024 operating cash flow exceeded €12bn (company disclosure), vs EBITDA of €17bn — implying cash conversion c. 70%, typical for capital-intensive regulated utilities [#A]
  • The gap between EBITDA and operating cash flow reflects: (a) interest payments on c. €55bn gross debt, (b) taxes, (c) working capital movements

  • [C] quality flag: precise FY2024 OCF figure from public sources is €12.0-12.5bn; exact figure to be verified against filing

  • Free cash flow: Operating cash flow €12bn less capex €17bn implies a structural free cash flow deficit of c. €5bn annually before asset disposal proceeds [#A]

  • IBE funds the FCF deficit through: (a) asset recycling (partial disposals of renewable farms to infrastructure funds), (b) hybrid/green bond issuance, (c) scrip dividend programme

  • This "build-operate-recycle" model is explicit in IBE's Capital Markets Day framework and is standard among European renewable majors; it is not a distress signal but it does mean "free cash flow" in the conventional sense is not the right metric

  • Asset recycling: IBE raised c. €2-3bn per year in FY2022-FY2024 through partial asset disposals; this is embedded in the business model and recurring — but it means reported FCF before disposals understates the investable cash generation [#A]

  • Q1 2025's exceptional net profit likely reflects a particularly large disposal tranche; FY2025 recycling target is c. €3-4bn per IBE's Capital Markets Day 2024 plan

  • Working capital: Limited NWC sensitivity for a predominantly regulated utility; receivables/payables dynamics are driven by tariff-setting cycles rather than commercial trading [#I]

  • No NWC deterioration signals in FY2024 or Q1 2025 public disclosures

  • Leverage: Net debt/EBITDA FY2024 c. 3.8x (IBE target: below 4.0x); net debt approximately €55bn [#A]

  • Leverage has been stable at 3.5-4.0x through the capex programme — management has delivered on the <4.0x commitment

  • FY2025: with EBITDA growing to c. €18-19bn and net debt growing c. €2-3bn annually, leverage ratio should continue declining modestly toward 3.5x

  • Dividend coverage: IBE paid €0.55/share dividend in FY2024 (c. €3.5bn total), with scrip dividend option reducing cash outflow to c. €2.0-2.5bn; dividend coverage on OCF basis c. 5x before capex [#A]

  • Dividend policy: 3-5% growth per year, with commitment to maintain through cycle; the FY2024 dividend was within this corridor

  • ❌ Scrip dividend dilution: IBE's scrip programme creates mild EPS dilution each year (c. 0.5-1% annual share count creep); this is below sector peers like EDP but is worth monitoring against the "EPS CAGR" headline

  • Capital return sustainability: At the current capex programme, cash dividends are fully funded by OCF; buybacks are absent — IBE is a growth-capex story, not a capital-return story [#A]

  • The investable case hinges on: (a) capex programme delivering regulatory RAB growth + contracted renewables EBITDA, (b) asset recycling remaining viable at current valuations, (c) refinancing of €55bn debt at reasonable rates as maturities roll

  • Sceptical counter-read: IBE's balance sheet looks healthy at 3.8x Net Debt/EBITDA today, but the €17bn annual capex programme plus rising interest rates on refinancing risk means leverage could surprise upward if disposal markets seize (infrastructure fund appetite declining), project delays occur, or EBITDA growth disappoints; the lack of true FCF coverage of the capex programme is the structural vulnerability that a rising-rate environment would expose

  • Key 2025 milestones: 3.5GW renewables additions targeted; Networks RAB growth c. 10-12%; AVANGRID New York rate case expected to resolve in FY2025

  • Management tone: Post-Q1 2025 commentary was notably confident — CFO described the quarter as demonstrating "the resilience and earnings power of the business model"; CEO highlighted US IRA monetisation as a growth driver being executed ahead of plan [#I]

  • This tone shift from "cautious optimism" at FY2024 to "confident" at Q1 2025 is meaningful given IBE management's historically conservative communication style

  • Hydrology: Q1 2025 benefited from above-average Iberian rainfall; H2 2025 assumes normal hydrology
  • US regulatory outcomes: AVANGRID NY/Maine rate cases assumed to resolve at or above IBE's embedded assumptions

  • Sandbagging vs overconfidence: IBE's L3Y track record (beating guidance by 1-8% each year) is consistent with modest sandbagging rather than overconfidence; the Q1 2025 raise mid-year is atypically proactive for IBE management and may reflect unusual confidence in the H2 2025 delivery [#A]

  • L1M/L3M/L12M revision momentum: In the 12 months prior to Q1 2025 results, IBE's FY2025 EPS consensus had been revised upward c. 8-12% cumulatively — positive pre-earnings momentum that the Q1 beat reinforced [#I]

  • Next inflection points:

  • CMD 2025 (expected late 2025): updated 3-year plan; market will want to see FY2027 targets

  • US IRA monetisation: forward PPAs and tax credit transfers being executed; pace will determine US earnings trajectory

  • Sceptical counter-read: The FY2025 guidance raise was enabled partly by Q1 disposal gains that management chose to keep in the headline net profit figure rather than strip out; if the remaining three quarters of FY2025 revert to c. €1.3-1.4bn per quarter (consistent with FY2024 run-rate), achieving ">€6bn" for the full year would require only €1.96bn in the remaining nine months — arithmetically comfortable, which means the "raise" may be less impressive than it appears

§7 — Strategic Execution + Operational Progress

J-front verdict: Strategic execution has been broadly on-plan across renewables additions, network investment, and US repositioning; the AVANGRID integration is the most material outstanding execution risk

  • M&A integration — AVANGRID: Iberdrola's US subsidiary (AVANGRID) has been the strategic priority since the 2020 PNM Resources acquisition attempt (ultimately failed/divested) and the ongoing AVANGRID Network + Renewables buildout [#A]
  • AVANGRID Networks (serving 3.4m customers in NY and ME) is subject to ongoing rate cases; the NY rate case is the single most material regulatory uncertainty in the 18M window

  • AVANGRID Renewables has >10GW under development in the US; IRA monetisation via tax credit transfers is proceeding, with c. $700-900m in IRA credits expected FY2025-FY2026

  • Cost programme: IBE has executed a multi-year procurement efficiency programme across renewables construction; turbine and cable costs remain elevated vs 2020-2021 but have stabilised from 2023 peaks [#A]

  • Labour and grid connection costs in offshore wind (particularly in the UK) remain elevated; IBE's UK offshore wind projects (East Anglia hub) have seen cost escalation consistent with sector peers

  • Renewables capacity additions: FY2024 additions c. 3.2GW net; Q1 2025 additions tracking to FY target of c. 3.5GW [#A]

  • Geographic split of additions: US (c. 40%), Spain (c. 25%), UK (c. 15%), rest of world (c. 20%)

  • Connection delays in UK and Spain have pushed some FY2025 targeted projects into FY2026; management has acknowledged this in public disclosures without quantifying the EBITDA impact

  • UK offshore wind — East Anglia: The East Anglia Array phases are IBE's flagship UK offshore wind programme; Phase 1 (714MW) is operational; Phase 2 and 3 face cost pressure and grid connection timelines are extending [#A]

  • This project is not a near-term EPS driver but is a significant balance sheet risk if construction costs continue escalating

  • Digital/transformation: IBE has invested in smart grid and EV charging infrastructure across Spain and UK; these are margin-accretive but small in EBITDA terms (c. 1-2% of group) [#I]

  • No material negative news on digital execution; no transformative positive news either

  • Portfolio rationalisation: IBE has been consistently executing the "build-operate-recycle" model — selling minority stakes in operational renewable farms to infrastructure funds (Macquarie, Copenhagen Infrastructure Partners, others) [#A]

  • FY2024 recycling transactions: c. €2.5bn proceeds; FY2025 pipeline c. €3-4bn; strong secondary market for operational renewables assets is a key enabler of IBE's capital model

  • Management changes: CEO Ignacio Galan remains; no material C-suite departures in FY2024-Q1 2025; AVANGRID CEO appointment (Pedro Azagra, also IBE board member) is stable [#I]

  • Succession risk for Galan (in role since 2001) is a long-term governance consideration but not an immediate catalyst
