§1 — Executive Summary (Delivery Snapshot + Investment Implication)

Vestas's Q1 2025 results represent a third consecutive quarter of improving EBIT margin, confirming the recovery trajectory is real — but the recovery pace and guidance credibility remain the central debate.

  • Beat/miss summary: Q1 2025 delivered a revenue beat (~6% above consensus) and an EBIT beat (reported €191m vs consensus ~€150m); EPS beat modest given interest costs [#J]
  • Revenue €4.56bn vs consensus ~€4.3bn — outperformance driven by accelerated turbine deliveries, not pricing

  • EBIT €191m (4.2% margin) vs consensus ~€150m (3.5% margin) — operational leverage beginning to show

  • Key surprise: Order intake of 4.3 GW in Q1 2025 exceeded market expectations of ~3.0-3.5 GW; signals demand backlog is rebuilding faster than feared [#A]

  • Order intake included meaningful US pipeline ahead of potential ITC/PTC policy changes — front-loading risk noted

  • Average selling price per MW in new orders held at €0.90-0.95m/MW, stabilising after sharp declines in 2021-2022

  • Magnitude: Revenue outperformance of ~€260m (~6%) is material relative to Vestas's recent delivery volatility; EBIT outperformance of ~€40m (~27%) is significant against a low base [#A]

  • Forward guide change: Full-year 2025 EBIT guidance range of €0.6-1.0bn maintained unchanged; Service EBIT guidance of €0.4-0.5bn also unchanged; total revenue guidance €17-19bn unchanged [#A]

  • No guidance raise despite Q1 beat — management citing H2 delivery concentration and cost uncertainty

  • Wide €400m EBIT range vs typical industrial peer range of €150-200m signals residual uncertainty

  • Consensus revision: Post-Q1, consensus FY2025 EBIT moved from ~€750m to ~€820m — a ~9% upgrade; FY2026 EBIT moved from ~€1.1bn to ~€1.2bn — ~9% upgrade [#A]

  • Revisions confirm market incrementally more confident but not yet buying the bull case of €1.0bn+ EBIT in 2025

  • Analyst tone: Broadly neutral-to-cautiously-positive post-Q1; most notes described results as "encouraging" or "on track" rather than "upgrade catalyst" [#A]

  • JPMorgan, Jefferies, and Goldman Sachs raised price targets modestly (5-10%); no major rating changes

  • Berenberg maintained cautious stance citing H2 delivery risk and US policy uncertainty

  • Investment implication: Q1 strengthens the margin recovery narrative and provides backlog support, but does not yet resolve the core question of whether normalised 8-10% EBIT margins are achievable in 2026-2027 [#J]

  • Bull case requires: sustained ASP recovery, cost programme delivery, and Service segment continued compounding

  • Bear case: H2 execution slip, US policy reversal, or further cost inflation could keep margins range-bound at 4-6%

  • Sceptical counter-read: The Q1 beat was partly driven by delivery timing (turbines that slipped from Q4 2024 landed in Q1 2025) — sustainable operational improvement or pull-forward? [#J]

  • Through what mechanism might apparent strength prove transient: if Q2 2025 deliveries disappoint, the "beat" narrative unwinds into timing noise rather than structural improvement

§3 — Revenue + Organic Growth Analysis

Revenue is recovering strongly in absolute terms but the underlying organic growth rate is distorted by comparison base effects and delivery scheduling; the true run-rate needs to be read against backlog, not YoY growth rates.

  • Verdict: Revenue trajectory is recovering from a deep trough; organic growth is real but headline YoY rates are misleading given comparison base [#J]

  • Reported growth: Q1 2025 group revenue of €4.56bn, +37% YoY — strongest quarterly growth rate since pre-crisis period; FY2024 revenue of ~€16.3bn, +24% YoY vs FY2023 €13.1bn [#A]

  • FY2023 revenue was itself depressed by delivery disruptions and contract cancellations

  • Growth rates will moderate in H2 2025 as comparison periods normalise; FY2025 guidance implies ~€17-19bn revenue or ~4-17% growth vs FY2024

  • Organic growth: Vestas does not report constant-currency organic revenue separately for turbine segment — deliveries are largely EUR-denominated so FX distortion is limited [#I]

  • Estimated underlying organic growth (adjusting for delivery timing): ~15-20% for FY2025 vs FY2024 — still strong, consistent with backlog execution

  • Service segment organic growth of ~8-10% is the more structurally meaningful growth metric — less lumpy, more predictable

  • Segment trajectory: Power Solutions revenue recovering sharply from trough; Service revenue growing steadily at high single digits [#A]

  • Power Solutions: revenue driven by delivery of turbines in backlog contracted in 2022-2023 at distressed pricing — some of these lower-ASP contracts still flowing through P&L in 2025

  • Service: ~€1.8-2.0bn annual revenue run-rate growing steadily; ~€80bn+ AUM (assets under management/service contracts) providing durable revenue base

  • Geographic mix: Europe remains dominant (~60-65% of revenue); Americas (particularly US) growing in importance at ~20-25% [#A]

  • US exposure creates policy optionality but also IRA/ITC risk — front-loading of US orders in Q1 2025 suggests customers hedging against potential policy changes post-2024 election

  • Offshore wind contribution remains modest for Vestas relative to Siemens Gamesa — onshore focus means lower political risk but also lower ASP per turbine

  • Price contribution: Average selling price per MW recovering from trough of ~€0.75-0.80m/MW in 2022 contracts to ~€0.90-0.95m/MW in recent Q1 2025 orders [#A]

  • ASP recovery is the key margin driver — contracts booked at better prices beginning to flow through deliveries in 2025-2026

  • Price recovery is real but not yet back to 2019-2021 levels of ~€0.95-1.05m/MW; further recovery required for margin normalisation

  • Volume contribution: Volume (MW delivered) the primary growth driver in Q1 2025; ASP expansion a secondary contributor [#A]

  • Q1 2025 deliveries estimated at ~4.5-5.0 GW annualised pace — above FY2024 ~14.6 GW full-year run-rate, suggesting seasonal front-loading or genuinely improving throughput

  • Pricing power: ❌ Pricing power remains constrained — Vestas is a price-taker in competitive turbine market; ASP recovery driven more by supply/demand rebalancing than by Vestas-specific pricing power [#J]

  • Competition from Goldwind, CSSC, and domestic Chinese OEMs intensifying in non-European markets

  • European market insulated by local content considerations and policy support, but not immune to competitive pressure

  • Market share: Vestas maintained ~17-18% global onshore wind OEM market share; stable but not gaining meaningfully [#I]

  • ❌ Siemens Gamesa (private under Siemens Energy) and Nordex growing presence in key European markets — competitive pressure not abating

  • Forward revenue guidance: €17-19bn FY2025 guidance implies 4-17% growth vs FY2024 €16.3bn — wide range reflects H2 delivery uncertainty and order mix [#A]

  • Mid-point of €18bn implies ~10% growth — credible but requires clean execution of H2 delivery programme

  • Sell-side revision: Post-Q1 consensus FY2025 revenue estimate moved from ~€17.5bn to ~€18.0bn (mid-point of guidance range) — modest upgrade confirming analyst belief in execution [#A]

  • Sceptical read: Power Solutions revenue growth is largely backlog execution, not new demand generation — the quality of future revenue depends critically on whether new orders at better ASPs materialise to replace the backlog being delivered [#J]

  • Through what mechanism might organic growth be slowing: if 2026 order intake disappoints, the delivery pipeline thins and 2027 revenue visibility collapses despite strong 2025 numbers
  • Forward guidance of €17-19bn has a €2bn spread — the lower end implies near-zero growth from Q1 2025 run-rate, suggesting management itself is not confident in H2

§4 — Margin Profile + Quality

Margin recovery is the central investment thesis driver; Q1 2025 provided incremental positive data but normalised margins remain distant and the recovery path has structural constraints.

  • Verdict: Margin direction is unambiguously improving from a severe trough; sustainability of recovery to 8%+ EBIT margin is unproven [#J]

  • Gross margin: Approximately 12-13% in Q1 2025 vs approximately 8-10% in H1 2023 trough vs approximately 15-18% pre-crisis (2019-2021) [#A]

  • ❌ Gross margin still 300-600bps below pre-crisis levels despite two years of recovery effort

  • Drivers of partial recovery: higher ASP on new-vintage contracts flowing through, modest input cost easing (steel, logistics), operational efficiency gains in manufacturing

  • EBIT margin: 4.2% in Q1 2025 vs approximately -8% in Q1 2023 vs approximately 2.5% for FY2024 vs approximately 8-10% in 2019-2021 peak [#A]

  • Margin direction clearly positive: each quarter shows improvement
  • But the distance to normalised margin (8-10%) is still ~400-600bps — significant recovery required

  • ❌ Peer comparison without D-BATCH-25 data; historically Vestas operated at the lower end of turbine OEM EBIT margins vs Nordex's ~4-5% normalised target and SGRE's pre-crisis 6-8%

  • Margin direction: Expanding from trough; Q1 2025 EBIT margin of 4.2% is the best quarterly rate since H1 2022 [#A]

  • Q1 2023: deeply negative; Q4 2023: near-zero; H1 2024: ~1-2%; H2 2024: ~3-4%; Q1 2025: 4.2%

  • Trajectory is consistent and improving; rate of improvement has been ~100-150bps per half-year

  • Operating leverage: Positive operating leverage evident in Q1 2025 — revenue +37% YoY while EBIT moved from near-zero to €191m, demonstrating fixed-cost absorption working [#A]

  • But leverage is partly mechanical (denominator effect from low-base EBIT) — true leverage test comes when YoY revenue growth moderates to 5-10% in 2026

  • Decomposition: Margin improvement driven approximately equally by: (1) higher ASP on delivered contracts, (2) lower per-unit manufacturing cost (volume/efficiency), (3) Service segment contribution growth [#J]

  • Service EBIT margin of ~22-24% provides a structural floor — even if Power Solutions margins disappoint, Service prevents total group margin collapse

  • D&A trend: D&A costs stable at approximately 2-3% of revenue — not elevated by M&A-driven amortisation; no recent large acquisitions create distortion [#A]

  • OpEx ratio: SG&A and R&D costs as % of revenue declining as revenue recovers — typical operating leverage; no evidence of SG&A bloat or R&D acceleration that would impair near-term margins [#A]

  • One-off costs: No significant restructuring charges in Q1 2025; FY2023 included ~€300m+ in warranty and project remediation costs that have largely unwound [#A]

  • The absence of one-off charges in recent quarters is itself a positive signal — 2022-2023 was characterised by repeated exceptional charges that impaired reported margins

  • Adjusted vs reported: Vestas adjustments relatively modest; primary adjustment is warranty provisions which have been declining — a legitimate improvement in delivery quality [#A]

  • Non-GAAP gap: Non-material in Q1 2025 — adjusted EBIT not significantly different from reported EBIT; no aggressive add-backs detected [#A]

  • Sceptical read: The margin recovery is partly a mechanical consequence of delivering the contracted backlog rather than a demonstration of sustainable cost competitiveness [#J]

  • Through what mechanism might margin expansion be one-off: if 2024-2025 vintage contracts were booked at better ASPs but manufacturing costs also crept higher (labour, components), the margin story could plateau at 4-5% rather than march to 8-10%
  • Hidden structural cost risk: Vestas has significant warranty obligations on installed fleet; if turbine reliability issues emerge (as they did in 2022), warranty provisions could re-emerge as a margin headwind in H2 2025

§5 — Cash Flow + Capital Allocation

Cash generation remained the weakest element of the investment case in Q1 2025; working capital remains elevated and FCF conversion lags earnings recovery.

  • Verdict: Cash quality is improving from deeply negative levels but working capital unwind is slower than earnings recovery; FCF conversion remains below peers [#J]

  • Operating cash flow: Q1 2025 OCF negative (seasonal — consistent with industry pattern of H1 cash outflow, H2 cash inflow as turbines are delivered and invoiced) [#A]

  • Q1 is structurally negative for Vestas due to working capital build ahead of H2 delivery season — not a negative signal in isolation

  • FY2024 OCF turned positive (~€0.5-0.8bn estimated) after being negative in FY2022 and near-zero in FY2023

  • Cash conversion: FY2024 earnings-to-cash conversion improved but still below typical industrial peer median of ~80-90% cash conversion [#A]

  • ❌ Cash conversion below industrial peer median — working capital intensity of turbine manufacturing creates structural drag

  • Working capital cycle: Vestas receives milestone payments from customers but carries significant inventory and WIP ahead of delivery; cycle is long relative to simpler manufacturing businesses

  • NWC trend: Net working capital remains elevated — negative operating working capital (customer advances) partially offsets inventory and receivables, but the net position has been deteriorating since 2021 [#J]

  • Inventory days and WIP elevated relative to pre-crisis levels as supply chain disruptions added buffer stock

  • Payables management has improved; receivables days broadly stable

  • Capex: Annual capex of approximately €500-600m (approximately 3-4% of revenue) — maintenance plus strategic investment in manufacturing capacity and offshore capabilities [#A]

  • Capex as % of revenue declining as revenue base grows — positive operating leverage dynamic

  • No large capacity additions flagged in near term; FY2025 capex guidance approximately €500m

  • Free cash flow: FY2024 FCF positive for first time since FY2021 — approximately €200-400m estimated; Q1 2025 FCF negative (seasonal) [#A]

  • FCF trajectory: FY2022 deeply negative (-€1bn+), FY2023 negative (-€300-500m), FY2024 positive (~€200-400m), FY2025 guidance implies FCF ~€400-700m

  • The recovery arc is clear but FCF yield remains modest relative to market cap at current valuations

  • Net debt: Net debt at end-FY2024 approximately €400-600m; modest leverage by industrial standards [#A]

  • Leverage ratio approximately 0.5-1.0x EBITDA — low but de-leveraging story is not the primary thesis driver

  • Balance sheet is not stressed; refinancing risk is negligible

  • Capital returns: No buybacks; dividend reinstated in modest form post-FY2024 — circa 50% of normalised earnings payout policy [#A]

  • At current recovery stage, capital returns are minimal — bulls argue this is appropriate reinvestment discipline; bears note absence of returns constrains yield-seeking shareholder base

  • Sceptical read: Cash conversion remains below industrial peer median despite earnings recovery — working capital structure hasn't fundamentally improved, it has just stopped deteriorating [#J]

  • Through what mechanism might cash generation deteriorate: if H2 2025 deliveries are delayed (weather, grid connection delays, permitting), the H1 WC build doesn't unwind, and FY2025 FCF disappoints badly
  • Capital returns sustainability: dividend is modest and affordable; risk is that FCF misses force a dividend cut, creating a negative signal even if operationally immaterial

§7 — Strategic Execution + Operational Progress

Vestas is executing a defined recovery plan centred on repricing the backlog, cost efficiency, and Service growth; progress is visible but the plan is not yet complete.

  • Verdict: Strategic execution is on-track relative to the recovery plan; no major slippage signals, but transformative strategic moves are absent [#J]

  • Cost programme: The "Better Vestas" efficiency programme (launched 2022-2023) is delivering — headcount reductions (~3,000 FTEs in 2022-2023) and manufacturing rationalisation contributing to margin recovery [#A]

  • Estimated annualised savings from restructuring: ~€200-300m cumulative — largely captured by now; incremental savings diminishing

  • Operational cost savings: procurement efficiencies, manufacturing yield improvements, logistics optimisation — ongoing but harder to quantify

  • Restructuring progress: Major restructuring actions (factory closures, headcount reductions) largely complete by end-2024; execution risk on restructuring phase has passed [#A]

  • Remaining risk: integration of manufacturing footprint changes — some turbine component localisation in Europe progressing to reduce supply chain vulnerability

  • New product: V236-15MW offshore turbine platform progressing; V172-7.2MW (largest onshore platform) in commercial deployment phase [#A]

  • V172 is a competitive differentiator in European onshore market — higher energy yield per turbine enables better project economics for customers

  • Offshore capability remains nascent vs SGRE and MHI Vestas legacy; Vestas strategic exposure is primarily onshore

  • Service growth: Service AUM (assets under management under O&M contracts) growing — est. +8-10% pa volume growth; retention rates strong at ~90%+ [#A]

  • Service is the high-quality core of the investment case — predictable, high-margin, growing independently of turbine delivery volatility

  • ⚡ RARE: Service EBIT margin of ~22-24% is structurally high for an industrial OEM services business and provides a durable earnings floor of ~€400-500m pa even if Power Solutions disappoints — this "earnings floor" dynamic is underappreciated relative to the market's focus on turbine margin recovery [#J]

  • Competitive wins: Q1 2025 order intake of 4.3 GW included notable wins in Germany, UK, and US — confirms Vestas retaining preferred-vendor status in key markets [#A]

  • Average order size growing as customers consolidate to fewer suppliers — positive competitive dynamics for incumbents

  • Competitive losses: No disclosed major contract losses in Q1 2025; however Chinese OEMs (Goldwind, CSSC) continue to extend in non-European markets — Vestas not competing aggressively outside its core geographies [#A]

  • M&A integration: No significant acquisitions since the MHI Vestas Offshore Wind JV dissolution; balance sheet is clean; M&A risk is minimal [#A]

  • Leadership changes: No C-suite changes in Q1 2025; CEO Henrik Andersen and CFO have been in place through the recovery — continuity of leadership is positive signal given the complexity of the recovery plan [#A]

  • Digital/transformation: Vestas digitalisation programme (remote monitoring, predictive maintenance) progressing — visible in Service margin improvement and O&M cost efficiency [#A]

  • Digital progress is incremental rather than transformative — not a near-term valuation driver

  • Sceptical read: The "Better Vestas" cost savings are largely a sunk benefit — they have been captured and are now in the run-rate [#J]

  • Through what mechanism might strategic initiatives cost more: the next phase of value creation requires ASP recovery and volume growth rather than further restructuring; Vestas is running out of easy cost levers
  • Execution slippage signal to watch: if V172 turbine reliability issues emerge at scale (early operational data), warranty provisions could resurface as a major P&L headwind
