Section 2 – The 80/20 (bottom line up front) 8 findings

Section summary
  1. The company provides highly predictable, inflation-linked infrastructure returns driven by structural data growth in emerging markets.
1.
Passive infrastructure provision: The business builds and maintains physical telecom towers and power supply systems, earning recurring lease revenue from mobile network operators who install their own active radio equipment on the structures
2.
The core investment case: The company is transitioning from a capital-intensive build phase to a highly cash-generative harvest phase, driven by margin-accretive colocation growth. This operating leverage is expected to fund rapid deleveraging and accelerating shareholder distributions
3.
Primary earnings swing factor: The single most critical variable for forward earnings is the tenancy ratio, which measures the average number of operators hosted per tower and dictates the absorption of fixed ground-lease and maintenance costs
4.
Unbroken historical growth trajectory: The company has delivered ten consecutive years of uninterrupted core earnings expansion, growing adjusted earnings from $54 million in 2015 to $471 million in 2025 despite multiple macroeconomic crises
5.
Accelerating recent momentum: First-quarter 2026 results demonstrated exceptional volume acceleration, with the company adding 1,406 tenancies year-to-date and driving a 14% year-on-year increase in adjusted earnings
6.
Principal downside risk: The most severe threat to the earnings trajectory is a potential slowdown in network rollout spending by major telecom customers, which would stall tenancy additions and strand invested capital
7.
First-look verdict: Yes, the predictable contractual revenue profile and extreme operating leverage warrant deeper investigation, as the company screens at a significant valuation discount to developed-market peers despite offering superior structural growth

Section 3 – What the company does 14 findings

Section summary
  1. The business operates a highly defensible, high-margin shared infrastructure model insulated by extreme barriers to entry and long-term customer lock-in.

Business model and operational footprint

1.
Core passive infrastructure offering: The business assumes responsibility for the physical steel towers, land leases, security, and mission-critical power generation, allowing telecom operators to focus exclusively on their active network routing equipment
2.
Colocation growth strategy: The model relies on building a tower with an anchor tenant and subsequently hosting additional operators on the same structure, sharing the fixed capital base and dramatically expanding site-level returns
3.
Power infrastructure provision: Because grid reliability is poor in many African markets, the company acts as a vital energy provider, guaranteeing near-continuous uptime via solar, hybrid, and diesel generator backups
4.
Pass-through energy economics: While power provision increases gross revenue, fuel costs are largely passed through to operators via contractual escalators, meaning core earnings growth is driven by tenancy volumes rather than energy markups

Customers and contract economics

5.
Highly consolidated customer base: The company relies heavily on blue-chip multinational telecom groups, with 98% of revenue sourced from major operators and roughly 72% concentrated in its top four clients
6.
Predictable contracted revenue: Earnings visibility is exceptionally high, underpinned by non-cancellable agreements featuring initial terms of ten to fifteen years and an average remaining initial life of 6.7 years
7.
Embedded inflation protection: To defend margins against emerging-market volatility, lease agreements contain strict contractual consumer price index escalators and explicit linkages to local power and fuel prices
8.
Hard currency insulation: The business mitigates local currency depreciation risk by operating in several structurally dollarized or euro-pegged jurisdictions, securing nearly 70% of its core earnings in hard currencies

Competitive advantage and industry dynamics

9.
Formidable barriers to entry: The sector is insulated by the immense capital requirements needed to build national tower grids, alongside the complex operational capabilities required to maintain 99.99% power uptime in remote African geographies
10.
Significant switching costs: Relocating active radio equipment from one tower to another causes unacceptable network downtime and incurs heavy engineering costs, effectively locking operators into existing sites for decades
11.
Structural demand tailwinds: The operating environment is characterised by rapid population growth and exceptionally low baseline mobile penetration, driving a forecasted quadrupling of data consumption across Africa and the Middle East by 2030
12.
Consolidated market positioning: The company controls an estimated 68% of all marketable independent towers across its footprint, operating in market structures that are largely protected from intense infrastructure overbuilding
13.
Other side of the story: Sceptics argue that the extreme reliance on just four major telecom groups exposes the tower operator to immense pricing pressure during contract renewals, potentially compressing future margins

Section 5 – The possible investment case 11 findings

Section summary
  1. The investment case looks highly compelling, driven by a predictable combination of structural data demand, intense operating leverage, and an imminent shift toward shareholder distributions.

Structural data growth and tenancy additions

1.
Exponential data consumption: The primary revenue driver is the explosion of digital connectivity across Africa, where mobile operators are forced to densify networks to support a projected 400% surge in data traffic by 2030
2.
Accelerating operator investment: The plausibility of this volume growth is high; major customers like Airtel Africa are publicly confirming accelerated capital expenditure programmes to handle network congestion after reporting >50% data usage growth
3.
Mechanism of failure: This volume driver would fail if leading telecommunications providers suddenly consolidated their operations, merged networks, and subsequently decommissioned overlapping tower leases to save costs

Operating leverage and margin expansion

4.
High incremental returns: The margin expansion thesis is highly plausible because adding a second or third tenant to an existing tower generates a nearly 80% flow-through to core earnings with minimal incremental capital outlay
5.
Fixed cost absorption: Because underlying ground lease payments and basic maintenance capital expenditures are largely static, every new colocation lease disproportionately inflates site-level return on invested capital
6.
Mechanism of failure: This leverage driver would break if the company is forced by competitive or government pressure to rapidly construct thousands of new single-tenant rural sites, which carry significantly lower initial returns and dilute overall margins

Deleveraging and shareholder returns

7.
Inflecting cash generation: The distribution thesis is highly credible as the company has officially inflected into surplus cash generation, tripling statutory free cash flow to $66 million in 2025 as the legacy build-out phase matured
8.
Inaugural capital returns: Proving the thesis, management initiated a firm $75 million share buyback programme alongside a new $25 million annual dividend schedule, signalling a definitive pivot toward yield
9.
Compound probability requirement: The bull case requires these three drivers to operate independently; even if volume growth decelerates slightly, the sheer cash conversion of the existing contracted base can still fund aggressive share buybacks and deleveraging
10.
Other side of the story: Sceptics argue that the required capital intensity to build 5G-ready sites and upgrade green energy systems will consistently drain free cash flow, indefinitely delaying the promised surge in shareholder yield

Section 6 – Valuation 6 findings

Section summary
  1. The shares appear exceptionally cheap against developed-market peers, trading at a steep discount that arguably over-penalises the business for its emerging-market geography.
1.
Significant peer discount: Morgan Stanley notes the stock trades at just 7.4x estimated 2028 EV/EBITDA, representing a severe 35-40% discount to US and European tower operators that average closer to 14.1x
2.
Attractive cash flow yield: Deutsche Bank calculates the company offers an 8.9% adjusted free-cash-flow yield for 2026, screening materially richer than the 6.2% average yield available from massive US tower incumbents
3.
Supportive price targets: Sell-side price targets are clustered tightly with significant upside; Jefferies targets 285p and Deutsche Bank targets 285p, both implying roughly 40% upside from the current 203p trading level
4.
Implied market expectations: The current suppressed multiple suggests the market is pricing in a permanent geopolitical risk discount and expressing deep scepticism regarding the company's ability to safely repatriate African cash flows
5.
Other side of the story: Valuation bears argue the discount is entirely justified, noting that a higher cost of equity and persistent local currency depreciation structurally cap the multiples emerging-market infrastructure assets can command

Section 7 – Key risks and the other side 8 findings

Section summary
  1. The primary risks stem from operating in volatile frontier markets, though the long-term contractual structures heavily insulate the core earnings base.

Macroeconomic and currency exposure

1.
Local currency depreciation: While contracts are technically linked to hard currencies, severe local devaluations stress the underlying telecom operators who must pay higher dollar-equivalent lease fees using depreciating local consumer revenues
2.
Emerging market volatility: Exposure to jurisdictions with histories of geopolitical instability, such as the Democratic Republic of Congo, constantly threatens physical site security and creates chronic fuel supply chain vulnerabilities

Customer concentration and capex delays

3.
Network operator consolidation: If major in-country customers merge to survive macroeconomic shocks, they will inevitably rationalise their overlapping networks, leading to lease cancellations and a direct hit to the critical tenancy ratio
4.
Capital expenditure deferrals: Slower network rollout plans by key clients, triggered by high interest rates or regulatory tax burdens, would immediately freeze the company's volume growth engine and stall margin expansion

Technological disruption and alternative infrastructure

5.
Low-earth orbit satellite threat: Constellations like Starlink are aggressively targeting the African connectivity gap, representing a long-term alternative that could eventually bypass terrestrial tower networks entirely in rural zones
6.
Crucial early warning signal: Investors must vigilantly monitor the quarterly tenancy addition run rate; any sequential miss against the 600-per-quarter target would immediately signal weakening customer demand
7.
Other side of the story: Proponents counter that satellite technology lacks the capacity density required for urban African centres, ensuring terrestrial towers remain the absolute backbone of data transmission for the foreseeable future

Section 9 – Net read and what to check next 5 findings

Section summary
  1. The stock demands immediate deeper work, offering a rare combination of double-digit structural earnings growth, inflecting cash yields, and a depressed entry multiple.
1.
Strongest bullish argument: The compounding mathematics of adding high-margin colocations to an existing, sunk-cost steel portfolio will generate immense free cash flow that the market is currently ignoring
2.
Other side of the story: The relentless reality of operating in high-friction African markets means that unpredictable macroeconomic shocks, fuel crises, and currency crises will perpetually cap the valuation multiple
3.
Next verification step: Verify the exact mechanics and historical reliability of the consumer price index and power-cost escalators within the master lease agreements across the top three markets
4.
Next research action: Commission a deep-dive analysis into the specific network densification budgets and 5G rollout timelines of Airtel Africa and Vodacom to validate the projected tenancy pipeline
