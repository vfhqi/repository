Section summary
  1. Aker Solutions operates an asset-light energy services model that is highly concentrated on the Norwegian Continental Shelf and heavily dependent on its sister company, Aker BP.

Core Segment and Revenue Structure

1.
Diverse reporting segments: The company operates through two primary reporting segments, which are Renewables and Field Development, and the asset-light Life Cycle segment, which optimizes field performance and longevity
2.
Dominant segment revenue: Renewables and Field Development represents the largest segment, generating NOK 46.06 billion or 72.93% of the company's total customer contract revenues of NOK 63.20 billion in 2025

Section 5 – The possible investment case 13 findings

Section summary
  1. The forward investment case for Aker Solutions is extremely hard to make because the high-margin Aker BP portfolio rolls off in 2026, leaving a structural earnings vacuum.

Forward Earnings Drivers and Plausibility

1.
OneSubsea earnings contribution: The first driver is equity income from OneSubsea, which contributed NOK 749.00 million to 2025 net income, with a high plausibility of growth driven by a USD 9.00 billion cumulative booking target
2.
Integration risk failure: The mechanism by which OneSubsea would fail to deliver is higher-than-expected integration and restructuring costs depressing joint venture margins below the historical 19.40% level
3.
Renewable backlog expansion: The second driver is the execution of offshore wind and carbon capture projects, which must replace oil and gas revenues in the Renewables and Field Development segment
4.
Highly questionable profitability: The plausibility of this driver is low, as demonstrated by NOK 600.00 million in gross provisions on loss-making offshore wind projects in the first half of 2024
5.
Contract risk failure: The failure mechanism is the potential for further cost overruns on fixed-price EPC contracts, which carry asymmetric downside risk
6.
Emerging transition opportunities: The fourth driver is the expansion of emerging low-carbon technologies, such as carbon capture and hydrogen engineering studies, which management expects to scale in Europe and APAC
7.
Highly speculative wind economics: The plausibility of offshore wind economics remains highly speculative, as illustrated by global offshore wind challenges where developers are postponing bottom-fixed projects due to marginal profitability

Bull Case Aggregation and Dependencies

8.
Reimbursable Life Cycle services: The third driver is brownfield maintenance and modification contracts in the Life Cycle segment, which generated NOK 15.01 billion in revenue in 2025
9.
Moderate brownfield visibility: The plausibility of this driver is medium, supported by long-term frame agreements providing visibility, although limited by the small overall scale of the Norwegian Continental Shelf
10.
Customer spending failure: This driver would fail if key operators, particularly Equinor and Aker BP, significantly reduce their brownfield capital budgets in response to falling commodity prices
11.
Aggregated bull case dependencies: For the overall bull case to work, all three drivers must fire simultaneously under a compound probability model because OneSubsea's growth cannot offset the massive volume decline in core offshore engineering
12.
Other side of the story: The bull case could unexpectedly materialize if Equinor reverses its decision and accelerates the final investment decision for the Wisting field development before late 2027, revitalizing the oil and gas pipeline
