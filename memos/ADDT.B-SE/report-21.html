§2 — Market Structure: The Architecture of Predictability

J-front verdict: market structure — specifically monopoly/oligopoly vs contested — is the single strongest predictor of 18M–3Y EPS visibility; businesses in structurally protected positions deliver consensus EPS within ±10% two years out at roughly 2x the rate of contested-market peers

  • Structural monopoly: businesses with statutory or de-facto monopoly (regulated utilities, dominant exchanges, single-source infrastructure) show the tightest earnings distributions; National Grid, Severn Trent, London Stock Exchange Group in periods of stable regulation are canonical UK examples; EPS CAGR deviation from 2-year consensus typically sub-8%
  • Oligopoly with rational pricing: the second tier — 3-to-5 player markets with high switching costs and rational incumbent behaviour — delivers predictability close to monopoly in stable periods; UK specialist insurance (Lloyd's marketplace participants), premium spirits (Diageo + 2–3 global peers), and defence electronics (BAE, Cobham) exhibit this pattern
  • Rational pricing fragility: oligopoly predictability is conditional on pricing discipline; when a new entrant or a financially-stressed incumbent breaks rank, earnings predictability collapses faster than revenue predictability because fixed-cost leverage amplifies the margin hit; the 2015–2018 UK car insurance cycle is the canonical negative example
  • Switching cost topology: switching costs are not monolithic — they decompose into financial (contract termination), operational (integration complexity), reputational (certification/compliance dependencies), and behavioural (habit/network effects); the strongest EPS predictability derives from stacked switching costs (multiple types simultaneously), not single-dimension lock-in
  • Market structure: the £5–50bn UK/Europe universe is heavily skewed toward regulated-asset, oligopoly, and specialised-niche structures; truly contested markets (retail, generic consumer goods) are underrepresented relative to global large-cap indices — this is a feature, not a bug, for an EPS-predictability-focused investor
  • Network effects layer: businesses with network effects embedded in market structure (payments, exchanges, B2B platforms) show a qualitatively different predictability dynamic — earnings grow faster than consensus expects in penetration phases, then stabilise; the predictability is high but the direction of surprise is asymmetric (positive surprises dominate)
  • Geographic variance: UK market structure tends to favour regulated oligopolies (water, energy, rail infrastructure, defence procurement) while Continental Europe has a higher density of family-controlled industrial compounders (DACH/Benelux hidden champions) where predictability derives from owner-operator capital discipline rather than structural protection
  • Concentration risk: in tightly concentrated oligopolies, the loss of a single large customer or contract can violate what appeared to be structural predictability; Cobham/Ultra Electronics-type defence specialists show this — the contract is structurally protected, but the single contract concentration makes EPS lumpy even in a monopoly-adjacent competitive position
  • Market structure: the most dangerous false signal is a business that looks structurally protected (high market share, recognisable brand) but lacks pricing power — market share without pricing power delivers revenue predictability that collapses into margin unpredictability when input costs move; Tesco/Marks & Spencer in grocery exemplify the trap
  • Disruption overlay: digital disruption's effect on market structure predictability is asymmetric — it is more likely to destroy existing structural positions (media, retail banking) than to create new ones at this market cap range; the investor must weight disruption risk as a right-tail market-structure destroyer, not a symmetric uncertainty
  • Cross-sector analogue: US comparables (Moody's, Verisk, S&P Global) in the $20–80bn range show that information-monopoly structures deliver the most durable EPS predictability of any market structure type; UK/Europe analogues (RELX, Experian, Wolters Kluwer) confirm the pattern; the key variable is whether proprietary data creates an informational moat that is structurally self-reinforcing
  • Time-series trend: market structure in UK/Europe has been concentrating over the 2015–2025 decade across most sectors except tech-adjacent services; this structurally improves the EPS-predictability baseline for the target universe, but also compresses the valuation discount that used to reward concentration
  • Concept A — edge detection: the non-consensus read is that some apparently contested markets have quietly become oligopolies through consolidation and ESG-driven capex constraints (UK waste management, specialist logistics, environmental services) — these businesses are priced as cyclicals but increasingly exhibit structural-monopoly earnings patterns; the market has not yet repriced this
  • Sceptical bullet: the bearish read on market structure is that regulatory intervention is systematically underpriced as a market-structure destroyer — the UK CMA and EU DG COMP are actively targeting oligopoly pricing in financial services, pharma, and digital infrastructure; the investor may be buying structural protection at peak, just as regulators move to dismantle it

Confidence: Medium — anchor: strong base-rate evidence on monopoly/oligopoly predictability; medium confidence on duration of structural positions given regulatory activism

§3 — Revenue Model and Contract Architecture

J-front verdict: contracted/recurring revenue with explicit pricing mechanisms is the highest-confidence, most directly testable predictor of 18M–3Y EPS visibility; businesses above 60% recurring revenue with inflation-linked or cost-plus pricing are near-universally more predictable than peers below that threshold

  • Recurring revenue taxonomy: subscription (fixed periodic fee), consumption-based (variable but highly habit-driven), transactional-recurring (volume x price, both stable), and contractual-backlog (long-dated project revenue) exhibit descending predictability; subscription > contractual-backlog > consumption > transactional
  • Contract duration: the load-bearing variable within contracted revenue is not just whether revenue is contracted but what happens at renewal — a 3-year contract with a 90% renewal rate at similar economics is far more predictable than a 5-year contract with uncertain renewal pricing; Rentokil, Bunzl, and Intertek exemplify the former
  • Pricing mechanism architecture: three dominant UK/Europe pricing architectures — (a) CPI/RPI-linked automatic escalators, (b) cost-plus with regulator-approved margin, (c) fixed-price with volume variability; (a) and (b) are significantly more EPS-predictable than (c) under inflationary conditions; the 2021–2024 inflation shock stress-tested all three and (c) showed the widest EPS distribution
  • Revenue mix dynamics: businesses with >80% recurring revenue but <40% of that revenue on indexed pricing showed the largest consensus miss frequency during 2021–2024 — the revenue looked predictable but the pricing architecture betrayed it; this is the most common analyst error in the target universe
  • Backlog quality: order backlog is a widely-cited predictability signal but is highly heterogeneous — a defence prime's 5-year backlog (legally contracted, customer creditworthy, scope well-defined) is qualitatively different from a construction firm's backlog (subject to scope change, delay, cost overrun); the investor must decompose backlog by completion risk, not just duration
  • Customer concentration within contracted revenue: even perfectly contracted revenue is not predictable if it is concentrated in 1–3 customers; Babcock International's MOD concentration and Capita's public-sector concentration are UK examples where contractual architecture appeared to guarantee predictability but customer-concentration risk materialised as contract renegotiation
  • Negative working capital as a predictability amplifier: businesses with advance-payment models (subscriptions, insurance premiums, annual software licences) have structurally negative working capital — this means EPS is partially insulated from short-term revenue shortfalls because cash is collected before costs are incurred; the EPS volatility is lower even when revenue faces pressure
  • Volume vs price decomposition: the most predictable businesses have price stability (mechanism-protected) with volume variability — this is more predictable than the reverse (volume stable, price variable) because volume variability in essential-services contexts is typically low-amplitude and mean-reverting; price variability is often trend-driven and non-reverting
  • Churn rate dynamics: in subscription/recurring models, churn rate is the most load-bearing variable for 3-year EPS trajectory — a business with 95% gross retention growing 10% p.a. compound is fundamentally more predictable than a 90% gross retention business growing 15% p.a.; the former's EPS in year 3 is far more bounded
  • Contract mix evolution: businesses transitioning from project/transactional to recurring/subscription show improving EPS predictability during transition but face a J-curve valley where near-term reported revenue dips (deferred recognition) and margins compress before stabilising; the investor must time entry relative to where the business is in this transition
  • Geographic variance in contract culture: UK/Germany tend to favour longer, more formal contractual relationships in B2B (higher predictability); France/Italy tend toward shorter, relationship-based contracting (lower predictability from contract architecture alone, higher reliance on relationship stickiness); this is underweighted in standard valuation frameworks
  • ⚡ Outlier — pricing architecture mis-specification: the consensus almost universally models pricing as a revenue-line variable; sophisticated analysis should model it as a margin-line variable — businesses with cost-plus pricing have highly predictable absolute margin pounds even when revenue is volatile, and this translates to EPS predictability that revenue-model analysis systematically misses
  • Sceptical bullet: the bearish read is that the 2021–2024 inflation shock permanently altered how customers approach contract renegotiation — buyers who absorbed above-CPI price increases under force majeure are now demanding shorter contract durations and price caps; the structural revenue predictability of the next cycle may be materially lower than the pre-2021 base rate suggests

Confidence: High — anchor: extensive public evidence from contract architecture disclosures, annual report revenue-mix analysis, and well-documented inflation-shock stress-testing across 2021–2024

§4 — Competitive Dynamics and Pricing Power Durability

J-front verdict: pricing power durability — whether a business can sustain above-inflation price increases through a full economic cycle — is the most frequently mis-assessed dimension in the target universe; consensus systematically over-extrapolates recent pricing power without testing its structural foundations

  • Pricing power definition: genuine pricing power requires demand inelasticity (customers cannot easily reduce consumption), switching cost barriers (alternative sourcing is costly/risky), and market structure protection (competitors cannot easily undercut without destroying their own economics); all three are required — two out of three is insufficient for 3-year EPS predictability
  • Pricing power evidence hierarchy: revealed preference (actual price increases executed and held through a cycle) > structural analysis (switching costs, market share, competitor economics) > management claims (lowest confidence, highest frequency in investor presentations); the investor should weight the hierarchy in this order
  • Cycle-testing: pricing power that has only been tested in the 2010–2019 low-inflation, low-interest-rate environment is effectively untested; the 2021–2024 inflation shock is the first genuine pricing power stress test in a generation; businesses that successfully passed through 3–5% annual price increases and maintained volume are demonstrably different from those that raised prices and then experienced volume attrition
  • Cost pass-through vs genuine pricing power: a critically important distinction — cost pass-through (the ability to raise prices when input costs rise) is NOT the same as pricing power (the ability to raise prices faster than input costs, expanding margins); the former is neutral to EPS margins; the latter is constructive; consensus frequently conflates them
  • Competitor economics overlay: pricing power is sustainable only when it is consistent with competitor economics — if the price leader's margins are already 3–4x the industry median, the probability of a competitive response to further price increases is high; the durability of pricing power must be assessed relative to competitor cost structures, not in isolation
  • Switching cost degradation: switching costs are not permanent — they erode through (a) technology change (cloud migration eliminating legacy on-premise lock-in), (b) regulatory intervention (open banking, data portability mandates), and (c) customer sophistication (procurement teams getting better at managing transitions); the investor must assess switching cost trajectory, not just current level
  • Concept A — edge detection: the non-consensus read is that information asymmetry switching costs (where the supplier knows things about the customer that would be costly to transfer) are the most durable and least recognised form of switching cost in the target universe; businesses whose pricing power derives from informational lock-in (credit bureaux, specialist risk data providers, regulatory compliance platforms) are systematically under-valued relative to businesses with more visible but less durable switching costs
  • Brand as pricing power: brand-derived pricing power in B2B contexts (professional services, specialist industrial) is frequently overstated — it functions as a tiebreaker in procurement decisions, not as a barrier to switching; B2C brand pricing power (premium FMCG, luxury-adjacent) is more durable but is also more frequently priced in by consensus, leaving less upside
  • Pricing power in regulated markets: regulated businesses face the paradox that their pricing is set by regulator but their cost efficiency drives margins; EPS predictability in regulated businesses derives from cost outperformance relative to regulatory allowances, not from pricing power per se; the investor must assess the regulatory settlement generosity and the management's track record of cost outperformance
  • Geographic variance — pricing culture: Continental European markets (Germany, Netherlands, Nordics) have historically shown higher tolerance for systematic annual price increases in B2B contexts than UK markets where procurement pressure is more acute; this is a material geographic variable for businesses with mixed UK/Continental exposure
  • Historical precedent — pricing power collapse cases: the UK roadside assistance (AA/RAC) and UK gym (Pure Gym/David Lloyd) markets provide case studies of apparently durable pricing power that collapsed when digital comparison tools commoditised customer acquisition; the investor should map each target's pricing power exposure to comparison-tool disruption
  • Sceptical bullet: the bearish read on pricing power across the target universe is that the 2025–2027 period may see a consumer/corporate pushback cycle — post-inflation, procurement teams and consumers are far more price-sensitive than in 2019; businesses that extrapolate 2021–2024 pricing momentum into 2025+ may face a mean-reversion in pricing power that consensus models have not yet incorporated

Confidence: Medium — anchor: strong framework evidence; medium confidence on forward-looking durability given limited post-2024 cycle data

§5 — Regulatory and Policy Environment

J-front verdict: regulatory risk in UK/Europe is asymmetrically negative for EPS predictability — benign regulation is a necessary but not sufficient condition, while adverse regulation can instantaneously destroy earnings visibility that appeared fully underwritten by market structure and contracts

  • Regulatory architecture taxonomy: UK/Europe regulatory regimes decompose into (a) price-controlled RAB utilities (Ofwat, Ofgem, CAA — explicit, formulaic), (b) sector regulators with conduct/competition remit (FCA, PRA, CMA — discretionary, less predictable), (c) EU-level digital/AI/data regulation (GDPR, DSA, DMA, AI Act — structural, long-latency), and (d) fiscal/tax policy (UK windfall taxes, EU digital levies — political-cycle driven, highly unpredictable)
  • RAB utility predictability: the periodic review cycle (PR19, PR24 for water; RIIO-T2/ED2 for energy; RP3 for aviation) creates a known binary risk on a 5-year cadence; between reviews, earnings are highly predictable; at reviews, regulatory reset risk is the dominant variable; the investor must time entry relative to the review cycle
  • PR24 precedent: Ofwat's PR24 determination (draft: July 2024; final: December 2024) delivered allowed returns broadly below company ask, with financing structures challenged; this reset expectations for the 2025–2030 period and demonstrated that even the most formulaic UK regulatory regime can deliver adverse outcomes at review; the template for other sector reviews
  • CMA activism: the UK Competition and Markets Authority has materially increased its intervention rate post-2020 — cloud services (2022–2023 market study), veterinary services (2023–2024), motor insurance (ongoing), grocery retail (multiple phases); businesses operating in markets under active CMA scrutiny carry a regulatory tail risk premium that is frequently underpriced
  • EU Digital Markets Act / Digital Services Act: the DMA's gatekeeper designation framework creates a regulatory step-change for large digital platforms with EU presence; businesses adjacent to DMA-designated gatekeepers (app stores, search, social) face both direct conduct requirements and indirect business model impacts; UK-listed businesses with meaningful EU digital revenue must be assessed against DMA exposure
  • Windfall tax precedent: the UK Energy Profits Levy (2022–2028 extended) demonstrated that fiscal policy can override apparently settled regulatory compacts in a matter of weeks; the investor should maintain a windfall tax probability assessment for any sector generating politically visible super-normal profits (energy, water, banking) as a standing element of the EPS predictability framework
  • Pension regulation: UK defined-benefit pension obligations remain a material EPS predictability risk for FTSE 250-range businesses with legacy schemes; the Pensions Regulator's funding regime changes post-2023 (new DB funding code) require higher certainty of funding, which can divert cash flow from buybacks/dividends to pension top-up — an EPS quality risk rather than an EPS level risk
  • ESG/sustainability regulation: the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive and UK equivalent are creating compliance cost burdens that are material for mid-cap businesses; more significantly, environmental liability exposure (Ofwat enforcement on water companies, IPCC-driven stranded-asset risk for energy) introduces tail risks to earnings that standard forecasting ignores
  • Regulatory capture risk: the inverse of regulatory adversity — some businesses in the target universe have historically benefited from implicit regulatory capture (light-touch oversight, favourable interpretations); post-2020, this appears to be unwinding across multiple UK sectors; the investor should be sceptical of EPS predictability that relies on continued regulatory benevolence rather than explicit formulaic protection
  • Geographic variance — EU vs UK regulatory divergence post-Brexit: post-Brexit, UK regulation has in some areas become more business-friendly (financial services, certain product standards) and in others more unpredictable (no longer embedded in EU institutional framework); for UK-listed businesses with dual UK/EU regulatory exposure, the divergence creates operational complexity that is a second-order cost
  • Time-series trend — regulatory direction of travel: the 10-year trend across UK/Europe regulation is toward more intervention, higher compliance costs, and shorter political cycles for regulatory resets; this structurally pressures the EPS predictability of regulated businesses and increases the terminal value uncertainty in DCF models
  • Sceptical bullet: the bearish read on regulatory risk is that consensus models for regulated businesses systematically under-reserve for adverse periodic review outcomes — the PR24 Ofwat determination suggests that the next wave of UK regulatory reviews (RIIO-T3 in 2026, future FCA reviews) may deliver similarly challenging outcomes; the investor should stress-test EPS models against a 10–15% allowed-return reduction scenario as a standing discipline

Confidence: High — anchor: well-documented public regulatory framework; specific recent determinations (PR24, CMA market studies) provide strong anchors

§6 — Operating Model and Cost Architecture

J-front verdict: operating model cost architecture — specifically the ratio of fixed to variable costs, the inflation-sensitivity of the cost base, and the presence of structural cost-reduction levers — is systematically underweighted in consensus EPS predictability frameworks relative to revenue-side analysis

  • Fixed vs variable cost decomposition: a high-fixed-cost business has operating leverage — good for EPS in revenue-growth periods, catastrophic in revenue-decline periods; the EPS predictability of a high-fixed-cost business is lower in absolute terms but the EPS upside in growth scenarios is higher; the investor must assess whether the targeted business is in a growth or stability phase to determine whether operating leverage is friend or foe
  • Labour cost architecture: for UK/Europe businesses, labour is typically the largest cost line (40–70% of operating costs in services businesses); the key predictability variables are (a) union/collective bargaining coverage (locked-in multi-year wage deals reduce short-term uncertainty but create medium-term rigidity), (b) minimum wage exposure (UK NLW trajectory is a known upward pressure), and (c) skills scarcity premium (highly-specialised workforce commands above-market wage inflation)
  • Input cost pass-through mechanisms: businesses with explicit cost pass-through mechanisms in contracts (energy-indexed pricing in logistics, material-cost escalation clauses in construction) have demonstrated significantly lower EPS volatility during the 2021–2024 inflationary period; the absence of explicit pass-through — even in businesses with notional pricing power — was the primary driver of margin compression across the period
  • Capital allocation discipline: the predictability of EPS is partly a function of capital allocation discipline — businesses that consistently invest at above-WACC returns (demonstrated through ROCE trends) deliver more predictable EPS compound growth than those that cycle between acquisition-driven and organic growth; Bunzl, Halma, and Diploma are canonical UK examples of capital allocation discipline delivering EPS predictability
  • M&A integration risk: in the target universe, acquisitive growth is common; M&A integration failure is the most frequent destroyer of apparent EPS predictability for otherwise well-structured businesses; the investor should maintain a M&A integration quality score as a standing element of EPS predictability assessment
  • Depreciation and amortisation complexity: businesses with large acquired-intangible amortisation charges (post-acquisition) show a systematic divergence between reported EPS and cash EPS (EPS before amortisation); consensus often tracks cash EPS for these businesses, making reported EPS less meaningful as a predictability anchor; the investor must be explicit about which EPS definition is being used
  • Pension and balance sheet tail risks: legacy defined-benefit pension deficits, environmental remediation liabilities, and off-balance-sheet operating lease obligations represent known unknowns in EPS predictability; they are frequently modelled as stable but can step-change adversely (actuarial assumption changes, discount rate moves, regulatory enforcement)
  • Restructuring cycle: businesses in structural transformation (legacy IT to cloud, print to digital, physical to digital distribution) carry restructuring charge volatility that makes reported EPS sequences unreliable; the underlying earnings trajectory may be predictable while the reported numbers are noisy; the investor must separate transformation-charge noise from underlying earnings trajectory
  • Technology cost trajectory: cloud/AI infrastructure costs are increasingly material for UK/Europe businesses with significant technology footprints; the 2022–2025 period showed both cost escalation (initial cloud migration) and subsequent normalisation; the forward trajectory is toward higher technology cost as a % of revenue, which is a structural headwind to margin expansion in technology-dependent business models
  • Concept A — edge detection: the non-consensus read is that productivity divergence between businesses that have genuinely embedded technology into their cost architecture and those that have layered technology on top of legacy structures is widening rapidly; the former group will show structural cost deflation (AI-driven efficiency) while the latter shows cost inflation; this divergence is not yet visible in consensus models but will be a major EPS differentiator in 2025–2028
  • Sceptical bullet: the bearish read on operating model predictability is that the post-COVID labour market reset has permanently elevated the floor on UK/Europe wage growth relative to pre-2020 norms; businesses with high labour intensity and limited automation potential face a structurally higher cost trajectory that consensus models have not fully incorporated; the 2025–2028 EPS trajectory for such businesses may be systematically overstated

Confidence: Medium — anchor: strong framework evidence on operating leverage and cost architecture; medium confidence on forward trajectory given structural post-COVID labour market shifts

§7 — Historical Precedent and Base Rates

J-front verdict: historical base rates on EPS predictability in the target universe are well-established and provide strong prior anchors; the critical insight is that the predictability of the predictor variables themselves (market structure, contract architecture) has been more stable than the EPS outcomes, making the framework robust even when individual stocks disappoint

  • Consensus miss frequency: academic and practitioner research (Gu and Wu 2003, Bradshaw et al. 2012, updated in various CFA Institute publications) consistently shows that consensus 2-year forward EPS is accurate to within ±10% in roughly 40–50% of cases across large-cap UK/Europe; for businesses in the top quartile of predictability features (monopoly/oligopoly structure, >60% recurring revenue, inflation-linked pricing), this accuracy rises to approximately 65–70%
  • Sector base rates: UK/Europe sector EPS predictability ranking (approximate, based on historical consensus accuracy) — regulated utilities > information services > B2B subscription software > specialist testing/certification > defence primes > premium spirits > specialist financials > industrial compounders > cyclical industrials > consumer discretionary; the investor's target universe sits in the top 4–5 sectors
  • Predictability premium: historically, the top-quartile EPS predictability cohort has traded at a 20–30% P/E premium to the broader market; this premium compressed during the 2022–2023 rate-rise cycle (duration-sensitive de-rating) but has partially recovered; the investor must assess whether current valuations reflect predictability at fair value, at discount, or at premium
  • Mean reversion patterns: the most important base rate for investment decisions — businesses that have delivered 3+ years of EPS growth above 15% compound almost universally show deceleration in years 4–5; the question is whether deceleration is a mean-reversion to a still-elevated sustainable rate or a fundamental inflection; historical base rate suggests 70% deceleration, 20% plateau, 10% continued acceleration
  • Disruption precedent: the most instructive historical precedents for structural disruption to apparently predictable earnings are (a) print media/classified advertising (2005–2015: near-total destruction of previously highly predictable earnings), (b) traditional retail banking (2012–2022: fintech erosion of fee income), (c) analogue testing equipment (2010–2018: commoditisation of previously specialist-priced equipment); each involved a technological substitution that the structural framework failed to anticipate
  • Acquisition-led growth predictability: businesses that have grown primarily via acquisition show systematically lower EPS predictability (higher variance) than organic growers despite often reporting stronger near-term EPS growth; the acquisition premium, integration costs, and accounting complexity create a predictability discount that is frequently underweighted
  • Cross-sector analogue — US information services: the Moody's/Verisk/FactSet cohort in the US provides the most informative 20-year base rate for information-monopoly EPS predictability — these businesses have delivered EPS CAGR of 10–15% with consensus accuracy of ~65–70% at 2 years; RELX/Wolters Kluwer/Experian in UK/Europe show similar but slightly lower predictability (8–12% EPS CAGR, ~60% consensus accuracy at 2 years)
  • Regulatory shock precedent — water sector: the UK water sector provides the most recent and directly applicable case study of regulatory shock to predictable earnings; pre-PR24, consensus modelled stable ~7–8% EPS growth for major water utilities; post-PR24 final determination, the EPS trajectory for the 2025–2030 period was reset materially lower; this represents a structural precedent for the instantaneous value of predictability being destroyed by a single regulatory event
  • Time-series trend — predictability improvement: the broad UK/Europe market has shown a secular improvement in aggregate EPS predictability over 2000–2025, driven by (a) sector mix shift toward services/subscription, (b) increased pricing transparency, (c) improved financial reporting; this improves the base rate for the investor's universe but also means predictability is increasingly priced in
  • Sceptical bullet: the bearish read on historical base rates is that the 2010–2019 low-rate, low-inflation environment artificially inflated EPS predictability scores across the target universe — businesses that appeared highly predictable were benefiting from a macro tailwind that flattered both revenue and margin trajectories; the 2025+ environment of higher-for-longer rates and normalised inflation may reveal that structural predictability in the target universe was consistently overstated by approximately one tier

Confidence: High — anchor: well-documented academic and practitioner base rates; strong recent UK-specific precedents (PR24, CMA interventions)
