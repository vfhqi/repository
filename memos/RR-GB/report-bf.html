EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (BLUF)

Rolls-Royce operates as a global engineering group across three core divisions: Civil Aerospace generating 52% of revenue with 14,263 in-service engines powering four out of five new-generation widebody platforms, Defence contributing 24% with a UK nuclear submarine monopoly, and Power Systems delivering 24% through high-speed diesel and gas engines for mission-critical applications. The company employs over 43,000 people globally with an £88.1bn total order backlog providing vast multi-year visibility. Civil Aerospace operates a razor-razorblade model, typically selling original equipment at low or negative margins and relying on long-term service agreements for lifetime profitability, making the model highly sensitive to short-term disruptions in air traffic. Services account for 69% of Civil Aerospace revenue, primarily driven by engine flying hours under TotalCare contracts that transfer time-on-wing and shop-visit cost risks to Rolls-Royce in exchange for predictable, recurring airline payments. Defence equipment is sold profitably with stable, fixed margins, as military customers prioritize self-sufficiency and in-house maintenance, leading to a higher mix of profitable upfront equipment sales.

Structural demand drivers and secular tailwinds

Power Systems is experiencing explosive growth, with power generation orders surging 50% year-over-year in Q1 2026, driven by hyperscalers demanding high-integrity prime and back-up power for massive AI infrastructure build-outs, where the division holds a dominant 20-25% market share in mission-critical back-up systems for global data centers. Geopolitical tensions and the Ukraine conflict have catalyzed a 15% order intake increase in governmental Power Systems, with major recent platform wins including EJ200 engines for European Eurofighters and F-130 engines for the US B-52 bomber fleet. The global commercial aftermarket is expanding rapidly, with 2026 size estimates recently upgraded from $100bn to $135bn, while global air transport demand is projected to double by 2050, representing a 3.1% CAGR that outpaces baseline economic growth. European defense spending reached a record €343bn in 2024, providing a massive 19% year-over-year tailwind for military aerospace providers, while prolonged delivery delays of new aircraft have structurally aged the global fleet, driving elevated demand for high-margin MRO services and spare parts.

Customer relationships and switching costs

Once an engine is integrated onto a specific airframe, switching to a competitor is virtually impossible for operators, as airlines are entirely captive to the OEM for the 25-30 year lifespan of the certified aircraft. At the OE stage, Rolls-Royce is entirely beholden to Airbus and Boeing for new commercial aircraft placements, with being sole-source on the A350 tying Rolls-Royce's widebody volume growth entirely to Airbus's ability to navigate its own supply chain constraints. Rolls-Royce's exclusive focus on widebody platforms excludes it from the highest-volume segment of the commercial aerospace market, as the narrowbody segment accounts for 76% of the global aircraft fleet, a massive profit pool currently monopolized by CFM and Pratt & Whitney. The Middle East represents a concentrated demand node, accounting for 10-15% of Rolls-Royce's widebody engine flying hours, meaning any geopolitical disruption in this region uniquely impacts Rolls-Royce's cash flows compared to peers with heavier domestic narrowbody exposure.

Operator quality and transformation execution

Disruption forces and long-term growth adjacencies

Developing a new commercial engine requires an upfront investment exceeding $10bn, severely limiting the threat of new entrants, as decades-long product lifecycles and steep learning curves mean that unproven startups cannot realistically contest the prime engine integration stage. GE and Safran's RISE project targets a 20% fuel burn reduction, potentially capturing narrowbody dominance if Airbus adapts airframes, as the open-rotor design completely eliminates the fan casing, offering immense propulsive efficiency but introducing critical noise and safety challenges. Rolls-Royce is leveraging its submarine nuclear expertise to build a highly differentiated Small Modular Reactor business, perfectly targeting the global energy transition by providing a capital-light route to emission-free, reliable baseload electricity, with management estimating a total addressable market of more than 400 SMR units globally by 2050. Having exited the narrowbody segment in 2012, Rolls-Royce structurally misses out on the industry's largest and fastest-growing profit pool, as narrowbody aircraft are projected to double by 2050, representing a massive ~$50bn annual market effectively locked by competitors, though management is actively pursuing a return to narrowbody propulsion, utilizing UltraFan technology to target next-generation platforms launching around 2038.
