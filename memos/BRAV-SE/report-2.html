§1 — INTERNAL CHANGE FORCES (Leadership, Strategy, Restructuring)

Verdict: [#J] Internal change forces are net positive and accelerating — the Sweden restructuring and Denmark turnaround are the two most tangible company-specific drivers of margin improvement in the 12-24 month horizon, operating largely independently of the cycle.

CEO continuity: [#J] Mattias Johansson's 11-year tenure as CEO is primarily a stability asset — consistent strategy execution (branch density, service mix shift, M&A cadence) without strategic drift; the risk is institutional conservatism slowing response to ASCA scale challenge. [#A] Johansson stated post-Q1 2026: "positive organic growth after two years of defensive positioning is a testament to our strategic focus on margin protection and cost efficiency" — language signals discipline maintained through cycle. [#I] Appointed 2014; no succession signals visible in public filings.
- Tenure advantage: continuity of branch network strategy (Bravida Range, centralised procurement, service mix targets) — no re-learning cost.
- Tenure risk: ASCA integration is moving at pace; Johansson's decade-long "steady state" posture may under-resource the competitive response.
- No capital markets day scheduled publicly in 2026 — absence of investor guidance event limits strategic signalling.

Restructuring savings: [#J] Sweden one-division consolidation (Jan 2026) is structurally significant — it eliminates duplicated regional management layers and aligns procurement/HR/IT overhead across 95 Swedish acquisitions historically run semi-autonomously. [#A] SEK 65m annual savings targets administration, management headcount, and overlapping back-office — not front-line technician numbers; does not impair service capacity. [#I] SEK 70-90m one-off restructuring cost (FY2026); SEK 20m absorbed Q1; SEK 50-60m expected Q2-Q3.
- Transmission mechanism: Restructuring savings → permanent operating cost reduction → EBITA margin expansion of ~35-40bp at current revenue base; if revenue base recovers as cycle turns, operating leverage magnifies to ~50-70bp incremental EBITA margin contribution.
- Timing is deliberate: clean run-rate cost base arrives in Q4 2026 / Q1 2027 precisely as Swedish construction volumes are forecast to accelerate (+4.2% CAGR 2026-2029).
- Risk: if cycle recovery is slower than forecast, cost savings are real but volume leverage does not materialise — savings become a floor not an amplifier.

Denmark turnaround: [#J] Christian Alsø's execution in Denmark is the most compelling evidence of Bravida management quality in a decade — a 560bp+ margin recovery in 18 months is operationally sophisticated and demonstrates that the Bravida branch model can be fixed when broken. [#A] Turnaround pillars: (1) project portfolio clean-up (run-off of fixed-price contracts signed at wrong prices 2021-22), (2) service mix shift (DK service up from ~40% to ~59%), (3) selectively declining unprofitable new installation bids. [#I] DK EBITA margin progression: FY2023 trough ~0-1%; Q4 2024 4.0%; FY2025 5%; Q4 2025 6.6%; Q4 trajectory implies exit rate above group average.
- Transmission mechanism: DK EBITA normalisation from trough → adds ~SEK 100-150m of recoverable group EBITA vs 2023 base; Denmark is ~12-13% of group revenue (~SEK 3.3-3.5bn).
- Alsø has no public succession risk; appointed 2023 and mid-turnaround — high retention probability.
- Remaining risk: some unprofitable legacy projects still in DK backlog as of Q1 2025 commentary; completion risk could cause brief Q2-Q3 2025 lumpiness (management guided this out for FY2026).

Project selectivity post-2022: [#J] Deliberate margin-over-volume discipline (declining fixed-price installation bids with insufficient margin) has structurally improved BRAV's project quality relative to 2021-22; FY2025 EBITA margin improvement (+80bp vs FY2024) despite -2% organic revenue is direct confirmation. [#A] Strategy explicitly articulated: "prioritise margin over volume"; group EBITA margin improved in Sweden, Norway, Denmark YoY despite volume contraction in all geographies. [#I] Q1 2025 EBITA margin 4.5% vs 4.0% Q1 2024; FY2025 group EBITA margin ~5%.
- Risk: as cycle recovers, competitive pressure to chase volumes re-emerges — the discipline test is whether BRAV maintains selectivity in a rising market.

Sceptical counter (§1): Johansson's strategy is a known formula (density + service + M&A) that ASCA now executes with 50% more capital. Denmark's 6.6% exit rate masks the fact that Q4 is seasonally Bravida's strongest quarter; FY2025 at 5% is not exceptional. Restructuring savings of SEK 65m are 0.4% of revenues — margin-accretive but not transformative.

§2 — DEMAND ENVIRONMENT & CYCLE POSITION

Verdict: [#J] The Nordic construction cycle has turned — Q1 2026 backlog and order intake data confirm inflection — but the recovery is uneven (Sweden/Denmark/Norway ahead; Finland lagging) and the shape (gradual 4-5% CAGR not a V-shaped snap-back) means volume leverage arrives slowly, not in a single quarter.

Cycle position: [#J] Bravida entered 2026 past the worst of the down-cycle; organic growth returned Q1 2026 for the first time in eight quarters; the company is positioned near the start of a multi-year installation recovery rather than mid-cycle. [#A] Sweden residential construction bottomed 2023-24; forecast +4.2% CAGR 2026-2029. Norway construction +3.6% CAGR 2026-2029. Denmark +5.8% CAGR to 2030. [#I] Bravida Q1 2026 organic growth +1% vs -6% Q1 2025; backlog SEK 16,727m (+7% YoY); order intake SEK 8,236m (+5%).
- Transmission mechanism: Cycle recovery → installation volumes recover → revenue base expands → operating leverage on fixed cost base (branch overhead, management, procurement) → EBITA margin expansion toward 7%+ target.
- Sweden residential: Riksbank rate at 1.75% (down from 4.0% peak in 2023); mortgage affordability improving; building permits trending up from 2023 trough.
- Infrastructure driver is counter-cyclical to residential: public investment in electricity grid, defence facilities, data centres not correlated to residential permits — provides floor during construction downturns.

Sweden market: [#J] Sweden is Bravida's largest market (~55% of group revenue) and is showing earliest signs of recovery; restructuring savings arrive simultaneously — powerful combination. [#A] Southern Sweden specifically cited as a late-recovering geography (caught up in Q4 2025); order intake growth in Sweden driving group-level backlog expansion. [#I] Sweden Q1 2026: organic growth returned; construction sector CAGR forecast +4.2% 2026-2029.
- Residential recovery is rate-sensitive: if Riksbank needs to re-tighten (e.g., unexpected inflation), recovery could stall at 2-3% vs 4%+ base case.

Norway market: [#J] Norway is the most stable division — margins improved in 2025 through cost discipline despite volume softness; oil/gas-adjacent industrial installations provide a second demand channel not present in Sweden/Denmark. [#A] Norway construction CAGR forecast +3.6% 2026-2029; oil/gas investment stable due to high oil prices. [#I] Norway FY2025: sales declined but margin improved (lower admin costs); no restructuring cost impact.

Denmark market: [#J] Denmark is Bravida's fastest-recovering division on margin; market itself growing at 5.8% CAGR — tailwind to the turnaround is strengthening, not weakening. [#A] Copenhagen infrastructure investment (metro extensions, data centres, renovation mandates) provides non-residential installation demand. [#I] DK Q4 2025 EBITA margin 6.6%; service now ~59% of DK revenue.

Finland market: [#J] ❌ Finland remains the outlier negative — margin 1.3% Q1 2026 despite the fact that order intake (+33%) and backlog (+57%) forward indicators have inflected sharply; 2-3 quarter lag before financial recovery is visible. [#A] Helsinki area and Ostrobothnia cited by management as first signs of genuine demand recovery; write-downs in specific branches FY2025 (branch-level P&L not group-level impairment). [#I] Finland Q1 2026: net sales SEK 537m (-2% YoY); EBITA margin 1.3%; but backlog +57% — leading indicators are strongly positive.
- Transmission mechanism: Finland order backlog surge → revenue recognition begins Q3-Q4 2026 → EBITA margin recovers toward group level by end-2026 / early 2027 — 3-4 quarters lag is normal for installation projects.

Sceptical counter (§2): The Nordic construction recovery is a 4-5% CAGR, not a V-shape; at 4% CAGR, installation volume contribution to revenue growth is limited. BRAV's organic growth in Q1 2026 was +1% — barely above zero and likely benefits from an easy comparable. Infrastructure tailwinds (data centres, defence) are real but represent a small share of total installation volume; their share of Bravida's backlog is not disclosed.

§3 — COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE CHANGES (incl. Assemblin-Caverion)

Verdict: [#J] The Assemblin-Caverion merger is the most significant structural competitive event in Nordic technical services since Bravida's own IPO in 2016; it permanently shifts the competitive equilibrium in large-account and cross-border tendering against BRAV, while simultaneously improving market pricing discipline in small-to-mid ticket service work — net effect is ambiguous but BRAV's relative position has weakened at the top end.

Assemblin impact: [#J] ASCA at SEK 41.1bn revenue (FY2025), 7.9% adjusted EBITA margin, and 21,900 employees across 10 countries is now categorically larger and more profitable than Bravida (SEK ~27-28bn revenue, ~5% EBITA margin); the 1.5x scale advantage is not bridgeable by BRAV organically — M&A must accelerate or the gap widens. [#A] ASCA FY2025: adjusted EBITA SEK 3,238m (margin 7.9%); synergies SEK ~180m p.a. realised; 17 acquisitions in 2025 adding SEK 744m acquired sales. BRAV FY2025: EBITA ~SEK 1.4bn (margin ~5%); 4 acquisitions adding SEK 454m sales. [#I] ASCA formed April 2024; HQ Stockholm; PE-owned (Triton).
- Scale advantage transmits to: (1) larger national framework contracts (ASCA can self-perform more scope), (2) cross-border tendering (ASCA has presence in 10 countries vs BRAV's 4), (3) procurement leverage (ASCA integrating two procurement systems — when complete, potentially matches BRAV's Bravida Range advantage).
- Counter-argument: ASCA is PE-owned (Triton) with integration friction still ongoing — management bandwidth consumed by merger; BRAV is a listed company with operational agility and long-tenured CEO aligned to the Bravida model.
- Transmission mechanism: ASCA competitive pressure → BRAV may lose some large-tender market share → partially offset by improved pricing discipline at mid-market level (fewer desperate small competitors chasing below-cost pricing when top-tier competition is rationalised).

Assemblin impact (M&A pipeline): [#J] ASCA's 17-acquisition pace in 2025 (vs BRAV's 4) suggests ASCA is aggressively consolidating the sub-scale local installer pool that BRAV traditionally targeted; BRAV's M&A pipeline may face higher competition for attractive targets and compressed multiples at the margin. [#A] BRAV historic cadence: 10-15 acquisitions per year (2014-2023 average); 2025 pace (4 deals) is materially below historical rate — market conditions cited, but ASCA competition for targets cannot be ruled out. [#I] BRAV: 166 acquisitions 2014-2025 (SEK 12.5bn acquired sales). ASCA: 17 deals in 2025 alone.
- Positive read: some targets may prefer Bravida (listed acquirer, long-term cultural continuity) over PE-owned ASCA integration uncertainty.

Other competitive dynamics: [#J] Sub-scale independent installers (the traditional BRAV acquisition pool) have been further weakened by 2022-25 margin compression and rising material costs — this increases both BRAV's acquisition pipeline quality (motivated sellers) and its pricing power in service tenders where independents can no longer compete on full lifecycle offering. [#A] Sweden unemployment now 8.2% despite skills shortage in trades — economic softness weakens small operators' ability to retain staff without Bravida-scale retention tools. [#I] Sweden labour market structural mismatch: high general unemployment alongside persistent trades skills shortage.

Sceptical counter (§3): ASCA's 7.9% EBITA margin advantage vs BRAV's 5% may reflect integration-period efficiency improvements and PE-driven cost cutting that is not sustainable at ASCA scale. ASCA being PE-owned means it will eventually IPO or sell — the acquirer may not sustain 17-deal M&A cadence indefinitely. Bravida's local branch model (180+ branches) is inherently harder to replicate at ASCA's centralised integration pace.

§4 — MIX SHIFT & BUSINESS MODEL EVOLUTION (Service/Installation)

Verdict: [#J] The service mix shift from ~48% toward a stated target of ~55% is the most durable, margin-enhancing change force operating within Bravida's direct control; Denmark's success at 59% service demonstrates that the 55% target is achievable across the group — but it is a 3-5 year journey, not a 12-month catalyst.

Mix shift: [#J] Service revenue is structurally higher margin (lower fixed-price risk, recurring framework agreements, multi-year contracts) than installation; every percentage point of mix shift toward service improves EBITA margin resilience in cyclical downturns and commands a higher EV/EBITDA multiple — the shift is therefore both earnings-quality and valuation-quality enhancing. [#A] FY2025: group service revenue held firm while installation declined; overall EBITA margin improved despite revenue contraction — direct empirical evidence that mix shift is working as a margin buffer. [#I] FY2025 service ~50% of group revenue; DK already ~59%; Sweden and Norway ~45-48% (implied).
- Transmission mechanism: Service mix +1pp per year → EBITA margin +10-15bp per year (estimated, based on service premium to installation margin observed across Nordic peers) → accumulated over 3-4 years: ~50bp permanent margin improvement.
- Framework agreements with public-sector and blue-chip clients (hospitals, airports, industrial facilities) are the primary mechanism: multi-year, recurring, index-linked to inflation — near-zero project execution risk.

Mix shift (Bravida Charge): [#J] EV charging infrastructure installation is a net new service revenue category that did not exist at scale pre-2022; Bravida Charge division signals recognition that energy transition creates structurally new service franchises, not just installation volume. [#A] EV charging infrastructure is by definition a service relationship (maintenance contracts, 24/7 monitoring) not a one-time installation — creates recurring revenue. [#I] Bravida Charge launched as named division; specific revenue not disclosed.

Mix shift vs INSTAL peer comparison: [#J] ❌ Bravida's ~50% service mix lags INSTAL's model; however INSTAL FY2025 group EBITA margin 5.9% vs BRAV ~5% — gap is narrowing and INSTAL itself showed margin pressure in 2025; the comparison reveals BRAV is on the right trajectory but execution speed matters. [#A] INSTAL Sweden saw EBITA and margin decline 2025; Finland EBITA margin 8.5% at INSTAL (BRAV Finland 1.3%) — demonstrates the performance ceiling available if BRAV executes well in Finland. [#I] INSTAL FY2025 group EBITA margin 5.9% (vs 6.4% FY2024); order backlog SEK 9,510m.

Sceptical counter (§4): Service mix targets have been cited by Bravida management for multiple years; progress from ~45% (2020) to ~50% (2025) implies only +1pp per annum — at this pace, reaching 55% takes until 2030. Meanwhile ASCA is actively targeting service growth on a larger base. INSTAL's Finland margin (8.5%) vs BRAV Finland (1.3%) reveals that BRAV's service delivery capability in Finland is structurally weak, not just cycle-impaired.

§5 — M&A AS CHANGE FORCE

Verdict: [#J] M&A remains a core value-creation mechanism for Bravida — 166 deals since 2014 at disciplined EV/EBIT multiples (typically 5-8x) — but the pace slowed sharply in 2025 (4 deals vs 10-15 historical average) and ASCA is now a competing consolidator; the M&A engine is running below optimal but the balance sheet (net debt 0.6x EBITDA ex-leases) provides full capacity to re-accelerate.

M&A engine: [#J] Bravida's M&A track record (166 acquisitions over 11 years, SEK 12.5bn acquired sales) is the primary mechanism by which it builds branch density, acquires local customer relationships, and extends the Bravida Range procurement advantage — each new branch is immediately plugged into centralised purchasing, generating material cost savings. [#A] FY2025: 4 acquisitions (SEK 454m acquired sales); management commentary: "interesting acquisition discussions ongoing continuously" but "difficult to identify targets in weak/uncertain market." Q1 2026: Bravida initiated SEK 100m share buyback (1 May - 9 July 2026) — suggests management prioritises buyback over M&A at current valuations. [#I] Net debt ex-leases: SEK 990m = 0.6x EBITDA; cash: SEK 895m; total acquisitions capacity (to 2.0x EBITDA) implies ~SEK 2.0-2.5bn M&A headroom.
- Transmission mechanism: Acquisitions → plug into Bravida Range (cost synergy day 1) → branch achieves Bravida-level margin within 2-3 years → EV/EBIT 5-8x entry → internal IRR ~15-20% (management guided over cycle).
- 2025 slowdown has two potential causes: (1) seller pricing expectations elevated post-2021 M&A boom, (2) ASCA competing for targets. Both resolve over time as cycle resets seller expectations.

M&A pipeline quality: [#J] Sub-scale installers that survived 2022-25 margin compression without cutting corners are higher quality targets — the down-cycle has performed a natural selection function on the target pool. [#A] History shows BRAV has consistently found 10-15 deals per year over an 11-year period across multiple market cycles — discipline has not failed. [#I] Most recent disclosed deal: Elpalko, November 2025.
- Geographic M&A bias: Sweden has 95 of 166 historic deals — most concentrated expansion potential now in Norway, Denmark, and Finland where density is lower.
- ASCA acquiring in Norway Q1 2025 (Åge Nilsen in Northern Norway) — direct M&A market competition is visible.

Sceptical counter (§5): SEK 100m share buyback in May 2026 is small relative to SEK ~14-16bn market cap — more symbolic than capital-returns signalling. More concerning: if management cannot find enough M&A at right prices in a down-cycle (when sellers are typically most motivated), this raises questions about pipeline quality. ASCA's 17 deals in 2025 on the same target universe suggests ASCA is either outbidding BRAV or accessing different targets.

§6 — STRUCTURAL / REGULATORY TAILWINDS & HEADWINDS

Verdict: [#J] Energy transition and EPBD renovation mandates create a durable, non-cyclical demand floor for multi-technical installation services through 2030 that structurally reduces BRAV's worst-case scenario vs prior construction cycles; the labour market paradox (general unemployment high, trades skills scarce) is a persistent headwind to margin but also a structural pricing power enabler for operators with retention tools.

Regulatory tailwind: [#J] EU Energy Performance of Buildings Directive — requiring Member States to transpose by May 2026 and reduce housing stock energy consumption 16% by 2030 vs 2020 — mandates installation work (insulation, heat pumps, smart controls, solar) across the existing building stock; this is the single largest regulatory driver of multi-technical installation demand in Europe in the 2020s. [#A] Nordic countries (Sweden, Norway, Denmark) have ambitious existing-building renovation targets aligned with EPBD; Norway and Denmark already have feed-in incentives for heat pump installation. Bravida's HVAC competence positions it as the natural execution partner for heat pump retrofit rollout. [#I] EU EPBD: national law by May 2026; 16% reduction target by 2030; average Nordic home heating is ~60-70% fossil fuel dependent — retrofit scale is enormous.
- Transmission mechanism: EPBD mandates → building owners required to invest in energy-efficiency upgrades → HVAC, electrical and plumbing installation volumes grow → BRAV as Nordic incumbent with breadth across all four countries captures disproportionate share.

Data centre electrification: [#J] Nordic data centre expansion (Iceland, Sweden, Finland favoured for cooling efficiency and renewable energy) creates multi-year, large-ticket electrical installation contracts at margins that typically exceed residential installation — this is a structural sub-sector growth driver independent of residential cycle. [#A] IEA projects global data centre electricity demand doubling 2022-2026; Nordic location advantage (cold climate = free cooling, renewable grid) makes region a preferred destination. Bravida Q1 2026 commentary explicitly names data centres as a growth vertical. [#I] Bravida has not disclosed data centre revenue share; but cites it as a key segment alongside defence facilities.

Defence infrastructure: [#J] Nordic defence spending increases (Sweden/Finland NATO membership, Norway >2% GDP target) translate directly into installation contracts — military facilities, command centres, hardened infrastructure all require multi-technical installation. [#A] Sweden's NATO accession (March 2024) and Finland's (April 2023) have triggered multi-year defence infrastructure programmes. [#I] Bravida Q1 2026 commentary: "defence facilities" cited as growth vertical alongside data centres and infrastructure.

Labour dynamic: [#J] The Nordic trades labour market paradox — 8.2% general unemployment in Sweden alongside persistent electrician/plumber shortage — cuts two ways: (1) wage cost pressure as trades workers command premiums; (2) pricing power for BRAV because sub-scale competitors cannot retain technicians at scale. [#A] Norway wage increases budgeted at 3.4% for 2026; Denmark real wage growth positive (3.1% pay rise vs 1.4% inflation); Sweden real wage growth barely positive (3.3% pay vs 2.8% inflation). All three markets have structural skills mismatches in trade categories. [#I] Bravida ~12,000 employees; retention at scale is a competitive advantage over single-branch operators.
- Transmission mechanism (positive): BRAV's scale and brand → better technician retention → lower recruitment cost → structural operational advantage in skills-scarce market → pricing power vs sub-scale rivals who cannot staff complex projects.
- Transmission mechanism (negative): Trade wage inflation +3.5% p.a. → labour cost base grows faster than general inflation → margin pressure if not passed through in service contract pricing; risk is highest in Norway (labour cost is largest unit cost component).

Headwind — interest rate sensitivity: [#J] Bravida's residential installation exposure (~35-40% of installation revenue estimated) remains rate-sensitive; if Riksbank or Norges Bank need to re-tighten, the cycle recovery could be shallower than current +4% CAGR forecasts suggest. [#A] Sweden Riksbank rate 1.75% — still below neutral but off peak 4.0%; further easing is conditional on CPI convergence to 2%. [#I] Sweden CPI: 2.8% (2025); modest improvement required for further easing.

Sceptical counter (§6): EPBD implementation timelines in Nordic countries are politically contested; national transposition quality varies. Heat pump retrofit demand is real but funding mechanisms (government subsidies) can be withdrawn — Denmark removed heat pump subsidies in 2023, causing installation volumes to drop sharply before recovering. Data centre growth is real but Bravida's disclosed share of this vertical is unquantified; the market may assign more structural credit to this than delivered revenue warrants.

FULCRUM DRIVER ASSESSMENT

Context: A Fulcrum Driver is a company-specific change force separable from sector beta — a driver of material EPS/margin improvement that BRAV's sector peers do not share.

FD Candidate 1: Centralised Procurement System (Bravida Range + Ivalua platform)
- Evidence for: 25-30% material cost advantage vs sub-scale peers is already embedded in Bravida's cost base; Ivalua digitisation extends this advantage further; ASCA's procurement system is currently fragmented post-merger (integrating two separate platforms) — temporary window in which BRAV's procurement edge is widest.
- Evidence against: Procurement advantage is structural and existing — it is a moat, not a change force unless the Ivalua digitisation step-changes the magnitude of the advantage (not yet confirmed).
- Change dynamic: Ivalua implementation represents the next phase — moving from negotiated framework agreements to fully digitised spend transparency and supplier performance analytics. If this reduces materials cost by a further 3-5%, the EPS impact is material (~SEK 50-100m EBITA uplift on SEK 28bn revenue base).
- Verdict: PLAUSIBLE — but requires evidence that Ivalua digitisation is generating incremental savings (not yet in public data). The advantage is real; whether the current change phase is inflecting it is uncertain.

FD Candidate 2: Denmark Turnaround
- Evidence for: Turnaround is executed, not projected — 560bp margin recovery is in the financial record. Denmark exits 2025 at 6.6% quarterly EBITA margin, above group average. Christian Alsø's service mix restructuring is structural not one-time.
- Evidence against: Q4 2025 6.6% is seasonally strongest quarter; FY2025 5% is more representative. Denmark is ~12-13% of group revenue — full normalisation adds ~SEK 100-150m EBITA to group, or ~70-100bp group EBITA margin uplift. Meaningful but not transformative at group level.
- Verdict: PLAUSIBLE — turnaround is real and company-specific; peer Instalco did not execute an equivalent divisional fix in the same window. Fully realised: adds ~70bp group EBITA margin.

FD Candidate 3: Service Mix Shift toward 55%
- Evidence for: Group margin outperformed group revenue in FY2025 (EBITA grew while revenue fell) — direct evidence that service mix shift is working as a margin-quality enhancer in real time.
- Evidence against: Progress is slow (1pp/year) and shared directionally with all Nordic installation peers (ASCA, INSTAL). Not company-specific in kind, only in pace of execution.
- Verdict: UNCERTAIN — mix shift is value-creating but not differentiated vs peers in direction; only differentiated if BRAV executes faster (3-4 years to 55%) than sector median.

Synthesis / Overall FD Verdict:
- Primary FD: Denmark Turnaround + Sweden Restructuring Savings arriving into cycle recovery — this two-event combination is company-specific (not available to peers), time-specific (both complete by Q4 2026 / Q1 2027), and cycle-timed (savings land as volumes recover). Together they add ~80-120bp to group EBITA margin over 18 months from a purely internal execution base.
- Secondary FD candidate: Centralised Procurement digitisation (Ivalua) — if cost savings evidence emerges, upgrade to primary.
