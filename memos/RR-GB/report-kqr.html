EXECUTIVE SUMMARY 2 findings

Section summary
  1. The key question of whether Rolls-Royce's civil aerospace cash generation recovery is structural or cyclical is partially resolvable with high confidence on the directional trajectory but material uncertainty regarding magnitude and timing of normalisation beyond 2027.

The central investigation determines whether the civil aerospace engine services cash generation recovery extends beyond the current shop-visit catch-up cycle or if it is temporarily amplified by deferred maintenance and fleet age dynamics that will normalise by 2027. The landscape evidence supports a verdict that structural margin improvements are genuine and durable , but the current free cash flow levels remain artificially elevated by cyclical tailwinds that will inevitably moderate. The investment case therefore represents a hybrid structure rather than a pure structural compounder or temporary cyclical trade. The most critical structural supports validated through the research include unprecedented management-led transformation that has already seen civil margins expand dramatically to a 20.5% underlying operating margin , time-on-wing improvements and AI-driven efficiencies that permanently lower maintenance costs against fixed-revenue service agreements , and uncapped escalation clauses tied to inflation which have allowed revenue growth to outpace supply chain cost increases . These foundations represent permanent competitive repositioning rather than transient pandemic recovery effects. However, the research confirms that near-term cash flows are significantly flattered by cyclical factors. The net balance of LTSA creditors will surge to £15.4bn by 2028 as cash collection accelerates due to higher flight hours ahead of actual maintenance outlays , creating a timing mismatch where cash receipts based on fleet growth significantly anticipate profit recognition which only occurs when actual maintenance costs are incurred during shop visits . This accounting dynamic means that current free cash flow overstates sustainable run-rate economics by an estimated £600m to £900m annually.

The fleet age and utilisation dynamics provide substantial insulation against near-term normalisation risks. The global commercial passenger fleet's average age has climbed to 12.5 years in 2026 up significantly from 10.6 years in 2018, while aircraft retirements remain at historic lows of approximately 1.2% for widebodies and 1.6% for narrowbodies in 2026 well below the long-term historical average of 2.5% to 3.5% . Combined with a structural supply deficit of widebody aircraft projected to persist through the end of the decade , these dynamics ensure that aftermarket demand remains elevated through at least 2028 regardless of traffic normalisation scenarios. The shop-visit cycle dynamics reveal the transition point between cyclical peak and structural normalisation. Total shop visits grew 10% to 1,440 in 2025 with management expecting volumes to peak between 1,480 and 1,550 in 2026 before normalising , while time-on-wing extension targets shifted from 40% initial improvement to greater than 100% across in-production Trent engines . This suggests that 2027 represents the critical inflection year where cyclical volumes decline but are offset by structural cost reductions, making cash flow trajectory beyond that horizon the true test of investment durability . The pricing power sustainability remains the most contested element with medium confidence in base case assumptions. Contract margins have improved by 17 percentage points since 2022 driven by new high-margin agreements and systematic renegotiation of older terms , yet airlines push back against rising operational costs

raising questions about execution risk . The means-motive-opportunity test suggests airlines have limited near-term leverage due to fleet constraints but will aggressively renegotiate once aircraft supply normalises post- 2028. The cross-stock implications reveal asymmetric risk distributions across the aerospace value chain. If widebody aftermarket demand normalises sharply MTU Aero Engines faces disproportionate downside risk given its 90% profit exposure to civil aftermarket and acute vulnerability to V2500 retirements , while normalisation that cools aftermarket pricing power would shift margin capture back toward original equipment manufacturers like Airbus who are actively seeking to rebalance the value chain . This dynamic suggests that pure-play aftermarket proxies embed higher terminal risk than integrated manufacturers.

The supply chain paradox represents a critical third-order dynamic where constraints ironically protect margins. In a genuine outlier signal of supply chain desperation lessors are currently tearing down one-year-old Boeing MAX and Airbus Neo aircraft specifically to harvest parts highlighting a severe structural deficit in the spare parts ecosystem , while turnaround times for engine maintenance remain structurally elevated at roughly 180 days ensuring that even if flying hours normalise the MRO backlog will sustain aftermarket volumes well into 2028 . This suggests that supply-side constraints may prove more durable than demand-side headwinds , extending the elevated margin environment beyond consensus expectations. The fundamental unresolvable elements centre on airline utilisation opacity and unpredictable macroeconomic shocks. Rolls-Royce remains highly exposed to the Middle East with 7.6% of its global hours flown in the region during the first quarter of 2026 leaving it vulnerable to prolonged airspace closures , while elevated jet fuel prices which more than doubled in April 2026 due to Middle East tensions could eventually force airlines to accelerate retirement of older less fuel-efficient Trent 700 and RB211 aircraft if the cost environment destroys passenger

demand . These exogenous variables introduce irreducible uncertainty into any multi-year projection.

The capital allocation risks amplify downside scenarios if structural assumptions prove optimistic. Rolls-Royce's recent commitment to a £7bn to £9bn share buyback programme over three years risks stranding capital at cycle-peak valuations rather than insulating the balance sheet against future shocks , particularly if inflation structurally cools while raw material and labour shortages persist creating a severe margin headwind just as the backlog of deferred shop visits reaches its costly peak by 2027 . This timing mismatch creates binary outcome dependency where management's execution in the next eighteen months determines whether capital returns prove accretive or destructive.

1.
Other side of the story: The assumption that 2027 normalisation is inevitable may fundamentally misread the aerospace supply chain where structural skilled labour deficits and fundamental lack of Tier 3 forging capacity may permanently cap aerospace output making the current high-margin low-volume aftermarket paradigm the new permanent reality . If supply constraints prove intractable rather than transient, Rolls-Royce's current valuation may actually understate terminal economics rather than overstate them, inverting the entire cyclical versus structural debate and suggesting the key question itself may be asking the wrong directional assumption

Section 6 – Data gaps 2 findings

Section summary
  1. The resolution of the key question is fundamentally constrained by opacity in airline-level utilisation data , incomplete onerous contract disclosure , and missing granular shop-visit economics , requiring a combination of expert interviews, regulatory filings, and proprietary industry datasets to achieve high-confidence answers on the structural versus cyclical nature of Rolls-Royce's aftermarket recovery.

The central investigation into whether Rolls-Royce's civil aerospace engine services cash generation recovery is structural and durable beyond the current shop-visit catch-up cycle remains partially indeterminate due to critical information asymmetries between management guidance and verifiable operational data. The most material gap centres on airline-specific engine flying hour utilisation patterns at the individual carrier and route level, which would definitively clarify whether the current 115% to 120% of 2019 flying hours represents genuine structural

fleet expansion or merely a temporary reallocation of capacity. Rolls-Royce remains highly exposed to the Middle East, with 7.6% of its global hours flown in the region during the first quarter of 2026 , yet no public disclosure breaks out flying hours by specific airline customer or regional sub-segment beyond aggregate totals, making it impossible to stress-test the impact of prolonged geopolitical disruptions or carrier bankruptcies.

A second fundamental data void exists around the granular economics of individual Long-Term Service Agreement renegotiations , particularly the split between inflation-linked escalators, unbundled life-limited parts pricing, and minimum flight hour guarantees. Most legacy long-term service agreements contain uncapped escalation clauses tied to inflation which have allowed revenue growth to outpace supply chain cost increases , but management has not disclosed what percentage of the LTSA book contains these favorable clauses versus legacy fixed-price contracts , nor the average remaining contract duration by engine platform. This gap can only be partially resolved through direct expert interviews with former Rolls-Royce commercial executives who negotiated these agreements, supplemented by waiting for the 2026 annual report which may provide enhanced segment-level LTSA profitability disclosure under investor pressure for transparency. The shop-visit cost structure and margin profile by engine type represents another critical blind spot that prevents definitive resolution of whether time-on-wing improvements will genuinely translate into durable cash generation. Management expects total shop visits to peak between 1,480 and 1,550 in 2026 before normalising , yet no public data reveals the average cost per shop visit, the split between labour and parts, or the margin differential between early-life and mature engine interventions . An expert consultation with a recently departed MRO facility general manager could triangulate these unit economics, while regulatory filings from joint venture partners like SAESL in Singapore might indirectly reveal labour cost structures through their own financial statements. A particularly opaque area concerns the quantum and timing of remaining onerous contract reversals , which management has repeatedly flagged as a significant earnings driver. The company successfully reversed £694m of previously recognised onerous contract losses in 2025, yet still recognised £433m in additional contract losses during 2025 due to prolonged supply chain challenges and escalating inflationary costs . No disclosure identifies which specific airline contracts or engine platforms carry the largest remaining provisions , nor does the company provide a forward-looking schedule of expected reversals beyond vague statements about completion through 2026. This gap can be addressed through careful analysis of quarterly filings to track the pace of provision releases , combined with targeted expert calls with former finance directors who understand the internal forecasting methodology. The structural capacity of the global MRO supply chain remains deeply uncertain, preventing confident forecasting of whether current turnaround time improvements are sustainable or merely a temporary reprieve. Turnaround times for engine maintenance have improved from peak tightness but remain structurally elevated at roughly 180 days , yet no industry-wide dataset tracks utilisation rates at individual MRO facilities or the skilled labour availability by geography . This critical gap requires commissioning a proprietary supply chain mapping study from a specialist aerospace consultancy like IBA Group or ICF, which maintains granular capacity databases unavailable through public sources, or alternatively conducting a series of expert interviews with MRO network heads at competing engine manufacturers to triangulate bottleneck severity.

The retirement assumptions for legacy Trent 700 and RB211 fleets represent another material uncertainty that directly impacts the durability thesis. Management expects no change to the retirement profile of the Trent 700, noting that the majority of the fleet is contracted into the 2030s , but no public airline fleet plans extend beyond three years with sufficient granularity to validate this claim against actual aircraft lease expirations. Resolving this gap demands accessing proprietary aircraft lease databases from Cirium or Ascend by Cirium , which track individual tail numbers and contract expirations, combined with expert interviews with aircraft leasing company chief commercial officers who have direct visibility into airline renewal intentions. A less obvious but strategically critical data void concerns the competitive positioning and customer acceptance of specific time-on-wing improvements , particularly the Trent 1000 Phase 1 high-pressure turbine blade upgrade. The Trent 1000 XE upgrade programme, featuring new high-pressure turbine blades that double time-on-wing, has successfully won back customers like LATAM Airlines for new Boeing 787 orders , yet no public data reveals the retrofit penetration rate across the existing Trent 1000 installed base , nor whether airlines are genuinely experiencing the promised durability improvements in real-world harsh-environment operations. This can be addressed through targeted expert interviews with airline engineering vice presidents at major Trent 1000 operators like British Airways or Air New Zealand, who can provide ground-truth validation of claimed performance improvements. Finally, the allocation of civil aerospace R&D spending between sustaining engineering for existing platforms versus new technology development remains obscure, limiting confidence in management's ability to simultaneously deliver time-on-wing improvements while investing in future UltraFan commercialisation. The company deployed a £1.75bn investment securing a 50% capacity increase through new facilities in Singapore, Germany, and China , but the company does not break out capital expenditure or R&D spending by individual engine programme , preventing independent validation that the Trent XWB life extension programme is adequately resourced without cannibalising UltraFan development. This gap can be partially closed by waiting for enhanced segment-level capital allocation disclosure in future investor days , or through expert consultations with former chief technology officers who understand internal budget allocation processes.

1.
Other side of the story: If the most critical data gaps–particularly airline-level utilisation patterns, granular LTSA economics, and shop-visit unit costs–prove fundamentally inaccessible due to competitive sensitivity and commercial confidentiality , then the key question may be inherently unresolvable through public market research alone, forcing investors to accept a structurally higher risk premium and treat the investment thesis as a probabilistic bet on management execution credibility rather than a data-driven conviction call on durable cash generation

Section 7 – Second and third-order implications 13 findings

Section summary
  1. A structural normalisation by 2027 would trigger severe cross-stock rotations away from pure-play aftermarket providers toward original equipment manufacturers, while supply chain constraints ironically protect near-term margins by forcing unprecedented teardowns of young aircraft to feed the MRO ecosystem.

Cross-stock and competitive dynamics

1.
Vulnerable pure-plays: If widebody aftermarket demand normalises sharply, MTU Aero Engines faces disproportionate downside risk given its 90% profit exposure to civil aftermarket and acute vulnerability to V2500 retirements alongside ongoing GTF recall challenges
2.
Strengthening OEM leverage: A normalisation that cools aftermarket pricing power would shift margin capture back toward original equipment manufacturers like Airbus, who are actively seeking to rebalance the value chain after years of engine manufacturers dominating aerospace profitability
3.
Accelerated narrowbody re-entry: The means-motive-opportunity test suggests that if Rolls-Royce's widebody cash generation proves purely cyclical, management has the motive to accelerate an UltraFan-based re-entry into the narrowbody market, leveraging its strong balance sheet to partner with Airbus on next-generation platforms
4.
Divergent US exposure: General Electric's blended portfolio of defense and commercial services provides a structural hedge against a purely cyclical widebody normalisation, particularly as its LEAP installed base is projected to double by 2030 and generate aftermarket profits matching the CFM56

Third-order supply chain effects

5.
Extreme asset cannibalisation: In a genuine outlier signal of supply chain desperation, lessors are currently tearing down one-year-old Boeing MAX and Airbus Neo aircraft specifically to harvest parts, highlighting a severe structural deficit in the spare parts ecosystem
6.
Entrenched MRO bottlenecks: Turnaround times for engine maintenance have improved from peak tightness but remain structurally elevated at roughly 180 days, ensuring that even if flying hours normalise, the MRO backlog will sustain aftermarket volumes well into 2028
7.
Used material squeeze: Independent used serviceable material providers face a paradox where accelerated aircraft retirements would increase part supply but compress their pricing power, directly threatening the current margin profiles of teardown specialists
8.
PMA adoption acceleration: As OEMs struggle to supply critical components, leasing agreements are increasingly accommodating Parts Manufacturer Approval alternatives for consumables, shifting third-order value toward alternative part manufacturers like HEICO

Capital allocation and non-consensus scenarios

9.
Aggressive capital redeployment: If the cash recovery is deemed cyclical rather than durable, Rolls-Royce's recent commitment to a £7bn to £9bn share buyback programme over three years risks stranding capital at cycle-peak valuations rather than insulating the balance sheet against future shocks
10.
Geopolitical margin buffers: Regional traffic rerouting to avoid Russian and Middle Eastern airspace continues to artificially inflate widebody fuel consumption and flight times, providing a hidden third-order tailwind to Rolls-Royce's time-on-wing service revenues
11.
Unintended decarbonisation delays: A cyclical normalisation driven by high fuel prices and lower airline profitability would severely constrain the capital available for airlines to adopt sustainable aviation fuels, delaying the industry's net-zero transition
12.
Other side of the story: The assumption that supply chain bottlenecks will eventually resolve ignores the reality that structural skilled labour deficits and a fundamental lack of Tier 3 forging capacity may permanently cap aerospace output, making the current high-margin, low-volume aftermarket paradigm the new permanent reality
