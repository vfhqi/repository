KEY FINDINGS - Concentrated: Getlink is structurally a GBP/EUR mismatch play — the Channel Tunnel's economics generate approximately half of revenues in GBP (Eurotunnel Le Shuttle + Eurostar access charges) but the company reports in EUR, meaning every ±10% move in GBP/EUR drives a ~4-5% swing in reported group revenues before hedging

  • Revenue currency: EUR and GBP together constitute ~97-98% of group revenues; USD is operationally immaterial at the group P&L level; GBP share has been structurally stable at ~48-52% of revenue L3Y with no significant trend away from the mismatch
  • Cost currency: Cost base is split roughly EUR-heavy (French-side labour, capex, maintenance) and GBP-light (UK-side operations), creating a structural natural short-GBP position in operating income — revenue is more GBP-exposed than costs, so a falling GBP is unambiguously bad for margins
  • Translation dominates: For a company with no significant USD revenue, Getlink's primary FX risk is a single cross-rate — GBP/EUR — and it is entirely translational in the consolidated accounts; transaction exposure is limited because cross-channel commercial contracts are typically sterling-priced with the UK counterparty, then consolidated in EUR at group
  • Hedging policy: Getlink does not systematically hedge translation exposure (standard practice for European infrastructure companies); it does deploy limited financial hedging on transactional flows in subsidiary treasury operations; overall hedging discipline is partial and structurally limited by the translation/transaction split
  • Sensitivity (load-bearing): A sustained 10% GBP depreciation vs EUR translates to approximately EUR 35-45m EBITDA headwind (translation) at 2024 group levels; at ~EUR 520m EBITDA, that is a ~7-9% EBITDA impact — material for a utility-adjacent infrastructure name where consensus growth assumptions are typically 3-8% per annum
  • Macro FX outlook: GBP/EUR in a structurally uncertain zone over N12-24M; EUR weakness vs USD is a secondary concern; net FX outlook for Getlink is mildly negative-to-neutral — GBP weakness risk (UK fiscal stress, BoE-ECB rate divergence) outweighs GBP strength scenarios for the 2026-27 window
  • Thesis impact: FX is a credible 200-400bps annual EPS volatility source for Getlink; it is not a thesis-killer but it IS the most under-modelled risk in a standard DCF that assumes static GBP/EUR; consensus sell-side models appear to use spot or short-dated forward GBP/EUR, creating potential EPS estimate volatility when spot moves materially
  • Hedge break point: Because Getlink's primary exposure is translational (not transactional), there is no financial hedge programme that materially insulates reported EBITDA from a sustained GBP/EUR move; investors should model this as fully unhedged on a 12M+ horizon
  • ⚡ Outlier signal: The GBP/EUR concentration in a single cross-rate, combined with absent translation hedging and the structural natural-short-GBP position in margins, means Getlink has one of the clearest FX-as-operating-leverage dynamics in European infrastructure — the market rarely prices this explicitly

§1 — Revenue by Currency

J-front verdict: Revenue is heavily GBP/EUR binary — approximately half in GBP, half in EUR, with USD operationally negligible; diversification is absent and there is no multi-year trend toward reporting-currency stabilisation

  • Reporting currency: EUR; Getlink SE is a Paris-listed, Luxembourg-domiciled holding company; all consolidated financials denominated in EUR
  • GBP revenue %: Approximately 48-52% of group revenues are generated by GBP-denominated or GBP-linked activities
  • Eurotunnel Le Shuttle freight and tourist revenues are priced in GBP (UK-side pricing dominant); road haulage and car/passenger shuttle tariffs are published in sterling for UK originating traffic
  • Eurostar SA (now separately structured post-2023 restructuring) pays access charges to Getlink under a concession framework; these access charges have GBP-linked components calibrated to UK CPI and UK traffic volumes
  • ElecLink (the electricity interconnector subsidiary) earns capacity revenues in GBP via GB power market auctions conducted in sterling on the National Grid side
  • EUR revenue %: Approximately 46-50% of group revenues in EUR
  • French-side shuttle revenues, French Motorail legacy flows, and French government-related payments are EUR-denominated
  • ElecLink French-side capacity auction revenues are in EUR via French power market mechanisms
  • Administrative and concession-related revenues billed from the French concession entity are EUR
  • USD revenue %: Sub-2%; Getlink has no material USD revenue stream; occasional USD-invoiced freight contracts exist but are immaterial at group level
  • Other currency %: Sub-1%; no material exposure to EM currencies, CHF, SEK, or other crosses

§2 — Cost by Currency

J-front verdict: Cost base is EUR-heavy (~60-65% EUR, ~30-35% GBP), creating a structural natural-short-GBP position in operating income — revenues are more GBP-exposed than costs, so margin contracts when GBP weakens

  • COGS currency mix:
  • Maintenance and infrastructure costs: ~55-60% EUR (French-side tunnelling maintenance, French regulatory compliance costs, French-side wage bill which is the larger headcount side); ~35-40% GBP (UK-side maintenance teams, UK regulatory costs including ORR compliance, UK-side safety and security obligations)
  • Energy costs: split EU ETS / French grid tariff vs UK National Grid system charges ; ElecLink's operational cost is EUR + GBP mixed at the interconnector management level; net ~50/50 on energy COGS
  • Opex currency mix:
  • Central overhead: predominantly EUR (Luxembourg/Paris holding company costs, French management, French statutory obligations)
  • UK-side management: GBP; estimated 25-30% of opex in GBP
  • Marketing / commercial: mix of GBP and EUR, roughly pro-rata to revenue split
  • Net opex mix: ~65% EUR / ~30% GBP / ~5% other
  • Capex currency mix:
  • Rolling stock refurbishment (Le Shuttle locomotives and wagons): majority EUR-denominated via European rail suppliers (Alstom, Bombardier/Alstom legacy fleet); estimated ~70% EUR
  • Tunnel maintenance capex: split French contractor vs UK contractor ; tunnel is physically 50/50 but French-side maintenance volume is larger in EUR terms historically
  • ElecLink capex (now largely complete post-2022 commissioning): was predominantly EUR + GBP split ~60/40; ongoing capex is minimal
  • Net capex currency: ~65-70% EUR / ~25-30% GBP
  • Natural hedge ratio:
  • Revenue GBP ~50%, Cost GBP ~30-35% — the natural hedge is partial, not full
  • GBP-exposed EBITDA: approximately GBP 80-100m of EBITDA is generated in GBP terms and then translated; this is the unhedged translation exposure pool
  • Natural hedge shortfall: ~15-20% of revenues represent "unmatched" GBP exposure (GBP revenue with EUR cost underneath)
  • Mismatch — revenue vs cost:
  • The mismatch is structurally pro-cyclical: when the Channel Tunnel delivers volume growth (UK-originating traffic), GBP revenue grows faster than GBP costs; but if GBP/EUR weakens simultaneously, the EUR-reported margin gain from volume is partially offset by FX translation
  • Key mismatch node: UK hauliers paying sterling freight tariffs vs French maintenance contracts paid in EUR — the margin on UK freight is GBP revenue / (part GBP + part EUR) cost, and it is dynamically sensitive to GBP/EUR
  • Margin FX sensitivity: A 10% GBP depreciation on the unmatched ~15-20% of revenue (EUR ~100-130m at group revenue levels of ~EUR 1.3-1.5bn) translates to a gross margin compression of approximately EUR 10-13m on the revenue side; with partially matched cost relief (~30% of cost in GBP, so ~3-4% of costs fall in EUR terms), net margin impact is approximately EUR 6-10m EBITDA per 10% GBP move from cost-currency mismatch alone (separate from the full translation calculation)

§3 — Translation Exposure

J-front verdict: Translation is the dominant FX mechanism for Getlink — GBP/EUR translation drives ~4-6% annual EBITDA volatility in reported terms with no systematic financial hedge programme in place

  • Reported FX impact L1Y (FY2024): GBP/EUR full-year average ~1.17 (vs ~1.15 in FY2023); modest GBP strength was a tailwind; estimated FX translation contribution to reported revenue growth: approximately +EUR 15-20m (~+1.2% on group revenues of ~EUR 1.3bn); EBITDA translation tailwind: ~EUR 8-12m
  • Reported FX impact L2Y (FY2023): GBP/EUR full-year average ~1.15 (vs ~1.18 in FY2022); the post-Truss GBP weakness was still weighing; estimated translation headwind to revenue: approximately -EUR 30-40m (-2.5%); EBITDA headwind: ~EUR 15-20m; this partially masked strong volume recovery post-COVID

§4 — Transaction Exposure

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is secondary and limited for Getlink — the business model generates revenues and costs in matched currency zones at the operating unit level; economic FX impact is primarily a translation phenomenon rather than a true transaction exposure

  • Transaction exposure definition (applied): Cash flows that require actual currency conversion — i.e., where Getlink receives GBP and must convert to EUR to service EUR-denominated obligations (debt, dividends, French-side costs), or vice versa
  • Key transaction exposure points:
  • GBP-to-EUR remittance: GTM UK (the UK concession vehicle) earns GBP revenues and must upward-remit dividends or intercompany loans to the EUR-reporting group; this cash conversion creates a real economic FX exposure at the point of conversion; timing of remittance is partially within management's control (natural deferral hedge)
  • GBP debt service: Any residual GBP-denominated debt at the UK entity level is serviced from GBP revenues — this is well-matched and represents a natural hedge at that entity level; the exposure arises only if GBP revenues fall below GBP debt service, which is a credit rather than FX concern
  • Eurobond interest + principal: Getlink's EUR Eurobond issuances are serviced from the EUR cash pool; French-side EBITDA provides EUR coverage; this is clean (no transaction exposure)
  • Capex contracts: Rolling stock and major maintenance capex occasionally involves EUR-denominated contracts payable from GBP cash pools (if the UK entity is contracting with European suppliers); this creates a real transaction exposure at the time of procurement; estimated magnitude: EUR 20-50m per major capex cycle
  • Magnitude vs translation exposure:
  • Translation exposure: ~GBP 650-700m of GBP-denominated annual revenue and ~GBP 200-220m of GBP-denominated annual EBITDA subject to translation
  • Transaction exposure: estimated GBP 50-100m per annum of intercompany remittances + EUR-denominated capex; this is approximately 10-15% of the total FX exposure pool
  • Transaction exposure is approximately 1/7th to 1/10th of translation exposure in magnitude — translation clearly dominates
  • Pricing pass-through:
  • Le Shuttle tariffs are set annually/biennially by management; there is no automatic FX pass-through mechanism in commercial tariff books; Getlink can and does adjust tariffs in GBP terms to reflect UK-market competitive dynamics, but there is no contractual FX indexation on the commercial (non-concession) passenger and freight services
  • ElecLink capacity auctions: prices are set by market (National Grid GB and RTE FR auction mechanisms); Getlink is a price-taker on capacity revenues; no FX pass-through possible — pure market exposure
  • Eurostar access charges: set under the concession agreement with indexation mechanisms; GBP-denominated with periodic review; these are not directly FX-linked but the GBP pricing is structurally stable, meaning they do not move to compensate for GBP/EUR shifts
  • Cost pass-through: French concession costs include regulatory obligations; no systematic ability to pass EUR-cost inflation through to GBP-priced customers; cost/revenue FX mismatch is absorbed in margins
  • Transaction hedging in practice: Getlink's treasury disclosures indicate use of forward foreign exchange contracts and currency swaps at subsidiary level for near-term cash flow management; disclosed notional amounts suggest hedging of approximately 6-12 months' forward transactional exposures; this is standard corporate treasury practice and covers the transaction exposure pool adequately; it does NOT address translation exposure

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on transaction exposure is that apparent pricing flexibility in Le Shuttle tariffs is procyclical — when GBP weakens, UK consumer purchasing power falls in EUR terms, meaning Getlink faces pressure to hold GBP tariffs flat or reduce them to maintain volume; the pricing "pass-through" option is therefore constrained precisely when FX stress is highest; in a 15-20% GBP depreciation scenario, the company cannot raise GBP tariffs to compensate for EUR-reporting loss because the underlying UK traveller is also squeezed — the pass-through assumption built into base-case models is unlikely to hold in the stress scenario where it matters most

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Hedging discipline is partial and structurally limited — transaction exposures are managed with standard short-duration financial hedges but the dominant translation exposure is formally unhedged; this is common practice for European infrastructure but leaves reported earnings materially FX-sensitive on any 12M+ horizon

  • Hedging mandate: Getlink's treasury policy (per annual report disclosures) acknowledges currency risk management as a treasury objective; the policy distinguishes between translational exposures (accepted as unavoidable structural features of a cross-border business) and transactional exposures (actively managed); the mandate is explicit in not requiring translation hedging
  • Hedge horizon — transaction: Approximately 6-12 months for forward contracts on transactional cash flows; this is a short-duration programme by infrastructure standards; longer-duration hedging of 18-24M appears absent or minimal based on disclosed notional amounts
  • Hedge ratio — transaction: Estimated 50-80% of forward 6-12M transactional FX exposure hedged; Getlink's disclosures in the financial risk section of the annual report note forward contracts and swaps in place; specific percentage coverage is not always explicitly disclosed
  • Hedge ratio — translation: 0%; Getlink does not hedge translation exposure; this is standard for the sector and rational (cost of translation hedges over multi-year horizons is typically prohibitive; natural hedging is the preferred mechanism) but means the EUR-reported P&L is fully exposed to GBP/EUR spot on the translation pool
  • Hedge instruments: Forward foreign exchange contracts (primary); currency interest rate swaps (on specific debt instruments); limited use of options; instrument mix is conservative and conventional — no exotic derivatives
  • Translation hedging — formal policy absence: No evidence in Getlink's annual reports of any structural translation hedge (e.g. GBP bond issuance at parent level to create a natural liability offset against GBP assets); the parent's debt stack is EUR-denominated; this is a structural choice that maximises cheapest-funding access in EUR capital markets at the cost of unhedged translation
  • Hedge effectiveness L3Y:
  • FY2022: hedge gains on transactional forwards modestly positive (GBP broadly stable on annual average basis; no extreme moves sustained through the hedge horizon); effectiveness broadly neutral
  • FY2023: hedge programme would have partially offset GBP weakness on the transactional portion (~10-15% of total exposure); the majority of FX impact was translational and therefore unhedged; disclosed hedge gain/loss in OCI and P&L was immaterial relative to total FX impact
  • FY2024: minor GBP recovery; hedge programme net neutral; no significant hedge gain or loss reported
  • Empirical effectiveness observation: The hedge programme is effective at smoothing transactional FX volatility (its stated purpose) but provides zero insulation against translation — which is where >85% of the economic FX exposure sits; effectiveness measured against total FX exposure is therefore structurally low
  • Hedge cost: Forward premiums on GBP/EUR hedges at current rate differentials (BoE base rate vs ECB rate) create a cost-of-carry; with BoE policy rate above ECB by ~50-100bps historically through 2023-2024, GBP forward contracts have been near-flat to marginally negative cost (the interest differential is embedded in the forward rate); hedge cost is not a significant P&L distortion
  • Recent hedge realisation: No specific large hedge gain/loss events disclosed in FY2022-FY2024 that suggest programme mismatch; the absence of large disclosed unrealised losses suggests the transactional hedge programme is sized and rolled without significant timing mismatches
  • IFRS 9 hedge accounting: Getlink applies IFRS 9 for financial instruments; qualifying hedging relationships are designated as cash flow hedges with effective portion in OCI; the small reported amounts in the hedging reserve confirm the transactional-only scope
  • Board mandate transparency: The annual report's "Financial Risk Management" section describes the governance framework; risk oversight sits with the Board's Audit Committee; management has discretion in hedge instrument selection within the policy framework; the disclosure is adequate but not class-leading in specificity (no explicit hedge ratio or horizon targets disclosed numerically)
  • Natural hedging structural reliance: Getlink's primary risk management tool for translation is the partial natural hedge in the cost base (~32% of costs in GBP against ~50% of revenues in GBP); this is the backstop and it is only ~60% effective at the operating-income level; the ~40% residual translation exposure (GBP revenue in excess of GBP costs as a share of group EBITDA) is fully uninsured

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on hedging discipline is that the disclosed policy conflates "we hedge transactional exposures" with "we manage FX risk" — but since transaction exposure is <15% of total FX exposure and translation is >85%, the programme manages the smaller bucket while leaving the larger bucket fully exposed; a GBP/EUR move of any sustained duration (>12M) will flow entirely through reported EBITDA with no hedge buffer; the programme disclosure gives an impression of active management that does not survive decomposition into translation vs transaction components
