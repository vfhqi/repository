§1 — Industry Overview + Value Chain Economics

OEM International occupies a specific and structurally differentiated position within the broader industrial distribution sector: it focuses on value-added distribution of technical components (motion, fluid power, pneumatics, electronics, electromechanical) to OEM manufacturers — not MRO (maintenance, repair, overhaul) distributors and not broadline catalogue houses.

  • Industry size: The European technical component distribution market (OEM's served domain) is estimated at €60-80bn addressable; the broader European industrial distribution market (including MRO) is €250-300bn, per sector trade bodies and distributor annual reports [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: OEM's current revenue (~SEK 4.5-5bn, ~€400-420m) implies a market share of 0.5-0.7% of served addressable — indicating substantial headroom even within existing geographies
  • Sub-bullet: The Nordic home market (Sweden, Denmark, Finland, Norway) represents ~60-65% of OEM revenue; Central/Eastern Europe and newer geographies the balance
  • Growth trajectory: European industrial component distribution has grown at GDP+ historically — approximately 3-5% nominal CAGR (L10Y), with cyclical swings of ±10-15% around the trend; N2Y consensus implies 4-6% volume/price growth assuming soft industrial recovery [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: OEM's organic growth has exceeded the sector rate at ~6-8% CAGR (L5Y), implying share gains on top of market growth
  • Sub-bullet: European industrial output (the key demand driver) has been subdued in 2023-24; a recovery trajectory is priced into sector consensus but remains uncertain given German industrial weakness
  • Structural driver (technology complexity): Rising product complexity in OEM customers' own manufacturing processes increases the value of technical specification advice — distributors who can support the design-in stage of component selection earn higher margins and stickier relationships [#A]
  • Structural driver (outsourcing): OEM manufacturers increasingly outsource component sourcing to specialist distributors rather than managing direct supplier relationships — each outsourced category adds to the addressable market for value-added distributors like OEM International [#A]
  • Geographic split: Nordic markets are mature (3-4% growth, high penetration); Central/Eastern Europe is the structural growth engine (8-12% CAGR in served markets, lower penetration of value-added distribution vs broadline) [#A]
  • Fragmentation: The European technical component distribution market is highly fragmented — no single player holds >5% pan-European share; OEM's home Nordic markets are somewhat more consolidated around 3-5 serious players per product category [#A]
  • Consolidation: The trend is toward slow consolidation driven by supplier rationalisation (principals prefer fewer, larger distributors who invest in technical capability) and customer preference for single-source relationships; OEM is a consolidator, not a consolidation target [#J]

§3 — Barriers to Entry + Exit + Disruption Forces

  • Verdict: Barriers to entry in OEM's served niche are moderately high and compound over time — the combination of supplier relationship depth, technical expertise, specification lock-in, and geographic density creates a moat structure that vanilla entrants struggle to replicate within investment-relevant time frames [#J]

  • Capital requirement: Low absolute capital required to start a distribution business — but meaningful capital required to build the supplier relationships, technical staff, and SKU breadth that makes OEM defensible; new entrant capex is people/IT/working capital heavy rather than fixed asset heavy [#A]

  • Scale economy: Volume rebates from principals (typically 3-8% additional margin beyond list distributor pricing at high volumes) require sustained purchasing scale to unlock; new entrants cannot replicate OEM's rebate structure without multi-year volume commitment [#A]
  • Switching cost: Component specification lock-in is the dominant barrier — once OEM's component is designed into a customer's product, switching distributor requires re-qualification, re-testing, and supply chain re-approval; for SME OEM manufacturers this switching cost is disproportionate to the saving [#J]
  • Sub-bullet: Estimated switching cost equivalent to 6-18 months of component value for a qualified production component — a structural stickiness that few distribution models replicate
  • Technology barrier: OEM's ERP/catalogue/technical specification database (130,000+ SKUs, cross-referenced by application and supplier) represents a years-in-building proprietary asset; not protectable by IP but expensive and time-consuming to replicate [#A]
  • Distribution barrier: OEM's geographic density (branch network, local technical sales teams) in Nordic markets creates a last-mile advantage — customers value local technical support, which requires local presence that a distant competitor cannot replicate without capex [#A]
  • Regulatory barrier: Low direct regulatory barrier to entry (distribution is not a licensed activity); however, supplier authorisation requirements (e.g. Parker Hannifin authorised distributor certification) create indirect barriers — securing authorised status requires demonstrated technical competence and volume commitment [#A]
  • Lock-in mechanism: Supplier authorisation networks create a two-sided lock-in: suppliers will not authorise just any distributor, limiting who can legitimately represent their products at full technical capability [#J]
  • Structural exit: Low barriers to exit at the business level (no heavy fixed assets), which perversely means competitive exits are more common than in capital-intensive sectors — underperforming regional distributors can exit without stranding capital, keeping the market cleaner [#A]

Disruption forces

  • Disruption force (digital procurement): E-commerce and digital procurement platforms are the primary disruption force — Amazon Business, RS Components online, and Würth's digital platform are compressing margins for standard, price-comparable components; OEM's defence is the technical/specification components where digital self-serve is insufficient [#J]
  • Sub-bullet: OEM has invested in its own digital presence (e-catalogue, online ordering) to defend the tail of its SKU range against digital substitution — partially successful, but the risk compounds as platforms improve AI-assisted specification tools
  • Disruption force (direct distribution): Major principals (Festo, SMC, Parker) have direct sales arms that could, in theory, bypass distributors for large customer accounts; in practice, the SME fragmentation of OEM's customer base makes direct distribution economically unviable for principals [#A]
  • Innovative entrant: AI-assisted specification tools (e.g. integrating LLM-based component selection into procurement platforms) represent a medium-term disruption threat to OEM's technical advice moat — not immediate but the trajectory is worth monitoring [#J]
  • Vanilla entrant: Traditional broadline distributors expanding their technical capability (RS Components, Farnell) represent a slow-burn encroachment; pace is constrained by their structural cost bases and different business model DNA [#A]
  • Adjacency entry: Chinese component manufacturers (direct-to-customer, digital, price-competitive) are entering European markets in lower-specification categories — a growing pressure at the commodity end of OEM's catalogue that could erode the long tail of the SKU portfolio [#J]
  • Substitute: Direct procurement by OEM customers from manufacturers (cutting out the distributor) is theoretically substitutable but economically unattractive for fragmented SME customers due to minimum order quantities and lack of multi-supplier aggregation capability [#A]
  • Disintermediation: The structural trend is anti-disintermediation for value-added distributors (increasing product complexity, outsourcing of procurement) and pro-disintermediation for broadline catalogue distributors (digital self-serve); OEM sits on the safer side of this divide [#J]

Political / regulatory dynamics

  • Regulatory barrier: EU single market rules facilitate OEM's geographic expansion (no customs friction in EU expansion); no material regulatory barrier to OEM's business model in existing or target markets [#A]

  • Political SWOT: Reshoring of European manufacturing (positive for regional industrial component demand), EU industrial policy (Chips Act, Net Zero Industrial Act) generating incremental industrial investment — net positive for OEM's addressable market [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: The inversion case on barriers + disruption: The switching cost moat is real but it is bounded to the component design-in moment — once specifications are set the relationship is sticky, but OEM must continuously win new design-in opportunities to grow; if AI-assisted specification tools mature faster than expected (3-5Y rather than 7-10Y), the technical advisory value proposition could be partially commoditised; direct principal distribution to large accounts (which OEM's growth increasingly requires) is a latent risk if principals reconfigure channel strategy; Chinese component substitution at the commodity tail of the SKU portfolio could structurally reduce the long-tail margin that partially subsidises the technical sales force [#J]
