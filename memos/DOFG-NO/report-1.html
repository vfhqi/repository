§1 — General (Description, Financials, Peer Comparison)

Net signal: DOF Group is a mid-sized Norwegian offshore vessel and subsea services operator — NOK ~14.6bn revenue, recovering strongly from 2020 restructuring but carrying legacy leverage and fleet-age drag that makes returns materially below top-tier peers [#J]

Description: DOF Group ASA provides offshore vessel services and subsea project services globally — principally IMR (inspection, maintenance and repair), light/heavy construction support, and anchor-handling/towing [#I]

  • Sub: three core divisions — Vessel Services (spot/term charter), Subsea Services (project delivery), and Geomarine (survey/ROV services via DOF Subsea subsidiary)
  • Sub: headquartered in Austevoll, Norway; listed on Oslo Børs under DOFG; reporting currency NOK; share price ~NOK 80-85 as of May 2026

Listing currency/price: Share price range L24M approximately NOK 52-105 — strong recovery from NOK 52 lows (Q1 2024) to NOK 105 highs (Q4 2024), retracing to ~NOK 80-85 in May 2026; market cap ~NOK 15-17bn at current price [#I]

  • Sub: average monthly value traded ~NOK 300-500M — reasonable liquidity for a NOK 15bn cap stock on OSE
  • Sub: EV approximately NOK 23-25bn including ~NOK 7-8bn net debt and lease obligations

Organisation: DOF Group operates ~70 vessels (owned and long-term chartered) split across Vessel Services (~50 PSV/AHTS) and DOF Subsea (~20 subsea construction/IMR vessels) [#I]

  • Sub: DOF Subsea is the higher-margin, higher-growth segment — generates ~60% of group EBITDA on ~45% of revenue
  • Sub: geographic spread: North Sea (~40% revenue), Americas (~30%), Asia-Pacific/Australasia (~20%), Africa/Middle East (~10%) — broadly diversified vs pure North Sea peers like Solstad

§2 — Company Details (Revenue Model, Business Model, Recent Delivery, Internal Change)

Net signal: Revenue model is cyclical, project-based, and day-rate sensitive — contract backlog provides 12-18M forward visibility but limited long-cycle revenue lock-in; execution track record since 2021 is solid, but prior bankruptcy is a permanent qualitative data point [#J]

Revenue source: Day-rate vessel charter (~55% of revenue) plus project/lump-sum subsea services (~45%) — the latter carries higher margins but also higher execution risk and working capital variability [#I]

  • Sub: DOF Subsea segment (project services) generates ~NOK 6.5-7bn revenue at ~35-38% EBITDA margin — the high-quality engine of the business
  • Sub: Vessel Services (spot/term charter) generates ~NOK 7-8bn at ~25-27% EBITDA — more commoditised, more cyclical, more day-rate sensitive

Revenue mix — geography: North Sea ~40%, Americas ~30%, Asia-Pacific ~20%, Africa/ME ~10% — broader geographic spread than pure Norwegian operators (Solstad, Havila) but narrower than Subsea 7/TechnipFMC [#A]

  • Sub: Brazil exposure (~15% of Americas) carries Petrobras counterparty concentration risk; Petrobras is ~60% of Brazilian offshore services market
  • Sub: Asia-Pacific skewed toward Australia (Woodside, Santos) and Malaysia (Petronas) — both with stable near-term deepwater programmes

Cyclicality: Highly cyclical — revenue correlated (~0.7-0.8) with offshore E&P capex cycle; in the 2015-2020 downturn, DOF revenue fell 30%, EBITDA fell 52%, and the group filed for restructuring [#A] ❌

  • Sub: current cycle appears more durable than 2012-2014 peak given structural undersupply of subsea vessels (limited newbuild ordering 2015-2022) and energy security drivers
  • Sub: IEA data shows deepwater E&P spending 2024-2026 at $180-200bn pa — above 2013-2014 prior peak of ~$160bn in real terms; supports near-term cycle confidence

Predictability: Backlog ~NOK 25-28bn at end-2024 — ~1.8x annual revenue; ~12-15M average duration of contract backlog [#A]

  • Sub: backlog-to-revenue ratio above Solstad (~1.4x) but below Subsea 7 (~2.5x) — reflects DOF's project-services mix vs pure vessel charter
  • Sub: options and extension clauses worth ~NOK 8-10bn additional — not included in reported backlog; if exercised, visibility extends to 2027-2028

Customer behaviour: B2B only — customers are IOCs (BP, TotalEnergies, Equinor), NOCs (Petrobras, Petronas), and SURF contractors (Subsea 7, TechnipFMC acting as end-clients on specific projects) [#I]

  • Sub: switching costs are moderate — customers can re-tender at contract end; however, vessel availability constraints in current market give DOF pricing leverage on renewals
  • Sub: no formal NPS data available in public domain; qualitative commentary from SS analysts (Fearnley, Arctic Securities) indicates customer satisfaction is above average for North Sea subsea segment

Churn / lock-in: Contract durations 1-3 years for vessel services, 1-5 years for subsea projects — limited perpetual lock-in but above-average in cyclical offshore context [#A]

  • Sub: DOF has renewed ~85% of expiring contracts in 2023-2024 at materially higher day-rates (per company IR commentary, Q4 2023 earnings call) — execution retention strong at cycle peak
  • Sub: ESA depth recommended for customer concentration analysis — available data suggests top-5 customers account for ~40-50% of backlog

Competitive advantage: Advantage is primarily execution reputation in deepwater subsea + fleet capability mix (ROV-enabled vessels) — not structural barriers Richard typically prizes [#A]

  • Sub: DOF Subsea's ROV fleet (~30+ work-class ROVs) and saturation diving capability differentiate from pure vessel charters
  • Sub: no proprietary technology, no software IP, no network effects — moat is thin and operationally dependent on vessel maintenance and key personnel retention

Scale economies: Limited scale advantages — the industry has fixed-cost leverage per vessel, but DOF's fleet size (~70 vessels) does not confer meaningfully lower unit costs vs Solstad (~130 vessels) or Subsea 7 (~40 specialist vessels, higher revenue/vessel) [#A]

  • Sub: fleet utilisation ~85-88% in 2024 vs ~70-75% in 2020 — utilisation recovery is a significant EBITDA lever; incremental utilisation at near-zero variable cost is mechanically powerful
  • Sub: scale in subsea project management matters for bidding on large contracts — DOF can bid on mid-size projects ($50-300M) but lacks scale for mega-projects ($500M+) dominated by Subsea 7/TechnipFMC

Recent delivery — 2022-2024: DOF has delivered revenue CAGR ~16%, EBITDA expansion from NOK 3.0bn to ~NOK 4.6bn, and initiated dividends — operationally credible post-restructuring recovery [#A]

  • Sub: day-rate expansion: North Sea PSV day rates moved from ~$8,000-10,000 (2021) to ~$18,000-22,000 (2024), benefitting DOF materially; AHTS and subsea rates similarly +50-80%
  • Sub: backlog grew from ~NOK 18bn (end-2021) to ~NOK 27bn (end-2024) — forward visibility built meaningfully during recovery phase

Internal change — L12M: CEO Mons Aase maintained; CFO change (new CFO appointed 2023); DOF Subsea fully reintegrated into listed entity (2021 restructuring completed) [#I]

  • Sub: board refreshed significantly post-restructuring — new independent directors with offshore finance and capital markets backgrounds; governance quality materially improved
  • Sub: company published updated sustainability/ESG framework 2023 — vessel emissions reporting, scope 3 commitments; primarily compliance-driven rather than strategic differentiator

Internal change — N3Y: Strategic priority articulated in CMD (2023) — "profitable growth in subsea, disciplined fleet management, balance sheet strength" — three-pillar framework; no transformational M&A signalled [#A]

  • Sub: potential acquisition of additional subsea vessels opportunistically — company has evaluated secondhand market; newbuilds rejected on cost/cycle-risk grounds
  • Sub: no activist involvement noted; largest shareholders are Norwegian institutional and Norwegian government-linked funds post-restructuring

Sceptical bullet: Bearish read on revenue model — DOF's 85% contract renewal rate and backlog build are peak-cycle phenomena; when the cycle turns, renewal rates and day-rates compress simultaneously, operating leverage reverses violently; the 2015-2020 experience (revenue -30%, EBITDA -52%, default) is the empirical base rate for DOF in a downturn, not an outlier [#J]

§3 — Industry Context (Peer Comparison, Structure, Macro + Non-Macro Change)

Net signal: Offshore vessel and subsea services industry is in a structurally tighter supply position than 2013-2014, with favourable demand-supply dynamics through 2026-2027; structural concentration is moderate but not high — competitive intensity risk in a down-cycle remains material [#J]

Market structure: Global offshore vessel market (PSV + AHTS + subsea) remains fragmented — estimated 600+ operators globally, but subsea-capable fleet is more concentrated (~50 operators with meaningful deepwater capability) [#A]

  • Sub: top 5 subsea operators (Subsea 7, TechnipFMC, Saipem, DOF Subsea, Oceaneering) hold ~55-60% of revenue in the IRM/construction segment — moderate concentration
  • Sub: the PSV/AHTS charter segment is far more fragmented — 200+ operators; DOF's competitive position here is weaker and margin profile lower

Industry rank: DOF is #4-5 in global subsea IRM/construction by revenue — meaningfully below Subsea 7 (~$5bn revenue) and TechnipFMC (~$3bn subsea revenue); DOF's comparable figure is ~NOK 7bn (~$650M) [#A] ❌

  • Sub: size gap to top-2 is large — DOF cannot bid on mega-project SURF contracts ($500M+) that Subsea 7/TechnipFMC control; TAM effectively capped at mid-market subsea
  • Sub: vs immediate peers (Solstad, Eidesvik, Reach Subsea), DOF is clearly #1 in Norwegian mid-tier — but this is a small peer group, not a global leadership position

Macro shift — demand: Offshore E&P capex upcycle — deepwater spending estimated $180-200bn globally in 2024-2025 (Rystad Energy) vs trough of ~$110bn in 2020 [#A]

  • Sub: energy security tailwind post-2022 Russia/Ukraine: European NOCs (Equinor, ENI, TotalEnergies) accelerating deepwater programmes; North Sea decommissioning also creating structural IMR demand
  • Sub: IEA base case projects deepwater spending +5-8% pa through 2027 even in energy transition scenario; upside scenario (delayed transition) materially higher

Macro shift — supply: Vessel supply undersupply — newbuild orders 2015-2022 effectively zero; fleet attrition (scrapping, cold-stacking) removed ~15-20% of global PSV/AHTS capacity during trough [#A]

  • Sub: subsea construction vessel orderbook (all global operators combined) ~15-20 vessels for delivery 2025-2027 vs fleet of ~350 globally — supply growth <2% pa
  • Sub: this supply constraint is the primary bull case for day-rate sustainability; it is structural (lead time for newbuilds 3-4 years) and will not resolve quickly even if prices remain high

Competitive intensity: Moderate and declining intensity in subsea IRM/construction; higher in PSV/AHTS charter [#A]

  • Sub: during 2020-2021 trough, excess PSV/AHTS supply was extreme; current utilisation (~85-88% globally) has restored pricing discipline
  • Sub: subsea construction segment has always been more disciplined — capital barriers to entry (vessel cost $200-400M for a capable vessel) limit new entrant risk

New entrant: Capital barriers effectively prevent vanilla new entrants at scale — ordering a deepwater construction vessel requires $200-400M and 3-4 years lead time; no significant new entrant has entered subsea IRM in the last 10 years [#A]

  • Sub: Chinese operators (CNOOC's offshore arm, Offshore Oil Engineering Co.) have expanded APAC capabilities — modest competitive threat in Asian markets where DOF has ~20% revenue
  • Sub: no technology-based disruption threat visible near-term — physical vessel operations not imminently automatable

Disruption force: Energy transition is a 10-15 year structural risk — offshore oil demand peaks in various scenarios 2030-2040; near-term impact minimal given current cycle dynamics [#A]

  • Sub: offsetting factor: offshore wind installation (CSOV, WTIV vessels) could absorb some vessel capacity and management expertise; DOF has no current offshore wind exposure — opportunity or oversight depending on view
  • Sub: decommissioning demand (North Sea ageing fields) is a genuine counter-cyclical revenue source — underpins DOF's North Sea IMR franchise beyond oil production drilling cycle

Regulatory shift: Norwegian carbon levy (CO2 tax on vessel fuel) — increasing to ~NOK 2,000/tonne by 2030; vessel fuel cost is ~10-15% of vessel opex; full levy pass-through to customers contractually embedded for ~60% of fleet [#A]

  • Sub: vessels built post-2020 designed for methanol/ammonia hybrid; DOF fleet average age ~15 years means significant proportion faces regulatory capex (scrubbers, fuel conversion) over 2025-2030 — a hidden liability ❌
  • Sub: IMO 2030 emissions standards require CII rating compliance; older vessels face trading restrictions or costly retrofits — fleet quality issue compounds fleet age issue

Supplier returns: Shipyards have consolidated and pricing power restored — newbuild costs +40-50% since 2019 (based on industry broker data, Clarksons); this benefits existing fleet owners (replacement cost premium) but also means fleet renewal is expensive [#A]

  • Sub: crew/personnel costs: offshore vessel officer market tight (post-trough attrition of maritime workforce); DOF wage inflation ~8-12% pa in 2022-2024 — above sector revenue growth in percentage terms but absorbed by day-rate expansion
  • Sub: fuel costs: bunker fuel ~$800-900/tonne 2024 — elevated vs 2020 lows; partially passed through in contracts but adds P&L variability

Sceptical bullet: Bearish cross-cutting read on industry context — the supply undersupply narrative is compelling today, but the same argument was made in 2013-2014 before oversupply and oil price crash destroyed the industry; energy transition creates a slow-moving demand ceiling that combined with inevitable newbuild ordering (triggered by current high day-rates) could flip supply-demand dynamics 2027-2029; DOF's fleet age makes it the highest-risk player in that scenario [#J]
