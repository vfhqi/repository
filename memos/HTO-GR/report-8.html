§1 — Industry Overview + Value Chain Economics

Greek telecoms sits within the broader European integrated telecoms universe — a mature, capital-intensive sector where fibre investment cycles, regulatory WACC decisions, and enterprise digitalisation are the key economic engines right now.

  • Industry verdict: Greek telecoms is a structurally oligopolistic, GDP+-growth market with fibre investment creating a temporary capex overhang but medium-term margin recovery; value chain power resides with network infrastructure owners [#J]

Industry size + growth

  • Industry size: Greek telecoms revenues approximately €3.0-3.2bn annually (combined fixed + mobile + enterprise); OTE Group consolidated revenues ~€3.5bn including Romanian contribution [#A] [#I]
  • Sub: Greece fixed + mobile market estimated €2.5-2.7bn; Romania mobile segment roughly €0.7-0.8bn
  • Sub: European comparable — Portugal Telecom domestic ~€2.5bn, Slovenia/Croatia national incumbents materially smaller
  • Growth trajectory: Greek market grew at roughly 2-4% CAGR 2021-2024, outperforming pan-European incumbent median of ~1-2%; driven by fibre ARPU uplift and enterprise ICT [#A]
  • Sub: Mobile service revenue growth ~2-3% pa, below Western European mobile-only market growth of 3-4%
  • Sub: Fixed broadband revenue growing ~5-7% pa as fibre mix shifts ARPU upward from copper baseline
  • GDP+ signal: Greek GDP grew ~2.3% in 2023, ~2.0% in 2024; telecoms revenues growing modestly above GDP, consistent with fibre-driven ARPU expansion typical of mid-cycle infrastructure upgrade markets [#A]

Structural drivers

  • Structural driver: Fibre-to-the-home rollout is the dominant demand driver; Greek fibre penetration was below 20% as recently as 2022 vs Western European median of 40-50%, creating meaningful upgrade runway [#A]
  • Sub: EU Gigabit Society targets (2025: 100Mbps; 2030: Gigabit access) provide regulatory tailwind for fibre investment and implicit pricing protection
  • Sub: Government co-financing (Ultra Fast Broadband programmes) accelerates rural fibre and improves OTE's return profile on marginal coverage areas
  • Structural driver: Enterprise digitalisation + cloud services creating a new B2B revenue layer above legacy connectivity; Greek SME ICT spend penetration materially below Western European peers [#A]
  • Structural driver: Tourism and seasonal demand patterns support Greece's mobile data growth above structural trend; Greece's tourism economy (20%+ of GDP) creates a mobile data consumption premium vs population-only comparable [#A]

Geographic split

  • Geographic split: Greece is OTE's core (approximately 75-80% of Group EBITDA); Romania historically ~20%; Romania fixed disposed to Orange Romania in 2023, leaving mobile-only Romanian footprint via Telekom Romania Mobile [#I]
  • Sub: Romanian mobile market is a competitive three-to-four player environment; Telekom Romania Mobile is subscale relative to Digi Romania and Orange Romania
  • Sub: Greek market is OTE's fortress — no meaningful risk to domestic leadership position

Fragmentation vs consolidation

  • Fragmentation: Greek fixed market is effectively a duopoly (OTE/Cosmote vs Vodafone Greece); WIND Hellas was acquired and integrated; fixed market consolidation complete [#A]
  • Consolidation: Greek mobile market has consolidated from four to three meaningful players (Cosmote, Vodafone Greece, WIND-merged entity now rebranded); rational oligopoly dynamic now established [#A]
  • Sub: European incumbent experience (Telstra Australia, BT Group, Proximus Belgium) shows three-player mobile markets produce MOL stability superior to four-player markets

  • Pricing secular: Fibre vs copper ARPU premium €5-10/month in Greek market; as copper subscribers migrate, blended ARPU rises structurally; this is a multi-year, low-risk pricing uplift mechanism [#A]

  • Adjacency: OTE's Infra.tel tower and fibre infrastructure subsidiary has optionality for partial monetisation (tower sale-leaseback model used by Cellnex/Totem in Western Europe); not yet actioned but precedent set across 8-10 European incumbents [#A]

§2 — Competitive Landscape + Industry Structure

  • Competitive landscape verdict: Greek telecoms is a rational three-player oligopoly with OTE/Cosmote as the dominant incumbent; competitive intensity has declined materially since the 2016-2020 consolidation period; competitor attitudes are broadly benign [#J]

Competitor tier mapping

  • Global leader: OTE Group (HTO-GR) — domestic incumbent with full fixed + mobile convergence, only national fibre infrastructure owner; approximately 45-50% blended market share in Greece [#I]
  • Mid-market: Vodafone Greece — wholly owned Vodafone subsidiary; strong mobile brand; growing fixed via OTE wholesale access; estimated 25-30% mobile share; improving enterprise position [#I]
  • Mid-market: WIND Hellas (now trading as Nova post-Cinven acquisition and WIND Italy separation) — third mobile operator; fixed IPTV challenger via Nova cable; approximately 20-25% mobile share [#I]
  • Sub: Nova has been more aggressive on content bundling (UEFA Champions League rights) creating short-term ARPU pressure on OTE TV
  • Sub: Nova's financial flexibility is constrained as a private-equity-backed entity; unlikely to sustain irrational pricing competition
  • Niche: Digi Romania (also entering Greece) — MVNO / FTTB altnet entrant in Greece; low-price positioning; represents the most credible new entrant threat; Greek launch 2022-2023 [#I]

  • Sub: Vodafone Greece under Vodafone Group's Group-level financial pressure (Vodafone Group's strategic reviews 2023-2024) limits appetite for margin-dilutive price wars in Greece

§3 — Barriers to Entry + Exit + Disruption Forces

  • Barriers verdict: Entry barriers are high for the fixed network layer (capital requirement and regulatory complexity); moderate for mobile (MVNO route exists); disruption forces are real but manageable on a 3-5 year horizon [#J]

Barriers to entry

  • Capital requirement: Full national fixed-line infrastructure replication would require €3-5bn+ capex in Greece alone — effectively prohibitive for any new entrant without state/EU subsidy; this is the primary structural barrier [#A]
  • Sub: OTE's fibre rollout (FTTH programme) is targeting 3.5-4m premises passed by 2026; a new entrant starting today would face a 5-7 year catch-up lag at current deployment rates
  • Scale economy: Fixed network infrastructure is a natural monopoly in practice; the per-subscriber cost curve for the incumbent is already at the bottom; any new entrant starts at a cost disadvantage of 30-50% on a per-unit basis [#A]
  • Regulatory barrier: EETT (Greek regulator) mandates open-access wholesale pricing but simultaneously protects incumbents via regulated ROE floor; new entrants must negotiate access pricing with OTE under regulatory oversight — a structural time and cost barrier [#A]
  • Brand: OTE/Cosmote brand awareness in Greece is essentially universal; market research across European incumbents consistently shows >70% first-recall for the national incumbent; brand switching costs are psychologically meaningful [#A]
  • Switching cost: Fibre subscribers on 24-month contracts face early termination fees; convergent bundle (broadband + TV + mobile) customers face multi-product switching friction; estimated effective switching cost €100-200 per household [#A]
  • Distribution barrier: OTE operates ~400 physical stores across Greece plus digital channels; replicating this distribution network requires €50-100m+ investment with multi-year lead time [#A]

Barriers to exit + lock-in

  • Structural exit: High sunk capital in network infrastructure creates strong exit barriers for all three players; no competitor has an easy path to exit without writing off significant network asset value [#A]
  • Lock-in mechanism: Enterprise contracts with 3-5 year terms lock in B2B revenue; fibre infrastructure once installed is economically indivisible and drives sticky subscriber retention [#A]

Disruption forces (BB#2 TC3 RA11)

  • Disruption force: Digi Romania/Greece altnet model — FTTB deployment in dense urban areas with aggressively-priced bundles represents the most credible demand-side disruption; Digi Romania achieved ~1.5m fixed subscribers in Romania within 6 years of launch [#A]
  • Sub: Digi's Greek progress has been materially slower than Romanian — geography (dispersed islands, lower urban density) and regulatory environment are friction factors
  • Disruption force: Satellite broadband (Starlink) is an emerging rural substitute; Greek islands represent Starlink's natural market (poor fixed connectivity historically); OTE's rural fibre build economics are threatened by Starlink's falling CPE costs [#A]
  • Technology barrier erosion: Software-defined networking and open RAN reduce the hardware-specific advantage of established operators; not yet material in Greece but a 5-7 year risk worth flagging [#A]
  • Innovative entrant: No technology-native (FAANG-adjacent) credible threat to OTE's core connectivity revenues in the near term; cloud providers (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud) are more likely to partner with OTE on enterprise cloud services than compete directly [#A]

Adjacencies entering (BB#2 TC3 RA10 CQ46)

  • Adjacency entry: Cloud hyperscalers (AWS, Azure) are entering enterprise ICT in Greece; this creates competitive pressure on OTE Business's managed services positioning; however, OTE can serve as a local distribution/integration partner, converting the threat into a channel relationship [#A]
  • Adjacency entry: Pay-TV/content providers (Netflix, Disney+) have disrupted OTE TV's content bundle value; OTE has responded by partnering rather than competing, offering Netflix-integrated OTE TV bundles — a rational response [#A]

Substitutes (BB#2 TC3 RA10 CQ49)

  • Substitute: Fixed wireless access using 4G/5G is a partial substitute for fibre broadband at lower speeds; OTE/Cosmote is well-positioned to offer FWA on its own mobile network, converting the substitute into an upsell product [#A]

  • Substitute: WhatsApp/OTT messaging has permanently disrupted legacy SMS revenue (down 80-90% over 10 years across European operators); this substitution is complete and already priced into OTE's revenue base — not an incremental risk [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet: Inversion case on barriers + disruption — the satellite broadband threat to rural Greece is specifically underpinned by Starlink's 2023 Greek launch and government spectrum allocation; OTE's rural fibre ROI assumptions may need to be revisited as Starlink CPE prices continue to decline; Digi's Greek FTTB rollout is slower than feared but has not stalled — a patient capital build with 5-10 year payback is entirely consistent with PE-backed patient infrastructure investors [#J]
