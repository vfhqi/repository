[W] SCMN-CH — Value-Chain Analysis [AS] @ 19-May-26

Stage: ESA | Role: Researcher | Source: [AS] | Template: Q12 v2.1 (D-RSR-30)
Cohort/Sector Peer Context: No cohort or sector peer research available — all comparative anchors sourced from Swisscom's own disclosed data, Swiss regulatory filings, EU telecom sector base-rates, and sell-side commentary. Peer gaps flagged in QC.

KEY FINDINGS - Verdict — chain overall: Swisscom's value-chain position is structurally defensive but dynamically under moderate pressure on three fronts — B2B pricing, spectrum/network capex costs, and gradual retail channel disintermediation — none individually deal-breaking, but cumulative margin drag is real [#J]

  • Customer-side verdict: Highly atomised residential base (~2.5m broadband + ~6m mobile) insulates revenue against single-customer concentration risk; B2B/enterprise segment is more concentrated and faces more active competitive bidding, but still anchored by long-dated, multi-service contracts [#J]
  • Switching-cost verdict: Multi-play bundling (broadband + TV + mobile) creates genuine 3-4 year stickiness; B2B switching costs are structurally higher (network integration, SLA dependencies); pure-mobile price sensitivity is rising as MVNO/sub-brand competition intensifies [#J]
  • Supplier-side verdict: Network equipment vendors (Ericsson, Nokia, Cisco) hold moderate leverage given 5G rollout capex dependency; energy costs a rising input line; content rights (Swisscom TV) increasingly multi-sourced, reducing any single content-supplier hold [#J]
  • Competitor verdict: Sunrise (now post-UPC integration) is the sharpest competitive threat — converged triple-play competitor now with national cable footprint; Salt growing in price-led mobile but limited in fixed; Swisscom has held ~55-57% mobile revenue share and ~55% broadband share through the integration period [#J]
  • Channel verdict: Swisscom is predominantly direct-to-customer — own retail stores, digital self-service, and direct SME sales dominate; third-party indirect channel is small and declining as a share; digital-first challenger brands eroding the indirect retail foothold [#J]
  • Cross-chain signals verdict: Equipment vendor earnings calls (Ericsson, Nokia) confirm continued Swiss operator capex commitment but note pricing competition among operators — consistent with Swisscom's guided stable capex; expert network calls (anonymised) flag B2B re-procurement cycles shortening [#J]
  • NWC verdict: Swisscom's working capital is structurally negative (subscriber billings collected in advance of service delivery); DSO has remained stable ~30-35 days; capex payables compression risk flagged by one sell-side analyst as vendors tighten payment terms post-COVID [#J]
  • Bear synthesis: The most credible value-chain bear case is a simultaneous deterioration of B2B contract margins (competitive re-bid + Sunrise enterprise push) + energy/spectrum cost inflation that management cannot fully pass through, compressing EBITDA margin from ~32% toward 29-30% over 3 years [#J]
  • Confidence in value-chain favourability: MEDIUM-HIGH — chain position is genuinely defensive; the bear case requires concurrent multi-dimension deterioration, which is possible but not base-case [#J]

§1 — Customer Analysis (concentration + relationships)

Swisscom serves ~2.5 million retail broadband subscribers, ~6 million mobile subscribers, and a sizeable SME/enterprise B2B segment — one of the highest penetration-rate incumbents in European telecom.

  • Verdict — §1: Customer concentration risk is materially low on the residential side; B2B enterprise sub-segment is more concentrated but mitigated by multi-year, multi-service contracts; no single customer exceeds an estimated 1-2% of group revenue [#J]

  • Top-1 concentration: No single residential or corporate customer publicly disclosed as exceeding 1% of CHF 11.1bn group revenue (FY24); Swiss government/federal entities collectively represent an estimated 3-5% of B2B IT Services revenue — below the 5% single-customer threshold that typically flags concentration risk in EU telecom peers [#A]

  • Sub: EU incumbent telco median: top-1 customer typically <2% of revenue for diversified operators (Deutsche Telekom, Proximus, Telenet pattern) — Swisscom consistent [#A]

  • Top-3 concentration: Residential segment is atomised (2.5m+ broadband, 6m+ mobile); B2B IT Services (Swisscom IT Services entity) has greater enterprise concentration — estimated top 5 B2B clients ~15-20% of IT Services sub-revenue (~4-5% of group) based on disclosed contract awards [#A]

  • Sub: Swisscom has disclosed multi-year managed IT services contracts with Swiss federal government, SBB (Swiss Federal Railways), Migros Group, and several cantonal administrations [#A]

  • Major customer — federal/public: Swiss federal and cantonal government entities are the most identifiable large-customer cluster; SBB (Swiss Federal Railways) disclosed as a long-term managed network + IT services client; these are sticky, low-churn, contract-anchored clients [#A]

  • Sub: Public-sector contracts in Swiss telco have renewal rates consistently above 90% per Swisscom's own commentary in investor presentations [#A]

  • Major customer — enterprise private: Migros, Nestlé, and Swiss financial institutions (UBS, Credit Suisse legacy entities) cited as enterprise IT Services / connectivity clients; UBS/CS consolidation post-2023 merger creates modest churn risk in the enterprise banking segment [#A]

  • Sub: Credit Suisse IT contract legacy is an acknowledged revenue headwind in Swisscom's IT Services segment commentary (FY23 results call) [#A]

  • Customer profile — residential: Predominantly Swiss-resident households; above-average income profile (Swiss GDP per capita ~CHF 90k); high willingness-to-pay historically supports premium ARPU (~CHF 70-75/month blended mobile, above EU median of ~€20-30) [#A]

  • Sub: Swiss residential customers are among the least price-elastic in European telecom — OFCOM consumer survey data consistently shows service reliability rated above price as switching motivation [#A]

  • Contract length — residential: Broadband and convergent bundle contracts typically 12-24 months with auto-renewal; multi-play (broadband + TV + mobile) effectively extends economic lock-in to 3-4 years even when formal contract is 12 months [#J]

  • Sub: Contract length is in line with EU telco peer median (12-24 months for convergent plays) — not an outlier [#A]

  • Contract length — B2B: Enterprise managed services and IT outsourcing contracts typically 3-5 years with service extension options; SBB contract publicly disclosed as 7-year managed network deal — structural anchor [#A]

  • Sub: Long B2B contract terms are consistent with Swiss market norms and slightly above EU enterprise telco median (~3 years per Analysys Mason benchmark) [#A]

  • Customer churn — mobile: Swisscom disclosed postpaid mobile churn of ~10-11% annualised (FY23-24) — below the EU incumbent telco median of ~12-15% and well below challenger/MVNO median of ~20%+ [#A]

  • Sub: Churn has held stable despite Sunrise/UPC integration completing in 2023 — no material acceleration observed in disclosed H1-24 figures [#A]

  • Customer churn — broadband: Broadband churn is lower than mobile — estimated 7-9% annually based on subscriber count movement disclosures; below EU broadband incumbent median (~10-12%) [#A]

  • Sub: Swisscom's FTTH rollout (targeting 90%+ Swiss coverage) is a churn-reduction mechanism — customers on fibre churn at lower rates than copper in comparable EU markets [#J]

  • Retention rate signal: Swisscom management (FY24 results call) noted "customer loyalty metrics improving" in residential — NPS not publicly disclosed in numeric form but characterised as "above market benchmark" [#A]

  • Sub: Sell-side [AS-UBS] notes Swisscom consistently scores #1 in Swiss customer satisfaction surveys (Connect magazine Swiss market ranking 2022-24) [#A]

  • Satisfaction signal — B2B IT Services: IT Services segment has faced some client criticism on project delivery timelines — flagged in Swisscom's own FY22 annual report as a focus area; recovery in client satisfaction scores noted in FY24 [#A]

  • Sub: One industry expert call (anonymised CIO, Swiss financial services, [AS-Expert]) noted "Swisscom IT delivery quality has improved materially in 2023-24 vs 2021-22 trough" [#A]

  • Lost customer signal: Credit Suisse business represents a disclosed headwind as UBS integrates its acquired entity — Swisscom IT Services segment guided to absorb ~CHF 50-70m revenue headwind over 2024-26 from CS rationalisation [#A]

  • Sub: No other major named enterprise customer loss disclosed publicly in FY23-24 reporting period [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet — §1: What's the bearish read on customer relationships? The Credit Suisse/UBS consolidation headwind may be larger than guided if UBS in-sources further IT services; more broadly, Swisscom's IT Services segment may be losing ground to hyperscaler (AWS, Azure) adoption among Swiss enterprises, which would structurally shrink the addressable B2B managed-services base even while existing contracts renew — an erosion that would not yet show in churn statistics [#J]

§2 — Switching Costs + Pricing Leverage

  • Verdict — §2: Switching costs are meaningfully above EU telco median for residential multi-play and B2B enterprise; pricing leverage is partially constrained in mobile by MVNO/sub-brand competition but Swisscom has demonstrated consistent ARPU maintenance in broadband and convergent bundles [#J]

  • Switching cost — product: Residential multi-play bundle switches require simultaneous coordination of broadband (physical line changeover), TV (equipment + subscription migration), and mobile (SIM/number port) — estimated 4-6 hours customer effort and 2-4 week service gap risk; strong inertia factor [#J]

  • Sub: Comparable EU markets (BT, Proximus) show multi-play churn 30-40% below single-play — Swisscom's convergent penetration (~50%+ of broadband customers also hold Swisscom mobile) amplifies this stickiness [#A]

  • Switching cost — integration (B2B): B2B enterprise clients have deep network/IP infrastructure integration with Swisscom's managed services platform — switching requires re-tendering, new vendor qualification, parallel-run period, and staff retraining; estimated 6-18 months transition for a major enterprise [#J]

  • Sub: [AS-Expert] (anonymised former Swisscom enterprise sales director): "Once a client is on our managed SD-WAN platform, displacement is very rare — maybe once in 7-10 years at contract break" [#A]

  • Switching cost — training (residential): Low — Swisscom apps and interfaces are standard; residential training switching cost is not a structural barrier [#J]

  • Switching cost — qualification : ❌ Paradox: Swisscom's FTTH rollout reduces switching costs over time — as competitor access to Swisscom's fibre network (via wholesale/IRU agreements mandated by ComCom) improves, Sunrise and Salt can offer FTTH-grade product without the customer needing to change physical infrastructure [#J]

  • Sub: ComCom mandated FTTH wholesale access from 2024 — this is a structural switch-cost reducer vs the copper era; medium-term watch item [#A]

  • Pricing pass-through — residential: Swisscom raised retail broadband prices by ~CHF 5/month in 2023 (first meaningful price increase in 5 years) with minimal disclosed churn response; consistent with Swiss consumer price-inelasticity pattern [#A]

  • Sub: [AS-Deutsche Bank] notes this increase added ~CHF 50-60m incremental ARPU revenue in FY23 with no apparent volume loss — positive pricing power signal [#A]

  • Pricing pass-through — mobile: ARPU trend is mixed — blended mobile ARPU has been under modest pressure (~-1 to -2% pa) from mix-shift toward lower-cost plans, offset partially by upsell to higher-data plans; net effect near-flat [#A]

  • Sub: [AS-Berenberg] flags that Wingo (Swisscom's sub-brand) cannibalises some value-end revenue — management argues this is defensive retention, not pricing-power loss [#A]

  • Renewal pricing — B2B: B2B enterprise contracts at renewal are increasingly subject to competitive tender — multiple sell-side analysts (UBS, ZKB) note Sunrise enterprise team has become more aggressive in pursuing Swisscom B2B accounts since 2023 [#A]

  • Sub: Swisscom has disclosed modest compression in IT Services EBITDA margin (FY22-23) partly attributed to competitive pricing concessions on contract renewal [#A]

  • Competitive bidding — enterprise: ComCom and SECO public procurement rules require competitive tendering for federal IT contracts above threshold values — Swisscom participates as incumbent but cannot prevent competitive bids [#A]

  • Sub: [AS-Expert] (anonymised Swiss federal procurement consultant): "Swisscom typically wins re-tenders for large federal contracts because of the incumbency advantage and security clearance advantage — but it does have to price competitively now, which wasn't always true 10 years ago" [#A]

  • Customer pushback — volume loss signal: No material disclosed volume loss in residential; in mobile, data from BAKOM (Swiss regulator) shows Swisscom's SIM market share declined from ~58% to ~55% between 2019-2023 — modest but real share transfer to Salt and Sunrise [#A]

  • Sub: The share loss is concentrated in price-sensitive youth/prepaid segments — convergent, postpaid households remain largely sticky [#A]

  • Concession signal — B2B: IT Services EBITDA margin guidance revised lower in FY23 (guided to ~8-9% from prior ~10-11%) — Swisscom attributed this partly to competitive pressure at renewal and partly to delivery investment; pricing concession component not cleanly isolable [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet — §2: What's the bearish read on switching costs and pricing leverage? FTTH wholesale access mandated by ComCom is the structurally underappreciated switching-cost reducer — as fibre passes more Swiss homes and wholesale terms improve, the physical-infrastructure moat that underpinned Swisscom's residential stickiness will erode; Sunrise and potential new entrants can increasingly offer equivalent service without the customer facing switching friction; this mechanism plays out over 3-5 years, beyond Richard's typical 18-24 month hold, but pricing leverage could begin softening earlier as competitive offers improve in quality [#J]

§3 — Supplier Analysis (concentration + input costs)

  • Verdict — §3: Supplier landscape is moderately concentrated in network equipment (Ericsson, Nokia duopoly) and energy; Swisscom has partially de-risked single-source exposure through dual-vendor strategy; content rights and IT hardware suppliers are well-diversified; capex-cycle dependency on network vendors is the primary leverage point [#J]

  • Supplier concentration — network equipment: Ericsson and Nokia collectively supply virtually all of Swisscom's RAN (radio access network) and core equipment — Swisscom disclosed a dual-vendor 5G rollout strategy (Ericsson for mobile core + selected RAN, Nokia for complementary RAN) but is structurally dependent on this duopoly [#A]

  • Sub: Ericsson Q4-23 and Q1-24 earnings calls confirm Swiss market as "stable, committed customer" — no disclosed pricing disputes; Ericsson's Swiss revenue meaningful but not disclosed as a material customer individually [#A]

  • Supplier concentration — fixed network: Cisco dominant in IP routing/switching for Swisscom's fixed backbone; Huawei was partially displaced from Swisscom's network post-2019 security review — replacement costs absorbed but concentration on Cisco/Nokia for fixed increased [#A]

  • Sub: Geopolitical risk from Huawei displacement is largely resolved historically; residual risk is Cisco pricing power on backbone upgrades [#A]

  • Single-source risk — FTTH passive infrastructure: Swisscom builds its own FTTH civil infrastructure (ducts, cables) using a basket of civil engineering contractors — no single-source dependency; Swiss construction market is competitive [#J]

  • Sub: Key civil suppliers include Cablex (partially Swisscom-owned) — partial vertical integration reduces single-source risk in civil rollout [#A]

  • Long-term partnership — network vendors: Swisscom disclosed multi-year equipment supply agreements with both Ericsson and Nokia (standard telco practice); these partnerships include service and maintenance, providing cost predictability [#A]

  • Sub: [AS-JP Morgan] notes that Ericsson and Nokia have been moderating equipment price inflation post-supply-chain disruption of 2021-22; price environment for network equipment is easing in 2024 [#A]

  • Input cost trend — network equipment: Deflating from 2023 peak — Ericsson and Nokia earnings calls (Q3-23 through Q1-24) both reference easing component costs and improved supply chain normalisation; equipment cost inflation that peaked at ~10-15% above 2020 baseline is moderating toward +3-5% [#A]

  • Sub: [AS-JP Morgan] estimates that for European incumbents, network equipment capex per unit is 5-8% below 2022 peak levels by 2024 — supportive of Swisscom's guided stable capex envelope [#A]

  • Input cost trend — energy: Electricity costs remain elevated vs 2019 baseline — Swiss electricity prices rose sharply in 2022-23 following European energy crisis; Swisscom disclosed energy costs as ~3-4% of opex, with CHF 50-70m incremental cost in FY23 vs FY21; some recovery in 2024 as spot prices ease [#A]

  • Sub: Swisscom has partially hedged electricity costs through forward contracts and is investing in energy efficiency (network consolidation reduces power consumption per bit) [#A]

  • Input cost trend — labour: Swiss labour market is tight and wages are structurally elevated (Swiss median tech salary 30-40% above EU equivalent); Swisscom's headcount reduction programme (IT Services restructuring) is offset by wage inflation on retained staff [#A]

  • Sub: Labour is Swisscom's largest opex line (~35-40% of opex) — wage growth of ~2-3% pa is a persistent cost tailwind that requires either volume growth or efficiency gains to absorb [#A]

  • Input cost trend — spectrum: Swiss 5G spectrum auction (2019) cost Swisscom ~CHF 196m; next renewal/refarming cycles are not imminent (licences to 2034) — limited near-term spectrum cost pressure, but future auction pricing uncertain [#A]

  • Sub: EU spectrum cost benchmarks vary widely (UK, Germany paid much more for 5G than Switzerland) — Swisscom's relatively low historical spectrum cost is a structural advantage [#A]

  • Multi-source — content rights (Swisscom TV): Swisscom TV content rights are sourced from multiple providers — major sport rights (Swiss football, Champions League) obtained through multi-year deals, typically 2-4 years; no single content owner holds disproportionate leverage [#A]

  • Sub: Loss of a single sports rights bundle (e.g., if Sky Sports/DAZN overbid on Swiss football) would be a churn risk for Swisscom TV but is not existential — Swisscom TV is a retention mechanism, not a profit centre [#J]

  • Supplier bargaining power — network vendors: Moderate-to-high — the Ericsson/Nokia duopoly means Swisscom cannot easily walk away from either vendor; however, dual-vendor strategy provides some negotiating leverage and switching optionality (albeit at high transition cost) [#J]

  • Sub: Ericsson Q1-24 earnings noted "pricing discipline maintained across European accounts" — not indicating meaningful concessions to large incumbents [#A]

  • Supply chain risk — geopolitical: Reduced vs 2021-22 peak — semiconductor supply chains have normalised; Huawei displacement largely complete; Taiwan-strait risk remains a background scenario but not an immediate supply constraint [#J]

  • Sub: Nokia and Ericsson have diversified manufacturing away from single-geography concentration post-COVID — supply chain resilience improved per both companies' investor day disclosures [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet — §3: What's the bearish read on supplier dynamics? The most underappreciated risk is labour cost compounding — Swiss wage inflation at 2-3% pa on a 35-40% opex cost base creates a ~0.7-1.2pp EBITDA margin headwind annually that Swisscom must continuously offset through efficiency; if the IT Services restructuring programme (headcount reduction targeting ~800 roles over 2023-25) encounters union resistance or delays, the efficiency offset disappears; secondarily, if Ericsson/Nokia were to merge or if one exited the market, Swisscom's negotiating position on network equipment would deteriorate sharply — a low-probability but high-impact single-source scenario [#J]

§4 — Competitor Analysis (market share + responses)

  • Verdict — §4: Swisscom has held market leadership positions across mobile (~55-57% revenue share) and broadband (~55% share) despite Sunrise/UPC integration; competitive intensity is rising in enterprise B2B but remains sub-disruptive — no evidence of irrational pricing that would trigger a value-destructive price war [#J]

  • L3Y share trend — mobile revenue: Swisscom mobile revenue share (BAKOM data, Swiss regulator): ~58% in FY20 → ~56% in FY22 → ~55% in FY24 estimated — gradual erosion of ~3pp over four years, largely attributable to Sunrise post-UPC integration completing its customer base consolidation [#A]

  • Sub: Rate of share loss has decelerated in 2023-24 vs 2021-22 transition period — consistent with Sunrise integration disruption now largely absorbed [#A]

  • L3Y share trend — broadband: Swisscom broadband subscriber share: ~58% in FY20 → ~56% in FY22 → ~54-55% FY24 estimated (BAKOM); Sunrise is gaining ~0.5-1pp pa in broadband as cable/HFC network improves post-UPC merger [#A]

  • Sub: Salt has minimal fixed broadband presence — it is a mobile + FTTH niche player in urban areas; not a material broadband share threat at present [#A]

  • Segment share — B2B enterprise: Sunrise has publicly stated enterprise market as a growth priority post-2023; exact B2B market share data not publicly available for Switzerland; Swisscom management estimated ~60-65% enterprise connectivity market share in FY23 investor day [#A]

  • Sub: [AS-ZKB] notes Sunrise hired a new enterprise sales leadership team in 2023 and has been winning some medium-enterprise accounts from Swisscom, particularly in Zurich/German-speaking Switzerland [#A]

  • Geographic share — regional: Swisscom is strongest in German-speaking Switzerland (Zurich, Bern, Basel) and weakest relatively in French-speaking Romandy where Salt has a historical retail presence; no geographic region represents a material gap in Swisscom's network quality or coverage [#A]

  • Sub: Italy exposure via Fastweb adds an entirely separate competitive dynamic (Fastweb vs Telecom Italia, WindTre, Vodafone Italy) that is outside this Swiss-market analysis [#A]

  • Competitive response — price (Sunrise): Sunrise has pursued a value-for-money positioning rather than pure low-cost — Sunrise FTTH + mobile bundles priced ~10-15% below Swisscom equivalents; not a predatory price war, but a persistent pressure band [#A]

  • Sub: [AS-Berenberg] notes Sunrise's pricing discipline has improved post-UPC merger (less promotional intensity than 2021-22 integration period) — normalising competitive environment [#A]

  • Competitive response — product (Sunrise): Sunrise launched converged FTTH + 5G bundle "Sunrise Home" in 2023, directly competitive with Swisscom's "inOne" — product parity has improved materially vs 2020 when Sunrise lacked fixed broadband at scale [#A]

  • Sub: [AS-UBS] argues Sunrise Home product is now "good enough" for ~30-40% of Swiss households but Swisscom's service quality + brand premium retains the majority [#A]

  • Competitive response — price (Salt): Salt operates a price-led strategy in mobile — significantly below Swisscom pricing (~30-40% cheaper on equivalent data plans); Salt's strategy is volume-based but it lacks the enterprise and fixed product suite to threaten Swisscom's convergent positioning [#A]

  • Sub: Salt MVNO/wholesale model limits capex; it is a price disruptor in mobile but not a convergent market threat at current scale [#A]

  • Competitive response — M&A: Sunrise filed a successful bid for Citycable Lausanne in 2024, reinforcing its Romandy cable footprint — incremental Sunrise convergent strength in French-speaking Switzerland; Swisscom's position in Romandy is less dominant anyway so marginal impact limited [#A]

  • Sub: No major M&A reshaping of the Swiss competitive landscape is currently anticipated — ComCom has historically been restrictive on further consolidation [#A]

  • Pricing discipline — market level: Swiss telecom market pricing has been broadly rational — no evidence of the type of destructive price war seen in France (Iliad impact on Orange/SFR) or Germany (Drillisch disruption); Swiss market has two strong players + one price-disruptor in mobile [#A]

  • Sub: [AS-Deutsche Bank] notes Swiss ARPU levels are among the highest in Europe and both Swisscom and Sunrise have financial incentive to preserve premium pricing [#A]

  • Innovation rate — 5G: Swisscom deployed 5G coverage to ~99% of the Swiss population by 2021 — first European operator to reach this coverage milestone; competitive advantage in 5G network quality is acknowledged by independent benchmarks (Ookla, umlaut) [#A]

  • Sub: Sunrise and Salt have caught up on 5G coverage in major urban areas but remain behind Swisscom on rural 5G — the coverage gap is a retention tool in rural residential [#A]

  • New entrant threat: No credible new network entrant in Switzerland — high spectrum costs, dense FTTH build requirements, and small addressable population (~8.7m) create prohibitive entry barriers; virtual/MVNO entrants (Aldi Mobile, M-Budget) operate on Swisscom's network wholesale [#J]

  • Sub: MVNO growth dilutes Swisscom's retail mobile ARPU indirectly but supports wholesale revenue — net effect is modest ARPU mix-down, not volume loss [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet — §4: What's the bearish read on competitive dynamics? Sunrise's enterprise push is more consequential than the mobile share trends suggest — B2B connectivity and IT services are higher-margin segments, and even modest (5-10pp) enterprise share loss to Sunrise could be disproportionately damaging to EBITDA if concentrated in the most profitable mid-market accounts; the innovation threat that is most under-priced is hyperscaler (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud) adoption by Swiss enterprises displacing Swisscom IT Services managed compute/cloud — Swisscom is not well-positioned against hyperscalers' scale economics, and this migration is secular, not cyclical [#J]

§5 — Distributor + Channel Analysis

  • Verdict — §5: Swisscom's predominantly direct distribution model is a structural advantage — direct-to-customer channels (own retail, digital, direct enterprise sales) account for an estimated 80-85% of revenue; indirect retail channel is small but faces ongoing relevance pressure from digital-first channels [#J]

  • Direct-to-customer — retail stores: Swisscom operates ~130 own retail stores across Switzerland — among the densest telco retail footprints per capita in Europe; stores serve both residential and SME customers; primary acquisition and retention touchpoint [#A]

  • Sub: Store count has been gradually optimised (from ~150 in 2018 to ~130 in 2024) — consistent with industry shift to digital-first without abandoning physical presence [#A]

  • Direct-to-customer — digital: MySwisscom app reported ~4m active users as of FY24 investor materials — significant digital self-service penetration; online acquisition now estimated >50% of new residential connections [#A]

  • Sub: Digital channel growth reduces cost-to-serve (no retail commission) and provides first-party data on customer behaviour — positive unit economics trend [#J]

  • Indirect — independent retail: Third-party electronics retailers (MediaMarkt, Interdiscount) sell Swisscom SIM and broadband bundles; estimated <10% of new residential acquisitions via indirect retail — low dependency, limited channel leverage over Swisscom [#A]

  • Sub: MediaMarkt also carries Sunrise and Salt — Swisscom's product is not exclusive at indirect retail; however, Swisscom's brand strength means it typically gets shelf prominence [#A]

  • Channel concentration: No single indirect channel partner represents a meaningful revenue concentration risk — total indirect channel estimated <15% of consumer revenue; Swisscom's own stores + digital dominate [#A]

  • Sub: This is a favourable structural position vs, e.g., US cable operators that depend heavily on third-party installation/distribution networks [#A]

  • Distributor leverage: Minimal — Swisscom's brand and network quality mean it does not need to pay meaningful distribution premiums to third-party retailers; MediaMarkt/Interdiscount do not have pricing leverage over Swisscom's wholesale access terms [#J]

  • Channel evolution — DTC shift: Consistent trend toward digital-first — online sales as % of residential new connects has grown from ~30% (2018) to >50% (2024 estimated); own retail store traffic declining but stores maintained for complex product sales and retention visits [#A]

  • Sub: [AS-Expert] (anonymised former Swisscom retail strategy director): "The economics clearly favour digital — cost per digital acquisition is maybe 40-50% of a store acquisition; management is accelerating DTC digital shift in 2024-25" [#A]

  • Platform — wholesale/MVNO: Swisscom acts as a wholesale platform supplier to MVNOs (Aldi Mobile, M-Budget Mobile, others) — this is a distribution channel in reverse: Swisscom supplies third-party brands that then retail to price-sensitive segments Swisscom doesn't want to serve directly [#J]

  • Sub: MVNO wholesale revenue is structurally lower margin than retail but contributes to network utilisation and reduces the incentive for MVNOs to seek alternative infrastructure [#J]

  • Disintermediation risk: OTT players (WhatsApp, FaceTime) have long since disintermediated Swisscom's voice/SMS revenue — this is historic and priced in; the forward disintermediation risk is in enterprise IT Services where cloud platforms bypass Swisscom's managed services middleware [#J]

  • Sub: IT Services is ~10-12% of Swisscom group revenue — the disintermediation risk is contained but directionally negative [#J]

  • Channel margin — retail stores: Own store network is a cost centre with rising opex (Swiss retail labour, prime locations); digital migration is the efficiency lever but requires maintaining physical presence for elderly/non-digital customer segments [#A]

  • Sub: No specific store-level P&L disclosed; sell-side [AS-Citi] estimates retail network costs ~CHF 150-200m pa — roughly 1.3-1.8% of revenue, in line with EU peer benchmark range [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet — §5: What's the bearish read on channel dynamics? The DTC digital shift is positive for cost but creates risk of reduced customer intimacy — digital-only customers may have lower brand loyalty and higher propensity to price-compare online (comparison sites like comparis.ch already facilitate transparent price switching); if Swisscom accelerates store closures faster than digital loyalty can be built, it may inadvertently reduce the retention stickiness that physical touchpoints provide, particularly in the 50+ age demographic that represents a high-value, high-ARPU segment [#J]

§6 — Cross-Value-Chain Signals (LOAD-BEARING for forward credibility)

  • Verdict — §6: Cross-chain signals are broadly consistent with Swisscom's management commentary — supplier calls confirm stable capex demand, customer-proximate signals (enterprise IT decisions) show modest cloud migration headwinds, competitor signals confirm rational pricing; the one divergence is that expert network calls indicate B2B re-procurement cycles are shortening faster than management guidance implies [#J]

  • Supplier — customer demand (Ericsson Q4-23 call): Ericsson CEO Börje Ekholm stated: "Western European operators remain on track with 5G deployment commitments" — consistent with Swisscom's guided CHF 1.7-1.8bn annual capex; no disclosed demand pull-back from Swiss/CE European operators [#A]

  • Sub: Ericsson specifically called out "stable recurring demand from tier-1 European incumbents" — aligns with Swisscom's guided capex stability [#A]

  • Supplier — pricing pressure (Nokia Q1-24 call): Nokia CFO Marco Wiren noted "equipment pricing is normalising post-supply-chain disruption; we are not seeing meaningful incremental price pressure from operators" — supports Swisscom's ability to maintain capex envelope without cost overrun [#A]

  • Sub: Nokia flagged some margin pressure on services/software contracts — watch whether Swisscom's Nokia service agreements carry similar dynamics [#A]

  • Supplier — energy (VERBUND AG / Swiss utility proxies): Swiss electricity spot prices in 2024 have moderated from 2022-23 peaks (European Energy Exchange data); Axpo (Swiss utility) FY23 report confirms industrial energy contract renewals pricing lower than 2022-23 peak hedged rates — energy cost tailwind for Swisscom's FY24-25 opex vs FY22-23 peak [#A]

  • Sub: Swisscom's energy hedging policy means the benefit lags spot — full relief in energy costs expected to flow through 2024-25 based on hedge roll patterns disclosed in FY23 annual report [#A]

  • Customer — demand outlook (SBB investor day 2023): SBB (Swiss Federal Railways, major B2B customer) investor day materials reference ongoing investment in digital infrastructure — no indication of IT outsourcing contract termination or in-sourcing strategy; SBB explicitly referenced Swisscom partnership as strategic [#A]

  • Sub: SBB's capex plan through 2030 includes digital network modernisation — a potential source of incremental contract expansion for Swisscom IT Services [#A]

  • Customer — demand outlook (Swiss banking sector proxies): UBS integration of Credit Suisse is creating a rationalisation of IT vendor panels — UBS investor materials reference intent to consolidate IT vendors post-merger; Swisscom IT Services is exposed to this consolidation but also positioned as a potential winner if UBS consolidates onto its platform [#A]

  • Sub: Net effect of CS merger on Swisscom IT Services revenues guided as -CHF 50-70m headwind over 2 years — manageable but clearly a negative demand signal [#A]
