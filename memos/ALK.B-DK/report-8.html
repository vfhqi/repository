§1 — Industry Overview + Value Chain Economics

J-front verdict: The global AIT market is a structurally growing, specialty-pharma-favoured niche — ~€1.5-2.0bn addressable for leading players, GDP+2-3pp CAGR, with margin power concentrated firmly at the innovator-manufacturer stage of the value chain [#J]

Industry size: Global AIT market estimated at ~€1.2-1.5bn in 2023 (broader allergy immunotherapy and diagnostics ecosystem ~€3-4bn); ALK's served market (injectable SCIT + sublingual SLIT tablets) is the prescription, branded manufacturer segment [#A]
- Sub-bullet: CAGR forecasts cluster at 7-10% pa through 2028-2030 across multiple public industry forecasts (Allied Market Research, GlobalData), with SLIT-tablets growing faster (~12-15%) than legacy SCIT injectables (~3-5%) [#A]
- Sub-bullet: The US is the largest single geography (~35-40% of AIT revenue); Germany/DACH is the second-largest (~20%); France, UK, Nordics, Japan, and China are material but smaller [#A]

Structural driver: Allergy prevalence rising secularly — WHO and EAACI data show 30-40% of developed-world populations affected by allergic rhinitis; asthma allergic subtype ~300m globally; driving physician-pull demand independent of promotional spend [#J]
- Sub-bullet: Diagnosis penetration is very low — fewer than 5% of allergy sufferers receive disease-modifying AIT; vast under-treatment creates secular demand runway unrelated to competitive intensity [#A]
- Sub-bullet: Regulatory tailwind: EMA's standardised dossier framework for allergen products and FDA's BLA pathway for SLIT-tablets create regulatory barriers that simultaneously protect incumbents while restricting generics [#A]

Geographic split: EU5 (Germany, France, UK, Italy, Spain) account for ~50% of global branded AIT revenue; US contributes ~30-35%; rest-of-world ~15-20% [#A]
- Sub-bullet: Germany is structurally the most attractive AIT market — broad reimbursement (GKV covers SCIT + SLIT-tablets), high physician acceptance, ALK #1 position [#A]
- Sub-bullet: US AIT market is structurally different — historically SCIT-dominated via allergist-compounded mixtures not subject to BLA; FDA's SLIT-tablet approvals (Odactra for HDM 2017, GRASTEK/Ragwitek for pollen) create a regulated layer ALK participates in [#A]

Fragmentation: The global AIT market is moderately concentrated at the top (top 3 players — ALK, Stallergenes Greer, LETI Pharma — account for ~60-70% of branded prescription revenues) with long tail of regional players and hospital pharmacy compounders [#A]
- Sub-bullet: SCIT segment (injectable) is significantly more fragmented due to custom-compounded extracts (unregulated in US) and regional European specialty producers [#A]
- Sub-bullet: SLIT-tablet segment is more concentrated — ALK and Stallergenes Greer hold the majority of EMA/FDA-approved SLIT-tablet dossiers; this sub-market is consolidating [#A]

Consolidation: Trend direction is toward further concentration in SLIT-tablets specifically — Stallergenes Greer sold to private equity (Montagu) in 2015, ALK organic, others subscale; capital requirements for dossier maintenance + clinical development favour incumbents [#J]
- Sub-bullet: Regional SCIT players in secondary EU markets are declining or exiting — ALK has been selectively withdrawing from subscale markets (several EM and Southern European SCIT markets discontinued 2019-2022) [#A]

§3 — Barriers to Entry + Exit + Disruption Forces

J-front verdict: AIT barriers to entry are genuinely high — regulatory dossiers, clinical evidence, manufacturing quality, and physician trust combine to create multi-year and multi-hundred-million-euro entry costs; disruption forces are real but slow-moving [#J]

Regulatory barrier: EMA and FDA require species-specific allergen BLAs or standardised dossier approvals for each allergen-route combination — typical development timeline 8-12 years, investment €150-300m per dossier, with no certainty of approval [#A]
- Sub-bullet: Vanillin new entrant attempting to replicate ALK's grass-pollen SLIT-tablet would face 8-12 year runway from Phase I to approval — this is a structural moat, not a governance artefact [#A]
- Sub-bullet: US regulatory complexity is amplified by **FDA's biologics pathway * for SLIT-tablets vs EU centralised/national dossier paths; dual-path compliance cost is prohibitive for subscale entrants [#A]

Capital requirement: ALK's own R&D spend runs at ~DKK 500-700m pa (~€65-95m pa; ~15-18% of revenue) — the sustained investment needed to maintain and expand a SLIT-tablet dossier portfolio is a significant entry barrier for smaller players [#A]
- Sub-bullet: Manufacturing GMP compliance (Good Manufacturing Practice) for allergen products requires dedicated clean-room facilities, allergen-specific QC, and regulatory surveillance; capex-intensive and difficult to outsource [#A]

Scale economy: Allergen standardisation, clinical programme infrastructure, and medical affairs/field force coverage exhibit meaningful fixed-cost structure — ALK's revenue scale (~€500m) provides a per-unit cost advantage over entrants at €100-200m scale [#A]
- Sub-bullet: Marketing and prescriber-education investment is largely fixed per geography — ALK's medical affairs teams in Germany/France/US represent fixed-cost moats that smaller entrants must replicate in full [#A]

Switching cost: Physician switching costs are moderate — once a physician has prescribed a SLIT-tablet course (3+ years treatment duration), the patient and physician are committed for the treatment episode; however switching at the next new-patient prescription is easy [#A]
- Sub-bullet: Patient switching costs are low within a season but behavioural inertia (3-year treatment courses, established allergy practice protocols) creates de facto stickiness [#A]

IP: Core SLIT-tablet patents for grass pollen (Grazax) and HDM (Acarizax) are protecting period-specific — Grazax patent protection has been running off in parts of Europe, creating some generic/biosimilar risk for older products; newer formulations extend IP life [#A]
- Sub-bullet: ❌ IP barrier is weakening for older SLIT-tablet products — generic competition in Grazax (grass pollen) was a concern from c.2020; ALK has countered with formulation improvements and new molecules [#A]

Distribution barrier: Specialty pharma distribution (allergist channel, hospital clinic, GP) is established and accessible — distribution is not a primary barrier; it is a cost and execution item [#A]

Lock-in mechanism: Once a physician practice has committed to ALK as primary SLIT-tablet supplier (trained staff, stocked formularies, reimbursement codes established), switching is operationally inconvenient — the lock-in is institutional, not contractual [#A]

Disruption force — biologics substitution: Dupixent (Sanofi) and other IL-4/IL-13 biologics (Fasenra, Tezspire) compete for the severe allergic patient pool — not directly substituting AIT but capturing the "most treatable" and "highest-acuity" allergic patients [#J]
- Sub-bullet: Mechanistic difference: biologics suppress symptoms without disease modification; AIT modifies the immune response (potentially curative); this distinction supports AIT positioning but requires sustained physician education to maintain [#A]
- Sub-bullet: Payer risk: if biologics gain head-to-head payer preference (cost-effectiveness argument for dupilumab vs 3-year AIT course) in Germany or France, AIT reimbursement could narrow [#J]

Disruption force — technology (digital/AI): No credible near-term disruption from digital therapeutics or AI-driven AIT alternatives; the immunological mechanism requires biological allergen exposure — AI accelerates target identification but does not substitute for allergen dressings [#A]

Disruption force — food allergy entrants: Food allergy OIT (oral immunotherapy) is an adjacent innovative-entrant space — Aimmune's Palforzia, DBV's Viaskin; ALK has explicitly exited this space; food allergy AIT does not directly compete with ALK's inhalant-allergy franchise [#A]
- Sub-bullet: ALK's peanut AIT programme was discontinued in 2017-2018 — a strategic retreat rather than a disruption event, but it signals the difficulty of food allergy as adjacency [#A]

Adjacency entry: Dermatology/biologic companies (AbbVie, Regeneron) are entering severe atopic disease but not AIT specifically; their commercial path bypasses the allergist AIT consultation, it does not displace it [#A]

Substitutes: The primary substitute for prescription AIT is symptomatic treatment (antihistamines, nasal corticosteroids — cheap, generic, no regulatory barrier) — this is the true competitor at population level, not SLIT-tablet alternatives [#J]
- Sub-bullet: Symptomatic treatment's generic pricing anchors the cost-benefit case for AIT — payers compare 3-year AIT course cost vs lifetime symptomatic medication cost; in Germany this comparison favours AIT under GKV rules [#A]

Sceptical bullet (§3): The inversion case on barriers is that IP erosion + biologic competition compound simultaneously — if Grazax/grass-pollen generic entry materialises in remaining EU markets at the same time that Dupixent captures GP-referred severe allergic rhinitis patients, ALK's total addressable patient pool narrows from both ends; the regulatory barrier that protects incumbents also protects the biologics players, creating an asymmetric moat structure where new AIT entrants are blocked but biologic substitution is not [#J]
