KEY FINDINGS (BLUF) — J→A→I ORDER

Verdict: Elia is among Europe's most compelling regulated infrastructure compounders at a structural inflection point — the energy transition mandates rapid RAB growth, and Elia holds monopoly franchises in two of the continent's most investment-intensive grids.

  • Franchise quality: Elia holds two legally protected monopoly franchises — Belgian high-voltage grid and 80.4% of 50Hertz (one of Germany's four TSOs) — delivering a near-zero-churn customer base and regulatory tariff certainty across multi-year reset periods. No competitor can enter either market; the moat is perpetual by legislation. [#J]

  • RAB growth velocity — exceptional vs peers: Group RAB reached €18.5bn at end-2024 (up 27.8% year-on-year) and is guided at >20% average annual growth to 2028 under a €31.6bn capex plan — the fastest RAB compounding among all listed European TSO peers. Terna (Italy) runs c.€16bn RAB at c.6% CAGR; REE (Spain) c.€10bn at c.4% CAGR; Elia's growth trajectory is 3–5x the sector median. [#J]

§1 — GENERAL

J-front verdict: Elia is a large-cap Belgian-listed regulated utility with an unusually high-velocity RAB growth profile driven by two legally mandated capex programmes — it is more accurately described as a regulated infrastructure compounder than a static utility.

Elia Group SA/NV is a Belgian holding company owning: (1) Elia Transmission Belgium (ETB) — Belgium's sole high-voltage electricity transmission system operator (TSO); and (2) 80.4% of Eurogrid International, which owns 100% of 50Hertz Transmission GmbH — one of Germany's four TSOs. Listed on Euronext Brussels since June 2005 (ticker: ELI), Elia is headquartered at Keizerslaan 20, Brussels. The group currently employs approximately 4,500 people across both operating subsidiaries.

§2 — COMPANY DETAILS

J-front verdict: Elia's revenue model is structurally simple and almost entirely de-risked from demand, pricing, and competitive forces — RAB growth translates mechanistically into regulated profit, with the key variables being regulatory allowed return and capex execution speed.

Revenue Model — Regulated Tariff Architecture

  • Belgium (ETB) — CREG tariff 2024–2027:
  • CREG (Commission for Electricity and Gas Regulation) approves a 4-year tariff in advance, covering allowed depreciation, operating costs, and a regulated equity return
  • The 2024–2027 tariff was approved November 2023; represents a c.77% cumulative rise over 2020–2023 tariffs, reflecting the €6.4bn Belgian investment plan
  • Allowed equity return: tied to 10-year Belgian government bond rate (OLO10Y); the tariff model assumes OLO10Y of 3.27%; actual OLO10Y tracking this level in 2025 means tariff assumptions broadly valid
  • Gearing target: 40% equity / 60% debt; deviation from target affects allowed return calculation

  • Pass-through mechanisms: Balancing costs, network losses, system services, and offshore levies are largely passed through to grid users, protecting ETB's net margin

  • Germany (50Hertz) — BNetzA 4th Regulatory Period (2024–2028):

  • BNetzA (Bundesnetzagentur — Federal Network Agency) regulates under a cost-plus incentive framework
  • Revenue allowance = base year 2021 costs, adjusted by general productivity factor (Xgen), individual efficiency factor (Xind = 100% for 50Hertz, confirming benchmark efficiency), and inflation (with 2-year lag)
  • Equity return: base rate of 2.3% for assets entering before January 2024; for new assets from 2024, a floating base rate applies (linked to market rates, improving as rates rise)
  • 50Hertz's Xind of 100% is a meaningful positive signal — no efficiency deduction applied, confirming BNetzA views 50Hertz as an efficient operator at the efficiency frontier.

  • EEG surcharge (Renewable Energy Sources levy): passed through to consumers; creates revenue volatility at gross level but is earnings-neutral; the large German revenue swings year-on-year (€3.6bn Germany vs prior year) are largely EEG movements

  • Revenue mix (2024 adjusted net profit):

  • Germany (50Hertz, at Elia's 80.4% economic share): €307.9m — c.60% of group
  • Belgium (ETB): €213.8m — c.40% of group
  • Germany's profit contribution growing faster: 50Hertz RAB is larger (c.€11.6bn+ at 100%) and growing faster than Belgium (c.€4.3bn), so the profit mix will shift further toward Germany through 2028.

Business Model — How Regulated Value Is Created

  • Core mechanic: RAB × allowed equity return rate = regulated equity income. Grow RAB; earn more. The mechanism is simple, transparent, and auditable. Elia publishes RAB figures quarterly.
  • Capex = earnings-accretive (not dilutive): Unlike most businesses, capital expenditure at Elia is earnings-accretive. Each euro invested in a commissioned grid asset begins earning a regulated return. The risk is timing: assets must be commissioned (in service) to earn; construction-in-progress does not earn.
  • Incentive payments: Both CREG and BNetzA frameworks include outperformance mechanisms — costs delivered below the allowed amount are retained for part of the regulatory period. 50Hertz's 2024 adjusted net profit of €307.9m (+€89.4m vs 2023) was partly driven by opex outperformance.
  • Allowed cost recovery: Full recovery of prudently incurred capital. "Prudent" determination is the key regulatory judgment — major projects (Princess Elisabeth Island, SuedOstLink) require regulatory sign-off to be included in RAB.

Customer Side — Captive, Zero Churn

  • Customer base: Legally mandated grid users include: distribution system operators (DSOs), large industrial users connecting at high voltage, independent power producers (offshore wind farms, conventional generators), and interconnector operators. No customer has any choice of TSO.
  • Revenue certainty: Tariff revenue is effectively a regulatory toll — electricity flows across Elia's wires regardless of retail electricity price, demand level, or fuel mix. Volume risk is negligible; small volume deviation mechanisms exist but are secondary.
  • Regulatory moat as competitive moat substitute: Elia's moat is the legally mandated exclusive franchise under EU Directive 2009/72/EC (recast as Directive 2019/944) — EU unbundling rules prohibit competing TSO entry. This is a statutory, permanent moat.

§3 — INDUSTRY CONTEXT

J-front verdict: European electricity transmission is in a once-in-generation mandatory build-out cycle; Elia is positioned at the intersection of the two most investment-intensive TSO geographies on the continent — the structural tailwind is unusually visible and policy-mandated rather than demand-dependent.

European TSO Sector Structure

  • EU regulatory architecture: EU Directive 2009/72/EC (recast Directive 2019/944) mandates ownership unbundling of TSOs from generation and retail; TSOs must operate as regulated monopolies under national regulators; ENTSO-E coordinates pan-European planning via the Ten-Year Network Development Plan (TYNDP)
  • Four German TSOs: 50Hertz (Elia 80.4%), TenneT (Dutch state-owned, divesting German operations), Amprion (majority RWE-owned pension fund consortium), TransnetBW (majority EnBW, Baden-Württemberg). Each operates a defined geographic zone; zones are non-overlapping and non-contestable.
  • Single Belgian TSO: Elia holds the exclusive legal mandate for transmission at 30–400kV; no competitor is legally permitted; the Elia franchise is embedded in Belgian federal energy law
  • TenneT German divestiture (relevant context): TenneT is divesting its German TSO operations (may create further consolidation dynamics); Germany's four-TSO model is politically established and not expected to change

Energy Transition Tailwinds — Scale and Mandate

  • ENTSO-E TYNDP 2024 investment quantum: >100,000km new transmission lines needed across Europe; total grid investment requirement cited at €477–584bn by 2030–2040 in various EU Commission and ENTSO-E analyses. European TSOs face external funding needs of c.€250bn by 2030 alone. This is a policy-mandated, legally backstopped investment supercycle. [#I]
  • Offshore wind — Belgium: National Energy and Climate Plan targets 5.8 GW offshore wind by 2030 (from c.2.3 GW today); virtually all new capacity connects via Elia's grid and via Princess Elisabeth Island. The island connects 3.5 GW of new offshore wind, with Phase 1 (0.7 GW) operational by 2028 and full 3.5 GW by 2030.
  • Offshore wind — Germany (50Hertz zone): Germany targets 50 GW offshore by 2030 (from c.8 GW today); 50Hertz's Baltic/North Sea zone is one of the primary expansion areas. 50Hertz plans to invest €5.6bn in offshore connections alone by 2026, targeting 7 GW connection capacity. [#I]
  • North-to-south DC corridors (Germany): Germany's grid imbalance — abundant wind in the north (50Hertz zone), industrial demand in the south — requires major DC transmission capacity. SuedOstLink (540km, 50Hertz's section: 270km through Saxony-Anhalt/Saxony/Thuringia) and SuedLink are the flagship projects; first power flow targeted 2027.
  • Electrification demand growth: EV penetration, heat pump rollout, and data centre demand growth all increase grid stress; demand for transmission capacity grows even independent of renewable integration.

Competitive Intensity — Zero by Design

  • Natural monopoly: Electricity transmission is a textbook natural monopoly. Duplicating grid infrastructure is economically irrational and legally prohibited under EU unbundling rules.
  • No competitive threat exists: Unlike generation, retail, or even gas distribution, TSOs face no competitive threat from technology substitution, new entrant, or disruptive business model. This is a structural characteristic, not a temporary condition.
  • Only "competition" is for regulatory capital: The sole competitive dimension is whether Elia can demonstrate to CREG and BNetzA that its investments are prudent and should be included in RAB. Regulatory disallowance is the only value-destroying mechanism in this business.

Disruption Forces — Long Runway, Low Near-Term Risk

  • Distributed generation / micro-grids: Localised generation reduces some long-distance flows but cannot substitute bulk offshore wind transmission capacity; net effect on TSO capex is modestly negative at 2035+ horizon, not material to 2025–2030 thesis. [#I]
  • Grid-scale battery storage: Reduces peak grid stress; supportive to grid stability but complements rather than substitutes transmission capacity; TSOs may earn regulated returns on co-located storage assets in future regulatory frameworks. [#I]
  • HVDC technology learning curve: Ongoing improvements in HVDC (High-Voltage Direct Current) technology reduce cost of long-distance transmission — this is actually supportive of Elia/50Hertz's capex plans, as both are leading HVDC deployers in Europe, and lower HVDC costs improve project economics. [#I]
  • Hydrogen infrastructure: Emerging demand for dedicated hydrogen pipelines creates potential additive RAB opportunity (electrical grid interfaces with electrolysers); Elia has positioning via Fluxys relationships and WindGrid but this is a 2030+ optionality, not a 2025–2028 driver. [#I]

Sceptical bullet (§3): ENTSO-E's €584bn investment figure and TYNDP targets have a poor historical delivery track record — the TYNDP 2010–2020 cycle saw approximately 50% of planned investments delivered on time. Elia's own project pipeline (Princess Elisabeth Island — world's first offshore energy island; SuedOstLink — 270km DC underground cable) involves genuinely novel engineering with no precedent; delivery slippage is a real risk embedded in the ambitious capex projections. [#A]

§4 — BB#2 FOUNDATION QUALITY

J-front verdict: Elia scores strongly on franchise quality, value chain protection, and industry structure; the RONIC-vs-WACC question on legacy 50Hertz assets is the primary quality caveat, and the operator transition (Gustin succeeding Peeters) is the near-term execution variable to track.

§4.1 — Operator Quality

  • Bernard Gustin (CEO from January 2025, born 1968): Former CEO of Brussels Airlines (2008–2018), former co-CEO Lineas (largest private rail freight operator in Europe, 2022–2024). Independent Board Director and Chairman of Elia Group, ETB, and Elia Asset since May 2017 — deep familiarity with Elia at board level over 8 years. MBA from Solvay Business School. Elected Chairman of 50Hertz supervisory board (August 2025 — signals hands-on engagement with German business). Aviation and rail backgrounds differ structurally from regulated grid infrastructure management — the test over 2025–2028 is whether Gustin can maintain the capital execution velocity Peeters established at the €5bn/year level. [#A]

  • Chris Peeters (CEO 2012–Oct 2023) — the architect: Peeters transformed Elia from a purely Belgian TSO into a European infrastructure champion. Key strategic moves: consolidated 50Hertz ownership from 60% to 80.4% (2018); launched the offshore wind strategy and Princess Elisabeth Zone vision; built the capex plan from c.€500m/year to €4.8bn/year; grew group RAB from c.€3bn to c.€14.5bn over 11 years. Under Peeters, Elia's strategic positioning relative to the energy transition was exemplary — he foresaw the TSO investment supercycle a decade before it became consensus. Track record is arguably A-tier for a European utility CEO. [#A]

  • Catherine Vandenborre (interim CEO Oct 2023–Jan 2025): Maintained execution momentum through the transition; delivered a record €4.8bn capex year in 2024; executed the €2.2bn equity raise. Strong financial stewardship during a complex transition — demonstrated continuity under pressure. [#I]

  • Management depth: New Executive Management Board announced December 2024 signals institutional continuity. WindGrid subsidiary and EGI advisory arm suggest management depth and forward-looking capability below board level. [#I]

§4.2 — Advantaged Business (Moat, Generate, RONIC)

  • MOAT — Regulatory franchise + natural monopoly:
    Elia's moat is among the most durable available in European equities. The legally mandated exclusive franchise — protected by EU Directive 2019/944 and Belgian/German national law — cannot expire, be competed away, or be disrupted by technology within any foreseeable investment horizon. The moat's only "renegotiation" is periodic regulatory reset (every 4–5 years), where the return rate — not the franchise itself — is adjusted. [#J]

  • GENERATE — RAB trajectory and earnings engine:

  • End-2024 RAB: €18.5bn group (+27.8% YoY). End-2025 RAB: €22.6bn (+22.5%). Guided >20% annual average growth 2024–2028.
  • Belgium RAB: c.€4.3bn (2024); capex plan c.€9.4bn 2024–2028 implies RAB roughly doubling by 2028.
  • Germany RAB (100% 50Hertz): c.€11.6bn start of 4th period (Jan 2024), growing at €3.5–4bn/year; implies c.€25–28bn at 100% by 2028 (Elia economic share c.€20–22bn).

  • Implied group RAB 2028: c.€35–42bn — roughly doubling from 2024, directly mechanistically driving earnings doubling over same period assuming WACC flat.

  • RONIC vs WACC — the critical value creation question:

  • Belgium: Allowed RoE c.5–6% (OLO10Y + spread); cost of equity c.7–9%; RONIC broadly in line with or slightly below WACC → value creation broadly neutral on Belgian new investment.
  • Germany legacy assets (pre-2024): Base equity return rate 2.3% — materially below any reasonable estimate of Elia's equity cost of capital. Investing at 2.3% equity return vs c.7–9% cost of equity is value-dilutive in an accounting sense; this is the principal quality caveat on the 50Hertz earnings contribution.
  • Germany new assets (post-2024): Floating base rate linked to market rates — with the 10-year Bund yield at c.2.5–3% in 2024–2025, new assets earn closer to 2.5–3% base rate + spread, still below cost of equity but improving meaningfully.

  • Framework from 2029: If WACC model sets allowed equity return at c.4–5%, the German business could approach value-neutral or marginally value-creative territory. This is the key upside scenario from the regulatory reset. [#J]

  • FCF profile: Deeply negative in the current capex cycle. Operating FCF post-capex will remain significantly negative through 2028. This is entirely expected for a TSO in build-out mode and does not impair the investment thesis; but it confirms the business is a RAB-and-earnings story, not a FCF story. Valuation must be done on regulated EV/RAB or P/E multiples. [#A]

§4.3 — Value Chain

  • Demand side: All electricity generators, all distribution operators, and all large industrials connecting to Elia's grid are legally mandated users. There is no elasticity of demand — transmission is essential infrastructure with no substitutes. Volumes grow with electrification trends. [#I]
  • Route to market: Regulated tariff — the "price" Elia charges is entirely set by CREG and BNetzA. No commercial sales function; no pricing risk; no contract negotiations with grid users. The regulatory approval process is Elia's "market access" and "pricing" function. [#I]
  • Supply chain — concentration risk:
    HVDC cable and transformer supply is concentrated among a small number of manufacturers: NKT (Denmark/Germany), Prysmian (Italy), Nexans (France) for cables; ABB, Siemens Energy, GE Vernova for transformers and substations. 50Hertz has already contracted c.€4.6bn in cable deals with NKT and Prysmian. All four German TSOs plus UK, Dutch, Danish, and Belgian TSOs are simultaneously ramping capex — HVDC cable manufacturing capacity is stretched, creating delivery lead-time risk that could push commissioning dates to the right and defer regulated earnings. [#A]
  • Permitting risk: Large-scale transmission infrastructure (SuedOstLink, Princess Elisabeth Island) requires multi-year permitting processes across multiple jurisdictions. German planning law has historically been a bottleneck; the SuedOstLink received full planning approval only after a multi-year process. [#A]

§4.4 — Industry Structure

  • Belgium: Elia is the sole designated Belgian high-voltage TSO. The franchise covers 5,800km of high-voltage lines and 36 substations. Market share: 100% — the concept of market share is irrelevant in a legal monopoly. [#I]
  • Germany: 50Hertz is one of four TSOs, covering northeast Germany — approximately 25% of German land area but c.30% of Germany's onshore wind capacity (wind-rich coastal and northern regions). 50Hertz's zone coincides with the heaviest concentration of German wind generation — giving it structurally the highest mandatory grid investment density among the four German TSOs, supporting the outsized capex programme. [#J]
  • European ranking by RAB: Elia Group at c.€22.6bn (end-2025) ranks 3rd–4th in Europe after National Grid (UK, c.£55bn RAB) and RTE (France, state-owned, unlisted). Among listed European TSOs, Elia is the fastest-growing. [#I]

  • EU Green Deal / Fit for 55 / REPowerEU: European legislation mandates 42.5% renewables by 2030 and 55% GHG reduction — driving mandatory grid investment for at least a decade. Elia's investment plan is formally embedded in the Belgian and German National Energy and Climate Plans. [#I]
