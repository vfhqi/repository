§4 — Board Composition + Independence

J-front verdict: Board is structurally captured — half employee representatives (codetermination law), half municipal/political shareholder appointees — genuine independent oversight of management is limited by design; signal structurally weak for capital-market governance norms, adequate for German municipal-utility context

Board size:
- Aufsichtsrat size: 20 members — typical for a German company of Mainova's size under the Mitbestimmungsgesetz (Codetermination Act); 10 shareholder representatives + 10 employee representatives
- German context: This is mandatory by law for German companies with >2,000 employees; Mainova cannot reduce below this structure without structural corporate transformation; it is not a governance choice but a legal constraint

Independent directors:
- Shareholder side (10 seats): Dominated by representatives of the major shareholders — SFH (Stadtwerke Frankfurt Holding, itself owned by the Stadt Frankfurt) + smaller municipal/strategic shareholders; seats are allocated politically based on Frankfurt city council composition; "independent" in the capital-market sense does not apply
- Employee side (10 seats): Worker representatives + union (IG BCE, ver.di) representatives; these are independent of management in a formal legal sense but aligned with labour interests rather than equity holder interests
- Genuine capital-market independence: Effectively zero; no director fits the standard definition of "independent non-executive director" per UK Corporate Governance Code or equivalent; this is a structural feature of German codetermination, not a specific governance failure of Mainova

Board chairperson:
- Aufsichtsrat Vorsitzender: Typically the senior shareholder representative from SFH/Stadt Frankfurt; the chair position is politically significant and held by a senior Frankfurt municipal figure; the chair sets the Aufsichtsrat agenda and has the casting vote; this concentrates effective board control with the municipal owner

Director tenure pattern:
- Shareholder-side tenure: Linked to Frankfurt city council election cycles (4-year terms) and political appointments; turnover is driven by local elections rather than board performance or independence norms
- Employee-side tenure: Typically longer-tenured; works council and union representatives accumulate significant institutional knowledge and can be effective monitors of operational decisions (especially labour-relations matters) even if less focused on capital allocation

- Politically driven rotation: Following Frankfurt local elections (most recently 2021, next 2025/26), the shareholder-representative side of the Aufsichtsrat rotates partially to reflect coalition changes at Frankfurt City Hall; the 2025/26 period is likely to see some board composition change following the 2021 elections' downstream effects and any 2025 council changes

Committee composition:

- Compensation committee (Personalausschuss): Sets Vorstand compensation and performance conditions; composition reflects the same shareholder/employee split; compensation decisions are politically influenced (municipal owner has salary discipline constraints; employee side has natural sympathy for pay); this creates a moderating effect on compensation (neither aggressively high nor aggressively performance-linked)

Diversity profile:

§6 — Governance Structure + Red Flags

J-front verdict: Governance is clean in the compliance sense (no restatements, no auditor changes, no regulatory sanctions) but structurally captured in the ownership/voting sense; no classic governance red flags, but the structural subordination of minority shareholder interests is a permanent feature

Voting rights structure:
- Share structure: Mainova AG is a publicly listed AG on the Frankfurt Stock Exchange, but with a highly concentrated ownership; Stadtwerke Frankfurt am Main Holding holds approximately 74.9% of shares; the remaining ~25.1% constitutes the free float (institutional and retail minority shareholders)
- No dual-class structure: Mainova uses a standard one-share-one-vote structure; there is no dual-class mechanism; the concentration of control is purely ownership-based, not structural voting-rights-based
- Effective control: SFH (and through it, the City of Frankfurt) exercises absolute control over all extraordinary resolutions, dividend policy, Vorstand appointments (via Aufsichtsrat), and strategic direction; no anti-takeover defence is needed because the controlling shareholder is immovable
- Golden share / anti-takeover provisions: No golden share; the municipal ownership stake is itself the anti-takeover protection; no poison pill or staggered board needed

Related-party transactions:
- Municipal service agreements: Mainova has commercial relationships with the Stadt Frankfurt and affiliated municipal entities (water utility, public transport, waste management) — these are disclosed as related-party transactions in the annual report under IAS 24; they include energy supply contracts, infrastructure sharing agreements, and service contracts
- Assessment of RPT quality: The related-party transactions are disclosed and described in general terms; Mainova does not provide the granularity (pricing basis, arm's length benchmarking) that would allow independent assessment of whether terms favour or disadvantage Mainova vs market; for a municipal utility, some below-market services to the city are structurally expected (social tariffs, municipal infrastructure support)

- Material weaknesses: None disclosed in public filings; HGB and IFRS financial statements have received clean audit opinions consistently
- Restatement history: No restatements identified in L5Y public filing review; financial reporting appears conservative and consistent year-to-year

Legal / regulatory issues:
- Regulatory framework: Mainova's network business is regulated by the Bundesnetzagentur (German Federal Network Agency) under German energy regulation; Mainova has no visible history of material regulatory sanctions or fines; normal cost-of-service regulation applies
- Civil litigation: No material class actions or civil litigation visible in public domain; typical for a German municipal utility with a stable, captive customer base
- Competition law: German energy markets have faced various Bundeskartellamt (antitrust) reviews; no Mainova-specific competition law proceedings identified in public domain
