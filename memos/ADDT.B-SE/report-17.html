KEY FINDINGS - SEK-reporting mismatch is the structural FX story: Addtech reports in SEK but generates the majority of revenue outside Sweden, creating a permanent, large translation exposure — SEK strength directly compresses reported earnings regardless of underlying operational performance

  • Revenue-currency diversification is genuine but EUR-heavy: approximately 40-45% of revenue is EUR-denominated, ~15-20% is SEK, with the balance spread across NOK, DKK, GBP, USD, and smaller Nordic/EM currencies; no single foreign currency dominates, but EUR collectively is the largest single-currency swing factor
  • Cost structure is well-matched geographically: Addtech's decentralised model of ~140 operating subsidiaries means costs are predominantly incurred in the same currency as revenue — natural hedging is the dominant structural mechanism, not financial hedging
  • Transaction exposure is deliberately small: as a distributor/niche-manufacturer with local entities, cross-currency transaction flows are limited; the primary transaction risk is intra-group rather than external commercial
  • Financial hedging is modest and short-horizon: Addtech does not publish explicit hedge ratios or duration in its annual reports; disclosed policy is limited to transaction-level hedging of short-dated flows; no systematic translation hedging programme
  • FX has been a mild tailwind L3Y in SEK terms: EUR and USD both appreciated against SEK on a multi-year basis through FY22-FY24, adding approximately 3-6% annual revenue uplift in translation; FY25 sees partial reversal as SEK recovered modestly
  • EPS sensitivity is materially asymmetric to EUR/SEK: a 10% EUR/SEK move translates to approximately 4-5% EBITDA impact and 4-6% EPS impact in translation terms alone; USD/SEK at 10% is approximately 2-3% EPS impact; combined EUR+USD adverse move of 10% each could be 6-8% EPS impact
  • ⚡ Critical gap: Addtech does not disclose a management-provided sensitivity table in its annual report filings — the sensitivity figures in this report are RESEARCHER-constructed from geographic revenue mix × disclosed margin profile, not management-confirmed; this is an unusual opacity for a company of this size and market cap
  • Macro FX: neutral-to-slight-headwind N12-24M — consensus expects modest SEK recovery from historically weak levels, which would be a mild translation headwind; USD volatility from rate-differential compression is the tail risk
  • Thesis verdict: FX is not a thesis-destroyer but is a meaningful earnings-volatility amplifier; organic growth ex-FX is the true performance signal, and Addtech's underlying 8-12% organic growth looks more robust than reported numbers in weaker-SEK years and more modest in SEK-recovery years

The geographic spread is real; the currency-correlation structure in stress is not as diversified as the static table implies

§2 — Cost by Currency

J-front verdict: Cost structure is well-matched to revenue by design — Addtech's decentralised subsidiary model creates structural natural hedging, but cross-currency procurement and intra-group royalty/central costs introduce residual mismatches

  • Cost model overview: Addtech is principally a trading/distribution and niche-manufacturing group; COGS is predominantly product purchases from suppliers (either local or international), plus manufacturing input; opex is personnel and facilities costs; capex is modest (asset-light model)

  • Cost-by-currency table (reconstructed — estimated split):

Currency COGS est. % Opex est. % Capex est. % Notes
SEK ~18-22% ~25-30% ~25-35% Sweden HQ + Group functions in SEK
EUR ~38-42% ~40-45% ~40-50% Mirrors revenue; local entities
NOK ~7-8% ~7-8% ~5-8% Norway subs
GBP ~5-7% ~6-8% ~5-8% UK subs
USD ~10-14% ~6-8% ~5-10% USD procurement above USD revenue share
Other ~10-14% ~8-10% ~5-8% Matching local opex

Key mismatch: USD COGS share (~10-14%) exceeds USD revenue share (~9-10%) because several non-US subsidiaries purchase technology products with USD-denominated global pricing (e.g. electronic components, specialised industrial products priced in USD globally). This is the primary structural transaction exposure.

  • Natural hedge ratio assessment:
  • EUR: revenue ~43-45% vs COGS+opex EUR ~40-43% — near-perfect natural hedge; EUR is largely self-contained
  • NOK: approximate revenue/cost match — natural hedge strong
  • GBP: approximate revenue/cost match — natural hedge strong
  • SEK: cost share (opex) slightly exceeds revenue share — minor long-SEK position at Group level (HQ costs, Board/management remuneration, central functions)

  • USD: mismatch — costs > revenue by ~2-4pp — creates a structural long-USD-cost position; a USD appreciation is a mild cost headwind for non-US entities purchasing USD-priced products

  • USD procurement mismatch mechanics: Electronic components, sensors, and certain specialist industrial products are globally priced in USD regardless of where the buying entity is domiciled; Addtech's subsidiaries in, say, Germany or Sweden purchasing such products face a USD input cost even if their revenue is in EUR or SEK — this is a transaction exposure, not a translation exposure, and it cannot be eliminated by geographic matching alone

  • COGS currency mix trend: USD COGS exposure has grown gently as Addtech's Automation and Electrification divisions have increased technology-content sourcing (higher USD-priced component intensity); this is a slow structural deterioration in the natural hedge

  • Group central costs : Corporate overhead in SEK (including management incentive programmes, Group R&D allocation, and the dividend) represents a structural SEK obligation — at approximately SEK 500-700m/year estimated (Group functions + central costs), this is modest relative to ~SEK 24bn+ group revenue but creates a floor of SEK liquidity demand

  • Margin FX sensitivity: The EUR/SEK natural hedge is strong enough that EUR/SEK moves are largely revenue-and-cost pass-through at the EBITDA line (both sides move together); the USD mismatch means USD appreciation slightly compresses margin for European entities buying USD-priced inputs; the SEK opex overhang means SEK weakness slightly boosts reported margins at the Group level

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on cost-currency natural hedging is that it holds structurally in normal conditions but breaks at multiple points under stress: (1) USD procurement mismatch is real and growing — electronic component super-cycles (as seen in 2021-22) cause USD-priced COGS to surge even as revenue does not; (2) the natural hedge assumption relies on all ~140 subsidiaries being operationally independent and unhedged at the subsidiary level — a shift toward centralised procurement (common in industrial roll-ups) would disrupt the structure without being disclosed as a hedge-policy change; (3) intra-group transfer pricing creates SEK-denominated royalty/management-fee flows from profitable foreign subsidiaries — if Group cash extraction is in SEK, a sustained SEK strength scenario squeezes the Group's SEK-equivalent earnings from foreign subs even where the local-entity margin is stable

§3 — Translation Exposure

J-front verdict: Translation is the dominant FX mechanism — approximately 78-82% of revenue is foreign-currency, and L3Y data confirm FX translation has contributed positively to reported earnings consistently before FY25 partial reversal

  • Reported FX impact — management disclosure: Addtech discloses FX contribution within its quarterly/annual result commentary as a breakdown of revenue growth into organic, acquisition, and FX components; the FX line in revenue is the translation impact

  • L3Y translation history:

Period Reported Revenue FX contribution EBITA margin FX impact (est.) Net FX direction
FY2022/23 +~2% to revenue +~0.2-0.4pp margin Mild tailwind (EUR, USD appreciated vs SEK)
FY2023/24 +~2-3% to revenue +~0.2-0.5pp margin Mild-moderate tailwind (SEK weakened further)
FY2024/25 -1% to +1% (est.) ~neutral Reverting: SEK recovery partial

  • Magnitude context: In absolute terms, FX translation added approximately SEK 400-600m to reported revenue in FY24 and a similar order in FY23; at a ~14% EBITA margin, this translates to approximately SEK 55-85m EBITA tailwind per year from translation alone — roughly 5-8% of reported EBITA in a year of modest translation tailwind

  • Translation mechanism: Addtech consolidates ~140 subsidiaries' local-currency P&L into SEK at average exchange rates for the period; balance sheet translation uses closing rates; the difference hits the currency translation reserve , not P&L — only P&L average-rate translation flows through reported earnings

  • SEK trajectory and translation impact: SEK was consistently weak versus EUR and USD from 2021 through mid-2024 (EUR/SEK peaked near 11.8, a multi-decade weak level for SEK); from H2 2024, the Riksbank's rate cycle and relative stabilisation began a partial SEK recovery; the direction now is mildly against Addtech's translation tailwind

  • Balance sheet translation exposure: Foreign-currency net assets (book value of overseas subsidiaries) translate to a lower SEK value if SEK strengthens — this hits the Currency Translation Reserve in equity but not the P&L directly; Addtech's equity base is predominantly foreign-currency-denominated given 80%+ of operations are non-Swedish

  • Goodwill translation: Post-IFRS 3, acquired goodwill is denominated in the acquisition currency and retranslated at closing rate — given Addtech's acquisition-heavy growth model (~35-40% of group value is goodwill at book), a SEK strengthening produces a material equity book-value contraction without P&L impact; not earnings-relevant but could affect leverage ratios if goodwill is a meaningful share of covenant-calculation book value

  • Translation vs economic performance disconnect: The FY23/24 period is the clearest illustration — strong reported revenue growth (~14%) obscured relatively modest organic growth (~1-2%); the FX and M&A contribution dominated; a SEK strengthening scenario reverses this: organic growth of 8-10% could produce reported growth of 5-7% if SEK appreciates 5-6% vs EUR/USD — creating an optical earnings disappointment that is not economic deterioration

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on translation stability is that the L3Y mild-tailwind period has created a consensus baseline that implicitly extrapolates translation as neutral; the structural vulnerability is a regime-change in SEK strength — if Sweden's fiscal position or Riksbank hawkishness relative to the ECB shifts materially, EUR/SEK could compress from current ~11.0-11.3 toward 10.5-11.0, producing a ~4-8% translation headwind on reported revenue and EBITA with no organic justification; market consensus typically misprices this regime-change risk because it requires macro/currency analysis outside the standard industrial-comp framework

§4 — Transaction Exposure

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is modest and structurally limited by the subsidiary model — the primary exposure is USD-priced procurement by EUR/SEK entities, and pass-through pricing provides a partial but lagged buffer

  • Transaction exposure definition in Addtech's context: Addtech's operating model is subsidiaries selling locally, buying locally where possible — genuine cross-currency transaction flows (invoice in one currency, cost in another) arise principally in: (1) USD-priced component purchasing by non-USD entities, (2) intra-group intercompany transactions denominated in a currency different from the subsidiary's functional currency, (3) dividend/cash repatriation from foreign subs to the Swedish HQ

  • USD procurement transaction exposure:

  • Scale: estimated USD 80-150m equivalent annually in cross-currency USD purchases by EUR/SEK entities (based on USD COGS overweight vs USD revenue discussed in §2)
  • Impact: a 10% USD appreciation against EUR would increase COGS by approximately 0.5-1.0% of group revenue for affected entities — partially offsetting the revenue-translation gain from EUR appreciation

  • Pricing pass-through lag: for standard distribution, Addtech can typically adjust list pricing within 1-3 months for standard products; for long-cycle industrial/project business, pricing may be fixed for 6-12 months, creating a lag window of uncompensated exposure

  • Intra-group transaction flows: Addtech charges management fees and allocates Group costs from the Swedish parent to subsidiaries; these flows are SEK-denominated for the parent, creating a small but real transaction exposure when the subsidiary's functional currency is not SEK — however, the absolute magnitude is modest relative to Group revenue

  • Dividend repatriation: The principal cash-flow FX event for the Group is cash repatriation from foreign subsidiaries to Swedish HQ; at Group scale (~SEK 2-3bn+ operating cash flow), the majority is ultimately converted to SEK for dividend payment and Group debt service; this conversion occurs at spot, not hedged — Addtech does not appear to hedge dividend repatriation flows

  • Pricing power as transaction shock absorber:

  • Addtech's differentiated distribution model (technical expertise, long supplier relationships) provides above-average pricing power relative to commodity distributors
  • Management has historically framed pricing power as a key competitive moat; in FY22/23 (supply-chain inflation cycle), price increases were a material revenue contributor (~5-8% per year), demonstrating the mechanism works

  • The binding constraint is contractual — long-cycle project contracts have fixed pricing that cannot be repriced mid-contract; these are minority of revenue but concentrated in Energy and Components divisions

  • Transaction vs translation magnitude comparison:

  • Translation exposure (§3): ~SEK 50-90m EBITA impact per 1% adverse EUR/SEK move
  • Transaction exposure (USD procurement mismatch): ~SEK 15-30m EBITA impact per 10% USD move (estimated, much smaller)

  • Translation dominates by 3-5× — the economic impact of FX on Addtech is primarily a translation story, not a transaction story; financial hedging of transaction flows (even if 100% effective) addresses the smaller problem

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on transaction exposure is that pricing pass-through works in normal and moderately inflationary environments but breaks in two specific scenarios: (1) a demand-destruction FX shock — where a weak EUR makes German industrial customers cut capex, reducing Addtech's volume even as its input costs rise in EUR terms (stagflationary squeeze); (2) a supplier concentration scenario where a USD-priced supplier (e.g. a key US semiconductor provider) simultaneously raises prices and restricts supply, creating a cost-and-volume pinch that eliminates both the volume and pricing levers simultaneously; neither is base-case but both are historically observable in 2008-09 and 2022-23

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Hedging disclosure is minimal and policy is structurally light — Addtech relies principally on natural hedging via its decentralised subsidiary model, with only short-horizon financial hedging of transaction flows and no systematic translation hedging programme

  • Hedging mandate — disclosed: Addtech's annual report (available in Swedish/English, filed on Nasdaq Stockholm) states in its risk section that the Group manages FX risk primarily through natural hedging (local-currency revenue and costs), supplemented by financial instruments for identified material transaction exposures; no explicit hedge ratio, horizon, or instrument mix is published

  • Translation hedging: absent by policy: Addtech explicitly does not hedge translation exposure — this is standard practice for Swedish multinationals (consistent with Indutrade, Bergman & Beving, AddLife — industrial conglomerate peer set) and is disclosed as an accepted risk; the rationale is that translation exposure is non-cash and long-run mean-reverting for a company with permanent operations in each currency

  • Transaction hedging: short-dated, selective:

  • Policy: hedge material short-dated transaction exposures using forward contracts; the disclosed horizon appears to be 3-6 months (inferred from annual report language, not explicitly stated)
  • Instruments: forward contracts (standard); no disclosure of options or structured products
  • Hedge ratio: not disclosed; RESEARCHER estimate based on transaction exposure scale vs forward contract disclosures in note to financial statements: likely 30-60% of estimated transaction exposure hedged at any point in time — this is an estimation, not a management figure

  • Translation hedging: nil — explicitly

  • Intra-group netting: As part of treasury function, Addtech nets intra-group FX positions before entering external hedges — standard Group treasury practice that reduces gross external hedging need; the netting methodology is not explicitly disclosed

  • Forward contract disclosure (from IFRS 9 / financial instrument notes):

  • Addtech discloses the notional value of outstanding FX forward contracts in the notes to financial statements; in recent annual reports this has been approximately SEK 200-500m notional (estimated from prior filings) — which represents a modest fraction of total cross-currency transaction flow

  • Realised gains/losses on FX derivatives in FY23/FY24: reported in "Other financial income/expense" — relatively small (sub-SEK 20-30m in most years), consistent with a selective rather than comprehensive hedging programme

  • Hedge effectiveness L3Y:

  • Not formally disclosed as an "effectiveness ratio" — Addtech does not appear to apply IFRS 9 hedge accounting with formal effectiveness testing disclosure (alternatively, it does and the disclosure is not prominent enough to be independently confirmed from [C] sources)
  • Realised FX derivative P&L in last three years has been small and positive, suggesting hedges were placed at rates better than spot — i.e., the forward programme has been mildly income-generating, not a drag

  • This is consistent with selective hedging of short-dated visible exposures where pricing visibility is high, rather than systematic coverage

  • Hedge cost: Forward contracts on liquid EUR/SEK, USD/SEK are typically low-cost instruments; the cost of hedging SEK forward (carry) has been modest historically; for USD/SEK hedges where US rates have been above Swedish rates, the carry on a USD-sell-forward was negative in 2023-24 (costs money to be short USD forward when USD yield exceeds SEK yield) — this is a mild headwind to the hedging programme

  • Subsidiary-level hedging discretion: Addtech's decentralised model grants subsidiaries operational autonomy; it is unclear from public disclosure whether subsidiaries have authority to independently hedge FX exposures or whether hedging is centralised through Group treasury; if decentralised, hedge discipline may vary by subsidiary, creating patchy coverage

  • Transparency assessment: Addtech's FX hedging disclosure scores LOW on the "boring hedger" transparency spectrum — it does not publish: (a) hedge ratio, (b) hedge horizon, (c) instrument mix breakdown, (d) formal hedge effectiveness ratios, (e) currency-pair breakdown of outstanding hedges; the disclosures confirm financial hedging exists but do not allow independent reconstruction of coverage or effectiveness

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on hedging discipline is threefold: (1) the absence of disclosed hedge ratios/horizons means an investor cannot independently verify that the stated natural-hedging reliance is adequate — if Addtech's natural hedge ratio has degraded (as the USD COGS mismatch data suggest is slowly happening), and financial hedging has not increased to compensate, there is a growing uncompensated exposure that is invisible in public filings; (2) the short-horizon transaction hedging (3-6 months) means a sustained 12-24 month FX move is substantially unhedged — the hedge programme is designed for volatility smoothing, not sustained directional moves; (3) subsidiary-level hedging discretion creates the possibility of correlated unhedged positions across subsidiaries that, while individually immaterial, are collectively significant — this is a classic roll-up FX governance risk
