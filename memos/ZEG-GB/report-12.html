§1 — Customer Analysis (concentration + relationships)

Zegona serves a mass-market B2C and SME subscriber base inherited from Vodafone España; the customer profile is geographically diversified across Spain but brand-impaired at acquisition. The analytical frame is subscriber retention and ARPU, not key-account concentration.

  • Verdict: No single-customer concentration risk, but aggregate retention quality is below mid-tier EU cable peers at point of acquisition — the improvement trajectory is the investment thesis, not the starting position [#J]

  • Customer profile: Mass B2C + SME — Vodafone España had ~3.4m mobile contract, ~2.0m broadband, ~1.5m TV subscribers at acquisition (Dec-2023) — no single customer >1% revenue [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Customer base skews urban (Madrid, Barcelona, Valencia corridors) — higher ARPU but also higher competitive intensity from DIGI and MásMóvil

  • Top-1 concentration: Not applicable — B2C mass market; no customer concentration risk in traditional sense; B2B/SME segment represents est. ~15-20% revenue but no named enterprise anchor [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Contrast: UK cable B2B operators (VM O2 business) can see top-10 enterprise = 10-15% revenue — Zegona's profile carries no comparable concentration but also no enterprise anchor upside

  • Contract length: Spanish telco market dominated by 12-24 month contracts with statutory right to cancel after minimum term (per LGT 2022 consumer rights reform); no long-form multi-year lock-up on residential [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: LGT 2022 consumer protection rules lowered contractual switching barriers — regulatory tailwind for competitors, headwind for Zegona retention

  • Customer churn: Industry-wide mobile contract churn in Spain ran 14.5% annualised in 2024 (CNMC Quarterly Report Q3-2024) — Vodafone España historically at upper end of this range [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Zegona disclosed H1-2024 mobile churn running above acquisition baseline — management attributed to brand uncertainty during transition; no explicit churn figure disclosed publicly

  • Sub-bullet: Peer reference: UK cable/mobile (VM O2) reported ~1.1% monthly contract churn = ~13% annualised in FY2023; Zegona likely running 1.2-1.5% monthly in transition period

  • Retention rate: Broadband churn structurally lower than mobile in converged households — Spanish broadband churn ~9-10% industry annualised (CNMC 2024) vs mobile 14-15% [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Converged quad-play households estimated at 35-40% of Vodafone España base at acquisition — these have meaningfully lower blended churn; protection in the existing converged base

  • Satisfaction signal: Vodafone España ranked #3-4 of major Spanish operators in OCU consumer satisfaction surveys (2022-2023) — below Telecable/Euskaltel cluster but ahead of Orange on network quality [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: NPS data not publicly disclosed; regulatory complaint ratios to CNMC show Vodafone España at sector-median complaint rate (not worst, not best)

  • Lost customer signal: Post-acquisition subscriber trends (H2-2023 to H1-2024): mobile contract base declined approximately 80-100k net in first two quarters post-close — in line with management guidance but still a contraction signal [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Competitors MásMóvil and DIGI reported net adds of 120k and 90k respectively in same period (CNMC data) — Zegona was a net donor to competitors during transition

  • Major customer — B2B: No named anchor corporate accounts disclosed; SME base estimated at 200-250k business connections — broadly distributed, no concentration [#A]

  • Contract mix: Residential = predominantly 12-month rolling (post-LGT 2022); SME = 24-month standard; no evidence of multi-year enterprise contracts generating visibility [#A]

  • Satisfaction signal — network: Umlaut/Connect network quality benchmarks (2024) rated Vodafone España network as "Good" (score 845/1000) — below Movistar (890) but above Orange (821) and DIGI (780); network quality is a retention asset if maintained [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: Apparent retention improvement in H2-2024 management commentary may reflect the easy-comp effect of transition-period elevated churn normalising — not structural stickiness improvement; true test is whether 2025 churn tracks below 2023 baseline; no public data yet confirms this; late-cycle vs structural distinction cannot be resolved at ESA depth

§2 — Switching Costs + Pricing Leverage

  • Verdict: Switching costs are moderate — quad-play bundling provides meaningful but not exceptional stickiness; pricing leverage is constrained by DIGI's structural low-cost anchor in the market [#J]

  • Switching cost — product: Mobile SIM-only switching = near-zero friction; Spanish number portability is 24-hour (among EU fastest); broadband switching requires engineer visit but typically completed <5 working days [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Low friction on standalone products is a structural weakness — Zegona's moat depends entirely on converged bundle penetration

  • Switching cost — integration: Quad-play households (mobile + broadband + TV + fixed) face co-ordination cost of simultaneously switching four services — estimated 3-5 hours consumer time + service gap risk [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Peer data: UK Ofcom research (2023) shows converged households switch at 40-50% the rate of single-service customers — Spanish CNMC data directionally consistent (no published exact ratio)

  • Switching cost — equipment: Vodafone España router/set-top-box estate is proprietary — switch requires equipment return + new installation; adds ~€50-100 consumer cost if new provider doesn't waive [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: DIGI and MásMóvil waive installation costs as acquisition incentive — partially neutralising this switching cost

  • Pricing pass-through: Zegona announced 3-5% CPI-linked tariff increases in Jan-2025 (Investor Update Jan-2025) — broadly in line with Orange España and Movistar; below UK operators (BT/VM O2 running CPI+3.9%) [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: DIGI did NOT match — held pricing flat in Jan-2025; creating a divergence between incumbent and challenger pricing trajectories

  • Pricing discipline: ❌ DIGI's market entry strategy has structurally depressed achievable ARPU for entry-tier SIM-only — pricing at €8-12/month for 30GB vs Zegona/Vodafone España at €25-35 [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Movistar España ARPU: ~€43 blended (Q4-2024); Zegona (Vodafone España): ~€37; DIGI: ~€15 — DIGI not yet threatening premium convergence bundle but anchors market expectations downward

  • Customer pushback: No explicit management disclosure of pricing pushback; however, net subscriber losses in transition period are consistent with price-sensitive customers leaving for DIGI/MásMóvil rather than renewing at incumbents' tariffs [#A]

  • Renewal pricing: B2C renewal pricing in Spain is typically at or slightly below new-customer acquisition pricing (inverted: acquisition discounts are common) — loyalty premium model (BT, VM) not yet established in Spain [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Inverted pricing model is a structural profitability challenge — acquiring customers at discount, retention requires matching competitor promotions

  • RFP pressure: SME segment shows moderate RFP activity — IT managers benchmark providers every 2-3 years; Zegona/Vodafone España brand recognised in SME, but price competition from Telefónica Empresas and Orange Business is intense [#A]

  • Competitive bidding: Retail price comparison via Rastreator.com, Kelisto.es generates price transparency and implicit RFP pressure even in residential — ~25-30% of Spanish residential customers use comparison sites before renewal (CNMC survey 2023) [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The converged-bundle switching cost narrative is compelling structurally but requires that Zegona actually retains quad-play penetration as the base churns through — if single-service mobile customers (lower stickiness) comprise a disproportionate share of lost subscribers, the remaining base is more concentrated in higher-cost converged households, making further losses more expensive; this dynamic hasn't been quantified in public disclosure

§3 — Supplier Analysis (concentration + input costs)

  • Verdict: Supplier landscape is moderately concentrated on infrastructure vendors; input cost trajectory is slightly deflationary on opex but capex intensity remains high; Huawei exposure is the latent geopolitical risk [#J]

  • Critical input — network infrastructure: Vodafone España network runs on Ericsson , Nokia (core/IMS), and Huawei (legacy RAN + fixed) — typical three-vendor structure for a mid-tier European operator [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Huawei equipment in Spanish fixed and legacy mobile network — Spain has not yet implemented a formal Huawei ban (unlike UK's 2027 deadline) but EU CySA+ risk framework creates medium-term rip-and-replace pressure

  • Supplier concentration — RAN: Ericsson is primary RAN vendor for Vodafone España (as per Vodafone Group vendor disclosures pre-separation); Nokia secondary for core [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Ericsson Q1-2025 earnings call: Europe segment revenue flat-to-down 3% YoY — management flagged "selective operator capex pauses" including in Iberia; signals Zegona is not accelerating network investment at this stage

  • Input cost trend — spectrum: CNMC scheduled 700MHz + 3.5GHz licence renewal process for 2026-2028 — spectrum costs likely to increase modestly vs 2018 assignment pricing; Zegona will bear this as incumbent [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Peer reference: UK 700MHz/3.5GHz refarming cost VMO2 est. £150-200m over 3Y — Spanish scale implies lower absolute number but directionally upward for Zegona

  • Inflation pressure — energy: Spanish electricity prices (OMIE spot) averaged €80-90/MWh in 2024 vs €250/MWh peak 2022 — material opex relief vs acquisition period; energy = ~8-12% of network opex for cable operators [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Deflation tailwind: energy normalisation is a genuine 2024-2025 margin tailwind; est. €20-30m YoY opex saving for a network of Vodafone España's scale

  • Supplier concentration — content: TV content licensing — LaLiga, Champions League, HBO Max/Warner deals inherited from Vodafone España; LaLiga rights held primarily by Movistar+ (Telefónica) — Zegona/Vodafone España licenses sub-rights [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: ❌ Content dependency on Movistar+ sub-licensing is a structural vulnerability — Telefónica could price sub-rights above market or withdraw them at renewal to disadvantage a direct competitor; next LaLiga rights window est. 2027

  • Long-term partnership: Ericsson/Nokia managed services agreements typically 3-5Y — Vodafone España inherited multi-year managed services contracts; Zegona confirmed continuation in H1-2024 investor comms [#A]

  • Supply chain risk — Huawei: ⚡ RARE: Huawei represents an estimated 30-40% of Vodafone España's fixed access network (copper/fibre DSLAMs) — materially above post-UK-ban replacement thresholds; if EU implements mandatory replacement, cost could exceed €300-500m (analyst estimate range, no Zegona disclosure) [Cross-ref: §9] [#A]

  • Geopolitical risk: Spain's regulatory environment on Huawei remains ambiguous; CNMC has not mandated removal but Secretaría de Estado de Telecomunicaciones is monitoring EU CySA+ implementation — binary risk event over 3-5Y horizon [#A]

  • Deflation tailwind — handsets: Device procurement costs (smartphones for subsidised postpaid) have stabilised after 2022-2023 component-cost spikes — Ericsson and Nokia device divisions flagging ASP normalisation in 2024-2025 earnings calls [#A]

  • Supplier bargaining power: Ericsson and Nokia have moderate leverage — alternative is Huawei (which carries regulatory risk) or Samsung (limited European mobile infrastructure footprint); in practice, vendor switching cost for Zegona is very high (2-3Y migration), giving vendors above-average leverage at contract renewal [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The energy deflation tailwind is real but one-time in nature — 2024-2025 benefit does not recur at same magnitude in 2026-2027; if spectrum costs rise, Huawei replacement pressure crystallises, and energy reverts, the three-vector input-cost squeeze could materialise simultaneously; this is not base case but is a credible tail

§4 — Competitor Analysis (market share + responses)

  • Verdict: Spanish telecoms competitive intensity is structurally high and structurally deteriorating for Zegona's position — the MásMóvil/Orange merger creates a funded, larger challenger while DIGI continues to take price-sensitive subs [#J]

  • L3Y share trend — mobile: Zegona/Vodafone España mobile contract share: ~18-19% (CNMC 2021) → ~16-17% (CNMC 2024) — steady erosion of ~1-1.5pp over three years; not catastrophic but directionally negative [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Context: Movistar España stable at ~31-33%; MásMóvil group ~27-28% (including Pepephone/Yoigo); Orange España ~22% pre-merger; DIGI ~4-5% and growing

  • Geographic share: Zegona's cable infrastructure covers ~7.5m homes passed (HFC + FTTH overlay) — concentrated in urban/suburban Spain; rural Spain is Movistar/wholesale territory where Zegona has limited presence [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Urban competitive intensity is highest — DIGI's urban-first rollout model directly targets Zegona's core footprint

  • Segment share — convergence: Converged (multi-service) customer share: Movistar leads (~40% of converged HHs); MásMóvil/Orange (post-merger) ~35%; Zegona ~15%; DIGI minimal (low TV offering) [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Converged segment is where Zegona has durable position — the question is whether the overall converged HH market grows fast enough to compensate for share loss in standalone mobile

  • Competitive response — M&A: MásMóvil + Orange España merger (EC approved Nov-2024, closing Q1-2025) creates the second-largest Spanish operator by revenue (~€3.8bn combined) with strong B2B capabilities and improved network economics [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Merged entity inherits MásMóvil's cost-efficiency + Orange's enterprise B2B footprint — directly competitive with Zegona's SME base

  • Competitive response — price: DIGI held pricing flat in Jan-2025 price increase cycle — deliberate market-share-over-margin strategy; DIGI parent Digi Communications NV (Romania) explicitly guided "Spain growth at cost of margin" in FY2024 results [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Digi Communications NV FY2024 call (Feb-2025): "We are not participating in price increases in Spain — we see an opportunity to add 300-400k subscribers in 2025 from customers exiting competitors during their price rises"

  • Competitive response — capacity: Telefónica España FTTH build now covers 29m homes passed (~90% of Spanish territory) — wholesale access obligations mean DIGI and MásMóvil can ride Movistar FTTH infrastructure without owning it; Zegona's owned HFC/FTTH is ~7.5m HP only [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: ❌ Movistar FTTH wholesale availability structurally reduces the infrastructure advantage of owning cable plant — competitors can match Zegona's service quality in ~80% of Spain without owning network

  • Pricing discipline: Movistar and Zegona maintained CPI-linked increases in Jan-2025 — Telefónica España FY2024 call (Feb-2025) confirmed "pricing environment remains rational among incumbents"; caveat: incumbents' definition of rational excludes DIGI [#A]

  • Innovation rate — FTTH: Zegona's FTTH upgrade programme (HFC → FTTH overlay) is 2-3 years behind Movistar's completion timeline; Orange España (pre-merger) had already completed FTTH overlay — Zegona is a late network upgrader [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Peer capex intensity: Movistar España capex/revenue ~18-20% at peak FTTH build; Zegona guidance ~15-17% — below peer investment pace, suggesting possible quality gap building

  • New entrant threat: DIGI's Spanish operation is not "new" (entered 2019) but is in aggressive growth phase — subscriber CAGR 40%+ (2021-2024, Digi Communications filings) — effectively behaves as a disruptive new entrant for retention purposes [#A]

  • Disruption signal: Fixed wireless access from Telefónica/Movistar 5G not yet material in Spain but represents medium-term threat to Zegona's fixed broadband base in semi-urban coverage — Ericsson 5G FWA revenue guidance (Global) rising 35% YoY (2024 earnings) [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The MásMóvil/Orange merger's competitive impact is widely flagged but may be under-priced in timing — integration-period disruption (2025-2026) could initially reduce the merged entity's competitive effectiveness, giving Zegona a 12-18 month window; the risk is that Zegona management uses this window for financial optimisation rather than network investment, deferring the competitive reckoning rather than resolving it

§5 — Distributor + Channel Analysis

  • Verdict: Distribution is predominantly direct but retail channel dependency persists; DIGI's low-cost digital-direct model represents structural disintermediation pressure on Zegona's higher-touch retail economics [#J]

  • Direct-to-customer: Online and inbound telesales represent ~55-60% of new customer gross adds (Zegona Investor Day Nov-2024 estimate); growing share as CPA-conscious management rationalises retail commissions [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Trend direction: post-acquisition management has explicitly guided to higher digital channel mix — target ~65-70% DTC by end-2025 (Investor Day Nov-2024)

  • Indirect — retail: El Corte Inglés, MediaMarkt, Fnac, and specialist phone retailers (Phone House) account for est. ~30-35% of gross adds — high-traffic, brand-building but high commission per gross add [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Commission cost: retail channel CPA est. €80-120/subscriber (industry estimate) vs €30-45 for digital direct — channel mix shift to digital is a margin improvement lever

  • Channel concentration: No single retail partner >10% of gross adds estimated — Phone House is likely largest specialist channel but Vodafone España historically distributed through broad multi-retailer network [#A]

  • Distributor leverage: Retail partners have moderate leverage — they carry all four major Spanish operators simultaneously; can prioritise competitor promotions if commission rates or product quality diverge [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Telefónica's Movistar has historically paid higher retail commissions to prioritise shelf presence — Zegona competing on product/brand, not commission rate, in retail channel

  • Platform: MVNO/wholesale channel: Zegona hosts ~2-3 MVNOs on its network (est., not publicly disclosed) — incremental revenue with minimal incremental cost but not material to group revenue [#A]

  • Channel evolution: ❌ DIGI uses pure-digital self-service model (no retail presence) — acquires at €15-25 CPA vs Zegona's blended ~€60-80 — structural cost disadvantage in the price-sensitive segment where churn is highest [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Long-run implication: Zegona's retail channel investment is appropriate for premium convergence but over-serves the lower-ARPU mobile-only segment it is already losing

  • DTC shift: Management committed to reducing retail channel dependency — closure/rationalisation of owned Vodafone España retail stores (est. 400-500 stores at acquisition); target store count post-rationalisation not publicly disclosed [#A]

  • Channel margin: Retail channel gross add economics deteriorating as DIGI anchors consumer price expectations downward — the per-unit economics of a retail-acquired subscriber at €80 CPA are marginal if ARPU is declining toward €25-30 [#A]

  • Service level pressure: Retail partners expect Zegona to maintain competitive promotional offers (free months, handset subsidies) — any reduction in promotional intensity risks retail partner de-prioritisation [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The shift to digital-direct is structurally correct but execution risk is real — Spanish consumer telecoms customers, particularly 45+ demographic, rely on in-store advice for complex bundle decisions; aggressive store closure could accelerate churn in the highest-ARPU converged-bundle segment that Zegona most needs to protect; management has not disclosed a store-rationalisation schedule with churn impact modelling

§8 — NWC + Working Capital Trends

  • Verdict: NWC structure is a structural tailwind — subscription telcos collect upfront, pay suppliers 60-90 days deferred; Zegona benefits from negative working capital but capital intensity limits cash conversion quality [#J]

  • DSO: Spanish telco residential billing typically monthly direct debit — DSO ~25-30 days (industry standard); SME billing net-30 terms; no significant receivables extension risk in current economic environment [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Spain household broadband non-payment rates ~2-3% (CNMC 2024 debt-default data) — within normal range; not a NWC stress signal

  • DPO: Infrastructure vendor payment terms (Ericsson, Nokia) typically 60-90 days (standard European telco practice); content licensing (LaLiga sub-rights) paid quarterly; DPO ~65-75 days estimated [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Negative NWC = structurally favourable — Zegona receives cash from subscribers before paying suppliers; contribution ~€100-150m structural working capital benefit vs asset-heavy peers who carry inventory

  • Inventory days: Handset inventory: modest — Spanish market increasingly SIM-only (handset detachment rate ~55-60% of new activations per CNMC 2024); less inventory exposure than 2019-era subsidised device model [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: Deflation tailwind: SIM-only trend reduces handset subsidy cost and inventory risk simultaneously; positive NWC trajectory as detachment continues

  • Cash conversion cycle: Estimated CCC: ~(25) days negative (DSO 28 - DPO 65 + Inventory 12) — aligned with UK cable (VM O2 ~(20-25) days negative per company disclosures) [#A]

  • NWC trend: Subscriber loss in 2024 created a one-time negative NWC unwind — departing subscribers reduce deferred revenue; est. €30-50m NWC outflow associated with net subscriber decline in H1-H2-2024 [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: If subscriber base stabilises in 2025 (management guidance), this NWC headwind reverses — DDs should model NWC sensitivity to subscriber trajectory carefully

  • Customer payment delay: No evidence of systematic payment delay by customers — Spanish telco direct-debit culture is strong; bad debt ratio stable [#A]

  • Inventory obsolescence: Legacy CPE (set-top-boxes, routers) on customer premises — est. 1.5-2m units at age >4Y; replacement cycle creates capex obligation but not an NWC inventory risk [#A]

  • Capital intensity offset: Despite favourable NWC, maintenance capex + FTTH overlay capex absorbs most of operating cash flow — net free cash flow conversion (FCF/EBITDA) est. ~15-25% in 2024-2025 vs European cable median ~30-35% (Liberty Global disclosures) [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: ❌ Below-median FCF conversion is the critical ESA-stage financial signal — favourable NWC structure exists but capex demands leave limited genuine free cash for deleveraging at the pace management implies
