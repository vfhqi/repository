§1 — Executive Summary

§2 — Key Driver #1: Regulated RAB / Capex Execution

  • J-front verdict: Highest-conviction driver — €41bn capex plan (2025-2027), ~80% regulated/contracted, is mechanically the largest EPS growth engine; delivery has been consistent L3Y [#J]

  • Driver definition: Iberdrola invests in regulated electricity networks (UK via ScottishPower, Spain via i-DE, US via Avangrid) and earns a regulated return on the growing asset base; RAB growth compounds into rising EBITDA [#J]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Higher RAB → higher regulated EBITDA (via allowed return × RAB) → EPS growth at regulated leverage ratios; each €1bn of RAB added at ~5-6% allowed return yields ~€50-60m incremental EBITDA [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Regulated earnings are valued at a premium multiple (lower risk, higher visibility); growing RAB expands absolute earnings and can expand multiples if market re-rates utility premium [#J]

  • Company guide: Management targets €41bn capex 2025-2027; ~80% directed to networks and regulated/contracted assets; implies ~€14bn per year average, up from ~€12bn in prior cycle [#A]

  • Management commentary: CEO has consistently framed Iberdrola as a "network and renewables" compounder; Q4 2024 and CMD materials emphasise execution track record and visibility of regulated pipeline [#J]

  • Track record: FY2021-2024 capex delivery broadly on plan; regulated EBITDA grew consistently; Avangrid was the main execution drag (permitting delays, rate case timing) but showed improvement by H2 2024 [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Guidance appears achievable but not conservative — step-up from prior cycle requires sustained permitting throughput, procurement efficiency, and no regulatory disruptions [#A]

  • SS consensus: Broad consensus that RAB/capex execution is the primary EPS driver; major brokers (Barclays, JPMorgan, Santander, UBS — cited in public research summaries) model ~8-10% regulated EBITDA CAGR on current plan [#A]

  • Consensus changes L12M: Positive drift — Avangrid progress and CMD capex upgrade led to modest upward EPS revisions through H2 2024/early 2025 [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Successful full delivery of €41bn plan with targeted returns = +15-20% cumulative EPS contribution over 24M window versus a stalled programme [#A]

  • Recent delivery: FY2024 networks EBITDA grew ~8-10% YoY (public guidance range); Avangrid rate case progress positive; UK networks capex on track per ScottishPower regulatory commitments [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: Regulated EBITDA compound growth ~7-9% annually 2021-2024; consistent with plan assumptions; no major miss years in regulated segment [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: No cohort data available this run; European utility peer RAB growth typically 5-8% annually for large integrated networks — IBE-ES at upper end of this range based on own-facts [#J] (quality_flag: thin_peer_context)

  • Macro tailwind: Electrification megatrend drives incremental network investment needs; EU grid investment requirements ~€600bn to 2030 (EU Commission estimates) structurally supportive [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Construction cost inflation pressures capital efficiency; higher-for-longer interest rates increase the cost of project finance and reduce NPV of long-duration regulated assets [#A]

  • Visibility: High — regulatory frameworks in Spain, UK, US provide multi-year visibility; capex approved within regulatory programmes has low cancellation risk [#J]

  • Bear case: Avangrid permitting/regulatory delays resurface; UK RIIO-ED3 (post-2028) settlement disappoints on allowed returns; inflation persists, eroding capex efficiency [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Capex delivery runs 15-20% below plan in any 12M window; regulated EBITDA growth decelerates to 4-5%; EPS consensus downgraded 5-8% [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: ~5-8% EPS downgrade in a partial-miss scenario; ~15% in a severe disruption (regulatory + permitting combination) [#A]

  • Red flags: Avangrid has been the chronic underperformer; watch US regulatory proceedings and state-level permitting; no structural resolution visible yet [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: Is the capex step-up from ~€12bn to ~€14bn per year achievable without supply chain and permitting bottlenecks that have historically plagued European utilities attempting similar accelerations? [#J]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — regulated frameworks provide earnings visibility; management delivery track record L3Y broadly consistent; key risk is Avangrid execution, not structural

§3 — Key Driver #2: Renewables Pipeline + PPA Monetisation

  • J-front verdict: Significant driver but carrying more execution risk than networks — 12GW+ target additions by 2026 is ambitious; PPA pricing environment supportive but merchant exposure and permitting introduce uncertainty [#J]

  • Driver definition: Iberdrola builds and operates wind (onshore/offshore) and solar assets; revenues are locked via long-term PPAs or merchant sale; growing installed capacity compounds contracted EBITDA [#J]

  • Mechanism to EPS: MW additions → contracted EBITDA growth → EPS; quality of PPAs (duration, price, counterparty) determines earnings predictability and valuation multiple [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Renewables EBITDA attracts higher multiples than merchant power (visibility premium); PPA locks reduce discount rates applied by the market [#J]

  • Company guide: 12GW+ additions targeted 2025-2026; offshore wind (Baltic Eagle, East Anglia Hub), onshore wind and solar across Spain, US, Mexico, Brazil; ~80% of new capacity contracted at point of investment decision [#A]

  • Management commentary: Management has consistently emphasised PPA coverage ratios as proof of contracted visibility; CMD 2024 cited >95% of 2024-2026 EBITDA under contract or regulatory framework [#J]

  • Track record: FY2021-2024 renewables additions broadly on plan; some delays in offshore wind (supply chain); onshore execution stronger; PPA pricing has been firm as corporate demand for green power accelerates [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Achievable but execution-sensitive — offshore wind supply chain remains strained globally (vessel availability, turbine lead times, subsea cable constraints) [#A]

  • SS consensus: Brokers generally credit renewables pipeline as additive to EPS growth; concern areas are offshore wind execution and merchant price exposure in Spain and Brazil [#A]

  • Consensus changes L12M: Mixed — PPA signing momentum positive; offshore wind delivery timelines extended modestly; net effect neutral to slightly positive on 24M estimates [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Full 12GW delivery at modelled returns = +8-12% EPS contribution over 24M; partial delivery (say 70% of target) = +5-7%; misses compress to +2-3% and drag on sentiment [#A]

  • Recent delivery: FY2024 renewables capacity additions ~4-5GW (public guidance); offshore Baltic Eagle commissioning nearing completion; East Anglia Hub longer-dated [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: Renewables EBITDA CAGR ~10-12% 2021-2024; consistent with IBE-ES positioning as a scale global renewables developer [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: No cohort data available; European offshore developers broadly experiencing 12-18M timeline slippage vs 2021-era targets — IBE-ES appears relatively more resilient on delivery based on own-facts [#J] (quality_flag: thin_peer_context)

  • Macro tailwind: Corporate PPA demand structurally strong (tech sector data centre buildout, EU Green Deal obligations); onshore wind permitting reform in Spain and Germany supportive [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Offshore wind supply chain under strain; turbine prices elevated; subsea cable lead times 3-4 years; FOREX on USD-denominated procurement [#A]

  • Visibility: Medium — contracted PPA revenue is high visibility; MW delivery timeline is moderate visibility (permitting, supply chain); merchant price component is low visibility [#J]

  • Bear case: Offshore wind delays push 12GW target into 2027+; PPA prices soften as European power market oversupplied; Brazil/Mexico political risk affects returns [#J]

  • Miss scenario: 12GW adds revised to ~8-9GW by mid-2026; renewables EBITDA growth decelerates from ~10% to ~5-6%; ~5% EPS downgrade [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: ~3-6% EPS downgrade in partial-miss scenario; ~8-10% if offshore programme materially slips and PPA pricing deteriorates concurrently [#A]

  • Red flags: Offshore supply chain constraints are a sector-wide structural issue; East Anglia Hub timeline remains uncertain; Brazilian hydrology risk adds non-wind volatility [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: PPA contracting at >95% sounds reassuring, but at what price and duration? If PPAs are signed at below-target returns due to competitive pressure from cheaper European solar, does the "contracted visibility" metric actually mask a quality-of-return deterioration? [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — PPA demand is genuine and contracted coverage is real, but offshore execution and supply chain risk are material enough to cause timeline and return slippage at current ambition scale

§4 — Key Driver #3: Interest Rate and Inflation Dynamics

  • J-front verdict: A two-edged driver that has been headwind-to-neutral L12M — higher rates hurt RAB NPVs and finance costs, but inflation pass-through in regulated frameworks provides partial offset [#J]

  • Driver definition: Iberdrola's earnings are sensitive to interest rates via: (a) cost of debt on ~€50bn gross debt, (b) regulated return formulae linked to risk-free rates, (c) NPV multiples applied by market to long-duration earnings [#J]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Higher rates → higher net finance costs → lower EPS; but also → higher allowed returns in future regulatory resets → higher regulated EBITDA (lagged, 3-5 year reset cycles) [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Rate rises compress utility multiples mechanically (long-duration DCF effect); rate cuts would support re-rating; market has de-rated IBE-ES P/E from ~22x (2021) toward ~17-19x (current) as rates rose [#A]

  • Company guide: Management guides to average cost of debt ~4.0-4.5% through 2027; hedging programme covers ~90% of 2025-2026 debt exposure; net finance cost guidance stable vs FY2024 [#A]

  • Management commentary: CMD 2024 emphasised inflation-linkage in regulated revenues as a natural hedge; highlighted 2025-2026 debt maturity profile as manageable; guided to reducing leverage ratio [#J]

  • Track record: FY2021-2024 saw finance costs rise but largely within guided ranges due to hedging programme; management credibility on this driver is medium-high [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Guidance appears reasonable given hedging coverage, but assumes rates do not spike materially beyond forward curve; a 150bps adverse move would challenge guidance [#A]

  • SS consensus: Consensus models ~4.0-4.3% average cost of debt 2025-2026; broadly aligned with management; sensitivity analyses suggest ~€100-150m EBITDA impact per 50bps rate change on unhedged portfolio [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: 100bps favourable rate move (via market re-rating + lower finance cost) = +5-10% EPS/valuation uplift; 100bps adverse = -5-8% EPS drag [#A]

  • Recent delivery: Finance costs held within guided range FY2024; hedging programme effective; ECB rate cuts beginning in H2 2024 provided modest tailwind to forward curve [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: Net finance costs rose ~20-25% 2021-2024 on rate environment; EPS growth was preserved via EBITDA growth outpacing finance cost increases; a narrowing of that buffer would be concerning [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: No cohort data; European utilities with heavy regulated networks (SSE, National Grid, Enel — public reference) have shown similar rate sensitivity (~5-8% EPS per 100bps); IBE-ES comparable [#J] (quality_flag: thin_peer_context)

  • Macro tailwind: ECB easing cycle underway — ECB cut rates 4× in H2 2024 / early 2025; forward curve implies further reductions; meaningful tailwind to both finance costs and re-rating potential [#A]

  • Macro headwind: US rates stickier — Fed more cautious; Avangrid cost of debt elevated; USD-denominated debt carries higher absolute rates than EUR [#A]

  • Inflation linkage: Most of IBE-ES's regulated revenues in Spain and UK are partially or fully CPI-linked; inflation run-rate above historical averages supports allowed revenue uplifts within current reset periods [#A]

  • Visibility: Medium — near-term hedged (high visibility); 2026-2027 refinancing needs are the open exposure; ECB trajectory is consensus-estimated but not certain [#J]

  • Bear case: Inflation re-accelerates, ECB reverses course, rates reprice 150bps higher; refinancing of 2026-2027 maturities at unexpectedly high rates; utility sector de-rates across the board [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Finance costs 10-15% above guidance; ~3-5% EPS downgrade; concurrent market multiple compression to ~14-15x amplifies share price damage [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: In a severe scenario (rates +200bps, re-rating): ~15-20% share price downside purely from multiple compression even if EPS holds [#A]

  • Red flags: €5-7bn debt maturities in 2026-2027 are the key watch item; refinancing risk if spreads widen materially [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: Management's hedging programme provides near-term comfort, but what happens when hedges roll off into a potentially higher-for-longer environment in 2026-2027? The EPS protection may be borrowed time [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — ECB easing trajectory is a genuine tailwind and hedging provides near-term protection, but 2026-2027 refinancing and rate-path uncertainty warrant ongoing monitoring

§5 — Key Driver #4: Regulatory Resets Across Geographies

  • J-front verdict: Highest case-level risk driver — regulatory resets in Spain (electricity distribution), UK , and US (Avangrid rate cases) occur across 2025-2028 and together cover the majority of regulated EBITDA [#J]

  • Driver definition: Regulated utilities operate under periodic regulatory reviews that set allowed returns on RAB for multi-year periods; adverse resets directly impair EBITDA growth visibility and valuation [#J]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Lower allowed return on RAB → lower regulated EBITDA per unit of RAB → EPS growth decelerates or falls; adverse regulatory reset can impair the compounding economics of the entire capex programme [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Regulatory reset uncertainty causes multiple compression pre-announcement; adverse outcome causes permanent re-rating (lower earnings, lower visibility premium); positive outcome can drive re-rating [#J]

  • Spain: CNMC electricity distribution regulatory review for 2026-2030 period in progress; current framework provides ~5.5-6% allowed return on RAB; market anticipates broadly stable outcome but downside scenario of 50-75bps cut exists [#A]

  • UK (RIIO-ED3): Ofgem RIIO-ED3 consultation covers 2028-2033 period; ScottishPower Networks is a major asset; RIIO-ED2 outcome (current period) was tighter than prior settlements; risk of further tightening in ED3 [#A]

  • US (Avangrid): Multiple state rate cases pending/in progress across New York (NYSEG, RG&E, CMP); track record of delays and contested outcomes; rate cases are 18-24 month processes; each incremental case matters for US EBITDA trajectory [#A]

  • Company guide: Management describes regulatory environment as "supportive and constructive" across all three geographies; CMD 2024 presented base case of stable-to-improving allowed returns [#J]

  • Management credibility: Mixed — Spain and UK management-regulator relationships are professional; Avangrid has had adversarial proceedings in New York; credibility on US regulatory outcomes is lower [#A]

  • SS consensus: Brokers generally assume regulatory status quo in Spain and UK; Avangrid is modelled with a discount; consensus does not assume a benign upside regulatory surprise [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: A 50bps reduction in Spanish allowed return on ~€30bn Spanish RAB = ~€150m EBITDA drag; ~3-4% EPS impact; UK equivalent magnitude similar [#A]

  • Recent delivery: Spain CNMC process ongoing; Ofgem RIIO-ED2 has been tracking; Avangrid New York rate cases showing incremental progress but still contested [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: No major adverse reset occurred 2021-2024 in Spain or UK; regulatory outcomes were within guided expectations; this is a future risk, not a historical pattern [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: Governments need grid investment — electrification policy pressure reduces political appetite for punitive regulatory cuts; energy security narrative broadly supportive of network returns [#J]

  • Macro headwind: Consumer energy cost sensitivity — high household energy bills create political pressure to reduce distribution charges; regulators may feel pressure to cut allowed returns [#A]

  • Visibility: Low on US, medium on Spain/UK — Avangrid rate cases are unpredictable; Spanish CNMC process is more transparent [#J]

  • Bear case: Spain CNMC cuts allowed return 75bps; UK Ofgem signals tighter RIIO-ED3 framework; Avangrid settles US rate cases below management expectations; all three adverse simultaneously = stacked downside [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Triple adverse regulatory outcome across 2025-2027 windows = ~8-12% cumulative EPS downgrade; permanent impairment to RAB growth economics [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: Adverse triple reset + multiple compression from lower earnings quality = potential 20-25% share price downside in tail scenario [#A]

  • Red flags: Avangrid's history of contested regulatory proceedings; consumer advocacy groups opposing ScottishPower in Ofgem process; Spanish political environment less predictable post-coalition complexity [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: The "governments need grid investment" narrative is real but circular — governments also need to be seen protecting consumer bills from rising network charges; the regulatory cycle has historically lagged the political cycle with adverse surprises in reset years [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — stable-to-constructive regulatory outcome is the base case across all three geographies, but the asymmetry is adverse (downside scenarios are larger in magnitude than upside), making this the highest risk driver in the portfolio

§6 — Key Driver #5: FX Translation (USD/GBP/BRL/MXN)

  • J-front verdict: A structural drag with limited management control — ~40% of EBITDA is non-EUR; currency headwinds have been a recurring annual drag; partially hedged but not eliminable [#J]

  • Driver definition: Iberdrola consolidates earnings from UK , US , Brazil , Mexico into EUR; EUR strengthening reduces reported EBITDA and EPS in EUR terms [#J]

  • Mechanism to EPS: EUR/GBP movement: ~£700m UK regulated EBITDA; 5% GBP depreciation = ~€35m EBITDA loss; EUR/USD: Avangrid ~$1.5-2bn EBITDA; 5% USD depreciation = ~€75-100m drag [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: FX is typically modelled out by sophisticated analysts but adds to EPS volatility; unexpected currency moves cause earnings misses even when underlying operations deliver [#J]

  • Company guide: Management guides to underlying constant-currency metrics; acknowledges FX as a reported EPS headwind in recent years; partial hedging via local currency debt issuance [#A]

  • Management commentary: CMD 2024 noted BRL and MXN as having been headwinds in FY2023-2024; US dollar broadly supportive at current levels; management does not guide on FX directly [#J]

  • Track record: FY2022-2024: BRL depreciation contributed ~€200-300m cumulative EBITDA headwind on a constant-currency basis; MXN broadly flat to slightly negative; GBP post-Brexit range-bound [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Guidance on constant-currency metrics flatters reported performance — investors receive reported EUR numbers, not constant-currency; the difference matters at 15-20x multiples [#A]

  • SS consensus: Most brokers model currency assumptions broadly at forward curve; consensus EPS already reflects some FX drag; surprises are possible in either direction [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Full-year 10% BRL depreciation from current levels = ~€60-80m EBITDA; 10% USD depreciation = ~€150-200m; combined = ~3-5% EPS impact [#A]

  • Recent delivery: FY2024 showed modest FX tailwind from USD stability; BRL/MXN less adverse than FY2023; modest net positive vs prior year [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: Net FX headwind ~€200-400m cumulative EBITDA 2021-2024 (own-facts estimate from reported vs constant-currency disclosures) [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: No cohort data; European utilities with EM exposure (Enel, EDP — public reference) have shown similar pattern of recurring FX drags on reported numbers [#J] (quality_flag: thin_peer_context)

  • Macro tailwind: USD relative strength in 2024-2025 has been broadly supportive for USD-denominated earnings translated to EUR; potential for continuation if US growth outperforms [#A]

  • Macro headwind: BRL and MXN structural fragility — fiscal positions in Brazil and Mexico create ongoing depreciation risk; political risk in Mexico (energy nationalisation rhetoric) adds to MXN exposure [#A]

  • Visibility: Low — FX is inherently unpredictable; forward curve provides only a probability-weighted estimate; tail risks (EM crisis, political shock) cannot be hedged at scale [#J]

  • Bear case: BRL depreciates 20%+, MXN weakens further, GBP weakens on UK fiscal concerns; cumulative drag ~€400-600m EBITDA; ~6-8% EPS headwind in worst year [#A]

  • Miss scenario: Consensus already embedded with moderate FX assumptions; a 10% adverse move beyond consensus in two currencies simultaneously = ~3-4% EPS miss in a given year [#A]

  • Red flags: Mexico political risk on energy policy is a latent non-linear risk; Brazilian fiscal trajectory bears watching [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: Management's practice of emphasising constant-currency metrics in CMD presentations is structurally optimistic; every year that BRL or MXN weakens materially, the "underlying" narrative is operationally true but financially misleading for EUR-reporting purposes [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Low-medium plausibility as a positive driver — FX is a structural volatility source, not a growth lever; base case is mild headwind continuation, not a tailwind; difficult to assign directional conviction

§7 — Key Driver #6: Capital Structure + Leverage Management

  • J-front verdict: Defensive driver with moderate EPS optionality — Iberdrola targets FFO/Net Debt ~22-24%; successful deleveraging reduces refinancing risk and supports dividend sustainability; not a growth driver but a floor [#J]

  • Driver definition: Iberdrola carries ~€50bn gross debt; managing leverage ratio (FFO/Net Debt) within target bands, extending maturities, and maintaining investment-grade ratings underpins financial stability and dividend policy [#J]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Lower net debt → lower net finance costs → higher EPS; maintaining IG rating avoids credit spread widening; hybrid/green bond issuance at below-market rates provides incremental efficiency [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Capital structure confidence supports dividend sustainability; dividend yield is 4-5% and is a key valuation anchor for income-oriented institutional holders; any dividend cut risk would trigger de-rating [#J]

  • Company guide: Target FFO/Net Debt ~22-24%; Net Debt/EBITDA below 4.5x through 2027; management guides to €10bn+ equity proceeds from asset rotation and minority stake sales to fund capex without equity dilution [#A]

  • Management commentary: CMD 2024 reiterated balance sheet self-funding model; asset rotation (selling minority stakes in renewables projects, network companies) is the key capital efficiency tool [#J]

  • Asset rotation track record: IBE-ES has been consistent L5Y in asset rotation — sold stakes in UK networks, Brazilian assets, Mexican wind farms; proceeds reinvested in new capex; model has worked operationally [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Asset rotation depends on buyer market — infrastructure fund appetite for renewables and network stakes has been strong, but rate sensitivity means buyer return requirements are elevated; deals have taken longer to close in 2023-2024 [#A]

  • SS consensus: Consensus broadly comfortable with balance sheet; no solvency concerns; leverage ratios within sector norms for regulated utility; debate is whether asset rotation pace meets capex funding needs [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Successful de-leveraging trajectory (50bps FFO/ND improvement per year) = €100-150m annual finance cost reduction; +2-3% EPS annually [#A]

  • Recent delivery: FY2024 FFO/Net Debt ~22-23% (within target); green bond issuance at competitive spreads; no credit rating actions adverse [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: Leverage broadly stable 2021-2024 despite large capex programme; asset rotation has funded incremental investment without balance sheet deterioration [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: Infrastructure fund demand for green assets remains strong; green bond market pricing has been supportive for investment-grade utilities [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Higher-for-longer rates increase buyer required returns on asset rotation deals; multiple compression in private infrastructure markets reduces proceeds from stake sales [#A]

  • Dividend: Current dividend ~€0.55/share; yield ~4-5% at current price; dividend growth guidance +5% annually; well-covered by earnings and FCF [#A]

  • Visibility: High on near-term — leverage ratios within guidance; IG rating secure; near-term refinancing covered by hedging programme; 2026-2027 the open window [#J]

  • Bear case: Asset rotation market freezes (rate shock); buyer appetite for infrastructure stakes collapses; Iberdrola forced to choose between slowing capex or straining balance sheet [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Asset rotation delivers 50-60% of targeted proceeds; capex plan trimmed by €3-5bn; EPS growth decelerates by 2-3 percentage points; rating maintained but perception weakens [#A]

  • Red flags: Asset rotation pipeline must be executed under market conditions; interest rate sensitivity of infrastructure valuations creates latent execution risk [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: The "self-funding model" has worked when infrastructure multiples were high (2017-2022); in a structurally higher rate world, the recycling model may require selling assets at lower valuations than assumed, creating a structural funding gap [#J]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility as a defensive driver — balance sheet management is competent and within targets; near-term funded; risk is in the 2026-2027 refinancing and asset rotation execution window, not the immediate period

§10 — Driver Synthesis + Sceptical Synthesis

  • J-front verdict: The strongest bearish case for IBE-ES is a regulatory reset + rate shock combination — these two drivers are correlated (both triggered by a macro inflection), simultaneously impair earnings and multiple, and are partially outside management's control [#J]

  • Driver dependency: Drivers #1 (RAB/capex) and #4 (regulatory resets) are directly linked — the capex programme earns returns only if regulatory frameworks permit adequate returns; if #4 fails, #1's value is undermined [#J]
