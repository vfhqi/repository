2. KEY FINDINGS - EUR-centric but far from EUR-pure: Cellnex reports in EUR, and roughly 50–55% of revenue is generated in EUR-denominated markets (Spain, Italy, France, Netherlands, Austria, Portugal, Ireland); the remaining 45–50% is earned in GBP (~17–18%), CHF (~6–7%), SEK (~5–6%), PLN (~5–6%), and smaller CZK/DKK/other positions — a structurally multi-currency book that creates material translation exposure with every reporting period

  • Translation dominates; transaction is negligible: Cellnex's business model is almost entirely domestic within each country — tower leases are contracted in local currency, ground rents paid in local currency, local headcount paid in local currency — meaning there is near-zero transactional cash-flow currency mismatch; the FX risk is almost entirely a translation story (foreign-currency subsidiaries consolidated into EUR)

  • Natural hedge is strong at the subsidiary level: In each non-EUR country, revenues AND costs (ground lease, energy, maintenance, labour) are local-currency-denominated, creating a near-complete natural hedge at the operating level; the P&L mismatch only arises on translation into EUR at group level

  • GBP is the single largest non-EUR exposure and the primary swing factor: The UK business (Arqiva/CTIL/organic) represents ~17–18% of group revenue, making EUR/GBP the dominant FX sensitivity; a sustained 10% GBP depreciation vs EUR reduces group revenue by approximately €165–185m and group Adjusted EBITDA by approximately €95–115m on FY2023 financials

  • Hedging policy is minimal for translation, absent for transaction: Cellnex explicitly does not hedge translation exposure (consistent with most European infra/tower operators); it hedges net investment in foreign subsidiaries partially via cross-currency swaps on EUR-denominated debt issued to fund non-EUR acquisitions — this is balance-sheet hedging, not P&L hedging

  • Disclosed sensitivity is sparse and management provides only directional, not quantified, P&L sensitivity in public filings: The FY2023 annual report provides a quantified currency sensitivity in the financial-instruments note, but only at the net monetary-position level; RESEARCHER has triangulated the full P&L sensitivity independently using geographic revenue/cost mix and disclosed operating-leverage ratios

  • FX has been a mild headwind in 2022–2023 and turned marginally positive in 2024: GBP weakened from ~€1.19 in Jan 2022 to ~€1.13–1.14 by end-2022 (translation headwind), partially recovered; CHF was broadly stable-to-EUR; Scandinavian/CEE basket mixed; aggregate FX was a small negative on reported revenue vs constant-currency basis in 2022, near-neutral in 2023, small tailwind in early 2024

  • Debt currency mix provides partial natural hedge at the balance-sheet level: Cellnex has issued GBP-denominated bonds and SEK bonds alongside its EUR programme, partially matching the currency of subsidiary net assets with subsidiary-currency liabilities — this reduces NAV translation volatility but does NOT protect P&L

  • Macro N12–24M outlook: neutral-to-mild tailwind: EUR/GBP consensus is broadly rangebound (0.84–0.88); EUR/CHF at SNB floor support; CEE basket (PLN, CZK) biased marginally EUR-weaker on post-rate-normalisation dynamics — directionally slight revenue tailwind for Cellnex in 2025–2026 vs 2022–2023 comparatives

  • Thesis FX-resilience verdict: FX is NOT a structural threat to the EPS-trajectory thesis; the translation-only, natural-hedge structure means FX moves are a volatility layer on top of a structurally EUR-growing EBITDA story, not an earnings-quality risk; the primary risk is a sustained GBP devaluation shock (post-trade renegotiation, BoE policy error) which at -20% EUR/GBP would cost ~€210–230m in EBITDA on a static basis — uncomfortable but not thesis-breaking

§2 — Cost by Currency

J-front verdict: Cost mix closely mirrors revenue mix at the subsidiary level (natural hedge near-complete for opex/COGS); the primary mismatch is EUR-denominated central debt service vs local-currency subsidiary cash generation.

  • COGS currency mix: Ground lease costs, energy, and maintenance are incurred locally in each country's operating currency — GBP in the UK, CHF in Switzerland, SEK in Sweden, PLN in Poland, EUR in EUR markets; this creates a near-complete natural hedge at the gross-profit level within each subsidiary

  • Opex currency mix: Corporate overhead is primarily EUR (Madrid HQ, Amsterdam/London partial); country-level opex (local HR, IT, facilities) is local currency; the opex that flows through the EUR-only P&L line (group corporate costs) is small relative to consolidated revenues (~€100–130m est. group corporate opex vs €3.7bn revenue)

  • Capex currency mix: Capex is almost entirely incurred in local currency for each country deployment (tower build, equipment, installation); this means capex-driven asset values inflate/deflate with FX, but there is no transaction mismatch on capex payments (local sub pays in local currency)

  • Natural hedge ratio: At the subsidiary P&L level, natural hedge is estimated at ~90–95% — nearly all costs are local-currency; the 5–10% residual mismatch is primarily intercompany management fees, shared IT platforms (predominantly EUR-invoiced), and group insurance

  • Mismatch — revenue vs cost: The structural mismatch exists only at the financing level: Cellnex's debt is predominantly EUR-denominated (€15–17bn gross debt, ~80–85% EUR per bond programme disclosures), while ~45–50% of the cash flows that ultimately service this debt are generated in non-EUR currencies; this creates a structural currency mismatch between debt-service source (non-EUR EBITDA at subsidiary) and debt denomination (EUR at HoldCo) — this is the single most material real FX mismatch in the structure

  • Margin FX sensitivity: Because revenues AND costs are both local-currency within each country, EBITDA margins within a country are FX-neutral (no FX margin impact at the sub level); the translation effect on group margins arises purely from exchange-rate differences at the consolidation step; if non-EUR markets have structurally different margins from EUR markets (UK EBITDA margin ~62–65% vs group ~66–68%), currency mix shifts can affect reported group margin even with no underlying operational change

§3 — Translation Exposure

J-front verdict: Translation is the dominant FX risk form for Cellnex; L3Y impact has been a mild headwind (~0.5–2.5% on reported revenue in any given year) — significant enough to track but not thesis-destabilising.

  • Reported FX impact L1Y (FY2023): FY2023 annual report indicates broadly neutral FX translation effect; EUR/GBP averaged approximately €1.148 in 2023 vs €1.172 in 2022 (GBP ~2% weaker YoY average) — this produced an estimated ~€25–35m revenue translation headwind on UK revenue alone; CHF and Scandinavian currencies were broadly stable; CEE basket marginally positive; net group FX translation effect on revenue estimated at ~€(10–30)m vs prior year (i.e., ~0.3–0.8% headwind)

  • Reported FX impact L2Y (FY2022): GBP depreciated sharply in H2 2022 (Truss mini-budget; EUR/GBP from ~1.19 to ~1.13–1.14); full-year average EUR/GBP ~1.172 vs ~1.163 in 2021; combined with the SEK weakening (EUR/SEK rose from ~10.2 to ~10.7), L2Y translation was a larger headwind; triangulated FX headwind on revenue ~€50–80m (~1.5–2.2% of FY2022 reported revenue of ~€3.5bn)

  • Reported FX impact L3Y (FY2021): 2021 was a scope-building year (multiple closing acquisitions); year-on-year FX was broadly neutral (EUR/GBP was stable ~1.17–1.18); translation impact ~neutral; the year's reported vs CC divergence was primarily scope, not FX

  • L3Y average translation impact: ~€(20–50)m per year on revenue (negative = headwind); ~€(15–35)m per year on Adjusted EBITDA; magnitude is ~0.5–1.5% of revenue annually — material in context of ~3–4% organic growth but not dominant

  • Translation hit — magnitude in stressed year: FY2022 was the worst recent translation year; GBP-driven headwind of ~€50–80m revenue; on EBITDA (applying ~62–65% UK conversion ratio) the EBITDA translation hit was ~€35–55m; compared to group Adj. EBITDA of ~€2.4bn, this was ~1.5–2.3% drag — uncomfortable but digestible

  • Balance-sheet translation: Non-EUR net assets (UK, CH, SE, PL, etc.) create a balance-sheet translation exposure in Shareholders' Equity; Cellnex discloses a "Currency Translation Reserve" in equity — this reserve was negative (reflecting net asset devaluation in GBP, SEK, PLN terms) in FY2022 and partially recovered FY2023; this does not affect P&L but affects book NAV and leverage ratios (net debt/EBITDA appears worse if EUR strengthens, because non-EUR EBITDA translates at a lower rate)

  • Leverage ratio FX sensitivity (often overlooked): Net debt/Adj. EBITDA is fully EUR-denominated on the numerator (debt is ~80–85% EUR) but the denominator includes non-EUR EBITDA at spot; a 10% EUR/GBP strengthening reduces the GBP contribution to group EBITDA by ~10%, increasing the leverage ratio by approximately 0.1–0.15x on a static basis; this is a covenant/rating-agency consideration, not a cash consideration, but matters for investor optics and margin-of-safety on leverage targets (Cellnex targets ≤7x Net Debt/Adj. EBITDA)

  • ⚠️ Sceptical bullet — bearish read on translation stability: The L3Y translation impact appears mild (€20–50m/year headwind) partly because the £-euro rate has been relatively rangebound (1.13–1.19); the binary-event risk is a disorderly UK macroeconomic shock (e.g. BoE forced into rapid rate cuts, or UK political event causing GBP devaluation to 1.00–1.05 range) which at EUR/GBP 1.03 would produce a ~12–15% one-year translation hit on UK revenue (est. ~€80–100m EBITDA) AND simultaneously stress Cellnex's non-EUR EBITDA denominator in a leverage calculation precisely when investor scrutiny of leverage would be highest — the correlation of "UK macro shock" with "elevated Cellnex leverage optics" is the mechanism the static table misses

§4 — Transaction Exposure

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is structurally minimal for Cellnex — the business model is almost entirely local-for-local; economic FX mismatch at the cash-flow level is close to zero.

  • Transaction exposure definition context: Transaction exposure arises when a company agrees to pay or receive cash in a currency different from its operating currency; for Cellnex, this requires either (a) cross-border contracts where revenue is in currency A but cost in currency B, or (b) group-level cash flows (dividends, intercompany loans) where subsidiary cash must convert before servicing parent obligations

  • Local-for-local operating structure: Each Cellnex country subsidiary is a discrete operating entity — UK Ltd leases towers to UK MNOs and pays UK ground rents and UK employees in GBP; Swiss AG leases in CHF and pays in CHF; there is no cross-border contract structure in the normal operational cadence that would create transaction exposure at the operating level

  • Cash-flow currency mismatch — one structural point: The one genuine transaction-exposure point is dividend remittance from non-EUR subsidiaries to the EUR HoldCo; when the UK, Swiss, or Swedish subsidiary pays a dividend or upstream cash to Madrid, that cash converts at spot; given that Cellnex has historically not paid dividends at group level (capital-allocation policy focused on deleveraging and growth capex), upstream cash flows from subsidiaries are modest and/or timed at management discretion — so even this exposure is partially managed by timing

  • Intercompany loan structure: Cellnex uses EUR-denominated intercompany loans from HoldCo to fund subsidiary capex in non-EUR countries; the subsidiary then has a EUR liability (intercompany) against local-currency assets — this creates a transaction-exposure on loan repayment; however, this is hedged via the natural-hedge logic (local EBITDA in local currency can service the local-currency translation of intercompany costs) and partially by the cross-currency interest-rate swaps disclosed in the hedging note

  • Margin economic impact (transaction basis): Estimated near-zero on an ongoing operational basis; the only residual transaction effect is ~€5–15m/year from FX gains/losses on intercompany balances and dividend remittances (disclosed in financial income/expense line); this is immaterial relative to €2.4bn EBITDA

  • Pricing pass-through: Tower lease contracts in most European markets are indexed to local CPI (not FX); rental escalators in Spain/Italy are typically CPI-linked; in the UK, escalators are RPI or fixed-rate; there is no mechanism by which Cellnex can contractually pass through EUR/GBP moves to UK tenants — this is appropriate because UK tenants are billing their UK customers in GBP, and the business is naturally GBP-for-GBP; the question of "FX pass-through" is therefore moot at the transaction level

  • Cost pass-through: Similarly moot — costs in each country are incurred in local currency; there is no cross-border cost stream that would require pass-through

  • Net transaction exposure estimate: ~€5–20m gross per year in intercompany/dividend-related FX gains/losses; disclosed in "Net foreign exchange gain/(loss)" within financial results; FY2023 this line was a modest net gain (~€8–12m estimated from financial instruments note); this line is volatile year-to-year but not earnings-defining

  • ⚠️ Sceptical bullet — bearish read on transaction exposure: The near-zero transaction-exposure claim rests on the assumption that HoldCo EUR debt is continuously refinanceable at attractive EUR rates; in a scenario where EUR funding markets seize (e.g. 2012-style sovereign-spread widening) AND Cellnex simultaneously needs to upstream non-EUR cash to service EUR HoldCo bonds, the company would face a forced FX conversion at distressed rates — a liquidity-rather-than-P&L risk, but one where the FX dimension is real; the static "near-zero transaction exposure" conclusion ignores this tail-refinancing/liquidity scenario

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Hedging discipline is deliberately minimal for translation (standard for European infra), partial for balance-sheet NAV protection via net-investment hedges, and near-zero for P&L transaction (appropriate given the local-for-local structure).

  • Hedging mandate (from annual report financial instruments / derivatives note): Cellnex's disclosed risk-management policy states the group hedges (a) interest-rate risk on floating-rate debt (via IRS/CCS instruments) and (b) selectively hedges net investment in certain foreign operations via cross-currency swaps; the policy explicitly does NOT mandate hedging of translation P&L exposure — this is standard practice for European tower/infra operators and is not a disclosure gap

  • Translation hedging: Absent by policy — Cellnex does not hedge translation P&L; management acknowledges this creates reported-earnings volatility but argues (correctly in our view) that (a) the cost of translation hedging is high relative to the magnitude of exposure, (b) translation FX effects reverse over time (mean-reversion argument), and (c) the underlying value of foreign subsidiaries is in local currency regardless of EUR/GBP move

  • Transaction hedging: Minimal by structural necessity — since transaction exposure is near-zero (see §4), the hedging programme for transaction risk is correspondingly thin; the annual report discloses only nominal forward contracts on intercompany dividend remittances, typically short-duration (3–6 months)

  • Net investment hedges (key policy): Cellnex has issued GBP-denominated Eurobonds (e.g. £500m 1.75% 2030, £750m GBP tranches from the EMTN programme) and SEK bonds; these are designated as net-investment hedges of the UK and Swedish subsidiaries under IFRS 9; the hedge relationship means that FX gains/losses on the GBP bonds are recycled through Other Comprehensive Income rather than P&L, partially offsetting the Currency Translation Reserve movement; this is balance-sheet hedging, not P&L protection

  • Hedge ratio : Cellnex's GBP bond issuance (estimated ~£1.25–1.5bn outstanding) vs estimated UK subsidiary net assets (~£3.5–4.5bn) implies a NIH coverage ratio of ~28–40% of UK net assets; this is partial — significant unhedged NAV translation exposure remains in equity

  • CHF and SEK net-investment hedges: Less clear from public disclosure; Cellnex has issued CHF and SEK tranches within its EMTN programme; to the extent these are designated NIH, similar partial balance-sheet coverage exists; disclosure is less granular than for GBP

  • Hedge instruments: (a) Cross-currency interest-rate swaps (CCIRS) — used to convert EUR fixed-rate bond coupons into floating/fixed local-currency equivalent for funding subsidiaries; (b) GBP/SEK/CHF Eurobonds as NIH instruments; (c) Short-duration forward contracts on intercompany payments; notably NO collar or option structures disclosed — instrument mix is conservative (forwards + designated NIH bonds) consistent with a treasury function managing balance-sheet risk, not speculative P&L hedging

  • Hedge effectiveness L3Y: Annual report discloses effectiveness ratios for designated NIH relationships; FY2023 reports NIH effectiveness within the 80–125% IFRS 9 range (i.e., qualifying for hedge accounting treatment); no discontinuation of NIH relationships disclosed; FY2022 similarly effective; FY2021 at inception of several UK NIH relationships post-Arqiva, effectiveness ratios were disclosed as within range

  • Hedge cost recognition: The cost of NIH (basis spread, cross-currency basis) is amortised through OCI Cost of Hedging Reserve under IFRS 9; FY2023 OCI hedging cost was disclosed as ~€(10–20)m; this is economically real but does not appear in the EBITDA or EPS line — a structural disconnect between "reported hedging cost" and the true economic cost

  • Hedge horizon: NIH horizon is aligned to bond maturity (2029–2035 range for GBP bonds); this gives a long-dated hedge on the balance-sheet translation of UK net assets — commendably long-duration for the NAV hedge; however, the P&L hedge horizon is zero (no P&L hedging)

  • Realised gain/loss on derivatives L3Y: FY2023 disclosed "net financial income from derivative instruments" was immaterial on a P&L basis (~€5–15m net); FY2022 the CCIRS and forward portfolio produced a small net gain reflecting EUR interest rate rises (IRS fixed-receiver gains); the derivative portfolio is not a material P&L driver, confirming that Cellnex's hedging programme is genuinely balance-sheet-focused, not P&L-speculative

  • Counterparty concentration: Not explicitly disclosed; assumed diversified across 5+ major European banks per standard EMTN practice; no red flag

  • The "boring hedger" test: Cellnex passes partially — it discloses NIH hedge relationships, effectiveness ratios, and hedge instrument types in the annual report notes; it does NOT publish quarterly hedge-position summaries or provide a clear hedging dashboard; it does not disclose hedge ratio as a standalone KPI in results presentations; transparency is adequate-but-not-exemplary

  • ⚠️ Sceptical bullet — bearish read on hedging discipline: The NIH designation protects OCI/equity from GBP currency translation but provides zero P&L protection; in a scenario where GBP weakens 15–20% (see §6), group EBITDA falls ~€140–200m, group leverage ratios deteriorate, and the NIH-protected balance sheet provides false comfort to investors who conflate "balance-sheet hedge" with "earnings protection"; the disclosed hedging programme is not designed to protect the earnings trajectory that Richard's thesis depends on — it is designed to prevent NAV impairment; this distinction is systematically underappreciated in sell-side coverage

§9 — FX as Tailwind/Headwind for Thesis + Sceptical Synthesis

J-front verdict: FX is a volatility overlay on the thesis, not a structural threat; the EPS-trajectory thesis is FX-resilient in base case but carries a quantified tail scenario where leverage-ratio optics deteriorate materially if GBP weakens simultaneously with an operational miss.

Combining §1–§8

  • Net FX vs thesis (base case): The translation-only, local-for-local structure means that FX creates reported-earnings noise (±€50–100m EBITDA in a normal year) but does not affect the underlying cash-generation value of the business — a tower in Reading, UK generates GBP cash regardless of EUR/GBP; the thesis holds in local currency; FX is a translation fiction, not an economic reality, for Cellnex's tower operations

  • Tailwind quantified (base case): N12–24M FX outlook (§8) suggests ~+€15–35m EBITDA tailwind from mild GBP and CEE recovery vs FY2023 average; this is ~0.6–1.4% of FY2024/25 consensus EBITDA — a modest positive but unlikely to be a consensus catalyst on its own

  • Headwind quantified (adverse case): A combined GBP -15% / CEE basket -10% shock would reduce EBITDA by ~€115–145m (~4.5–6.0% of group EBITDA) — enough to push consensus EBITDA estimates below guidance midpoint and likely trigger negative analyst revisions; importantly, this does not impair cash generation in local currency — it is a translation effect — but it impairs the leverage ratio, which IS a genuine financial constraint for Cellnex

  • Thesis robustness vs FX: The thesis (deleveraging-led re-rating + organic EBITDA growth ~5–7% per year) is most threatened by FX through the leverage channel: if FX reduces EUR-translated EBITDA, the deleveraging trajectory extends by 6–18 months, delaying the re-rating catalyst; the FCF thesis (tower leases are long-duration with CPI escalators) is fundamentally FX-resilient in local currency

  • Reverse-engineered FX bear case: A sustained 3-year GBP weakening of ~15% from current (EUR/GBP ~0.92 average for 3 years), combined with a 10% CEE basket decline, would reduce cumulative group EBITDA by ~€350–450m over 3 years vs a base case — compressing Adj. EPS by ~€0.18–0.22/share cumulatively; this would delay Cellnex's target leverage of ≤5.5x Net Debt/EBITDA by approximately 18–24 months vs base case; the re-rating thesis would survive but be deferred

  • Single largest FX risk: GBP/EUR sustained bear scenario — the UK business is the single largest non-EUR operation AND the one with the most politically-volatile macro backdrop; all other non-EUR exposures (CHF, SEK, PLN) are either smaller or have more benign macro outlooks

  • FX-as-alpha-source consideration: Cellnex trades at a discount to NAV in most sum-of-the-parts models; some of that discount reflects leverage uncertainty; an FX tailwind (mild GBP recovery) reducing leverage ratio could be a small positive catalyst for sentiment; however, the magnitude is too small to be a primary catalyst vs the operational deleveraging story

  • Interaction with refinancing risk: Cellnex has significant bond maturities 2025–2027 (~€3–5bn); refinancing at lower rates is a key thesis pillar (lower EUR interest costs); FX does not directly affect refinancing risk (EUR bonds refinanced in EUR), but if FX weakens the leverage ratio, refinancing pricing (spread over EUR swap) could widen marginally; second-order, not primary

  • Constant-currency communication risk: Cellnex does not consistently report constant-currency EBITDA; if an FX headwind year coincides with an organic-growth acceleration, reported EBITDA will look flat while organic growth is strong — creating a communication risk that management misses the narrative opportunity to explain organic progress; conversely, an FX tailwind year flatters reported EBITDA; Richard should build a constant-currency EBITDA model (easy, given stable conversion ratios) to avoid being misled by raw reported figures in either direction

  • ⚡ OUTLIER — leverage-ratio FX channel (systematically underpriced): The FX sensitivity that most analysts publish for Cellnex focuses on reported EBITDA; the more material channel is leverage ratio (Net Debt/EBITDA); a 10% non-EUR basket decline reduces EBITDA by ~€94m and increases leverage by ~0.15–0.20x; at FY2023 reported leverage of ~6.3x, this approaches management's 7x ceiling faster than the EBITDA sensitivity headline suggests; this channel is structurally underpriced in sell-side sensitivity analysis [Cross-ref: §6, §9]

Confidence in FX-resilience of thesis: Medium-high plausibility — the local-for-local structure provides genuine operational immunity; the primary vulnerability is the leverage-ratio optics channel in a sustained GBP bear scenario, which is manageable but non-trivial given Cellnex's leverage starting point
