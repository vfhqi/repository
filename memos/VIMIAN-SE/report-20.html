3. §1 — EXECUTIVE SUMMARY: Top-3 Most-Diagnostic Questions

  • VERDICT — top-3 crystallised: Three questions most discriminate bull/bear/false-friend — organic quality, M&A-stripped growth, and the weekly leading indicator management actually uses
  • Q-1 (organic mix probe): "Within each of your four specialty verticals, what percentage of revenue is genuinely proprietary product vs. distributed or co-developed — and how has that ratio moved in the last 24 months?"
  • Q-2 (M&A-stripped scenario): "If Vimian made zero acquisitions in the next 24 months, what is your internal central estimate for organic revenue CAGR and EBITDA margin exit-rate?"
  • Q-3 (leading-indicator probe): "What is the single weekly or monthly KPI that most reliably leads your confidence in annual guidance — and what is it showing right now?"
  • Why these three are load-bearing: Each answer will either validate the compounding organic thesis, reveal acquisition-dependence, or expose sandbagging/visibility quality — the three key hypothesis discriminants from Q19 ACH matrix
  • Sceptical bullet: Questions the analyst might NOT want to ask — "Have you ever lost a major vet-clinic customer to a competitor?" — because fluent denial is hard to cross-check, but a hesitant answer is the most diagnostic signal; Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 1 says go there anyway

4. §2 — THEME 1: Revenue Drivers + Visibility

  • VERDICT — revenue-side: Organic growth quality is the axis of highest uncertainty; public disclosure blends acquisition contribution into headline growth in ways that make independent validation difficult — questions below are diagnostic

Q2.1 — Volume vs. price split

  • Question: "For FY24, what percentage of your reported organic growth was pure volume growth vs. pricing — and how does that split vary by specialty vertical?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely cite mid-single-digit pricing and low-single-digit volume, with dermatology/ophthalmology outperforming on price
  • Good answer: Specific per-vertical breakdowns with directional trends — shows granular internal tracking
  • Bad answer: Blended group-level answer only, or deflects to "we don't manage that way" — obscures the real quality of growth
  • Follow-up probe: "Which vertical has the highest pricing sensitivity — where would a 100bps price increase cause measurable volume loss?"
  • Diagnostic note: Volume-heavy growth in a specialty animal health model implies product adoption momentum (bull); price-heavy growth that is decelerating into FY25 implies ceiling risk (bear); inability to split = false-friend signal on reporting quality

Q2.2 — End-market segmentation + vet-clinic channel dynamics

  • Question: "What proportion of revenue flows through independent vet clinics vs. corporate vet groups — and is the corporate-group share growing, stable, or declining?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely indicate corporate vet groups are growing share of Vimian's mix; they have explicitly partnered with consolidators
  • Good answer: Specific percentage split + directional trend + commentary on whether margin profile differs — shows channel-level P&L awareness
  • Bad answer: Vague reference to "broad distribution" without channel-level data — suggests management doesn't track or doesn't want to disclose
  • Follow-up probe: "Do corporate vet groups extract meaningfully different pricing than independents — and is that margin differential widening?"
  • Diagnostic note: Growing corporate-vet-group share with margin compression = pricing power erosion (bear); growing share with stable margins = channel mix tailwind (bull); mix-shift opacity = false-friend

Q2.3 — Customer concentration + churn / retention

  • Question: "What is the revenue contribution of your top 10 customers as a percentage of group revenue — and what was churn at that cohort over the last two years?"
  • Expected answer: Specialty pharma roll-ups at this stage typically have top-10 customers at 20–35% of revenue; management may resist precise disclosure
  • Good answer: Willingness to give a directional range AND confirm that top-10 churn has been low (sub-5% annually) — confirms stickiness thesis
  • Bad answer: Deflects entirely or cites "diversified customer base" without data — the answer to a concentration question is always a number, not a narrative
  • Follow-up probe: "Have you lost any customer relationship representing >1% of group revenue in the last 36 months — and if so, why?"
  • Diagnostic note: Low churn among top customers = durable revenue (bull); any >1% customer loss they struggled to name = credibility signal on retention claims

Q2.4 — Forward order book / visibility

  • Question: "What percentage of FY25 budgeted revenue is already contracted, committed, or highly predictable vs. genuinely uncertain as of today?"
  • Expected answer: Animal health specialty products have meaningful recurring revenue from formulary/contract cycles; management likely claims 60–75% visibility
  • Good answer: A specific percentage with clear definitional discipline (what counts as "contracted") — higher is better, but definitional rigour matters more than the number
  • Bad answer: "We have strong recurring revenue" without a quantified visibility percentage — recurring revenue claim without data is management-speak
  • Follow-up probe: "Is visibility higher or lower than this time last year — and what's driving the delta?"
  • Diagnostic note: Visibility deterioration YoY = demand softening or contract-cycle risk (bear); visibility improvement = compounding formula-lock (bull)

Q2.5 — Share gain / loss

  • Question: "In your core dermatology and ophthalmology verticals, are you gaining or losing market share — and what is your evidence base for that assessment?"
  • Expected answer: Management will assert share gain; the diagnostic question is what evidence they cite (third-party market data, internal sales velocity, clinical adoption rates)
  • Good answer: Reference to specific third-party data sources or vet-clinic-level adoption metrics — shows they track share independently, not just from their own sell-through
  • Bad answer: "We believe we are gaining share" without evidence base — unverifiable assertion; the worst answer is one that sounds confident but is derived purely from their own shipment data
  • Follow-up probe: "Which competitor has most surprised you in the last 18 months — and in which vertical?"
  • Diagnostic note: A competitor they can name specifically + explain why = competitive-map awareness (bull); "no major competitive threats" = either dominant position or monitored-blind-spot (requires further probe)
  • Sceptical bullet: A fluent, specific answer on share gain is NOT comfort — it may be rehearsed; the diagnostic signal is whether management volunteers a vertical where they are losing ground

5. §3 — THEME 2: Margin Trajectory + Levers

  • VERDICT — margin-side: The decentralised operating model creates structural opacity on where margin is actually generated; the integration cost overhang from recent acquisitions is the key uncertainty — questions below probe whether EBITDA margin improvement is structural or phased-cost-timing

Q3.1 — GM driver + pricing-cost spread

  • Question: "At gross margin level, what is your cost of goods sold trajectory — and how much of the FY24 GM improvement was structural pricing vs. input-cost tailwind?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely cite both pricing discipline and some input-cost relief (API costs, logistics) in FY24
  • Good answer: Clear attribution between pricing effect and input-cost tailwind, with forward visibility on whether input-cost tailwind persists or reverses — specificity signals real P&L management
  • Bad answer: "Gross margin improved as expected" without causal decomposition — unhelpful; every analyst already knows GM moved, the question is why and whether it's durable
  • Follow-up probe: "If input costs re-inflated 5% in FY25, what is your best estimate of GM compression — do you have pricing headroom to offset?"
  • Diagnostic note: Clear pricing offset capacity = genuine pricing power (bull); input-cost-dependent GM improvement that can't be offset = fragile margin profile (bear)

Q3.2 — Operating leverage + decentralised cost structure

  • Question: "In your decentralised model, where does operating leverage actually accrue — at the brand/vertical level or only at group central functions?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely cite shared services savings at group level + scale benefits in distribution; they may resist brand-level granularity
  • Good answer: Can identify specific cost lines where group scale creates leverage for individual brands — e.g. regulatory filing costs, pharmacovigilance, distribution — and can size the benefit
  • Bad answer: "Our decentralised model creates natural efficiency" — circular answer that explains nothing about where leverage is actually captured
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the incremental EBITDA margin on the next €50m of organic revenue growth, holding M&A constant?"
  • Diagnostic note: Incremental EBITDA margin >40% on organic growth = real operating leverage (bull); incremental margin <25% = scale-insensitive cost base (bear)

Q3.3 — Mix shift

  • Question: "How is the revenue mix shifting between your higher-margin proprietary products and lower-margin distributed products — and what is the absolute margin gap between those two pools?"
  • Expected answer: Management consistently messaging proprietary mix improvement; the diagnostic is whether they can quantify the margin gap between proprietary and distributed
  • Good answer: Specific gross margin differential (e.g. "proprietary products carry ~X% higher gross margin than distributed") and a clear trajectory for mix improvement
  • Bad answer: Deflects to "we focus on overall group margins" — inability to distinguish proprietary vs. distributed margin is either a systems gap or a disclosure choice, both are concerning
  • Follow-up probe: "If distributed revenue grew 20% faster than proprietary in FY25 due to a large tender win, how would that impact reported group GM?"
  • Diagnostic note: Inability to answer the counterfactual = P&L transparency gap (false-friend signal)

Q3.4 — R&D adequacy + pipeline investment

  • Question: "What is your R&D spend as a percentage of revenue — and is that level sufficient to maintain product competitiveness over a 5-year horizon without licensing external IP?"
  • Expected answer: Animal health specialty R&D spend at roll-ups is typically 4–8% of revenue; Vimian may be on the lower end given acquisition-driven growth strategy
  • Good answer: Specific R&D% with a forward commitment — "we are increasing to X% because our pipeline requires it" — signals organic innovation investment not just buy-and-hold
  • Bad answer: "We supplement R&D with acquisitions" — legitimate but raises the question of what happens when the acquisition pipeline dries up or prices increase
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the longest you could run at current R&D spend levels before product portfolio competitiveness would materially deteriorate?"
  • Diagnostic note: Long runway answer (>5 years) without evidence = complacency (bear); short runway with active pipeline = honest (bull)

Q3.5 — FX pass-through

  • Question: "Vimian has material USD and EUR revenue exposure — what percentage of your cost base is naturally hedged, and how do you manage residual FX on a rolling basis?"
  • Expected answer: Some natural hedge through USD-denominated input costs vs. USD revenue; management likely has a partial hedging policy
  • Good answer: Can specify hedge ratio, hedge horizon, and the net P&L sensitivity to a 10% EUR/USD move — shows FX as a managed risk, not a surprise
  • Bad answer: "FX is broadly neutral" without quantification — if it were truly neutral the answer would be a number
  • Follow-up probe: "In the FY24 result, what was the actual FX translation impact on EBITDA — both transaction and translation?"
  • Diagnostic note: Inability to separate transaction vs. translation FX = CFO-level disclosure gap (false-friend signal)

6. §4 — THEME 3: Capital Allocation + Balance Sheet

  • VERDICT — capital allocation: The M&A-funded growth strategy creates a structural tension between leverage normalisation and deal-pipeline replenishment; management's actual capital allocation discipline (vs. stated RONIC hurdles) is unverified at DD depth

Q4.1 — RONIC discipline

  • Question: "What is your internal minimum RONIC hurdle for acquisitions — and can you walk me through a deal that you passed on in the last 18 months because it failed the hurdle?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite a hurdle (likely 12–18% RONIC at deal entry); the deal they passed on is the diagnostic element
  • Good answer: Can name a specific deal category or profile they passed on AND articulate the specific financial reason it failed the hurdle — proves the hurdle is real, not aspirational
  • Bad answer: "We are disciplined acquirers" without a specific example of discipline — this answer is always available and reveals nothing
  • Follow-up probe: "Has the RONIC hurdle changed since interest rates moved — and if so, did you re-underwrite any in-portfolio acquisitions?"
  • Diagnostic note: No example of a rejected deal = RONIC hurdle is rhetoric (bear); specific rejected deal + re-underwriting evidence = real discipline (bull)

Q4.2 — Leverage target + balance sheet normalisation

  • Question: "What is your target net leverage range at steady-state — and what is the sequenced path to reaching it if you make no further acquisitions?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely target sub-3x net leverage at steady-state; the timeline to reach it organically (without asset sales) is the diagnostic element
  • Good answer: Specific leverage target with a defined timeline and FCF generation assumptions underlying it — shows CFO has modelled the path, not just stated a preference
  • Bad answer: "We are comfortable with current leverage and see a path to reduction" — non-committal language that leaves the analyst unable to pressure-test the assumption
  • Follow-up probe: "At current FCF generation, how many years to reach your leverage target without further equity issuance — what's the base case?"
  • Diagnostic note: Timeline >4 years at current FCF = either leverage is higher than comfortable, FCF is weaker than guided, or M&A appetite exceeds deleveraging intent (bear)

Q4.3 — M&A philosophy + pipeline

  • Question: "Describe the current M&A pipeline — how many assets are you actively evaluating, what size range, and in which verticals?"
  • Expected answer: Management will be deliberately vague on live pipeline; the diagnostic is whether they describe a healthy deal flow or hint at scarcity
  • Good answer: Can characterise pipeline by deal-count range and size profile without naming targets — "we are actively evaluating 4–6 assets, predominantly in the €20–80m range" — signals active but disciplined
  • Bad answer: "There are always interesting assets for the right price" — generic non-answer; or "the market is frothy, we are patient" — may be true but is also cover for an empty pipeline
  • Follow-up probe: "Has the average asking multiple in your target universe moved materially in the last 12 months — up or down?"
  • Diagnostic note: "Multiples have increased, we are being selective" = honest but potentially problematic for a growth thesis dependent on deal flow (bear); "multiples have come down, we see opportunity" = pipeline re-engagement (bull)

Q4.4 — Working capital management

  • Question: "How has your cash conversion ratio — operating cash flow as a percentage of EBITDA — trended over the last three years, and what are the key working capital movements?"
  • Expected answer: Specialty pharma roll-ups sometimes show cash conversion below 80% due to inventory build and integration-related WC drag; Vimian's integration activity could be the driver
  • Good answer: Can cite specific cash conversion % per year + explain the directional drivers — e.g. "integration-related WC consumed X% in FY23, normalising in FY24" — demonstrates FCF quality awareness
  • Bad answer: "We have strong cash conversion" without a number — the most diagnostic CFO questions always resolve to a number; narrative without numbers is not an answer
  • Follow-up probe: "What is your normalised cash conversion expectation at steady-state — i.e., when integration-related WC drag has fully unwound?"
  • Diagnostic note: Normalised cash conversion <80% = structural FCF leak (bear); normalised >90% = high-quality earnings (bull)

7. §5 — THEME 4: Strategy + Competitive Position

  • VERDICT — strategy/competitive: The specialty animal health vertical focus is a genuine differentiation; the diagnostic question is whether the moat is thickening or thinning as larger competitors (Zoetis, IDEXX, Elanco) allocate more resource to the same specialty niches

Q5.1 — Moat trajectory

  • Question: "Over the last 3 years, has your pricing power in your core proprietary products increased, stayed stable, or declined — and what is driving the trend?"
  • Expected answer: Management will assert increasing pricing power; the diagnostic is the evidence basis and vertical-level variation
  • Good answer: Product-level pricing trend data + explanation of what drives the pricing power (clinical differentiation, formulary lock-in, switching cost) — shows real competitive moat analysis
  • Bad answer: "We have strong brand recognition in our verticals" — brand is not a moat unless it translates to pricing; narrative moat claims without pricing data are not investable signals
  • Follow-up probe: "What would cause you to lose pricing power in dermatology specifically — what does the threat landscape look like in 3 years?"
  • Diagnostic note: Management that can articulate a specific threat and a specific defensive response = strategically self-aware (bull); "we are the leader, we don't see meaningful competition" = dangerous complacency (bear)

Q5.2 — Adjacency expansion + win condition

  • Question: "Which specialty verticals beyond your current four are you most actively considering entering — and what is the capital requirement and timeline?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely cite dentistry, neurology, or oncology as potential adjacencies; the diagnostic is whether they have a concrete plan or an aspirational list
  • Good answer: Specific adjacency with a build/buy/partner decision already taken, a capital envelope, and a timeline to meaningful revenue — shows strategic specificity
  • Bad answer: "We are always looking at new verticals where we can add value" — the non-answer answer; strategic optionality without commitment is not a strategy
  • Follow-up probe: "If you had to rank your adjacency options by expected RONIC, which would be highest and why?"
  • Diagnostic note: Can rank by RONIC = thinks like an operator-allocator (bull); can't rank = strategy is not financially disciplined (bear/false-friend)

Q5.3 — Competitive response / disruption risk

  • Question: "What is the most aggressive competitive move a large animal health company (Zoetis, Elanco, Dechra) has made in your specialty verticals in the last 18 months — and how are you responding?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely reference larger players increasing specialty focus; the diagnostic is whether they treat it as a real threat or dismiss it
  • Good answer: Specific competitor action + specific Vimian response (formulation investment, clinical partnership, pricing adjustment) — proves competitive awareness and response speed
  • Bad answer: "Large players focus on mass market; specialty is not their priority" — this was true 5 years ago; the thesis that large players cede specialty niches is weakening as the specialty premium attracts capital
  • Follow-up probe: "At what revenue scale does a specialty vertical become attractive enough for a Zoetis to acquire or build into — and where are your core verticals relative to that threshold?"
  • Diagnostic note: Awareness that large players set a scale threshold = sophisticated competitive modeling (bull); dismissal of large-player risk = blind spot (bear)

8. §6 — THEME 5: Management Quality + Operator Track Record

  • VERDICT — management quality: CEO/CFO credibility on execution is high relative to public track record; the unverified questions are compensation structure alignment and sandbagging discipline — both are load-bearing at DD

Q6.1 — Sandbagging discipline

  • Question: "Looking at FY22–FY24, how many times did you meet, beat, or miss your initial annual guidance — and where were the misses, if any?"
  • Expected answer: Management will assert strong delivery; the diagnostic is whether they can be specific about misses and their causes
  • Good answer: Honest acknowledgment of any miss + causal explanation that is specific and non-blame-deflecting — "Q2 FY23 organic growth was 200bps below our guide because of X" — builds credibility
  • Bad answer: "We have a strong track record of delivery" without specifics, or inability to recall a guidance miss — either means they haven't missed (verify independently) or are glossing
  • Follow-up probe: "On your current FY25 guidance, what are the two or three assumptions that most worry you — where is the guidance most sensitive to being wrong?"
  • Diagnostic note: Can name 2–3 specific guidance-sensitivity assumptions with directional commentary = self-aware operator (bull); "we feel good about guidance" = sandbagging language that reveals nothing (false-friend)

Q6.2 — Power of incentives / compensation structure

  • Question: "Can you walk me through your personal compensation structure — what percentage is fixed, what metrics drive short-term incentives, and what are the vesting conditions on your equity?"
  • Expected answer: Management may be reluctant to give granular detail beyond public disclosure; the diagnostic is what they reveal voluntarily vs. what they redirect
  • Good answer: Clear articulation of EBITDA and organic revenue growth as STI drivers, with equity vesting tied to absolute share price or RONIC — aligns with long-term shareholder value
  • Bad answer: STI driven by total reported revenue (not organic) or EBITDA-before-integration-costs — these metrics can be gamed via acquisitions without creating shareholder value
  • Follow-up probe: "Does your equity vest faster if you exceed an M&A volume target, or is it purely absolute EBITDA and share price — i.e., is deal count in the incentive structure?"
  • Diagnostic note: Deal count in incentive structure = management incentivised to do deals regardless of quality (structural bear signal); RONIC and organic growth dominate = aligned operator (bull)

Q6.3 — Crisis playbook / communication clarity

  • Question: "Think of the most difficult quarter Vimian has had operationally since listing — what happened, what did you tell investors, and what would you do differently in hindsight?"
  • Expected answer: Management will self-select a manageable crisis; the diagnostic is the candour of the "what would I do differently" response
  • Good answer: Specific quarter + honest self-critique — "we communicated X before we had full information, which created unnecessary volatility" — signals communication maturity
  • Bad answer: "We have navigated challenges well" without a specific example — every management team thinks they have; the question is designed to get a real answer
  • Follow-up probe: "Is there a piece of investor feedback about Vimian's communication quality that has stuck with you — positive or negative?"
  • Diagnostic note: Investor criticism they've internalized = growth mindset operator (bull); defensive response to the critique question = fragile communication culture (bear)

Q6.4 — Hiring posture + culture archetype

  • Question: "How many of your current senior leadership team (top 20) were with Vimian pre-listing — and what is the annual voluntary attrition rate at that level?"
  • Expected answer: Post-IPO management teams at roll-ups often see meaningful leadership churn as the equity-incentive calculus changes; management may not know voluntary attrition by level
  • Good answer: Can cite the pre-listing retention rate + voluntary attrition data + explanation of how they attract talent in a market where large pharma competes for the same profiles
  • Bad answer: "We have a strong team" without retention data — retention is a fact, not a feeling; inability to cite it is a signal
  • Follow-up probe: "Who are the one or two senior leaders most difficult to replace if they left — and what retention mechanisms are in place for them?"
  • Diagnostic note: Key-man concentration acknowledged and mitigated = self-aware management (bull); "the whole team is irreplaceable, no key-man risk" = cannot self-assess (false-friend)

9. §7 — THEME 6: Risk + Downside (Pre-mortem Cross-ref)

  • VERDICT — risk/downside: From Q19 ACH matrix, three highest-plausibility failure modes are: (1) organic growth disappointment masked by acquisition contributions, (2) leverage constraint preventing opportunistic M&A at cycle trough, (3) large-player competitive entry into core specialty niches — questions below are targeted at these three

Q7.1 — Bear-case probe: organic growth disappointment

  • Question: "If your organic growth rate stepped down from current levels to 4–5% in FY26, what would be the most likely cause — and how quickly would you be able to diagnose it vs. report it to investors?"
  • Expected answer: Management may initially resist the scenario; if engaged, they will likely cite macro vet-visit volume softness or pricing pressure in one vertical
  • Good answer: Engages the scenario honestly — names the specific leading indicator that would flag the step-down 6–9 months before it shows in reported numbers — proves they have a monitoring mechanism
  • Bad answer: "We don't see that scenario as realistic" — refusing to engage a downside scenario in a DD-level meeting is itself a red flag
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the KPI you would monitor that would give you earliest warning of that scenario materialising?"
  • Diagnostic note: Can name a specific early-warning KPI = real operational visibility (bull); won't engage downside = managing the narrative (false-friend signal)

Q7.2 — Leverage constraint / failure mode test

  • Question: "In a scenario where your EBITDA fell 15% vs. budget and credit markets tightened simultaneously, what is your covenant headroom and what actions would you take first?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely cite covenant headroom levels and a waterfall of defensive actions (capex cut, M&A pause, working capital release)
  • Good answer: Can cite specific covenant levels + specific covenant headroom at current run-rate + a ranked waterfall of defensive actions that protects the core business — proves stress-test planning
  • Bad answer: "Our balance sheet is robust" without covenant specifics — covenant headroom is a number, not a narrative; any CFO who cannot cite it in a DD meeting is unprepared or avoidant
  • Follow-up probe: "Has the covenants package been tested by the lender group — do you have any waiver history in the last 36 months?"
  • Diagnostic note: Waiver history they didn't volunteer = credibility flag (bear); clean covenant history with specific headroom data = balance sheet transparency (bull)

Q7.3 — Customer churn vector

  • Question: "In specialty animal health, vet-clinic purchasing decisions are often influenced by clinical key opinion leaders (KOLs) — have you ever lost a KOL relationship and seen associated revenue impact? If so, how large?"
  • Expected answer: Management unlikely to have disclosed this publicly; they may deny KOL churn or quantify it as immaterial
  • Good answer: Acknowledges the KOL relationship risk, can characterise the scale of KOL-linked revenue, and can describe the mitigation strategy (KOL engagement programs, clinical education)
  • Bad answer: "We don't rely on individual KOLs; our products stand on their clinical merits" — this is unlikely to be fully true in specialty animal health where clinical influence networks matter
  • Follow-up probe: "What is your annual investment in clinical/KOL engagement — and how do you measure the ROI on that spend?"
  • Diagnostic note: Cannot measure KOL-engagement ROI = potential allocation inefficiency; can measure = disciplined commercial investment (bull)

Q7.4 — Regulatory exposure

  • Question: "What percentage of your proprietary product revenue is subject to regulatory re-approval risk in the next 36 months — and are there any products where re-approval is not assured?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely assert high confidence in re-approval pipeline; the diagnostic is any product where they indicate uncertainty
  • Good answer: Specific percentage of revenue subject to re-approval cycle + the most complex re-approval case they are managing + timeline — shows regulatory pipeline awareness
  • Bad answer: "Our regulatory affairs function is strong, we have a good track record" — the question is about future risk, not past track record; narrative answer on past performance doesn't address the forward question
  • Follow-up probe: "Is there any single product that, if it failed re-approval, would cause >5% group revenue impact?"
  • Diagnostic note: Any "yes" to the follow-up = concentration risk in regulatory pipeline (bear); confident "no" without being able to explain why = unchecked assertion (false-friend)

Q7.5 — False-friend probe: acquisition-accounting opacity

  • Question: "How do you account for acquisition-related costs in your reported EBITDA — and what is the total acquisition-related cost add-back in FY24 as a percentage of adjusted EBITDA?"
  • Expected answer: Vimian, like most roll-ups, adjusts for acquisition costs; the diagnostic is the size of the add-back and whether it has grown as a percentage of adjusted EBITDA
  • Good answer: Specific percentage add-back with a clear policy on what qualifies as acquisition-related — and a stated commitment to the add-back declining as integration completes
  • Bad answer: "These are non-recurring costs that reflect our growth strategy" — "non-recurring" recurring costs are the oldest trick in the roll-up playbook; if acquisition costs are a permanent feature of the strategy, they should be treated as operating costs
  • Follow-up probe: "In 5 years, if Vimian is executing fewer acquisitions per year, what happens to adjusted EBITDA — does it converge toward statutory EBITDA or remain diverged?"
  • Diagnostic note: Convergence answer = honest operator (bull); "we will always have some integration activity" = the add-back is permanent, which means adjusted EBITDA is a permanently misleading metric (bear/false-friend)
  • Sceptical bullet: Q19 ACH-matrix items NOT covered by any question in this memo: (1) Vimian-specific geographic concentration risk (EU vs. US revenue split vulnerability to a US regulatory change) — this is unaddressed and should be added to the live meeting; (2) key-supplier concentration for API inputs has not been explicitly probed

10. §8 — CROSS-CUTTING META + NON-OBVIOUS QUESTIONS

  • VERDICT — non-obvious: The most load-bearing unasked questions sit at the intersection of management self-awareness and the incentive architecture — questions management would rather not be asked, which is precisely why they must be

Non-obvious angle probes

  • Non-obvious angle 1 (WH Smith probe): "What is the most surprising piece of customer feedback — from vets or vet clinics — that Vimian received in the last 12 months, that you didn't expect?"
  • Why non-obvious: Forces management out of scripted messaging; the surprise element reveals what customers think vs. what management wants investors to think; expect a defensive pivot to positive feedback — probe the negative directly if they dodge

  • Diagnostic note: "Nothing surprising, we know our customers well" = either extraordinary customer intimacy or no real listening mechanism; "we were surprised that X% of vets in a key market weren't aware of product Y" = honest vulnerability disclosure (bull signal on communication candour)

  • Non-obvious angle 2 (edge-detection probe): ⚡ "If you had to identify the metric that, if it moved against you by 20%, would most threaten the investment thesis — and tell me what that metric is showing right now, in real time — what would it be?"

  • Multiple-truths probe: "I accept that Vimian has a strong organic growth story AND that the acquisition strategy has added significant value. AND YET — what is the version of the Vimian story where both of those things are true but the investment still disappoints? Walk me through that scenario."
