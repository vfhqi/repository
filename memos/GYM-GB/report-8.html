Market Size, Growth, and Penetration

The UK health and ﬁtness market has demonstrated consistent structural growth, reaching a total market size of
£6.5 billion in 2025. Total gym membership in the UK stands at an estimated 11.3 million individuals, reﬂecting
a compound annual growth rate of 3.1% since 2012. Overall market penetration increased signiﬁcantly to 16.6%
of the population in 2025, up from 15.9% the previous year. The low-cost ﬁtness segment remains the primary
engine of this industry expansion, capturing signiﬁcant market share from traditional mid-market operators.
This value segment is highly consolidated at the top, with the two leading operators accounting for 81% of all low-
cost members. Despite this rapid historical rollout, a recent market study indicates the UK has the potential to
support an additional 600 to 850 low-cost gyms, pointing to a decade of further growth runway.

Structural Barriers to Entry and Exit

Capital Expenditure Requirements The initial capital outlay required to build a modern low-cost gym represents a
formidable barrier to new entrants. Industry standards dictate that a typical value gym ﬁt-out in the UK costs
between £70 and £90 per square foot. For a standard 15,000 to 20,000 square foot facility, this translates to
an upfront investment of approximately £1.27 million per site. Because these operators rely on low monthly
yields, they require high membership volumes to break even, meaning any miscalculation in site selection can
result in stranded capital.

Scale Economics and Procurement Power Incumbents beneﬁt from a massive purchasing advantage that cannot
be easily replicated by smaller chains. Basic-Fit, as the largest purchaser of ﬁtness equipment in Europe,
leverages its volume to negotiate heavily discounted hardware, extended warranties, and rapid replacement
windows. This scale extends to marketing eﬃciency and back-oﬃce operations, where the cost of developing
centralized mobile applications, AI customer service chatbots, and remote monitoring systems is amortized
across thousands of clubs.

Property Availability and Landlord Negotiations Securing appropriate real estate is increasingly diﬃcult, as
optimal gym locations require speciﬁc zoning, heavy ﬂoor-load capacities, and signiﬁcant square footage in
densely populated catchments. Market leaders hold a distinct advantage here, as their strong balance sheets
and proven covenant strength make them the preferred tenants for landlords looking to ﬁll large, vacant retail park
spaces. Furthermore, established players can utilize advanced multi-variance statistical models to accurately
predict local membership headroom before signing a lease, signiﬁcantly de-risking their property pipeline
compared to smaller competitors.

Technology Infrastructure The modern budget gym is fundamentally a technology business, relying heavily on
custom-built digital ecosystems to manage access, billing, and member engagement without on-site staff.
Operators utilize sophisticated remote camera systems, automated turnstiles, and self-service kiosks to maintain
24/7 operations safely and eﬃciently. Building and maintaining this proprietary software infrastructure
requires millions in ongoing tech capital expenditure, creating a technological moat that prevents independent
gyms from proﬁtably replicating the low-labor, high-margin model.

1. Industry Overview

The UK fitness market generated revenue of approximately £5.7bn in 2024. Low-price gym membership accounted for roughly 41% of all private-sector members, with approximately 806 low-cost clubs operating. The Gym Group and PureGym are the two dominant operators; Anytime Fitness (177 sites) and JD Gyms (~80 sites) are secondary players.

UK market penetration is approximately 15-16% of the adult population — significantly higher than continental Europe. The low-cost segment benefits from structural price migration: members leaving mid-market (Nuffield, Virgin Active) for lower-cost alternatives, and first-time gym-goers entering at the value end.

UK landlord appetite for gym operators is strong and growing. Gyms anchor footfall in retail parks and generate long-term income; The Gym Group has specifically benefited from this dynamic as retail parks restructure away from fashion retail.

For the full European market context, see the BFIT-NL Q8 memo Section 1.

2. Value Chain & Economics

The value chain is identical in structure to the European low-cost model. UK-specific notes:

  • Landlords: Retail park landlords actively court low-cost gym operators. This gives The Gym Group and PureGym negotiating leverage on rent and fit-out contributions.
  • Energy: UK energy costs were a 2022-23 headwind; stabilised in 2024. The Gym Group has targeted energy efficiency improvements, including solar installation on some OOT sites.
  • Staff: The Gym Group retains more staff per club than Basic-Fit's model. It has 245 sites but runs some workforce-dependent locations (13 identified in 2024 that drag aggregate ROIC). Removing these brings ROIC to 27%.
  • Technology: Less proprietary than Basic-Fit; the app and access systems are key but The Gym Group has invested in data and digital platform meaningfully in 2024.

3. Competitive Landscape

UK low-cost tier:
- PureGym: 422 UK clubs, ~2m members total (UK + international). The UK market leader. Backed by Leonard Green & Partners. Revenue approximately £600m (2024 estimate). Aggressive expansion pace (~80 new sites/year vs The Gym Group's 12 in 2024).
- The Gym Group: 245 UK clubs, ~906k avg members (2024). £226m revenue.
- JD Gyms: 80+ sites, budget/mid positioning, backed by JD Sports.
- Anytime Fitness: 177+ franchised sites UK. Different model (franchised, 24/7, no classes).

Mid-market (indirect competition):
- Nuffield Health, Virgin Active, David Lloyd: losing members to low-cost. Structural share donors.
- Bannatyne Group: regional, private, 60+ sites.

Competition intensity: Duopoly dynamic in low-cost UK. PureGym and The Gym Group dominate the operator and landlord landscape. A new entrant at scale would need billions in capital and years of real estate negotiation — this is not happening.

Direction of change: Gradual market-share accretion to the two leaders from independents and mid-market. PureGym growing faster than The Gym Group in site count; The Gym Group growing faster on ARPM.

4. Barriers to Entry & Exit

UK-specific notes (structural drivers identical to European market):

  • Real estate: Planning for large gyms in UK retail parks involves significant lead times (12-24 months from site identification to opening). This creates a natural pipeline advantage for established operators.
  • Brand: In the UK, PureGym and The Gym Group have high consumer awareness in the low-cost segment. A new entrant would face a cold-start marketing problem in markets where both leaders operate.
  • Technology: The Gym Group's app and access system have evolved over several product cycles. Replication would take 2-3 years for a new operator.
  • Exit barriers: Long leases, equipment depreciation, and redundancy costs (more staff-dependent than Basic-Fit). Selling a UK low-cost gym estate would require finding a PE buyer or a trade acquirer. Liquidity exists but exit is not frictionless.

- Membership: predominantly £20-22/month, recurring direct debit. ARPM £20.81 in 2024.

- Secondary revenue: short-term day-pass/flex membership, personal training affiliate, vending.

Geographic footprint: UK-only. This is both a strength (deep UK market knowledge, established landlord network) and a structural constraint (no international diversification, fully correlated to UK consumer).

Cost structure:
- Rent: largest fixed cost. Long-term leases, 10-15 years typical.
- Staff: more than Basic-Fit — some sites staffed more heavily, reflecting planning conditions or site type.
- Energy: significant, targeted for efficiency improvement.
- Central overhead: technology, marketing, management.

- Strong real estate relationships (particularly OOT landlords)
- Clean balance sheet vs European peers
- Demonstrated ARPM pricing power (7% in 2024)
- #2 position in a consolidating duopoly market

Structural competitive disadvantages:
- UK-only: no geographic diversification
- Material scale gap vs PureGym (~422 vs 245 clubs)
- Slower new site rollout than market leader
- No premium tier / group classes (pure no-frills; less ARPM upside than mid-market adjacent operators)

7. Business Model Mechanics

Revenue drivers:
1. New site additions (net 12 in 2024; expected acceleration)
2. Fill rate of existing clubs (members/club)
3. ARPM (7% growth in 2024 — the highest driver of incremental revenue in 2024)

Cost structure: Rent is fixed; staff is semi-variable (peak hours staffing); energy is variable but partially hedged; corporate overhead is largely fixed.

Capital intensity: Each new site requires £1.2-1.5m of upfit capex (varies by landlord contribution). Maintenance capex ~4-6% of revenue. Total CapEx in H1 2025: £51.2m (£17.3m maintenance, £33.9m expansionary) — this is a material commitment relative to a £226m revenue base, reflecting planned acceleration.

NWC dynamics: Structurally negative/cash-generative. Members pay in advance, suppliers paid in arrears. FCF conversion is high at maturity.

Cash conversion: FCF generation increased to £37.5m in 2024 (2023: £27m). This is real, tangible FCF from a growing, maturing estate.

Operating leverage: Strong. EBITDA grew 24% on 11% revenue growth in 2024. Each additional member above club-level breakeven flows through at near-100% incremental margin. Fixed cost base means revenue growth is a force multiplier on EBITDA.

What breaks the model:
- Consumer income shock causing membership cancellation at scale
- Rent reset on lease renewals at materially higher rates (currently benign — landlords need gym operators)
- PureGym accelerating growth to 600+ clubs, tightening the prime real estate pipeline
- Energy cost structural step-change without pass-through mechanism

8. Key Success Factors

Pricing power is the most underappreciated lever. The 2024 ARPM growth of 7% was achieved without membership decline. Sustainable annual ARPM growth of 3-5% would transform the earnings profile over 5 years. The risk: if PureGym does not follow on price, The Gym Group faces a price-gap problem at the margin.

Site rollout pace matters. At 12 net sites/year the estate reaches 290 clubs by 2028. At 30 net sites/year it reaches 360. Scale economics (overhead absorption, marketing, equipment) compound with site count. Acceleration is the single largest lever management can pull.

Out-of-town strategy is proving prescient. OOT locations offer larger footprints, lower rent per sq ft, easier planning, and landlord fit-out contributions. The Gym Group's 60 OOT sites already outperform.

Sustainable margin level: EBITDA margin (less rent) of 20-25% at scale for a well-run UK low-cost operator. Gym Group at 21% is at the low end of achievable but growing fast.

9. Industry Evolution

UK-specific:
- The proportion of private-sector members in low-cost clubs has risen from 35% to 41% in 3 years. This structural shift is not reversing.
- Landlord appetite for gyms as retail park anchors continues. Out-of-town as a format will grow further.
- Mid-market operators (Nuffield, Virgin Active) are ceding share. They cannot compete on price without destroying their cost model.
- Digital fitness: threat is limited and empirically demonstrated to be complementary.

Consolidation: No credible consolidation between PureGym and The Gym Group — size and competition concerns would attract CMA review. More likely: The Gym Group acquires regional operators as they fail.

Digital evolution: The Gym Group is investing in data, app, and flex membership products. A tiered digital offering (Basic-Fit model) would be a meaningful ARPM catalyst if executed.

Pricing power: Moving to operators. The Gym Group's 7% ARPM increase in 2024 is evidence. The question is sustainability — can this be repeated in 2025 and 2026 without PureGym undercutting?

10. Sceptical View

Bull case: 25-27% ROIC on mature sites justifies aggressive expansion.
Bear case: ROIC includes a selection bias — 13 underperforming workforce-dependent sites are explicitly excluded to reach 27%. The total estate ROIC is 25%. New sites face competition for prime OOT locations from PureGym, JD Gyms, and leisure operators. Pipeline quality may deteriorate as the best locations are taken.

Bull case: 7% ARPM growth in 2024 proves sustainable pricing power.
Bear case: One year is not a trend. PureGym explicitly targets a sub-The-Gym-Group price point in several markets. If PureGym holds pricing lower and The Gym Group raises, The Gym Group risks losing price-sensitive members in dual-market locations. Elasticity is not zero.

Bull case: Out-of-town strategy is a durable competitive advantage.
Bear case: PureGym is now the most acquisitive operator in the retail warehouse market (25 new openings in 2024, 32 in 2025). The OOT advantage is eroding. The Gym Group is getting crowded out by a better-capitalised rival.

Bull case: UK low-cost market will double by 2026 (PwC/Gym Group joint analysis).
Bear case: This projection was made in a more benign UK consumer environment. UK real wages have faced sustained pressure; gym membership churn tends to spike in economic downturns. The penetration growth narrative assumes consumer spending ability that may not materialise uniformly.

Bull case: Clean balance sheet allows acquisitive growth.
Bear case: Clean balance sheet is an opportunity only if management has the ambition and capability to deploy capital fast. The pace of 12 net sites in 2024 is below what the opportunity demands. If management does not accelerate meaningfully in 2025-26, the opportunity cost of a clean balance sheet compounds.

Structural competitive position: YELLOW. The Gym Group operates a sound model in a growing UK market with genuine pricing power and strong real estate relationships. The constraints are real: UK-only exposure, a material scale gap to PureGym, and a pace of expansion that risks ceding the growth window to the market leader. A strong execution year (30+ new sites, sustained ARPM growth) would upgrade this to Orange-trending-Green.
