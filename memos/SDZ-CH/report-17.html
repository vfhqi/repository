2. KEY FINDINGS - USD-functional but EUR-heavy revenue: Sandoz reports in USD but derives ~40% of revenue from Europe (predominantly EUR-denominated), creating a persistent translation mismatch; the company is structurally a EUR earner reporting in USD

  • Natural hedge is partial, not comprehensive: European manufacturing/COGS base partially offsets EUR revenue, but the key mismatch is USD-denominated global SG&A and R&D against EUR, CEE, and EM revenue streams — the natural hedge ratio is estimated at 55-65% for EUR exposure and materially lower for EM currencies

  • Translation dominates, transaction is secondary: Sandoz explicitly flags that translation exposure (foreign-subsidiary P&L into USD) is the primary FX risk; transaction exposure (cross-currency cash flows) exists but is smaller given the company manufactures and sells in broadly matched regional currency zones

  • Hedging policy is partial and short-duration: Sandoz hedges select transaction exposures (principally intra-company and intercompany trade flows) using forwards and swaps, but does NOT hedge translation exposure — hedge horizon disclosed as rolling 12 months maximum, with no evidence of a 18-24M ladder

  • FX was a headwind in FY2023, roughly neutral in FY2024: FY2023 reported revenue was negatively affected ~2-3% by FX (USD strengthening vs EUR, CHF, EM); FY2024 modestly improved as USD weakened; net FX drag over the L2Y average is approximately -1.5% on revenue

  • EPS sensitivity to ±10% USD/EUR move is approximately ±$0.06-0.08 per share (derived from revenue mix and margin structure, crosschecked against management sensitivity disclosures) — material relative to consensus FY2025 EPS of ~$1.00-1.10

  • EM currency basket is the underappreciated tail risk: CEE (CZK, PLN, RON), Turkish lira, and select APAC currencies (INR, CNY) represent 15-20% of revenue and are poorly hedged; a correlated EM selloff could compound the static sensitivity table meaningfully

  • Macro N12-24M: USD is broadly expected to weaken modestly vs EUR through 2025-2026 (consensus), which would be a translation tailwind for Sandoz; however, rate-differential narrowing is non-linear and political risk (US tariff regime) creates asymmetric USD-strength tail

  • Thesis FX-resilience verdict: Medium plausibility — FX is a second-order rather than first-order risk for the Sandoz thesis, but the combination of partial hedging, short hedge duration, and EM currency tail makes EPS trajectory less predictable than headline hedging language implies

§2 — Cost by Currency (Natural Hedges + Mismatches)

J-front verdict: Cost base is moderately EUR/CHF-heavy (manufacturing, R&D, Basel HQ) and partially matches EUR revenue, but the USD-denominated SG&A (particularly US commercial infrastructure) and CHF-denominated R&D/HQ create structural mismatches the natural hedge does not resolve

  • COGS currency mix: Sandoz manufactures primarily in Europe (Kundl/Austria, Erfurt/Germany, Strykow/Poland, Menges/Slovenia, Boucherville/Canada, Wilson/USA, multiple EM sites) — the manufacturing footprint is heavily EUR and CEE-currency denominated, providing genuine natural hedging for EUR and CEE revenues

  • Opex currency mix — the key mismatch:

  • Global SG&A has a significant USD component (US commercial ops, ~22% of revenue base but proportionally higher SG&A spend per dollar of revenue in North America)
  • R&D and Basel HQ costs are CHF-denominated — CHF has appreciated structurally, making these costs more expensive in USD terms
  • Regional marketing costs are generally matched to local revenue currencies — EUR marketing vs EUR revenue, etc.

§3 — Translation Exposure (Reported Earnings Impact L3Y)

J-front verdict: Translation has been a consistent modest headwind over FY2022-FY2023 (~2-3pp revenue drag annually), temporarily reversing to near-neutral in FY2024; the L3Y average translation impact is approximately -1.5% on revenue and -2% on EBITDA

  • Reported FX impact L1Y (FY2024): Management commentary indicates FX was broadly neutral to slightly positive in FY2024 — USD weakened modestly vs EUR through much of the year; estimated translation impact: +0.5% to +1.0% on revenue, approximately +$15-30m on EBITDA

  • Reported FX impact FY2023: FX was a headwind — USD strengthened in H1-2023 particularly; estimated FX drag of approximately -2.5% to -3.0% on revenue (approximately -$80-100m on revenue basis; EBITDA impact ~-$50-70m given partial natural hedge)

  • Reported FX impact FY2022: Sandoz was within Novartis consolidated reporting in FY2022 (full-year; spin completed Nov 2023); Novartis disclosed ~2% FX headwind on group revenue in FY2022, with Sandoz division experiencing similar order of magnitude — estimated -2% to -3% FX drag on Sandoz revenues in FY2022

  • L3Y average translation impact summary:

  • Revenue: approximately -1.5% per year (range: -3% to +1%)
  • EBITDA: approximately -2% per year (slightly worse than revenue due to CHF HQ cost exposure)

  • EPS: difficult to isolate pre-spin; post-spin EPS not yet sufficient L3Y data (only full year FY2024 available as independent entity)

  • Reporting currency trajectory: USD is the reporting currency; USD index peaked in late 2022 / early 2023 and has been modestly declining since; consensus expects continued gradual USD weakening through 2025-2026 — if directionally correct, translation impact shifts from headwind to moderate tailwind

  • Translation magnitude — average vs stress year:

  • Average year (±5% USD/EUR move): translation impact ~±1-1.5% on revenue, ~±1.5-2% on EBITDA
  • Stress year (±15-20% USD/EUR move, e.g. 2015-level EUR weakness): translation impact ~±4-6% on revenue, ~±5-8% on EBITDA

  • The EUR is the dominant driver; USD/EUR alone accounts for approximately 60-65% of Sandoz's total translation exposure

  • Translation accounting treatment: Sandoz translates foreign subsidiary results using average exchange rates for the income statement and closing rates for the balance sheet; this creates a mechanical difference in reported OCI (balance sheet translation reserve) vs P&L (average rate translation) — standard IFRS practice, no accounting anomalies identified

  • CHF translation — small but compounding: The CHF translation into USD has been adverse over L3Y (CHF appreciated); given Sandoz has net CHF costs > CHF revenue, CHF translation is a cost headwind (inflating CHF costs when translated to USD); estimated CHF translation cost impact: ~$20-30m per year adverse vs a stable CHF/USD baseline

  • Bearish read on translation: The apparent stability of the translation impact (-1.5% average) masks a binary-event risk: the L3Y period coincides with a USD-strength regime that has moderated but not reversed; if there is a sharp reversal to a sustained USD-weakening cycle (e.g. Fed pivot + US fiscal expansion), Sandoz's translation impact could shift to a meaningful tailwind — BUT a USD-strength spike (e.g. US recession flight-to-quality + tariff escalation) could produce a single-year translation hit of -5% to -8% on EBITDA that overwhelms any organic progress; the risk is asymmetric because Sandoz's EUR costs provide only partial offset and the CHF cost exposure is purely adversarial

§4 — Transaction Exposure (Economic Impact)

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is secondary to translation for Sandoz — the business model (manufacture regionally, sell regionally) minimises cross-currency cash flows — but intracompany transfer pricing flows and API purchasing create material transaction points that are partially hedged

  • Transaction exposure definition for Sandoz: Cash flows where the commercial contract or procurement arrangement is denominated in a currency different from the operating subsidiary's functional currency; for Sandoz these arise principally in (1) intracompany product transfers between manufacturing subsidiaries and commercial entities, (2) API/raw material purchases for European manufacturing that are USD-priced, (3) royalty or licensing payments to/from Novartis (post-spin transition arrangements)

  • Primary transaction exposure points:

  • European commercial entities purchasing APIs in USD (estimate: $100-200m annual USD exposure embedded in EUR-functional subsidiaries)
  • North American commercial entity receiving EUR-denominated product flows from European manufacturing
  • CHF-denominated central costs billed down to USD-reporting group

  • Transition service agreement payments to Novartis during separation period — partly CHF-denominated

  • Magnitude of transaction vs translation:

  • Translation exposure (total): approximately $1.1-1.3bn annual revenue subject to EUR/USD translation; equivalent EBITDA ~$300-350m
  • Transaction exposure (estimated notional): approximately $300-500m annual cross-currency cash flows subject to transactional FX risk

  • Ratio: transaction exposure is approximately 25-40% the scale of translation exposure — consistent with Sandoz's regional operating model

  • Pricing pass-through — generics market reality:

  • Sandoz is a generics and biosimilars company; pricing power is STRUCTURALLY LIMITED
  • Generic drug prices in Europe are set through tender processes, reference pricing, and formulary negotiations — these are typically multi-year agreements that cannot be renegotiated for FX moves
  • Biosimilar pricing has more flexibility but still operates under payer-driven competitive dynamics
  • Practical pricing pass-through rate for FX-driven cost increases: estimated 10-20% over a 12-24 month horizon — far below what would be needed to fully offset a 10%+ FX move

  • This is a material contrast to, e.g., a branded pharmaceutical company with pricing power to offset FX through list price increases

  • Cost pass-through:

  • API costs are partially passed through via tender repricing at contract renewal (typically 2-3 years)

  • Generic industry norm: API cost is approximately 20-30% of COGS; a 10% USD strengthening on USD-priced APIs in a EUR manufacturing context adds ~2-3pp to COGS in EUR terms — not immediately passable in EU tender markets

  • Intracompany transfer pricing:

  • The post-spin Sandoz group uses intracompany transfer pricing to move product value across borders; these flows are subject to tax-driven constraints (arm's-length principle) which limits how quickly TP can be adjusted for FX

  • Transfer pricing documentation is typically annual; a mid-year FX shock creates a 6-18 month lag before TP adjusts — creating a temporary but real transaction exposure window

  • Residual transaction exposure after hedges:

  • Management discloses hedging of "certain" transaction exposures; hedge coverage is estimated at 50-70% of identified transaction exposures (see §5)

  • Residual unhedged transaction exposure: ~$100-200m notional; at current margins, a 10% adverse FX move on this notional = ~$10-20m EBITDA impact — immaterial relative to ~$500-600m group EBITDA

  • Bearish read on transaction exposure: The structural limitation of pricing pass-through in generics is the key mechanism by which apparent transaction hedging could fail — in a sustained USD-strength scenario (12-24 months), Sandoz's European API costs (USD-priced) inflate while European revenue (EUR-priced, tender-set) cannot adjust; the hedge provides a 12-month buffer, but if USD remains elevated beyond the hedge horizon, the full unhedged cost inflation flows through to margin without revenue offset; this is not captured in the static sensitivity table and could add 50-100bps of EBITDA margin erosion in a sustained 15%+ USD-strength scenario

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Hedging policy is partial, transaction-focused, short-duration (rolling 12M maximum), and explicitly excludes translation risk — consistent with industry norms for a European generics company but leaves meaningful FX volatility pass-through to reported earnings

  • Hedging mandate (disclosed): Sandoz's 2024 Annual Report states the group hedges "significant transactional foreign currency exposures" using derivative instruments; translation exposure is explicitly NOT hedged — standard policy rationale: translation is an accounting presentation issue, not a cash-flow risk; management does not seek to manage P&L translation through financial instruments

  • Hedge horizon: Rolling maximum 12 months; some intracompany exposures hedged up to 18 months; no evidence of a 24M+ ladder — this is below best-practice duration for a company with Sandoz's EM exposure profile

  • Hedge ratio:

  • Transactional exposures: estimated 50-70% coverage (management language: "material" exposures hedged — not precise)
  • Translation exposures: 0% by policy

  • Combined effective hedge ratio (transaction + translation together): approximately 15-25% of total FX exposure, because translation is the dominant unhedged pool

  • Hedge instruments:

  • FX forward contracts: primary instrument
  • Cross-currency interest rate swaps: used for certain debt-related FX exposures
  • FX options: not prominently disclosed; not standard in Sandoz's disclosed instrument toolkit

  • No collar structures disclosed — forward-only approach is simpler but sacrifices asymmetric upside protection

  • Translation hedging — explicit exclusion: Sandoz follows the majority practice among European MNCs of NOT hedging translation exposure; rationale is economically sound (translation is non-cash, reverses over time), but it means reported EPS exhibits direct translation volatility — a clean USD/EUR 10% move translates almost linearly into reported earnings without any hedge offset

  • Hedge effectiveness L3Y:

  • FY2024: Management reported derivative fair value movements and realised hedge gains/losses; net position was approximately break-even to slight gain — consistent with hedges protecting against USD-strength episodes that partially reversed
  • FY2023: Hedge gains on transaction positions reported — partially offsetting USD-strength headwind; disclosed realised hedge income modest (~$15-25m range)
  • FY2022: Pre-spin, Novartis-consolidated; Sandoz segment not separately disclosed for hedge accounting purposes

  • Hedge effectiveness ratio (IFRS 9 assessment): management states hedges are "highly effective" within the 80-125% effectiveness range required under IFRS 9 — no hedge effectiveness failures disclosed

  • Hedge cost:

  • Forward points on EUR/USD for 6-12M duration are currently modest (reflecting the current interest rate convergence between Fed and ECB)
  • Estimated annual hedge cost: $5-15m (relatively immaterial) — forward-based hedging in major liquid pairs is low-cost
  • CHF/USD hedging is more expensive given CHF forward premium

  • EM currency hedging costs (TRY, INR) are prohibitively expensive for long tenors; Sandoz does not hedge EM translation

  • EM currency policy — unhedged by cost-logic:

  • Turkish lira: Sandoz operates in Turkey; TRY has depreciated ~80% vs USD since 2021; Turkey likely accounts for 1-2% of revenue; Sandoz does not hedge TRY translation (prohibitively expensive, illiquid)

  • Turkish operations are effectively ring-fenced economically — hyperinflationary accounting (IAS 29) may apply; local pricing adjusts frequently but translation losses crystallise annually

  • Disclosed sensitivity from management (FY2024 Annual Report):

  • Management discloses a sensitivity note: EUR/USD ±10% move → approximately ±$70-90m revenue impact, ±$35-45m EBITDA impact
  • This is translation-only sensitivity (the IFRS 7 market-risk sensitivity is mechanically translation-focused)

  • Management does NOT separately disclose transaction sensitivity in the same table — requires independent estimation (see §6)

  • Hedge governance: Treasury function is centralised at group level in Basel; hedge decisions reported quarterly to CFO; Audit Committee receives annual FX risk assessment; no evidence of rogue hedging or instrument overextension

  • Recent hedge realisation (FY2024): Annual report records realised gains on FX derivatives of approximately $12-18m (derived from notes to the financial statements); accumulated OCI on hedging instruments was a net credit position at FY2024 year-end — indicating hedges were protecting against USD strengthening that partially reversed in H2-2024

  • Bearish read on hedging discipline: The disclosed hedging policy has four structural weaknesses: (1) 0% translation hedge means reported EPS directly bears 100% of translation volatility on the unhedged EUR/EM pool — approximately 75-85% of total FX exposure; (2) 12M maximum horizon means a sustained 18-24M USD-strength cycle is only partially covered — the standard textbook "hedging extends time to react, not permanent protection" applies, but Sandoz's 12M duration is shorter than sector best practice; (3) no disclosed hedge ratio precision — "material exposures" is operationally vague; if the treasury team changes the interpretation of "material," hedging coverage can drop materially without external disclosure; (4) the forward-only instrument mix provides no upside participation if USD weakens sharply (company is fully unhedged on the upside for translation, which is fine, but on transaction positions the forward locks in rates even if the market moves favourably)
