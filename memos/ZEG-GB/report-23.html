1. DEFINITION

The 2025 US tariff escalation represents the most aggressive protectionist policy shift since the Smoot-Hawley era, with effective US tariff rates moving from ~2.5% (2024 average) toward 20-25% blended across trading partners, with China-facing rates exceeding 100%. The thematic encompasses both the direct tariff mechanics and the broader deglobalisation shock: supply chain restructuring, trade diversion, currency realignment, and a reordering of comparative advantage.

Primary transmission to European corporate earnings: Three channels operate simultaneously. First, direct: European exporters with material US revenue face margin compression as tariffs either absorb into their P&L or reduce unit volumes. Second, indirect: Chinese goods diverted from the US seek European market outlets, compressing pricing for European manufacturers competing domestically. Third, financial: USD weakness (triggered by US fiscal credibility concerns) creates a structural EUR/USD headwind that translates at approximately 2-4% EPS impact per 10% dollar depreciation for exposed exporters.

Time horizon: 3-5 years. The tariff architecture, once institutionalised through executive order and lobbying capture by protected US industries, is politically sticky regardless of administration change.

Confidence in persistence: High. Trade policy reversal requires Congressional action or a successor administration with explicit free-trade mandate — neither is the 2025-2028 base case.

2. BENEFICIARY SUMMARY

European companies generating the majority of revenues domestically — particularly those in regulated sectors, defence, infrastructure, and import-substitution positions — benefit from the combination of relative earnings predictability, accelerating government spending tailwinds, and a market that re-rates stability at a premium during global uncertainty regimes. The beneficiary is not just "safe" — it actively gains from the reordering.

3. AT-RISK SUMMARY

European exporters with concentrated US revenue exposure, globally integrated manufacturing footprints, and limited ability to reprice face a compound threat: tariff walls compressing margins, Chinese goods diversion undermining domestic pricing, and USD weakness eroding translated earnings. Luxury goods, automotive, speciality chemicals, and capital equipment are the primary casualty sectors.

5. BENEFICIARY PROBABLE SETUPS

Ideal Entry Profile

Valuation: Beneficiary stocks in the regulated/domestic cluster are not cheap on headline multiples (utilities at 15-18x forward EPS is typical). The setup is not a value re-rating — it is an earnings stability premium in a world where growth earnings are being cut. Buy at modest premium to historical average, not at discount.

Momentum: Look for earnings estimate revision stabilisation (not upgrade) as the signal. In uncertainty regimes, a company that holds its EPS estimate while the market cuts peers' is already outperforming on a risk-adjusted basis.

Earnings trajectory: The most powerful beneficiary setup is a company that can guide to 5-8% EPS CAGR with high confidence for 3 years. In a market cutting growth estimates, this looks like 15-20% EPS outperformance vs. the index by year 2.

Catalysts That Crystallise the Thesis

  • NATO/EU defence budget announcements (each increment above 2% GDP is a volume catalyst for defence primes)
  • Anti-dumping duties on Chinese goods entering the EU (pricing floor restoration for European manufacturers)
  • EUR/USD move beyond 1.15 (triggers visible EPS upgrades for USD-cost beneficiaries; watch airline sector)
  • Earnings guidance confirmation at H1 results — companies reaffirming full-year guidance while peers cut stand out sharply
  • Dividend increases from regulated utilities (signals management confidence; re-rating catalyst for income rotation)

Risks Even for Beneficiaries

  • Overvaluation risk: Defensive re-rating can run ahead of earnings by 6-12 months. UK utilities reached 20x+ forward PE in 2022 before correcting sharply when rates rose. Monitor P/E vs. bond yield spread — if the spread compresses below 200bps, the premium is exhausted
  • Regulatory reset risk: Regulated utilities face periodic price control reviews (UK water: PR24 ongoing). An adverse regulatory outcome eliminates the thematic tailwind regardless of macro
  • Crowding: If the consensus rotates hard into European defensives, the alpha opportunity compresses. Watch positioning data and relative performance vs. MSCI Europe Defensives Index
  • Second-order demand destruction: A deep European recession (tail risk from trade war escalation) hurts even domestic companies through volume decline — particularly relevant for housebuilders and consumer-facing services

Setup Mapping

  • Demand-driven earnings acceleration: Defence primes (Rheinmetall, BAE) — order book converts to revenue with 18-36 month lag; EPS upgrades are sequential and predictable
  • Margin expansion from input cost advantage: Airlines (fuel cost relief from USD weakness) — short-duration catalyst, 6-12 months
  • Re-rating from defensive premium: Regulated utilities — multiple expansion thesis; works in rising uncertainty, reverses if rates spike again
  • Import substitution margin floor restoration: European industrials in steel/chemicals/building materials — medium-term, 12-24 months, requires anti-dumping enforcement confirmation

7. AT-RISK PROBABLE SETUPS

Typical At-Risk Stock Profile at Entry

Earnings vulnerability: The consensus model still assumes 8-12% EPS growth for FY2025 built on US revenue growth assumptions that are now structurally impaired. The risk is not a 1-year miss — it is a multi-year EPS trajectory reset. The stock is priced for the old growth vector.

Valuation assumption: Many at-risk names trade at 18-25x forward PE, justified by historical US growth rates. A 10-15% EPS cut with a turn of multiple compression produces 25-35% drawdown from peak — the compound risk is severe.

Balance sheet stress: Less acute in 2025 for investment-grade names, but leveraged industrials (Forvia: ND/EBITDA >3x) face refinancing pressure if earnings deteriorate.

Triggers That Crystallise the At-Risk Thesis

  1. Q1/Q2 earnings misses with US-specific commentary — management flagging US volume softness or customer destocking is the first public signal; stock typically down 10-15% on the day
  2. FY guidance cuts — companies that guided optimistically into 2025 and now face explicit US tariff headwinds; Burberry, Diageo, and Rentokil are the names to watch most closely
  3. EUR/USD move above 1.15 — triggers visible EPS downgrade cycle for USD-revenue names; EUR strength amplifies the volume problem with a translation problem
  4. Chinese anti-dumping tariff removal or failure — if EU does not enforce trade defences against Chinese goods diversion, European manufacturers in at-risk sectors face a pricing floor collapse
  5. Automotive OEM production cuts — Ford/GM announcing US production cuts flows through to European tier-1 supplier order books within 60-90 days; Valeo, Continental, Forvia see order intake decline precede P&L impact by one quarter

Temporary vs. Structural At-Risk: The Critical Distinction

Temporarily at-risk (can recover in 12-24 months):
- Companies with high US exposure but pricing power sufficient to pass tariff costs through to US consumers (luxury goods with inelastic demand at the ultra-high end — Richemont more than Burberry)
- Companies actively restructuring US supply chains (building US manufacturing capacity within 18-24 months eliminates the tariff exposure)
- Businesses where the US tariff is offset by a margin benefit elsewhere (e.g., USD input cost reduction for companies with USD raw material procurement)

Structurally at-risk (3-5 year earnings impairment):
- Companies where US growth was the valuation argument and no credible alternative growth vector exists (Rentokil post-Terminix — North America is >50% of the business; there is no pivoting away from this)
- Automotive supply chain participants where Chinese EV displacement AND US tariffs operate simultaneously on a sector under structural transition
- European luxury brands dependent on aspirational US consumers where tariff-driven price increases collide with a softening US consumer — Burberry is the clearest example, already in operational distress pre-tariff
- Companies with USD revenue and EUR fixed costs and no ability to manufacture locally in the US within the relevant investment horizon

Setup Mapping

  • Earnings deterioration: Automotive tier-1 suppliers — thin margins, high fixed costs, order book decline precedes P&L by 1-2 quarters; exit before the miss, not after
  • Multiple compression: US-growth-dependent names (Rentokil) — the re-rating from "growth" to "ex-growth" is a 3-5 turn PE compression event; this is the primary return driver for the short thesis
  • Liquidity trap: Small/mid-cap European exporters with >30% US revenue, limited analyst coverage, and illiquid stocks — when guidance cuts, the bid evaporates; avoid on the long side entirely
  • Sequential guidance erosion: Diageo and Burberry have already demonstrated the pattern — multiple consecutive guidance cuts create permanent credibility discount; each successive cut is met with more severe re-rating as investors lose confidence in management forecasting

What the Prior Episodes Tell Us

  • 2018-2019 US-China trade war: European industrials with China/US supply chain exposure (automotive, capital equipment) underperformed MSCI Europe by 12-18% over the 18-month escalation period. Recovery came only when the Phase 1 deal provided certainty, not on tariff reduction per se
  • 2008 USD spike (though different causation): European exporters with USD revenue but EUR costs saw EPS cuts of 15-25% over 2 years; the lesson is that currency + volume compression is non-linear — companies that cut guidance once almost always cut again
  • 2022 energy crisis: Domestically anchored European companies (regulated utilities, local services) outperformed MSCI Europe by 8-15% during peak uncertainty. The pattern of "domestic stability premium" in uncertainty regimes is consistent and well-documented
  • Richard's prior (beneficiary = domestic/import-substitution; at-risk = US-revenue exporters): Research confirms the directional prior is correct. Nuance: import-substitution benefit is real but lagged (12-24 months behind anti-dumping enforcement); domestic beneficiary re-rating is leading (precedes earnings improvement by 2-3 quarters). Do not wait for earnings confirmation on the long side; do not wait for guidance cuts on the short side

APPENDIX: APM SCORING GUIDE

Beneficiary scoring (A-F):
- A: Domestic revenue >70% + regulated/contracted cash flows + defence/infrastructure tailwind. Match 3+ Critical beneficiary attributes
- B: Domestic revenue >50% + 1-2 Critical beneficiary attributes + FCF yield >5%
- C: Mixed exposure; beneficiary and at-risk attributes roughly balanced
- D: US revenue 10-20% + some pricing power offset; modest net headwind
- E: US revenue >20% + USD translation risk + limited hedge; match 2-3 Critical at-risk attributes
- F: US revenue >30% + thin margins + EPS consensus built on US growth + no US manufacturing alternative. Structural impairment. Rentokil, Burberry (current state), and automotive tier-1 (Valeo, Forvia) are the reference names for F-grade

Key scoring rule: A stock scores A-B only if it actively benefits (not just avoids harm). A stock scores E-F if the thematic specifically attacks its earning power through identifiable and quantifiable channels. C is the default for companies with <10% US revenue and no specific beneficiary tailwind — neutral, not a beneficiary.
