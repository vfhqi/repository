EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (BLUF)

Section 1 – Business Model + Organisation 24 findings

Section summary
  1. Mercialys operates a highly resilient, index-linked revenue model, pivoting successfully from Casino-dependent malls to a diversified, high-yielding 'Shop*Park' format in dynamic French regions

Core business and portfolio structure

1.
Description – core business: Mercialys is a pure-play retail real estate operator focusing on dominant, accessible shopping parks in dynamic French regions
2.
Portfolio – concentration: The portfolio comprises 47 assets, with the 34 flagship 'Shop*Parks' driving 96% of total appraisal value
3.
Format – ShopPark: This proprietary hybrid model blends the diverse offering of a mall with the low-cost efficiency of a retail park
4.
Scale – right-sized: Sites are deliberately scaled between 50 to 150 retailers, avoiding the overcapacity risks of mega-malls with 200+ units
5.
Geography – strategic polarization: Assets are concentrated in high-growth coastal, cross-border, and Sun Belt regions, completely avoiding the highly saturated Parisian market
6.
Scale economy – local dominance: The strategy relies on deep local market share, targeting catchment dominance with over 3 million annual visitors per core asset

Revenue model and pricing mechanics

7.
Revenue model – highly indexed: The rental stream provides an excellent inflation shield, with 96% of leases explicitly linked to the French commercial rent index (ILC)
8.
Revenue mix – guaranteed base: Purely variable rents are structurally marginalized, representing a negligible 1.7% to 2% of the total rental base
9.
Pricing power – occupancy cost: Tenant financial health is protected by a stable, low occupancy cost ratio of 10.9%, well below destination-mall peers
10.
Driver count – multi-anchored: Operations are driven by food anchors, but exposure to the troubled Casino group has been actively diluted in favour of diverse operators
11.
End market – tenant diversity: Excluding food anchors, no single retail tenant accounts for more than 2% of the total annualized rental income
12.
Recent delivery – robust collection: The essential-needs positioning ensures cash-flow stability, yielding a sustained rent collection rate consistently exceeding 97%

Organisational structure and internal change

13.
Internal change – vertical integration: Mercialys completed the internalization of its technical and rental management functions in late 2022, regaining full operational control across the value chain
14.
Internal change – external growth: The 2025 acquisition of ImocomPartners adds an asset-light growth engine, bringing 33 retail parks and €670m AUM under third-party management
15.
Revenue mix – fee generation: This third-party management platform provides a new revenue vector, generating recurring management fees without the associated balance-sheet capital intensity
16.
Leadership change – stable continuity: The agile executive committee, led by CEO Vincent Ravat, operates with a lean workforce of under 200 highly specialized expert employees
17.
Organisational structure – local embedding: The operational model is heavily decentralized, with over 50% of the workforce permanently located directly at the regional sites

Strategic positioning and structural demand

18.
Strategic objective – everyday low price: The merchandising mix is rigidly focused on value, with 100% of major French discount operators like Action and Normal present
19.
Customer behaviour – essential needs: The portfolio targets frequent, short-dwell-time visits, capturing consumers prioritising practical, necessity-driven purchasing over experiential leisure
20.
Competitive advantage – omnichannel integration: Mercialys operates proprietary digital tools and local logistics hubs, generating 1 million package pick-ups that drive 25% incremental on-site purchases
21.
Switching cost – locational scarcity: Severe French regulatory constraints on greenfield commercial developments (Zero Net Artificialisation) create structural scarcity and high barriers to entry for competing sites
22.
Cyclicality – recession resistant: The heavy weighting toward food, health, and discount retail provides a powerful economic shock-absorber during periods of constrained household purchasing power
23.
Other side of the story: reliance on traditional hypermarket anchors leaves the model exposed if the French hypermarket format suffers a structural, irreversible decline in footfall

Section 3 – Industry Structure + Concentration + Value Chain Economics 21 findings

Section summary
  1. stringent anti-sprawl regulations create high barriers to entry for French retail real estate, structurally favouring dominant existing operators despite near-term indexation headwinds

Industry size and structural growth

1.
Industry size – investment contraction: French commercial real estate investment plunged 48% year-on-year to €1.9bn in Q1 2026, reflecting a cautious macroeconomic environment
2.
Retail investment – relative resilience: Despite the broader transaction slowdown, retail remained the most active asset class, capturing €849m or nearly 45% of total Q1 2026 investment
3.
Growth trajectory – indexation trough: The commercial rent index (ILC) turned negative in late 2025, reaching -0.5% year-on-year due to declining construction costs
4.
Macro shift – forward inflation: Indexation is expected to remain flat throughout 2026 before gradually accelerating to approximately 2% by 2028 as inflationary pressures return
5.
Regulatory shift – rent control rejection: The French Senate formally rejected commercial rent control proposals, preserving the structural upside for landlords' rental indexation models

Value chain economics and margin pool

6.
Margin pool – tenant pressure: Retailers face a highly fragmented and competitive French grocery market, resulting in structurally low operating margins around 1.5% to 3.0%
7.
Pricing leverage – property owners: Top-tier landlords maintain pricing power over retailers, achieving average rental uplifts of 2% to 5% on lease renewals
8.
Value chain stage – physical stores: Despite e-commerce growth, physical retail remains dominant, accounting for 87% of total retail sales across the European market
9.
Operating cost – ESG mandates: The French Tertiary Decree imposes mandatory capital expenditure on landlords, requiring a 40% reduction in building energy consumption by 2030
10.
Value accrual – local dominance: Landlords positioned in high-growth regional hubs capture a disproportionate share of footfall and tenant demand relative to saturated urban centres

Industry structure and concentration

11.
Fragmentation – broad supply: The overall European retail property market remains highly fragmented, with shopping centres accounting for only 20% of total retail space
12.
Industry rank – major operators: The French market is led by scaled, listed REITs including Unibail-Rodamco-Westfield, Klépierre, Carmila, and Mercialys
13.
Consolidation – tenant rationalisation: Hypermarket operators like Carrefour are actively rationalising their networks, driving downsizing that creates both vacancy risks and redevelopment opportunities
14.
Share stability – market polarisation: Retailer demand is sharply polarising, with premium centres capturing almost all tenant interest while secondary sites face abandonment
15.
Competitiveness trend – widening gap: National commercial vacancy worsened to 16.8% in shopping centres, highlighting the growing risk of obsolescence for sub-scale or poorly located assets

Disruption forces and gravitational pull

16.
Structural driver – supply constraints: The "Zero Net Artificialisation" (ZAN) law creates a structural moratorium on new retail developments, rendering existing commercial sites increasingly rare
17.
Gravitational pull – convenience formats: Consumer preferences are heavily shifting away from massive destination malls toward accessible, mid-sized retail parks and essential-needs convenience centres
18.
Format shift – retail park outperformance: Retail parks accounted for 17% of transactional volume, matching shopping centres, supported by lower occupancy costs and high structural resilience
19.
New entrant share – tightly capped: Severe planning constraints and environmental regulations ensure that the threat of disruptive new physical real estate entrants remains exceptionally low
20.
Other side of the story: escalating ESG compliance costs and shifting e-commerce penetration could permanently impair the viability of mid-tier retail assets, driving widespread obsolescence
