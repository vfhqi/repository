§9 — Sceptical Synthesis (Cross-cutting)

  • Verdict: The single strongest bear case is Baltic Power execution failure — a 2+ year delay or material cost overrun would collapse the FY2026-2027 EPS thesis, stranding investors in a high-leverage, low-cash-conversion company with limited near-term catalysts [#J]

  • Reverse-engineered bear — primary: Baltic Power construction risk is the central binary; the bull case requires a ~PLN 20bn+ project to be delivered on time and on budget in a global offshore wind environment where cost overruns have become the sector norm (Orsted UK impairments, Siemens Gamesa losses) [#J]

  • PEP-PL's partners (Equinor, Orlen) are strong but not immune to supply chain and weather risk [#I]

  • A 24-month delay to first power shifts the EPS step-change from FY2026-2027 to FY2028-2029, destroying IRR for anyone buying at current prices [#J]

  • Single largest risk: Offshore wind sector cost inflation is structural — turbine prices rose ~30-40% globally between 2020 and 2024; if Baltic Power's CAPEX envelope is materially exceeded, project returns fall below the contracted revenue level and PEP-PL's economics deteriorate [#A]

  • Management has not disclosed detailed capex sensitivity analysis publicly — this opacity is itself a concern [#J]

  • Early warning — leverage: Net debt is rising rapidly; if EBITDA growth disappoints (operating asset underperformance or Baltic Power delay), debt covenants could come under pressure — covenant terms not publicly disclosed in detail [#A]

  • Leverage build without commensurate operating cash flow is the classic pre-stress pattern in project-heavy developers [#J]

  • Early warning — guidance pattern: Three consecutive offshore timeline revisions (2022-2025) is a data pattern, not isolated noise — management's construction forecasting has been systematically optimistic [#J]

  • Each revision has been framed as external/sector-driven; at some point the market prices in management-specific execution risk [#J]

  • Breaking point — power prices: If Polish wholesale electricity prices fall materially (e.g. German-driven duck curve spreading east, overcapacity from aggressive RES addition), the ~20% merchant/uncontracted portion of PEP-PL's revenue faces structural compression [#A]

  • Would also reduce economics of future RES auctions, slowing growth pipeline replenishment [#J]

  • Peer-relative downside: If Grenergy or another Western European developer with better margin profile and diversified geography trades at similar or lower multiples than PEP-PL, the case for owning PEP-PL (with its single-country, single-project concentration risk) becomes harder to make on relative basis [#J]

  • Governance overhang: Orlen's 56% ownership creates potential for related-party transactions (off-take agreements, service contracts, project financing terms) that may not maximise minority shareholder value — this is a persistent structural bear point [#A]

  • Orlen's own strategy has been volatile under political pressure in Poland; strategic alignment assumed in the bull case may not be durable [#J]

  • Earnings quality bear synthesis: Large GAAP-to-adjusted gap + weak cash conversion + construction-dependent margin expansion = an earnings profile that requires extraordinary trust in management's adjusted metrics; a sceptic can construct a scenario where "true" economic earnings are 40-50% below adjusted figures [#J]

  • Structural decay — sector-level: The Polish RES sector's grid connection constraints are getting worse, not better; PSE (Polish grid operator) has been chronically underfunded relative to the pace of RES deployment — any peer company (or PEP-PL itself with future pipeline) faces increasing grid connection risk beyond Baltic Power [#A]

Confidence in thesis: MEDIUM — the structural setup is compelling (offshore scarcity, policy tailwind, Orlen backing) but single-project concentration and construction execution risk make the FY2026-2027 EPS thesis highly binary; insufficient information from [C] sources alone to reach HIGH confidence
