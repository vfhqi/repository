KEY FINDINGS — J→A→I Order

Verdict: Pernod Ricard is a structurally impaired quality compounder in a self-inflicted trough — brand architecture remains world-class but the business has lost operating leverage, is carrying uncomfortable leverage, and faces simultaneous headwinds from three of its four largest markets. The question is not whether recovery happens but whether the recovery timeline is 18 months or 48 months.

  • Fulcrum: The single most important near-term driver is US destocking completion + volume recovery, not India, not China. India is the 3–5Y compounding story; the US is the FY27 EPS swing factor. [#J]
  • Destocking: Still incomplete in the US as of Q3 FY26 (April 2026). Q3 showed organic stabilisation (+0.1%) with volumes back to +4% group-wide, but the US contracted -12% organically in Q3 — no clean bill of health yet. [#A]
  • China is a structural impairment, not a cyclical trough: Cognac anti-dumping tariffs (34.9% on EU Cognac imports), shift in consumption patterns at banquets and weddings, and Martell's -21% H1 FY26 decline suggest the China contribution will not recover to prior peaks without a political resolution. [#J]
  • India is the genuine option value: 13% of group sales, growing 11% in Q3 FY26, #1 market by volume, potential IPO optionality surfacing in April 2026 — a listed Indian subsidiary could catalyse a rerating of the parent. [#A]
  • Margin gap to Diageo is the Elliott thesis, and it remains wide: Pernod's operating margin is approximately 26.9% (FY25) vs Diageo's ~28% underlying — a 100–150bps gap persists despite successive improvement plans. The €1bn efficiency programme (FY26–29) is the mechanism for closure. [#A]
  • Leverage is uncomfortable at 3.8x ND/EBITDA (H1 FY26): Target is sub-3x by FY29. At current EBITDA run-rate, this requires either earnings recovery or asset disposals — the India IPO route is one valve. [#I]
  • Pricing power was real in FY23 (+8% price/mix) but has stalled: In FY24–26, volumes have declined alongside softer pricing; the pricing ceiling appears to have been reached in several Western markets and de-premiumisation is a growing risk in the US. [#A]
  • Brand portfolio is coherent and durable: Jameson (#1 Irish whiskey globally, >10m cases), Absolut (top-3 global premium vodka), Ballantine's and Chivas (Scotch), Martell (Cognac) — hard SRCA, but Martell is currently impaired and Absolut faces vodka category headwinds. [#A]
  • Elliott's 2018–19 activism left a governance residue: Patricia Barbizet installed as lead independent director; board refreshment has occurred; but the Ricard family retain control and operational decisions remain family-influenced. [#I]
  • Right-to-Left reversal scenario: Pernod at 25%+ IRR from current levels requires: US volumes recovering +5–8% in FY27, China stabilising at new floor, India growing 10%+ pa, and €300–400m of EBITDA from efficiency delivery. All four simultaneously is a high bar. [#J]

§1 BUSINESS OVERVIEW

Verdict: Pernod Ricard is the world's second-largest spirits group — a genuinely global, premium-weighted portfolio with structural brand advantages, but currently navigating the most complex operating environment in a decade under a CEO who is also the controlling family's representative.

  • Scale: Net sales of €10,959m in FY25 (year ended June 2025); the global #2 spirits group by revenue behind Diageo . [#I]
  • Operates in ~160 markets; employs ~19,000 people; listed on Euronext Paris. [#I]

  • Spirits cohort comparison: Diageo ~£17.1bn revenue (FY25); Campari ~€2.8bn; Rémy Cointreau ~€1.3bn. Pernod sits firmly in the upper tier of the global spirits duopoly. [#I]

  • Portfolio architecture: Organised around 14 Strategic International Brands (SIBs), c.17 Strategic Local Brands (SLBs), and a long tail of specialty brands. [#I]

  • SIBs account for the majority of revenue and profit contribution; SLBs (e.g. Royal Stag, Seagram's in India) are high-volume, lower-margin anchors in emerging markets. [#I]

  • Key categories: Irish whiskey (Jameson), Scotch (Chivas Regal, Ballantine's, The Glenlivet), Cognac (Martell), Vodka (Absolut), Rum (Malibu, Havana Club), Gin (Beefeater, Monkey 47). [#I]

  • Geographic mix (FY25): [#I]

  • Americas: €3,154m (~29% of net sales) — US is largest single market
  • Asia/Rest of World: €4,635m (~42%) — includes India (#1 by volume), China, travel retail
  • Europe: €3,170m (~29%) — includes home market France

  • ❌ Asia/RoW is the highest-weight region yet simultaneously the most challenged: China in structural decline; India bright but India revenue is local-market spirits (different margin profile from imported brands). [#J]

  • Channel mix: [#I]

  • On-trade (bars, restaurants, hotels) and off-trade (retail/e-commerce) split roughly 30/70 in most Western markets.
  • Travel retail is a material channel for Martell/Cognac — directly exposed to the China anti-dumping ruling. [#A]

  • India is predominantly state-regulated off-trade; channel dynamics are structurally different (state government licensing). [#I]

  • Strategic priorities under Alexandre Ricard: [#A]

  • "Convivialité" brand values framework; premiumisation as the central pillar; digital/e-commerce investment; sustainability (carbon targets by 2030).
  • FY26–29 €1bn Operational Efficiencies programme launched as the primary mechanism to close the margin gap with Diageo.
  • Portfolio rationalisation: disposed of Wines business and Imperial Blue (India) to sharpen spirits focus. [#I]
  • No CEO change: Alexandre Ricard (grandson of Paul Ricard, co-founder) remains Chairman and CEO as of May 2026. He became CEO in February 2015. [#I]

  • ❌ Family founder–CEO structure introduces governance tension: Elliott pressed precisely on lack of independent board oversight and absence of performance accountability mechanisms typical of non-family peers. [#J]

  • Sceptical check: The premiumisation narrative has driven Pernod's strategy for a decade. Euromonitor (May 2026) declares premiumisation has "hit a wall." Super-premium-plus spirits fell 15% in 2025. If the strategic thesis (trade-up consumer) is in structural retreat, the entire playbook requires revision. [#J]

§2 FINANCIAL PROFILE

Verdict: Revenue is declining for the second consecutive year; margin stability is being defended through cost cuts rather than volume leverage; and leverage at 3.8x ND/EBITDA is above the spirits sector comfort zone. This is a business in a financial trough but not in distress.

  • Revenue trajectory (L3Y organic): [#I]
  • FY23: +10% organic (€12,137m reported) — peak of post-COVID spirits boom; +8% price/mix, +1% volume
  • FY24: ~+1% organic (€11,598m reported) — sharp deceleration; volumes declining, price/mix insufficient to offset
  • FY25: -3.0% organic (€10,959m reported) — second year of contraction; FX drag -€277m from TRY, ARS, INR
  • FY26 9M (Apr 2026): -4.4% organic YTD; Q3 alone +0.1% — early stabilisation signal [#A]

  • ❌ vs Diageo: Diageo's FY25 organic net sales were -1.5%, outperforming Pernod by ~150bps on a two-year average. [#A]

  • EBITDA margin: [#A]

  • H1 FY26 EBITDA margin: ~34.3% (EBITDA €1,804m on revenue €5,253m)
  • Operating margin: 26.9% FY25 — flat for second consecutive year despite volume decline, reflecting successful cost control
  • Diageo operating margin: ~28% underlying (FY25) — remains ~100–130bps ahead of Pernod on operating basis

  • Elliott's original analysis (2018/19) identified a 5 percentage point gap; that has narrowed to ~1–1.5pts but cost cutting has been the vehicle, not leverage. [#J]

  • EPS trajectory: [#I]

  • H1 FY26 EPS: €4.04 (semester ended December 2025)
  • FY25 annual EPS: approximately €8–9 (implied from operating margin and interest load; full year EPS not separately stated in available data)
  • The net debt load (~€11.2bn at H1 FY26) means interest cost is a meaningful drag on EPS; rising rates 2022–24 compounded this. [#A]

  • ❌ EPS is tracking below FY23 peak levels due to simultaneous: volume decline, FX, higher interest costs. [#A]

  • Free cash flow: [#I]

  • FY25 FCF: €1,133m (+€170m vs FY24) — improved via working capital release (inventory reduction)

  • FCF improvement is a genuine positive signal: sell-through normalisation is happening even if slowly. [#A]

  • Balance sheet / leverage: [#I]

  • Net debt FY25: €10,727m
  • Net debt H1 FY26: €11,168m (seasonal timing; dividend payments in H1)
  • ND/EBITDA FY25: 3.3x; H1 FY26: 3.8x (higher on EBITDA deterioration + seasonal debt build)
  • Target: below 3.0x by FY29 — requires ~€1.5–2bn EBITDA recovery or debt reduction from disposals

  • ❌ At 3.8x, Pernod is notably more leveraged than Diageo (~2.5x) and Rémy Cointreau (~1.8x). The leverage means less financial optionality in a downturn. [#J]

  • Sceptical check: The margin has been "defended" at 26.9% through cost cuts, not volume recovery. Efficiency gains without top-line growth eventually hits a floor. If organic decline continues into FY27, the margin defence narrative becomes harder to sustain without cuts that harm brand investment. [#J]

§3 BRAND PORTFOLIO QUALITY

Verdict: The top-tier brands (Jameson, Ballantine's, Absolut) have genuine hard SRCA — scale distribution, brand heritage, and consumer loyalty built over decades. Martell is impaired by China policy, not brand quality. The long tail of 100+ brands is a complexity treadmill risk that Diageo has historically managed better.

  • Portfolio breadth overview: [#I]
  • 14 Strategic International Brands, ~17 Strategic Local Brands; total portfolio exceeds 100 brands
  • Breadth creates distribution scale advantages (must-stock status with major distributors worldwide) [#A]

  • ❌ Breadth also creates management complexity: R&D, marketing budgets, and management attention spread thin vs mono-category peers like Rémy Cointreau [#J]

  • Jameson (Irish Whiskey): [#A]

  • ⚡ RARE: Jameson is the #1 Irish whiskey globally at >10 million nine-litre cases — a genuinely dominant franchise. The entire Irish whiskey category has compounded at ~10% pa for two decades.
  • In FY25/H1 FY26 Jameson showed good growth and was cited as a bright spot alongside Ballantine's
  • Volume resilience in the US has been stronger than the category average; Irish whiskey benefits from the "not Scotch, not bourbon" positioning in a tariff-contested environment [#A]

  • Key risk: Irish whiskey category maturation — the decade of rapid growth may be tapering [#I]

  • Absolut (Vodka): [#A]

  • Top-3 global premium vodka; Swedish provenance positioning
  • Showed "very strong ongoing growth" per FY25/Q3 FY26 management commentary
  • ❌ Vodka category faces headwinds: RTD cannibalisation, tequila taking share in the US, category commoditisation at lower price points [#J]

  • Absolut's geographic over-indexing to the US makes it sensitive to the destocking cycle [#I]

  • Chivas Regal (Scotch, blended premium): [#A]

  • Premium Scotch; strong in Asia and emerging markets (Brazil, duty-free)
  • Cited as growing in Brazil (+) and Canada (+) in Q3 FY26

  • China exposure is a structural negative given Scotch's association with cognac-style gifting and luxury consumption patterns [#I]

  • Ballantine's (Scotch, blended): [#A]

  • Strong European franchise; growing in H1/Q3 FY26
  • Resilient volume performance; benefits from Scotch's EU-home-market distribution strength [#I]

  • Lower premiumisation upside than Chivas or The Glenlivet [#I]

  • Martell (Cognac): [#A]

  • ❌ Severely impaired: H1 FY26 -17%; Q3 FY26 China still -7% organic; 34.9% EU anti-dumping tariff on Cognac into China still in force
  • Excluding China, Martell grew ~+20% in H1 FY26 — South Africa, US performing
  • Travel retail recovery in Q2 as Martell resumed some duty-free sales in China — but this is partial and fragile [#A]

  • The China cognac anti-dumping investigation appears politically driven (retaliation for EU EV tariffs); resolution timeline is uncertain [#J]

  • The Glenlivet (Single Malt Scotch): [#I]

  • 1 single malt in the US; premium positioning intact

  • Destocking in the US has affected Scotch category sell-through; recovery expected as inventories normalise [#I]

  • Flywheel vs Treadmill assessment: [#J]

  • The core SIBs (Jameson, Absolut, Ballantine's) exhibit flywheel characteristics: scale, pricing power, distribution moats, growing geographies (India, Africa, LatAm).
  • The long tail (100+ brands) and SLB complexity in India (state-regulated, low-margin local brands) look more like a treadmill — high operational overhead, margin dilution, management distraction.

  • Diageo has historically managed portfolio focus better (sold Baileys-adjacent non-core assets more decisively). Pernod has been slower to rationalise; recent Wines and Imperial Blue disposals are step changes in the right direction but more rationalisation remains possible. [#J]

  • Moat assessment (Moats & SRCA framework): [#A]

  • Hard SRCA: Brand heritage (Jameson 1780, Martell 1715, Chivas 1801, Absolut 1979), aged inventory, production geography locks (Irish, Scotch, Cognac AOC).
  • Soft SRCA: Distribution network (17 market companies, proprietary route-to-market in key markets), consumer loyalty, bartender/on-trade relationships.

  • Weak SRCA: No switching cost for consumers (spirits are substitutable at the category level); pricing power is real but not absolute. [#J]

  • Sceptical check: Pernod's brand portfolio breadth is often cited as a strength. It is equally a constraint on strategic clarity. Diageo with a narrower but more concentrated top-tier portfolio (Johnnie Walker, Guinness, Tanqueray) tends to generate stronger brand equity metrics and higher margins. More brands does not mean better compounding. [#J]

§4 GEOGRAPHIC EXPOSURE

Verdict: The geographic mix is a double-edged sword: India is structurally outstanding, China is structurally impaired, the US is cyclically challenged, and Europe is stable but low-growth. Three of four key markets are currently negative — this is the crux of the bear case.

  • Americas (~29% of net sales): [#A]
  • US: largest single market; organic decline in both FY25 and FY26 (Q3 FY26: -12% organic)
  • Destocking the primary driver of US weakness — not end-consumer demand collapse
  • EU retaliation on American whiskey (50% tariff increase) creates indirect benefit: less US bourbon competition in some markets, but US as a market is directly impaired by inventory cycle
  • Canada: strong growth in Q3 FY26, driven by Bumbu, Absolut, and Jameson [#I]

  • Brazil: continued solid growth for Ballantine's, Absolut, Chivas Regal — a structural growth story [#I]

  • Asia/Rest of World (~42% of net sales — highest weight): [#A]

  • India: 13% of group sales (#1 by volume, #2 by value); 11% revenue growth in Q3 FY26; see §7 for full India analysis
  • China: in structural decline; Martell+Scotch heavily impacted by anti-dumping tariffs and shifting consumption norms; China was historically ~10% of group sales; now meaningfully lower and falling
  • ❌ Travel retail: partially recovering but China duty-free restrictions have constrained the key Cognac channel [#A]

  • Africa + Middle East: growing well, cited as performing in Q3 FY26 [#I]

  • Europe (~29% of net sales): [#I]

  • Home market; generally stable; Scotch and gin categories perform reliably
  • France (Pastis/Ricard heritage) is a mature category; limited growth
  • UK: Jameson and Absolut growing; but cost-of-living pressure affecting on-trade volumes [#I]

  • EU as a whole: organic -2% FY25; not a growth engine but a stable cash contributor [#I]

  • India vs China — structural comparison: [#J]

  • India: volume-driven, premiumisation tailwind, ~1.4bn population with rising middle class, growing legal drinking age population, favourable demographics; Pernod #1 by volume (Royal Stag, Blenders Pride)
  • China: wealth/gifting-driven (B2B culture); already under regulatory scrutiny (anti-corruption campaign since Xi era dampened gifting); now anti-dumping tariffs add economic barrier; demographic headwinds (shrinking working-age population)

  • The China situation is not simply cyclical destocking — it is a structural reorientation of consumption patterns reinforced by political dynamics. [#J]

  • Sceptical check: Pernod's Asia/RoW weighting (~42%) is both its greatest opportunity (India, Africa) and its greatest current liability (China, tariff exposure). The geographic diversification story is valid in 5+ years; over 18–36 months, it is net negative because China headwinds are acute while India benefits accumulate gradually. [#J]

§5 DESTOCKING CYCLE

Verdict: The destocking cycle is in late stages but not complete, particularly in the US market. Q3 FY26 volume recovery (+4% group) is the first hard evidence of sell-through normalisation, but the US market (-12% organic in Q3) remains the key outstanding watch item. Full cycle completion is more likely H2 FY26 / H1 FY27.

CQ-C1 addressed here — see also §12

  • Origin of the overhang: [#I]
  • Post-COVID spirits demand surge (FY21–FY23) led distributors and retailers to over-order; sell-in significantly exceeded sell-through
  • The five largest listed spirits producers held a collective ~$22bn in unsold aged inventory at peak — the highest in over a decade

  • Pernod specifically built inventories in high-value Scotch and Cognac (long maturation lead times) during the boom years [#A]

  • US destocking status: [#A]

  • Q1 FY26 (Jul–Sep 2025): US organic -16% — aggressive destocking by large retailers
  • Q2 FY26 (Oct–Dec 2025): US organic still deeply negative (H1 FY26 overall -5.9%)
  • Q3 FY26 (Jan–Mar 2026): US organic -12% — still contracting but sequential improvement vs prior quarters

  • ❌ US is NOT yet through the destocking cycle: the sequential improvement is real but the US market remains in contraction as of the most recent data point (April 2026). [#J]

  • Group volume signal: [#A]

  • Q3 FY26 group volumes: +4% — first quarter of volume growth in multiple years
  • Strategic International Brand volumes: +3% in Q3

  • These are sell-in volumes (shipment to trade), not consumer sell-through — the distinction matters; improvement in sell-in can lag actual consumer demand recovery [#I]

  • China destocking: [#A]

  • Separate dynamic to the US: China is both a destocking issue AND a structural demand/tariff issue
  • Anti-dumping tariffs on EU Cognac (34.9%) imposed during 2024; remain in place as of May 2026
  • Q3 FY26 China: -7% organic — some improvement from H1 FY26 (-21%) but still in decline

  • Travel retail partially restored for Martell but duty-free is a fraction of prior peak [#I]

  • Evidence for cycle completion: [#A]

  • FCF improvement in FY25 (+€170m): driven by reduction in finished goods inventory — a direct measure of destocking progress
  • Q3 volume return to growth: earliest hard data point suggesting the US channel is beginning to normalise

  • Management guidance: H2 FY26 improvement skewed / FY26 full-year revised to -3% to -4% organic — implies H2 recovery needed; if achieved, confirms US cycle turning [#J]

  • Sceptical check: The industry destocking narrative has been used to explain disappointing results for six consecutive quarters. Every quarter management has forecast H2 recovery. Q3 stabilisation (+0.1% organic) is consistent with narrative but not yet the decisive breakout. The test is whether H2 FY26 (January–June 2026) delivers the organic improvement that has been promised. [#J]

The pricing playbook worked once; it is less available now.

CQ-C3 addressed here — see also §12

  • Historical pricing record (FY21–FY23): [#I]
  • FY23: organic +10%; price/mix +9%, volume +1% — genuinely exceptional pricing realisation
  • Price increases were taken across SIBs: Jameson, Absolut, Chivas all raised RSPs (recommended selling prices) in US, UK, France

  • Consumer accepted: volumes remained broadly flat despite meaningful price increases — a textbook demonstration of brand pricing power during an unusual macro environment [#A]

  • FY24 — pricing ceiling reached: [#A]

  • Organic +1%; price/mix contribution positive but volume decline accelerated
  • Volume weakness was partly attributed to destocking, partly to consumer pushback on price

  • Sequential volume recovery in H2 FY24 did not materialise as originally forecast [#I]

  • FY25 — volume/price tradeoff deteriorating: [#A]

  • Organic -3%; volumes declining; price/mix contribution reduced
  • Management language shifted from "premium pricing" to "defending margins" — a qualitative signal of reduced pricing headroom

  • ❌ The spirits category showed de-premiumisation in the US: consumers trading down within category (e.g. from premium to standard vodka), and trading out of spirits into RTDs and wine/cider [#J]

  • FY26 data: [#A]

  • H1 FY26: organic -5.9%; volumes -7%; implied price/mix approximately flat to slight positive

  • Q3 FY26: volumes +4% but organic +0.1% — implies mild negative price/mix in Q3; volumes recovering faster than value. This is the key concern: recovery being led by volume not price/mix, suggesting pricing ceiling has been reached. [#J]

  • Spirits industry context: [#I]

  • Super-premium-plus spirits fell 15% in 2025 across the industry

  • Sceptical check: The classic quality compounder in spirits is supposed to compound through the cycle — price/mix up, volumes resilient. Pernod in FY24–26 has experienced the opposite: price ceiling hit, volumes falling. If Q3 FY26's "volume recovery without price recovery" dynamic persists into FY27, it implies organic growth recovery will be volume-led at lower margins, not the high-quality price/mix-led recovery investors are modelling. §7 INDIA OPPORTUNITY

Verdict: India is the most credible structural growth engine Pernod possesses — #1 market by volume, growing double digits, with a potential IPO catalyst that could unlock substantial valuation arbitrage. This is not a question of whether India grows; it is a question of whether India grows fast enough to offset China and US weakness within a 36-month investment horizon.

CQ-C2 partially addressed here — see also §12

  • Scale and market position: [#A]
  • India: 13% of group net sales (~€1.4bn); #1 market by volume, #2 by value
  • India revenue FY25: ₹27,446 crore (~€3bn at local currency — note: this includes SLBs which are reported separately from group net sales; group net sales figure is lower due to local currency/transfer price dynamics) [#I]
  • Pernod is the #1 player in India by volume in the organised sector; brands: Royal Stag, Blenders Pride (SLBs), plus imports (Chivas, Jameson, Absolut)

  • Indian Spirits market is the world's largest by volume (Scotch whisky sector alone: India is #1 global consumer) [#I]

  • Growth rate: [#A]

  • 5Y average annual revenue growth in India: ~8%
  • FY25 India: strong growth cited; +6% YTD FY26; Q3 FY26: +11% revenue growth — outperforming all other major markets
  • Premiumisation within India: imported brands (Jameson, Absolut, Chivas) growing double digits; SLBs (Royal Stag) growing mid-single digits

  • ⚡ RARE: India is simultaneously Pernod's largest volume market AND its fastest-growing major market — a rare combination in any consumer staples franchise anywhere globally. [#J]

  • Margin profile: [#I]

  • India margins are structurally different from group margins: SLBs (local brands) carry much lower gross margins (~30–35%) vs SIBs (imported brands, ~55–60% gross margin)
  • EBITDA margins in India (for the Indian subsidiary, Pernod Ricard India Private Limited) are estimated at 6–8% on the local statutory accounts — lower than group margins
  • However: the India contribution is accounted for in group results at a transfer-price level; the economic value accrues to the group through royalties, transfer prices, and equity income [#A]

  • ❌ Margin dilution from India growth: as India grows as a share of group revenue, the mix shift towards lower-margin SLBs puts structural pressure on group gross margins, partially offsetting the top-line growth [#J]

  • IPO optionality: [#A]

  • April 2026: Bloomberg reports Pernod Ricard has begun work on a potential IPO of its Indian subsidiary
  • Company statement: "no decision yet; exploring strategic options"
  • Valuation arbitrage rationale: Diageo's Indian subsidiary (United Spirits Ltd) trades at >60x P/E vs Pernod parent at ~13–15x; an Indian listing could value Pernod India at 40–60x local earnings, implying significant embedded value not captured in parent valuation

  • Estimated Indian subsidiary valuation at IPO: potentially €8–12bn based on Diageo India comparable, vs Pernod group market cap of ~€28–32bn — meaningful relative to the whole [#J]

  • Regulatory risks in India: [#I]

  • Indian alcohol market is state-regulated; different tariff/duty structures in each of 28+ states
  • Import duties on Scotch: ~150% (Basic Customs Duty) — significant constraint on SIB volume growth; India Free Trade Agreement with UK (under negotiation) could be transformative if Scotch duty is reduced
  • Frequent state-level policy changes (prohibition in some states, advertising bans, retail licensing restrictions) create unpredictable operating environment [#I]

  • Recent positive: several states moving towards "model shop" liberalisation of retail, which benefits organised players like Pernod [#A]

  • Is India the fulcrum driver (N1–3Y)? [#J]

  • India contributes ~13% of revenue but with a 3–4 year lag before margin quality catches up to volume scale
  • The fulcrum driver for FY27 EPS is not India — it is US volume recovery and the €1bn efficiency programme

  • India is the 5–10Y compounding story; any investor underwriting Pernod primarily on India is running the right thesis on the wrong timeline [#J]

  • Sceptical check: The India story is real. The risk is that India's contribution, while growing, is structurally lower margin than the group average, meaning faster India growth arithmetically dilutes consolidated margins even as it supports top-line. At the same time, India's regulatory unpredictability (state policy, prohibition risks, duty structures) means the growth rate is not guaranteed. [#J]

§8 MANAGEMENT AND STRATEGIC PIVOT

Verdict: Alexandre Ricard's dual Chairman-CEO structure creates a governance tension that has been structurally unresolved since Elliott's 2018–19 campaign. The addition of a lead independent director (Barbizet) was a concession; the family retains control. The strategic pivot to efficiency (€1bn programme) and portfolio focus (Wines disposal, Imperial Blue disposal) is directionally correct but arrived late relative to peers.

  • Leadership structure: [#I]
  • Alexandre Ricard: Chairman and CEO since February 2015; grandson of Paul Ricard; holds dual mandate
  • Board: 15 directors including 2 employee representatives and 8 independent directors (as of FY25 Annual Report)
  • Société Paul Ricard and holding entities retain a controlling interest (~21% economic stake with amplified voting rights through double voting shares) [#A]

  • Patricia Barbizet: installed as Lead Independent Director (January 2019, following Elliott campaign); former CEO of Christie's; role is to oversee relations with investors on governance matters [#I]

  • Elliott Management — history and current status: [#A]

  • December 2018: Elliott disclosed >2.5% stake and launched campaign for: (1) independent board members, (2) more ambitious operational improvement plan, (3) margin closure vs Diageo
  • Elliott's specific concerns: market share losses across key segments; successive improvement plans failing to generate operating leverage; operating margin at 5pp discount to closest peer (Diageo)
  • Company response: accepted Barbizet as LID; engaged on governance; rejected external CEO narrative
  • Current status (May 2026): Elliott's stake is not disclosed at current levels; the initial activism campaign resulted in governance improvements but did not force a CEO change or strategic overhaul of the magnitude Elliott originally demanded [#I]

  • The €1bn efficiency programme (FY26–29) is the closest thing to the operational improvement plan Elliott originally called for — arriving ~6 years after the campaign [#J]

  • Strategic portfolio decisions (FY24–26): [#A]

  • Divested Wines business (c.€500m revenue) — sharpening spirits focus
  • Divested Imperial Blue (large-volume Indian whisky) — removing a low-margin local brand drag in India
  • These are the right moves directionally; however, they also reduce revenues and complicate like-for-like comparisons [#I]

  • Note: Group Structure impact on FY26 9M reported sales: -€393m, primarily from these disposals [#I]

  • €1bn Operational Efficiencies programme: [#A]

  • FY26–FY29: target €1bn of cumulative efficiencies
  • Areas: organisational restructuring, procurement, distribution rationalisation, marketing efficiency
  • Q: What is the margin impact? Management target is annual organic operating margin expansion from FY27 onward; absolute EBITDA uplift from efficiencies, partially reinvested in brand/distribution [#I]

  • ❌ Risk: efficiency programmes that cut sales force/marketing in a volume-recovery phase can starve the growth engine at precisely the wrong moment [#J]

  • CEO succession risk: [#I]

  • Alexandre Ricard is 49 (born 1977); no obvious succession plan disclosed
  • Family control means CEO succession is not a typical governance process — family consensus determines outcome

  • This is an asymmetric risk: in a distress scenario (prolonged earnings decline, leveraged balance sheet), the combination of family control + CEO inflexibility is a governance trap [#J]

  • Sceptical check: The governance structure at Pernod is the most significant non-financial risk.
