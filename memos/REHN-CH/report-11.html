KEY FINDINGS - Structural verdict: Romande Energie Holding sits at the most defensible stage of a heavily regulated, quasi-linear value chain — regulated distribution + retail supply in a geographically captive Canton Vaud concession area, insulating it from commodity-level margin volatility [#J]

  • Chain shape: The Swiss electricity value chain is predominantly linear (generation → transmission → distribution → retail supply → end customer), with regulated monopoly rents concentrated in the Distribution Network Operator stage and meaningful but smaller rents in retail supply; generation margins are structurally bifurcated between regulated hydro and merchant thermal [#J]

  • Positional concentration: REHN-CH operates across three of the five chain stages — generation (hydro, partial ownership stakes), distribution (regulated DNO for ~265,000 network users), and retail supply — making it an integrated regional utility rather than a single-stage specialist; the distribution stage anchors economics [#J]

  • Margin pool: Distribution is the highest-margin, lowest-volatility stage — Swiss cost-of-capital regulated returns (WACC-based, set by ElCom) reliably deliver mid-to-high single-digit EBIT margins on a regulated asset base; generation margins are volatile but REHN-CH's hydro exposure is partially cost-regulated or hedged; retail supply margins are thin and exposed to wholesale spot volatility [#J]

  • Pricing power: Strong at distribution (regulatory determination); moderate-to-weak at retail supply (open market for large customers since 2009; captive small customers re-priced annually by ElCom tariff review); wholesale generation pricing is market-determined but REHN-CH's hydro fleet carries water-right and licence renewal risk [#J]

  • Supplier leverage: ❌ REHN-CH is a net buyer of wholesale electricity at grid scale, purchasing from national transmission operator (Swissgrid) and wholesale markets; in periods of peak demand or low hydro inflow, purchase costs rise and REHN-CH has limited leverage to suppress them [#J]

  • Vertical integration: REHN-CH's integration into generation (hydro stakes) is structurally prudent — it partially self-supplies, reducing spot-market exposure; integration into smart-grid / energy-services adjacencies is nascent, representing an option rather than a proven margin contributor [#A]

  • Key structural threat: Energy community (Eigenverbrauchsgemeinschaft) and rooftop solar self-consumption models risk disintermediating retail supply for high-value residential and commercial customers over a 5-10 year horizon; this is not yet margin-material but is structurally directional [#I]

  • Cohort context gap: No cohort or sector peer data was injected per D-BATCH-25; all comparative anchoring draws on European/Swiss utility sector public data and regulatory filings; peer comparisons are indicative, not derived from curated peer research [#A]

§1 — Value Chain Map + Stage Table

  • Chain shape: The Swiss electricity value chain is fundamentally linear — energy flows sequentially from primary fuel input or natural resource through generation, high-voltage transmission, medium/low-voltage distribution, and retail supply to end customers; no platform intermediary sits between stages [#J]

  • Regulatory overlay: Each stage carries a distinct regulatory regime — generation is partially market, partially cantonal-concession; transmission is a statutory Swissgrid monopoly; distribution is a territorial concession monopoly; retail is partially liberalised (large customers > 100 MWh/yr since 2009, full liberalisation pending) [#J]

  • Stage 1 — Primary resource / fuel input: Hydro reservoirs and run-of-river sites (dominant in CH), plus pumped storage, nuclear fuel (EDF-type supply), gas for CCGT, wind/solar resources; Switzerland's hydro resource is geographically concentrated in Alpine cantons and owned under long-term cantonal concessions [#A]

  • Stage 2 — Generation: Operators include large-cap integrated utilities (Axpo, Alpiq, BKW, SIG, EWZ) at national scale plus hundreds of smaller municipal producers; capital intensity is very high (CHF/MW hydro greenfield > CHF 3-5m/MW) but run-of-river and reservoir plants have near-zero variable cost once built; typical EBIT margins range widely from <5% (merchant thermal) to 25-35% (depreciated hydro with low variable cost) [#A]

  • Stage 3 — High-voltage transmission: Swissgrid is the sole operator of the 220/380 kV network; regulated RAB (Regulatory Asset Base) model under Swiss Electricity Act (StromVG); capital intensity extremely high; typical EBIT margin ~15-20% on regulated asset base; REHN-CH has no presence at this stage [#A]

  • Stage 4 — Distribution : Territorial monopoly concessions granted by municipalities; ~700 distribution system operators (DSOs) in Switzerland, ranging from large cantonal utilities to tiny municipal operators; REHN-CH is a mid-large DSO serving Canton Vaud with ~265,000 network users; regulated cost-plus model with WACC-based return approved by ElCom; capital intensity high (network capex/RAB ~3-5% per annum); typical EBIT margin 12-20% on regulated RAB [#A]

  • Stage 5 — Retail supply (energy commodity + services): Electricity retailer facing end customers; partially liberalised in CH; residential and SME customers remain in regulated tariff zone; large commercial/industrial customers can switch to market; margins thin (typically 2-6% EBIT) due to wholesale pass-through, customer-acquisition cost, and credit risk; REHN-CH operates as retailer across its concession footprint [#A]

  • Stage 6 — End customer: Households, SMEs, large industry, public entities in Canton Vaud; electricity consumption in Switzerland ~58 TWh/yr total; per-capita consumption among highest in Europe (~7,000 kWh/yr) due to electric heating and industrial base [#A]

  • Hub-and-spoke caveat: While the chain is structurally linear, Swissgrid acts as a de facto hub for cross-border flows (interconnector management, balancing, RE-guarantee of origin certificates); smart-grid evolution may eventually introduce a data/platform layer, but this is nascent [#I]

§2 — Romande Energie Holding's Position Within the Chain

  • Positional verdict: REHN-CH is a multi-stage integrated regional utility with its economic centre of gravity firmly in regulated distribution (Stage 4), supported by partial upstream generation ownership and a retail supply business serving its concession footprint [#J]

  • Stage occupied — distribution: REHN-CH is the designated DSO for a large portion of Canton Vaud; this is a territorial monopoly with no competitive alternative for network services; the regulated RAB model provides earnings visibility; concession terms run for decades [#J]

  • Stage occupied — generation: REHN-CH holds stakes in hydro generation assets; these are minority positions rather than full ownership of generating capacity; total equity generation capacity attributable to REHN-CH is estimated at several hundred MW (exact stake-weighted MW subject to annual report verification) [#A]

  • Stage occupied — retail supply: REHN-CH acts as default supplier for captive customers (residential, SME) and competes in the open market for large commercial customers; the retail book is margin-thin but customer-retentive due to tariff inertia and switching friction [#A]

  • Upstream integration: Partial — REHN-CH does not own a full generation portfolio but its hydro stakes provide a natural hedge against wholesale price spikes; it is not integrated into fuel supply (no coal/gas upstream) as its generation mix is overwhelmingly hydro [#A]

  • Downstream integration: REHN-CH directly serves end customers (no third-party retailer intermediary), which is typical for Swiss DSOs that bundle network + supply; it is integrating further downstream into energy services (EV, heat pump, solar) but this is early-stage [#A]

  • Peer integration vs. Swiss peers: Large Swiss peers (Axpo, Alpiq, BKW) are more heavily weighted to generation and trading, making them more volatile; mid-tier peers (SIG Geneva, Groupe E Fribourg, EWZ Zurich) are similarly distribution-anchored; REHN-CH's integration depth is broadly peer-consistent for a cantonal DSO [#A]

  • Holding company structure: REHN-CH is the listed holding entity; the operating subsidiary (Romande Energie SA) executes the utility operations; Canton Vaud and Lausanne municipality are reference shareholders (c.60% combined), aligning capital-allocation incentives with network-investment continuity rather than short-term yield maximisation [#A]

  • Multi-stage integration rationale: Integration across generation + distribution + retail is the dominant model for Swiss cantonal utilities because it allows cross-subsidisation of risk, single-customer relationship management, and regulatory legitimacy as a public-interest operator; pure-stage specialists are rare in Swiss context [#A]

  • Coordination benefit: Owning distribution + retail creates data advantages — metering data, consumption profiles, and grid-edge visibility that pure retailers or pure generators lack; this is the foundational asset for future energy-services positioning [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on REHN-CH's multi-stage integration is cross-subsidisation risk masking structural weakness — the regulated distribution RAB may be internally cross-subsidising a loss-making or sub-cost-of-capital retail/generation book; ElCom's ring-fencing requirements mandate accounting separation, but investor visibility into true segment margins is imperfect; through what mechanism might the integration prove a margin drag? If ElCom tightens allowed network returns below cost of capital for an extended period, the distribution anchor weakens and the retail/generation book offers insufficient offset [#I]

§4 — Margin Distribution Across the Chain

  • Margin pool verdict: Distribution (Stage 4) is the dominant profit stage in Swiss electricity, followed by low-cost hydro generation; transmission is regulated away from private ownership; retail supply is structurally thin; energy services are pre-profit [#J]

  • Most-profitable stage — distribution: Swiss DSOs earn regulated returns on network RAB; ElCom-approved WACC historically in the 3.5-5.0% real range on regulated asset base; combined with depreciation and opex recovery, this produces EBIT margins of 12-20% on revenue — the highest-quality, most durable margin in the chain [#A]

  • Margin driver — regulatory: Distribution margins are protected by ElCom regulation; the cost-benchmarking methodology penalises inefficiency but guarantees a return on prudently-invested assets; this is a structural moat, not a competitive one [#J]

  • Most-profitable stage (commercial) — low-cost hydro: Fully amortised run-of-river and reservoir hydro carries near-zero variable cost (water is free, staff minimal); when wholesale electricity prices are elevated (as in 2021-2022, when Swiss spot averaged CHF 160-200/MWh vs. 10-yr average ~CHF 50-60/MWh), EBIT margins on hydro generation can exceed 40-50%; these are volatile, not durable [#A]

  • Margin driver — differentiation: Generation margin is not differentiated — electrons are fungible; margin accrues to cost position (water cost ~zero vs. gas at CHF 60-80/MWh input), not brand or product differentiation [#A]

  • Margin-thin stage — retail supply: ❌ Retail supply EBIT margins are structurally thin: Swiss regulated tariff retail earns ~2-4% EBIT (net of wholesale cost pass-through, network tariff, taxes, and billing overhead); competitive open-market supply to large customers is margin-dilutive without proprietary generation backing [#A]

  • Margin-thin stage — transmission: Transmission (Swissgrid) earns a regulated return but is not available to private equity investors; irrelevant to REHN-CH's margin pool directly [#A]

  • Margin trend — distribution: Swiss network tariffs were subject to ElCom downward pressure in 2022-2025 as ElCom benchmarked DSO costs more aggressively; capital-intensive smart-meter rollout is being partially recovered via allowed capex, but the allowed WACC has been compressed in line with falling risk-free rates; distribution margins are stable but on a mild downward slope vs. 2015 peaks [#A]

  • Margin trend — generation: Hydro generation margins are cyclical around wholesale power prices; European electricity market integration means Swiss spot follows German/French base load with an Alpine premium; post-2022 energy crisis, wholesale prices have normalised, compressing hydro margins back toward long-run average [#I]

  • Margin trend — retail: Full market liberalisation (all Swiss customers permitted to switch supplier) is pending legislative confirmation; if passed, residential margin risk increases as high-value customers exit to competitive retailers, leaving REHN-CH with higher-cost, lower-consumption base [#I]

§5 — Power Dynamics + Pricing Leverage

  • Power dynamics verdict: REHN-CH holds strong structural pricing power at the distribution stage (regulatory determination insulates it from customer pushback) but faces moderate supplier leverage from wholesale power markets and rising customer-exit risk at retail supply as liberalisation progresses [#J]

  • Pricing power — distribution: Effectively unlimited at customer level — network tariffs are set by ElCom, not negotiated; customers cannot refuse to pay or switch provider; pricing power is not competitive but regulatory, which is structurally stronger than market-based pricing power [#J]

  • Pricing power — retail supply (captive): Strong for regulated residential/SME customers — annual ElCom tariff reviews cap price but also protect floor; REHN-CH cannot price-gouge, but neither can customers exit; retail tariff includes network + energy + taxes; total bill is non-negotiable for captive customers [#A]

  • Pricing power — retail supply (liberalised large customers): ❌ Weak — large commercial customers (>100 MWh/yr) have been able to switch to Axpo Retail, Alpiq Retail, or direct-market procurement since 2009; REHN-CH competes on price and reliability; no structural moat; churn risk is real, particularly for large industrial customers where margins are already thin [#A]

  • Customer concentration: Highly diversified — the residential/SME customer base spans ~265,000 network users; no single customer likely exceeds 1-2% of retail supply revenue; concentration risk is low relative to industrial-focused utilities; Swiss utilities sector median for top-customer concentration is <5% of revenue [#A]

  • Switching cost — network: Infinite — there is literally no alternative network for a customer in Canton Vaud; this is the most powerful switching-cost moat in any industry stage [#J]

  • Switching cost — retail supply: Moderate (captive segment) to low (open-market segment); captive residential customers face administrative friction and limited awareness of alternatives; open-market large customers face low switching cost once procurement team is engaged [#A]

  • Substitutes — network: ❌ Nascent but real — large-scale on-site generation + storage can allow industrial customers to operate as islanded micro-grids, bypassing the network tariff obligation (within Swiss regulatory limits); this is a 10-15 year structural threat, not an immediate one [#I]

  • Substitutes — retail supply: Growing — rooftop solar self-consumption, energy communities (Eigenverbrauchsgemeinschaft per revised StromVG), and direct PPAs allow some customers to source a portion of their electricity outside REHN-CH's retail book; this is the most credible near-term substitution threat at retail supply stage [#I]

  • Supplier leverage — wholesale market: ❌ REHN-CH is a net buyer of wholesale electricity (its generation stakes do not fully self-supply its distribution footprint); wholesale prices are set by EPEX Spot (European Power Exchange), Swissgrid balancing markets, and cross-border interconnector flows; REHN-CH has no market power to suppress purchase prices; European spot prices are volatile (range CHF 20-250/MWh in the 2018-2024 window) [#A]

  • Supplier leverage — grid services: Swissgrid sets transmission tariffs but these are regulated and passed through to end customers via ElCom-approved network tariffs; REHN-CH acts as conduit, not margin-bearer, for transmission costs [#A]

  • Regulatory barrier: The concession agreement for distribution is the deepest structural barrier — obtaining a new distribution licence in REHN-CH's territory is essentially impossible; the political economy of Swiss cantonal utilities is strongly protective of incumbents [#J]

  • Segment-specific power — public lighting / municipal services: REHN-CH provides public lighting and metering services to Canton Vaud municipalities; these are captive contracts with very low churn given the concession relationship; pricing is formulaic [#A]

  • Scale economy: REHN-CH's ~265,000 network user base is sub-scale vs. the largest Swiss DSOs (EWZ serves ~250,000 supply points in Zurich alone; SIG serves Geneva canton); however, network capex per customer in Canton Vaud is structurally higher due to the predominantly rural/semi-rural distribution terrain, meaning scale disadvantage is partially offset by terrain-adjusted allowed costs in ElCom benchmarking [#A]

  • Dictated terms — ElCom: ElCom's periodic network tariff reviews are the single most powerful pricing constraint; REHN-CH must demonstrate efficient costs or face disallowance; the efficiency benchmarking methodology penalises operators with above-median costs — REHN-CH's terrain-complexity allowance partially mitigates this [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on power dynamics is a dual squeeze — ElCom compresses distribution tariffs while wholesale power prices spike (requiring REHN-CH to buy expensive in the open market to serve captive customers at regulated retail prices); the 2021-2022 energy crisis demonstrated exactly this transmission-pricing lag, where REHN-CH (and all Swiss DSOs) briefly faced negative retail margins on commodity cost before tariff adjustments were approved by ElCom; the mechanism can recur [#I]

§6 — Competitive Map at Romande Energie Holding's Stage

  • Competitive intensity verdict: At the distribution stage, competition is structurally absent — territorial monopoly concessions preclude head-to-head rivalry; competitive intensity is highest at retail supply where Axpo, Alpiq, and BKW retail arms compete for large customers [#J]

  • Stage concentration — distribution: Switzerland has ~700 DSOs but the competitive dynamics are irrelevant — each operates within a fixed territorial concession; no DSO competes with another for customers in the other's territory; the relevant competition is for ElCom efficiency benchmarks, not for end customers [#J]

  • Competitor — leader (generation): Axpo is Switzerland's largest generator (~40 TWh/yr domestic output), followed by Alpiq and BKW; REHN-CH's hydro stakes make it a minor participant at generation stage, not a leader; the generation stage has a clear three-player oligopoly at scale [#A]

  • Competitor — challenger (distribution, benchmarking): In ElCom's efficiency benchmarking, REHN-CH is compared against peer DSOs of similar size; the most relevant benchmarks are Groupe E (Fribourg canton), EWL (Lucerne), and similar mid-tier cantonal DSOs; if REHN-CH's costs are benchmarked as inefficient, ElCom can disallow portions of its allowed revenue — this is the competitive threat at distribution stage [#A]

  • Scale-cost dimension — distribution: Larger DSOs (EWZ, SIG) benefit from denser urban networks with lower cost-per-km-of-network; REHN-CH's rural/semi-rural terrain in Canton Vaud structurally inflates cost per network user; this is a structural disadvantage in ElCom benchmarking unless terrain adjustment is adequate [#A]

  • Competitor — specialist (retail supply): Alpiq Retail and Axpo Retail operate as specialist retailers competing for large C&I customers across Switzerland; these competitors have stronger generation backing than REHN-CH, enabling competitive pricing on market-based supply to large customers [#A]

  • Geographic dimension: REHN-CH's distribution concession is geographically fixed to Canton Vaud (and some adjacent areas); expansion of the distribution footprint is only possible via M&A of adjacent DSOs — e.g., acquisition of small municipal operators within or adjacent to the concession [#A]

  • Customer-segment dimension: REHN-CH's retail supply competition is confined to the large-customer open-market segment; residential and SME are captive under current law; competitive exposure is therefore proportional to the share of supply revenue from large commercial/industrial accounts [#A]

  • Stage fragmentation — energy services: The energy-services adjacent market (EV, solar, efficiency) is highly fragmented with low barriers to entry; new entrants including solar installers (Enova, Solarify), EV infrastructure operators (Plug Power, ChargePoint, local operators), and energy-management software firms compete; REHN-CH has customer-relationship advantage but not technology leadership [#I]

  • Differentiation dimension: At distribution, differentiation is irrelevant (monopoly); at retail supply, REHN-CH can differentiate on green product labelling (Naturemade Star certified hydro), local brand loyalty (cantonal identity), and bundled service offers; differentiation is modest but real for residential customer retention [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish competitive read at REHN-CH's stage is ElCom benchmarking ratchet risk — as Swiss utilities invest in smart metering and digitise operations, the efficiency frontier rises; REHN-CH's terrain complexity allowance may be challenged or revised downward; through what mechanism might competitive pressure materialise? Not from rival DSOs, but from ElCom adopting a more stringent benchmarking model that reclassifies REHN-CH's cost base as inefficient, triggering a network revenue reduction [#I]

§7 — Competitive Maps at Adjacent Stages + Vertical Integration Threats

  • Adjacent-stage verdict: REHN-CH faces moderate upstream concentration risk (Swissgrid monopoly at transmission; oligopoly at generation) and a structurally intact but liberalising downstream stage; vertical integration threats from suppliers or customers are low probability in the short-to-medium term given regulatory barriers [#J]

  • Supplier — leader (wholesale generation): Axpo is the dominant wholesale electricity seller in Switzerland; Alpiq and BKW follow; REHN-CH is a net buyer from these entities in periods of self-supply shortfall; these three entities have significant market power in bilateral wholesale markets, though the spot market (EPEX Spot) provides some price transparency [#A]

  • Supplier consolidation — generation: Swiss generation is moderately concentrated and unlikely to consolidate further given cantonal ownership structures; Axpo, Alpiq, and BKW are substantially publicly owned; further consolidation would require cantonal political decisions [#A]

  • Supplier — transmission (Swissgrid): Swissgrid is the statutory transmission operator; it charges regulated tariffs; there is no competitive alternative for REHN-CH to procure transmission services; Swissgrid does not have commercial incentive to integrate into distribution and is legally prohibited from doing so under StromVG unbundling rules [#A]

  • Supplier vertical integration threat — generators into distribution: Low probability in short-to-medium term — Axpo, Alpiq, and BKW are already partly integrated (BKW is the most vertically integrated Swiss utility, owning DSO operations in Bern canton alongside generation); however, territorial concession barriers prevent them from entering REHN-CH's Canton Vaud distribution territory without a legislative change [#A]

  • Customer — leader: REHN-CH's customers are dominated by residential households and SMEs; there is no single dominant large customer; the industrial base in Canton Vaud includes food & beverage (Nestlé at Vevey is a large electricity consumer), chemicals, and precision engineering; these large industrials are the most credible customer-exit vector [#A]

  • Customer consolidation: The customer base is structurally fragmented — ~265,000 network users; large customers are not consolidating in a way that increases their individual bargaining power over REHN-CH; Nestlé and similar large corporates have some leverage but represent a small share of total volumes [#A]

  • Customer vertical integration — self-generation: The most credible integration threat from the customer side is large industrial or commercial customers investing in on-site solar + storage to reduce grid dependency; this bypasses REHN-CH's retail supply book and, at sufficient scale, reduces network usage charges (load reduction); the economics are improving with falling solar/storage costs [#I]

  • Customer vertical integration — energy communities: Eigenverbrauchsgemeinschaften (energy communities) under revised Swiss energy law allow groups of customers to self-organise around shared generation (e.g., roof-top solar on apartment blocks, business parks); this is partial disintermediation of REHN-CH's retail supply at the micro-scale; individually small, but cumulatively material over 10 years [#I]

  • Adjacent threat — large-scale PPA bypass: Swiss industrial buyers (Nestlé, ABB, LafargeHolcim) can negotiate direct Power Purchase Agreements with generators (Axpo, Alpiq), bypassing REHN-CH's retail book entirely; the wholesale market mechanism is available to any buyer >100 MWh/yr; REHN-CH's retail margin on these customers is under structural threat [#A]

  • ⚡ RARE: Regulatory unbundling escalation: Switzerland's pending full market liberalisation (repeatedly delayed but politically advancing) represents an adjacent-stage structural change that is more disruptive than typical EU liberalisation cycles because Switzerland is not EU-bound and has unique cantonal utility ownership structures; if full liberalisation proceeds, the legal separation between DSO and retail supply becomes mandatory, and REHN-CH's ability to cross-subsidise retail from distribution profits would be legally removed — this is structurally significant and under-discussed in consensus utility analysis [Cross-ref: §8] [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet: The most credible vertical integration vector is not a competitor entering REHN-CH's territory, but REHN-CH's own large customers internalising generation + storage, effectively climbing up the value chain from customer to micro-generator; the mechanism is economically rational for any Swiss commercial/industrial customer with suitable roof area and capex capacity; the pace is constrained by payback periods (currently 8-12 years for commercial solar + storage in Switzerland), which lengthen if wholesale prices stay low [#I]

§8 — Structural Changes + Evolution

  • Evolution verdict: The Swiss electricity value chain is in a slow but structural transition — from a fully vertically integrated, state-owned, regulated model toward a partially liberalised, decentralised, digitised ecosystem; REHN-CH's distribution moat is durable but the retail supply business faces a decade-long erosion vector [#J]

  • Stage consolidation — distribution: Gradual consolidation of the ~700 DSO landscape is structurally overdue; ElCom's efficiency benchmarking implicitly pressures sub-scale operators to consolidate or partner; REHN-CH, as a mid-large DSO, is a potential consolidator of adjacent smaller operators — this is an upside option [#I]

  • Stage fragmentation — energy services: Energy services (EV, solar, demand response, efficiency) are attracting high new-entrant flow — private-equity-backed solar rollout platforms, technology firms (Tesla Energy, SolarEdge), and pan-European aggregators (Eneco, E.ON) are entering local markets; the energy services stage is fragmenting, not consolidating [#I]

  • Vertical integration trend — DSOs moving downstream: Swiss DSOs (including REHN-CH) are integrating into energy services downstream to defend customer relationships and capture new revenue; BKW is the most advanced (BKW Engineering subsidiary, smart building services, distributed energy); REHN-CH is following this path but at an earlier stage [#A]

  • Disintermediation — energy communities: The revised StromVG (Swiss Electricity Act, partially in force 2023-2025) has formalised the energy community (Eigenverbrauchsgemeinschaft) framework, creating a legal channel for partial retail-supply disintermediation; uptake is slow but the legal foundation is now established [#I]

  • DTC trend — direct corporate PPAs: Large Swiss corporates are negotiating multi-year direct PPAs with generators, bypassing retail supply entirely for baseload; this trend is nascent in Switzerland vs. Nordic or UK markets but structurally follows the same logic [#I]
