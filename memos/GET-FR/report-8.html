§1 — Industry Overview + Value Chain Economics

Getlink sits at the intersection of cross-Channel transport infrastructure, European rail freight, and electricity interconnection — three industry sub-segments with distinct growth, margin and structural dynamics. The tunnel itself is the value chain's fixed, irreplaceable node.

  • Industry verdict: cross-Channel transport is a slow-growth, oligopolistic market with structurally above-inflation tariff potential and a power interconnection adjacency that opens a higher-growth earnings layer; net industry structure is favourable for Getlink [#J]

Industry size:
- Industry size: passenger: the cross-Channel market (all modes: tunnel shuttle + Eurostar + ferry) carried ~30-35 million passengers pa pre-COVID; 2023 recovery to ~28-30m pa; total revenue pool across modes ~€3.5-4.5bn pa [#A]
- Industry size: freight: cross-Channel road freight ~2 million truck units pa; rail freight (Getlink-operated + open-access operators) ~1.3-1.5m units pa; combined freight revenue pool ~€1.5-2.0bn pa [#A]
- Industry size: electricity interconnection: ElecLink (1GW IFA2-parallel cable through the tunnel) is operational since 2022; capacity market value ~€100-250m pa depending on UK/Continental price spread; small pool today but structurally growing as energy transition accelerates [#A]

Growth trajectory:
- Growth trajectory: passenger: L3Y (2022-24) CAGR ~8-12% reflecting post-COVID recovery; N2Y (2025-26) forecast growth moderates to ~2-4% pa as recovery normalises, roughly GDP+ for UK/France [#A]
- Growth trajectory: freight: L3Y CAGR ~2-4%; structural shift to rail freight on carbon grounds supports volume upside but road-to-rail switch is slow in practice; N2Y ~3-5% pa plausible [#A]
- Growth trajectory: electricity: ElecLink revenue highly volatile (spread-dependent) but structural direction positive; European power interconnection investment programme under EU Green Deal targets +100GW of cross-border capacity by 2030 — macro tailwind [#A]
- GDP+: historical: cross-Channel traffic has historically tracked UK-France bilateral trade and leisure demand at ~GDP+0.5-1%; Getlink's revenue exceeds volume growth via real tariff increases [#J]

Structural driver:
- Structural driver: carbon/modal shift: EU and UK decarbonisation targets explicitly favour rail over road freight and short-haul aviation; tunnel rail inherently lower-carbon than either alternative — structural long-term volume tailwind [#J]
- Structural driver: UK-EU trade volume: post-Brexit trade normalisation and any future UK-EU trade facilitation (e.g. customs arrangement evolution) directly affects cross-Channel freight volumes; current drag vs pre-Brexit trajectory is a potential positive catalyst [#A]
- Structural driver: energy transition: cross-Channel electricity price differentials driven by renewable intermittency growth create durable interconnector arbitrage opportunity; ElecLink is positioned directly on this secular trend [#J]

Geographic split:
- Geographic split: the market is definitionally bilateral (UK-France) with no geographic diversification; Eurostar extends to Belgium and Netherlands but ~80%+ of revenues are UK-France corridor [#I]

Concentrating gravitational pull (TC4.RA15):

- Consolidation to advantaged: capital-intensive ferry and tunnel assets naturally consolidate toward operators with balance sheet depth; Getlink's concession structure is the ultimate advantage — cannot be replicated [#J]

Competitor attitudes (TC4.RA16):
- Benign-ness: ferry operators (DFDS, P&O) prioritise ROCE over volume — evidenced by capacity rationalisation post-COVID and P&O's 2022 restructuring which exited volume-maximising labour contracts in favour of cost structure improvement [#J]
- MOL stability: Getlink has not observed material margin-on-lane erosion from ferry competitor price aggression post-2015; ferry pricing has tracked inflation broadly [#A]
- Shark attack risk: low — no competitor has the capital, the access to tunnel capacity, or the political permission to launch a structural price war; even P&O's 2022 crisis (staff dismissals) did not translate to Getlink share-loss once P&O resumed operations [#J]
- Volume vs ROCE: both DFDS and P&O (post-2022 restructuring) prioritise ROCE over volume; there is no value-destroying price competitor in the market [#J]

Competitiveness trends (TC4.RA17):
- Trend direction: net positive — ferry capacity has rationalised post-COVID; Eurostar recovery supports tunnel access revenue; ElecLink opens a new revenue stream without competitive threat; open-access passenger rail (future) is a mild new-entrant signal but primarily incremental to Getlink [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: the benign competitor attitude thesis is premised on high capital costs and rational incumbency — P&O Ferries' 2022 labour-law crisis demonstrated that distressed incumbents can behave irrationally; if P&O or DFDS face financial stress, margin-destructive pricing cannot be ruled out; additionally, if Eurostar's open-access expansion brings a well-capitalised rail operator (e.g. Trenitalia, Renfe) to the London-Paris route, competitive intensity in the highest-margin passenger segment rises

§3 — Barriers to Entry + Exit + Disruption Forces

  • Barriers verdict: barriers to entry in fixed-link infrastructure are among the highest in any European industry — capital requirement, political/treaty necessity, and regulatory complexity combine to make new-entrant risk negligible over any 10Y horizon; disruption risk is more meaningful in adjacent ferry and rail segments [#J]

Capital requirement:
- Capital requirement: new fixed link: a second Channel tunnel or bridge would cost an estimated £10-20bn+ in 2024 money based on comparables (Thames Tideway ~£4.5bn for much smaller infrastructure; HS2 ~£70bn for comparable-scale surface rail); no public or private entity has advanced a credible proposal since the 1990s design-stage alternatives were rejected [#A]
- Capital requirement: ferry entry: moderate at ~€500m-1bn for a new operator fleet; entry is not impossible but economics (thin margins, incumbency) deter rational new entrants [#A]
- Capital requirement: rail operations: open-access passenger rail entry requires rolling stock (~€50-100m for fleet), path access, safety certification, and commercial viability — barrier is moderate for well-capitalised operators (Evolyn, Trenitalia cited publicly as interested) [#A]

Scale economy:
- Scale economy: tunnel operations: fixed costs of tunnel operation (ventilation, safety, maintenance) are largely independent of volume; incremental margin on additional trains/vehicles is very high; Getlink benefits from extreme operating leverage at the infrastructure layer [#J]
- Scale economy: ferry: fleet scale determines port slot access and fuel purchasing advantage; DFDS and P&O hold structural slot advantages at Dover-Calais that cannot be replicated quickly [#A]

Regulatory barrier:
- Regulatory barrier: the Channel Tunnel is governed by a 1987 UK-France Treaty of Canterbury and a concession agreement — any new fixed link would require bilateral treaty renegotiation at the highest government level; this is not a commercial barrier but a sovereign-level barrier [#J] ⚡
- Regulatory barrier: rail access: Getlink is a regulated infrastructure manager; third-party train operators pay access fees set under a regulatory framework; this structure prevents Getlink from excluding competitors but also provides a revenue floor on any increased usage [#A]

Switching cost:
- Switching cost: for car/coach passengers, switching between tunnel and ferry is low (price-sensitive at the margin); for freight shippers with established terminal relationships and scheduling, switching is moderate; for rail freight operators, terminal investment and path slot relationships create moderate lock-in [#A]

Disruption forces (TC3.RA11):
- Disruption force: short-haul aviation cost decline: if aviation decarbonisation stalls and ultra-low-cost carriers cut London-Paris to £30-50 return, tunnel leisure passenger economics could be pressured; currently not the direction of travel (SAF mandates raise aviation costs) [#A]
- Disruption force: autonomous heavy goods vehicles: if autonomous trucking disrupts road freight economics, the demand base for cross-Channel truck slots could shift; this is a 10Y+ risk, not ESA-horizon [#A]
- Disruption force: UK-EU political: Brexit created volume uncertainty; any further UK-EU trade restriction (e.g. tariff escalation) directly reduces freight demand; conversely any UK-EU rapprochement is a positive catalyst [#J]
- Disruption force: Eurostar financial distress: Eurostar required shareholder rescue in 2021 (Getlink and SNCF injected capital); structural recurrence of financial distress at Eurostar would reduce tunnel access revenue [#A]

Adjacencies entering (TC3.RA10.CQ46):
- Adjacency entry: open-access rail operators (Evolyn, Midnight Trains, Trenitalia) are the most credible adjacency entrants into the passenger rail segment; they pay access fees to Getlink (positive) but compete with Eurostar (mixed for Getlink's equity stake) [#A]
- Adjacency entry: power interconnectors: National Grid's IFA2 and the planned North Sea Link demonstrate that electricity interconnectors are a growing adjacent market; ElecLink does not face new tunnel-route competition but could face pricing pressure from expanded total cross-Channel power capacity [#A]

Substitutes (TC3.RA10.CQ49):
- Substitute: short-haul aviation: fastest growing substitute for Eurostar passenger but structurally disadvantaged on city-centre time and carbon; not a tunnel substitute for freight [#A]
- Substitute: ferry (for tunnel): the primary substitute; stable competition for 30 years; economic substitution is marginal and weather/reliability-dependent [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet: the barrier narrative is overwhelming but should not make the analyst complacent — the open-access rail entrant risk is real on a 3-5Y horizon and could structurally dilute Eurostar's margin (and therefore Getlink's equity-accounted contribution); more importantly, political risk from either UK or French governments seeking to renegotiate concession economics (tariff cap, windfall tax on ElecLink profits) is a non-zero risk that capital barriers do not protect against

§5 — Advantaged Business + SRCA Depth

  • Advantage verdict: Getlink's SRCA (Sustainable and Reinforcing Competitive Advantage) is among the most structurally durable in European listed transport infrastructure — the physical concession monopoly creates customer, production, regulatory, and scale moats that mechanically compound; gravitational pull is strong [#J]

MOAT (RA4 — CQ16-CQ22):

  • Customer moat: switching cost (passenger): Getlink's Eurotunnel shuttle (car/coach) is the only fixed-link crossing — switching requires accepting ferry weather-dependency and longer journey time; brand preference + convenience drives repeat usage at ~65% repeat passenger rate [#A]
  • Customer moat: freight shippers: freight shippers with established Just-In-Time supply chains running through Dover-Calais have invested in tunnel-specific scheduling infrastructure; switching to ferry introduces time variability; moderate but real switching cost [#A]
  • Production moat: physical irreplaceability: the tunnel is the only fixed link between UK and Europe; no production substitute exists at any price; this is the deepest production moat in European listed transport [#J] ⚡
  • Production moat: maintenance expertise: 30 years of tunnel operational knowledge, safety protocols, and maintenance scheduling creates a knowledge moat that cannot be rapidly replicated even if a second crossing were to be built [#A]
  • Scale moat: fixed-cost leverage: the tunnel's fixed-cost base (ventilation, maintenance, safety) is substantially invariant to throughput volume; each incremental vehicle or train adds near-pure-contribution margin; this scale advantage is unreplicable for new entrants at a lower cost structure [#J]
  • Regulatory moat: concession to 2086: the 1994-2086 concession (99 years from operational start) is a treaty-backed exclusive operating right; no competitor can legally operate a fixed link on the Dover Strait without a new UK-France bilateral treaty — a sovereign barrier [#J]
  • Culture moat: safety culture is deeply embedded and regulatory-mandatory; this creates a cultural selection effect for talent that values process rigour — similar to nuclear operators, it is not easily disrupted by cost-focused entrants [#A]
  • High relative scale: Getlink's tunnel operations dwarf any ferry operator in per-route capital intensity; Europorte (rail freight subsidiary) is among the top 3 open-access freight operators in France by revenue [#A]
  • Large realistic TAM: cross-Channel TAM is definitionally bounded by the UK-Europe corridor; within that corridor, Getlink captures ~35-40% of total revenue pool today; if open-access rail grows to 10-15% of tunnel capacity, TAM expands; ElecLink adds a structurally different TAM (power interconnection) [#A]

OPERATE (RA5 — CQ23-CQ26):

  • SCU: shuttle capacity utilisation: Eurotunnel shuttle operates at ~70-75% of published capacity in peak periods; below optimal but above the ~60% threshold typically needed for high-fixed-cost infrastructure; post-2024 demand recovery approaching 2019 peak levels [#A]
  • SCU: Eurostar utilisation: Eurostar utilisation recovered to ~80-85% of 2019 seat-km levels in 2023; full 2019 capacity restoration pending rolling stock delivery (new Siemens trains ordered) [#A]
  • RSCU: relative service cost per unit: Getlink's cost per vehicle-crossing is structurally below ferry operators on like-for-like basis due to fixed-cost leverage; specific RSCU benchmarks are not publicly disclosed but cost-per-lane-metre data from company filings supports non-inferiority vs ferry [#A]
  • Pricing power utilised: Getlink has applied above-inflation tariff increases consistently since 2016; freight tariffs rose ~5-8% pa in 2022-23 vs UK CPI ~5-7%; passenger shuttle prices rose broadly in line with inflation; evidence of real pricing power application, not just conjecture [#J]
  • Scale economies shared: Getlink does not explicitly share scale economies with customers (this is a concession, not a competitive-market operator pursuing customer loyalty via price); economies are retained as margin rather than competed away — appropriate for a monopoly infrastructure operator [#J]

GENERATE (RA6 — CQ27-CQ36):

  • Revenue growth: L3Y revenue CAGR ~15-20% (COVID recovery driven); N2Y consensus ~4-7% pa normalised; structural baseline ~4-6% pa (volume + inflation tariff) plus ElecLink volatility layer [#A]
  • EPS growth: L3Y EPS CAGR has been distorted by COVID recovery phasing; N3Y normalised EPS CAGR consensus ~8-12% pa; ElecLink upside scenarios push to 12-15% if spreads remain elevated [#A]
  • Immediate scope to deploy capex at high RONIC: post-ElecLink, the large-capex cycle is substantially complete; maintenance capex ~€150-200m pa; limited new growth capex identified at 20%+ RONIC scale in N3Y [#A] ❌
  • Post-tax RONIC: ElecLink cable RONIC estimated 15-25%+ post-tax (spread-dependent); core shuttle RONIC is lower (~8-12%) given concession debt structure; blended RONIC ~12-18% — solid for infrastructure, below the 25-30%+ BB#2 threshold [#A] ❌
  • NI to FCF conversion: Getlink's FCF conversion has been ~70-80% of EBITDA post-maintenance capex; concession amortisation accounting (non-cash) creates reported NI-to-FCF divergence; true cash generation is strong [#A]
  • Post-tax ROCE: ROCE is suppressed by the large concession intangible asset base (~€7-8bn gross book value); post-tax ROCE ~6-9% — below the BB#2 15% threshold on reported figures; on tangible asset or incremental basis the picture is more favourable [#A] ❌
  • Post-tax ROTC: ROTC (Return on Total Capital including intangibles) is in line with ROCE given capital-light operational structure above the concession base; ~8-12% est [#A]
  • Gross margins: tunnel operations gross margin ~75-80% (high fixed-cost infrastructure standard); consolidated gross margin ~60-65% including Eurostar consolidation effects [#A]
  • EBIT margins: consolidated EBIT margin ~30-35%; Eurotunnel standalone EBIT margin ~45-50%; Eurostar drags consolidated margin [#A]
  • Operating leverage: genuine and strong — incremental EBIT margin on additional tunnel volume ~70-80%; this is one of the purest operating leverage structures in listed European transport [#J]
  • Low tax rate / EPS leakage: French corporate tax rate ~25%; UK operations subject to UK CT ~25%; no structural low-tax advantage; concession amortisation provides significant tax shield reducing effective rate to ~18-22% in some periods [#A]

Gravitational pull (RA7 — CQ37-CQ38):

  • Gravitational improvement: SRCA vs peers: each year that passes without a competitor fixed link (which will never be built in any ESA/DD-horizon period) strengthens Getlink's relative concession value; volume recovery post-COVID mechanically improves unit economics through fixed-cost leverage — no heroic management action required [#J]
  • Gravitational improvement: financial outputs: as ElecLink spreads normalise to a structural level (rather than the 2022-23 spike), revenue visibility improves; freight volume secular growth mechanically compounds; the fixed-cost leverage ensures each percentage point of volume growth delivers ~1.5-2x EBIT growth — a mechanical force, not heroic dynamism [#J]

Improvement scope (RA8 — CQ39-CQ42):

  • Peer precedent: comparable infrastructure concessionaires (Vinci Airports, Ferrovial, Abertis) have demonstrated 15-20%+ EBIT margin improvement cycles following major capex completion phases, as fixed costs are absorbed and utilisation grows; Getlink is entering this post-capex phase [#A]
  • Big savings: the most material improvement opportunity is Eurostar cost structure — post-COVID Eurostar restructured (2022-23) reducing headcount and renegotiating catering/maintenance contracts; estimated €50-100m pa structural cost improvement vs pre-COVID base [#A]
  • Many small savings: terminal efficiency, dynamic freight pricing optimisation, digital booking conversion improvement, energy consumption reduction via tunnel ventilation optimisation — multiple sub-€20m pa opportunities across the operational base [#A]

  • Plausibility from strategy: Leriche's strategy explicitly targets these improvement vectors; the commercial leadership hire and digital investment are congruent with the revenue improvement playbook; plausibility is high [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: the GENERATE metrics on ROCE (6-9%) and RONIC blended (~12-18%) are materially below BB#2 ideals — this is a structural feature of concession-accounting, not a cycle effect; BB#2 will likely grade TC2.RA6 as a relative weakness; the MOAT (CQ17 production monopoly) partially compensates but does not fully offset the financial-quality gap vs capital-light compounders; the improvement scope narrative depends on ElecLink spreads not normalising to very low levels (which would remove the highest-RONIC asset's contribution)
