1. INDUSTRY OVERVIEW

The European health and fitness market reported record membership of 75.5 million at year-end 2025, a 5.8% increase year-on-year. Revenue grew 9.1% to €39.1bn. Both metrics surpassed previous records. The top 20 operators collectively generated €7.4bn in 2024, up 15% YoY, accounting for roughly 19% of total market revenue. The remaining 81% is fragmented across thousands of independent and mid-sized operators.

Overall European population penetration stands at 9.3% (10.6% of the adult population aged 15+). This is significantly below the United States, which operates at approximately 20-22% adult penetration. The gap represents structural growth potential. Country-level penetration varies markedly. Norway and Sweden sit at 19-21%, reflecting decades of fitness culture, government wellness promotion, and high per-capita incomes. The Netherlands and Belgium are at approximately 16-17%. The UK sits near 15%. France trails at 12-14%, though Basic-Fit's aggressive expansion is driving rapid membership growth there. Germany is approximately 15-18%.

The budget/low-cost gym segment has outperformed the broader market across every year since 2011. Club count in the low-cost category compounded at over 50% CAGR from 2011 to 2019 in key markets. Post-pandemic, the trend resumed. Affordability positioning is counter-cyclical: high-inflation environments in 2022-23 accelerated a value-seeking shift from premium gyms to budget operators. The average budget gym membership runs at €20-25/month in continental Europe and £18-25/month in the UK — roughly one-third of the cost of mid-market operators.

Three structural demand drivers underpin the growth. First, health consciousness is rising across all age cohorts, accelerated by the pandemic's focus on physical resilience. Second, the inflationary cost-of-living squeeze has made budget gyms the logical substitution for premium gym memberships. Third, the 18-34 demographic is the core budget gym user and is expanding as a share of urban population across all key markets.

Digital fitness proved a temporary competitor. Peloton and Mirror captured significant consumer interest and media narrative during 2020-21. By 2022-25, the narrative reversed. Peloton revenue contracted three consecutive years. The gym industry recovered and exceeded pre-pandemic membership levels. Consumer research consistently shows that most gym users prefer in-person training environments for accountability, social context, and equipment access unavailable at home. Digital/app fitness settled as a complementary offering, not a substitute.

Consolidation is progressing, though markets remain nationally fragmented. Basic-Fit operates across Netherlands, Belgium, Luxembourg, France, Spain, Germany (via Clever Fit). PureGym operates UK, Switzerland, and has entered Europe. McFIT (RSG Group) dominates Germany alongside Clever Fit. Fitness World operates Denmark. In the UK, PureGym, The Gym Group, and JD Gyms constitute the top three budget operators. The Nordics are dominated by SATS with limited pan-European penetration by global players.

Geographic fragmentation persists at the national level due to language, regulatory, and consumer preference differences. There is no single pan-European brand equivalent to Planet Fitness in the US. BFIT is the closest proxy, operating across six countries. Pan-European scale is likely 5-10 years away from being fully realised.

2. VALUE CHAIN AND ECONOMICS

The gym operator sits in the middle of a three-stage value chain: upstream (landlords, equipment suppliers, fit-out contractors) → operator → downstream (individual members, corporate wellness accounts, health insurers).

Landlords lease commercial space — typically former retail stores, retail parks, standalone units, or repurposed industrial — to gym operators under long-term leases of 10-20 years. The post-2019 structural decline in retail has created a favourable supply environment for gym operators. Landlords in secondary retail parks and former department store units have actively sought gym tenants as stable, long-term, traffic-generating occupants. This has driven improved lease terms in many markets: rent-free fit-out periods of 6-12 months, landlord contributions to fit-out capital (sometimes €100-300k per site), and competitive base rents. Prime high-street and shopping centre locations retain landlord pricing power; secondary locations favour the operator.

Rent as a percentage of revenue is the central economics variable. Pre-IFRS 16, typical budget gym rent runs at 18-25% of revenue for a mature club. Under IFRS 16, operating leases are capitalised as right-of-use assets with corresponding lease liabilities, and the P&L shows depreciation plus interest rather than rent. This inflates EBITDA by the full rent amount, inflates assets and liabilities, and creates the perception of higher profitability and higher debt simultaneously. All three operators report both pre-IFRS 16 and post-IFRS 16 metrics. Investors must use EBITDA less rent (or EBITDA before IFRS 16) for comparability.

Equipment supply is moderately concentrated. The major suppliers are Technogym (Italy, premium), Life Fitness , Precor (US, Arden portfolio), and Matrix (Johnson Health Tech). Budget gym operators lean toward Life Fitness, Matrix, and Precor for cardiovascular and resistance equipment, with Technogym more prevalent in premium segments. Switching costs are moderate: equipment is interchangeable, but operator relationships, volume discounts, and maintenance contracts create some stickiness. Equipment is a significant upfront capex component (£300-600k per site) and a lower-margin business for suppliers than the operator's core membership economics.

Fit-out contractors are local/regional specialists. Operators at scale (BFIT, PureGym, GYM) have gained purchasing power through high-volume rollout programmes. All-in capex per new club runs at approximately €800k-€1.5m for BFIT (continental), £1.0-1.5m for GYM, and NOK 7-12m for SATS. This is considerably below the €2m-5m for a mid-market gym. Costs have been driven down through standardised fit-out specifications, scale procurement, and competition among fit-out suppliers for volume contracts.

Variable vs fixed cost structure skews heavily fixed for budget gym operators. Once a club is open, the primary cost variables are: staff (which budget operators minimise via 24/7 staffless models), utilities (electricity for HVAC, lighting, equipment; estimated 8-12% of revenue), consumables (cleaning, maintenance), and marketing. Rent (fixed lease) and depreciation represent 40-50% of total operating cost at a club level. Labour at budget operators is a small fraction compared to premium gyms. BFIT has 0.5-1.0 FTE per club in some markets. GYM employs approximately 4-5 FTE per site. SATS, as a higher-service model, runs 8-12 FTE per site due to group exercise instructors and reception.

Unit economics at maturity: a budget club opened at £1.2m capex in the UK reaches EBITDA-positive within 12-18 months. Mature EBITDA (pre-IFRS 16 adjusted) at a GYM Group site runs at approximately 47-50% for the highest-performing cohorts. Average across the estate is 35-40%. At these margins, a £1.2m investment earning £500k EBITDA represents a roughly 40% cash-on-cash yield at maturity — extraordinary economics for a physical asset-light business. BFIT's per-club economics are similar at scale; SATS earns lower per-club absolute margins but charges higher ARPM.

The downstream customer has no intermediary in the direct-to-consumer model. Members join online or in-club, pay monthly or annually, and interact directly with the operator. B2B corporate wellness is a distinct channel, most developed for SATS, where employers subsidise memberships and SATS invoices corporates directly. This provides lower churn, higher LTV customers, and a degree of volume stability absent from pure direct-to-consumer models.

3. COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE

Pan-European and UK. Basic-Fit is Europe's largest operator by membership (6.0m members, 2,184 clubs including Clever Fit franchise network). PureGym is second by club count (~600 clubs, 2.1m members), with a UK-dominant position and growing Swiss and European footprint. RSG Group (McFIT) owns approximately 300+ clubs across Germany and continental Europe. Clever Fit, now part of BFIT, has 454 franchise clubs primarily in Germany/DACH. Actic (Sweden, now part of BFIT indirect) and Fitness World (Denmark) complete the top tier.

UK market. PureGym, The Gym Group, and JD Gyms are the three national budget chains. PureGym is the UK market leader with approximately 600 UK clubs and is accelerating to 300+ more sites. GYM Group operates 245 sites and remains the lowest-cost national chain (Standard memberships at £25.10 vs PureGym at broadly comparable pricing). JD Gyms, part of the JD Sports Group, operates approximately 50-60 clubs and is growing fast. Anytime Fitness (franchise) has approximately 200+ UK locations. The budget segment accounts for approximately 41% of UK private gym memberships. Mid-market operators (Nuffield, Virgin Active, David Lloyd) face continued market share erosion to budget operators from below and premium boutique studios from above.

Nordics. SATS is the dominant operator across Norway, Sweden, Denmark, and Finland with 274 clubs and 757k members. Fresh Fitness (branded under SATS) serves the lower-cost tier within Norway. Key competitors are Actic (Sweden/Norway, mid-tier), Fitness World (Denmark), and the growing Treningshelse/Sporty brand in Norway with 93+ locations. Fitness World has a significant position in Denmark (~170 clubs). The Norwegian market is the most penetrated fitness market in Europe (19-21%) and approaching saturation at the mainstream segment; growth is driven by switching from inactive to active rather than from competitor switching.

Continental Europe. France is BFIT's largest market with 800+ clubs. Competition is fragmented: Neoness, Moving, and independent operators. The 24/7 staffless model that BFIT pioneered in Benelux is now being extended to France following Q1-26 regulatory approval. Spain is BFIT's next growth frontier. Germany (via Clever Fit franchise) gives BFIT approximately 450-500 club presence instantly.

Competitive intensity. The budget gym segment is experiencing rising competitive intensity in its most mature markets (UK, Netherlands/Belgium). However, competition manifests primarily in location density rather than price. No major budget operator has embarked on a price war: all operate in the €18-27/month range with limited differentiation. The structural growth in membership volumes means that competitive intensity has not yet translated into material margin compression for established scale operators. New site ROICs remain healthy.

Peloton and digital. Peloton's collapse post-pandemic (revenue -22% FY2023, continued contraction) validated the view that at-home fitness is not a structural competitor to budget gyms. Apple Fitness+ and other app-based platforms generate engagement but do not demonstrably substitute gym attendance.

4. BARRIERS TO ENTRY AND EXIT

Capital. Opening a single budget gym requires £1-1.5m in the UK and €0.8-1.5m on the continent. Scaling to a network of 50+ clubs requires £50-75m of incremental capital. This is not insurmountable for a well-capitalised entrant, but it creates a meaningful barrier for entrepreneurs and small operators. The major threat to incumbents comes from well-capitalised national or international rollout players (PureGym entering new markets, JD Gyms accelerating), not from single-site independents.

Location. Finding appropriate space in catchment areas is a growing constraint. Budget gym operators need 1,000-2,000 sqm in accessible locations with good car parking. The supply of such units was abundant post-2019 as retail vacancy rose, but it is tightening as operators claim the best sites. Incumbents that have secured prime catchment areas have a defensible first-mover advantage: a second budget gym rarely opens within 500m of an established operator because the economics are mutually destructive.

Brand and switching costs. Member inertia is real but driven by proximity, not brand loyalty per se. A member switches if a competitor opens closer. NPS for budget gym operators is moderate (not high); the product is largely undifferentiated at the basic level. However, GYM Group has invested heavily in app functionality, class booking, and digital member engagement, creating incremental switching costs via the digital relationship.

Technology. Club management systems (access control, membership management, payment processing) are off-the-shelf. The differentiation sits in the customer-facing app, virtual class libraries, and data analytics for club-level optimisation. BFIT, GYM, and SATS all have proprietary app platforms; this represents a moderate barrier against smaller operators but not against well-resourced competitors.

Regulatory. 24/7 staffless operation requires country-specific regulatory permits. France only approved this in Q1-26, giving BFIT a significant cost advantage over competitors who will face the same regulatory approval timeline. Germany has varying state-level rules. This regulatory complexity creates a time-based barrier: early movers gain 12-24 months of cost advantage before competitors can replicate.

Exit. Lease commitments create high exit barriers. A 15-year lease signed on a club that subsequently underperforms is a significant sunk cost. Operators cannot easily exit poor-performing locations without lease assignment or sublet. This manifests as a risk on the downside (forced to operate loss-making clubs) and as a barrier to entry on the upside (competitors cannot lease a site that is contractually occupied).

6. BUSINESS MODEL DIFFERENCES

BFIT operates a highly standardised, low-cost, high-volume model. A typical BFIT club runs 24/7 (in Benelux, increasingly France), offers no personal training or group classes as standard, charges €19-25/month (country-dependent), and runs with minimal staffing. The value proposition is: clean facility, good equipment, unlimited access, at the lowest price in the market. Revenue per club is relatively modest (approximately €830k annualised at the FY25 run rate), but with 1,710 clubs and lean cost structure, aggregate economics are compelling. The franchise model addition via Clever Fit is capital-light: BFIT earns franchise fees and royalties without owning the club assets.

GYM Group operates a similar no-frills model in the UK but has made technology a genuine differentiator. The GYM Group app offers class bookings, workout tracking, and virtual class access. Members access over 245 sites. ARPMM of £21.60 is at the lower end of the budget segment; the Standard membership headline price moved to £25.10 in June 2025. GYM has also developed a PT (personal training) marketplace: self-employed PTs pay GYM a commission/licence fee, generating ancillary revenue without staffing cost. This capital-light ancillary model is underappreciated.

SATS occupies the premium end of the budget segment. Clubs are typically 2,000-5,000 sqm, staffed during peak hours, offer a full group exercise timetable, have pools in some locations (ELIXIA brand, higher-end), and charge NOK 600-700/month. The business model is meaningfully different from BFIT/GYM: higher revenue per member, higher service cost, but also a more defensible position in markets where the value proposition is not purely price. Corporate wellness is a meaningful B2B revenue source for SATS, providing lower-churn, higher-LTV customer cohorts.

Which model has the best unit economics? At pure margin level, BFIT's 24/7 staffless model is the most capital-efficient once franchise fees are stripped out. GYM's mature site ROIC of 27% (30% ex problematic sites) is outstanding and approaching BFIT's comparable metric. SATS has lower club-level ROIC due to higher capex (larger clubs, more services), but earns higher absolute ARPM. Best scalability: BFIT's franchise model, once operational, is the most scalable — capital-light, geographically replicable, management-light.

7. KEY SUCCESS FACTORS

Member retention is the single most important operational lever. Budget gym economics depend on volume. Churn drives the need for constant new member acquisition. The industry rule of thumb is that new members are most at risk of lapsing in the first 45 days (the "45-day cliff"). Operators run automated engagement workflows — check-in prompts, class bookings, welcome messages — to get new members through the first six weeks. Operators that achieve higher retention require lower gross member adds to maintain or grow net membership. GYM has invested heavily in its app engagement infrastructure to reduce early churn.

ARPM growth is the P&L multiplier. For GYM Group, a £1/month increase in ARPMM generates £8.3m in annual revenue with near-100% flow-through to EBITDA (zero incremental cost). The ability to raise prices 3-5%/year without churn is the difference between a good and a great budget gym business. Evidence so far: BFIT grew ARPM from €24.24 to €24.91 (+2.8%) in FY25. GYM Group grew ARPMM from £20.44 to £21.60 (+5.7% H1 to H1). SATS grew ARPM 7% in Q1-25, moderating to 3% in Q4-25. Pricing power is real but not unlimited.

New club ramp speed determines capital efficiency. A club that reaches 80% of mature membership in 12 months generates ROIC materially faster than one that takes 24 months. Club ramp is driven by: location quality, local marketing intensity, population density, and existing brand awareness in the catchment. GYM Group's accelerating opening pace (16 new FY25, 20-22 planned FY26) is a bet on continued strong ramp performance from the new cohort.

Operating leverage manifests as the mature club base grows relative to the expanding-club headcount. A business with 245 clubs has significantly better operating leverage than one with 100 clubs. Central costs (tech platform, marketing, management) are spread across more club revenue. This is why BFIT's EBITDA margin has been structurally expanding even as revenue growth has required ongoing club capex.

Brand NPS and digital engagement are rising in importance. Members who actively use the gym operator's app book classes, track progress, and engage with the PT marketplace. Higher engagement correlates with lower churn. GYM Group reports strong app uptake. SATS runs a hybrid physical-digital model with SATS Online. BFIT's app is more basic but is being upgraded progressively.

8. INDUSTRY EVOLUTION AND STRUCTURAL RISKS

Technology disruption risk is lower than feared. The Peloton episode demonstrated the limits of at-home fitness as a gym substitute for the mass market. AI personal training (form feedback, workout prescription via camera-based analysis) is emerging but is a gym amenity enhancement, not a substitute. Wearables data integration is additive to gym engagement, not a competitor. The structural risk from technology over the next 5 years is minimal for budget gym operators.

Regulatory risk is country-specific and primarily upside. France's approval of 24/7 staffless operation for certain BFIT clubs in Q1-26 is a €10m annual cost saving. This regulatory evolution mirrors what occurred in Benelux in the 2010s. Jurisdictions that follow (Germany, Spain) will progressively open the door to staffless models. Regulatory reversal is theoretically possible but practically unlikely given the safety track record of staffless operations.

Consolidation at the operator level is accelerating. BFIT's Clever Fit acquisition is the clearest evidence. The European budget gym market is moving toward 3-4 national champions per country with one dominant pan-European operator . Mid-tier operators face a squeeze: they are too expensive for budget consumers and not experiential enough for premium consumers. The M&A opportunity set for BFIT and PureGym to acquire distressed mid-tier chains is expanding.

Pricing ceiling risk is real in the most mature markets. Norwegian gym membership penetration is approaching 20%+. The next wave of SATS members are harder to acquire and more price-sensitive. BFIT's Benelux markets are well-penetrated; membership growth there is slower than France. However, volume growth (getting more visits per existing member, attracting non-gym users) and ARPM growth can compensate for slowing net new member additions.

Energy cost structure: large gym facilities (10,000+ sqm in some BFIT clubs) consume significant electricity for HVAC, lighting, cardio equipment, and showering facilities. The 2022-23 European energy crisis created material cost headwinds. Costs have partially normalised. BFIT hedges approximately 75% of energy needs. SATS and GYM face similar dynamics but have smaller per-club footprints. Energy is a 5-10% revenue cost and a watch item, not an existential threat.

9. SCEPTICAL VIEW

Bull: Budget gyms are counter-cyclical. Bear response: The evidence is mixed. During the 2008-09 recession, gym membership did decline globally. Planet Fitness and the budget model were in earlier stages in 2008; the current budget gym ecosystem is more developed. However, a severe recession involving widespread consumer income stress would likely see some membership churn even at £20-25/month. The "cost-of-living" counter-cyclical argument holds for mild recessions where consumers trade down from premium; it does not hold in deep downturns where all discretionary spending contracts.

Bull: ARPM can compound 5%+/year. Bear response: Evidence from BFIT (+2.8% FY25) and SATS (+3% Q4-25) suggests growth is decelerating toward the 3% range. GYM Group's ARPMM history shows 7% FY24 to 4% FY25. The budget segment faces a structural constraint: price increases above general wage growth risk triggering churn, particularly in lower-income member cohorts who account for a disproportionate share of budget gym membership. At a £25/month price point, even a £2/month increase is an 8% rise. Budget gym members are price-sensitive.

Bull: Market penetration can reach 30%+ across Europe. Bear response: Norway is at approximately 20% and is already showing membership growth deceleration. The Netherlands and Belgium, BFIT's home markets, are mature. Achieving broad European penetration of 30% requires converting non-gym users who have no current gym habit — a fundamentally different and harder task than converting premium gym users or lapsed members. Habit formation is slow. Penetration in France (12-14%) has room to grow, but the conversion cost is high.

Bull: Operating leverage accelerates margins significantly. Bear response: BFIT's EBITDA margin grew only 11% despite 17% revenue growth in FY25. Margin leverage is slower than the model suggests because energy costs, rent escalations in lease renewals, and staff cost inflation offset fixed cost leverage. The path from 24.5% to 30%+ adjusted EBITDA margin is realistic but requires years of controlled expansion, not rapid rollout.

Structural position ratings:
- BFIT: YELLOW — scale leader, franchising pivot is strategically correct, but integration risk, leverage, and execution dependency on 2026 guidance are real
- GYM: GREEN — cleanest balance sheet, strongest ROIC trend, technology differentiation, clear UK runway
- SATS: YELLOW — premium Nordic position is defensible but CMD target is ambitious, Treningshelse is growing, and NOK currency limits international investor appeal
