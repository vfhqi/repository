§1 — General (Description, Financials, Peer Comparison)

J-front verdict: Beijer Alma is a Swedish SEK 12–14bn market-cap industrial holding company; its financial profile is solid but not exceptional — mid-teens EBIT margins and mid-teens ROCE lag best-in-class European components peers by ~5–8pp [#J]

Description and Organisation

  • Description: Beijer Alma operates two distinct industrial businesses — Lesjöfors (spring and pressings manufacturing, ~75–80% of group EBIT) and Habia Cable (specialty cable and connector systems, ~20–25% of group EBIT) [#I]
  • Lesjöfors manufactures standard and custom springs, wire forms, flat springs, and pressed parts; distributes from stock (standard) and manufactures to spec (custom)
  • Habia Cable produces specialty cables for nuclear power, defence, offshore, and rail — niche applications requiring extreme environmental performance
  • Organisation: Beijer Alma AB is the listed parent (BEIA B); it operates as a holding company with decentralised management structures within each operating subsidiary [#I]
  • Lesjöfors has grown through ~30+ acquisitions since 1990s and operates ~30 manufacturing/distribution sites across Europe, Asia, and North America
  • Habia Cable is headquartered in Amal, Sweden, with sites in Sweden, Germany, and China
  • Listing: BEIA B (B-share, Stockholm Nasdaq OMX; SEK); BEIA A also listed but illiquid; primary reporting currency SEK [#I]
  • The Lindqvist family (founding family) controls the company through A-shares; institutional free float dominated by Swedish institutional holders (AMF, Handelsbanken Fonder, etc.)
  • Dual-class structure limits shareholder activism recourse — a structural governance consideration for the reader

  • 2023 organic decline of ~8–10% was the steepest since 2009, driven by automotive and electronics destocking; consistent with European spring sector declines observed at peers

  • 2024 recovery was modest (+3%) — market-level recovery slower than management had guided at start of year

  • Gross margins: Not separately disclosed in granular detail; estimated ~35–40% (Lesjöfors) and ~20–25% (Habia Cable) based on segment-level disclosures and broker triangulation [AS-Berenberg] [#A]

§2 — Company Details (Revenue Model, Business Model, Recent Delivery, Internal Change)

J-front verdict: Revenue is split ~72/28 between Lesjöfors and Habia Cable; the model derives its quality from Lesjöfors' repeat-order OEM base and M&A compounding — but both businesses face structural headwinds in their key end-markets heading into 2025–26 [#J]

Revenue Model

  • Revenue source: Two reportable segments — Lesjöfors Springs (industrial and vehicle springs, distribution + custom manufacturing) and Habia Cable (specialty cable/connectors for nuclear, defence, rail, offshore) [#I]
  • Lesjöfors: ~SEK 3,400–3,600m revenue (2024); Habia Cable: ~SEK 950–1,000m revenue (2024)

  • Lesjöfors revenue breakdown: ~55% standard/distribution springs, ~45% custom/engineered springs (higher margin, stickier)

  • Geography: Lesjöfors operates across ~30 countries; Europe ~70% of Lesjöfors revenue (UK, Germany, Scandinavia, Benelux largest); North America ~15%; Asia ~15% [#I]

  • Habia Cable: ~60% Europe (Sweden/Germany), ~20% Asia (China), ~20% rest of world

  • No single country >25% of group revenue — geographic diversification is genuine

  • End-market: Lesjöfors end-markets — automotive/vehicle (~30%), general industrial machinery (~35%), electronics/consumer (~20%), construction/other (~15%) [#A]

  • Habia Cable end-markets — nuclear/defence (~55%), offshore/oil and gas (~20%), rail (~15%), industrial (~10%)

  • The nuclear/defence mix of Habia Cable is both its niche moat and its strategic complexity for a potential buyer

  • Cyclicality: Lesjöfors is moderately cyclical — automotive and electronics are the highest-beta segments; revenue fell ~8–12% in 2023, recovered modestly in 2024 [#A]

  • Habia Cable is less cyclical (nuclear maintenance cycles are long and programme-driven) but has lower growth; order-driven with lumpy defence/nuclear contract wins

  • Predictability: Moderate; Lesjöfors' standard-stock distribution business (55% of Lesjöfors revenue) provides baseline predictability; custom orders add lumpiness [#A]

  • Habia Cable backlog/order book provides 6–12 month revenue visibility — better than typical capital goods businesses

  • Driver count: Lesjöfors driven by European industrial capex, automotive production volumes, electronics demand, and M&A activity; multiple drivers reduce single-point risk but all correlate during downturns [#A]

Business Model — Customer Side

  • Customer behaviour: Lesjöfors customers are predominantly OEM manufacturers and industrial maintenance buyers; springs are a low-cost, high-criticality component (cost ~0.5–2% of finished product, criticality high) — classic high-switching-cost profile [#A]
  • Custom engineered springs: qualification processes (6–18 months), drawing approvals, tooling investment create genuine switching costs; churn of custom clients is very low (estimated <5% pa) [AS-Berenberg]

  • Standard distribution: lower switching costs but local service, inventory depth, and technical support create stickiness — Lesjöfors' scale advantage in inventory breadth (~30,000 SKUs) is the moat here

  • Switching cost: Custom spring qualification is a genuine lock-in mechanism; automotive OEM customers rarely re-qualify spring suppliers mid-programme (5–7 year vehicle platform cycles) [#A]

  • Habia Cable: nuclear and defence cable certifications are highly specific; approved vendor lists change infrequently; switching costs are among the highest in the specialty cable space

  • Lock-in: Platform programme lock-in (automotive), certification lock-in (Habia nuclear/defence), and inventory depth lock-in (Lesjöfors distribution) are the three primary mechanisms [#J]

  • NPS / churn: No formal NPS disclosures; broker checks ([AS-Carnegie]) suggest Lesjöfors custom segment churn well below 5% pa; long-term relationships of 10–20+ years are cited in management presentations [#I]

Business Model — Scale Economies

  • Scale moat: Lesjöfors' pan-European inventory depth (~30,000 SKUs, stocked across 30 sites) is a scale advantage a regional competitor cannot easily replicate; local competitors typically hold 3,000–8,000 SKUs [#A]

  • Habia Cable's scale is modest — it is a niche specialist, not a scale player; scale advantages are limited within specialty cable

  • Operating leverage: Lesjöfors has moderate fixed-cost leverage (manufacturing overhead per unit falls with volume); the 2023 volume decline demonstrated this negatively — EBIT margins fell ~2.5pp on ~8% revenue decline, implying ~30% operational gearing [#A]

  • Habia Cable: higher fixed cost base relative to revenue (cable manufacturing equipment, certifications); less operating leverage on the upside

Recent Delivery — Last Three Years

  • Recent delivery (2022): Revenue +16% (M&A + strong post-COVID industrial demand recovery); EBIT margins +0.5pp; strong FCF; net debt/EBITDA below 0.8x — best year in recent history [#A]

  • Recent delivery (2023): Revenue -6% (organic -8–10%, M&A +2–4%); EBIT margins fell to ~11% — worst margin year since 2015; driven by destocking across automotive and electronics; management cited input cost inflation partially absorbed rather than passed through [#A] ❌

  • This was the clearest evidence of imperfect pricing power — management was unwilling/unable to pass through all cost inflation during a volume downturn

  • Recent delivery (2024): Revenue +3% (organic ~flat to +1%, M&A +2–3%); EBIT margin recovery to ~12%; management described market as "stabilising" but not yet recovering [#A]

  • Habia Cable showed volume improvement driven by nuclear maintenance projects and early defence order recovery; Lesjöfors recovery more muted

  • Recent delivery (2025 YTD): Q1-Q3 2025 data suggests continued gradual recovery; organic growth ~+2–3% YTD; margins tracking toward ~15% EBITDA for full year [AS-SEB] [#A]

Internal Change — L12M and N3Y

  • Leadership change: CEO Cecilia Felton appointed March 2021 (succeeded Peter Schön who held the role for 10+ years); Felton came from a background in Swedish industrial M&A and operational improvement [#I]

  • Felton's tenure has been defined by: (1) disciplined M&A continuation in Lesjöfors, (2) strategic review of Habia Cable, (3) margin improvement focus post-2023 downturn

  • Strategic review: Habia Cable strategic review announced Q3 2024; options include sale, JV, or retention with accelerated investment; no resolution as of Q1 2026 [AS-Carnegie] [#J]

  • This is the single most important near-term catalyst for the investment case; resolution would remove a significant overhang and allow re-rating

  • M&A activity (N3Y): Lesjöfors completed ~3–5 bolt-on acquisitions 2022–2024, including entries into Southeast Asia and reinforcement of UK market position; total M&A spend ~SEK 400–600m over period [#I]

  • No transformative (>SEK 1bn) acquisitions executed; discipline maintained on price discipline (reportedly declining deals at >8–9x EBITDA)

  • Financial goals: Management targets: organic growth above market, EBIT margin improvement to 13–15%+, leverage below 1.5x net debt/EBITDA, and progressive dividend [AS-Handelsbanken] [#I]

Sceptical bullet: The business model moat in Lesjöfors is real but narrower than it first appears — automotive destocking showed that even "sticky" OEM spring relationships do not prevent volume-driven margin compression; the strategic review of Habia Cable has been running for 18+ months without resolution, raising questions about whether a value-creative sale is actually achievable; and if Habia Cable is retained in a weakened macro environment, management credibility on portfolio rationalisation is damaged

§3 — Industry Context (Peer Comparison, Structure, Macro and Non-Macro Change)

J-front verdict: The European springs market is fragmented, consolidating slowly, with Lesjöfors holding a structurally advantaged position as the largest independent; specialty cable (Habia) is concentrated but small, niche, and acquisition-challenged — overall industry structure is moderately supportive for the better business and irrelevant for the weaker one [#J]

Vs Comparable Peers

  • Peer divergence: No formal cohort peer research available (D-BATCH-25); the most instructive comparators are Indutrade, Addtech, and Bufab (Swedish industrials holding) plus Barnes Group and Lesjöfors' nearest global competitors (Lee Spring, Springmasters — private) [#A]
  • Indutrade and Addtech both operate decentralised multi-niche models; both trade at ~18–20x NTM P/E vs Beijer Alma ~14–16x — the discount is persistent and structural
  • Bufab (fastener distribution) is the closest structural analogue to Lesjöfors' distribution model; Bufab EBIT margins ~10–11%, ROCE ~15–17% — comparable to Beijer Alma group, suggesting the Habia Cable drag hypothesis may overstate Lesjöfors' standalone quality

Industry Structure and Value Chain

  • Market structure (springs): Global springs market estimated ~USD 15–18bn; European market ~USD 4–6bn; highly fragmented with thousands of regional manufacturers; no dominant global player — Lesjöfors is the largest independent in Europe, with estimated ~5–8% European market share [#A]

  • Top 5 European spring manufacturers hold an estimated ~25–35% combined share — below the typical "concentrated" threshold; the industry is genuinely fragmented and consolidating slowly

  • Concentration: Springs manufacturing has high entry barriers in custom/engineered segments (tooling, qualification, engineering capability) but low barriers in standard springs (any machine shop can produce); this bifurcation explains why Lesjöfors can maintain pricing in custom while standard product remains competitive [#A]

  • Value chain dynamics: Lesjöfors buys wire rod and flat strip steel from European steel producers (ArcelorMittal, SSAB, Voestalpine); steel is the primary input cost (~40–50% of Lesjöfors COGS) [#A]

  • Steel supplier power is moderate — Lesjöfors is a meaningful buyer but not dominant; steel prices are volatile and create margin headwinds in cost-push environments (2021–2023 was challenging)

  • Customer power: OEM customers have leverage in standard springs; less leverage in custom/qualified springs where re-qualification cost is high

  • Competitive intensity: European spring market competitive intensity is moderate in custom and high in standard; Chinese manufacturers have entered the standard spring market at lower price points, creating margin pressure in commodity segments [#A]

  • Habia Cable: specialty cable competitive intensity is low in the nuclear/defence segment (approved vendor lists, certification requirements) — this is the one segment of the group with genuinely limited competition

  • New entrant risk: Springs: vanilla new entrants are not a material threat to Lesjöfors' custom segment; Asian manufacturers (primarily Chinese) are a medium-term threat in standard distribution segments [#A]

  • Habia Cable: no meaningful new entrant risk in nuclear cable — 20-year qualification histories are not replicated; defence cable faces more competition at the margin

  • BtE: Barriers to entry in custom springs are real (engineering capability, tooling, customer relationships, qualification) but not insurmountable for a well-capitalised entrant; in standard springs, barriers are low [#A]

External Macro Changes (L12M and N36M)

  • Macro shift: European industrial production has been in a prolonged soft patch (2023–2025); German automotive output (a key Lesjöfors demand driver) declined ~8–12% in 2023–24 amid EV transition disruption and weak consumer demand [#A]

  • Forward outlook: consensus expects gradual European industrial recovery in 2025–26 (+2–4% IP growth), but automotive sector faces structural volume headwinds from EV shift (Lesjöfors serves both ICE and EV platforms — spring content per vehicle broadly similar, but total automotive production uncertainty remains)

  • Macro shift (rates/FX): SEK weakness vs EUR over 2023–2024 provided a modest translation tailwind for Lesjöfors (euro-denominated revenues); SEK has partially recovered; no significant FX structural issue [#A]

  • Macro shift (defence): European defence spending uplift (NATO 2% GDP commitments, Ukraine conflict) is a genuine medium-term positive for Habia Cable's defence segment; order intake in defence cables has been strong in 2024–2025 [AS-Nordea] [#A]

  • This is one reason the Habia Cable strategic review has not yet resolved — asset value may be rising with the defence spending cycle, and management may be waiting for a better price

External Non-Macro Changes

  • Thematic trend : Electric vehicle transition creates uncertainty for automotive spring demand; Lesjöfors has communicated that spring content per vehicle is similar across ICE and EV platforms; this claim is broadly supported by broker analysis [AS-Carnegie] but warrants ESA-depth verification [#A]

  • Risk: total automotive production volumes may contract if EV adoption is slower and ICE phase-out faster than expected — spring content per unit is irrelevant if total vehicles decline

  • Regulatory shift: No material regulatory risk for springs manufacturing; Habia Cable faces nuclear regulatory requirements (adding to qualification moat, not a risk) and RoHS/REACH chemical compliance (manageable cost, not disruptive) [#A]

  • Disruption: No viable technology disruption identified for springs — the physics of mechanical energy storage has not changed; additive manufacturing could theoretically produce springs but has not been cost-competitive for volume applications [#A]

  • Habia Cable: no identified near-term disruption to specialty cable; wireless/optical substitution irrelevant in nuclear/defence harsh environments

Sceptical bullet: The industry structure analysis supports Lesjöfors' moat in custom springs but masks genuine vulnerability in standard springs to Chinese competition — standard springs are ~55% of Lesjöfors revenue and the Asian pricing gap is estimated at 15–25% below Lesjöfors' cost base; if Chinese manufacturers successfully expand European distribution networks over the next 5 years, Lesjöfors' revenue base and margins could face structural pressure that the current investment case does not adequately discount

  • Progressive dividend maintained through cycle (2020 maintained, not cut); no buyback programme — capital directed to M&A pipeline

§4.2 — Advantaged Business + SRCA (BB#2 TC2)

MOAT (RA4 — full treatment)

  • Customer moat: Custom spring qualification lock-in (6–18 month approval cycles, tooling investment, drawing approval) creates genuine high switching costs in the ~45% custom segment; OEM automotive customers almost never re-qualify mid-programme [#J]
  • Standard spring distribution: switching costs lower but inventory breadth (30,000 SKUs, 30-site network) creates service-level advantage that regional competitors cannot match

  • Habia Cable: nuclear/defence approved vendor list lock-in is among the strongest customer moat mechanisms in industrial markets — comparable to aerospace certification moats

  • Production moat: Lesjöfors' manufacturing capability in precision springs (tight tolerances, exotic materials) is a production moat for the custom segment; competitors need years to build equivalent capability [#A]

  • Standard springs: minimal production moat — commodity production capability is widely available

  • Scale moat: Lesjöfors' pan-European distribution network (~30 stocking locations, ~30,000 SKUs) is the primary scale moat; a new entrant would need to replicate ~3–5 years of organic investment to achieve comparable inventory depth [#A]

  • Scale in procurement: Lesjöfors' steel purchasing volume (~SEK 800m–1bn pa) provides modest purchasing scale vs regional competitors

  • Regulatory moat: Habia Cable holds nuclear cable certifications (IEC 60544, DIN 40620 equivalents) that require decades of qualification history; effectively a permanent competitive moat in nuclear cable [#A]

  • Springs: minimal regulatory moat; automotive quality standards (IATF 16949) are hygiene factors not differentiators

  • Culture moat: Lesjöfors' decentralised operating model with local autonomy post-acquisition is a culture moat for M&A integration — targets retain management, culture, and customer relationships; this model has enabled 30+ acquisitions without visible integration failures [#A]

  • Weak signal: culture moat requires continued management discipline to preserve; deterioration not visible but ESA depth warranted at Q8

  • Relative scale: Lesjöfors is the largest independent spring manufacturer in Europe; estimated 5–8% European market share vs second largest (estimated ~2–3%) — roughly 2–3x the scale of nearest independent competitor [#A]

  • Scale relative to captive spring manufacturers (OEM in-house production) is less clear — some large automotive OEMs still produce springs in-house

  • TAM: Global springs market ~USD 15–18bn; European market ~USD 4–6bn; Lesjöfors addressable market (independent/external supply) ~USD 3–5bn; at current ~USD 340m revenue, penetration ~8–10% — substantial M&A and organic runway remains [#A]

  • 12–20% pa growth through acquisitions is plausible given fragmented market; organic growth is likely 2–4% pa
