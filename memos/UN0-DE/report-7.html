§2 — Key Driver #1: Nordic/German Power Spread Recovery

  • Net verdict: Highest-impact, medium-plausibility driver — EPS sensitivity is asymmetric but power price trajectory is outside management's control and forward curves embed only modest upside [#J]

  • Driver definition: Uniper earns generation margin primarily through spark spreads (gas-fired power in Germany) and hydro margins (Norway/Sweden); a 10% move in clean spark spreads translates to approximately 15-25% move in Group EBIT per publicly disclosed sensitivity data [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Higher power prices → wider generation margins → EBIT expansion → EPS uplift; lower prices → margin compression → EPS dilution; the leverage is high relative to European integrated utilities peers where regulated earnings often buffer 40-60% of EBIT [#A]

  • Sub: Uniper's regulated/quasi-regulated share of EBIT is lower than E.ON or RWE; estimated 30-40% of EBIT is quasi-fixed (gas infra, capacity payments), leaving 60-70% exposed to market prices

  • Company guide: Management has not issued explicit power-price-point guidance for 2025-26; hedging disclosures indicate ~60-70% of near-term generation hedged at levels above current spot in Germany [#A]

  • Sub: Hedge book maturity is a critical variable — as 2025 hedges roll off into 2026-27, realised prices will converge toward forward curve levels, removing the current hedge tailwind

  • Management commentary: CEO commentary at FY24 results indicated "cautious optimism" on Nordic hydro recovery given reservoir normalisation; Germany gas peaker utilisation expected to be supported by capacity market payments through 2027 [#A]

  • Track record: Uniper's pre-2022 power spread delivery was broadly in line with sector; the 2022 crisis was driven by volume (gas supply) not price (Uniper was actually a beneficiary of high power prices in H2 2022); post-restructuring track record on spread management is only 18M old [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Guidance is deliberately vague on power price assumptions — management avoids embedding specific price decks post-2022 credibility damage; hedge disclosure is the primary signal [#A]

  • SS consensus: Publicly available sell-side (Berenberg, Deutsche Bank, JPMorgan) embeds TTF gas at approximately €30-35/MWh and German baseload at €65-75/MWh for 2025-26; consensus EPS appears to assume gradual margin normalisation not spike recovery [#I]

  • Sub: Berenberg (public note Oct-25) flagged downside risk if TTF falls below €25/MWh, which would compress spark spreads to near-breakeven for gas peakers

  • Estimated EPS impact: A 10% upward move in German baseload vs consensus assumptions implies approximately +€0.15-0.25 EPS (estimated from disclosed sensitivities); 10% downside implies −€0.15-0.25 EPS [#A]

  • Recent delivery: FY24 generation segment delivered broadly in line with hedged expectations; Nordic hydro volumes recovered toward long-run average after 2022-23 drought-driven shortfall; German gas peaker dispatch higher than expected due to cold Q1-24 [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: 2022 — exceptional losses from gas procurement obligations; 2023 — partial recovery as hedges rolled in at above-market prices; 2024 — normalisation; 2025-26 consensus embeds continued normalisation without step-up [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: Uniper has materially higher commodity price leverage than regulated utility peers — E.ON and RWE have regulated earnings buffers of 50-70% of EBIT; Fortum (Nordic peer) has similar hydro-price exposure; no sector peer context available via D-BATCH-25 [#I]

  • Macro tailwind: European power demand recovery (EV penetration, data centre growth, industrial electrification) supports medium-term demand; short-term (12-18M) supply additions (renewables) exert downward pressure on baseload prices [#A]

  • Macro headwind: European gas TTF has declined from 2022-23 peaks; structural decline in gas dependency reduces spark spread floors; renewable overbuild risk in Germany (offshore wind additions 2025-27) could compress baseload prices by €5-10/MWh vs current forwards [#I]

  • Visibility: Low-medium visibility — hedge book provides 12-18M price certainty; beyond that, commodity-market dependent; management controls hedge ratio but not underlying price levels [#A]

  • Plausibility — M: Power prices holding near current forward curve is medium plausibility; downside risk from renewable overbuild and TTF normalisation is meaningful; upside from cold winters or supply disruption is real but not bankable [#A]

  • Bear case: TTF normalises to €20-25/MWh by 2026; German baseload falls to €55-60/MWh as offshore wind additions increase; Uniper's gas peaker dispatch falls; spark spreads compress below €5/MWh; generation segment EBIT falls 30-40% vs consensus [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Warm winter 2025-26 + accelerated renewable additions → TTF and baseload both move lower → hedge book advantage exhausted by H2 2026 → FY26 EPS miss of 20-30% vs consensus [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: −20-35% EPS vs consensus in severe downside scenario [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: If renewable overbuild accelerates faster than consensus models (Agora Energiewende projects 15GW+ net wind/solar additions in Germany 2025-27), the baseload price compression could be structurally faster than Uniper's hedge book can offset [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — forward curve held, hedges intact, but macro price risk is real and the hedge tailwind fades materially into 2026-27

§3 — Key Driver #2: Gas Infrastructure Earnings Floor

  • Net verdict: High-plausibility stabilising driver — Uniper's German gas storage and transportation assets provide a quasi-regulated earnings floor that limits deep downside but constrains upside surprise [#J]

  • Driver definition: Uniper operates German gas storage facilities (including Rehden, one of Europe's largest) and midstream gas assets; revenues from capacity bookings and storage spreads provide partially contracted income independent of day-to-day commodity prices [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Storage capacity fees and spread capture → EBIT contribution at relatively low operating leverage; estimated €300-500M EBIT contribution annually from gas infrastructure assets (based on public segment disclosure) [#A]

  • Sub: Storage utilisation rates drive the variable component; fixed capacity fees provide the floor

  • Company guide: Management has guided to "stable" midstream/gas infrastructure contribution in 2025-26; no specific EBIT guidance number disclosed publicly, but 2024 guidance implied segment stability [#A]

  • Management commentary: FY24 results commentary indicated gas storage was "fully booked" for 2024-25 winter season, consistent with elevated European gas storage demand post-2022 crisis; management expects "continued strong demand" for storage capacity through 2026 [#A]

  • Track record: Gas infrastructure segment has been the most reliable earnings contributor in the post-2022 restructuring period; no material miss vs guidance on this segment in L6 reporting periods [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Guidance appears conservative — strong capacity booking demand and elevated storage arbitrage have delivered above-implied floor in recent periods; management appears to be sandbagging slightly [#A]

  • SS consensus: Sell-side broadly models this segment as stable (Deutsche Bank, JPMorgan public notes embed flat-to-slightly-growing gas infrastructure contribution); no broker identified as materially bullish or bearish on this segment specifically [#I]

  • Estimated revenue impact: Gas infrastructure segment estimated at €1.5-2.5bn revenue, €300-500M EBIT; a 10% upside on capacity bookings implies ~€30-50M EBIT upside (~5-8% EPS) [#A]

  • Recent delivery: Consistent delivery — winter 2023-24 and 2024-25 capacity bookings at high levels; storage arbitrage above pre-crisis norms as European gas markets remain structurally tighter than pre-2022 [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: 2022 — disrupted; 2023 — partial recovery with elevated storage spreads; 2024 — normalisation at structurally higher level than pre-2022; 2025-26 — consensus embeds modest decline as storage spreads normalise further [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: European gas storage operators (E.ON Infrastructure, SNAM in Italy) have seen storage revenues remain above pre-2022 norms in 2024-25; no specific peer comparator available from D-BATCH-25 [#I]

  • Macro tailwind: European gas security imperative (post-Ukraine) sustains demand for storage capacity; EU regulation requiring member states to maintain minimum storage fill levels structurally supports capacity booking revenue [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Gradual LNG import infrastructure build (German LNG terminals) diversifies supply and reduces storage arbitrage spreads over time; a rapid warm-weather normalisation could reduce storage fills and spread opportunity [#A]

  • Visibility: High-medium visibility — capacity bookings are contracted on seasonal basis; high forward booking rates visible; regulatory framework (EU Gas Storage Regulation) provides structural demand support [#A]

  • Plausibility — H: Storage demand structurally elevated post-2022; capacity booking track record strong; regulatory tailwind intact; the main risk is spread compression, not volume loss [#A]

  • Bear case: Warm winter 2025-26 + LNG supply normalisation reduces storage arbitrage; spread between winter/summer TTF compresses from €5-8/MWh to €2-3/MWh; variable storage income falls 30-40% but capacity fees remain; net EBIT impact −€100-150M [#A]

  • Miss scenario: Structural European gas oversupply from LNG + warm winters → storage arbitrage structurally lower → EBIT contribution declines 15-25% vs consensus by FY26 [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: −5-12% EPS vs consensus in moderate downside — meaningful but not thesis-breaking given the fixed-fee floor [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The regulatory framework supporting EU storage fill mandates is a 2022-driven policy response; if European energy security perceptions normalise (Russia ceasefire, LNG normalisation), political pressure to reduce regulatory storage requirements could reduce structural demand by 2027 [#J]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — contracted capacity bookings, regulatory support, and strong recent delivery provide credible earnings floor through 2026

§4 — Key Driver #3: Capital Returns Reinstatement

  • Net verdict: Medium-plausibility re-rating catalyst — dividend reinstatement would be a significant multiple expansion signal but is contingent on balance sheet milestones that are not yet confirmed achieved [#J]

  • Driver definition: Uniper suspended dividends during the 2022 crisis and subsequent nationalisation; reinstatement (or announcement of) a sustainable dividend policy would signal normalisation and attract income-oriented investors previously excluded [#J]

  • Mechanism to SP: Dividend announcement → re-rating from "distressed recovery" to "utility normalised" multiple → P/E expansion of 1-3x; also triggers inclusion in dividend-screened indices; improved institutional ownership mix [#A]

  • Sub: European utility sector median dividend yield is approximately 4-5%; Uniper currently yields 0%; re-rating to sector median would require multiple expansion consistent with dividend-matching share price adjustment

  • Company guide: Management has indicated dividend reinstatement is contingent on achieving net debt targets and regulatory clearance; FY24 results stated "decision expected in 2025" with no specific quantum or timeline committed [#A]

  • Management commentary: CFO commentary (public) indicated balance sheet "on track" for dividend reinstatement consideration in H2 2025; caution expressed around "macro uncertainties" before committing; no specific yield guidance [#A]

  • Track record: No post-restructuring track record on capital allocation commitments — this management team has not yet fulfilled a capital return commitment; prior management destroyed the dividend entirely in 2022 [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Management is deliberately cautious — the 2022 trauma creates institutional risk-aversion to premature capital return commitments; likelihood is they sandbag timing to avoid a repeat of the 2022 suspension [#A]

  • SS consensus: Publicly visible sell-side broadly models dividend reinstatement in 2025-26; JPMorgan (public note) models initial yield of 2-3% for 2025 with step-up to 4% by 2027; Berenberg more conservative, modelling 2026 first dividend [#I]

  • Sub: Barclays (public note) has noted potential for share buyback alongside or preceding dividend if excess capital accumulates above target leverage

  • Estimated EPS impact: Dividend itself is not EPS-accretive; but re-rating from ~10-11x P/E to European utility sector median ~13-14x P/E would imply 20-30% share price upside purely from multiple expansion [#A]

  • Recent delivery: No delivery yet — FY24 (reported early 2025) confirmed "on track" but no announcement; H1 2025 the key watch point [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: 2022 — dividend suspended; 2023 — no dividend; 2024 — no dividend; 2025 — first decision point; any announcement would be 3+ years after suspension [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: RWE reinstated dividends 12M after major restructuring; E.ON maintained dividends throughout restructuring via regulated earnings buffer; Fortum reinstated dividends 18M post-crisis; Uniper at 36M+ without reinstating is at the upper end of European utility restructuring timelines [#I]

  • Macro tailwind: ECB rate cut cycle reduces opportunity cost of dividend-yield equities; income investors returning to European utilities as bonds less attractive; timing could align well if ECB cuts 2025-26 [#A]

  • Macro headwind: German federal government residual stake (~25%) creates political overhang — government may prefer capital deployed in strategic energy assets over shareholder returns; dividend policy requires government alignment [#A]

  • Visibility: Medium visibility — balance sheet trajectory is visible and tracking well; the political dimension (government consent) is less visible [#A]

  • Plausibility — M: Balance sheet trajectory supportive; peer precedent positive; but government stake overhang and management caution create timing uncertainty within the 24M window [#A]

  • Bear case: German government blocks or delays dividend reinstatement citing strategic capital requirements for hydrogen/energy transition investments; market reads this as indication government prioritising strategic over shareholder value; multiple remains depressed [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Dividend announced but at yield below consensus expectation (1-1.5% vs modelled 2-3%) → income investors underwhelmed → re-rating materialises only partially → 5-10% share price upside rather than 20-30% [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: −15-20% share price vs bull case if dividend delayed to 2026 — opportunity cost relative to European utility sector re-rating [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The German government's stated commitment to "full re-privatisation" of Uniper is a positive signal, but the timeline for stake reduction is politically sensitive; a government distracted by fiscal consolidation debates may delay stake disposal decisions that could unlock full capital return flexibility [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — balance sheet trajectory is supportive but government overhang and management caution create genuine timing uncertainty within 24M

§5 — Key Driver #4: Cost Restructuring Completion

  • Net verdict: High-plausibility, moderate-impact driver — restructuring programme is on track per management disclosure; completion would provide 10-15% EBIT margin lift and validate management execution credibility [#J]

  • Driver definition: Post-2022 restructuring involves headcount reduction (approx. 3,000 FTE reduction announced), portfolio rationalisation (non-core asset disposals), and operational cost base reset; management targets €500-700M cumulative cost savings by FY26 [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Cost savings → EBIT expansion → EPS uplift independent of power prices; also signals management execution credibility → multiple expansion from restructuring risk premium reduction [#A]

  • Sub: Disposal proceeds reduce net debt, contributing to balance sheet milestone for dividend reinstatement (driver #3 dependency)

  • Company guide: Management has provided explicit cumulative savings targets of €500-700M by FY26; FY24 results confirmed "on track" with approximately 60% of targeted savings already achieved [#A]

  • Management commentary: CEO commentary at FY24 indicated restructuring programme "ahead of plan" on headcount; cost per MWh generation declining; IT cost optimisation running as expected [#A]

  • Track record: Restructuring execution has been the strongest area of management delivery since 2022; L6 reporting periods have consistently confirmed restructuring milestones on schedule or ahead [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Targets appear conservative — "ahead of plan" commentary at 60% delivery suggests final 40% may also deliver ahead; however, restructuring programmes typically have a "last mile" problem as easy cuts are taken first [#A]

  • SS consensus: Sell-side broadly models restructuring savings flowing through FY25-26; Deutsche Bank (public) models €550M of the €700M target achieving by FY26; Berenberg models full delivery [#I]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Full €700M cost savings vs €500M floor implies approximately €200M incremental EBIT upside; at 20% tax rate and ~1.1bn shares outstanding, this equates to approximately €0.14-0.18 EPS upside (approximately 8-12% EPS upside) [#A]

  • Recent delivery: Consistent on-track delivery — headcount reductions tracking to plan; IT cost savings confirmed; asset disposal programme delivering in line (several non-core disposals completed FY23-24) [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: 2022 — restructuring announced; 2023 — early phase delivery; 2024 — mid-phase (60%); 2025-26 — final phase; clean comparator once fully embedded [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: European utility restructuring peers (E.ON, Vattenfall publicly disclosed) typically deliver 80-90% of restructuring savings within the stated programme timeline; Uniper's tracking at the high end of this range [#I]

  • Macro tailwind: Labour market normalisation in Germany (modest wage inflation relative to 2023 peak) reduces the cost of workforce reduction settlements; outsourcing costs stable [#A]

  • Macro headwind: German labour protection regulation constrains pace of headcount reduction; co-determination (Mitbestimmung) structures may slow or complicate final-phase cost actions [#A]

  • Visibility: High visibility — management has been the most transparent on this driver vs others; milestone disclosure is regular and specific [#A]

  • Plausibility — H: 60% delivered, programme ahead of schedule, management credibility highest on this driver; completion is high plausibility unless German labour law creates unexpected friction [#A]

  • Bear case: Last-mile restructuring friction — remaining 40% of savings concentrated in more complex IT transformation and regulated infrastructure cost changes; delivery slips to FY27 rather than FY26; full savings not in run-rate within 24M [#A]

  • Miss scenario: Restructuring delivers 80% of target (€560M) by FY26; residual savings slip to FY27; EPS misses consensus by 4-6% in FY26; market reaction muted (not a major downside trigger) [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: −4-8% EPS in moderate miss scenario — manageable; this is not a thesis-breaker [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: Restructuring savings often face cost creep in the implementation phase — severance costs, IT migration costs, and temporary duplicate running costs can offset headline savings; management's "ahead of plan" commentary may be tracking gross savings not net of implementation costs [#J]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — 60% delivery confirmed, programme ahead of schedule; residual execution risk is modest and manageable within the 24M window

§6 — Key Driver #5: Hydrogen and LNG Portfolio Optionality

  • Net verdict: Low near-term plausibility; real tail upside beyond 24M — hydrogen investments are pre-commercial; LNG portfolio provides some near-term optionality but scale is insufficient to be a primary 24M EPS driver [#J]

  • Driver definition: Uniper is investing in green hydrogen production (Germany, Northern Europe) and operates LNG trading/import capacity; if hydrogen commercialises or LNG margins expand, this could be a step-change in earnings beyond the current restructuring recovery [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Hydrogen: requires large capex → no near-term EPS; longer-term (2028-30) potential earnings contribution if green hydrogen finds industrial demand at commercial prices; LNG: trading margin and capacity arbitrage → contributes to commodity earnings in near-term [#A]

  • Sub: LNG optionality is partially embedded in current earnings (management has disclosed LNG trading operations); the incremental from scale-up is the driver question

  • Company guide: Management has outlined hydrogen investment pipeline of €1.5-2bn through 2030; no near-term earnings contribution guided; "strategic option value" framing used consistently [#A]

  • Management commentary: CEO at FY24 indicated hydrogen is a "2028+ commercial reality" for Uniper; no earnings guidance embedded for hydrogen within 2025-26 financial plan [#A]

  • Track record: No track record on hydrogen commercialisation — this is a greenfield investment programme; LNG trading has a positive (if volatile) track record in L2Y [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Management framing is appropriately cautious on hydrogen timeline; any sell-side embedding hydrogen in 2025-26 estimates would be aggressive [#I]

  • SS consensus: Publicly available sell-side does not embed material hydrogen earnings in 2025-26 consensus; option value is modelled as DCF tail (if at all); JPMorgan has a separate hydrogen scenario sensitivity suggesting €0.5-1bn EBIT by 2030 in bull case [#I]

  • Estimated EPS impact (24M): Negligible near-term — hydrogen capex is an EPS drag, not driver, within 24M; LNG trading upside is ±€100-200M EBIT in exceptional market conditions [#A]

  • Recent delivery: LNG trading contributed positively in FY22-23 (high spread environment); FY24 contribution lower as LNG spreads compressed; hydrogen pilots announced but no commercial production [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: 2022-24 — LNG trading opportunistic upside; hydrogen pre-commercial investment phase; no baseline earnings from hydrogen [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: European utilities with hydrogen programmes (E.ON, RWE, Equinor) are similarly in pre-commercial phase; no European utility has hydrogen in current earnings run-rate at material scale [#I]

  • Macro tailwind: EU Hydrogen Strategy and IPCEI funding provide subsidy support for hydrogen capex; German industrial demand for green hydrogen is structurally positive [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Hydrogen demand development slower than policy targets — EU 2030 hydrogen production targets are at significant risk of miss based on current project pipeline; electrolyser cost declines needed to reach commercial viability are behind schedule [#A]

  • Visibility: Very low near-term visibility — hydrogen is a 2028+ option; LNG trading is market-dependent and unforecastable [#A]

  • Plausibility — L (24M): Hydrogen earnings are not a 24M driver; LNG trading upside is real but unpredictable and secondary; this driver matters at 36-60M horizon, not 24M [#A]

  • Bear case: Hydrogen capex deployed but demand fails to materialise by 2028-30; impairment charges on hydrogen assets; LNG trading losses in low-spread environment; net drag on EPS from capex without return [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Hydrogen investment timeline slips; capex exceeds budget; LNG spreads compress; combined EPS drag of −€0.05-0.10 per year from hydrogen capex without offsetting revenue [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: −3-6% EPS drag per annum from capex on unproductive hydrogen assets — manageable but EPS-dilutive in near term [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: ⚡ RARE: The gap between Uniper's hydrogen investment commitment (€1.5-2bn through 2030) and the acknowledged commercial timeline (2028+) creates a 3-5 year period of capital deployment without earnings return — a capital efficiency concern that is structurally different from the power-price risk and could become a sell-side focus point if hydrogen policy support softens under post-2025 EU budget constraints [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Low plausibility within 24M — hydrogen is a strategic option not a near-term EPS driver; LNG trading upside is real but unforecastable and secondary to power spreads

§7 — Key Driver #6: Regulatory and Political Risk

  • Net verdict: Tail risk driver — not an upside contributor but a potential thesis-breaker — German energy policy uncertainty and residual government ownership create asymmetric downside not currently priced by consensus [#J]

  • Driver definition: Uniper is subject to German and EU energy regulation; German federal government holds residual stake post-re-privatisation; risk that government uses ownership/regulation to impose pricing constraints, additional windfall taxes, or strategic asset obligations [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Adverse regulatory action → earnings cap or cost imposition → EPS miss → re-rating downward; political uncertainty → multiple discount (above-sector risk premium) [#A]

  • Sub: The mechanism is primarily through multiple compression rather than direct EPS impact (though windfall taxes would directly hit EPS)

  • Company guide: Management acknowledges regulatory uncertainty as a risk factor; no guidance on regulatory outcomes; engagement with government described as "constructive" [#A]

  • Management commentary: Management has consistently flagged that the German government's energy transition timeline (Energiewende 2.0) creates both opportunity and risk; capacity market design changes could affect gas peaker economics [#A]

  • Track record: Worst-case regulatory scenario already realised in 2022 — German government nationalised Uniper; subsequent re-privatisation is positive signal but the fact that nationalisation was possible creates permanent tail risk in investor perception [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Management is appropriately cautious — they cannot credibly promise regulatory outcomes; the risk is structural not company-specific [#A]

  • SS consensus: Sell-side broadly models no material adverse regulatory action in 2025-26; consensus does not embed windfall tax risk; this appears to be a shared blind spot across visible brokers [#I]

  • Estimated EPS impact: A windfall tax equivalent to 5% of generation revenues would imply approximately −€150-250M EBIT impact (−10-15% EPS); a capacity market redesign reducing gas peaker capacity payments could reduce EPS by 8-12% [#A]

  • Recent delivery: No adverse regulatory action in L18M — German government has moved toward re-privatisation, supporting positive regulatory trajectory; EU energy policy remains constructive for gas storage [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: 2022 — nationalisation (extreme); 2023 — regulatory stabilisation; 2024 — re-privatisation progress; 2025-26 — consensus assumes benign regulatory environment [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: European energy sector windfall taxes have been implemented in UK (Windfall Tax 2022), Italy, Spain; Germany explicitly avoided broad energy windfall tax in 2022-23; risk of belated implementation remains if fiscal conditions deteriorate [#I]

  • Macro tailwind: German fiscal consolidation pressure reduces appetite for subsidising energy transition (positive for market-based pricing); EU Taxonomy inclusion for gas generation provides some regulatory stability [#A]

  • Macro headwind: German federal election outcomes (Feb-25 election) introduce policy uncertainty; coalition dynamics around energy transition spending and capacity market design are not yet settled; new government may have different views on Uniper's strategic role [#A]

  • Visibility: Low visibility — regulatory outcomes depend on coalition politics, European policy evolution, and commodity markets; not in management's control [#A]

  • Plausibility — tail risk: Adverse scenario is low base-case probability (~15-25%) but high-impact; positive regulatory trajectory (re-privatisation) is the base case [#A]

  • Bear case: New German government introduces capacity market changes disadvantaging gas peakers; or EU windfall tax extended to cover gas storage arbitrage profits; EPS impact −15-25%; multiple remains depressed due to policy uncertainty [#J]
