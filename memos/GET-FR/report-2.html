Section 1: Brief company description

Getlink SE is a European low-carbon infrastructure and transport group that operates the Channel Tunnel and a portfolio of complementary mobility businesses.

In the 2025 financial year, the Group generated total consolidated revenue of €1,595 million.

The company's corporate structure and financial reporting are organized around three primary segments: Eurotunnel, ElecLink, and Europorte.

The Eurotunnel segment remains the historical core business, operating a unique bi-national land link that handles approximately a quarter of all trade between the United Kingdom and continental Europe.

Within Eurotunnel, the Shuttle Services division generates the majority of segment income, contributing €738 million in 2025.

The Shuttle operations physically transport heavy goods vehicles on "LeShuttle Freight" trains, alongside passenger cars, coaches, and motorcycles on "LeShuttle" trains.

The Railway Network division within Eurotunnel generated €411 million in 2025.

This network revenue is derived from access tolls paid by third-party high-speed passenger trains, such as Eurostar, and independent rail freight operators that utilise the tunnel infrastructure.

The Eurotunnel segment also houses Getlink Customs Services, an expanding border formalities unit that recently acquired ChannelPorts, Associated Shipping Agencies , and BIMS to facilitate frictionless UK-EU trade.

The ElecLink segment consists of a 1-gigawatt high-voltage direct current electricity interconnector installed safely inside the Channel Tunnel.

The primary revenue stream for ElecLink is the auctioning of physical transmission rights across varying timeframes, which accounted for 81% of the segment's 2025 revenue.

Participation in the French and British capacity markets provided an additional 16% of ElecLink's revenue by compensating the interconnector for ensuring security of electricity supply.

The remaining 3% of ElecLink's income is generated from ancillary services, such as reactive power, provided to the national Transmission System Operators.

Jefferies Research highlights that ElecLink operates under a 25-year regulatory exemption allowing it to retain its economic value, subject to a 50% profit-sharing mechanism with national grid operators once a 13% pre-tax internal rate of return is exceeded.

The Europorte segment is Getlink's private rail freight operator, covering the entire logistics chain from mainline haulage to industrial site shunting.

Europorte France is the primary subsidiary in this segment, handling mainline commercial rail haulage and accounting for 65% of Europorte's total revenue.

Socorail, another key Europorte subsidiary, provides complementary industrial logistics, wagon loading, and dedicated infrastructure management services on private branch lines.

Geographically, Getlink's core Eurotunnel operations physically connect the terminals of Folkestone in the United Kingdom and Calais in France.

The Europorte rail freight business generates the vast majority (74%) of its revenue domestically within France.

The remaining 26% of Europorte's revenue is generated from cross-border transport flows extending into Belgium and Germany.

Structurally, the Channel Tunnel was constructed and is operated under a bi-national Concession Agreement signed in 1986 pursuant to the Treaty of Canterbury.

Initially established for a 55-year term, the Concession Agreement was successfully extended twice and is now legally valid until 2086.

Under this long-term framework, Getlink operates the fixed link infrastructure entirely at its own commercial risk, without any government funding or sovereign financial guarantees.

Morgan Stanley notes that the Eurotunnel concession effectively functions in two distinct stages, featuring a pre-2052 period with a normalized tax rate of approximately 25%, followed by a post-2052 period where the tax rate sharply increases to 59%.

Morgan Stanley adds that this future tax step-up was structured as a long-term compensation mechanism for the initial 10-year extension of the concession granted by the states in 1994.

At the final expiry of the Concession in 2086, all immovable property and plant within the French concession area will unconditionally revert to the French State.

In the United Kingdom, freehold land is transferred to the government, which in return grants operating leases back to Getlink for the full duration of the Concession.

Section 2: Key components summary

Getlink SE has entered a transitional phase characterized by aggressive capital allocation resets, shifting shareholder structures, and structural shifts in cross-Channel competition. The following points summarize the major changes over the last 12-18 months and the anticipated trajectory through 2029.

Getlink SE's leadership structure is actively evolving, highlighted by the appointment of Géraldine Périchon as Deputy CEO and CFO in March 2024.

A streamlined, nine-member Executive Committee was established to accelerate strategic execution and operational performance across all business units.

Board dynamics are shifting with the co-optation of Andrea Mangoni, CEO of Mundys, as a non-independent director in July 2025.

Succession planning for Chairman Jacques Gounon is actively underway, with his current term set to expire at the 2026 Annual General Meeting.

The company's corporate activity has accelerated through targeted M&A, particularly within the rapidly expanding Getlink Customs Services division.

Getlink acquired ChannelPorts in April 2024, followed by the January 2025 acquisitions of Associated Shipping Agencies and Boulogne International Maritime Services .

Europorte expanded its infrastructure management footprint by acquiring a 67% stake in Electrofer SAS, a rail processing specialist, in June 2025.

Eiffage aggressively increased its stake in Getlink, purchasing an additional 1.74% on the open market in March 2026 to reach 29.4% ownership.

Morgan Stanley notes that Eiffage's stake is now dangerously close to the 30% mandatory takeover threshold, which likely constrains Getlink's ability to launch substantial share buybacks.

Mundys also strengthened its strategic position, announcing in March 2026 the acquisition of up to 9.5% of Getlink's share capital, aiming for up to 29.9% of voting rights pending regulatory approvals.

Getlink unveiled a bold new financial ambition at its February 2026 Capital Markets Day, targeting €1 billion in consolidated EBITDA by 2030.

For 2026, the company has guided for an EBITDA range of €820 million to €860 million, factoring in a normalized energy market and ongoing economic uncertainty.

Management completely reset the shareholder return policy, announcing a baseline 2026 dividend of €0.80 per share.

The dividend is slated to structurally increase by €0.05 annually over the next five years, reaching a target of €1.00 per share by 2030.

Jefferies Research calculates that this new dividend framework comes in approximately 23% to 30% ahead of prior median consensus estimates for the 2025-2030 period.

Despite an accumulating gross cash pile of nearly €1.5 billion, Getlink disappointed some market expectations by omitting any special dividends or share buyback programs during its recent strategic update.

Margin expansion is being driven by a "More for More" commercial strategy in the Shuttle business, which unbundles services to maximize pricing power and yield optimization.

Freight demand remains a severe macroeconomic headwind, with truck traffic declining 3% in 2025 due to a subdued UK economic environment and acute weakness in the automotive sector.

Conversely, passenger demand is demonstrating structural resilience, as Eurostar traffic hit a record 11.8 million passengers in 2025 following the reopening of the Amsterdam route.

Getlink anticipates massive disruption in the high-speed rail market, preparing for an influx of 10 million additional passengers over the next decade.

Growth in cross-Channel rail will be catalyzed by new market entrants, such as Virgin Trains and FS Group/Trenitalia, who plan to launch competing services around 2029 and 2030.

The implementation of the European Entry/Exit System between April and September 2026 presents a short-term regulatory hurdle but a long-term competitive advantage over the Port of Dover.

Morgan Stanley expects that incoming French minimum wage legislation for seafarers and the UK Emissions Trading Scheme will significantly raise cost bases for competing low-cost ferry operators.

Capital expenditure is guided to remain elevated at €170 million to €220 million annually until approximately 2031, covering heavy mid-life refurbishments and ERTMS signaling upgrades.

A major disruption risk centers on the ongoing normalization of electricity markets, which caused ElecLink's revenues to drop 20% in 2025 as the energy crisis subsided.

Section 3: Change in leadership

Getlink SE's leadership structure is anchored by the strict separation of the Chairman and Chief Executive Officer roles, a governance model successfully established in July 2020.

Yann Leriche serves as the Chief Executive Officer, having guided the infrastructure group through the pandemic and the complex post-Brexit transition.

In recognition of his strategic execution and operational discipline, the Board of Directors unanimously renewed Leriche's mandate as CEO for an additional four-year term in 2024.

Leriche's position was further solidified at the May 2025 Annual General Meeting, where shareholders overwhelmingly approved his reappointment as a Director of the company.

The Board also designated Leriche as Vice-Chairman in early 2026, though this appointment does not grant him any special additional powers.

Jacques Gounon continues to serve as the Non-Executive Chairman of the Board, providing stability and extensive binational diplomatic experience.

Gounon's current term as a Director is set to expire at the close of the 2026 Annual General Meeting.

To provide maximum flexibility for organizing his eventual succession, the 2025 General Meeting approved an amendment to the Articles of Association raising the Chairman's statutory age limit from 70 to 75.

A significant change in the executive hierarchy occurred in March 2024 when Géraldine Périchon was promoted to Deputy Chief Executive Officer (Directrice générale adjointe).

Périchon joined Getlink as Chief Financial Officer in 2020 and now directly oversees the Group's finances, information systems, CSR, purchasing, mergers and acquisitions, and legal affairs.

Jefferies Research notes that the Executive Committee generally meets on a fortnightly basis to manage the company's operational execution.

The Executive Committee has seen notable recent additions, including Julie Bagur, who was appointed Chief Business Development Officer in 2024 to drive external growth projects.

Didier Cazelles was brought in as Deputy Chief Executive Officer of Eurotunnel in October 2024, bringing over 20 years of operational experience from the SNCF group and Keolis.

In May 2025, Nicola Lyons joined the Group as Chief Human Resources Officer, filling a critical role on the Executive Committee to lead talent development and inclusion policies.

The Executive Committee now boasts a diverse composition, with women representing over 44% of its members, significantly exceeding the Group's initial 2023 feminization targets.

The average age of the Executive Committee members stands at 53 years, blending seasoned industry experience with dynamic new leadership.

Board composition is also evolving as major shareholders actively shape the governance framework.

In July 2025, Andrea Mangoni, CEO of Mundys, was co-opted to the Board as a non-independent director, replacing the resigning Jean Mouton.

Mangoni joins Elisabetta De Bernardi di Valserra in representing Mundys, Getlink's second-largest shareholder.

Eiffage, Getlink's largest shareholder, continues to exert influence through its proposed directors, Benoît de Ruffray and Marie Lemarié.

To comply with the European Women on Boards (WoB) Directive ahead of its 2027 application, the Board decided to reduce the number of Staff Representative Directors from three to two starting at the 2026 General Meeting.

Mark Cornwall and Stéphane Sauvage were elected to fill these two continuing employee representative seats through 2030.

Executive remuneration has been recalibrated to reflect the company's growth, with CEO Yann Leriche's fixed annual salary increasing from €550,000 to €600,000 effective July 2024.

Jefferies Research highlights that 45% of the CEO's variable compensation is tied to financial objectives, while 25% is linked directly to sustainability and climate targets.

Management is structurally aligned with shareholders, as executive officers are strictly required to retain at least 50% of their vested performance shares for the duration of their tenure.

Culturally, leadership is driving the "WAY forward" transformation program, which implements lean management and digital initiatives to enhance structural competitiveness.

This cultural shift was visually cemented in June 2025 when Getlink unveiled a modernized brand identity to align its operating subsidiaries with its long-term decarbonization ambitions.

Section 4: Change in strategy/structure

Getlink has fundamentally reoriented its corporate strategy to target €1 billion in consolidated EBITDA by 2030.

To achieve this, management is accelerating a controlled diversification strategy that leverages the unique bi-national infrastructure of the Channel Tunnel.

A core strategic priority is the aggressive expansion of the international High-Speed Rail market.

The group is actively preparing its infrastructure and regulatory framework to accommodate an influx of 10 million additional high-speed rail passengers over the next decade.

Within the historical Shuttle business, the commercial strategy has shifted toward a "More for More" model.

This New Fare Structure unbundles services to offer differentiated pricing, priority boarding, and premium lounge access, thereby maximizing yield per vehicle.

Furthermore, Getlink has structurally adapted to the post-Brexit environment by creating a dedicated new business line, Getlink Customs Services.

This division has been rapidly scaled through external growth, highlighted by the recent acquisitions of ChannelPorts, Associated Shipping Agencies , and BIMS.

The ElecLink electricity interconnector has transitioned from an ambitious development project into a mature asset generating 14% of group revenue.

Getlink operates this 1-gigawatt asset under a 25-year regulatory exemption framework valid through 2047.

Strategically, the company is now advancing plans for "ElecLink 2", a second 1000MW interconnector to be installed safely inside the South tunnel.

Regulators in France and Great Britain (Ofgem) have outlined their initial preference for ElecLink 2 as the next cross-border interconnector project.

Commercial operations for this replication project are tentatively targeted for the mid-2030s.

Jefferies Research notes that while ElecLink 2 offers significant structural upside, the required investment decision remains contingent on regulatory clarity regarding future cost and benefit mechanisms.

Europorte's structural evolution focuses on maintaining its position as the only profitable rail freight operator in Europe.

The subsidiary is executing a strategy of targeted diversification, expanding its Flex Express services across the French, German, and Belgian logistics corridors.

Management has structurally enhanced Europorte's infrastructure management capabilities through targeted M&A.

In June 2025, Europorte acquired a 67% stake in Electrofer SAS, a specialist in rail processing and the reloading of used rails.

Sustainability is deeply embedded in the corporate structure, with the 2030 CSR Roadmap serving as the operational blueprint for decarbonization.

Getlink has aggressively committed to reducing its Scope 1 and Scope 2 greenhouse gas emissions by 54% by 2030, compared to a 2019 baseline.

The centerpiece of this green strategy is the "Power to Traction" solar project, representing a world first in the global rail sector.

The company will install 80,000 photovoltaic panels at the Coquelles terminal to directly power the high-voltage overhead lines inside the Channel Tunnel.

This ambitious solar farm is projected to cover approximately 12% of the group's total annual electricity consumption by 2028.

Europorte is also contributing to the green corridor strategy by deploying alternative fuels such as Oleo100 and HVO across its locomotive fleet.

To finance this structural shift toward net-zero, Getlink successfully issued €600 million in dedicated Green Bonds maturing in April 2030.

The foundational structure of Getlink relies on the bi-national Concession Agreement, which securely grants operating rights for the Fixed Link until 2086.

Morgan Stanley highlights that the Eurotunnel concession effectively functions as a two-stage asset, characterized by a pre-2052 normalized tax period and a post-2052 phase where the tax rate structurally steps up to 59%.

To safeguard the longevity of this asset through the 2086 expiry, Getlink has structured a heavy mid-life refurbishment program for its Passenger Shuttle fleet.

Section 5: Change in priorities/ambition

Getlink SE has decisively shifted its corporate ambition from a post-crisis recovery mindset toward an aggressive, long-term growth trajectory, underscored by a new target to reach €1 billion in consolidated EBITDA by 2030.

Chief Executive Officer Yann Leriche explicitly defined this strategic pivot during the 2026 Investor Day, stating that the strong foundations laid in recent years enable the Group to enter a "new phase of execution, acceleration and value creation."

This intensified ambition is further reflected in a complete reset of the company's capital allocation and shareholder return priorities.

Management announced a baseline 2026 dividend of €0.80 per share, with a commitment to structurally increase this payout by €0.05 annually to reach €1.00 per share by 2030.

Morgan Stanley analysts observe that the Group's priority is heavily concentrated on the High-Speed Rail market, which represents the most significant medium-term value creation lever.

Getlink's ambition in the passenger rail segment is highly intensified, targeting an influx of 10 million additional high-speed rail passengers over the next decade compared to 2025 volumes.

Jefferies Research notes that management is highly confident in long-term traffic demand, specifically pointing to the reopening of the Amsterdam terminal and new line expansions into Germany and Switzerland as vital catalysts for this growth.

During recent earnings transcripts, CEO Yann Leriche highlighted a shifting commercial priority toward sophisticated yield management, indicating that the company is actively moving into "Phase 2" of its yield optimization strategy.

Leriche noted that while this second phase is slower to implement due to required system overhauls, it presents substantial opportunities to further refine pricing approaches and capture maximum value from customers.

In addressing the intensely competitive cross-Channel freight market, CEO verbal commentary reveals a firm belief that ferry operators will soon face unsustainable cost burdens.

Leriche specifically cited rising seafarers' wages, European Entry/Exit System facility investments, and incoming carbon surcharges tied to the EU Emissions Trading System as catalysts that will force ferries to adopt more rational, higher pricing behaviors.

Getlink is aggressively positioning itself as the undisputed leader in European green transport, anchored by its corporate motto: "Low Carbon, High Simplicity."

The Group's environmental ambition is formalized through an updated 2030 Corporate Social Responsibility Roadmap, which commits to reducing Scope 1 and 2 greenhouse gas emissions by 54% compared to a 2019 baseline.

Getlink has also introduced a new Scope 3 emissions priority, targeting a 15% reduction in intensity (per million euros of purchases) for supply chain and investment emissions by 2030.

To physically embed this green positioning into its infrastructure, management is prioritizing the "Power to Traction" solar program at the Coquelles terminal.

This ambitious solar installation represents a world first for the railway sector and is designed to cover approximately 12% of the Group's total electricity consumption by 2028.

A broader priority is to achieve 10% overall self-generated electricity across the company's operations by 2030.

Getlink's ambition to decarbonize heavy logistics was recently validated by the successful first crossing of a fully electric DAF freight truck on a LeShuttle Freight train.

This proof-of-concept crossing, utilizing megawatt-level charging infrastructure, reinforces the Group's strategic positioning to support the large-scale deployment of electric road transport across European supply chains.

Internally, leadership has prioritized the alignment of financial and environmental performance by introducing an internal carbon price and utilizing a unique "decarbonised margin" key performance indicator to guide major investment decisions.

Section 6: Financial profile

Getlink SE concluded the 2025 financial year with consolidated revenue of €1,595 million, a slight 1% decrease from 2024 primarily driven by the expected normalization of wholesale electricity markets impacting the ElecLink interconnector.

Despite the top-line contraction, the Group demonstrated strong operating leverage, generating a consolidated current EBITDA of €859 million in 2025, which represents a 4% year-over-year increase and exceeded management's initial guidance.

The core Eurotunnel segment remains a highly profitable infrastructure asset, achieving an EBITDA margin of 55.7% in 2025, a robust 0.8 percentage point improvement over the prior year.

Getlink has formally initiated a new phase of financial acceleration, establishing a bold medium-term target to reach €1 billion in consolidated EBITDA by the year 2030.

Free Cash Flow generation remains a defining characteristic of Getlink's financial profile, with the Group producing €374 million in FCF during 2025 after accounting for €88 million in scheduled debt repayments.

Jefferies Research notes that while historically strong, the company's free cash flow conversion may face compression in the medium term due to an elevated capital expenditure cycle, with annual capex modeled between €155 million and €225 million through 2031.

The Group's cash generation has enabled a complete reset of its shareholder return policy, highlighted by a proposed dividend of €0.80 per share for the 2025 financial year, marking a significant step-up from previous payouts.

Management has committed to a structural growth trajectory for this dividend, aiming to increase the payout by €0.05 annually until it reaches €1.00 per share by 2030.

Morgan Stanley highlights that this newly established dividend framework already places Getlink at or above the European Infrastructure sector average, offering a yield of approximately 4.5% compared to the sector average of roughly 4%.

Getlink's balance sheet features an exceptionally high gross cash position, which stood at €1,498 million at the end of 2025.

Morgan Stanley observes that this €1.5 billion cash pile is an oddity compared to the company's pre-Covid history, where it typically operated with a normalized gross cash balance of €300 million to €600 million.

Getlink has successfully executed a dramatic deleveraging trajectory over the past decade, significantly reducing its financial risk profile since the historical safeguard restructuring of 2007.

Total consolidated net debt was reduced by €184 million during 2025, bringing the outstanding balance down to €3,392 million by year-end.

Consequently, Getlink's net debt to current EBITDA ratio has dropped to 3.9x as of December 31, 2025, successfully crossing below the critical 4.0x threshold.

The debt structure is specifically designed for extreme long-term stability, with approximately 50% of the Group's debt maturities occurring post-2040.

The financial solidity of the Group has been formally recognized by major credit rating agencies, with both S&P Global Ratings and Fitch Ratings upgrading Getlink SE's corporate rating to BB+ in 2025.

Furthermore, S&P Global Ratings upgraded the long-term debt rating of Eurotunnel's securitization vehicle to BBB+, reflecting deep confidence in the asset's cash-generating capabilities.

Getlink proactively optimizes its capital structure to align with its green transport positioning, successfully issuing €600 million in dedicated Green Bonds maturing in April 2030.

The company's financial resilience was thoroughly tested and proven during recent industry crises, including the severe disruptions caused by the Covid-19 pandemic and the implementation of Brexit border controls.

During the height of these crises, Getlink implemented the "SHIELD" operational discipline program, which successfully delivered €40 million in structural cost savings between 2020 and 2022.

This historical track record of managing extreme volume shocks demonstrates the highly variable nature of the company's cost base and its ability to protect underlying cash flows during macro downturns.

Section 7: Value chain – Demand

Getlink's demand profile is characterized by distinct dynamics across its high-speed rail, passenger shuttle, freight logistics, and electricity transmission segments. The company is actively managing structural shifts in consumer behavior and macro-driven industrial cycles.

High-Speed Passenger Rail

Eurostar traffic achieved a record high of 11.8 million passengers in 2025, representing a 5% year-over-year increase.

This demand growth was heavily catalyzed by the reopening of the Amsterdam Centraal international terminal and solid performance on traditional London-Paris and London-Brussels routes.

Management is highly ambitious regarding long-term demand, preparing the infrastructure to accommodate an influx of 10 million additional high-speed rail passengers over the next decade compared to 2025 levels.

Morgan Stanley expects short-term fleet productivity gains of 20% from Eurostar to support traffic growth before new Alstom trains are delivered.

Jefferies emphasizes that the current low asset utilization rate of approximately 50% is primarily constrained by Eurostar's fleet capacity rather than a lack of underlying consumer demand.

Geographic expansion is a critical demand driver, with internal studies indicating that new direct routes to Germany and Switzerland possess a potential market of 2 million passengers each.

Passenger Shuttle (LeShuttle) and Tourism Trends

The LeShuttle passenger service captured a 56.1% market share of the Short Straits car market in 2025, transporting 2.23 million passenger vehicles.

Post-Brexit border complexities have structurally altered tourism patterns, causing single-daytrip traffic to plummet by 76% since 2019.

Conversely, long-stay leisure travel has demonstrated extreme resilience, growing 16% over the same pre-pandemic period.

To capitalize on this demand, Getlink implemented a "More for More" pricing strategy that unbundles services, driving a 3% increase in average shuttle yield during 2025.

Approximately 90% of LeShuttle customers book directly online, providing the company with a robust data ecosystem to optimize yield management and drive loyalty.

The passenger demand base is currently heavily concentrated, with 83% of travelers originating from the United Kingdom.

To diversify this demand, management is launching targeted acquisition campaigns focused on continental European markets, starting with the Netherlands.

The electrification of consumer transport is accelerating within the tunnel, with 111,600 fully electric cars utilizing the shuttle in 2025, accounting for 5.2% of total vehicle traffic.

Freight Volumes (LeShuttle Freight) and Trade Flows

Cross-Channel truck traffic contracted by 3% in 2025, heavily impacted by a subdued UK macroeconomic environment and acute weakness in the automotive manufacturing sector.

Despite these severe macro headwinds and intense ferry competition, LeShuttle Freight maintained a stable market share of 35.4% throughout the year.

Management is confident in a gradual demand recovery by 2030, driven by the projected 30% rebound in UK automotive production and the restructuring of European e-commerce supply chains.

Morgan Stanley expects green shoots for freight recovery to emerge from a projected >10% year-over-year growth in the automotive industry during 2026.

Jefferies notes that UK supply chain reshoring trends will serve as a critical catalyst for Getlink's future freight volume growth.

The recent diplomatic agreement to simplify sanitary and phytosanitary checks between the EU and the UK is anticipated to rapidly stimulate demand in the time-sensitive fresh products segment.

ElecLink Electricity Demand

The 1-gigawatt ElecLink interconnector continues to experience strong foundational demand, providing essential flexibility to balance the French and British national grids.

Commercial demand is highly contracted in advance; as of February 2026, 81% of the cable's capacity for the year had already been sold, securing €242 million in revenue.

Morgan Stanley highlights that recent capacity auctions continue to secure generous UK/France electricity spreads of approximately €25/MWh for 2026.

Jefferies observes that while extreme energy crisis pricing has normalized, current market spreads remain structurally higher than 2021 levels and the project's original business plan.

Beyond physical transmission, ElecLink benefits from strong demand in national capacity markets, successfully securing 900MW of certified delivery in France for 2026.

The most significant forward-looking change vector is the high-speed rail market, which sits on the precipice of a massive structural demand step-up (+10 million passengers) driven by the anticipated arrival of new competing rail operators.

Section 8: Value chain – Competition

Getlink SE operates in a highly competitive cross-Channel market, facing distinct competitive dynamics from maritime ferry operators for its Shuttle services and from airlines for its high-speed rail network.

Ferry Operators and the Short Straits Market

Eurotunnel competes directly with P&O Ferries, DFDS Seaways, and Irish Ferries for passenger and freight roll-on/roll-off traffic on the Short Straits.

In 2025, there are a total of 11 maritime ferry ships operating on the highly concentrated Short Straits market.

DFDS Seaways maintains the largest fleet with six vessels, split evenly with three on the Dover-Dunkirk route and three on the Calais-Dover route.

Irish Ferries, owned by Irish Continental Group, entered the Dover-Calais market in 2021 with a disruptive low-cost model and currently operates three ships.

Morgan Stanley highlights that P&O Ferries currently operates three vessels on the route, having reduced its fleet from a pre-pandemic peak of six ships.

To optimize frequency and protect margins against overcapacity, ferry operators have increasingly relied on space charter agreements, effectively consolidating their operational capacity.

Irish Ferries and P&O Ferries entered into a comprehensive space charter agreement covering both freight and passenger traffic.

This specific partnership materially increases the frequency of departures offered to customers, allowing the combined ferry schedule to compete more aggressively against the convenience of LeShuttle.

Evolving Ferry Cost Bases and Regulatory Pressures

Historically, ferry operators have utilized their lower cost bases to engage in intense price wars, directly pressuring Getlink's freight and passenger yields.

Getlink management believes new social regulations will significantly raise the ferries' labor costs and enforce rational pricing behavior across the market.

Morgan Stanley observes that the aggressive pricing dynamic is expected to reverse as ferries face mounting regulatory and environmental cost burdens.

The implementation of the Seafarers' minimum wage and resting time laws in France and the UK is specifically designed to counter the social dumping practices historically utilized by low-cost ferry operators.

Environmental compliance is adding further structural pressure, with the EU Emissions Trading System and the FuelEU Maritime regulation requiring ferries to sharply reduce their greenhouse gas intensity.

Jefferies notes that these unavoidable structural cost increases for ferries will likely constrain their ability to launch aggressive pricing moves, firmly reinforcing Getlink's infrastructure advantage.

To comply with these stringent environmental mandates, DFDS has announced intentions to invest approximately €1 billion in six battery-electric ships for the Channel by 2035.

Airline Competition and the High-Speed Rail Market

In the high-speed rail segment, Eurostar's primary competition comes from low-cost and legacy airlines operating between the United Kingdom and continental Europe.

The combined air and rail market between London and Paris stood at 9.9 million passengers in 2025, while the London-Brussels/Amsterdam corridor reached 8.2 million.

Eurostar maintains a dominant 76.9% market share against airlines on the core London-Paris route.

Conversely, Eurostar's market share against airlines on the London-Amsterdam route is much lower at 51.0%, indicating significant remaining potential for rail to capture air traffic.

Section 11: Value chain – Supply side

The geographic footprint of this direct supply chain is overwhelmingly local, with more than 99% of top-tier suppliers located in Europe.

Specifically, 69% of these critical tier-one suppliers are based in France, while 23% are located in the United Kingdom.

The highly specialized nature of Channel Tunnel equipment, which is often produced in small batches, creates a severe degree of structural dependency on a limited number of niche manufacturers.

This dependency recently materialized as a tangible operational risk when the termination of a supplier's contract forced Eurotunnel to completely reorganize its critical Passenger Shuttle mid-life refurbishment program.

To systematically mitigate this concentration risk, Getlink conducts recurring market checks every three to five years to identify alternative partners and diversify its procurement base.

The Group's cost structure remains predominantly fixed, with employee benefits representing approximately 46% of the Eurotunnel segment's highly inelastic cost base.

Morgan Stanley highlights that the implementation of the European Entry/Exit System structurally increased this labor burden, requiring additional permanent staff to manage border kiosks and mitigate terminal friction.

To offset inflationary salary pressures, Getlink relies on the "Eurotunnel Performance Plan", a lean management initiative that successfully extracted €25 million in structural operational savings between 2023 and 2025.

Railway infrastructure and rolling stock maintenance represent the second-largest supply-side burden, accounting for approximately 25% of Eurotunnel's fixed cost intensity.

Direct operations and maintenance sub-contracting costs remained tightly controlled, inching up only slightly to €132 million in 2025 from €131 million in 2024.

Getlink is actively transforming its maintenance supply chain through the integration of artificial intelligence and big data to shift from reactive repairs to predictive maintenance.

The deployment of the laser-based PRISM system enables real-time, in-service monitoring of train wheels, allowing for highly precise reprofiling that reduces material waste.

This specific AI intervention directly extends wheel lifespan by up to 25%, delivering material savings against the €6.5 million annual cost of axle and wheel replacement.

To further optimize maintenance labor and reduce tunnel downtime, Getlink commissioned a 150-meter physical replica of the Channel Tunnel.

This unique training simulator allows sub-contractors and internal engineering teams to test new equipment and rigorously practice complex repair techniques without interrupting the live 24/7 rail operations.

Energy sourcing represents a massive supply-side pivot for Getlink, as electricity historically accounts for approximately 8% of Eurotunnel's fixed costs.

The company recently faced a critical supply-side shock with the expiration of the French ARENH regulatory mechanism, which had previously guaranteed nuclear electricity at a highly subsidized tariff of €42 per megawatt-hour.

To adapt to the post-ARENH market reality, Getlink fundamentally overhauled its procurement strategy by securing two-thirds of its former ARENH volume through long-term Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs) and internal generation.

Management simultaneously introduced a highly sophisticated energy trading desk, spreading wholesale purchases over a rolling 24-month horizon utilizing strict stop-loss and take-gain mechanisms.

These procurement defenses were complemented by rigorous internal efficiency mandates, successfully stripping 14% of absolute electricity consumption out of the daily operating model.

As a result of these combined supply-side interventions, absolute electricity costs for the Eurotunnel segment actually declined from €57 million in 2024 to €45 million in 2025.

To permanently secure long-term energy independence, Getlink is actively constructing the "Power to Traction" solar farm at the Coquelles terminal.

Once fully operational, these 80,000 ground-mounted photovoltaic panels will supply between 10% and 20% of Eurotunnel's annual electricity requirements directly to the railway catenary lines.
