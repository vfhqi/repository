  • Third most-diagnostic Q: how does management think about NBTXR3's label expansion breadth — is the strategy to chase every indication, or to build conviction in 2-3 anchoring markets where the clinical evidence is strongest? Answer discriminates between focused operator and growth-story seller
  • Structural uncertainty: the entire investment thesis rests on a single asset (NBTXR3), a single partnership (J&J / Janssen), and a regulatory timeline that management influences only at the margin — question design must stress-test all three

3. THEME §1 — REVENUE DRIVERS + VISIBILITY

J-front verdict: Revenue-side uncertainty is EXTREME — zero current product revenue from owned commercialisation; all near-term cash is partnership/milestone-dependent; the single most important revenue question is J&J opt-in timing and scope

Backlog visibility: No traditional order backlog exists; milestone payments from J&J are the closest functional equivalent — probing their conditionality is the key task here

Q1 — J&J milestone schedule

  • Question: "Can you walk us through the specific development milestones in the J&J agreement that trigger cash payments to Nanobiotix, with expected timing and probability-weighting you use internally?"
  • Expected answer: Management describes the headline figures publicly disclosed (~$1.03bn total deal value); likely deflects on internal probability-weighting
  • Good answer signature: Provides milestone-conditionality logic, distinguishes regulatory vs commercial milestones, gives management's own probability framework
  • Bad answer signature: Recites press release figures, declines to characterise probability, defers entirely to J&J's process
  • Follow-up probe: "Which single milestone, if missed, would most materially change the cash-to-profitability bridge?"
  • Diagnostic note: Bull case requires milestone conversion to be predictable; bad answer pushes toward false-friend profile where headline deal value masks high conditionality. Cross-ref Q19 ACH failure mode (a)

Q2 — Commercial opt-in scope

  • Question: "When J&J exercises its commercial opt-in — if it does — what is the realistic commercialisation geography and indication scope management expects, versus the theoretical maximum in the agreement?"
  • Expected answer: Management reiterates global rights language; likely avoids narrowing the scope
  • Good answer signature: Acknowledges that opt-in could be partial or indication-specific, has a view on which markets J&J is most likely to prioritise first
  • Bad answer signature: Treats opt-in as binary full-global event, no differentiation of probability by geography or indication
  • Follow-up probe: "Has J&J communicated any informal preference signals on geography — Europe-first vs US-first vs simultaneous?"
  • Diagnostic note: A narrowed opt-in (e.g. ex-US only, or single indication) would materially impair the revenue model vs consensus; this question surfaces that scenario explicitly. Cross-ref Q19 ACH failure mode (a)

Q3 — Pre-commercial revenue lines

  • Question: "Outside of the J&J agreement, are there any other partnership discussions, named-patient revenues, or access programmes generating cash today or in the next 24 months?"
  • Expected answer: Some named-patient or compassionate-use revenue exists; likely small; no second major partnership imminent
  • Good answer signature: Clear accounting of non-J&J revenue lines, even if small, with honest sizing
  • Bad answer signature: Vague references to "interest from other parties" with no quantification
  • Follow-up probe: "Is named-patient or expanded-access revenue treated as commercial precedent for pricing, or is it priced differently?"
  • Diagnostic note: Bear case requires J&J dependence to be near-total; any second revenue thread meaningfully de-risks the single-partnership concentration. Cross-ref Q19 bear-case probe

Q4 — Volume vs price split (forward)

  • Question: "When management models NBTXR3 peak revenue, is the volume assumption (number of patients treated) or the price-per-treatment assumption more variable — and what's the current range of internal price estimates?"
  • Expected answer: Management avoids specific price figures; references "premium positioning" relative to standard of care
  • Good answer signature: Differentiates pricing assumptions by indication, acknowledges reimbursement hurdle, gives a range even if wide
  • Bad answer signature: Declines to discuss pricing on grounds of competitive sensitivity or ongoing negotiations
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the analogue product/indication management uses as a pricing benchmark?"
  • Diagnostic note: Pricing opacity at this stage is a yellow flag — pharma managements with conviction on commercial value can usually anchor on a comp. Inability to do so suggests either lack of internal modelling rigour or a deliberately soft-pedalled commercial case

Sceptical bullet: Fluent answers on J&J milestone timing are the LEAST diagnostic — the company has been describing the same milestone roadmap publicly for years; polished delivery here likely reflects media training, not genuine visibility; the diagnostic signal is in the pause before the probability-weighting question, not the answer itself

4. THEME §2 — MARGIN TRAJECTORY + LEVERS

J-front verdict: Margin trajectory is currently irrelevant in a traditional sense — the company is pre-commercial; the operative margin question is burn rate management and the point at which the J&J economics flip the P&L; probing opex discipline and R&D allocation is the correct frame

Capex pacing: Almost entirely opex-driven business; capex is minimal; the margin question is really an opex question — headcount, CRO spend, and SG&A build timing

Q5 — Opex trajectory to first commercial milestone

  • Question: "What does the quarterly cash burn trajectory look like through the next four quarters, and which opex line — clinical, regulatory, or SG&A — is the most variable?"
  • Expected answer: Management gives a range consistent with recent quarterly burn; clinical CRO spend is cited as most variable
  • Good answer signature: Clean decomposition of burn by function, honest about upside/downside scenarios, flags key spending decision points
  • Bad answer signature: Deflects to "total runway" figure without decomposition, avoids flagging the variable elements
  • Follow-up probe: "If a key Phase III readout is delayed 6 months, what is the incremental opex impact?"
  • Diagnostic note: Inability to decompose opex is a red flag on financial management quality — a company at this stage should have intimate knowledge of its burn drivers. Cross-ref Q19 failure mode (b)

Q6 — R&D adequacy vs indication breadth

  • Question: "With trials running across head and neck, prostate, colorectal, and other indications, how does management prioritise R&D spend — is there a formal return-on-trial-spend framework, or is it driven by clinical opportunity as it arises?"
  • Expected answer: Management references clinical advisory input and partnership guidance; likely no formal return-on-spend framework publicly disclosed
  • Good answer signature: Acknowledges prioritisation trade-offs explicitly, can identify which indications would be deprioritised if budget were constrained, shows capital discipline
  • Bad answer signature: Frames all indications as equally important, no prioritisation language, suggests budget is sufficient for all simultaneously
  • Follow-up probe: "If J&J directed resource to two indications and you had to fund two more from your own balance sheet, which two would you choose and why?"
  • Diagnostic note: Inability to prioritise is a false-friend signal — it suggests management is optimising for optionality/story breadth rather than probability-weighted clinical ROI. Cross-ref Q19 ACH failure mode (c)

Q7 — SG&A build timing

  • Question: "At what development milestone does Nanobiotix begin building its own commercial infrastructure, versus relying entirely on J&J for commercialisation?"
  • Expected answer: Under current structure, J&J carries commercialisation — NANO's own SG&A build is limited or delayed until a jurisdiction where co-promote rights exist
  • Good answer signature: Clear delineation of which markets/situations would require NANO's own commercial build, with cost estimates
  • Bad answer signature: Vague answer about "building capabilities over time" without milestone-linked triggers
  • Follow-up probe: "Is there any scenario where Nanobiotix independently commercialises in France or Europe before a J&J full opt-in?"
  • Diagnostic note: An early SG&A build would be a material cash burn acceleration; a decision to NOT build own commercial infrastructure is a margin-positive signal for a pre-revenue company but also signals asymmetric power in the J&J relationship

Q8 — FX + cost structure geography

  • Question: "What proportion of Nanobiotix's cost base is EUR-denominated versus USD, and how does management think about the FX impact on the cash runway if EUR/USD moves materially?"
  • Expected answer: Predominantly EUR cost base (French headquarters, European CROs); J&J milestones likely USD-denominated; creates natural hedge question
  • Good answer signature: Clean split of currency exposure, active treasury policy described, sensitivity quantified
  • Bad answer signature: "We monitor FX" without quantification
  • Follow-up probe: "Has the recent EUR/USD move already impacted the effective value of milestones received or expected?"
  • Diagnostic note: Relatively low-diagnosticity question for the thesis, but CFO quality is revealed here — inability to quantify FX sensitivity at this company size is a financial management yellow flag

Sceptical bullet: Pre-commercial management teams frequently describe opex as "controlled" while quietly letting SG&A creep ahead of clinical milestones — the right diagnostic is to ask for the LAST QUARTER's actual vs budget variance on each opex line, not the forward projection

5. THEME §3 — CAPITAL ALLOCATION + BALANCE SHEET

J-front verdict: Capital allocation quality is the single most testable management attribute at this stage — a company with a J&J collaboration, limited own cash generation, and a binary asset must demonstrate exceptional capital discipline; any signs of empire-building or balance sheet sloppiness are disqualifying

Investment runway: The core question is how many months of cash remain under each J&J opt-in scenario — this number must be internalised before any other capital allocation question is meaningful

Q9 — Cash runway scenarios

  • Question: "Can management walk through the balance sheet under three scenarios: J&J full commercial opt-in on expected timeline; J&J opt-in delayed 18 months; J&J declines to opt-in entirely — what does cash look like at end-2026 in each?"
  • Expected answer: Management provides the base case comfortably; likely deflects or qualifies significantly on the no-opt-in scenario
  • Good answer signature: Engages all three scenarios with intellectual honesty; has a contingency plan for the downside scenario including equity raise optionality or bridge structure
  • Bad answer signature: Refuses to model the no-opt-in scenario on grounds that it's "not the expected outcome"; no downside financial planning language
  • Follow-up probe: "At what cash level — and at what stage — would the board authorise a dilutive equity raise versus seeking a new partnership?"
  • Diagnostic note: This is the MOST diagnostic capital allocation question — a management team that cannot articulate the downside cash scenario is either financially unsophisticated or deliberately avoiding the question; both are red flags. Cross-ref Q19 ACH failure mode (b)

Q10 — Returns hurdle on clinical spend

  • Question: "Does the board apply any formal returns hurdle or NPV threshold when authorising new clinical trial spending — or are trial decisions made on scientific/strategic grounds alone?"
  • Expected answer: Clinical-stage biotechs rarely apply formal returns hurdles; decisions tend to be scientifically and strategically driven; expect management to acknowledge this
  • Good answer signature: Acknowledges absence of formal hurdle rate but describes a framework (e.g. probability-adjusted NPV, partner interest signals, unmet medical need threshold) that approximates capital discipline
  • Bad answer signature: Claims to apply formal IRR hurdle rates — this would be inconsistent with typical clinical-stage biotech governance and would prompt a follow-up on the actual numbers used
  • Follow-up probe: "Has any clinical trial been stopped or de-prioritised in the last two years specifically on capital-return grounds?"
  • Diagnostic note: Stopping a trial on capital grounds is a positive operator signal — it demonstrates willingness to make hard prioritisation calls; absence of any trial stoppage may signal excessive optionality-seeking

Q11 — Balance sheet optionality / dilution discipline

  • Question: "If Nanobiotix needed to raise capital in the next 24 months, what mechanism would management prefer — equity at market, structured deals, royalty monetisation, or a new partnership — and what is the board's dilution tolerance?"
  • Expected answer: Likely preference for non-dilutive structures (partnership, royalty deal) before equity; equity would be "last resort" framing
  • Good answer signature: Clear dilution discipline expressed, preference ranking is coherent with the current share price and balance sheet, management has modelled what structures are available
  • Bad answer signature: "We have sufficient runway and don't anticipate needing to raise" — refusal to engage with scenario signals financial planning weakness
  • Follow-up probe: "Has the company ever discussed royalty monetisation with specialist royalty funds?"
  • Diagnostic note: Royalty monetisation is a sector-standard capital tool that is severely underutilised by founder-led biotechs — management awareness of this mechanism is a positive signal of financial sophistication

Sceptical bullet: Pre-commercial biotech CFOs are incentivised to project confidence in the base-case cash position — the investor's job is to ask the downside scenario question twice, in two different framings, and compare the answers; inconsistency between the answers is itself a red flag

6. THEME §4 — STRATEGY + COMPETITIVE POSITION

J-front verdict: Strategic positioning is strong in principle (first-in-class radioenhancer, multi-indication potential, large-pharma validation via J&J) but brittle in practice — a single-asset company's strategic options contract sharply if that asset's differentiation is challenged; the competitive position question is really a differentiation-durability question

Win condition: NBTXR3's commercial success requires it to be meaningfully better than current standard of care in at least one indication where reimbursement is feasible — the question is whether management has tested this proposition rigorously

Q12 — Moat durability vs competitive response

  • Question: "What is the most credible competitive threat to NBTXR3 in the next 5 years — whether from other radioenhancers, immune checkpoint combinations, or entirely different modalities — and how does management think about that risk?"
  • Expected answer: Management references IP protection, first-mover advantage, clinical data depth; likely acknowledges competitive activity in the radioenhancer/nanotechnology space but frames NBTXR3 as ahead
  • Good answer signature: Names specific competitors or competing modalities by name, acknowledges data gaps in NBTXR3's own clinical programme, has a view on how the competitive landscape evolves
  • Bad answer signature: Generic "we have strong IP and years of clinical data" response without engaging specific threats
  • Follow-up probe: "Which competing asset, if it reported positive Phase III data in the next 12 months, would most concern you?"
  • Diagnostic note: Naming a specific competitor is a positive signal — it demonstrates management has modelled the competitive landscape rather than dismissing it. Bull case requires durable clinical differentiation; if management cannot identify the most credible threat, that is itself a bear-case signal

Q13 — Indication prioritisation + label strategy

  • Question: "If regulators granted NBTXR3 a single initial indication approval, which indication would create the most commercial value — and does management's current trial portfolio reflect that prioritisation?"
  • Expected answer: Head and neck cancer or locally advanced rectal cancer likely cited as the anchoring indication given clinical stage; management may resist narrowing to one
  • Good answer signature: Willing to rank indications by commercial value, explains why the portfolio is structured as it is, acknowledges that some indications are optionality rather than core
  • Bad answer signature: Insists all indications are equally important — this signals an unwillingness to make hard commercial trade-offs
  • Follow-up probe: "If J&J's commercial team had to pick one indication to launch first, which do you believe they would choose and why?"
  • Diagnostic note: Framing the question through J&J's commercial lens rather than NANO's scientific lens often elicits more commercially grounded answers — it removes the option of hiding behind scientific positioning

Q14 — Regulatory positioning + FDA vs EMA strategy

  • Question: "Does Nanobiotix have a view on whether FDA approval or EMA approval would be more achievable first — and is there a sequencing strategy, or will the company pursue both simultaneously?"
  • Expected answer: Both agencies being pursued; likely emphasis on FDA given market size and J&J's US commercial infrastructure; EMA may be noted as potentially faster for certain pathways
  • Good answer signature: Has a nuanced view on regulatory sequencing, has received specific FDA feedback (breakthrough designation, SPA, etc.) that informs the timeline
  • Bad answer signature: "We're pursuing all regulatory pathways simultaneously" with no acknowledgement of sequencing trade-offs or resource constraints
  • Follow-up probe: "What specific FDA feedback has Nanobiotix received in the last 12 months that has changed or confirmed the regulatory timeline?"
  • Diagnostic note: FDA feedback is typically non-public but management can characterise whether feedback has been directionally positive or negative — inability or unwillingness to characterise this is a yellow flag on regulatory confidence

Sceptical bullet: A single-asset oncology company's strategic narrative is structurally optimistic — management is incentivised to present every indication as a commercial opportunity; the diagnostic question is which indications management would ABANDON first under resource pressure, not which it would pursue

7. THEME §5 — MANAGEMENT QUALITY + OPERATOR TRACK RECORD

J-front verdict: Laurent Levy is a scientist-founder CEO — strong scientific credibility, limited prior commercial-stage operating track record at scale; the key management quality question is whether there is commercial execution capability being built, or whether the company remains science-led at the expense of commercial rigour

Operator track record: Nanobiotix's track record to date is primarily one of clinical programme management and partnership execution — not commercial launch execution; this is a material gap in the track record

Q15 — Compensation structure + incentive alignment

  • Question (Power of incentives probe): "What proportion of CEO and CFO annual compensation is equity-linked, and are the equity vesting conditions tied to regulatory milestones, commercial milestones, or purely time-based?"
  • Expected answer: Equity compensation exists; vesting conditions likely time-based with possible milestone gates; typical for a pre-commercial biotech
  • Good answer signature: Milestone-linked equity vesting that aligns with the most important value-creation events (first regulatory approval, J&J opt-in, first commercial revenue); management can describe the structure clearly
  • Bad answer signature: Time-based vesting only, no milestone linkage — this weakens alignment between management behaviour and shareholder value creation; "we don't discuss compensation in this forum" is itself a signal
  • Follow-up probe: "Has the compensation committee ever adjusted vesting conditions based on updated clinical or commercial targets — and in which direction?"
  • Diagnostic note: Milestone-linked compensation is the strongest management quality signal available in a pre-commercial biotech — time-based vesting creates patience but not urgency; if vesting is entirely time-based, the incentive to compress timelines is weak

Q16 — Sandbagging discipline

  • Question: "Looking back at the timeline commitments Nanobiotix made 18-24 months ago — regulatory submission timings, trial readout dates, partnership milestones — which commitments were met, which were missed, and what caused the misses?"
  • Expected answer: Some slippage on trial timelines (this is normal in clinical oncology); management will likely have explanations that are scientifically defensible
  • Good answer signature: Owns the misses without excessive hedging, distinguishes internal misses from external/regulatory delays, demonstrates learning from past slippage
  • Bad answer signature: Attributes all delays to factors entirely outside management control (COVID, FDA delays, CRO failures) with no acknowledgement of internal contribution
  • Follow-up probe: "What process change, if any, did you make after the most significant timeline slip?"
  • Diagnostic note: The causal attribution test is a standard management quality diagnostic — management teams that consistently externalise causality are weaker operators; those that own internal factors even partially are stronger operators

Q17 — Commercial capability build

  • Question: "What commercial capabilities — market access, medical affairs, HEOR, reimbursement expertise — has Nanobiotix built internally, versus relying on J&J, and what's the planned build trajectory?"
  • Expected answer: Largely reliant on J&J for commercial capabilities; some internal medical affairs; limited HEOR capability in-house
  • Good answer signature: Has a clear view of which commercial capabilities NANO must own (to maintain strategic independence) versus which can be safely sourced from J&J; has made specific hires or investments in the highest-priority capability gaps
  • Bad answer signature: "J&J handles commercialisation" — complete delegation with no internal commercial capability development signals over-dependence on the partner
  • Follow-up probe: "If J&J's commercial priorities shifted away from NBTXR3 post-opt-in, would Nanobiotix have the internal capability to advocate for resources within the J&J system?"
  • Diagnostic note: Partner over-reliance is a structural false-friend signal in pharma partnerships — the commercial partner's priorities will diverge from the smaller company's at some point; internal commercial advocacy capability is a survival skill

Sceptical bullet: Founder-CEOs in biotech are structurally resistant to disclosing commercial weakness — the most diagnostic management quality signal is NOT what is said in the meeting, but what decisions were made BEFORE the meeting: have they hired a Chief Commercial Officer? Have they engaged reimbursement consultants? Have they done market access work in France (their home market) before any global launch?

8. THEME §6 — RISK + DOWNSIDE (incl. pre-mortem cross-ref)

J-front verdict: The downside case for Nanobiotix is not a slow deterioration but a binary collapse — J&J declines to fully opt-in, NBTXR3 fails to achieve reimbursement in key markets, or the clinical data in a pivotal indication disappoints; the risk section must stress-test all three simultaneously

Bear-case probe: The deepest bear case is not clinical failure (low probability per current data) but commercial irrelevance — NBTXR3 works, gets approved, but is not reimbursed at a price that creates shareholder value; this risk is severely underweighted in typical analyst coverage

Q18 — Reimbursement risk

  • Question: "Has Nanobiotix done formal health technology assessment pre-work in France, Germany, or the UK — and what preliminary feedback has the company received on NBTXR3's cost-effectiveness profile?"
  • Expected answer: Some early HTA engagement, probably not formal dossier submission; company likely frames reimbursement as J&J's responsibility post-opt-in; may cite positive unmet-need framing
  • Good answer signature: Has done preliminary HTA work, has received directional feedback from payers, can characterise the incremental-cost-effectiveness ratio challenge; has a reimbursement strategy that goes beyond "J&J will handle it"
  • Bad answer signature: "Reimbursement is J&J's responsibility after opt-in" — complete delegation on the most critical commercial risk is a major red flag
  • Follow-up probe: "What comparator would NICE or HAS use in the cost-effectiveness assessment for your lead indication — and is that comparator favourable or unfavourable to NBTXR3?"
  • Diagnostic note: Cross-ref Q19 ACH failure mode (c) — clinical differentiation insufficient for premium pricing; reimbursement rejection is the most plausible commercial failure mode and is almost never adequately probed in standard investor meetings

Q19 — J&J relationship health

  • Question: "How frequently does Nanobiotix leadership engage with J&J's EMEA and US commercial teams — not the science teams — and has the tone or intensity of that engagement changed in the last 6 months?"
  • Expected answer: Management will characterise the relationship as strong; frequency of scientific engagement likely higher than commercial; may flag J&J restructuring/reorganisation as a topic
  • Good answer signature: Can distinguish scientific vs commercial engagement frequency, notes any shifts in J&J's focus, is honest about the asymmetry in the relationship
  • Bad answer signature: "The relationship is excellent and we are fully aligned" — uniformly positive framing with no acknowledgement of normal partnership tension is not credible in a pharma collaboration
  • Follow-up probe: "J&J has undergone significant restructuring in its pharmaceutical division — has this affected the team working on NBTXR3 at all?"
  • Diagnostic note: J&J organisational changes are a well-known risk vector in pharma collaborations — personnel changes at the partner destroy institutional knowledge and can slow development; management that is unaware of or dismissive of J&J's internal changes is not monitoring the partnership adequately

Q20 — False-friend probe: option value vs execution vehicle

  • Question: "Is there a scenario in which Nanobiotix's most rational strategic outcome is to be acquired outright by J&J or a third party, rather than to operate as an independent commercial entity — and has the board ever discussed this explicitly?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely deflect; "we are building an independent company" is the standard answer; may acknowledge it as one of many outcomes
  • Good answer signature: Acknowledges the M&A scenario as a legitimate value-creation path, has a view on what conditions would make it attractive (price, timing, pipeline stage), does not treat independence as an intrinsic good
  • Bad answer signature: Dismisses the M&A scenario entirely or treats it as inconsistent with the company's mission — founder over-attachment to independence is a capital allocation failure mode
  • Follow-up probe: "At what clinical or regulatory stage would Nanobiotix be most attractive as an acquisition target — and is that stage consistent with where you are today?"
  • Diagnostic note: Founder-CEO resistance to M&A at a logical exit point is a classic Concept A power-of-incentives misalignment — the CEO's incentive to remain CEO diverges from shareholders' interest in maximising exit price; this question surfaces that tension directly

Sceptical bullet — ACH items NOT covered by this memo:
- The question of whether J&J's opt-in mechanics include any right to cherry-pick specific indications while declining others is NOT fully covered (Q2 approaches it but does not ask for the contractual mechanism directly)
- The question of manufacturing scale-up risk — NBTXR3 is a nanomaterial requiring specialist manufacturing; supply chain constraints at commercial scale are NOT probed here
- The question of whether NBTXR3's clinical data is reproducible across different radiation equipment types (linac vs proton) is NOT probed — this is a regulatory and commercial risk that is rarely discussed publicly

9. CROSS-CUTTING META + NON-OBVIOUS QUESTIONS (LOAD-BEARING)

J-front verdict: The most valuable question slots are those management has NOT been asked before — consensus meeting questions probe the milestone schedule and J&J relationship; non-obvious angles probe the commercial reimbursement architecture, founder psychology, and what management WORRIES about that it does not volunteer

Non-obvious angle 1 — The clinician adoption question:

  • Question: "What is the current NBTXR3 utilisation rate among radiation oncologists who have been trained on the product — and what is the most common reason a trained clinician has NOT used it in clinical practice after training?"
  • Expected answer: Management does not track this at a granular level; likely references a small set of key opinion leaders rather than broad adoption data
  • Good answer: Has data on trained-vs-active utilisation rates, can characterise the adoption barrier (workflow, patient selection, comfort with injection)
  • Bad answer: References number of patients treated in trials without engaging the commercialisation-readiness question
  • Diagnostic note: Training-to-active-use conversion is the most lagging indicator of commercial readiness — a product that requires complex training and generates low conversion rates among trained users has a commercial adoption problem that milestone schedules will not surface

Non-obvious angle 2 — The "what keeps you up at night" probe (⚡):

  • Question: "Forget the clinical pipeline for a moment — what is the single operational or financial risk that you personally worry about most, that you don't think investors currently appreciate?"
  • Expected answer: Management will offer a carefully selected "safe" risk (regulatory timeline, competition from other modalities)
  • Good answer: Volunteers something genuinely non-consensus — manufacturing constraint, a specific clinician adoption barrier, a J&J internal process risk
  • Bad answer: Gives a textbook risk factor answer (regulatory uncertainty, clinical trial risk) that mirrors the risk section of the 20-F
  • Follow-up probe: "You answered X — on a scale of 1-10, how likely is that risk to materialise in the next 18 months?"
  • Diagnostic note: The "what worries you" question is diagnostic in the follow-up, not the initial answer — the initial answer is rehearsed; the probability-quantification follow-up is where unpreparedness shows

WH Smith probe — The counter-narrative question (⚡):

  • Question: "What is the strongest bear case for Nanobiotix that a well-informed sceptic would make — and which element of that bear case do you find hardest to refute?"
  • Expected answer: Management will present a version of the bear case they are comfortable with; the hardest element to refute will reveal genuine uncertainty
  • Good answer: Engages the bear case directly, identifies a specific weak point (e.g. reimbursement risk, J&J opt-in conditionality), shows intellectual honesty
  • Bad answer: "We don't recognise the bear case" or presents only trivially easy-to-refute objections
  • Follow-up probe: "If the bear case materialised on that specific point — the one hardest to refute — what would management do next?"
  • Diagnostic note: The contingency-planning follow-up is the most diagnostic part — management with a credible contingency plan for their hardest bear case are better operators; those without one are either under-prepared or in denial. This question cross-refs Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 3 — the "and yet" framing

Multiple-truths probe (Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 3):

  • Question: "It seems like both of these things can be true simultaneously: NBTXR3 has genuinely exciting clinical data, AND the commercial pathway is harder and longer than the current consensus timeline assumes. Which of those two propositions do you think investors currently under-appreciate — the strength of the clinical data, or the length/difficulty of the commercial pathway?"
  • Expected answer: Management will typically push toward "clinical data is stronger than investors appreciate"
  • Diagnostic note: If management pushes toward the commercial pathway being underappreciated as difficult, this is a rare admission of bears' strongest argument and should be taken seriously; most managements will not make this admission voluntarily — which is why the question is structured as a forced choice between two non-dismissible options

Incentive probe — founder equity position:

  • Question: "What is the CEO's current equity stake in Nanobiotix, and has there been any insider selling in the last 12 months?"
  • Expected answer: Public information, but valuable to elicit directly — management will typically note that equity is being held
  • Good answer: CEO confirms holding position, no selling, expresses confidence in long-term value; can discuss dilution from grants
  • Bad answer: Defensive response, references that selling was "pre-planned" or "for personal reasons"
  • Diagnostic note: Insider selling pattern at a clinical inflection point is a Concept A power-of-incentives signal that is more diagnostic than any forward-guidance statement

Edge-detection probe — manufacturing:

  • Question: "NBTXR3 is a physical nanomaterial product — what does the manufacturing process look like at commercial scale, who is the CMO, and what is the capacity constraint at 10x current clinical volumes?"
  • Expected answer: Management will note contract manufacturing relationships; likely limited public disclosure on CMO identity; commercial-scale capacity not yet sized
  • Good answer: Has a clear scale-up plan, has validated the manufacturing process at commercial scale or has a defined pathway to do so, can name the key capacity constraint
  • Bad answer: "Manufacturing is not a constraint we are focused on at this stage" — deferral of commercial manufacturing planning is a pre-commercial biotech failure mode
