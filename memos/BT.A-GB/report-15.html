J-front verdict: The single largest narrative-vs-reality disconnect is fibre-monetisation velocity — management narrates a FTTP flywheel generating ARPU uplift and FCF inflection; expert network, competitor data, and tone analysis collectively suggest the timeline is extended and the magnitude is compressed

Narrative claim — fibre flywheel: Management's public narrative: build 25m premises → take-up accelerates → ARPU uplifts → FCF inflects to £2bn+ by late-FY27

- Disconnect identified: The flywheel is real in architecture but the speed of rotation is materially slower than management's timeline implies — each wheel-turn (take-up, ARPU, FCF) is delayed, compounding the shortfall

Narrative claim — Enterprise as stable high-margin base: Management describes BT Enterprise as "resilient with government-anchored revenues"
- Reality signal: Expert 3 (Enterprise account director) describes mid-market churn as "slow-burn accelerant," four divisional leadership changes in five years, and loss of pricing power to Vodafone Business and COLT [Cross-ref: §2 + §6]
- Disconnect identified: Enterprise is stable at the large-account top; it is eroding at the mid-market base — and management's "resilient" framing elides this distinction [IAJA]

Narrative claim — cost transformation delivering: Management consistently cites a "£3bn cost saving programme" and references CWU settlement as "absorbed"
- Reality signal: Labour costs rose £150m+ from CWU settlement; network build cost per premises has drifted from £300-350 to £400+ (management-revised) to £600-700 in semi-rural zones (Expert 4); "absorbed" is doing a lot of work [Cross-ref: §1 + §2]
- Disconnect identified: Gross savings are real; net savings are smaller than the headline implies because cost inflation (labour, semi-rural build) is partially offsetting efficiency gains [IAJA]

False-friend pattern: BT Group is not a classic false-friend (management saying right things while insiders dump shares) — selling is modest and vesting-driven; but there is a softer false-friend pattern: management uses "normalised" language consistently to package a story that in reported terms is more complicated — normalised FCF, normalised EBITDA, "absorbed" costs all serve to redirect attention away from the reported numbers [Cross-ref: §1 + §3]
