[W] VOD-GB — Earnings & Fundamental Trends: Peer Comparative Analysis [C] @ 29-Apr-26

Analyst: Claude [C] Sub-agent | Date: 29 April 2026 | Stage: IG / Triaging | Ticker: VOD-GB (Vodafone Group plc)

KEY FINDINGS

- Germany is the pivotal swing factor, and the worst is genuinely behind it. The MDU/TV regulation hit (-3 million subscribers, -5% service revenue FY25) is a now-waning one-off. Q1-Q3 FY26 data confirm sequential recovery (+0.5% → +0.7% Germany service revenue growth), with the 1&1 wholesale deal reaching full run-rate in Q4 FY26. This is structurally important: Germany is ~32% of group service revenue, so even modest recovery has disproportionate group-level impact.
- VOD is decisively upgrading guidance in a peer group where most operators merely reaffirm. FY26 EBITDAaL guidance was lifted to the upper end of the €11.3–11.6bn range (from initial €11.0–11.3bn), making VOD one of the few peers in this cycle to guide up rather than down or sideways.
- Organic service revenue growth of 5%+ is peer-leading at the headline level, but the composition is flattered. Türkiye and Africa contribute the overwhelming bulk of the organic delta. Core European organic growth (ex-Turkey, ex-Africa) is more modest and masks the Germany drag that persisted through FY25 and H1 FY26.
- Deutsche Telekom remains the structural compounder of the group, with 4.7% organic EBITDA AL growth in 2025, 6% guided for 2026, EPS of €2.00 growing to €2.20, and the T-Mobile US engine providing persistent earnings uplift unavailable to pure-play European peers.
- Reported EPS at VOD is deeply misleading. FY25 saw a basic loss per share of -15.94 eurocents due to a €4.5bn non-cash impairment (Germany and Romania). Adjusted basic EPS improved from 7.47 to 7.87 eurocents — a modest but real progression. The gap between GAAP and adjusted metrics at VOD is wider than any peer and warrants persistent scrutiny.
- VOD's balance sheet is now credibly cleaner post-Spain/Italy disposals, with net debt of ~€22.4bn and leverage at 2.0x (bottom of the 2.25–2.75x target band), supporting a €4bn total buyback programme and a 2.5% dividend per share growth commitment for FY26.
- TIM is the most improved peer on margin trajectory. EBITDAaL up 6.5% in 2025 with a €400m buyback announced — this is a meaningful inflection for a stock that was structurally distressed through the NetCo disposal process.
- BT is the most constrained peer near-term, with revenue still declining (-2% FY25), EBITDA flat-to-marginally-up, and a long capex cycle (Openreach build) absorbing cash before free cash flow begins to normalise post-FY27.
- Sector-wide tailwind from consolidation narrative is real but priced in at many operators. J.P. Morgan's upgrade of European telecom outlook is driven primarily by regulatory liberalisation prospects; actual operational inflection is less clear. Operators are still largely cutting their way to earnings growth rather than growing revenue.

1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Overall Assessment: YELLOW

- Fundamental momentum ranking (top to bottom): Deutsche Telekom > TIM > Orange > Vodafone > Telefónica > Telia > BT. DTE is the clear leader by combination of revenue growth, EPS trajectory, and margin expansion. TIM has the most improved trajectory. VOD sits in the upper-middle of the group, ahead of TEF, Telia, and BT on a combined momentum basis.
- VOD's position within the peer group is improving but still transitional. The portfolio transformation (Spain and Italy disposed, UK consolidation via VMO2 on firmer footing) is largely complete. Management has declared the "building site closed" and guided toward a new growth phase. The market is beginning to believe it; the share price gained ~60-70% over the 12 months to early 2026.
- The three most significant trend reversals/accelerations in the peer group over L12M:
- (1) VOD Germany MDU drag beginning to fade — the most important single reversal for the name;
- (2) TIM delivery of all 2025 guidance targets for the fourth consecutive year, including EBITDAaL +6.5%, marking a clean break from its chronic underperformance history;
- (3) DTE's consistent upward revision cycle — Q3 2025 represented the third successive guidance raise, cementing its reputation as the most reliable compounder in European telecoms.
- Sector-wide tailwinds: Consolidation narrative (J.P. Morgan upgraded sector outlook; multiple M&A processes in Germany, Nordics, Italy plausible); copper switch-off cost reduction; fibre penetration monetisation; 5G wholesale deals (1&1 at VOD is the clearest near-term example); AI-driven cost reduction (customer care, network management).
- Sector-wide headwinds: ARPU growth remains structurally challenged — in real terms, European mobile ARPU has declined as operators have absorbed inflation; competitive intensity in Italy and Spain holding prices down; capex intensity remains elevated across the group as fibre and 5G rollouts continue.
- VOD-specific risks: Germany retail ARPU pressure (competitive mobile market); Türkiye/Africa organic growth flatters group headline but introduces FX and geopolitical risk; large non-GAAP adjustment gap creates optics risk; the VMO2 joint venture is not fully controlled and adds complexity to UK reporting.
- The strategic pivot is coherent: Della Valle has executed the disposal programme (Spain, Italy), crystallised €4bn+ of buybacks, initiated cost reduction (11,000 headcount by FY26), and redirected focus to markets where VOD has structural positions (Germany, UK via JV, Türkiye, Africa). The question is whether operational execution in Germany is now genuinely self-sustaining or dependent on the 1&1 windfall.
- VOD vs. the peer group on a "quality of growth" basis: DTE's growth is organic and balanced across geographies. VOD's group organic growth is concentrated in Türkiye/Africa and supplemented by a one-time wholesale deal. Orange's growth is steady but unspectacular in core France. Telefónica is the weakest on top-line momentum. TIM is improving fastest from the weakest base.
- Consensus and analyst commentary: Analysts broadly view VOD as a value recovery play with credible guidance. The key debate is whether Germany can sustain growth post-1&1 normalisation, and whether the cost savings will fall through to free cash flow or be reinvested. The share price re-rating to date reflects portfolio simplification more than fundamental earnings acceleration.
- Key sector theme for 2026: Consolidation. Germany (possible DTE/Vodafone anything? Unlikely. More likely within Drillisch/1&1 dynamics), Nordics (Telia), Italy (TIM/WINDTRE). The regulatory environment under the new EU competition commissioner is materially more permissive than the prior regime.

- VOD organic service revenue of 5.1% (FY25) is the strongest headline in the peer group, but this is almost entirely driven by Türkiye (hyperinflationary environment, lira-denominated pricing) and Africa. Stripping these out, European organic service revenue growth is materially lower. Germany dragged the group for the entirety of FY25 with -5% service revenue, masking solid performance in UK, Italy (before disposal), and other European markets.
- Q1 FY26 (to June 2025): Group organic service revenue +5.5%, with Germany still negative but sequentially improving. Türkiye and Africa remain key swing contributors.
- Q2 FY26 (to September 2025, H1 results): Group service revenue up 7.3% at the headline; organic +5.8%. Germany service revenue turned slightly positive (+0.5%), the first positive quarter in over a year. This was the key catalyst for the November 2025 guidance upgrade.
- Q3 FY26 (to December 2025): Organic service revenue +5.4% (slight deceleration from Q2's +5.8%, partially seasonal and partially reflecting Germany staying at only +0.7%). The 1&1 wholesale deal contributing to mobile service revenue +2.8% in Germany.
- DTE's 3.8% organic service revenue growth is more genuinely balanced — T-Mobile US domestic momentum, German fixed-line fibre penetration gains, and improving European subsidiary performance. DTE's growth has less FX/hyperinflationary distortion.
- Orange's 0.9% reported revenue growth masks a retail services line growing at +2.2%. The offset is declining wholesale revenues (-3.9%). France itself is growing only modestly, with the bulk of momentum in Africa/MEA (+13.9% EBITDAaL) and Europe ex-France (+3.2% EBITDAaL). MasOrange consolidation (Spanish JV with MásMóvil) expected to be a 2026-onwards earnings driver.
- Telefónica's €35.1bn revenue (2025), +1.5% organic constant-currency, reflects the disposal of Hispam operations which distorts reported numbers. Brazil (via Vivo) and Spain remain core; the ARPU improvement story in Spain is real but incremental.
- BT's -2% reported revenue decline (FY25) is the weakest in the group. Consumer and Openreach revenues are broadly flat to slightly declining; legacy Enterprise and Global revenues continue to roll off. However, the EBITDA improvement (+1%) demonstrates that cost reduction is ahead of revenue. BT's recovery is a FY27-28 story, not FY26.
- Telia's flat revenues (0%, CY25) and +1.5% service revenue growth miss the ~2% guidance target, with headwinds in Norway and Finland specifically. Telia is the most structurally constrained of the peers in terms of geographic concentration in Nordic markets with intense competition.
- Pricing power assessment: VOD has demonstrated some mobile ARPU growth in UK and Africa. Germany mobile ARPU is under competitive pressure. Orange France is achieving moderate price increases. DTE is passing through price in both German fixed-line and US wireless. BT has had to be more cautious given political sensitivity around Openreach access pricing. Overall sector pricing power is improving from a very low base as inflation partially works through.
- Volume trends: VOD's retained MDU customer base (4.2m of 8.5m) is a base from which to rebuild in Germany fixed-line. Mobile net adds across the group remain solid in Africa and UK. Europe ex-Germany is broadly stable to slightly positive in mobile market share terms.
- Sceptical interpretation: VOD's headline organic growth is largely a Türkiye/Africa story. Once those contributions are normalised (especially if lira strengthens or African currencies weaken), the underlying European organic growth rate is probably in the 2-3% range — consistent with sector norms, not peer-leading. Furthermore, the 1&1 wholesale contribution is a migration-driven step-up, not a recurring volume trend; the Germany ARPU compression in mobile remains unresolved.
- Forward guidance credibility: VOD's upgrade to the upper end of EBITDAaL guidance (€11.3-11.6bn) for FY26 appears credible given H1+Q3 year-to-date EBITDAaL of €8.5bn (+5.3%), implying H2 needs to deliver ~€2.8-3.1bn — in line with historical H2 phasing. DTE's guidance of €47.4bn EBITDA AL for 2026 is similarly well-supported by quarterly run-rates.

- VOD's margin profile is complex to interpret cleanly because the group reports EBITDAaL as a percentage of service revenue, not total revenue, and the geographic mix (high-margin African and Turkish operations vs. lower-margin European fixed-line) complicates peer comparison. On a group EBITDAaL basis, FY25 was €10.9bn on revenues of ~€37.4bn, implying approximately 29% reported margin. This is below DTE (~43%), BT (~40%), Telia (~39%), and closer to Orange (~31%) and Telefónica (~34%).
- Margin trajectory for VOD is improving. H1 FY26 EBITDAaL of €5.7bn on total revenues of approximately €18-19bn implies meaningful organic margin expansion. The 6.8% organic EBITDAaL growth significantly outpaces the ~5-6% organic service revenue growth, confirming positive operating leverage.
- Germany margin drag is the key suppressor. H1 FY25 Germany adjusted EBITDA fell 9.3% year-on-year (-8.2pp from MDU impact alone). This was the primary driver of FY25 group organic EBITDAaL growth being only 2.5% despite 5.1% service revenue growth — a meaningful gap that indicates the Germany drag was swallowing operating leverage gains elsewhere. As Germany stabilises in FY26, this gap should close.
- DTE's margin leadership reflects T-Mobile US operating leverage (strong mobile market share gains, high ARPU US market) plus German domestic cost management. At ~43% EBITDA AL margin on a very large revenue base, DTE is structurally in a different class. The guidance of ~6% organic EBITDA growth for 2026 implies ongoing margin expansion.
- TIM's margin improvement (+6.5% EBITDAaL to €3.7bn in 2025) from a lower base is the most dramatic trajectory in the group. The NetCo disposal (FiberCop sale to KKR completed) has fundamentally restructured the cost base, removing low-margin infrastructure revenues and concentrating the business on higher-margin service provision. TIM's 2026 guidance of +5-6% EBITDAaL growth from this cleaner base is credible.
- Telia's margin expansion (39.4% vs 38.2% in 2024) is impressive given flat revenues, driven entirely by cost efficiency. However, this model has limits — you cannot keep cutting costs faster than revenue indefinitely. The 2026 EBITDA growth guidance of ~3% on service revenue growth of ~2% is conservative and achievable.
- BT's EBITDA improvement despite revenue decline is also a cost-transformation story. The £5bn "digital first" programme and legacy headcount reductions are the driver. However, free cash flow is heavily constrained by Openreach capex (£5.0bn for FY26), and BT's £1.5bn normalised free cash flow is the weakest in the group relative to EBITDA.
- Orange's steady margin profile — EBITDAaL up 3.8% to €12.47bn — reflects disciplined management of the French cost base combined with incremental gains from Africa/MEA. France itself is a modest margin expander; the structural question is whether the MasOrange JV integration delivers the synergies promised.
- Decomposition of VOD margin moves: The primary driver of FY26 margin improvement is (1) Germany MDU drag fading, (2) 1&1 wholesale incremental contribution (high-margin), (3) group cost reduction programme (€1.4bn annual savings target by FY26), partially offset by (4) UK/JV complexity and (5) continued German retail ARPU pressure. There is genuine operating leverage in the model as fixed-cost networks absorb revenue growth.
- Sceptical interpretation: VOD's margin expansion in FY26 is not primarily driven by underlying demand and pricing power — it is driven by the non-recurrence of the MDU hit and the addition of a wholesale contract. Absent these, underlying European margin expansion would be more modest. The cost savings programme (11,000 headcount reduction) is real, but the timeline is FY23-26 so most of the benefit has already been taken. The question for FY27 is whether there is further self-help or whether the margin depends on revenue growth to hold its gains.

- VOD's adjusted basic EPS of 7.87 eurocents (FY25, up from 7.47 eurocents in FY24) is a modest but positive progression (+5.4%) on an adjusted basis. This is the "real" earnings metric for VOD as the reported figure is deeply distorted by non-cash impairment charges (€4.5bn in FY25, primarily Germany and Romania write-downs).
- The reported basic loss per share of -15.94 eurocents is a significant red flag from an accounting perspective. The magnitude of the impairment (€4.5bn) relative to the adjusted earnings base signals that VOD has been over-earning on an asset base whose carrying value has now been partially corrected. This is not economically meaningful for ongoing earnings, but it is a genuine signal about the quality of Germany assets and the risk that further impairments could follow if Germany does not recover.
- VOD buyback programme: €4bn total (€2bn from Spain proceeds, €2bn from Italy proceeds), of which €3bn is complete (as of H1 FY26 results). This is modestly EPS-accretive as the share count reduces. For context, with a market cap in the range of £25-30bn at current prices, a €4bn buyback represents roughly 13-16% of market cap — material, but not the primary driver of EPS growth.
- DTE's EPS trajectory is structurally the strongest: €2.00 adjusted EPS in 2025, guiding to ~€2.20 for 2026 (+10% growth). This is driven by T-Mobile US earnings growth, German domestic EBITDA progression, and a modest share count reduction. DTE's EPS growth is largely organic rather than financially engineered.
- Telefónica's negative reported net result (€-4.3bn in 2025) reflects non-recurring restructuring costs and Hispam disposal impacts. On an underlying basis, TEF is modestly cash-generative but faces structural headwinds (high leverage ~3x, LatAm exposure, pension costs). EPS trajectory is the weakest in the peer group on any honest reading.
- TIM's positive net profit of €519m (2025) — representing the first full clean year post-NetCo disposal — is a significant milestone. The €400m buyback is an explicit signal of capital return normalisation. EPS trajectory is improving from a very depressed base.
- Telia's reported EPS halved (SEK 0.90 from SEK 1.80) due to non-cash items and lower exceptional gains. The underlying FCF performance (SEK 9.6bn vs. SEK 8bn guided) is the better earnings quality signal.
- Leverage considerations: VOD's net debt/EBITDAaL of 2.0x (post-disposal) is the lowest in many years and comfortably within the 2.25-2.75x target band. This gives significant headroom for capital returns and absorbs the risk of Germany volatility. By contrast, Telefónica at ~3x and TIM (historically very high) are more constrained. DTE carries higher absolute debt but is strongly cash-generative through T-Mobile US dividends.
- Sceptical interpretation: VOD's adjusted EPS improvement is partly a function of lower net financing costs (debt reduction from disposals) and a smaller share count. Revenue-driven EPS growth is not the primary driver. If the disposal proceeds are now deployed (buybacks almost complete), the future EPS growth trajectory is more dependent on Germany recovery and organic free cash flow growth than on financial engineering. The €2.6-2.8bn adjusted FCF guidance for FY26 (then revised to deliver upper end at €2.4-2.6bn) shows modest growth, insufficient to dramatically rerate on a FCF/share basis without continued buybacks.
- Beat/miss record: VOD met or upgraded its FY25 and H1 FY26 guidance on EBITDAaL. Q3 FY26 reaffirmed guidance at the upper end. This is a sequential improvement from prior years where Germany drag caused repeated disappointments.

This represents a ~3% upgrade relative to the midpoint of initial guidance — meaningful for a business this size.
- Management conservatism assessment: The pattern of initial guidance → H1 upgrade → Q3 reaffirm is a classic "confirm and upgrade" cycle that suggests management had a clear line of sight on Germany recovery trajectory and chose to guide conservatively at the outset. This is the behaviour of a management team rebuilding credibility after the Germany disappointments of FY24-25.
- DTE's guidance record is exemplary — three consecutive upgrades in 2025 is a peer-leading track record. The move from initial 2025 organic EBITDA guidance to the final +4.7% outcome, and then the strong 2026 guidance of +6% organic, are underpinned by T-Mobile US's consistently strong execution.
- Orange raised EBITDAaL guidance during 2025 from low-single-digit to "at least +3.5%", and delivered +3.8%. The consistency between guidance and delivery is good. The 2028 CAGR guidance for adjusted EPS of ~10% per annum is ambitious and depends on MasOrange synergy delivery.

Revenue guidance was met, but EBITDA grew more slowly than expected in early quarters before Q4 acceleration. The pattern of downgrade-then-recover is less reassuring than the DTE or VOD upgrade cycles.
- BT's guidance is essentially flat — revenue ~£20bn, EBITDA £8.2-8.3bn, FCF ~£1.5bn for FY26. The investor pitch is that FY27 begins the capex reduction and FCF inflection. There is no guidance upgrade story here; BT is in a "proof of concept" phase where investors are waiting for capex to roll off.
- Sceptical interpretation of VOD guidance: The upgrade to upper-end guidance is real, but the range itself (€11.3-11.6bn) is only €300m wide. The upgrade from initial midpoint of €11.15bn to the new upper end of €11.6bn is a ~4% increase — positive but not dramatic. More importantly, the FY26 guided FCF has narrowed (initial €2.6-2.8bn, then "upper end" of €2.4-2.6bn implied), raising questions about whether FCF conversion is as clean as EBITDAaL momentum suggests.
- Patterns in guidance beats/misses (L12M): VOD has beaten EBITDAaL trajectory at every reporting point in FY26. This is a change from FY25 (where Germany was consistently a source of negative surprise). The risk of re-entry of Germany as a downside driver is real but diminishing.

7. SECTOR & VALUE CHAIN CONTEXT

Rating: YELLOW

- European telecom sector fundamentals are modestly improving but not transformationally so. The ING outlook for 2026 projects ~2% top-line growth and ~2.5% EBITDA growth across the sector — this is incremental recovery, not a cyclical surge. The improvement is driven more by cost discipline and the fading of specific drags (MDU regulation, COVID-era pricing pressure) than by genuine demand acceleration.
- Consolidation is the most powerful sector catalyst and is now genuinely in play. J.P. Morgan's upgrade of the European telecom sector in early 2026 is explicitly driven by M&A prospects under the new competition commissioner. The most credible near-term consolidation scenarios are Italy (TIM/WINDTRE, WINDTRE has been exploring options), Nordics (Telia/Norwegian operators), and potentially Belgium (Digi entry creating a 4-player market that is likely to consolidate). Germany consolidation is the most transformative scenario for VOD but politically complex (DTE dominant).
- End-market health for VOD: The UK/Germany/Africa/Türkiye exposure is mixed. UK business services growth is solid (GDP stable, digital demand structural). Germany enterprise is performing well but retail is competitive. Africa has structural tailwinds (penetration, data usage growth, payments integration). Türkiye provides high nominal growth but real economic volatility.
- Fibre: competitive differentiator or commoditisation risk? European operators are collectively spending heavily on FTTH rollouts (Orange 65.5m homes connectable; BT via Openreach 16m+ passed; VOD via VMO2 in UK; DTE own German network). The risk is that after the build, pricing power does not materialise because regulation constrains access pricing. VOD's German fixed network (HFC/cable plus fibre overlay) is a structural asset, but the MDU episode illustrated the regulatory vulnerability.
- 5G monetisation remains elusive. Only 18% of European consumers have opted for premium 5G plans. Operators are generating wholesale revenue (1&1 at VOD) but the direct consumer ARPU uplift from 5G is minimal. The long-term monetisation case (network slicing, private 5G, edge compute) is intact but remains a 2027-2030 story.
- AI and automation are early-stage margin contributors. All operators reference AI-driven customer care and network management as cost reduction levers. The quantified savings at this stage are modest, but the direction is clear. Telefónica and BT are furthest advanced in published AI transformation programmes.
- Competitive intensity: mixed. Italy and Spain remain intensely competitive with low ARPU. Germany is moderating as the market has effectively consolidated to DTE/VOD/Telefónica Deutschland. UK is a three-player post-VMO2 merger market (BT/EE, Virgin Media O2, Three/Vodafone UK). Nordic markets face some new entry (Digi in Sweden, Belgium) which is disruptive at the margin.
- Cyclical vs. structural: The improvement in European telecom fundamentals is primarily structural (consolidation, cost automation, copper switch-off) rather than cyclical. This is important — it suggests improvement is durable but not dramatic. The sector is not a macro recovery play; it is a slow structural compounder with M&A optionality.
- Regulatory environment: The shift in EU competition policy toward permitting consolidation is the single most important regulatory development. Historically, the EU blocked intra-market mergers (UK, Denmark cases). The new permissiveness creates value optionality for operators with scale, particularly VOD in Germany (where any consolidation of the market from 3 to 2 players would be highly value-accretive).
- Capital intensity remains elevated. Fibre and 5G capex peaks are roughly 2024-2027 for most operators. Post-peak, free cash flow should accelerate. This is the fundamental medium-term bull case for the sector — BT's FCF guidance of ~£3bn by 2030, VOD's guided FCF growth, and DTE's €19.8bn FCF for 2026 all point in the same direction.

8. QUALITY OF EARNINGS & ACCOUNTING

Rating: ORANGE

- VOD's non-GAAP/GAAP gap is the largest in the peer group and requires persistent vigilance. The gap between adjusted basic EPS (7.87 eurocents) and reported basic EPS (-15.94 eurocents) in FY25 is driven by impairment charges (€4.5bn), restructuring costs, and other adjustments. This is a wider gap than any of the other five peers on an absolute basis.
- The impairment charges are genuine economic signals. The €4.5bn write-down of Germany and Romania assets reflects the fact that carrying values (built up through the Unitymedia/Liberty Global cable acquisition) were too high given the MDU revenue loss and competitive environment. This is not a cosmetic adjustment — it represents real capital misallocation over the prior decade.
- Adjusted free cash flow is the most decision-relevant metric for VOD, computed as free cash flow before spectrum, restructuring costs, and integration capital. FY25 adjusted FCF of €2.5bn is the headline. The gap between operating cash inflow (~€15.4bn) and adjusted FCF (~€2.5bn) is large but largely explained by capex (approximately €7-8bn) and working capital movements.
