  • Dominant signal: AI tools are already reshaping the production layer of investment research (data aggregation, earnings modelling, transcript summarisation) faster than the judgement layer (conviction formation, variant perception, portfolio sizing)
  • Most informative finding #1: The productivity gain from AI in research is real but asymmetrically distributed — junior analyst tasks (comping, screening, note-drafting) are most exposed; senior analyst tasks (channel checks, management reading, portfolio construction logic) are least exposed, and this gap is widening not narrowing

§2 — Capability Landscape: What AI Tools Currently Do in Investment Research

  • J-front verdict: AI capability in investment research is real, material, and concentrated in the production and synthesis layers of the workflow — but consistently falls short at the insight-generation and conviction-formation layers where most alpha is claimed to originate
  • Data aggregation and structuring: AI tools (LLMs + structured-data pipelines) can now ingest and structure earnings transcripts, SEC filings, regulatory announcements, and news flows at scale and speed no human team can match — coverage that once required 5-10 analyst-hours per company per quarter is being compressed to minutes
  • Earnings model construction: AI-assisted financial modelling tools (e.g. Bloomberg's AI integrations, FactSet's AI layer, proprietary quant-shop tooling) can build initial three-statement models from filings with minimal human intervention, though output quality degrades meaningfully for companies with non-standard accounting or complex off-balance-sheet structures
  • Transcript summarisation and sentiment scoring: This is arguably the most mature AI application in investment research — NLP-based sentiment analysis of earnings calls, broker notes, and management commentary has been in production use at large asset managers since 2018-2020, well before the LLM wave
  • Alternative data integration: AI enables integration of satellite imagery, credit-card transaction data, job-posting trends, web-traffic signals, and app-download data into systematic screens — a task that was previously too labour-intensive for most fundamental shops to operationalise
  • Report drafting and summarisation: LLMs have materially reduced the time cost of producing first-draft research notes, earnings previews, and sector update summaries — some sell-side desks are now using LLM-assisted drafting for initiation reports, with senior analysts performing review rather than original composition
  • Screening and idea generation: AI-assisted screening (combining traditional financial metrics with alternative data signals) is now the dominant mode of idea generation at quant-oriented asset managers — the "what to look at" question is increasingly AI-mediated
  • Limitations — quantitative reasoning: LLMs remain unreliable on complex multi-step quantitative reasoning without explicit chain-of-thought scaffolding and verification; financial models generated by AI without human verification have documented error rates that are unacceptable for live investment decisions
  • Limitations — temporal awareness: AI models trained on historical data have inherent lag — they cannot detect regime changes, narrative inflections, or management-credibility signals that a human analyst on a live channel check or at a conference can detect in real time
  • Limitations — judgement under uncertainty: The most consistent limitation across AI tools in investment research is the inability to form calibrated probabilistic judgements — AI tools tend to produce confident-sounding outputs on questions where the correct epistemic posture is high uncertainty, which is precisely the posture required for variant-perception investing
  • Limitations — context sensitivity: AI tools struggle with the non-verbal and contextual signals that experienced analysts use to assess management quality, competitive dynamics, and customer behaviour — the "soft" data layer that often drives the most durable variant perception
  • Limitations — hallucination risk: LLM hallucination in financial contexts is a documented and material risk — in 2023-2024 several high-profile cases emerged of AI-assisted research outputs containing fabricated citations, incorrect financial figures, and misattributed quotes; this risk has not been eliminated, only partially mitigated via RAG architectures and verification layers
  • Capability frontier — multimodal analysis: The next capability wave (already in early deployment at frontier AI labs) involves multimodal analysis — reading charts, earnings presentation slides, and geographic data simultaneously — which expands the AI capability envelope toward more of the analyst workflow
  • Non-consensus read — the "last mile" problem: The consensus view frames AI as progressing linearly toward full analyst replacement; the non-consensus view is that the "last mile" of insight generation — variant perception formation — may be structurally resistant to AI substitution not because AI lacks intelligence, but because variant perception requires being wrong in a specific way relative to consensus, which AI models trained on consensus data are structurally biased against
  • Sceptical bullet: The analyst should ask: most capability assessments come from AI vendors or from AI-enthusiast practitioners — where is the rigorous independent audit of AI tool failure rates in live investment decision-making contexts? Evidence of capability from controlled benchmarks may not translate to live portfolio decisions under adversarial market conditions

Confidence — High — anchor: AI capability in production and synthesis layers is well-documented across multiple independent sources (academic NLP literature, practitioner surveys, sell-side technology adoption reports); limitations are validated by practitioner case studies

§3 — Adoption Landscape: Who Is Using AI and How

  • J-front verdict: Adoption is real but bifurcated — large quant shops have structurally integrated AI, while fundamental long-only firms are at the bolt-on/experimentation stage; the adoption gap between these cohorts is wide and may be widening
  • Large quant asset managers (AQR, Two Sigma, Renaissance, D.E. Shaw cohort): These firms have been using machine-learning-based investment processes for 10-20 years — the LLM wave is an incremental capability addition for them, not a structural transition; their competitive advantage is proprietary data pipelines and model governance, not LLM access per se
  • Mid-sized fundamental shops: Adoption is mostly at the "AI-assisted workflow" stage — using tools like Bloomberg's AI features, FactSet AI, or in-house LLM wrappers for transcript summarisation and screening; investment decision-making process has not been structurally redesigned
  • Sell-side research departments: Most major sell-side firms (Goldman, Morgan Stanley, JPMorgan, Barclays) have publicly announced AI integration into research workflows — focus is on productivity (more coverage per analyst, faster note production) rather than insight quality; analyst headcount has not materially declined as a result, though hiring pace has slowed
  • Hedge funds: More heterogeneous — a subset of fundamental hedge funds (particularly multi-manager platforms) have invested heavily in proprietary AI tooling; a larger subset are using off-the-shelf tools; a tail of smaller funds has minimal AI integration
  • Family offices and smaller managers: Adoption is nascent — primary barrier is cost and technical capability rather than philosophical objection; as AI tools become more accessible (SaaS pricing, no-code interfaces), this cohort is expected to adopt rapidly in 2026-2028
  • Adoption driver — coverage expansion: The most commonly cited adoption driver is the ability to cover more companies per analyst — AI tools are allowing sell-side analysts to expand coverage universes 20-40% without proportional headcount increases, which is a meaningful productivity gain
  • Adoption driver — speed: AI-assisted earnings note production and filing analysis has materially compressed the time from event to published research — a competitive advantage in the sell-side context where speed to client is valued
  • Adoption barrier — model governance and compliance: Regulatory uncertainty around AI-generated research (particularly around attribution, disclosure, and liability) is a meaningful adoption brake at regulated firms — the compliance function is a significant friction point
  • Adoption barrier — data provenance and hallucination risk: Compliance and risk teams at large asset managers have been slow to approve AI tools for live investment decision support, particularly after several documented hallucination incidents in 2023-2024
  • Adoption barrier — cultural resistance: Senior portfolio managers and analysts at fundamental shops often have strong priors about the value of human judgement — cultural resistance to AI adoption at the decision-making layer is documented and persistent, though it is weakening as younger cohorts move into seniority
  • Geographic variance: US adoption leads Europe — partly due to regulatory environment (EU AI Act adding compliance friction), partly due to concentration of AI talent and vendor ecosystem in the US; UK is closer to US than to continental Europe
  • Non-consensus read — the "shadow adoption" problem: Public adoption disclosures from asset managers dramatically understate actual AI use — practitioners report that informal, ungoverned use of consumer AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) in investment research workflows is widespread and largely invisible to compliance functions; the "official" adoption landscape misses a large fraction of actual use
  • Time-series trend: Adoption velocity has accelerated sharply since GPT-4 launch (March 2023) — the 2024-2026 window appears to be the inflection period for moving from experimentation to structured integration; 2027-2030 is the expected window for structural workflow redesign at fundamental shops
  • Sceptical bullet: Adoption surveys are typically self-reported and vendor-sponsored — the analyst should discount headline adoption percentages meaningfully; the more informative signal is headcount data, which shows sell-side research team sizes declining slowly (not explosively) through 2025, suggesting AI is currently a productivity tool, not a displacement accelerant

Confidence — Medium — anchor: adoption data is largely self-reported and vendor-adjacent; headcount data provides an independent cross-check but is lagged and aggregated; the bifurcation thesis is directionally well-supported but precise magnitude estimates are unreliable

§4 — Limitations and Failure Modes: Where AI Underperforms

  • J-front verdict: AI limitations in investment research are systematic and concentrated in the highest-value parts of the workflow — the failure modes are not random errors but structural biases that are unlikely to be fully resolved by scaling alone
  • Hallucination as a structural risk: LLMs generate plausible-sounding but factually incorrect financial data at rates that are unacceptable for investment decision-making without robust verification architectures — RAG (retrieval-augmented generation) mitigates but does not eliminate this risk
  • Overfitting to historical patterns: ML models trained on historical financial data have documented overfitting problems — backtests look compelling but live performance frequently disappoints, particularly in regime-change periods; this is the "backtest mirage" problem that has been a persistent failure mode in quant investing for decades, now amplified by AI tool proliferation
  • Consensus convergence bias: AI models trained on the same public data (SEC filings, news, earnings calls, broker notes) will generate signals that converge with consensus — this is structurally antagonistic to variant perception, where value is created by being different from consensus in a correct way
  • Temporal blind spots: AI tools with static training cutoffs have documented failure modes in fast-moving situations — market microstructure changes, regulatory shifts, management turnover, or competitive inflection points that occur after the training cutoff are invisible to the model without real-time grounding
  • Management quality assessment: Assessing management credibility, capital allocation discipline, and strategic clarity from earnings call transcripts is a task AI tools perform poorly — the textual signals available to AI lack the non-verbal, contextual, and relationship-derived information that experienced analysts use; practitioner feedback consistently rates this as the area of greatest AI underperformance
  • Novel company and situation analysis: AI tools trained on historical patterns struggle with genuinely novel business models, new industries, or discontinuous transitions — the LLM's strength is pattern-matching, which becomes a weakness when the relevant pattern does not exist in the training data
  • Cross-asset and macro integration: Investment research increasingly requires integrating equity-specific analysis with macro, rates, and credit signals — AI tools that are optimised for single-asset-class analysis perform poorly at this cross-asset synthesis, which is where many fundamental portfolio managers add most of their value
  • Uncertainty calibration: The most concerning failure mode for investment applications is AI tools' tendency to produce overconfident outputs on genuinely uncertain questions — portfolio managers and analysts who rely on AI-generated confidence intervals or probability estimates without understanding their calibration properties are taking on an invisible risk
  • Adversarial and strategic behaviour: AI tools trained on publicly available management communications are vulnerable to "narrative management" — where management teams strategically craft language to influence AI sentiment scoring systems; as AI adoption increases, this adversarial dynamic will intensify
  • Tail-risk and non-stationarity: AI tools are generally weak at identifying and pricing tail risks that arise from non-stationary processes — geopolitical discontinuities, regulatory step-changes, technological disruptions — precisely the risks that most catastrophically destroy investment returns
  • ⚡ Outlier signal — the "adversarial alpha" problem: A small but growing body of evidence suggests that as AI adoption in investment research increases, the alpha from AI signals decays faster than the alpha from human-sourced signals — because AI signals are more transparent and replicable, their crowding-and-decay dynamics are faster; this is the inverse of the conventional narrative and has significant implications for the long-run economics of AI-driven investment approaches
  • Non-consensus read: The consensus limitation narrative focuses on hallucination and data quality — the more important structural limitation is the epistemological mismatch between AI (pattern-matching, probability-weighted) and variant-perception investing (deliberately low-confidence, hypothesis-driven, willing to be wrong for structural reasons)
  • Sceptical bullet: The analyst should note that failure-mode documentation in AI investment research is almost entirely practitioner-self-reported or academic-simulation-based — there is no audited, independent record of AI tool failure rates in live investment decision-making at institutional quality; the failure modes may be worse than the current evidence suggests

Confidence — Medium — anchor: hallucination and overfitting failure modes are well-evidenced across academic and practitioner sources; management-quality and uncertainty-calibration limitations are consistently reported in practitioner interviews but lack quantitative validation; adversarial dynamics are emerging evidence only

§5 — Displacement Risk: Threat to Human Analyst Roles

  • J-front verdict: Displacement risk is real but role-stratified — junior analyst tasks face near-term displacement pressure, senior analyst and portfolio manager tasks face structural augmentation rather than replacement at the 3-year horizon; the distribution of risk is more nuanced than either the AI-enthusiast or AI-sceptic narratives acknowledge
  • Role-type stratification — the core framework: Investment research tasks can be decomposed into: (a) production tasks (data gathering, model building, note drafting, screening); (b) synthesis tasks (cross-source integration, pattern identification, scenario construction); (c) judgement tasks (conviction formation, variant-perception identification, position sizing); AI is currently strong at (a), developing at (b), and weak at (c) — displacement risk follows this hierarchy
  • Junior analyst exposure: Roles primarily focused on production tasks (financial modelling, data entry, earnings preview drafting, comp-table maintenance) face the highest near-term displacement risk — these tasks are well within current AI capability envelopes and are already being partially automated at large sell-side firms and asset managers
  • Mid-level analyst exposure: Roles involving synthesis (sector coverage, thematic research, multi-company comparative analysis) face medium-term pressure — AI can handle increasing portions of these tasks, but the human value-add remains in curating, challenging, and contextualising AI-generated synthesis; the role is being redesigned rather than eliminated
  • Senior analyst and PM exposure: Roles built on network-derived intelligence (management relationships, industry contacts, competitor channel checks), long-run pattern recognition (seeing multiple business cycles in a sector), and conviction formation (the willingness to hold a differentiated position under pressure) remain largely insulated at the 3-year horizon; these attributes are structurally AI-resistant because they depend on real-world relationships and experiential priors that AI cannot replicate from text data
  • Sell-side structural pressure: Sell-side research departments face compounded displacement pressure — AI is reducing the cost of producing research at the same time as MiFID II and its successors are compressing research budgets and the economics of sell-side research; the combination is creating structural pressure on headcount that is visible in hiring freezes, but the pace is below the most bearish narratives
  • Historical base rate — technology displacement in financial services: Prior waves of technology automation in financial services (quantitative screening in 1990s, electronic trading in 2000s, algo-driven execution in 2010s) consistently showed a pattern of role transformation rather than role elimination — headcount declined in automated task-categories but grew in oversight, strategy, and relationship roles; AI may follow a similar pattern, though LLMs' breadth of capability suggests the transformation may be deeper
  • Displacement timeline — practitioner consensus: Practitioner consensus (based on publicly available surveys and conference commentary) places material headcount impact on junior analyst roles in a 5-10 year window, not a 2-3 year window; the 2-3 year window is characterised by productivity gains and role redesign, not elimination
  • Non-consensus read — the "hollowing-out" risk: The consensus displacement narrative focuses on junior roles; the non-consensus risk is "hollowing out" of the middle layer — experienced mid-level analysts who provide institutional knowledge and mentorship may exit the industry as productivity tools reduce the need for their coordination role, leaving a gap in the talent pipeline that is not filled by AI and produces worse senior analysts in 10 years
  • Structural headcount data (sell-side): US broker-dealer research department headcounts were approximately 5,000-6,000 analyst-equivalent roles in 2024, down from ~8,000-9,000 in 2008 — a long-term structural decline that predates AI; the AI-related increment to this decline is not yet clearly isolatable from other structural forces (MiFID II, passive adoption, commission compression)
  • Cross-industry analogue — legal and accounting professions: Legal and accounting professions have faced similar displacement narratives around AI document review and audit automation — in both cases, automation accelerated throughput and compressed cost but did not eliminate roles; analyst displacement may follow this "augmentation with slow headcount pressure" pattern rather than the "rapid elimination" pattern
  • ⚡ Outlier signal — "AI analyst" as regulatory arbitrage: A small number of jurisdictions are exploring or have implemented frameworks under which AI-generated research can be attributed to a named AI "analyst" rather than a human analyst, creating a regulatory and liability structure that could accelerate displacement of human analysts faster than current practitioner consensus expects; this is tail risk at the current evidence level but worth monitoring
  • Sceptical bullet: The analyst should be cautious about displacement narratives that are driven by AI vendor marketing — there is a strong commercial incentive to overstate AI capability and displacement risk in order to accelerate procurement decisions; the most bearish displacement scenarios should be stress-tested against the actual pace of capability improvement in the specific tasks (judgement, uncertainty calibration) that matter most for investment alpha

Confidence — Medium — anchor: role-stratification framework is well-supported by task-decomposition analysis and practitioner interviews; displacement timeline estimates are highly uncertain and vary widely across sources; headcount data provides a directional cross-check but does not isolate AI-specific effects

§6 — Competitive Structure: AI-Natives vs Incumbents

  • J-front verdict: Competitive structure is bifurcating toward a two-tier landscape — AI-native firms with proprietary data pipelines and model governance infrastructure hold a structural advantage, but incumbent asset managers with deep relationship capital and client trust are not defenceless; the race is not as one-sided as the AI-disruption narrative implies
  • AI-native firm characteristics: A small cohort of AI-native investment firms has emerged (not to be confused with traditional quant shops) — these firms are characterised by: LLM-first research processes; proprietary alternative-data pipelines; systematic model validation and governance; and investment teams structured around AI output oversight rather than traditional analyst coverage
  • Incumbent advantages: Large fundamental asset managers retain meaningful structural advantages — client relationship capital, regulatory track record, institutional trust, and access to proprietary channel-check networks; these advantages are not AI-replicable in the near term and represent the primary moat against AI-native displacement
  • Data moat as competitive differentiator: The central competitive battleground is proprietary data — firms with unique, hard-to-replicate data sources (proprietary surveys, exclusive management access, channel-check networks, field research capability) retain a structural advantage that AI cannot commoditise without access to the underlying data
  • Sell-side competitive dynamics: Sell-side research is facing AI-driven commoditisation of its core product (company coverage notes, earnings models, sector reports) — the differentiated sell-side product increasingly lies in access (management access, corporate deal flow) and in high-touch relationship services, both of which are AI-resistant; mid-tier sell-side firms without either the scale of a Goldman or the specialisation of a boutique face the greatest structural threat
  • Financial data vendor landscape: The financial data vendor landscape (Bloomberg, FactSet, Refinitiv/LSEG, Morningstar) is both a beneficiary and a potential target of AI disruption — these firms are adding AI layers to their platforms, but the risk is that AI-native data aggregation tools (trained on the same public data) erode the premium that proprietary platforms command; the terminal value of financial data platforms is more uncertain than consensus prices in
  • Platform vs point-solution dynamics: The market is currently bifurcated between platform-integrated AI tools (Bloomberg AI, FactSet AI) and point-solution AI tools (standalone LLM-based research assistants, alternative-data AI tools); platform tools have adoption advantages but often lag point-solutions on capability; point-solutions are faster-moving but create workflow fragmentation
  • Network effects and learning loops: AI investment tools improve with use — firms that deploy AI earlier accumulate proprietary training data (interaction logs, analyst feedback, performance attribution) that can be used to fine-tune models; this creates a learning-loop dynamic that rewards early movers, though the magnitude of the advantage is unclear
  • Regulatory overhang: MiFID II, SEC AI guidance (evolving), and the EU AI Act create a regulatory overhang that affects all AI investment research tools — compliance costs are higher for AI-native firms than for incumbents (who have existing compliance infrastructure), which may slow AI-native adoption in regulated markets
  • Non-consensus read — the "quant convergence" thesis: The most intriguing non-consensus thesis is that widespread AI adoption is causing the fundamental-vs-quant distinction to collapse — as fundamental firms adopt AI-assisted screening and quant firms adopt LLM-based reasoning, the strategies may converge toward a similar middle ground, reducing the differentiation of both approaches and compressing fees across the board
  • Sceptical bullet: The competitive landscape analysis is hampered by the fact that most AI-native investment firms are private and do not publicly report strategy or performance — the analyst cannot benchmark AI-native vs incumbent investment performance with public data, and must rely on anecdotal and survey evidence; this is a material evidence gap

Confidence — Medium — anchor: structural competitive dynamics are well-supported by industry analysis; specific performance comparisons between AI-native and incumbent firms are not robustly evidenced in public data

§8 — Data Gaps

  • J-front verdict: The most material gaps are in live-performance data and independent audit of AI tool failure rates — resolving these gaps would substantially sharpen the displacement and competitive-structure conclusions

  • Critical data gap: Audited, survivorship-adjusted, net-of-fees alpha data comparing AI-assisted investment processes to traditional investment processes — this data does not exist in public form and would be the single most informative input for the case

  • Critical data gap: Independent (non-vendor-sponsored) audit of AI tool failure rates in live investment decision-making contexts — the current evidence base for failure modes relies on practitioner self-report and academic simulation, not live audited decision logs
  • Material gap: Headcount data that isolates AI-specific displacement effects from other structural forces (passive shift, MiFID II, commission compression) in sell-side research — the aggregate headcount trend is clear, but attribution is not
  • Material gap: Public financial data on AI-native investment firms — most are private; performance, strategy, and business model data are unavailable; this makes competitive-structure analysis reliant on anecdotal evidence
  • Material gap: Regulatory landscape map for AI-generated research — the rules are evolving and jurisdiction-specific; the compliance cost and regulatory friction dimension of the KQ is underdeveloped due to rapidly changing regulatory environment
  • Minor gap: Detailed role-level data on specific tasks being automated vs augmented within investment research workflows — practitioner interviews give directional guidance but lack the granularity needed to precisely map the displacement curve
  • Minor gap: Cross-industry analogue data on the speed of AI-driven role transformation in comparable knowledge-work industries (law, accounting, strategy consulting) with sufficient time elapsed to assess second-order effects
  • Resolvable via expert call: The live-performance data gap is partially resolvable via expert calls with practitioners at AI-native or AI-heavy investment firms — they will have internal data, even if not public; the key question is "what does your AI-assisted process's live track record look like vs your pre-AI process?"
  • Resolvable via expert call: The adoption bifurcation thesis would be significantly sharpened by expert calls with heads of research at both quant-oriented and fundamental-oriented asset managers — the current evidence is from surveys and public statements, which understate true adoption state
  • Unresolvable from desk: AI-native firm performance data — requires either direct access to fund performance records or regulatory disclosures that do not currently exist in public form
  • Wait-and-monitor: The regulatory landscape gap (AI-generated research disclosure and liability rules) should be monitored as the EU AI Act implementation and SEC AI guidance evolve through 2026-2028
  • Sceptical bullet: Some of the apparent data gaps may reflect the analyst looking in the wrong places — academic finance journals (SSRN, Journal of Financial Economics) contain a growing body of research on ML-generated alpha that has not been surfaced in practitioner-facing sources; the gap may be narrower than it appears if the search is broadened beyond industry-facing sources toward academic literature

§9 — Second + Third-Order Implications

  • J-front verdict: The non-consensus second-order implications of AI in investment research are more important than the first-order displacement narrative — the most consequential effects are structural and systemic, not role-level

  • Second-order — alpha half-life acceleration: As AI tools trained on the same public data are adopted by more managers, the signals they generate converge; converging signals lead to crowded positions; crowded positions have faster drawdown dynamics; the systemic result is that AI adoption may reduce market-wide alpha persistence and increase correlated drawdown risk across the asset management industry — this is a regulatory and systemic risk question, not just an individual firm question

  • Second-order — information hierarchy compression: AI enables smaller firms to approximate the information-processing capability of larger firms — the informational advantage that scale conferred in pre-AI investment research (more analysts, more coverage, faster processing) is being compressed; this could equalise competitive conditions between large and small managers, which would represent a structural shift in the asset management competitive landscape
  • Third-order — talent pipeline deterioration: If AI automates junior analyst tasks, the traditional training ground for future senior analysts (learning-by-doing on financial modelling, transcript reading, and sector initiation) is disrupted; the talent pipeline for the next generation of senior investment professionals may be weaker, reducing the quality of human judgement in the investment industry over a 10-15 year horizon — this is the "hollowing-out" risk discussed in §5
  • Second-order — vendor pricing power: As AI integration becomes a competitive necessity rather than a differentiator, the leverage in the investment research supply chain shifts toward AI and data vendors (Bloomberg, FactSet, AI model providers) and away from human capital (analysts); this increases vendor pricing power and creates a structural cost increase for investment management that is not yet fully priced into consensus cost models for the sector
  • Third-order — passive acceleration: AI-driven compression of active manager alpha (via signal commoditisation and crowding) would further accelerate the secular shift from active to passive management — an AI-adoption wave that paradoxically accelerates the case for index investing would be the most consequential (and least discussed) systemic implication of AI in investment research
  • Cross-pillar — management quality signal degradation: If AI tools become widely used to monitor and score management commentary, management teams will optimise their communications to game AI sentiment models — the informational content of earnings calls and management communications could decline as a result, degrading a key input for fundamental investment research; this is a Goodhart's Law dynamic applied to investment research
