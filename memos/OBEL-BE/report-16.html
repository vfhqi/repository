§6 — Governance Structure + Red Flags

J-front verdict: Mixed-clean — no acute red flags (no restatements, no probes, no class actions); structural governance concern is parent control over minority; some standard Belgian anti-takeover provisions

Voting rights structure: - Orange Belgium ordinary shares carry one vote per share; no dual-class structure, no golden share at OBEL-BE level - Orange SA's ~52% holding provides de facto control over all ordinary resolutions (simple majority) and, combined with potential allied minority holders, near-blocking power on special resolutions (75% threshold) - Belgian corporate law provides minority shareholder protections (squeeze-out at 95% threshold, mandatory offer at 30% triggers), but at 52% Orange SA is well below squeeze-out; minority shareholders have full public-market liquidity but limited governance voice - No poison pill or statutory anti-takeover provision identified beyond Belgian general law; the parent stake is itself the effective anti-takeover mechanism

Related-party transactions: - Orange Belgium discloses related-party transactions per IFRS 8 / Belgian code; material transactions include: - Roaming and interconnect settlements with Orange SA and other Orange-group entities (estimated €50-100m+ annually — consistent with group telco norms, at arm's-length stated pricing) - IT and systems services sourced from Orange group shared service entities (ITSF and equivalent); pricing disclosed as arm's-length per Remuneration/Audit Committee review - Brand licensing / royalty payments to Orange SA (percentage of revenue; industry standard for listed subsidiary of branded parent) - These related-party flows are structurally present and quantitatively meaningful; the arm's-length assertion has not been independently stress-tested in public analyst coverage - ⚡ flag candidate: brand royalty + IT service fee combination represents a material recurring cash leakage to parent that could be deployed for capex or shareholder returns — aggregate disclosed value not found in English-language summaries; warrants forensic review in Belgian statutory accounts

Audit history: - Statutory auditor: Deloitte (Bedrijfsrevisoren / Réviseurs d'Entreprises); long-standing relationship (confirmed L5Y minimum) - No auditor change in L5Y; consistent with OBEL-BE's stable corporate structure - No material weaknesses disclosed; no qualified audit opinions identified; no restatement history - Audit Committee composition: 100% independent (per §4 table); formally compliant

Legal/regulatory issues: - Belgian telecom regulatory framework (BIPT — Belgian Institute for Postal Services and Telecommunications) is the primary regulatory exposure; Orange Belgium has been involved in standard interconnect and spectrum regulatory proceedings — no material adverse ruling identified in L3Y - No SEC investigation (OBEL-BE is not a US-listed entity; Euronext Brussels listed only); no equivalent Belgian financial regulator (FSMA) investigation identified - No class action lawsuit identified; no material commercial litigation flagged in Annual Report beyond routine - Spectrum: 5G spectrum licences acquired 2022-23 in Belgian 5G auction; process was competitive but no governance/legal issue attached - No whistleblower or governance complaint signals found in public domain

Anti-takeover provisions: - Belgian corporate law provides standard protections; OBEL-BE has no separately-identifiable poison pill - Parent's ~52% stake is de facto anti-takeover; any hostile acquirer must engage Orange SA - This creates an asymmetric governance situation: parent can squeeze out remaining minority if stake reaches 95%, but probability of hostile takeover is negligible

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on governance is that the combination of (a) parent-controlled nomination process, (b) quantitatively meaningful related-party transaction flows (brand royalty + IT services + roaming settlements) that have not been independently benchmarked by the market, and (c) a Nomination Committee chaired by an Orange SA nominee, creates a structure where any governance failure would be difficult to detect until financial consequences are visible; the red flag that would invalidate the operator-quality thesis is discovery that related-party pricing (particularly brand royalty and IT service fees) is above market, extracting cash from OBEL-BE minority shareholders to benefit the parent

§8 — Governance Verdict + Sceptical Synthesis

J-front verdict (cross-cutting bear): The dominant governance risk is structural parent-alignment over minority shareholders — not acute misconduct but chronic structural misalignment between who runs the company and whose long-term value they are optimised to maximise

Reverse-engineered bear from §1-§7: - §1 bear: Pichon is a group rotator with sub-3yr OBEL-BE tenure; track record is stewardship not transformation; will be rotated out before fibre investment cycle completes - §2 bear: Castille's financial discipline masks potential capitalisation aggression in fibre OPEX/CAPEX boundary; no independent CFO challenge to parent-directed spending priorities - §3 bear: group-rotation model means institutional knowledge in CTO/fibre execution team rotates out at worst possible time (2025-2027 peak execution complexity) - §4 bear: Nomination Committee chaired by Orange SA nominee; independent directors selected from parent-screened pool; "independence" is procedural not substantive - §5 bear: no TSR condition; EBITDAaL-linked variable creates capitalisation incentive; ESG weighting dilutes financial rigour; parent-set compensation envelope limits local incentive structure - §6 bear: related-party flows (brand royalty + IT services) are quantitatively material and not independently benchmarked; the extraction risk is non-zero and structurally unchecked - §7 bear: Glassdoor "parent direction over local autonomy" theme signals a managed-subsidiary culture, not an entrepreneurial/independent operator culture; talent retention risk in technical functions

Single largest operator risk: - Parent-minority misalignment: Orange SA's incentive is to optimise the OBEL-BE entity within the group's European portfolio, which may not coincide with maximising OBEL-BE standalone minority shareholder value; capital allocation (fibre investment pace, related-party pricing, dividend policy) is ultimately group-directed - This is not a fraud risk or acute governance failure risk — it is a structural drag that depresses the probability of a positive governance surprise and elevates the probability of a decision that benefits the group at the expense of minority holders

Governance fragility: - The fragility point is the Audit Committee's inability to independently benchmark related-party transaction pricing; if brand royalty or IT service fees are above market, the financial leakage would not surface in disclosed financials (reported as cost lines, arm's-length asserted) until a third-party challenge forced disclosure - A minority shareholder activist could challenge related-party pricing; probability is low given ~48% float and typical Belgian institutional shareholder passivity
