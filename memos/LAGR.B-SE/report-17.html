KEY FINDINGS - MATERIAL TRANSLATION EXPOSURE: Lagercrantz reports in SEK but generates roughly 55-60% of revenue outside Sweden, creating a substantial and structurally persistent translation exposure — predominantly EUR, DKK (pegged to EUR), NOK, GBP, and a modest but growing USD/USD-proxy tail; SEK strength is the single largest earnings-dilution mechanism against the thesis

  • NATURAL HEDGE IS PARTIAL, NOT FULL: Decentralised operating model means subsidiaries largely incur costs in the same currency as their revenue, which is the most important structural protection — but group-level overhead (Swedish-krona-denominated central cost) and SEK-denominated debt service create an unhedged translation drag on EBIT margin when foreign subsidiaries' earnings are translated back into SEK
  • POLICY IS TRANSLATION-ACCEPTING, TRANSACTION-HEDGING: Disclosed policy hedges transactional (cash-flow) mismatches at subsidiary level on a rolling short-horizon basis; group does NOT hedge translation exposure, which is standard for decentralised European industrials but means reported SEK earnings are fully exposed to SEK appreciation
  • QUANTIFIED SENSITIVITY — CENTRAL ESTIMATE: A 10% SEK appreciation across all foreign currencies reduces reported SEK revenue by approximately SEK 600-700m (c.4-5% of FY24/25 revenue) and EBIT by approximately SEK 75-95m (c.5-7% of FY24/25 EBIT), with EPS impact of approximately 6-8% per 10% SEK move — this is MATERIAL against a 12-15% organic EPS growth assumption
  • RECENT HISTORY — NET TAILWIND REVERSING: SEK depreciated materially FY2021-2023, producing a multi-year FX translation tailwind that flattered reported growth; SEK has been stabilising/modestly appreciating from FY2024; forward outlook is SEK-neutral to mildly appreciating vs EUR (rate-differential normalisation), suggesting FX will shift from historical tailwind to neutral-to-slight-headwind over N12-24M
  • HEDGING TRANSPARENCY IS BELOW SECTOR AVERAGE: Lagercrantz does not publish a detailed hedge-position summary (ratio, duration, counterparties, notional), making independent hedge-effectiveness verification impossible; this is the weakest dimension of FX governance relative to better-disclosed sector peers
  • SUBORDINATE BUT REAL USD EXPOSURE: USD revenue via North American and export-linked subsidiaries is estimated at 5-10% of group — small in absolute terms but disproportionately important if USD weakens significantly (several subsidiaries appear to invoice in USD for products with SEK/EUR cost bases)
  • TRANSACTION RISK IS REAL BUT MANAGEABLE: Where subsidiaries export and invoice in a different currency from their cost base, transaction exposure exists; company policy is to hedge these at subsidiary level with short-duration forwards — not systematically disclosed at group level, which is the gap
  • EPS TRAJECTORY THESIS IS FX-DEPENDENT AT THE MARGIN: If SEK appreciates 10% vs EUR/DKK/NOK basket over N18M, the FX headwind alone could reduce reported EPS growth by 6-8 percentage points — turning a 15% reported EPS growth assumption into 7-9%, which is below the 25% IRR threshold trigger; FX is therefore a genuine risk-factor for the thesis, not a footnote

§1 — Revenue by Currency

J-front verdict: Lagercrantz's revenue is materially non-SEK — approximately 55-60% of revenue from outside Sweden — producing significant translation exposure concentrated in EUR/DKK, NOK, and GBP, with the SEK-reporting mismatch being the single largest FX risk vector

  • Reporting currency: SEK; all subsidiary local-currency results translated at average rate for the period into SEK for P&L purposes
  • Geographic revenue split (FY24/25 base): Sweden c.40-42% of revenue; Rest of Nordic (Denmark, Norway, Finland) c.25-28%; Rest of Europe (Germany, UK, Netherlands, other) c.18-22%; Americas/Other c.5-8%
  • DKK is pegged to EUR (±2.25% ERM II band) and treated analytically as EUR-proxy exposure; combining EUR + DKK-proxy produces c.28-32% of group revenue in EUR-currency bloc
  • NOK is freely floating and material; approximately 10-14% of group revenue in NOK
  • GBP approximately 5-8% of group revenue
  • USD/USD-proxy (Americas + some export) approximately 5-9%
  • Residual other currencies (PLN, CZK, CHF) approximately 3-5%

§2 — Cost by Currency

J-front verdict: Cost currency broadly matches revenue currency at the subsidiary level (structural natural hedge from decentralised model) but group-level SEK-denominated overhead and acquisition financing costs create a partial mismatch — the natural hedge is real but incomplete

  • COGS currency mix: Predominantly local-currency for each subsidiary — Lagercrantz's decentralised model means subsidiaries purchase inputs, manufacture (where applicable), and sell in broadly the same geographic market; COGS is estimated at 65-70% correlated with revenue currency by subsidiary
  • Key exception: subsidiaries that import components from Asia (USD/CNY-denominated sourcing) while selling in SEK or NOK — creates a USD-cost / local-revenue mismatch at sub-subsidiary level; not quantified at group level in public disclosure
  • Subsidiaries in electronics/components businesses (a meaningful sub-segment) are most likely to have USD-denominated input costs vs local-currency revenue
  • Opex currency mix: Largely local — staff costs are the dominant opex line and are local-currency by definition; Swedish group-level overhead (management, IT, IR, central finance) is SEK-denominated and creates a base of SEK cost regardless of revenue currency mix
  • Group overhead is estimated at SEK 80-120m per annum (small relative to SEK 14bn+ revenue but meaningful against group EBIT of approximately SEK 1.4-1.6bn)
  • Capex currency mix: Not disclosed with currency granularity; capex is predominantly local in nature (facility maintenance, machinery) and assumed to largely match local subsidiary currency; no major centralised capex programme that would create a concentrated currency mismatch
  • Natural hedge ratio (estimate): For revenue generated outside Sweden, approximately 70-80% of associated direct costs are in the same currency; 20-30% includes: group overhead allocation , USD-component inputs, cross-subsidiary sourcing; estimated overall natural hedge ratio approximately 65-75% of foreign-currency revenue

§3 — Translation Exposure (Reported Earnings Impact L3Y)

J-front verdict: Translation has been a consistent multi-year tailwind (FY2021-2023) due to SEK depreciation, contributing 2-5pp to annual reported revenue growth; the tailwind has materially faded and is now turning neutral-to-headwind, meaning past EPS growth rates overstate the organic engine by approximately 2-4pp in peak years

  • Reported FX impact L1Y (FY24/25): SEK was broadly stable vs EUR and DKK, modestly depreciated vs USD, and mixed vs NOK and GBP; net translation contribution to reported revenue growth estimated at -1% to +1% (effectively neutral); EBIT translation impact also approximately neutral
  • Reported FX impact L3Y average:
  • FY22/23: Estimated +3% to +5% translation tailwind to reported SEK revenue; SEK was materially weaker vs EUR (EUR/SEK touched 11.0+ in late 2022)
  • FY23/24: Estimated +1% to +2% translation tailwind — SEK began recovering but average rates still favourable vs prior period
  • FY24/25: Estimated -1% to +1% — effectively neutral; SEK mean-reverting
  • 3-year average tailwind: Approximately +2% to +3% per annum on revenue translation; materially flattered reported growth
  • Translation magnitude:
  • Average year: ±2-3% revenue impact from translation fluctuations is normal given currency mix
  • Stressed year (e.g. 10-15% SEK appreciation, as occurred in reverse 2021-2023): ±5-8% revenue translation impact is realistic; EBIT impact proportionally larger (5-9%) because SEK overhead base amplifies the effect
  • Reporting currency trajectory:
  • SEK/EUR: Was trading at historically weak levels (11.0-11.5) FY22/23; has since recovered toward 10.7-11.0 range; consensus expects gradual SEK strengthening as Riksbank policy normalises vs ECB
  • SEK/NOK: Broadly stable with modest SEK strength over L2Y
  • SEK/GBP: SEK mildly appreciated vs GBP over L2Y
  • SEK/USD: Mixed; USD strength 2022-2023 was a tailwind; USD moderation in FY24/25 removed that contribution
  • Absolute magnitude (estimated from revenue + currency mix):
  • FY24/25 revenue approximately SEK 14.0-14.5bn (estimate, pending final report)
  • Non-SEK revenue approximately SEK 8.0-8.7bn
  • A 10% SEK appreciation across the non-SEK basket would reduce reported revenue by approximately SEK 800-870m (c.5.5-6.0% of total revenue)
  • At group EBIT margin approximately 11-12%, a 10% SEK appreciation reduces EBIT by approximately SEK 88-104m (c.6-8% of EBIT)
  • EPS impact: approximately 6-8% per 10% SEK appreciation vs foreign-currency basket

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on translation is that the 2021-2023 SEK depreciation cycle has conditioned investors to model "low FX risk" because the FX effect was recently positive; if SEK converges toward long-run purchasing-power-parity vs EUR (approximately SEK 9.5-10.0, implying 10-15% appreciation from current 10.7-11.0), a sustained translation headwind of 5-9% on EBIT could persist for 2-3 years — a binary-event risk that isn't in consensus models that extrapolate the recent neutral-FX period

§4 — Transaction Exposure (Economic Impact)

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is real but secondary to translation; the decentralised model structurally limits cross-currency cash-flow mismatches, but USD-input-cost subsidiaries and inter-company arrangements create genuine transactional risk that is disclosed only vaguely

  • Transaction exposure definition: Occurs where Lagercrantz subsidiaries invoice customers in a different currency from their input cost base, or where group treasury movement of cash between currency areas creates conversion exposure
  • Primary transaction exposure points:
  • Electronics/component-adjacent subsidiaries sourcing in USD, selling in SEK or EUR — estimated to affect 15-25% of one or two sub-segments
  • Subsidiaries with USD-linked export revenue (e.g. marine, defence-adjacent) incurring costs in SEK/NOK — USD-revenue / SEK-cost mismatch
  • Inter-company royalty or management fee flows from foreign subsidiaries to Swedish parent (SEK-denominated charges to non-SEK subsidiaries) — creates a small structural transaction exposure
  • Cash-flow currency mismatch (estimate): Transaction exposure probably represents 10-15% of total FX exposure at group level, with translation representing 85-90%; consistent with Lagercrantz's own statement that it "does not have any significant transaction-based currency exposure" (proximate annual report language)
  • Margin economic impact: For the electronics-import subsidiaries, a 10% USD/SEK appreciation would reduce their gross margin by approximately 1-3pp (assuming 20-30% of COGS in USD); at group scale this translates to approximately 10-20 basis points on group gross margin — material at the segment level, minor at group level
  • Pricing pass-through: Lagercrantz's niche positioning in specialised products with limited alternatives supports pricing power; management has indicated ability to pass through cost increases including FX-driven cost inflation at the subsidiary level with a 3-6 month lag
  • Pricing pass-through is segment-specific: higher in proprietary products (most of the portfolio), lower in distribution/resale segments
  • The lag effect means a rapid 15-20% FX move would hit margins for one to two quarters before repricing takes effect
  • Cost pass-through ability: Similar dynamic — where Lagercrantz subsidiaries supply to large OEM customers with annual contracts, mid-year repricing may not be possible; the annual contract renewal is the natural pricing reset
  • Transaction vs translation magnitude: Transaction approximately 10-15% of total group FX risk; translation approximately 85-90% — this is the key decomposition

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on transaction exposure is that management's characterisation of "no significant transaction-based exposure" reflects a group-level netting view that obscures subsidiary-level concentrations; if the two or three electronics-component-importing subsidiaries together represent SEK 500-800m of revenue and 30% of their COGS is USD-denominated, the economic impact of a sustained USD strengthening episode is meaningful at segment level even if immaterial at group level — and those subsidiaries likely operate at above-average margins, making the EPS impact disproportionate to their revenue share

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Hedging policy is translation-accepting (no group translation hedge), transaction-hedging (subsidiary-level short-duration forwards for cash-flow mismatches), with limited central oversight and low transparency — broadly appropriate for the business model but below best-practice disclosure standards

  • Hedging mandate: Lagercrantz's stated policy (annual report risk management section) is to hedge "significant" transactional foreign currency exposures at the subsidiary level on a rolling basis; translation exposure is explicitly not hedged at group level — consistent with standard decentralised-industrials practice
  • Hedge horizon: Short — subsidiary-level hedges are described as short-term, consistent with a 3-6 month horizon for forward contracts; no evidence of 12-24 month laddering at group level; this is a meaningful duration-shortfall vs best practice
  • Hedge ratio: Not disclosed quantitatively; described qualitatively as covering "material" transaction exposures; researcher cannot independently verify hedge ratio from public disclosure — flagged as key gap
  • Hedge instruments: Forward contracts described as primary instrument; no evidence of options or collars in disclosed policy; this is a functional but limited instrument suite (forwards give certainty but no upside participation if FX moves favourably)
  • Translation hedging: Explicitly absent — consistent with group's stated policy; no net investment hedges (which some companies use to partially offset the balance-sheet translation of net assets in foreign subsidiaries) identified in financial statements
  • Transaction hedging: Present but not centrally consolidated — each subsidiary manages its own exposure; group treasury role appears to be coordination and monitoring, not centralised hedging
  • Hedge effectiveness L3Y:
  • FY22/23: SEK depreciation made hedging unrealised gains on any hedges held (i.e. hedges against SEK appreciation would have been out-of-the-money); net hedge gain/loss not separately disclosed — this is a disclosure gap
  • FY23/24: FX movements moderated; hedge effectiveness neutral to marginally positive
  • FY24/25: Similar — neutral environment made hedging largely irrelevant
  • Overall: Cannot benchmark effectiveness from public disclosure; the L3Y benign FX environment for Lagercrantz (SEK weakness = tailwind; no hedging needed to protect) means hedging effectiveness has not been stress-tested in a hostile environment
  • Hedge cost: Not separately disclosed; likely immaterial in magnitude given short-duration, low-notional forward contracts; typical cost for short-dated SEK/EUR forwards is 0.2-0.5% per annum on notional
  • Recent hedge realisation: No gain/loss on derivative instruments separately disclosed in IFRS 9 / hedge effectiveness table — this is a disclosure quality concern; it could mean hedging is trivially small, or it could mean disclosure is aggregated into cost lines
  • Board mandate and governance: Annual report states that the board sets the framework for FX risk management; no evidence of a published Treasury Policy document or quarterly hedge-position disclosure
  • Comparison to best practice: Best-practice decentralised industrials (e.g. Indutrade, Addtech peer-group) typically do publish at minimum: hedge notional outstanding, hedge ratio, hedge duration, and realised gain/loss; Lagercrantz's disclosure is below this standard
  • Net investment hedging: No formal net-investment hedging (balance-sheet translation of foreign subsidiary net assets) identified; this means the balance-sheet FX exposure is also unhedged, consistent with translation-accepting policy but creating balance-sheet equity volatility

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on hedging discipline is that the decentralised subsidiary-level model means hedging quality is only as good as each subsidiary's own treasury capability — and a portfolio of 60+ operating companies of varied size and sophistication will have highly heterogeneous hedging practice; there is no disclosed centralised consolidation of subsidiary hedge positions, meaning group-level transaction exposure could be significantly larger than management's qualitative disclosure implies; the absence of IFRS 9 derivative gain/loss disclosures is the most concrete warning signal

§6 — Quantified Sensitivity Analysis

J-front verdict: A 10% SEK appreciation vs the full foreign-currency basket reduces reported EPS by approximately 6-8%; EUR/DKK-bloc exposure is the largest single driver; FX sensitivity is a material factor in EPS trajectory modelling and is currently pointing to a neutral-to-mild-headwind for N12-24M

Translation vs Transaction Decomposition

  • Translation effect: Mechanical, reverses if FX reverses; represents approximately 85-90% of total FX impact; shows up as revenue and EBIT differential in reported SEK numbers
  • Transaction effect: Durable during a sustained FX shock; represents approximately 10-15% of total FX impact; shows up as margin compression in affected subsidiaries; does NOT reverse until contracts reprice (3-12 month lag)
  • Key principle: Do not conflate — a 10% SEK appreciation produces c.SEK 80-90m EBIT headwind from translation (mechanical, fully reversible if SEK weakens back) plus approximately SEK 10-15m additional EBIT impact from transaction channels (durable until repricing); total approximately SEK 90-105m per 10% SEK appreciation
