SECTION B — Executive Summary (~720 words)

Monopoly at an inflection point. Getlink controls the only fixed cross-Channel transport link until 2086 — a structural monopoly with uncapped shuttle pricing, a 50%+ EBITDA margin, and a near-investment-grade balance sheet. Multiple genuine change forces are converging on a 2026–2030 timeline: the high-speed rail market is opening to new operators (ending a 30-year Eurostar monopoly), ferry operators face a structural cost burden from EU/UK carbon and wage regulations that management expects to restore freight pricing power, and the European Entry/Exit System biometric border rollout (April–September 2026) positions the tunnel's digital infrastructure as materially faster than Dover/Folkestone ferry ports. These are real, specific, and in progress.

But three consecutive years of revenue contraction and a 33x+ valuation leave no margin for error. Revenue has declined from €1.80bn (2023) to €1.60bn (2025), freight truck volumes have fallen for three years running, and FCF compressed 21% in 2025 (€374m from €471m) as capital expenditure began stepping up to a €170–220m annual cycle through 2032. The stock is at new multi-year highs (€18.57 vs the February 52-week high of €18.24) after a 14% rally into the Capital Markets Day — the positive catalysts (dividend reset, €1bn 2030 EBITDA target) are now partly priced in at headline level.

Two competing hypotheses. Thesis A: multi-year structural EBITDA re-rate as the high-speed rail market opens, ferry pricing rationalises, and the Customs Services pivot compounds — the case for progressing to ESA. Thesis B: a quality value trap where FCF growth is absorbed by elevated capex (€170–220m vs ~€55m historically), the €516m ElecLink profit-sharing provision, and the post-2052 French tax step-up (25% → 59%) — the case for demanding a lower entry price. At Triaging stage, the change forces are sufficient to justify an ESA pass; the FCF bridge and valuation framework are the ESA's primary tests.

Stage qualitative test verdict: conditional pass. The case has genuine positive change forces — HSR market opening is structural and transformative; ferry regulatory burden is building and directional; EES advantage is near-term and concrete. The forces are medium-term in their full expression (2027–2030) rather than immediately visible in reported numbers. This is enough to progress; it is not enough to invest without ESA-level interrogation of the valuation and FCF bridge.

RA1 — Change forces? — summary
  • Structural convergence of concurrent change vectors: more genuine change forces are simultaneously in train (HSR market opening, ferry regulatory burden, EES border advantage, customs services pivot, dividend reset) than at any prior point since the 2007 safeguard restructuring — this is not a thin story based on one catalyst.
  • The dominant change force is structural, not cyclical: high-speed rail has been a Eurostar monopoly since 1994; Virgin Trains (~2030) and Trenitalia (~2029) ending that monopoly is not a temporary event — Getlink collects network access tolls on every additional passenger regardless of operator, meaning new entrants are revenue upside, not a competitive threat to the core concession economics.
  • Freight recovery is a secondary but credible change force: truck volumes -3% in 2025 and -3% YTD 2026 year-on-year, but the drivers are cyclical (UK automotive weakness, subdued macro) rather than structural; EU/UK SPS simplification and a projected 30% UK automotive production recovery by 2030 are concrete recovery catalysts with identifiable timelines.
  • ElecLink normalisation is a resolved rather than active headwind: the segment has transitioned from windfall mode (energy crisis 2022–2024) to stable infrastructure asset; the outstanding uncertainty is the €516m profit-sharing provision timing, not the underlying asset's long-term demand fundamentals — 81% of 2026 capacity pre-sold at €242m confirms sustained commercial viability.
  • Capital allocation reset is a concrete signal of management intent: dividend reset to €0.80 rising to €1.00 by 2030, stated to be 23–30% above prior sell-side consensus — this is management publicly committing to a distribution framework that requires disciplined FCF generation, not mere optimism.
  • Internal commercial evolution reinforces the external change story: "More for More" yield strategy (3% average shuttle yield uplift in 2025 despite 3% freight volume decline), Customs Services M&A (three acquisitions in 12 months), "WAY forward" lean management program — the internal initiatives are deliberately sequenced to compound with the external change forces.
CQ1 — Strong external change forces / tailwinds? (6 bullets)
  • HSR market opening is the largest single external change force: the Channel Tunnel's structural monopoly on high-speed rail between the UK and continental Europe ends when Virgin Trains and Trenitalia launch; Jefferies estimates the 2030 HSR passenger target is already conservative, sitting 5% below the prevailing buy-side consensus of +3.0m passengers — the setup is tilted toward positive surprise.
  • EU/UK regulatory burden on ferry operators is building and structural: Seafarers' minimum wage and rest time laws, EU Emissions Trading System inclusion for maritime, UK ETS for domestic shipping — Morgan Stanley expects these to break the price-war cycle that has been eroding LeShuttle Freight pricing since Brexit; the mechanism is irreversible once implemented.
  • EES implementation (April–September 2026) creates a durable border processing advantage: Getlink's digital infrastructure (biometric scanning, facial recognition, smart border technology) positions the tunnel's terminals as materially faster than Dover/Folkestone ferry ports, which lack the equivalent digitised processing capability — a near-term catalyst that converts regulatory complexity into competitive advantage.
  • European electricity market structural growth supports ElecLink's long-term demand: data centre growth (10 GW to 35 GW by 2030 in Europe), renewables intermittency requiring grid balancing, and ENTSO-E's 108 GW transmission capacity gap collectively guarantee that a 1 GW interconnector between France and the UK remains a high-utilisation critical infrastructure asset through the 2047 regulatory exemption.
  • EU/UK SPS agreement simplification is a freight-specific near-term tailwind: reduced border checks on fresh food and agricultural products directly accelerates time-sensitive flows — precisely the freight segment where Getlink's speed advantage over ferries is most defensible and where pricing power should be strongest.
  • Air-to-rail modal shift is structural and accelerating: Air France-KLM's "Air & Rail" intermodal partnership with Eurostar; London-Amsterdam Eurostar market share at only 51% (vs 76.9% on London-Paris) implies a large addressable gap — each new direct route to Germany or Switzerland (internal studies: 2m passengers per route) represents incremental toll revenue that requires no additional infrastructure spend by Getlink.
CQ2 — Strong internal change forces? (6 bullets)
  • Customs Services build-out is a strategically differentiated internal change: three acquisitions in 12 months — ChannelPorts (April 2024), ASA and BIMS (January 2025) — create a recurring B2B revenue stream from post-Brexit compliance complexity; pricing power is uncorrelated with transport volumes and grows with UK-EU trade regulation complexity, not against it.
  • "More for More" yield strategy is producing measurable financial traction: 3% increase in average shuttle yield in 2025 despite a 3% freight volume decline — the yield offset has partially cushioned the volume headwind and demonstrates that the unbundled service model is accepted by customers, not resisted.
  • Management depth has been meaningfully strengthened across the executive committee: Périchon promoted to Deputy CEO/CFO (March 2024), Cazelles (20+ years SNCF/Keolis experience) as Eurotunnel Deputy CEO (October 2024), Bagur as Chief Business Development Officer (2024) — the team executing the 2030 plan is better resourced and more operationally experienced than at the time of the CMD announcement.
  • Europorte's Electrofer acquisition (June 2025, 67% stake) extends infrastructure services capability: rail processing and track renewal is an adjacent market that positions Europorte for the heavy capex cycle ahead — the post-2030 Fixed Link mid-life refurbishment contracts are a natural revenue opportunity for a vertically integrated Europorte operation.
  • "Power to Traction" solar project directly addresses the largest variable cost line: 80,000 photovoltaic panels at Coquelles terminal covering 12% of total group electricity consumption by 2028 — cost reduction with a competitive differentiation bonus (Getlink's electrified transport vs diesel ferries) and alignment with corporate procurement ESG scoring.
  • CEO mandate continuity provides an unusual execution runway: Leriche's 4-year renewal in 2024 with mandatory equity retention requirements throughout; the Vice-Chairman designation in 2026 further anchors his strategic authority — this is rare governance stability for a major French listed company facing a medium-term transformation programme.
RA2 — Robust base? — summary
  • The structural base is as robust as European infrastructure gets: one physical asset, one concession agreement to 2086, no competing fixed link (estimated €14bn+ capex barrier to entry at 1994 prices), no government subsidy dependency — the legal and physical barriers to competition are absolute for 60 years.
  • But the financial base has clear cracks: 3 consecutive years of revenue decline (€1.80bn → €1.60bn), FCF down 21% in 2025 (€374m from €471m), and the capex cycle stepping up materially (€170–220m vs ~€55m historical) — the "robust base" is structural in nature, not financial in its current trajectory.
  • The deleveraging trajectory is a genuine credit to management discipline: net debt/EBITDA at 3.9x (below 4.0x for the first time), S&P and Fitch both upgraded to BB+ in 2025, CLEF securitisation vehicle at BBB+ — the balance sheet has been significantly de-risked over the decade since the 2007 safeguard restructuring.
  • Cost base has been proven under extreme volume stress: the SHIELD programme delivered €40m in structural savings during Covid; Eurotunnel's EBITDA margin at 55.7% is at multi-year highs despite revenue pressure — management has demonstrated genuine operating leverage discipline when required, not just in good times.
  • The €1.5bn gross cash pile is an unusual and underappreciated buffer: far above the historical "normal" of €300–600m; simultaneously covers the €516m provision exposure, the elevated capex cycle, and the committed dividend trajectory — the cash position buys time for the change forces to materialise without forcing a balance sheet distress decision.
  • The own-risk concession structure creates a clean equity story: Getlink bears all volume and financial risk with no state subsidies or guarantees — when change forces materialise (HSR volumes, freight pricing recovery), the upside flows directly to equity holders without regulatory sharing; the asymmetric downside of own-risk is already in the price history (2007 restructuring, Covid).
CQ1 — Absence of external headwinds? (6 bullets)
  • ElecLink normalisation is the most active headwind: 2025 revenue -20% to €225m from the energy crisis peak; the €516m provision for profit-sharing above the 13% IRR threshold is a real cash obligation whose timing management has not committed to — the magnitude is known, the cash schedule is not.
  • Freight market remains structurally challenged in the near term: DFDS, Irish Ferries, and P&O collectively operate 11 ships with active space-charter coordination, strengthening competitive capacity; the rational pricing inflection that management expects requires regulatory cost phase-in that is confirmed but not yet economically effective.
  • UK macroeconomic weakness is a persistent background headwind: 42% of consolidated debt is GBP-denominated, 83% of passenger demand originates from the UK, and UK GDP sensitivity means a prolonged slowdown extends the freight recovery timeline while dampening passenger shuttle growth — a UK recession is not the base case but is not the tail risk it was in 2021 either.
  • Post-2052 tax step-up (25% → 59%) is a long-dated but material DCF headwind: structured as compensation for the 1994 concession extension, this represents a structural transfer of value from equity to the French state; it is not trivial in any long-duration DCF and needs explicit modelling at ESA — the "2086 concession duration" argument is less linear than it first appears.
  • Dual-blockholder governance (Eiffage 29.4%, Mundys targeting ~29.9%) creates capital allocation constraints: share buybacks are effectively blocked (Eiffage is at the mandatory bid threshold margin); any major strategic decision requires informal coordination with both; corporate event risk (mandatory bid trigger) is elevated if either party makes an incremental open-market purchase.
  • UK business rates challenge is a live regulatory risk: the Valuation Office Agency's ~200% increase proposal for Eurotunnel's UK terminal assets was contested by management with final decision expected late March 2026 — the outcome post-dates the research data and needs confirmation; if upheld, it is a direct and quantifiable margin headwind on UK operations.
CQ2 — Well-invested base? (6 bullets)
  • Mid-life refurbishment programme is funded and underway: the elevated capex guidance (€170–220m pa through 2032 vs ~€55m historical) specifically accounts for the 30-year lifecycle asset renewal for the Passenger Shuttle fleet — this is appropriate investment, not a surprise, and management has been transparent about the cycle timing.
  • ERTMS signalling upgrade is a mandatory regulatory investment that also unlocks capacity: the European Rail Traffic Management System installation across both running tunnels is a capex commitment that improves the long-term safety and capacity of the asset — infrastructure investors should value this higher than maintenance capex.
  • ElecLink infrastructure fragility is a disclosed and relevant risk: the September 2024 to February 2025 technical suspension revealed single-point-of-failure vulnerability in a cable carrying 14% of group revenues — the 2025 operating data includes revenues from a period when ElecLink was partially offline; the operational reliability track record since commissioning in 2022 is short and has already included multiple outages.
  • Digital infrastructure investment is ahead of schedule and operationally effective: biometric EES systems, smart border technology, 90% direct online booking with a proprietary customer data asset — modest in capex terms but creating defensible service advantages and a yield-management data platform that will compound in value as the passenger market grows.
  • ElecLink 2 is not yet a committed investment but represents genuine optionality: regulatory review by Ofgem and CRE is underway; the €826m estimated investment (outside current guidance) is at a demonstrated IRR above 13% based on ElecLink 1's economics — approval probability is unknown but the option value is non-trivial at ESA stage.
  • Eurostar fleet order (Alstom Avelia Horizon, 30 firm + 20 optional, delivery 2031–2033) is the critical capacity investment: the 20% additional capacity per train is funded and contracted — but the 5-7 year delivery timeline means that HSR volume growth is physically constrained until 2031 regardless of new operator launches; this is the binding capacity constraint on the HSR growth story.
CQ3 — Past/present fundamental delivery congruency with thesis? (6 bullets)
  • EBITDA headline delivery is acceptable but declining in quality: 2025 beat of initial guidance (€859m vs €780–830m guidance range) was inflated by a €55m insurance compensation payout — ex-insurance, underlying delivery was €822m, only at the upper-middle of the guidance range; the quality of the beat matters and management would be aware investors can see through the insurance inclusion.
  • FCF conversion is deteriorating and this is the most important delivery risk: operating cash flow declined from €865m (2024) to €816m (2025); FCF declined from €471m to €374m (-21%) as capex began stepping up — the cash generation trajectory is moving in the wrong direction at exactly the moment management is committing to a progressive dividend policy.
  • Management freight guidance credibility is poor and this is a specific red flag: the freight recovery narrative has been deferred for at least two years; truck volumes have missed expectations in 2024 and 2025 consecutively; the "ferries will price rationally" thesis is not a new argument — this specific recovery forecast must be evaluated with appropriate scepticism at ESA.
  • ElecLink operational reliability has been inconsistent and concerning: a 5-month suspension (September 2024 to February 2025) and a shorter May 2025 outage represent two reliability failures in the asset's third and fourth year of operation — for a 25-year regulatory exemption asset, this early-stage reliability profile warrants serious scrutiny.
  • The dividend reset is concrete delivery on a stated change signal: €0.80/share for 2026 is publicly committed, distributed, and constitutes a real financial commitment — this is the one area of management delivery that is congruent with the positive thesis and should be weighted accordingly.
  • Customs Services M&A execution is proceeding as signalled: three acquisitions in 12 months is a credible execution pace; ChannelPorts is an established operator (not a speculative startup); the commercial logic (post-Brexit compliance complexity as a recurring revenue source) is being implemented, not just described.
CQ4 — Large change-from-current / mispricing? (6 bullets)
  • At €18.57 the stock is unlikely to be obviously mispriced: new 52-week highs after a 14% CMD rally means the headline catalysts (dividend reset, €1bn EBITDA target) are partly priced in — this does not mean mispricing is absent, but it shifts the burden of proof to ESA to identify where specific market underestimation resides.
  • The structural HSR upside (post-2030 +10m passengers) is plausibly unpriced: few sell-side models incorporate the full volume uplift from Virgin Trains and Trenitalia at validated commercial probabilities — the timing uncertainty (rolling stock regulatory hurdles, depot constraints) means the street is not actively modelling this as a base case, creating information asymmetry that ESA research can test.
  • ElecLink 2 represents material optionality not in consensus valuations: a second 1 GW interconnector at demonstrated economics (>13% IRR) would generate €200m+ in incremental EBITDA by the mid-2030s — at Getlink's current EV/EBITDA, this would be worth ~€2bn in enterprise value; the market is not pricing this in.
  • The bear-case mispricing risk is equally real: if the ElecLink provision crystallises as a large discrete cash event, if HSR new entrants are delayed beyond 2032, and if freight recovery doesn't emerge by 2028, the FCF generative capacity does not support a 33x multiple or the progressive dividend simultaneously — this scenario is also not the consensus base case.
  • Sum-of-parts analysis is structurally more revealing than EV/EBITDA for this asset: the pre/post-2052 tax structure, the ElecLink provision, and the ElecLink 2 optionality all require explicit segment-level modelling — the lack of a widely published SOP framework creates informational asymmetry that could be exploited with disciplined ESA work.
  • The case-from-current change is a medium-term story, not near-term: the freight recovery and HSR new entrant catalysts are 2027–2030 in expression; the EES border advantage is the one near-term (2026) catalyst that will show in 2026 traffic data — the mispricing, if it exists, requires a 24-36 month horizon to crystallise.
CQ5 — Low trading multiple? (6 bullets)
  • The stock is definitively not cheap: trailing P/E of ~36x at €18.57 (using 2025 underlying EPS) vs European infrastructure peers — Vinci 16.7x, Eiffage 15.0x, Aena 16.9x — Getlink trades at roughly 2x the peer group P/E multiple; the multiple compression risk is real and quantifiable.
  • The concession duration provides a partial but not complete premium justification: Getlink's 2086 concession vs Vinci's French motorways expiring 2035–2036 is a genuine structural advantage; but 2x premium requires the HSR optionality and Customs Services compound to materialise — it is not justified by current reported earnings power alone.
  • EV/EBITDA implied yield is thin given the FCF profile: ~6.2% EBITDA yield on a ~€13.9bn EV; after deducting the elevated capex cycle and the €516m provision overhang, the effective FCF yield is below 3% — thinner than most European infrastructure names at lower multiples.
  • The Ferrovial comparison (P/E ~51x) defines the upside valuation scenario: Ferrovial is priced for long-duration optionality in North American managed lanes — if the HSR market opening is genuinely transformative and the ElecLink 2 gains approval, a re-rating toward Ferrovial-type multiples over 5-7 years is arithmetically possible but demands above-consensus execution.
  • Dividend yield provides a genuine valuation floor: €0.80 dividend / €18.57 = 4.3% yield, growing mechanically to ~5.4% by 2030 at €1.00 per share — at or above European infrastructure sector average (Morgan Stanley estimates ~4%); this yield floor limits the downside to a scenario of dividend cut, which the cash pile buffer makes unlikely over a 2-3 year horizon.
  • Conclusion on valuation at Triaging: a yellow flag, not a red: the stock is not cheap enough to be an obvious value entry, but the HSR structural optionality and ElecLink 2 upside justify ESA-level work to determine whether the current multiple embeds or ignores these drivers — valuation is an ESA gate, not a Triaging disqualifier.

RA1 — Focused bet? — summary
  • Case reduces to two credible fulcrum drivers: (1) HSR market opening — new operators (Virgin Trains, Trenitalia) driving incremental access toll revenue on additional passengers; (2) ferry cost inflection — regulatory burden (ETS, minimum wages) forcing rational pricing and restoring LeShuttle Freight market share and yield — both are medium-term, well-defined, and structurally grounded.
  • The case holds together even if secondary segments are flat: a thesis that rests on Eurotunnel's HSR toll revenue growth alone (ignoring ElecLink and Europorte) is still a credible investment — this is the sign of a focused case, not a multi-factor complexity trap that requires everything to go right simultaneously.
  • The four key drivers for ESA are identifiable and bounded: (1) HSR new entrant launch timeline; (2) ferry capacity/pricing response to ETS and wage laws; (3) ElecLink provision cash timing; (4) FCF bridge 2026–2030 under elevated capex — four drivers is the right complexity level for a Triaging pass.
  • Customs Services is an additive simplification: recurring B2B compliance revenue with pricing power uncorrelated to transport volumes de-risks the thesis by adding a non-cyclical revenue stream — it does not add analytical complexity, it reduces the case's sensitivity to traffic volume cycles.
  • No spurious complexity elements are present: no exotic derivatives, no foreign currency earnings complexity beyond straightforward GBP/EUR, no minority interest obscuring the P&L, no multi-geography operating risk — the case structure is clean.
  • The main focus risk is over-counting ElecLink as a fulcrum driver: the €516m provision is a risk to manage, not a positive change force — if ElecLink is incorrectly framed as a growth driver at ESA, the case architecture becomes confused; the two-driver framing (HSR + freight pricing) is the correct lens.
CQ1 — 2 or fewer fulcrum drivers AND 4 or fewer key drivers? (6 bullets)
  • Fulcrum 1 (high conviction): HSR market opening — defined catalyst, named operators (Virgin Trains, Trenitalia), concrete economic mechanism (incremental access tolls per passenger), plausible timeline (2029–2030 launches); Getlink's economic exposure is clear and additive.
  • Fulcrum 2 (medium conviction): ferry cost rationalisation — the regulatory mechanisms are real (ETS, minimum wages) but the timing is uncertain; ferry operators' strategic pricing response is the unknown variable; this is a lower-conviction fulcrum at Triaging than the HSR market opening.
  • Key driver 1 — HSR new entrant viability: Virgin Trains has licensing but faces rolling stock regulatory hurdles and maintenance depot constraints — these are addressable but not trivial; Trenitalia's timeline is slightly ahead at ~2029; the regulatory pathway needs ESA-level verification.
  • Key driver 2 — Ferry pricing discipline: if DFDS and P&O maintain aggressive pricing despite their rising cost bases (absorbing ETS costs through margin compression rather than passing through to customers), the freight pricing recovery thesis weakens materially; the competitive response is not deterministic.
  • Key driver 3 — ElecLink provision crystallisation: when the €516m crystallises and in what cash profile (lump sum or spreading); whether the existing €1.5bn cash pile survives all simultaneous cash commitments (provision + capex + dividend) is a specific FCF bridge question.
  • Key driver 4 — FCF bridge integrity: can the business sustain the progressive dividend (€0.80 → €1.00) under elevated capex (€170–220m) and provision outflows simultaneously? — this is a quantitative question for ESA, not a qualitative concern at Triaging; the cash pile buffer makes it unlikely to become acute in the next 24 months.
RA2 — Narrow perimeter?
  • Geographic perimeter is as narrow as any European large-cap: UK-France core (>95% of revenue), Belgium/Germany minor (Europorte cross-border flows only) — no emerging market risk, no political instability, no multi-currency complexity beyond the bilateral GBP/EUR exposure.
  • Three clean, non-overlapping reporting segments: Eurotunnel (transport infrastructure), ElecLink (energy transmission), Europorte (rail freight services) — no inter-segment transfer pricing, no blended margin obscuring individual business performance.
  • Concession legal structure is stable and bilateral: Treaty of Canterbury (1986) framework has survived Brexit, multiple UK government changes, and two concession extensions without material dispute — the legal perimeter is not a source of surprise risk.
  • Dual-blockholder governance is the perimeter complexity outlier: Eiffage (29.4%) and Mundys (targeting ~29.9% voting rights) create a stakeholder management dimension that is unusual for a French CAC-listed company and that constrains capital allocation flexibility — any major capital decision requires implicit coordination with both, which is a practical constraint even if not a legal one.
  • Customs Services acquisitions are deliberate and adjacent: ChannelPorts, ASA, BIMS are all UK-EU border services businesses, directly adjacent to Getlink's core operating territory — not speculative diversification; they do not expand the perimeter in a way that creates new analytical complexity.
  • Europorte's French domestic network is the operational complexity element: 65% France-domestic revenues, subject to French rail liberalisation and SNCF competitive dynamics, requiring French regulatory relationships — at 11% of group revenue it does not threaten the central thesis, but ESA should verify Europorte's competitive moat in its domestic market.
CQ1 — 10 or fewer geographies and/or business units? (6 bullets)
  • Geographies: 2 core + 2 minor — UK and France dominate (>95% of revenue); Belgium and Germany are minor cross-border rail freight routes for Europorte; effectively a bilateral UK/France infrastructure play.
  • Business units: 3 formal segments + 1 emerging — Eurotunnel, ElecLink, Europorte are reported; Getlink Customs Services is becoming a de facto fourth unit but is not yet reported separately (below segment reporting threshold).
  • Well within the 10-unit guideline by a wide margin: the case architecture is cleaner than most European infrastructure operators; no subsidiary maze, no joint venture complexity of substance.
  • No uncontrolled economics in joint ventures: the concession is owned directly by Getlink SE; ElecLink is wholly owned; Europorte France and subsidiaries are majority owned — no minority drag complicating the consolidated P&L.
  • Bi-national regulatory framework adds governance complexity but is well-established: UK Ofgem + French CRE for ElecLink; UK Office of Rail and Road + French ARAFER for rail — two regulators per segment, but relationships are mature and not subject to surprise interventions.
  • Verdict: perimeter scores well — 4 effective geographies, 3-4 business units, clean segment structure; the case is one of the narrower perimeter infrastructure names available in European large-cap.
