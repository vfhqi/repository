3. §1 — EXECUTIVE SUMMARY: TOP-3 MOST-DIAGNOSTIC QUESTIONS

  • Q2 (revenue contract structure) — if ≥70% of forward revenue is contractually locked with defined renewal mechanics, bull case is more defensible; if the answer is ambiguous or management pivots to "strong relationships" language, that is bear-signal

  • Q11 (management incentive structure) — if LTI vesting is tied to absolute EPS or TSR over 3Y with no reset provisions, incentives align with the analyst's hold horizon; if vesting is tied to relative metrics or contains easy reset provisions, management is not flying the same plane as the investor

  • Q14 (churn / customer loss mechanism) — if management can name specific churn drivers and give a quantified retention rate with trend, that is genuine operational grip; if the answer is "churn is very low, customers love us," that is a false-friend red flag — no business has zero churn, and inability to name the mechanism suggests it hasn't been measured

4. §2 — THEME 1: REVENUE DRIVERS + VISIBILITY

Verdict — revenue-side signal: highest-uncertainty pillar; contracted vs discretionary split and renewal mechanics are the discriminating variables — forward visibility claim must be tested against actual contract structure, not customer-relationship language

Q1 — End-market segmentation and mix shift

  • Question: "Walk me through revenue by end-market for the last three years — what share is each segment, how has mix shifted, and which segment is growing fastest?"
  • Expected answer: management provides a segment breakdown with directional growth commentary; likely to lead with the fastest-growing segment
  • Good answer signature: quantified mix data with trend; management volunteers the segment that is shrinking and explains why
  • Bad answer signature: segment commentary without percentages; or all segments described as "growing well" with no differentiation
  • Follow-up probe: "Which segment, if it stalled, would most pressure overall growth — and what's the current leading indicator for that segment?"
  • Diagnostic note: fluent segment-level numerics with candid identification of a weaker segment = bull signal (operational grip); inability to differentiate = false-friend signal; if fastest-growing segment is also the most cyclically exposed segment, that is bear-signal — cross-ref Q19 ACH hypothesis H3 (cyclical flattery of structural narrative)

Q2 — Contract structure and revenue visibility (MOST DIAGNOSTIC)

  • Question: "What percentage of next-year's revenue is contracted or under framework agreements today — and what is the average contract duration and renewal mechanic?"
  • Expected answer: management cites a contracted revenue percentage; likely to be higher than the true economic number (contracted ≠ guaranteed)
  • Good answer signature: specific percentage with average remaining contract life; management distinguishes between hard-contracted, framework/preferred-supplier, and spot revenue
  • Bad answer signature: "we have very strong long-term relationships" without a percentage; or cites contracted revenue without addressing renewal mechanics
  • Follow-up probe: "Of the contracts up for renewal in the next 18 months, have any early renewal conversations started — and what pricing direction are those conversations taking?"
  • Diagnostic note: contractually-anchored revenue with defined renewal mechanics and early evidence of renewal = bull signal; relationship-language without contract structure = false-friend signal (durable-appearing revenue that reprices at will of customer); management knowing the renewal schedule = operational grip

Q3 — Volume vs price decomposition

  • Question: "Of your revenue growth over the last two years, how much was volume growth versus price increases — and what is your assumption for each component next year?"
  • Expected answer: management likely to cite a mix of both, with a tendency to attribute more to volume (pricing gains are less defensible in a downside scenario)
  • Good answer signature: explicit volume/price split with trend; management volunteers that pricing benefit is partially one-cycle and explains the normalised assumption
  • Bad answer signature: "we don't track it that way" or blending volume and price into a single "demand strength" narrative
  • Follow-up probe: "If pricing reverts to CPI-level or below, what does that do to your revenue growth assumption — is that already in your guidance?"
  • Diagnostic note: heavy pricing contribution to recent growth that management assumes persists = bear signal (earnings-quality risk); management who quantifies and stress-tests the pricing component = bull signal; cross-ref Q19 ACH H2 (pricing normalisation)

Q4 — Customer concentration

  • Question: "What percentage of revenue comes from your top 5 customers — and has that concentration increased or decreased over the last three years?"
  • Expected answer: management discloses approximate concentration; likely to frame as "well-diversified" regardless of actual figure
  • Good answer signature: specific percentage with directional trend; management identifies which customers are growing and which are stable; no single customer >15-20% unless contractually anchored
  • Bad answer signature: "we have a diversified customer base" without numbers; or top-5 concentration increasing without structural explanation
  • Follow-up probe: "For your top customer — what would the switching cost be for them to move to a competitor, and has that switching cost increased or decreased?"
  • Diagnostic note: high and increasing concentration without strong switching-cost foundation = bear / false-friend signal; declining concentration with maintained growth = bull signal (broad-based demand)

Sceptical close — §2:
- Sceptical note: fluent answers on all four revenue questions should increase rather than decrease caution — management who has been through multiple analyst meetings has rehearsed this material; the diagnostic edge is in the follow-up probes (particularly the renewal-conversation probe in Q2 and the pricing-reversal stress-test in Q3), where a prepared but honest answer differs visibly from a prepared but evasive one

5. §3 — THEME 2: MARGIN TRAJECTORY + LEVERS

Verdict — margin-side signal: operating leverage claim is the most testable — gross margin driver decomposition and mix shift are the discriminating variables; capex / opex deferral is the hidden risk that management is least likely to volunteer

Q5 — Gross margin driver decomposition

  • Question: "Walk me through gross margin movement over the last three years — what are the two or three largest drivers of expansion or contraction, and how do you expect each to trend?"
  • Expected answer: management cites input cost dynamics, mix shift, and pricing; likely to emphasise the most flattering driver
  • Good answer signature: itemised GM bridge (at minimum: pricing contribution, input cost, mix, efficiency); management identifies a GM headwind they are working to offset
  • Bad answer signature: "gross margins have been improving due to operational efficiency" without component-level decomposition
  • Follow-up probe: "If your fastest-growing product/service segment has a different gross margin than your legacy segments — is mix a GM headwind or tailwind going forward?"
  • Diagnostic note: if mix shift from high-margin legacy to lower-margin growth product is a structural GM headwind, bull case on EPS requires offsetting operating leverage — which needs to be tested separately (Q6); cross-ref Q19 ACH H4 (margin erosion via mix)

Q6 — Operating leverage and fixed-cost base

  • Question: "What is your fixed vs variable cost split at the operating level — and at what revenue growth rate does operating leverage become material?"
  • Expected answer: management cites a split and a leverage inflection point; likely to be optimistic on leverage timing
  • Good answer signature: explicit fixed/variable split with a stated revenue scale at which OpEx:Revenue ratio begins to fall; management references a historical period when leverage materialised as proof of concept
  • Bad answer signature: "we have a scalable model" without quantification of the leverage mechanics
  • Follow-up probe: "Has operating leverage materialised in any recent period — what was the revenue growth rate and what was the resulting EBIT margin expansion?"
  • Diagnostic note: historical evidence of leverage (e.g., a year of 15%+ revenue growth with 200bp+ margin expansion) is bull signal; leverage claim that has never been empirically demonstrated = false-friend signal

Q7 — R&D adequacy and reinvestment rate

  • Question: "What percentage of revenue do you invest in R&D or product development — how does that compare to peers, and is it adequate to defend your competitive position over 3-5 years?"
  • Expected answer: management cites a percentage and frames it as "competitive" — almost universally; the question is whether they can defend the adequacy claim
  • Good answer signature: specific R&D-to-revenue ratio with a stated view on peer range; management identifies a specific product capability gap they are closing with current R&D
  • Bad answer signature: "we invest at the right level for our business" without a number or peer reference
  • Follow-up probe: "If a competitor doubled their R&D spend relative to yours, what would you lose in competitive position — and over what timeframe?"
  • Diagnostic note: inability to articulate a specific competitive vulnerability from under-investment = either genuinely defensible position or unexamined complacency — follow-up probe distinguishes; cross-ref Q19 ACH H5 (competitive erosion via reinvestment gap)

Q8 — FX pass-through and pricing-cost spread

  • Question: "What portion of your cost base is FX-sensitive, and how do you protect gross margin when FX moves against you — do you pass through, hedge, or absorb?"
  • Expected answer: management describes a hedging programme and a pass-through mechanism; likely to understate the lag between cost pressure and price realisation
  • Good answer signature: specific hedge horizon with instruments; quantified pass-through lag; management acknowledges a period where FX was a GM headwind and describes how they navigated it
  • Bad answer signature: "we have a hedging programme" without horizon, instrument, or lag quantification
  • Follow-up probe: "In the worst FX quarter in the last three years, what was the actual gross margin impact before hedging gains — how much was absorbed?"
  • Diagnostic note: well-specified hedge programme with demonstrated pass-through in adverse conditions = bull signal; vague hedging claim with no lag quantification = potential earnings-quality risk in adverse FX environment

6. §4 — THEME 3: CAPITAL ALLOCATION + BALANCE SHEET

Verdict — capital allocation signal: stated RONIC discipline is easy to assert; the diagnostic test is the spread between claimed hurdle rates and the historical multiple/IRR on completed M&A — gap between stated and revealed preference is the load-bearing signal

Q9 — M&A philosophy and returns discipline

  • Question: "What is your acquisition hurdle rate, and for the last three acquisitions completed — what IRR are those actually tracking at versus the original underwriting?"
  • Expected answer: management states a hurdle rate (often 12-15% WACC-plus); actual tracking data will be either not provided or selectively presented
  • Good answer signature: specific hurdle rate with a stated post-acquisition tracking process; willingness to cite at least one acquisition that underperformed vs underwriting and what was learned
  • Bad answer signature: "we have a rigorous M&A process with a strong track record" without any specific tracking data; or all acquisitions described as on or ahead of plan
  • Follow-up probe: "What was the last acquisition you decided NOT to do — what price were you willing to pay, and what would you have paid that would have been the wrong price?"
  • Diagnostic note: management who can cite a deal they passed on and articulate why = RONIC discipline is operational; management who can only cite successful deals = potential confirmation bias / survivorship framing; cross-ref Q19 ACH H6 (M&A value destruction)

Q10 — Buyback vs investment return decision

  • Question: "When you choose between buybacks and organic investment, what is the explicit threshold — at what multiple or cash-on-cash return does buyback beat capex or bolt-on M&A?"
  • Expected answer: management frames buyback as opportunistic / residual after investment; most management teams have not explicitly quantified the crossover threshold
  • Good answer signature: management articulates a specific return comparison framework; references a period when buyback was the highest-return option and they executed accordingly
  • Bad answer signature: "we balance returns to shareholders with investment in the business" — this is a non-answer that reveals no decision framework
  • Follow-up probe: "At current share price, is buyback more or less attractive than your pipeline of organic investment — and what does that tell you about what you should be doing with cash right now?"
  • Diagnostic note: management who can explicitly state the crossover comparison = rational capital allocator (bull signal); inability to frame the crossover = capital allocation is instinct-driven, not framework-driven (false-friend risk at scale)

Q11 — Management incentive structure vs EPS trajectory (MOST DIAGNOSTIC)

  • Question: "Walk me through the long-term incentive structure — what metrics drive vesting, what are the performance thresholds, and has the board reset any targets in the last three years?"
  • Expected answer: management describes EPS and/or TSR targets; is unlikely to volunteer reset provisions unless directly asked
  • Good answer signature: LTI tied to absolute EPS or TSR over 3Y with no reset provisions; management voluntarily discloses performance against target and is current-year trajectory
  • Bad answer signature: LTI tied to relative metrics with loose peer group; or any reset provisions in the last 3Y that reduced the difficulty of vesting
  • Follow-up probe: "If you personally held no equity and were paid only a fixed salary, what strategic decision would you make differently?"
  • Diagnostic note (Concept A — power of incentives): this is the highest-diagnostic question in §4 and §6 jointly; compensation structure predicts capital allocation behaviour more reliably than stated philosophy; reset provisions are a specific red flag — they signal the board has rewarded management for underperformance; cross-ref Q19 ACH H7 (management incentive misalignment)

7. §5 — THEME 4: STRATEGY + COMPETITIVE POSITION

Verdict — strategy signal: moat trajectory and competitive response to new entrants are the discriminating variables; adjacency expansion claims require specific proof of repeatable playbook rather than opportunity narrative

Q12 — Win condition and competitive differentiation

  • Question: "When you win a new customer from a competitor, what is the primary reason they chose you — and has that reason changed over the last three years?"
  • Expected answer: management cites product quality, relationships, price, or service — likely to lead with the most flattering reason
  • Good answer signature: management identifies a single primary win reason with supporting evidence (win-rate data, customer feedback); acknowledges a dimension on which competitors are gaining
  • Bad answer signature: "customers choose us for our quality and service" — undifferentiated language that could describe any business
  • Follow-up probe: "What has been your win rate against your primary competitor over the last 12 months — and how does that compare to 3 years ago?"
  • Diagnostic note: declining win rate even against a "quality and relationships" narrative = moat erosion signal; management who knows the win rate = operational grip; management who doesn't track win rate = possible false-friend (appearing durable without measuring durability)

Q13 — Disruption risk and adjacency expansion

  • Question: "What is the most credible form of disruption to your core business over a 5-year horizon — and is your current investment posture adequate to respond?"
  • Expected answer: management identifies a technology or business-model threat; is likely to describe it as manageable and current investments as adequate
  • Good answer signature: management names a specific threat with a quantified estimate of market share at risk; describes a specific investment or partnership response; acknowledges the threat is not fully mitigated
  • Bad answer signature: "we don't see significant disruption risk given our relationships and scale" — this is a failure-to-examine answer
  • Follow-up probe: "If a well-funded new entrant with no legacy cost structure entered your market tomorrow — how long before a customer of yours would even consider switching?"
  • Diagnostic note: specific disruption threat + investment response + honest uncertainty = strategic seriousness (bull signal for moat sustainability); dismissal of disruption risk = complacency signal that, combined with high concentration and low R&D, is a false-friend red flag; cross-ref Q19 ACH H5

8. §6 — THEME 5: MANAGEMENT QUALITY + OPERATOR TRACK RECORD

Verdict — management quality signal: sandbagging discipline and communication clarity are the load-bearing variables; the incentive structure probe (Q11) is jointly owned by this theme and §4 — the follow-up question in Q11 ("what would you do differently without equity?") is the highest-diagnostic management-quality probe in the memo

Q14 — Churn / customer retention mechanism (MOST DIAGNOSTIC)

  • Question: "What is your annual customer retention rate — and when you lose a customer, what is the primary reason, and what do you do with that information?"
  • Expected answer: management cites a high retention rate; is less likely to have a structured loss-analysis process
  • Good answer signature: specific retention percentage with trend; management names the 1-2 most common churn reasons; describes a specific process change made in response to churn data
  • Bad answer signature: "our churn is very low, customers are very happy with us" without a number or loss-analysis process
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the largest single customer you've lost in the last three years — what happened, and what did you change as a result?"
  • Diagnostic note: inability to name a specific lost customer or churn reason = either business has genuinely zero churn (unlikely) or management has not examined the mechanism — the latter is a false-friend signal for apparent stability concealing fragility; naming a specific loss and a specific response = operator grip and intellectual honesty (bull signal)

Q15 — Sandbagging discipline and guidance calibration

  • Question: "Over the last 5 years, how many times has Roko met, beaten, or missed annual guidance — and what is your view of appropriate guidance conservatism?"
  • Expected answer: management describes a track record of meeting or beating; is likely to frame conservatism as prudent financial discipline
  • Good answer signature: specific track record (e.g., beat 4 of 5 years, miss 1 of 5); management distinguishes between operational outperformance and deliberate sandbagging; acknowledges the market-credibility cost of missing
  • Bad answer signature: "we always try to be conservative because we'd rather underpromise and overdeliver" without a specific track record to back it
  • Follow-up probe: "In the year you missed guidance, what was the earliest internal signal that you were going to miss — and at what point did you communicate that to the market?"
  • Diagnostic note: systematic beating by >5% on EPS = sandbagging (positive for earnings quality but worth flagging for expectations management); early communication of a miss = communication discipline; late communication = credibility risk in a downside scenario

Q16 — Power-of-incentives probe: compensation structure (Q20-MMO mandatory)

  • Question: "What fraction of your total compensation is equity vs cash, what are your personal ownership targets, and have you bought or sold shares on the open market in the last 24 months?"
  • Expected answer: management cites a meaningful equity component; is less likely to volunteer open-market transactions
  • Good answer signature: significant equity ownership (≥3-5x salary in stock); recent open-market purchases; no sales except for tax-withholding at vesting
  • Bad answer signature: equity ownership at minimum required level; recent sales beyond tax-withholding; or equity that has fully vested with no new accumulation
  • Follow-up probe: "If the share price dropped 40% tomorrow, what would you personally do — and what would the board do?"
  • Diagnostic note (Concept A — power of incentives): open-market purchases by management are a strong alignment signal; systematic selling beyond tax-withholding is a misalignment signal that overrides positive verbal commentary on strategy; this question is the Q20-MMO gate-required probe; cross-ref Q19 ACH H7

Q17 — Crisis playbook and communication under pressure

  • Question: "Describe the last time a significant operational or financial result was worse than you expected — what was your communication process internally and externally?"
  • Expected answer: management describes a challenge and their response; quality of the narrative reveals communication discipline and crisis instinct
  • Good answer signature: specific event with a specific internal/external communication sequence; management acknowledges what they would do differently; evidence of early stakeholder communication
  • Bad answer signature: difficulty recalling a significant negative event; or narrative focuses entirely on how the team "worked through it" without describing the communication process
  • Diagnostic note: management who cannot recall a significant operational challenge = either genuinely lucky or not examining their business critically; both possibilities are worth flagging; communication process under pressure predicts investor-relations behaviour in a bear scenario

9. §7 — THEME 6: RISK + DOWNSIDE (PRE-MORTEM CROSS-REF)

Verdict — risk signal: failure modes that management is least likely to volunteer (pricing fragility, customer concentration unwind, M&A integration drag) are the highest-diagnostic probes; false-friend probe on customer retention is the most non-obvious risk question

Q18 — Bear-case trigger and pricing fragility

  • Question: "If you had to write a 500-word bear case on Roko, what would the first paragraph say — and which of those bear-case risks are you most worried about internally?"
  • Expected answer: management will produce a bear case — the quality and specificity of that bear case is the diagnostic
  • Good answer signature: management identifies a specific, quantified risk (e.g., "if our top segment decelerates 300bp, EBIT goes X") rather than a generic macro risk; the bear case they name overlaps with the analyst's highest-probability failure modes (cross-ref Q19 ACH matrix)
  • Bad answer signature: bear case focuses entirely on macro (FX, interest rates, general demand softness) without any company-specific failure mode
  • Follow-up probe: "Which of those risks, if it started materialising, would you expect to see in your leading indicators first — and what are those indicators currently showing?"
  • Diagnostic note (Q19 ACH cross-ref): if management's stated bear case does NOT overlap with Q19 pre-mortem's H1-H3 highest-probability failure modes, that is a signal either that management is blind to the most-likely failure mode, or that the analyst's pre-mortem is miscalibrated — either way, the divergence is load-bearing information

Q19 — Customer churn vector and false-friend probe

  • Question: "In a scenario where one of your top-5 customers decided to dual-source or insource — what would be the first 6 months of P&L impact, and how would you detect that early?"
  • Expected answer: management will likely frame this as unlikely; the diagnostic is whether they have modelled the scenario
  • Good answer signature: management produces a quantified scenario (e.g., "top customer at X% of revenue, gross margin Y%, loss would impact EBIT by £Z — we would see it in order book 3 months before it hit revenue"); identifies a specific leading indicator they monitor
  • Bad answer signature: "our customers are deeply embedded, this scenario is very unlikely" — this is a non-answer to a scenario question
  • Follow-up probe: "Has any customer in the top 10 meaningfully reduced their share of wallet with you in the last three years — what happened and what was the result?"
  • Diagnostic note: false-friend probe — businesses that present as structurally sticky often have a concentration fragility that only becomes visible when a large customer reconsiders; management's ability to quantify the scenario = operational grip; inability to quantify = false-friend risk

Q20 — Regulatory exposure and forward regulatory posture

  • Question: "What is your most significant regulatory risk over the next 3 years, and are you currently ahead of or behind the curve in preparing for it?"
  • Expected answer: management identifies a regulatory area; is likely to describe preparation as "well advanced"
  • Good answer signature: specific regulation with a specific compliance investment and timeline; management acknowledges an area where they are still uncertain about the regulatory outcome
  • Bad answer signature: "we work closely with regulators and feel well-positioned" without specifics
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the single regulatory change that would most benefit a competitor at your expense — and how likely is that change over the next 3 years?"
  • Diagnostic note: the asymmetric-regulatory-benefit question (follow-up probe) is highly diagnostic — it forces management to examine a scenario they are not incentivised to volunteer; a thoughtful answer signals strategic depth; a defensive answer ("that wouldn't apply to us") signals either genuine protection or unexamined complacency

Which Q19 ACH-matrix diagnostic items are NOT covered by questions in this memo?
- Sceptical close — §7: ⚠️ Two ACH failure-mode categories are under-covered: (1) execution risk on a transformation or restructuring programme (if Roko is mid-transformation, no question directly tests execution velocity vs plan); (2) balance-sheet covenant triggers or refinancing risk in a rising-rate environment — if Roko carries material leverage, Q10 partially covers allocation discipline but no question directly tests covenant headroom or refinancing exposure; analyst should add a targeted balance-sheet stress question if leverage is a factor in the Q4-Q19 memos

10. §8 — CROSS-CUTTING META + NON-OBVIOUS QUESTIONS

Verdict — non-obvious angle: the questions management is most unprepared for are the ones that require them to rank, choose, or quantify a hypothetical rather than describe a current strength — these are the edge-detection probes that the consensus analyst meeting would not ask

Non-obvious angle — capex triage ⚡

  • WH Smith probe: "If you had to cut 20% of your capex programme tomorrow — which projects survive and which don't?"
  • Why non-obvious: forces management to reveal which capital commitments are truly value-creating vs defensive vs optionality spending; the ranking reveals actual strategic priorities better than any strategy presentation
  • Expected answer: management will likely resist the frame initially ("we wouldn't do that"), then produce a ranking if pressed; the ranking is the intelligence
  • Good answer signature: management can rank capital projects by IRR without hesitation; the surviving projects are growth-oriented and the eliminated projects are defensive or compliance-driven
  • Bad answer signature: "all our capital projects are important to our strategy" — inability or unwillingness to rank suggests capital allocation is not managed with explicit return discipline
  • Diagnostic note: capex triage probe surfaces actual capital-allocation quality — cross-ref Q9 and Q10; this is a stronger test of RONIC discipline than any direct RONIC question because it forces a choice rather than a description

Non-obvious angle — single metric / stewing trigger ⚡

  • Edge-detection probe: "What single metric, if it moved against you by 20% over the next 12 months, would most worry you — and what is the current trend on that metric?"
  • Why non-obvious: this question requires management to identify their own Achilles heel — they are not incentivised to answer candidly (Concept A), which makes a candid answer highly signal-rich
  • Expected answer: most management teams will name a macro metric (customer demand, FX) rather than an operational metric; the analyst should probe to get an internal/operational metric
  • Good answer signature: management names a specific operational metric (win rate, renewal rate, GM per unit, etc.); is willing to share the current trend; if the trend is negative, says so
  • Bad answer signature: "we monitor many metrics, but I'd say general economic demand" — macro deflection; signals either genuine exposure to macro or unwillingness to surface the real leading indicator
  • Follow-up probe: "Is that metric currently improving, stable, or deteriorating — and at what level of deterioration do you escalate it to the board?"
  • Diagnostic note: an operational metric named + a current trend disclosed + an escalation trigger described = very high operational grip (bull signal); macro deflection = either genuine macro exposure or managed non-disclosure

Non-obvious angle — false-friend customer satisfaction proxy ⚡

  • Multiple-truths probe (Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 3): "I accept that your customer satisfaction scores are strong AND retention is high — and yet, are there any signals that customers are satisfied but not growing their wallet-share with you? Is satisfaction masking a commercial stall?"
  • Why non-obvious: this question explicitly holds two truths simultaneously and asks management to examine whether the "and yet" is present — satisfaction and growth are not the same thing; a business can be deeply satisfying to its customers and still be commercially commoditised
  • Expected answer: management may not have separated satisfaction from wallet-share growth in their analysis; the question may prompt genuine reflection rather than a rehearsed answer
  • Good answer signature: management can separately quantify NPS or satisfaction vs revenue-per-customer trend; if revenue-per-customer is flat or declining despite high satisfaction, they acknowledge and explain
  • Bad answer signature: "our customers love us and that drives continued growth" — conflates satisfaction and commercial traction
  • Diagnostic note: satisfaction-without-wallet-share-growth = commercial commoditisation false-friend signal; this is the Q19 ACH H3 (structural vs cyclical) diagnostic at the customer level

Diagnostic crystalliser — the question that changes the analyst's mind:

  • Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 3 — and-yet probe: "I can see the bull case — recurring revenue, operating leverage, strong management track record — and yet, what would you tell me if you wanted me to NOT invest? What is the thing you worry about that isn't in the public filings?"
  • Why load-bearing: this is the single question most likely to surface a non-public concern — not because management will disclose material non-public information, but because the frame (please help me see the downside) invites honesty in a way that a standard bear-case question does not
  • Good answer signature: management names something specific and operational — a market dynamic, a competitive development, a customer behaviour trend
  • Bad answer signature: "I think this is a very good investment opportunity and I wouldn't discourage you" — total non-answer; signals either lack of self-examination or deliberate deflection

Cross-roads question — incentive probe meta:

  • Incentive probe: "In three years, if Roko's share price is exactly where it is today but the business has grown EPS by 40% — are you personally better off, worse off, or the same? Walk me through the maths."
  • Why cross-roads: this question separates equity-value creators from earnings managers — management who are compensated on earnings-per-share targets without a share-price hurdle may be incentivised to grow EPS via buybacks rather than by building enterprise value; the maths of their answer reveals whether their incentive structure rewards shareholder wealth creation or metric management

Stewing trigger — the question the analyst hasn't included because it feels uncomfortable:

  • Sceptical close — §8: the question the analyst most avoids is: "Has there been a period in the last 5 years where you, as an executive team, made a significant strategic mistake — and how did the board respond to that?" This question feels confrontational and personal, and management may respond negatively; but the answer to this question — the quality of self-examination, the board's response mechanism, the willingness to name a mistake — is more diagnostic of management quality than any competence question; the obstacle is the way
