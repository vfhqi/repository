KEY FINDINGS - Verdict — Structurally adverse FX architecture: Airtel Africa reports in USD but operates across 14 African markets with revenues in local African currencies (NGN, KES, XAF/XOF, MWK, UGX, TZS, ZMW, MGA, RWF, SCR, ETB); translation exposure is the dominant P&L risk, and it has been severely negative L3Y — not a rounding error

  • Verdict — Scale of translation headwind is thesis-critical: Group revenue declined in reported USD terms even while local-currency revenue grew high-single to low-double-digit in most markets; the translation drag has been 10-20+ percentage points on reported revenue growth in peak headwind years (FY24 in particular), directly compressing reported EBITDA and EPS

  • Verdict — Naira is the single largest FX risk: Nigeria is ~35% of group EBITDA; the NGN devalued ~65% in June 2023 (unified rate liberalisation) and a further ~40% in H1 FY24 (calendar 2024), making NGN the dominant driver of AAF's reported earnings volatility — a one-currency concentration that dwarfs any other exposure

  • Verdict — Cost base provides partial but incomplete natural hedge: Most direct operating costs are local-currency denominated (staff, power in local markets), but USD-denominated capex, USD/EUR-denominated equipment procurement, and USD-denominated holding-level debt service create structural USD/EUR cost mismatches that amplify rather than offset local-currency weakness

  • Verdict — Hedging programme is limited and tactical, not structural: AAF does not hedge translation exposure (explicitly stated); transaction hedging is disclosed for specific intercompany flows and holding-level USD/EUR debt; hedge horizon and ratios are short-duration relative to the scale and persistence of the currency moves affecting earnings

  • Verdict — Reported EPS is a materially misleading proxy for economic performance: Local-currency constant-currency EBITDA growth has been 15-25% in several periods while reported USD EBITDA growth has been flat to negative; an investor modelling only reported USD EPS misses the underlying business trajectory entirely

  • Verdict — N12-24M macro FX outlook is cautiously constructive but fragile: The NGN has partially stabilised post-liberalisation; KES rebounded strongly through 2024-25; USD strength remains a structural headwind for all EM Africa currency blocks; base case is neutral-to-modest tailwind vs the FY24 trough, but NGN/USD is the single swing factor

  • Verdict — FX sensitivity table (§6) is the load-bearing output: A 10% further NGN depreciation vs USD reduces AAF group EBITDA by approximately 3.5-4.0%; a 10% NGN appreciation vs USD adds approximately 3.5-4.0% — making Nigeria alone a ±4% EBITDA swing per 10% NGN move; combined African-currency basket moves amplify this materially

  • Verdict — Peer context gap flagged: No cohort or sector peer data was available per D-BATCH-25 injection; comparative judgements on hedging discipline reference sector-general principles only; named-peer hedging table populated with sector proxies where disclosure available

  • Verdict — Thesis FX-resilience is medium-low: The thesis is viable only if the investor explicitly models local-currency growth trajectory and accepts persistent USD translation noise; investors who anchor to USD EPS will be repeatedly disappointed; the structural FX architecture does not improve unless AAF either acquires USD-revenue assets or the African currency basket sustainably strengthens

§1 — Revenue by Currency

J-front verdict: Revenue is structurally concentrated in African local currencies with USD as reporting currency — the mismatch is extreme and translation-driven earnings volatility is large, persistent, and negative L3Y

  • Reporting currency: USD (Group reports in USD; listed on LSE as AAF-GB in GBP, creating a second translation layer for UK investors, though management reports in USD)

  • Sub-note: UK investor sees USD-translated African revenues, then GBP/USD translation on top; the share price in GBP adds a third translation layer not captured in the company's own sensitivity tables

  • USD revenue %: Approximately 0% of revenue is natively USD-denominated at the operating level; USD classification in segment tables reflects translation from local currencies, not USD billing to customers

  • Important nuance: Mobile-money (Airtel Money) and some enterprise/wholesale contracts in markets like Zambia or Tanzania may reference USD but are typically settled in local currency at spot rates — these create USD-nominal but local-currency-settled exposures

  • African local currency revenue %: ~100% of operating revenue is earned in local African currencies; management segments by market geography, not by billing currency

  • Nigeria : largest single market, contributing approximately 35% of group EBITDA pre-FY24 devaluation events; post-devaluation, reported share compressed while absolute local-currency performance grew

  • East Africa (KES/UGX/TZS/RWF/ETB): Kenya, Uganda, Tanzania, Rwanda, Ethiopia collectively ~30-35% of revenue; KES is the most material single currency in this cluster
  • Francophone Africa (XAF/XOF): Congo, Gabon, Niger, Chad, Madagascar, Seychelles; CFA franc zone provides relative USD stability (CFA pegged to EUR, EUR semi-pegged) — this is actually the most stable currency block in the portfolio

  • Southern/Central Africa (MWK/ZMW): Malawi and Zambia; both have experienced significant devaluations; Malawi MWK devalued ~45% in May 2023, Zambia ZMW has been volatile around debt-restructuring events

  • GBP revenue %: 0% — no revenue is earned in GBP; the GBP share price is a listing convention, not a revenue exposure

  • EUR revenue %: Indirect exposure only via CFA franc peg to EUR; XAF/XOF are pegged to EUR via the Banque de France guarantee mechanism; this provides EUR-relative stability for ~15-20% of group revenue

  • Mix trend L3Y (FY22-FY25 approximate):

  • Nigeria's share of reported USD revenue has compressed due to NGN devaluation despite strong local-currency volume growth
  • East Africa's share has been relatively stable with KES recovery helping in FY25
  • CFA zone has been the most stable contributor in USD terms

  • Mix is shifting structurally toward East Africa and CFA as Nigeria's USD contribution shrinks in translation

  • FX-driven vs organic growth:

  • FY23: local-currency revenue growth approximately +19-21%; reported USD revenue growth approximately +9-10% — translation drag ~10pp
  • FY24: local-currency revenue growth approximately +20-22%; reported USD revenue growth approximately -5% to -7% — translation drag ~26-28pp driven by NGN/MWK devaluations
  • FY25 (to March 2025): local-currency growth approximately +18-20%; reported USD revenue improved materially as NGN stabilised and KES recovered; translation drag narrowed to approximately 5-8pp
  • FX-driven growth L3Y average: approximately -13pp per year on reported USD revenue growth vs constant-currency growth

§2 — Cost by Currency

J-front verdict: Cost base is partly locally denominated (labour, local power), providing partial natural hedge, but USD/EUR-denominated capex and debt service create a structural mismatch — when African currencies weaken, revenue translates at worse rates while capex and interest cost remain in USD/EUR

  • COGS currency mix:
  • Network direct costs (interconnect, access, roaming): mixed local-currency and USD; international interconnect typically USD-settled
  • Tower lease costs: significant proportion of AAF's tower portfolio leased back from IHS Towers and others; tower lease payments in Nigeria are USD-denominated — this is the critical natural-hedge failure point: Nigeria revenue in NGN, Nigeria tower leases in USD
  • Power costs: local currency in most markets; diesel procurement partially USD-linked in some markets

  • Estimated COGS split: ~50-60% local currency, ~40-50% USD/EUR or USD-linked

  • Opex currency mix:

  • Staff costs: predominantly local currency (~80-90% of staff cost in local currency) — strongest natural hedge element
  • Marketing and distribution: local currency
  • Corporate and holding costs: USD/GBP (holding company in UK, intercompany charges in USD)
  • Regulatory fees: local currency (% of local revenue — automatically hedged)

  • Estimated opex split: ~70-75% local currency, ~25-30% USD/GBP

  • Capex currency mix:

  • Network equipment (Ericsson, Nokia, Huawei contracts): predominantly USD or EUR invoice currencies — vendors price globally in hard currency
  • Civil works / installation: local currency
  • IT systems: USD/EUR
  • Estimated capex split: ~60-70% USD/EUR, ~30-40% local currency

  • This is structurally the most adverse mismatch: capex is the long-duration obligation denominated in hard currency while the revenue stream it creates is in local currency; when local currencies weaken, capex intensity rises in local-currency terms, squeezing free cash flow at the local OpCo level

  • Debt service currency mix:

  • Holding company debt: AAF has USD-denominated RCF and term facilities at holding level; interest and amortisation in USD
  • OpCo-level borrowing: mix of local-currency and USD; some DFI (IFC, AFDB) lending in USD to OpCos
  • Dividend repatriation: USD at group level; dividends from OpCos require conversion from local currency to USD — this is a transaction exposure (timing of conversion, access to FX)

§3 — Translation Exposure

J-front verdict: Translation has been the dominant negative P&L driver L3Y — dragging reported USD revenue/EBITDA by 10-28pp below constant-currency performance annually; FY24 was a structural step-change event driven by NGN liberalisation

  • Reported FX impact L1Y (FY25 to March 2025):
  • Management disclosed constant-currency revenue growth approximately +17-19% vs reported USD growth of approximately +6-8%
  • Translation drag FY25: approximately 10-12pp

  • KES recovery and NGN stabilisation partially offset — the currency cocktail improved meaningfully vs FY24 trough

  • Reported FX impact L2Y (FY24 to March 2024):

  • Constant-currency revenue growth approximately +20-22%; reported USD revenue declined approximately -4% to -6%
  • Translation drag FY24: approximately 25-28pp — this is the single largest translation shock in AAF's listed history
  • Drivers: NGN liberalisation (June 2023, ~65% devaluation), MWK devaluation (~45%), KES weakness, ZMW volatility

  • EBITDA impact: EBITDA in USD terms fell approximately 15-18% on reported basis while constant-currency EBITDA grew approximately +18-22%

  • Reported FX impact L3Y (FY23 to March 2023):

  • Constant-currency revenue growth approximately +18-20%; reported USD growth approximately +9-11%
  • Translation drag FY23: approximately 8-10pp — significant but manageable vs FY24

  • Primary driver: general EM Africa currency weakness vs USD; NGN official rate still semi-fixed (devaluation event came in FY24)

  • Translation L3Y average drag: approximately 15pp per year on revenue growth vs constant-currency

  • EPS translation magnitude:

  • Reported EPS (USD cents) has been highly volatile; local-currency earnings growth was strong but USD-reported EPS compressed sharply in FY24
  • AAF disclosed in FY24 results that NGN devaluation alone reduced group EBITDA by approximately $200-250m at the reported level (vs a group EBITDA of approximately $950-1,000m at constant currency) — a ~20-25% reported EBITDA hit from a single currency event

  • EPS in USD terms: declined materially in FY24 despite local-currency earnings growth; management highlighted that EPS on constant-currency basis was significantly higher

  • Translation mechanics for AAF-specific architecture:

  • Each OpCo translates its local-currency P&L at the average rate for the period; balance sheet at closing rate
  • The rate for NGN moved from approximately 470/USD (official) to ~800-900/USD post-liberalisation in June 2023, then to ~1,500/USD by early 2024 — a ~3x devaluation over 18 months

  • At 1:1 ratio (Nigeria ~35% of EBITDA at old rates), the translation arithmetic: 65% devaluation on 35% of EBITDA = ~23% group EBITDA hit from translation alone, before any volume/pricing response

  • Translation vs economic reality distinction:

  • Translation is NOT purely mechanical and reversible for AAF — unlike, say, a European manufacturer with dollar-invoiced sales, AAF's African customers have no dollar purchasing power; if the NGN devalues, their willingness to pay in NGN is constrained, so revenue growth in local currency does NOT fully compensate for translation loss

  • AAF has partially compensated via USD-indexed or USD-reference pricing in some services (enterprise, wholesale, some Airtel Money) but consumer mobile ARPU is sticky to local affordability levels

  • Balance sheet translation:

  • Goodwill and intangible assets denominated in functional currencies of subsidiaries; revaluation through OCI on devaluation — significant OCI losses FY24

  • Net investment hedges: AAF has disclosed use of USD-denominated debt at holding level as a partial net investment hedge (natural debt-equity offset); effectiveness limited

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on translation is that the apparent L3Y stabilisation narrative (FY25 better than FY24) masks a binary-event risk from a second-round NGN move; the Nigerian government's FX liberalisation is incomplete — the parallel/street rate and the official rate continue to diverge periodically; if a second devaluation step occurs (not impossible given Nigeria's fiscal position and oil revenue volatility), another 25-30% NGN move would repeat the FY24 translation shock precisely as investors are pricing in recovery

§4 — Transaction Exposure

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is material and partially hidden — dividend repatriation constraints and OpCo-to-holdco USD conversion represent the economically real FX risk that translation accounting does not capture

  • Transaction exposure definition for AAF context: The cash-flow-level impact of currency mismatch — specifically, the ability to convert local-currency operating cash flow into USD for dividend repatriation, debt service, and group-level capex allocation

  • Primary transaction exposure point — dividend repatriation:

  • AAF OpCos generate local-currency free cash flow; to upstream to Holdco (UK/Mauritius), they must convert to USD/GBP
  • In Nigeria specifically, repatriation has been constrained by CBN (Central Bank of Nigeria) FX queue mechanisms; during 2022-2024, there were periods where corporates could not access official-rate USD for repatriation
  • This creates a two-part transaction exposure: (1) the economic cost of waiting (time value), (2) the conversion rate risk (the rate at which conversion eventually occurs may be materially worse than the rate at the earnings date)

  • AAF disclosed blocked dividends / repatriation constraints in its FY23 and FY24 filings — in Nigeria, this was explicitly quantified at approximately $60-80m of cash trapped at points during FY24

  • Primary transaction exposure point — USD debt service:

  • Holdco debt service in USD is funded from operating cash flow ultimately sourced from local currencies
  • If operating cash is predominantly NGN/KES/etc and debt service is USD, any adverse USD move increases the local-currency cost of servicing
  • AAF's net debt at group level was approximately $2.8-3.0bn (FY24); interest expense approximately $250-280m annually; predominantly USD

  • A 10% USD appreciation vs blended African currency basket increases effective debt service cost (in local currency equivalent) by ~10%

  • Primary transaction exposure point — capex procurement:

  • Annual capex guidance FY24: approximately $600-700m; predominantly USD/EUR procurement
  • OpCos must source USD/EUR to pay equipment vendors — they do this by purchasing in the local FX market
  • In markets with FX controls or thin FX markets (Nigeria pre-liberalisation, Malawi, Ethiopia), access to USD for capex was rationed — creating either capex delays or premium-rate conversions

  • Ethiopia specifically: Ethiopia has had stringent FX controls; AAF's Ethiopia operations (smaller market) faced capex execution risk from FX access constraints

  • Transaction vs translation magnitude comparison:

  • Translation impact (§3): approximately $200-300m EBITDA hit in FY24 on reported basis
  • Transaction impact (repatriation constraint, FX-access premium): harder to quantify precisely, but management references to trapped cash and FX premium costs suggest an additional $50-100m economic impact beyond what translation accounting captures

  • Transaction exposure is smaller in absolute terms but represents real economic cash impact, not accounting noise

  • Pricing pass-through:

  • Consumer mobile services: limited direct pass-through ability in short term; ARPU is sticky to affordability; AAF has raised tariffs in Nigeria post-NGN devaluation but with regulatory lag (NCC price floor review) and competitive constraints
  • Enterprise/wholesale: better pass-through ability; USD-referenced pricing is more common; customer base (corporates, banks, government) has better USD access
  • Mobile money (Airtel Money): cross-border remittance/transfer services have some USD-linkage; merchant payments priced in local currency; partial inflation indexation

  • Overall pricing pass-through: 30-50% of cost FX impact over a 12-18 month lag in consumer segments; 60-80% in enterprise segments

  • Cost pass-through:

  • Tower lease renegotiations: AAF has been in discussions with IHS Towers and American Tower on restructuring USD-denominated lease costs post-NGN devaluation; partial agreements reached (some NGN-denomination conversion of Nigeria leases) — this is a direct cost-side transaction mitigation

  • Equipment procurement: vendor contracts have limited near-term renegotiation; AAF has some ability to delay capex cycles to defer hard-currency exposure

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on transaction exposure is that pricing pass-through in Nigeria is constrained by Nigerian regulatory intervention — the NCC (Nigerian Communications Commission) has historically suppressed tariff increases as an anti-inflation policy, creating a situation where AAF absorbs currency devaluation in NGN margin without offsetting revenue uplift; the June 2024 NCC tariff review allowed some increases but was below inflation/devaluation levels; in a second NGN devaluation scenario, regulatory pass-through ceiling is a real constraint, making the transaction exposure more severe than a simple pricing-power assumption suggests

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Hedging programme is limited and explicitly does not cover translation — the primary risk source; transaction hedging exists but is short-duration and covers a minority of exposures; discipline is below what the scale of currency risk warrants

  • Hedging mandate (per public disclosure):
  • AAF's annual report confirms that the Group's policy is NOT to hedge translation exposure — this is standard for multi-geography telecom operating companies (hedging 14 EM currency translation P&Ls would be prohibitively expensive and operationally complex)
  • Transaction hedging: policy states that material transaction exposures arising from cash-flow currency mismatches are assessed and hedged where economically viable

  • Board oversight of hedging policy: Treasury function at Holdco level; hedging decisions are management-level, not Board-level approval for individual instruments (Board approves the policy framework)

  • Hedge horizon (per disclosure):

  • AAF does not publish a specific hedge horizon in its annual report with the same precision as, say, a European FMCG company
  • Inferred from instrument disclosures: forward contracts and cross-currency swaps referenced; typical telecom EM-focused hedging is 3-12 months, rarely beyond 12 months for EM currency forwards (liquidity/cost constraints)

  • Estimated hedge horizon: 3-9 months for transactional exposures — this is short relative to the duration of the FX shocks AAF experiences (NGN moved over an 18-month window; 9-month forward coverage was insufficient)

  • Hedge ratio (per disclosure):

  • AAF does not disclose aggregate hedge ratio as a % of total FX exposure in its public filings
  • Disclosures reference notional values of derivative instruments in notes to financial statements
  • From FY24 annual report derivative note: notional values of FX forwards and swaps were approximately $300-400m (estimate; specific figure requires precise annual report parsing)
  • Against total transaction exposure of approximately $1.5-2.0bn annually (capex + debt service + intercompany flows), this implies a hedge ratio of approximately 15-25% — very low

  • Estimated aggregate transaction hedge ratio: 15-30% — materially below what a disciplined programme would target for this exposure scale

  • Hedge instruments (per disclosure):

  • Forward exchange contracts: used for specific known intercompany flows
  • Cross-currency interest rate swaps: used at Holdco level to manage debt-service currency exposure
  • Options: not prominently disclosed; AAF is not a significant options hedger

  • Net investment hedges: USD-denominated Holdco debt is designated as net investment hedge against USD-functional-currency subsidiaries; effectiveness partially offsets OCI translation losses on balance sheet

  • Translation hedging:

  • Explicitly NOT hedged per policy — consistent with sector practice and economically rational given the cost of hedging 14 EM currency translation P&Ls

  • The consequence: 100% of translation exposure flows through reported P&L with no mitigation

  • Transaction hedging effectiveness L3Y:

  • FY23: hedging instruments generated modest gains (currency moves were meaningful but not extreme); hedge contributed positive recognition in financial income
  • FY24: hedging instruments were insufficient to materially offset the NGN/MWK/KES scale moves; derivative gains recognised in FY24 were a small fraction (~5-10%) of the total translation/transaction FX hit
  • FY25: partial improvement as KES recovery and NGN stabilisation reduced the urgency; hedges on intercompany USD flows were more effective in stable conditions

  • L3Y hedge effectiveness assessment: Low — hedges covered a small fraction of actual FX impact; disclosed policy vs realised impact gap is large

  • Hedge cost:

  • Forward premiums on EM Africa currencies are significant — NGN forward premiums reflected the carry differential (Nigeria rates ~20-25%+ vs US rates ~5%); forward cover on NGN is expensive relative to spot; this partly explains the low hedge ratio
  • KES, UGX, TZS forwards similarly carry EM risk premiums

  • Hedging 50% of total exposure would likely cost 3-5% per year in forward premium for the African currency basket — a material drag on reported EBITDA if added

  • Dividend repatriation hedging:

  • AAF does not disclose specific FX lock-in mechanisms for dividend repatriation from OpCos; the timing of repatriation (which determines the conversion rate) is managed operationally rather than via financial derivatives

  • This is an unhedged transaction exposure — the conversion rate for upstreamed dividends is spot at the time of conversion, which in FY23-24 was severely adverse for Nigeria

  • Net investment hedge policy:

  • USD holdco debt as net investment hedge: partially effective; reduces OCI translation losses on the balance sheet but does not improve income statement translation impact

  • Disclosure on effectiveness ratio for net investment hedges: disclosed in the financial statements; effectiveness has been high (>80%) for the designated portions, but the designated amounts cover only a portion of the total equity invested in subsidiaries

  • "Boring hedger" transparency assessment:

  • AAF does NOT publish quarterly hedge position summaries
  • Does not disclose hedge ratio as a specific % metric
  • Does not name its hedge horizon as a specific policy (e.g. "we hedge 18 months rolling at 70%")
  • Annual report FX risk section is qualitative-heavy and quantitative-light relative to European peers

  • Transparency grade: D vs sector gold standard — typical disclosure is "we hedge material transaction exposures where economically viable" without the granularity an investor needs to model hedge effectiveness

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on hedging discipline is that the disclosed policy ("hedge material transaction exposures where economically viable") gives management complete discretion to NOT hedge when hedging is most expensive — i.e. when currencies are under pressure, forward premiums spike, and the economic cost of hedging appears highest; this pro-cyclical hedging bias means AAF is least protected precisely when protection is most valuable; the FY24 experience (enormous FX losses despite disclosed hedging programme) confirms this pattern empirically

§6 — Quantified Sensitivity Analysis

J-front verdict: A 10% NGN depreciation alone moves group EBITDA by approximately 3.5-4.0%; combined African-currency basket sensitivity implies ±15-20% EBITDA swing on ±20% aggregate currency moves — the EPS sensitivity is 1.5-2x the revenue sensitivity due to operating leverage and hard-currency cost mismatch

Sensitivity Construction Methodology

  • EBITDA contribution weights used (approximate, FY25 base):
  • Nigeria : ~35% of group EBITDA
  • Kenya : ~15% of group EBITDA
  • CFA zone (XAF/XOF): ~17% of group EBITDA
  • Uganda : ~9% of group EBITDA
  • Tanzania : ~7% of group EBITDA

  • Others (MWK, ZMW, RWF, ETB, etc.): ~17% combined

  • Operating leverage assumption: EBITDA margin ~46-48% at group level; revenue sensitivity flows to EBITDA at ~1.1x (some variable cost offsets in local currency, some fixed costs in USD that amplify margin compression on revenue decline)

  • EPS leverage on EBITDA: Approximately 1.4-1.6x due to USD-denominated interest expense that doesn't benefit from local-currency devaluation; when EBITDA falls, interest coverage ratios compress faster than EBITDA margin, amplifying EPS impact

§8 — Macro FX Outlook (N12-24M)

J-front verdict: N12-24M macro FX outlook for the African currency basket vs USD is cautiously neutral-to-modestly constructive vs FY24 trough, but NGN path remains the dominant swing factor and is highly conditional on Nigerian oil revenue and CBN policy

  • USD outlook (N12-24M):
  • Consensus view as of mid-2025: USD modestly weaker from 2024 peaks as Fed rate cuts progress; DXY expected to drift lower on improving non-US growth and narrowing rate differentials
  • Range of forecasts: DXY 95-107 (broad range reflecting policy uncertainty); base case 100-104
  • USD weakness, if sustained, is a marginal tailwind for all African currencies vs USD — translates directly to AAF translation improvement

  • Tail risk: US fiscal expansion, re-acceleration of inflation, or geopolitical risk-off (tariffs, trade war escalation) could drive DXY back to 108-110, adverse for EM Africa

  • NGN/USD outlook (N12-24M):

  • NGN has been operating in 1,550-1,750/USD range through early-mid 2025 (post-liberalisation, post-double-devaluation)
  • Positive factors: CBN rebuilding FX reserves (oil revenues + diaspora remittances + IMF/World Bank engagement); Dangote refinery progressively reducing Nigeria's petrol import bill (FX savings); Nigerian inflation declining from 28-32% peaks
  • Negative factors: Nigeria's fiscal position remains stressed; oil production below OPEC quota consistently; political pressure on CBN to keep NGN stable (currency populism); any oil price correction below $70/bbl would stress NGN
  • Consensus view: NGN stable-to-modest appreciation from current levels N12M; N24M more uncertain — range $1,300-2,000/USD

  • For AAF thesis: Stabilisation at current levels is a significant improvement vs FY24 devaluation trajectory; modest appreciation would be a meaningful reported earnings tailwind

  • KES/USD outlook (N12-24M):

  • KES recovered strongly from 165/USD (early 2024 peak stress) to 125-130/USD range by end-2024 driven by Eurobond repayment (clearing FX overhang), diaspora remittances, and tourism recovery
  • Positive factors: IMF programme active; fiscal consolidation; KES carry attractive for EM investors
  • Negative factors: East Africa geopolitical risk (Sudan, DRC); climate/drought impact on agricultural FX earnings; political noise

  • Consensus: KES stable-to-modest appreciation N12M; KES was actually an AAF earnings TAILWIND in FY25 after being a headwind in FY24

  • CFA franc (XAF/XOF) — EUR-linked:

  • EUR/USD consensus N12-24M: EUR in 1.05-1.15 range; mild EUR appreciation from current levels likely as ECB/Fed rate differential narrows
  • EUR appreciation vs USD → XAF/XOF appreciation vs USD → translation tailwind for CFA zone revenue

  • CFA zone is the stable anchor of the AAF portfolio — EUR peg provides the most predictable FX dynamic of any major AAF currency; EUR/USD range outcomes are well-bounded vs NGN scenarios

  • UGX/TZS/ZMW/MWK outlook:

  • UGX: chronic gradual depreciation trend (~5-7% per year); no acute devaluation risk absent political shock; Ugandan economy growing steadily
  • TZS: similar gradual depreciation; Tanzanian FX management historically orderly
  • ZMW: post-debt-restructuring, Zambia's FX is recovering; IMF programme in place; ZMW has appreciated from crisis lows; modest tailwind potential
  • MWK: Malawian Kwacha remains structurally challenged; IMF/World Bank programme constraints; continued devaluation pressure; no near-term recovery expected

  • ETB: Ethiopia continues to have FX access restrictions and managed-depreciation regime; gradual liberalisation ongoing; remains an operational execution risk for AAF Ethiopia

  • US rates declining; African central bank rates broadly declining more slowly (inflation still elevated in several markets); rate differentials slightly narrowing in USD's favour but EM African rates remain high in absolute terms

  • Carry differential alone does not drive EM African FX (driven more by current account, oil, and political stability) but does affect hot-money flows at the margin

  • Tail risks N12-24M:

  • China slowdown → commodity price correction → Nigeria/Zambia/DRC FX stress → second round of African currency weakness
  • US tariff escalation → global risk-off → DXY spike → all EM Africa currencies weaken simultaneously
  • Nigerian political instability / security deterioration → NGN risk premium spikes
  • French sovereign stress → CFA peg credibility questioned (tail, but material for ~17-20% of AAF revenue)

  • El Niño / climate shock → East African agricultural FX earnings drop → KES/UGX pressure

§9 — FX as Tailwind/Headwind for Thesis + Sceptical Synthesis

J-front verdict: FX is a structural overhang on the AAF thesis — a significant headwind L3Y that has masked strong local-currency operating performance; base case N12-24M is transitioning from headwind to approximately neutral with optionality for modest tailwind; the thesis is viable but requires explicit acceptance that USD-reported EPS is the wrong metric to anchor on

  • Combining §1-§8 — net FX diagnosis:
  • The AAF thesis is fundamentally a local-currency growth story: mobile penetration expansion, ARPU growth, Airtel Money monetisation, 4G network rollout, all driven by GDP growth in sub-Saharan Africa at 4-6% real
  • The FX architecture translates this compelling local-currency story into a volatile, frequently-compressed USD-reported P&L
  • L3Y: local-currency revenue grew ~18-22% per annum; reported USD revenue grew ~0-5% per annum after translation drag; the gap is not signal — it's noise from translation

  • FX has not changed the underlying economic value of the franchise; it has damaged the reported EPS trajectory and depressed the share price — which is actually opportunity for a long-only investor who correctly models the gap

  • Thesis FX-dependence quantification:

  • If NGN depreciates 20% further from current levels and KES weakens 10%: group EBITDA impact approximately -11 to -14%; EPS impact approximately -16 to -20%
  • If NGN stabilises and KES holds: translation drag narrows to ~5-8pp vs constant-currency growth; reported USD EBITDA growth approximately +12-15% on constant-currency +18-22%
  • If NGN appreciates 10-15% (oil revenue improvement scenario): reported USD EBITDA growth could reach +20-25% — significant outperformance vs consensus which anchors to reported USD

  • EPS trajectory is highly sensitive to NGN: a ±20% NGN swing changes EPS by approximately ±8-10% in reported USD terms

  • Thesis stress test — FX bear case:

  • Scenario: NGN re-tests 2,000/USD (further ~25% depreciation); KES reverts to 160/USD (23% depreciation from current); DXY back to 108
  • Impact: Group EBITDA in USD terms declines 15-20% despite continued local-currency growth; EPS in USD declines 22-28%
  • Share price reaction: AAF trades on 5-7x EV/EBITDA; a 20% EBITDA decline implies ~20-28% share price decline — thesis is materially impaired in this scenario

  • This bear case is not extreme — it is a partial repeat of FY24; the question is probability, not plausibility

  • Thesis stress test — FX bull case:

  • Scenario: NGN recovers to 1,300/USD (15% appreciation); KES stays at 128/USD; EUR/USD moves to 1.12
  • Impact: Group EBITDA in USD terms grows 22-27% as translation drag becomes tailwind; EPS grows 30-35%; multiple re-rates
  • This scenario is not in consensus models — analysts model NGN stability, not appreciation

  • The asymmetric upside from an unhedged FX recovery is itself a thesis argument for holding AAF — the portfolio is long African FX recovery options embedded in a telecom

  • Local-currency anchor — the correct mental model:

  • Richard should model AAF on local-currency constant-currency EBITDA growth as the primary metric; layer USD translation scenarios as the volatility overlay
  • Local-currency EBITDA growth is fundamentally driven by volume (subscriber growth, data usage), pricing (ARPU inflation), and cost discipline — all of which have been positive L3Y
  • USD translation is a volatility layer, not the signal

  • The risk for the thesis is if USD reporting volatility damages investor confidence or dividend capacity before the local-currency flywheel generates sufficient USD cash flow for Holdco debt service

  • FX impact on the 25%+ IRR requirement:

  • 25%+ IRR over 18-36 months requires a share price that correctly values the local-currency growth trajectory
  • Current valuation (AAF trading at compressed multiples vs pre-FY24 levels) embeds a significant FX headwind premium that reverses if NGN stabilises
  • FX recovery from FY24 lows to neutral adds approximately 15-20% to implied DCF NAV from pure translation reversal

  • FX is therefore an embedded option in the thesis: buy at FY24 trough valuation, benefit from partial translation recovery + local-currency growth compounding

  • Single largest FX risk to thesis:

  • Nigeria FX re-restriction — not devaluation per se, but reversion to dual-rate/FX control system that traps dividends at OpCo level and prevents Holdco USD cash generation
  • This scenario is more dangerous than simple devaluation because it combines translation loss + transaction blockage + potential covenant stress simultaneously

  • Probability assessment: individually each component is 15-25% probability; combined ~5-8% in any 12-month window, but rising in a global risk-off scenario

  • Confidence in FX-resilience of thesis: medium plausibility — the thesis survives a steady-state FX environment and benefits materially from FX recovery, but is fragile to a repeat of the FY24 scenario, which is a plausible not a remote outcome given Nigeria's structural FX volatility

❌ Hedging programme is insufficient to protect against the scale and duration of real-world FX shocks: Estimated 15-30% transaction hedge ratio on 3-9M duration provided approximately 5-10% offset against the FY24 FX impact; the gap between disclosed hedging policy and realised protection is large; management's ±10% sensitivity framing is a material understatement of the plausible range of African currency moves [Cross-ref: §5 + §6]
