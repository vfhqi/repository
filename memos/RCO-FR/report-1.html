§1 Business Overview

Verdict: Rémy Cointreau is an ultra-premium spirits house that is, at its core, a one-category, two-market business: Cognac sold into China and the US. The Liqueurs & Spirits division (Cointreau, Bruichladdich, Mount Gay, The Botanist) provides diversification in label but not in scale.

Rémy Cointreau SA was formed in 1990 through the merger of the Hériard Dubreuil family's E. Rémy Martin & Cie SA and the Cointreau family's Cointreau & Cie SA. The group is listed on Euronext Paris with the families retaining 57.5% of capital and over 70% of voting rights.

  • Brand portfolio: [#I] Core brands are: Rémy Martin (VSOP, XO, Centaure, Club) and Louis XIII in Cognac; Cointreau liqueur; Bruichladdich and Port Charlotte single malts (Islay); The Botanist gin; Mount Gay rum (Barbados); Metaxa (Greek brandy-liqueur); St-Rémy brandy; Champagne Telmont (prestige Champagne). New Prestige Division (April 2026) groups Louis XIII, Telmont, Maison Psyché.
  • Revenue composition (FY2025): [#I] Cognac division ~62% of consolidated revenue (€611m); Liqueurs & Spirits ~38% (€374m); Cognac generates ~87% of group COP. ❌ Concentration in a single category exposed to simultaneous China + US headwinds is structurally high risk.
  • Geographic segmentation: [#I] RCO reports three geographic regions: Americas, APAC, EMEA. China sits within APAC; the US within Americas. China and the US together represent ~70% of Cognac division sales — an unusually concentrated exposure relative to Diageo or Pernod Ricard. EMEA is smaller and structurally lower-margin.
  • Cognac dominance by geography: [#A] Within Cognac: China historically the largest single market (estimated 25-35% of Cognac revenues at peak); US second (estimated 30-35% of Cognac revenues); Europe/ROW the balance. Both declined sharply in FY2023-25.
  • Liqueurs & Spirits geographic mix: [#I] Americas (48%) and EMEA (39%) dominate; APAC only 13%. Cointreau is the anchor brand — a global liqueur franchise, less China-dependent than Cognac. This partially cushions group-level China exposure.
  • Strategic positioning: [#A] Management's stated long-run objective is "purely exceptional" — prioritising the ultra-premium and super-premium tier over volume. Louis XIII as the pinnacle asset; Rémy Martin repositioned upward toward XO and above. Prestige Division creation (April 2026) formalises this.

Sceptical challenge: The "ultra-premium" strategic positioning is the right long-run answer but provides limited near-term earnings protection when China's anti-corruption campaign, economic slowdown, and consumer confidence collapse simultaneously hit the highest-price-point products hardest. Premium resilience is a bull-market phenomenon.

§2 Financial Profile

Verdict: Revenue has fallen more than 35% from the FY2023 peak in just two years; COP has compressed from ~28% margin to 22%; balance sheet leverage has nearly doubled to approaching 3x. This is a company in genuine financial distress at the operating level, not merely a cyclical dip.

  • Revenue trajectory L4Y: [#I] FY2022 (peak cycle): ~€1.50bn; FY2023: ~€1.45bn; FY2024: €1.19bn (-18.0% organic); FY2025: €984.6m (-18.0% organic); FY2026: €935m (+0.2% organic). Cumulative revenue decline from peak ~37%. ❌ Three-year contraction of this magnitude is peer group worst — Pernod Ricard FY2025 organic -3%, Diageo organic +1.7%.
  • Gross margin: [#A] FY2022 peak gross margin ~72%+; FY2024: 71.2%; FY2025: 70.6%. Resilient relative to volume loss — reflects price discipline and brand-mix management. Peers: Diageo gross margin c.60%, Pernod Ricard c.58%. RCO's gross margin premium to peers reflects ultra-premium positioning.
  • Current Operating Profit margin: [#A] FY2022 (peak): ~25.5%; FY2023: c.28.3% (organic peak); FY2024: €312m / 26.2% margin; FY2025: €217m / 22.0% margin. FY2026 guidance: COP organic decline low-double-digits to mid-teens. If guidance midpoint is -12% organic, FY2026 COP ~€190m at ~20.3% margin.
  • Operating leverage observation: [#A] Revenue fell 17.5% as reported in FY2025; COP fell 30.5% organic — showing high negative operating leverage. The converse (recovery leverage) is the core bull thesis: moderate volume recovery should produce outsized COP uplift given fixed cost structure of cognac production.
  • EPS trajectory: [#I] EPS Group share: FY2025 €1.22 (down -32.6% as reported). From the FY2022 peak EPS of ~€8+ (estimated), current trough EPS represents an ~85% contraction. Consensus expects further decline in FY2026 before stabilisation.
  • Balance sheet: [#A] Net debt: €675m at March-2025; €687m at Sept-2025. Net debt/EBITDA: 1.68x March-2024 → 2.40x March-2025 → 2.96x Sept-2025. ❌ Covenant risk is real if EBITDA falls below €230m threshold (covenant likely set at 3.0-3.5x). ROCE: 10.3% FY2025 vs sector median ~15-18% (Diageo ~20%+).
  • Inventory: [#I] RCO holds c.$2.1-2.4bn in ageing inventory — nearly double annual revenue and approaching market cap. This is structural (cognac requires multi-year/multi-decade ageing) but creates working capital drag and impairment risk if long-run demand weakens.
  • Dividend: [#I] Dividend maintained but shifted to scrip to conserve cash — a financial stress signal. Free cash flow deeply negative in FY2023-24; working capital optimisation improved FCF in FY2025 despite profit decline.
  • Cost programme: [#A] €85m cost savings achieved in FY2025 vs €50m originally targeted — management executing ahead of plan on the cost side. RC Forward plan (April 2026) targets further structural cost reduction; quantum TBD until November 2026 medium-term targets.

Sceptical challenge: The cost savings narrative is real but the maths is unfavourable: €85m of cost cuts cannot offset €500m+ of revenue contraction. The financial recovery is entirely contingent on China volume returning — cost-cutting is optionality preservation, not the thesis itself.

§3 Cognac Category Dynamics

Verdict: The global Cognac category is in a structural cyclical trough driven by simultaneous demand shocks in its two anchor markets; the category is not dying but the recovery timeline is measured in years, not quarters. The top-4 producers (Hennessy, Martell, Courvoisier, Rémy Martin) control ~80% of a structurally attractive premium category — oligopoly economics are intact.

  • Market structure: [#I] Global Cognac production is geographically locked to the Cognac appellation in Charente, France — a genuine regulatory moat. The Bureau National Interprofessionnel du Cognac controls volumes. Top-4 brands (Hennessy, Martell, Rémy Martin, Courvoisier) control ~80% of the global market. ⚡ RARE: This is one of very few spirits categories with a genuine geographic production monopoly combined with oligopolistic brand structure — pricing power is structurally embedded.
  • Volume decline context: [#I] Cognac exports broadly declined c.15-25% by volume 2023-25 driven by China + US dual shock. BNIC data showed double-digit export declines. This is a category-wide problem, not RCO-specific — but RCO's ultra-premium positioning makes it more China-dependent and thus more exposed.
  • US market dynamics: [#I] US historically the largest Cognac market by volume. Distributor over-stocking post-2021 boom led to aggressive destocking 2023-25. African-American consumer base (core Cognac demographic in US) showed consumption slowdown. GLP-1 and "sober curious" trends add secular pressure.
  • China market dynamics: [#A] China was the fastest-growing Cognac market pre-2023. Anti-corruption campaign (Xi Jinping), property market crisis, consumer confidence collapse, and trade tensions converged into a structural demand shock. China's anti-dumping tariff (July 2025: 32.2% on EU brandy) compounds this — though major houses secured price-floor exemptions.
  • GLP-1 headwind: [#A] Weight-loss medications (Ozempic, Wegovy) are identified as a sector-level headwind — reducing appetite for alcohol broadly; concentrated premium spirits may be more insulated (occasion-driven, not habitual) than mass-market beer or wine. Directionally negative for all spirits; magnitude in cognac specifically is uncertain but likely modest.
  • Tariff environment: [#I] US tariffs (Trump 2025): 30% on EU goods imposed; EU retaliatory measures potential. China anti-dumping duties (July 2025): 34.9% tariff for non-exempt producers; exemption for Hennessy, Martell, Rémy Martin via minimum price commitments. Net effect: cognac category faces simultaneously elevated trade barriers in both primary markets.
  • Competitive dynamics: [#A] Hennessy is 6.9m cases/year and uses its scale for distribution dominance. Martell (Pernod Ricard) and Rémy Martin are the tier-2 premium players. Courvoisier (Beam Suntory / Sazarac) is mass-premium. Rémy's differentiation is the super-premium/ultra-premium positioning. Competitive intensity at VS/VSOP tier is high; at XO and above, RCO has more defensible positioning.

Sceptical challenge: The Cognac category is structurally attractive but near-term dynamics are uniformly negative: trade barriers, consumer trade-down, inventory overhang, GLP-1, and Chinese consumer caution are all pointing the wrong direction simultaneously. "Premium resilience" is a narrative that requires a more stable macro-geopolitical backdrop than currently exists.

§4 China Exposure Deep-Dive

Verdict: China is the existential variable for RCO. The best-case is a slow grind recovery (2-3 years) driven by improving consumer confidence and Chinese New Year demand cycles; the worst-case is structurally lower volumes permanently, in a market where minimum price floors now constrain competitive positioning. The Q4 FY2026 data point is early and base-effect-driven, not a confirmed trend.

  • Revenue exposure: [#A] China and the US together account for ~70% of Cognac revenues (company guidance). China alone is estimated at 25-35% of Cognac revenues historically — equating to roughly 15-22% of total group revenues at peak. After three years of decline, China's absolute revenue contribution has fallen substantially — but it remains the primary recovery swing factor.
  • Consumer profile: [#I] Chinese cognac consumer: predominantly male, urban, 30-55 years old; consumption occasion = business gifting, banqueting, KTV entertainment. Anti-corruption campaign directly targeted these occasions. The shift toward female consumers (now 40% of China clientele, +2pp) is positive but insufficient to offset banqueting demand loss.
  • 2025 tariff context: [#A] China imposed provisional anti-dumping tariffs of 38.1% on cognac imports (October 2024), made permanent at 32.2-34.9% (July 2025) for 5 years. LVMH (Hennessy), Pernod Ricard (Martell), and RCO (Rémy Martin) secured exemptions contingent on minimum price floors. The minimum price commitment (reported: CNY46/L for VS to CNY613/L for XXO) effectively mandates premium positioning — a tailwind for brand premium but a constraint on volume recovery via price competition.
  • Recovery signals: [#A] Q4 FY2025 (Jan-March 2025): steep recovery noted (vs very low base); FY2026 Q4 (Jan-March 2026): Cognac +15.5% organic — described as "very favourable comparison base" driven by Chinese New Year demand. H1 FY2026 (Apr-Sept 2025): APAC region still down on full-period basis; China continued to slide. Q3 FY2026: improving. The Q4 positive is real but seasonally concentrated and not yet evidence of durable consumer demand recovery.
  • Recovery timeline: [#A] Consumer confidence recovery in China is correlated with property market stabilisation, macro-confidence, and resumption of business entertaining. Management and sector analysts broadly indicate 2-3 year normalisation timeline. No catalyst for rapid acceleration is currently visible.
  • Cultural risk: [#I] Chinese consumers are showing increased preference for domestic products (baijiu resurgence), value-for-money consciousness, and a cultural shift toward local luxury vs Western luxury. This structural trend is a genuine long-run risk beyond the cyclical destocking narrative.
  • Positive counterpoint: [#A] Growing female consumer base in China (40% of clientele); younger Chinese consumers are cognac-curious in gifting context; minimum price exemption reinforces premium positioning; RCO is culturally embedding (local partnerships, Chinese New Year limited editions). Louis XIII is a trophy asset that may actually benefit from luxury demand recovery as Chinese consumers re-engage with global prestige goods.

Sceptical challenge: The Q4 2026 China rebound is base-effect-driven (comparing against a severely depressed Q4 2025) and CNY-seasonal. A single quarter of 15.5% growth does not constitute "recovery" when cumulative losses over three years are 40%+. Declaring the cycle over is premature.

§5 US Market Dynamics

Verdict: The US destocking cycle is substantially complete — this is a genuine positive turning point — but the market then faces a new structural headwind from Trump tariffs (30% on EU goods). The US becomes a lower-growth market than consensus hoped 18 months ago.

  • Destocking cycle: [#A] US wholesale channel suffered aggressive over-stocking post-2021 lockdown rebound. Distributors cut orders 2022-25. RCO's Americas revenue fell 20.2% in FY2025 after a prior year decline. Management guided that destocking in the Americas would be finalised by early FY2026 — consistent with Q4 2025 sequential improvement signals.
  • Consumer consumption: [#I] Core Cognac demographic in the US (African-American consumers in southern states, urban markets) showed genuine consumption softening in 2024-25, not purely channel destocking. This is the harder-to-reverse element — consumption behaviours vs inventory position.
  • Tariff impact: [#A] Trump's 30% tariff on EU goods: RCO estimated initial gross COP impact of €35-40m; net impact guided at €20m for FY2026 after pricing and operational mitigations. The company has raised US prices to partially pass on tariff costs — volume response to price increases is being watched closely. 90-day tariff pause (April-July 2025) provided temporary relief.
  • Strategic response: [#A] RCO is increasing US marketing spend, improving route-to-market efficiency, and considering local bottling/blending options to reduce tariff exposure. The Americas saw sequential improvement in Q3-Q4 FY2026 — ahead of consensus.
  • Competitive dynamics in US: [#I] Hennessy's scale and distribution infrastructure give it an advantage in navigating tariff costs vs smaller producers. Rémy Martin's US share (~15-20% of Cognac market) provides some cushion but less flexibility than Hennessy to absorb costs.
  • GLP-1 impact on US: [#A] GLP-1 is cited as a structural spirits demand headwind in the US — reducing calorie-conscious consumers' alcohol intake. Premium/ultra-premium occasion-driven cognac is likely more insulated than regular spirits; the threat is real but probably not primary determinant.

Sceptical challenge: The destocking resolution narrative is real but the tailwind was already being partially priced in 2024-25. The tariff headwind is new and ongoing. Net, the US story has moved from "destocking ending = recovery" to "destocking ended + tariffs = neutral at best." That is a downgrade to the bull case.

§6 Pricing Power and Destocking

Verdict: Rémy Cointreau has demonstrated material pricing power (gross margin held at 70-71% through a 37% revenue decline) but has simultaneously experienced devastating volume destruction — the two cannot be presented as equally positive. The volumes are what pays the fixed costs.

  • Price increases taken: [#A] April 2023: RCO implemented list price increases across Rémy Martin VSOP and XO ranges. Gross margin held at 71.2% in FY2024 and 70.6% in FY2025 despite heavy volume declines — indicating real pricing power was exercised and partially offset production cost inflation. Peers: Diageo operating margin 28.2% (stable); Pernod Ricard operating margin expanding +64bps even in down year — suggesting RCO's pricing maintained but not built advantage.
  • Volume destruction: [#A] FY2024: Cognac volume -29.7%, price-mix +4.6%. FY2025: Americas -20.2%; APAC -18.2%; EMEA -13.8%. Combined effect: significant volume contraction with price partially offsetting. This is the classic premiumisation trade-off — "sell less, at higher margin, but fixed costs don't bend."
  • Destocking vs consumption decline: [#I] Critical distinction: in the Americas, destocking (channel inventory normalisation) was the primary driver of revenue decline; actual consumption at consumer level was less negative. In China, actual consumer demand collapsed — destocking and consumption decline compounded. Americas = cyclical; China = partially structural.
  • Normalisation timing: [#A] Americas destocking: substantially complete by Q1 FY2027 (April-June 2026) based on management signals and sequential sales data. China consumption: no firm guidance; management references "gradual recovery" without specific timeline.
  • Volume normalisation path: [#A] If China volumes recover to 70% of peak over 2 years and US volumes return to flat, consensus models suggest group revenue could return to €1.1-1.2bn range by FY2028. That implies COP margin recovery to 24-26% range and significant EPS leverage — this is the core bull case.
  • Price ceiling risk: [#I] China minimum price floor (government-mandated) could constrain volume recovery at entry-level price points (VS, VSOP). If Chinese consumers are price-sensitive in current environment, higher minimum prices = lower volumes — a paradox embedded in the tariff exemption structure.

Sceptical challenge: Pricing power without volume growth is not a sustainable business model — it is margin maintenance in decline. The narrative that "gross margin held" misses the point that COP fell 30%+ and the fixed cost absorption is deteriorating. Volume must recover for the investment thesis to work.

§7 Ultra-Premium Strategy — Louis XIII and Scarcity Economics

Verdict: Louis XIII is one of the most compelling scarcity assets in global luxury spirits — genuinely uncopyable, multi-generational ageing process, pricing effectively uncapped. This is the crown jewel that gives RCO its strategic identity. It is, however, a small-volume business that cannot be the earnings recovery vehicle.

  • Louis XIII product characteristics: [#A] Louis XIII consists of 1,200+ eaux-de-vie aged 40-100 years, sourced exclusively from Grande Champagne (top Cognac cru). Each decanter (750ml): c.€3,000-4,600 retail. Production is structurally inelastic — you cannot create new 100-year-old eaux-de-vie. Supply cannot meaningfully increase over any relevant investment horizon.
  • Scarcity economics: ⚡ RARE: Louis XIII exhibits genuine SRCA (Sustainable Resource Advantage) via the combination of geographic appellation lock-in, century-scale ageing requirement, and brand heritage — this combination is unreplicable by any competitor. No other spirits category has comparable supply-side hard barriers. [#I] Priced at multiple of XO (~€100-200); multiple of VSOP (~€40-60). Each price increase is permanent in market perception.
  • Flywheel assessment: [#A] Louis XIII fits the flywheel model: scarcity reinforces prestige → prestige enables further price increases → higher prices fund ageing inventory investment → ageing inventory deepens scarcity. This is the compound interest of luxury. However, the flywheel depends on aspirational demand being active — currently muted in China (primary market). [#I] Louis XIII is also growing as a lifestyle brand — porcelain plates launch (Sept 2025), Prestige Division branding — expanding beyond liquid into luxury goods.
  • Rémy Martin VSOP/XO tier: [#A] Rémy Martin VSOP and XO are high-quality premium cognacs but compete in the €40-200 range where Hennessy also plays. Brand preference in this tier is more contestable. RCO's strategy to push mix toward XO and above is correct directionally — but it depresses volume by design. [#I] Rémy Martin Club and Centaure age statements sit between VSOP and XO — part of the premiumisation ladder.
  • Prestige Division (April 2026): [#A] New organisational structure groups Louis XIII, Champagne Telmont (acquired 2020), and Maison Psyché (new perfume house). This is a deliberate signal that RCO is repositioning beyond cognac into a broader ultra-luxury house — reducing category concentration over the long term. Telmont Champagne is tiny but growing.
  • Capital allocation toward prestige: [#I] CAPEX and A&P investment increasingly concentrated on Louis XIII vs VSOP tier. Strategy is explicit: RCO wants to be a premium/luxury portfolio, not a volume spirits player. This is strategically coherent but means the recovery from current trough relies on Rémy Martin mid-tier volumes recovering (which is the more contested tier), not just Louis XIII growth.

Sceptical challenge: Louis XIII is an exceptional asset but accounts for a small fraction of total Cognac volumes. The investment thesis for RCO is not primarily a Louis XIII thesis — it is a Rémy Martin VSOP/XO thesis. And VSOP/XO recovery requires China and US consumer demand returning to a category that is simultaneously facing tariffs, cultural headwinds, and GLP-1. Louis XIII is the margin crown jewel, not the earnings engine.

§8 Management and Governance

Verdict: The family ownership structure provides strategic permanence and long-termism that is genuinely differentiating — no activist pressure, no short-termism. The CEO transition is a risk but Marilly's RC Forward plan signals strategic clarity. Execution track record under Vallat (who rode the boom and the bust) was mixed on the downturn management.

  • Family control: [#A] Hériard Dubreuil and Cointreau families collectively hold 57.5% of capital and >70% of voting rights via Orpar and Récopart holding structures. This is a locked controlling shareholder with a generational investment horizon. No realistic prospect of hostile takeover or activist intervention. ⚡ RARE: Double-voting rights (10+ year holdings) further entrench family control — creates governance stability unusual in European mid-cap spirits.
  • Chairwoman: [#I] Marie-Amélie de Leusse (Hériard Dubreuil family) serves as Chairwoman. Chair/CEO roles separated since 2004 — governance structure is formally appropriate. Board includes independent members (Sonia Bonnet-Bernard chairs Audit; Bruno Pavlovsky chairs Nomination/Remuneration).
  • CEO transition: [#A] Eric Vallat (CEO 2020-2025) oversaw the pandemic recovery peak and the subsequent demand collapse. He resigned effective summer 2025. Franck Marilly appointed CEO June 2025 — 30+ years in luxury/FMCG (Chanel, Shiseido, Unilever). Marilly's background in luxury brand management, operational execution, and global market development is well-suited to the RC Forward agenda. No spirits-specific operational experience is a risk.
  • RC Forward plan (April 2026): [#A] Five levers: (1) strengthen distribution network/route-to-market; (2) Revenue Growth Management — pricing, formats, promotions; (3) A&P investment reallocation toward higher-desirability markets; (4) global procurement optimisation; (5) organisational simplification. Three-year horizon. Medium-term financial targets deferred to November 2026. New structure: Luca Marotta (Deputy CEO), Ian McLernon (Group Chief Markets Officer, new Emerging Markets division), Mélanie Bulourde (COO/CSR), Clarisse Petit .
  • Capital allocation history: [#I] RCO has historically prioritised brand investment and inventory ageing over shareholder returns — appropriate for an ultra-premium house. Dividend maintained but pivoted to scrip. Acquisitions: Bruichladdich (2012), The Botanist, Mount Gay, Champagne Telmont (2020) — portfolio building at premium prices during the good years. No large M&A signalled currently.
  • Governance concern: [#I] Family control prevents the activist pressure that sometimes forces necessary restructuring. The risk is strategic inertia or delayed action in a downturn — management cut guidance multiple times in 2024-25 rather than resetting expectations early. The new CEO and RC Forward plan suggest the board has acknowledged the need for a different gear.

Sceptical challenge: Family ownership is strategically stabilising but also means management accountability is lower than in widely-held peers. Vallat's departure came after multiple guidance cuts and years of declining financial performance — a public company without family protection might have accelerated leadership change sooner. The new CEO has never run a spirits company.

§9 Key Risks

Verdict: RCO has a risk stack that is unusually concentrated — two markets, one category, elevated leverage — in a macro environment where multiple risks are materialising simultaneously rather than diversifiably.

  • China concentration: [#J] Single-country demand dependency is the primary structural risk. [#A] ~70% of Cognac (the 87% profit division) exposed to China + US; China alone estimated 25-35% of Cognac revenues. [#I] China consumer confidence, anti-corruption campaign, baijiu competition, and tariff minimum price floors are all negative variables. ❌ No meaningful geographic diversification of Cognac revenues — this is structurally embedded.
  • US tariff escalation: [#A] Trump 30% tariff on EU goods: net €20-35m COP impact in FY2026. Risk: tariffs escalate to 50% (threatened); or Trump tariff pause expires without agreement. 50% tariffs would double current impact to c.€40-70m — potentially de-rating the US market materially for a 2-3 year period.
  • Balance sheet/covenant risk: [#A] Net debt/EBITDA at 2.96x Sept-2025; will rise further if FY2027 COP declines another 10-15% as guided. If covenants set at 3.5x, buffer is thin. Bank waiver risk if EBITDA does not recover. Dividend scrip conversion reduces cash burn but signals financial constraint.
  • Cognac category cyclicality: [#I] Cognac has historically experienced deep multi-year demand cycles (1970s US bust, 1990s Japan bust, post-2008 weakness). The current China+US simultaneous bust is the most severe combined demand shock in the modern era of the category. Cycle duration is uncertain — historical precedent suggests 3-5 year recovery periods.
  • Currency headwinds: [#A] EUR appreciation vs USD is a persistent headwind for a euro-cost, USD-revenue business (Americas ~48% of L&S revenues, significant Cognac revenues). FY2026 currency impact on COP: -€25-30m guided. Dollar weakness under Trump trade policy could persist.
