Helios avoids Nigeria but DRC and Tanzania have residual local-currency exposure.

The bear concern is that this benefit is partially embedded in guidance already, and any operational setbacks in remote African deployments will compress margins without a quick fix.

This disconnect between strong fundamentals and weak share price reaction is a key signal: either the market is pricing in macro/geopolitical risk (oil price volatility, Middle East tensions cited in March 2026 reporting), or there is an embedded valuation discount for Africa EM infrastructure that no amount of operational delivery fully closes.

  10. The strongest bear scenario is a macro re-run of 2022: rate spike + acquisition misstep + FX pressure simultaneously. This combination drove the stock from 180p to 70p and could do so again. Individual risks are manageable; convergence risk is material.

Executive Summary

Helios Towers does not have a formal short-seller report. The bear case on HTWS is structural and macro-driven, not accounting or fraud-driven. Bears are concerned about: (1) a history of leverage mis-management, (2) the fundamental valuation discount attached to EM-Africa infrastructure, (3) political and currency tail risks across eight fragile markets, (4) the possibility that tenancy ratio expansion is slower and less valuable than bulls assume, and (5) the risk that any renewed capital deployment re-opens the leverage trap that defined 2022-2023.

The 2022-2023 collapse is the bears' strongest empirical evidence. Helios entered a $557m Oman acquisition at the peak of the global rate cycle, leverage reached ~4.8x at H1 2023, FCF conversion fell to 56% (from 96% in 2021), and the stock lost more than 60% of its value. This was not a fraud; it was a capital allocation error of the first order, made worse by external conditions management could not control. The pattern is repeatable.

The subsequent recovery — leverage to 3.4x, credit ratings to Ba3/BB-, FCF turned positive, first dividend announced — has been impressive and broadly in line with management's revised guidance. All seven covering analysts are bullish. Short interest has essentially evaporated. On a pure fundamental basis, with EV/EBITDA of ~5x and recurring FCF yield approaching double digits on 2026 estimates, the stock looks cheap relative to its Western tower company peers (Crown Castle, SBA Communications) trading at 15-20x EBITDA.

But the discount is not irrational. Africa EM infrastructure commands a structural discount for valid reasons: sovereign/political risk in DRC, Tanzania, Congo Brazzaville, Ghana; currency convertibility risk; counterparty risk (even investment-grade MNOs in frontier markets carry more credit risk than their Western equivalents); regulatory risk (tower regulation in some African markets is nascent and evolving); and most importantly, the demonstrated willingness of management to deploy capital aggressively in conditions that turned hostile.

The bear credibility rating is ORANGE: the structural concerns are real and the historical precedent is severe, but the immediate-term risk has materially declined following the deleveraging cycle and credit upgrades. Bears who pressed the trade in 2022 were right; bears who press it today at 3.4x leverage need a specific catalyst (macro shock, M&A relapse, or FX crisis in key markets) to be proven right.

4. Accounting & Governance Red Flags

Revenue recognition:
- Take-or-pay lease structure means MNOs pay whether or not they use tower capacity. This is structurally low risk for revenue recognition.
- Long-term contracted revenue backlog of US$3.7 billion reported, with 99% from multinational MNOs, average lease remaining 7.6 years.
- No identified customer disputes or material non-payment events in public filings.
- Risk to note: in stressed market conditions (e.g., if a regional MNO faces insolvency or contract renegotiation), take-or-pay clauses have been contested in some African markets historically. No HTWS-specific instance identified.

Non-GAAP adjustments:
- The company uses Adjusted EBITDA as its primary performance metric. This excludes depreciation and amortisation, share-based compensation, restructuring costs, and other items.
- A specific public challenge emerged from Morningstar's Q&A database: a shareholder formally asked in 2023 why FCF conversion declined from 96% of Adjusted Operating Income in 2021 to only 56% in 2022. Management's answer: the divergence was primarily caused by the scale of acquisition capex ($557m in 2022 alone). This is a legitimate but important distinction — the Adjusted EBITDA metric did not capture the true cash cost of the acquisition-led expansion phase.
- Adjusted EBITDA margin: 50.4% in FY2022 (down from 53.6% in FY2021), recovering to higher levels by FY2024 as tenancy ratios improved. The margin compression during the acquisition phase is a bear data point — the business is more cost-intensive at low tenancy than the EBITDA headline suggests.

Debt covenants and refinancing:
- Net debt history (approximate): ~$1.2bn (2020) → ~$1.9bn (2021, post-Senegal/Madagascar) → ~$2.6bn (2022, post-Oman) → peak leverage ~4.8x ND/EBITDA at H1 2023 → reduction to 3.4x by FY2025.
- Debt structure: HTA Group Ltd (holding company) issued USD bonds. In early 2026, Helios tapped the bond market with a new $500m note issue to refinance existing term facilities (prepaying $445m of term loans). This refinancing reduced financing costs and extended maturity profile.
- Key covenant risk: The company's medium-term target leverage range is 3.5x-4.5x. It is now below this range at 3.4x. Covenants in the bond indenture restrict dividend payments and new acquisition activity above certain leverage thresholds — these are both protective (limits management's ability to re-lever without bondholder consent) and potentially restrictive (limits shareholder returns if leverage pops back above limits).
- Convertible bond: $120m principal repurchased below par in FY2025, eliminating 41 million potentially dilutive shares. This was a sound capital allocation decision but consumed FCF that could otherwise have gone to equity holders.
- Interest rate risk: The debt is USD-denominated. A return to a high-rate environment would significantly increase refinancing costs; the FY2026 refinancing was executed in a relatively benign rate environment.

Currency and NWC:
- 71% of EBITDA in hard currencies. Remaining 29% in local currencies. Key soft-currency exposures: Tanzania (partial), Madagascar, Malawi.
- DRC is dollarised; Senegal and Congo Brazzaville are euro-pegged. These structures provide hard-currency protection.
- Tanzania generates ~30% of its revenues in USD or USD-spot, with similar mixes in Madagascar and Malawi.
- Receivables aging: Not specifically disclosed in public summaries. No material NWC deterioration signalled in filings reviewed.

Governance:
- Board structure: UK Corporate Governance Code 2018 compliance confirmed as of 31 Dec 2023.
- Audit Committee chair: Alison Baker. Members: Richard Byrne, Carole Wainaina.
- External auditor: major international firm (not changed recently per public records).
- Related-party transactions: The company has a historic link to Helios Investment Partners (a Pan-African PE firm with overlapping "Helios" branding but a separate entity). The IPO prospectus disclosed that the original Helios Investment Partners backing represented a governance risk vector at IPO. Post-IPO, Helios Investment Partners has been reducing its ownership over time.
- No identified material governance controversies, no CEO departures linked to misconduct, no regulatory sanctions in any operating market.
- Soros Fund Management was an early investor (reported at IPO in 2019). No current activist shareholder involvement identified.

Legal/regulatory:
- No material legal disputes identified in public filings.
- Tower regulatory regime in some African markets (particularly DRC) is evolving; changes to tower-sharing regulations, spectrum allocation, or foreign ownership rules could affect the business model.
- ESG: 30% emissions reduction target by 2025 referenced; 700,000 litres of diesel saved per quarter via solar conversions. No material regulatory enforcement actions identified.

6. Counter-Arguments to the Bull Case

Bull Thesis 1: Long-term contracted revenues from investment-grade MNOs

Bear rebuttal: "Investment-grade" in an African context does not carry the same weight as an A-rated US telecom. Airtel Africa trades on speculative-grade credit metrics in several of its operating markets. MTN Group has faced regulatory pressure, licence disputes, and FX repatriation difficulties in Nigeria and other markets. Orange and Vodacom are stronger credits but represent smaller shares of HTWS revenues. The "investment-grade" framing is a sales pitch, not a legal protection. If an MNO restructures, abandons a market, or renegotiates contracts under political pressure, the take-or-pay clause provides legal recourse in environments where enforcement can be slow and costly. More pointedly: the contracted revenue backlog of $3.7bn is an undiscounted accounting construct — applying a realistic risk-adjusted discount rate for African sovereign/counterparty risk significantly reduces its NPV.

Bull Thesis 2: Tenancy ratio expansion toward 2.5x+

Bear rebuttal: The tenancy ratio was 2.0x in FY2019 pre-COVID, fell below 2.0x after the 2022 acquisition spree, and only recovered to 2.2x by FY2025. Essentially no net tenancy expansion over six years until recently. The path to 2.5x+ requires: (a) continued MNO network investment in markets where fiscal pressures and high-cost USD debt are squeezing MNO capex budgets; (b) 5G rollout generating incremental tower demand, but 5G in sub-Saharan Africa is nascent and the capex cycle is uncertain; (c) no further acquisitions that dilute the ratio again. IHS Towers, the direct comparable, has a tenancy ratio of only 1.51x despite operating in larger markets — suggesting the African tower market may be structurally slower to add co-locations than management assumes.

Bull Thesis 3: Solar/hybrid power programme reduces diesel opex, drives margin expansion

Bear rebuttal: The programme has delivered savings (~700,000 litres of diesel per quarter) and the FY2024 EBITDA margin did expand by ~2pp. However: (a) this benefit is now partially priced into guidance — the incremental uplift from ongoing conversions is diminishing as the programme matures; (b) solar equipment requires capital investment, maintenance capex in remote African locations, and skilled technicians — the operational complexity is higher than for a simple lease escalation; (c) battery technology degradation in hot climates, theft of solar panels, and supply chain disruptions for components add operational costs that partially offset the diesel savings; (d) diesel savings have a natural ceiling — once all towers are converted, the incremental benefit disappears and the market re-rates the stock on its steady-state economics, not its transition story.

Bull Thesis 4: Deleveraging from 5-6x toward 3x as EBITDA grows

Bear rebuttal: Management has demonstrated exactly once (2021-2022) that it will re-lever aggressively if acquisition opportunities arise. The Oman acquisition at $495m in December 2022 — the largest single deal in company history — was executed at the worst possible time: peak global interest rates, peak leverage. Oman's growth "doesn't look very attractive" per Africa Report analysis, and South Africa (another acquired footprint) has limited organic growth prospects given mobile subscriber growth of ~2.3% CAGR and 4G penetration already exceeding 70%. The bear thesis is not that deleveraging is impossible — it has happened — but that the management team's stated willingness to return to 3.5x-4.5x "medium-term range" is insufficient reassurance against the risk of another capital allocation error. The current 3.4x leverage is below their own stated target range, which implies the company may consider this headroom for new deployment. The bond indenture provides some protection, but motivated management has navigated around bond covenants before.

Bull Thesis 5: Unique pan-African footprint with limited direct competition

Bear rebuttal: The "unique" footprint argument is weaker than it appears: (a) IHS Towers (now being acquired by MTN Group) operates across overlapping African markets; American Tower has a pan-African presence; Indus Towers signalled African expansion in 2025; (b) in each individual market (Tanzania, DRC, Ghana etc.), MNOs can build their own towers — the incentive to sell/leaseback depends on MNO balance sheet pressure; if MNO balance sheets improve, the tower sale-leaseback pipeline shrinks; (c) political risk is not priced into the "moat" narrative — the DRC is a chronically unstable operating environment and regulatory changes (e.g., nationalisation of infrastructure, forced contract renegotiation) are not negligible tail risks; (d) the Oman acquisition (outside sub-Saharan Africa) is a strategic outlier that exposes the company to a very different competitive and regulatory environment with limited synergies with the African core.

Bull Thesis 6: Re-rating as FCF generation improves

Bear rebuttal: The re-rating thesis has been in play since approximately 2022-23 and has not materialised in share price terms. The stock fell 9% on the day of record FY2025 results (March 2026) and is trading at ~100-115p versus analyst targets of 220-250p. Three possible explanations for this persistent discount: (a) the market is right and analysts are wrong — Africa EM infrastructure deserves a structural discount that models consistently underestimate; (b) macro/geopolitical overhang (Middle East tensions cited in March 2026 as the reason for the results-day decline) is temporarily suppressing the stock; (c) there is no natural buyer for mid-cap London-listed Africa infrastructure — institutional investors who would typically hold this have ESG concerns about African political risk, or sector-allocation constraints. The re-rating depends on a catalyst that has not yet arrived: a sustained dividend yield, a take-private bid, or a prolonged period of macro calm. Without a catalyst, the persistent discount suggests the market has a different view of intrinsic value than the bull consensus.

7. Historical Pattern

The 2022-2023 Collapse (~180p to ~70p)

The collapse was multi-causal and sequential:

  1. Leverage trajectory: Net leverage at H1 2021 was ~3.2x. The company then executed four major acquisitions: Senegal (2021), Madagascar (2021), Malawi (2022), and Oman (December 2022). Acquisition capex in FY2021 was $238m; in FY2022 it was $557m. Net leverage rose to 4.1x (Q3 2022), then to 4.8x (H1 2023).

  2. Rate cycle timing: The Federal Reserve began its most aggressive hiking cycle in 40 years in March 2022. Helios Towers, with ~$2.6bn of USD-denominated net debt and a business model dependent on growth capex funded by debt markets, was directly exposed to the repricing of EM risk premiums and the higher cost of new debt.

  3. FCF conversion collapse: In 2022, FCF conversion fell from 96% to 56% of Adjusted Operating Income — a documented and publicly-questioned decline (Morningstar shareholder Q&A archive, 2023). The acquisition of Oman involved not just deal costs but ongoing integration, buildout, and operating costs in a new market with limited track record.

  4. EBITDA margin compression: FY2022 EBITDA margin fell from 53.6% to 50.4% — unusual for a tower company at scale, reflecting the higher cost of running newly acquired, low-tenancy-ratio sites.

  5. Market sentiment: UK small/mid-cap with Africa EM exposure during a global risk-off environment. The stock was caught in a combination of: sector de-rating (infrastructure multiples compressed globally as rates rose), EM discount widening, and company-specific leverage concerns.

No prior activist short attack or formal allegation of misconduct was identified as a driver of the 2022-2023 decline.

Management response to sceptical views

Management has consistently engaged with investor concerns via formal results calls and investor days. The H1 2025 results transcript shows management directly addressing capex guidance misses and leverage trajectory. No material defensive or evasive response pattern identified.

Recovery pattern

Recovery from the ~70p trough to ~115p (approximate late 2025/early 2026) was driven by: (a) pivot to organic growth (no new acquisitions post-Oman); (b) tenancy additions outperforming (FY2025: 2,538 tenancies, a record); (c) FCF turned positive ($18.7m in FY2024, rising to $208m on a recurring basis in FY2025); (d) credit upgrades to Ba3/BB-; (e) convertible bond buyback removing dilution overhang. The stock has not re-rated to analyst target levels despite all of the above — implying persistent discount unexplained by near-term fundamentals alone.

9. Risk Assessment — Bear Scenario

Modelling the bear scenario

Baseline bear (not extreme): Assumes tenancy growth slows from 2,500 per year to ~1,500-1,800 per year; EBITDA growth halved from ~12% to ~5-6%; no new acquisitions but capex overshoot by 15-20%.

  • EBITDA trajectory: $471m (FY2025) → ~$495-510m (FY2026) vs management guidance $510-525m. Miss vs guidance: ~3-5%.
  • FCF impact: at lower tenancy adds + capex overshoot, recurring FCF of $130-150m vs guidance $210-225m. Material underdelivery.
  • Leverage: if EBITDA disappoints by 5-10% and FCF is lower, net leverage re-rises to ~3.8-4.0x rather than management's implied sub-3.5x path.
  • Share price: on ~5x EV/EBITDA (current approximate multiple), a 5-10% EBITDA miss compresses the equity value significantly because leverage effects amplify at this capital structure. Bear base case price: 70-85p.

Severe bear scenario: Macro shock (rate spike + EM FX crisis in DRC/Tanzania + MNO capex freeze). Comparable to 2022-23 conditions.

  • EBITDA growth stalls; FCF turns negative again; leverage re-rises above 4.5x.
  • Credit rating at risk of downgrade back to B territory.
  • Debt market access becomes expensive; management potentially needs equity issuance.
  • Share price: 25-40p in severe scenario.

Probability assessment:
- Bear base case (70-85p): 20-25% probability over a 12-month horizon. Requires macro deterioration or specific operational miss, not a certainty.
- Severe bear case (25-40p): 5-10% probability. Requires a 2022-style convergence of macro shock + acquisition error + rate spike. The current lower leverage (3.4x) provides more buffer than existed in 2022 (leverage was already rising before the shock hit).
- Bull base case (150-220p, partial re-rating toward consensus targets): 35-40% probability over 12 months.
- Sideways/range-bound (90-130p): 30-35% probability.
