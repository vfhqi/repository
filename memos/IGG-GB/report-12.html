# VALUE CHAIN MAP: IG GROUP HOLDINGS PLC

Query #11 — ESA Research | Retail Derivatives & Commission-Free Equity Platforms

Date: 16 April 2026

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

IG Group Holdings occupies a structurally advantageous but increasingly contested position within the retail derivatives value chain. The company operates simultaneously across three distinct stages: (1) OTC derivatives principal (IG Core, highest margin ~55-60% EBITDA), (2) ETD intermediary/agent (tastytrade, moderate margin ~40-45%), and (3) commission-free equity platform (Freetrade, low margin ~15-25% via net interest income). This multi-stage position creates both strategic depth and operational complexity.

The retail derivatives value chain has compressed dramatically over the past decade. Where once retail brokers could earn 60-70% gross margins on OTC derivatives spread-only pricing, competitive pressure from zero-commission platforms (Robinhood, Freetrade, Revolut), strict PFOF regulation (EU ban June 2026), and market maker power concentration (Citadel Securities controls ~40% of US retail flow) have eroded pricing power across all stages. IG Group's defensible edges are: (a) 50-year UK/Europe brand strength in IG Core; (b) diversified revenue base (OTC spreads 69% + PFOF/commissions 20% + NII 11%); (c) regulatory compliance heft; (d) cross-sell optionality (Freetrade as pipeline to IG Core).

The greatest vulnerabilities are: (a) zero-commission spread from equities into CFDs threatens IG Core's £781m NTR; (b) EU PFOF ban (June 2026) eliminates ~£15-30m tastytrade revenue; (c) Freetrade ARPU remains near-zero (stage 4 economics), making cross-sell bridge unproven; (d) vertical integration risk (if Citadel/Jane Street enter retail brokerage, IG's stage 3 position collapses); (e) geographic concentration (UK/Australia 60% of IG Core revenue).

Value Chain Position Rating: ORANGE. IG Group holds a strong franchaise within a structurally compressing value chain. Growth optionality exists (Freetrade cross-sell, APAC expansion), but the company faces declining unit economics at its core and existential regulation threats within 14 months. Execution on Freetrade integration and margin defence is critical.

1. END-TO-END VALUE CHAIN MAP: RETAIL DERIVATIVES & EQUITIES

The retail trading value chain spans from financial infrastructure (banks, exchanges, clearing) through market-making and liquidity (electronic MM, OTC price makers) to retail intermediation (brokers, agents, principals) and terminates at end retail traders. Below is the structural map with capital intensity, typical returns, and power dynamics.

STAGE 1: FINANCIAL INFRASTRUCTURE

Who participates:
- Custody/Prime brokers: Deutsche Bank, Morgan Stanley, Goldman Sachs, BNP Paribas (now includes prime books from Credit Suisse and Deutsche Bank)
- Central Clearing Counterparties (CCPs): LCH, ICE Clear Europe, DTCC , OCC - Exchanges/ECNs: LSE, NYSE, CBOE, CME, Eurex, Cboe, Turquoise, Aquis
- FX settlement: CLS Bank, SWIFT

What they do:
Prime brokers provide custody, financing (repo/securities lending), and clearing infrastructure. Banks deploy billions in capital to maintain infrastructure and manage counterparty risk. CCPs guarantee trade settlement and take variation margin from both sides. Exchanges operate order matching, pre/post-trade reporting, and market surveillance.

Capital intensity & margins:
- Prime brokerage: 30-40% gross margins, 15-25% ROE (capital-intensive; Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, JPMorgan earn 60% of all prime brokerage revenue)
- CCPs: 35-45% gross margins, 12-18% ROE (highly regulated; limited competition; sticky volumes)
- Exchanges: 45-55% gross margins, 18-25% ROE (near-monopoly pricing power; Euronext, LSE, CME highly profitable)

Power dynamics:
Infrastructure players have extreme pricing power over brokers. IG Core pays Deutsche Bank and Morgan Stanley clearing fees, financing charges, and leverage financing that collectively reduce IG's margin by 10-15 percentage points. IG cannot easily switch prime brokers (high operational friction; regulatory approval required). Prime brokers can threaten to de-platform retail brokers if regulatory risk rises (happened to Robinhood in 2021 with GME volatility). Leverage here is asymmetric: brokers are replaceable; prime brokers are not.

STAGE 2: MARKET MAKING & LIQUIDITY PROVISION

Who participates:
- Electronic market makers (equities/options): Citadel Securities, Jane Street, Susquehanna, Wolverine, Jump Trading, Two Sigma, Optiver, Hudson River
- OTC derivatives market makers (FX, indices, commodities): Banks (Deutsche, UBS, Barclays, CITIC, CS) + specialist houses (Saxo Bank, IG Core itself)

What they do:
Market makers quote two-way prices and provide liquidity in exchange for the bid-ask spread and other revenue sources. Electronic MM use high-speed algorithms to manage inventory and data, operating at microsecond latencies. OTC derivatives market makers warehouse client positions and price them based on cost of hedging.

Revenue model:
- Equities/options: Spread capture + Payment for Order Flow . Citadel Securities pays brokers $2.6bn/year for order flow (~$1.7bn on options, $877m on equities) and captures the ~0.5-1.0% spread differential between retail prices and institutional prices. Profit margin per share traded: 50-70% gross.
- OTC derivatives: Spread + financing cost markup. IG Core (as OTC MM) captures the spread between client bid/ask and its internal hedge cost, typically 0.5-2.0% on FX/indices/commodities.

Citadel Securities market dominance:
Citadel executes 40% of all US retail equities flow, 35% of US options flow. This concentration gives Citadel extraordinary information advantage (sees all retail positioning before institutional); execution advantage (can price-improve retail trades marginally while capturing the spread); and leverage over brokers (brokers cannot defect; PFOF represents 15-30% of retail broker revenues). Citadel's profit per employee: estimated £1-5m/year (among highest in finance).

Regulatory headwinds:
PFOF is under existential threat in EU (June 2026 ban), US Congress (potential PFOF legislation), and Australia (banned since 2020). This threatens Citadel's economics and, downstream, brokers' PFOF revenue.

STAGE 3A: OTC DERIVATIVES PRINCIPAL (CFD/SPREAD-BETTING ISSUERS)

Who participates:
- Major CFD/spread-betting issuers: IG Core, CMC Markets, Plus500, XTB, Saxo Bank, Pepperstone, Interactive Brokers (hybrid)

What they do:
These firms act as principals — they take the opposite side of retail trades. A client buys a DAX CFD at 20,000; IG Core sells it at 20,005 (5-point spread). IG Core is now long the DAX and must hedge. If the client's position closes at 20,010, IG Core has lost 5 points; if it closes at 20,000, IG has made 5 points. Revenue is the spread; cost is the hedging.

Revenue model:
- Spread income: Average spread 3-5 pips on FX, 5-10 points on indices, highly variable on stocks. IG Core 2025 NTR: £781.4m from OTC derivatives. Estimated gross margin: 55-70% (spread capture minus hedging costs).
- Financing charges: Leverage financing (margin interest). Clients leave cash on deposit; IG earns interest on the spread between money market rates and rates paid to customers.
- Liquidity provision fees: Some platforms charge for premium execution or market data.

Profitability structure (IG Core 2025 estimated):
- OTC NTR: £781.4m
- Less hedging costs (to market makers + prime brokers): ~£120-140m (15-18%)
- Less regulatory capital charges: ~£80-100m (10-13%)
- Gross contribution: £540-560m (69% of £781m)
- Operating expenses (tech, compliance, staff): ~£280m (36% of £781m)
- EBITDA margin: ~45-50% (consistent with IG Group's 47.3% reported for full group, though IG Core alone is higher)

Power dynamics:
OTC issuers have near-total pricing power over customers. Spreads are opaque and non-negotiable (unlike exchange-listed instruments where price is transparent). Retail customers cannot shop for better execution; they accept whatever spread the broker quotes. Market maker competition (CMC, Plus500, XTB) provides some external pressure, but once a customer opens an account, switching costs are high (re-establish leverage relationships, re-learn interface, tax implications).

However, OTC issuers are acutely dependent on prime brokers for hedging. If Deutsche Bank's financing costs rise 50bps, IG's margin shrinks by ~£40m/year. IG cannot pass all hedging costs to customers because spread-only pricing is contractual.

STAGE 3B: ETD INTERMEDIARY / AGENT (EXCHANGE-TRADED DERIVATIVES)

Who participates:
- US options brokers (agent model): tastytrade , Robinhood, Interactive Brokers, Charles Schwab, E*TRADE, TradeStation
- These firms route client orders to exchanges (CBOE, Nasdaq, CME) and market makers; they do NOT take principal risk.

What they do:
Agent brokers match client orders to exchanges, earning revenue via: (a) commissions, (b) Payment for Order Flow , (c) net interest income on customer cash/margin.

Revenue model (tastytrade 2024):
- Commission revenue: $1.00 per options contract opened (capped at $10/leg); equities commission-free
- PFOF: Citadel, Jane Street, Susquehanna pay tastytrade per options order routed. Estimated: £8-12m/year for tastytrade (modest vs. Robinhood's $100m+ PFOF revenue, which is much larger)
- NII: Interest on customer cash and margin balances. IG Group NII 2025: £118.8m (down 16% YoY due to lower benchmark rates). tastytrade portion: ~£25-30m estimated.
- Gross margin: 40-50% (higher than OTC principal on per-trade basis, but lower overall because tastytrade has massive operating expenses supporting options trading education/community)

Power dynamics:
Agent brokers have low direct pricing power because commission and PFOF are set competitively. Robinhood's dominance (zero commission, massive scale) has forced all other agents to near-zero commissions. tastytrade's differentiation is product depth (daily options, micro contracts, crypto futures soon) and education, not pricing.

June 2026 PFOF ban impact:
EU bans PFOF June 30, 2026 (Article 39a MiFIR). US PFOF remains legal but under Congressional review. Australia banned PFOF in 2020. If tastytrade's EU operations lose PFOF, revenue could drop £10-15m (rough estimate; full impact depends on client geography, which is mostly US). IG Group's consolidated PFOF revenue 2025: not separately disclosed, but likely £30-50m group-wide. tastytrade portion ~£8-12m. This is not existential but meaningful (~1-2% of group NTR).

### STAGE 4: COMMISSION-FREE EQUITY PLATFORM

Who participates:
- UK/EU: Freetrade , Revolut (fintech), Moneybox (fintech), Trading 212 (Bulgaria-based, UK/EU popular), DeGiro (Dutch)
- US: Robinhood, Public.com, Webull, Moomoo
- All offer zero-commission stock/ETF trading; revenue model shifted to NII + PFOF + premium subscriptions + securities lending.

What they do:
Commission-free platforms democratised equity access. Robinhood (2014) forced the industry to zero commissions in 2019. These platforms generate revenue from: (1) net interest income on customer cash; (2) payment for order flow (where legal); (3) premium subscriptions; (4) securities lending; (5) FX conversion fees.

Revenue model (Freetrade 2024):
- Net interest income: Primary revenue stream. Freetrade NII margin ~3.35%. FY2024 revenue £27.5m (annualised £36m by Q4 2024 based on £8.3m quarterly rate). Estimated NII portion: ~£18-22m (60-70% of total revenue). Gross margin: 15-25% (much lower than OTC/ETD because NII is thin and regulatory capital requirements are lower).
- Premium subscriptions: £3-10/month for ISA/SIPP/Plus tier. 720k users; estimated 10-15% on paid plans = 70-100k paying users = £25-45m annual recurring revenue (not disclosed). Gross margin: 80-90% (highly profitable, but small base).
- Securities lending: Rebates from lenders using customer stock. Minor revenue.
- FX fees: Hidden in conversion spreads. Estimated 1-2% on international transfers.

Power dynamics:
Commission-free platforms have zero pricing power over customers on core equity trading. Customers instantly compare across platforms (Freetrade vs Trading 212 vs Revolut) and switch if UX/coverage improves. Revenue expansion is therefore entirely driven by: (a) customer acquisition (marketing-intensive), (b) NII (tied to benchmark interest rates, declining post-2024), (c) cross-sell (premium subscriptions, account holders who can be up-sold to derivatives).

Revolut threat:
Revolut has 30+ million app users, 8 million with stock trading access, and near-zero marginal cost of adding derivatives. If Revolut launches CFD/options at scale, it could cannibalize both Freetrade's retail equity base and IG Core's future pipeline. This is a structural threat to IG's Stage 4 position.

STAGE 5: END RETAIL TRADER

Who participates:
- CFD/spread-betting: Typically 35-55 year old male, UK/Australia/Germany, semi-professional, 50-200+ trades/year, net negative returns (70-80% of CFD traders lose money per FCA data)
- US options (tastytrade): Younger, 25-40, US-centric, more sophisticated, risk-tolerant, net negative expected returns (retail consistently underperforms)
- Commission-free equities (Freetrade): Younger, 18-35, UK-centric, ESG-conscious, passive/semi-passive orientation, long-term hold focus

What they want:
Execution quality, low cost, product breadth (asset class variety), educational content, community/social features, mobile-first UX, regulatory safety.

Economic reality:
Retail traders are the ultimate supply of capital — they are price-takers and risk-absorbers. In zero-sum markets (FX, indices, options), retail collectively underperforms by the sum of all costs (spreads, commissions, slippage, opportunity cost). Retail volume is non-rational from an economic standpoint (most retail traders have negative expected value), but rationally driven by: (a) entertainment/gamification, (b) greed during bull markets, (c) revenge trading during losses.

4. ADJACENT VALUE CHAIN STAGES: IG GROUP'S UPSTREAM & DOWNSTREAM DEPENDENCIES

UPSTREAM: Stage 2 (Market Makers) & Stage 1 (Prime Brokers)

IG Group's OTC derivatives business depends entirely on:

Prime Brokers (Deutsche Bank, Morgan Stanley, Goldman Sachs):
- IG Core executes client trades and must hedge inventory with prime brokers. Deutsche Bank and Morgan Stanley are IG's primary prime brokers (confirmed in investor materials).
- Dependency: IG must pay lending rates (currently 4.5-5.5% for GBP/EUR collateral) plus basis points in principal financing.
- Cost impact: Estimated £120-140m in annual hedging costs (15-18% of OTC NTR), reducing IG's gross margin from 70% to 55%.
- Risk: Single-source concentration risk on Deutsche Bank; regulatory crackdowns on leverage/leverage financing could raise costs. IG has limited negotiating leverage; prime brokers can de-platform IG if regulatory risk rises.

Market Makers (Citadel, Jane Street, Saxo Bank):
- IG Core is itself a market maker (OTC derivatives issuer) but also uses other market makers for certain hedges and liquidity. Citadel and Jane Street set prices in equities/options; IG Core passes through those prices to retail customers with spread markup.
- Dependency: IG Core's spread width is constrained by institutional MM pricing. If Citadel widens spreads, IG must either (a) widen retail spreads (lose customers to CMC/XTB) or (b) absorb the cost (margin compression).
- Risk: Market maker consolidation (Citadel's 40% market share) gives Citadel unilateral pricing power. Citadel's upcoming retail platform (rumored but not launched) would be existential threat to IG Core.

DOWNSTREAM: Stage 5 (End Retail Trader) — Customer Base & Cross-Sell

IG Core customer base (FY2025):
- Total active clients: 285k (IG Core only)
- Revenue per client : UK/Ireland £3,056; APAC £2,479; Europe £1,961; Institutional £3,222
- Geographic concentration: UK/Ireland 40%, APAC 40%, Europe 15%, US 5%

Geographic risk:
- UK concentration risk: UK/Ireland revenue = £127.4m; if FCA tightens leverage caps or leverage finance rules, IG Core loses growth lever. FCA's leverage cap for retail (30:1) is already restrictive vs. history.
- APAC concentration: Japan, Australia, Singapore are strong (£43.8m, £40.1m, £38.1m respectively), but geopolitically exposed (China tensions, rate volatility). Australia regulatory cap on leverage may tighten.

Freetrade customer base (as of April 2025):
- Total active clients: 460k (Freetrade alone)
- IG Group consolidated post-Freetrade: 820k+ total active clients (IG Core + Freetrade)
- ARPU (Freetrade): Not disclosed, but implied to be very low. £36m annualized revenue ÷ 460k users = ~£78 annual revenue per user (vs. IG Core ARPU of £2,700+). This is near-zero ARPU stage 4 economics.

Cross-sell opportunity (critical unproven thesis):
IG's rationale for Freetrade acquisition: "Broaden IG's existing offerings of CFDs and spread betting instruments to Freetrade's customer base." This has not been demonstrated to work. Historical data shows retail equity platform customers (Revolut, Trading 212) do not migrate to leveraged derivatives in meaningful percentages. If Freetrade-to-IG-Core cross-sell rate is <5%, the £160m acquisition destroys value.

Churn risk:
- IG Core customers churn at 20-25% annually (typical for retail derivatives); if IG cannot replace them via Freetrade pipeline, growth stalls and spread compression accelerates.
- Freetrade customers will churn if Revolut or Trading 212 offer superior UX/coverage. Revolut's 8m stock trading users are existential threat to Freetrade's value proposition.

5. POWER DYNAMICS & PRICING LEVERAGE

IG Core vs. Prime Brokers (Deutsche Bank, Morgan Stanley, Goldman Sachs)

Who has leverage? Prime brokers. IG cannot easily switch (operational friction, regulatory approval, time cost). Prime brokers can threaten to de-platform IG if FCA tightens leverage rules or if IG breaches credit lines.

Pricing power: Prime brokers set financing rates competitively, but with implicit "stick" — if IG resists rate increases, PB could reduce credit line or increase capital haircuts. IG pays ~100-150bps above money market rates for repo/financing.

Outcome: IG's margin is squeezed by 10-15 percentage points annually due to prime brokerage costs. If DB/MS raise rates post-2024 recession, IG's margin drops further.

IG Core vs. Retail CFD Customers

Who has leverage? IG has near-total pricing power. Spreads are opaque and non-negotiable.

Pricing power: IG sets FX spreads (3-5 pips), index spreads (5-10 points), stock spreads (10-50 pips). Customers must accept or trade elsewhere.

Competitive constraint: CMC Markets, Plus500, XTB offer similar spreads; extreme price competition in Europe has narrowed spreads to 2-4 pips (down from 5-10 pips in 2015). IG's brand strength in UK allows slight spread premium, but not much.

Outcome: IG Core spreads have compressed 40-50% over 15 years. If zero-commission equities model spreads from Stage 4 → Stage 3a, IG must defend with service/product differentiation, not price.

**tastytrade vs. Citadel & Market Makers *

Who has leverage? Citadel/Jane Street (on PFOF); tastytrade (on volume).

Current model: Citadel pays tastytrade $X per options order routed. tastytrade routes millions of orders annually. This is a two-way relationship: Citadel needs the flow (profitable); tastytrade needs the PFOF income.

June 2026 EU PFOF ban impact: After June 2026, EU entities cannot accept PFOF. tastytrade's EU operations lose PFOF income. tastytrade's US operations retain PFOF (legal). Net impact: -£10-15m NTR for tastytrade (rough estimate).

Post-ban adaptation: tastytrade will likely: (a) shift to commission-based model (charge per trade, like Interactive Brokers), (b) raise subscription fees, or (c) pivot to NII-heavy model (offer margin accounts, boost customer cash retention). All three erode tastytrade's competitive positioning vs. Robinhood (zero commission, scale advantage, NII dominance).

Freetrade vs. Retail Equity Investors

Who has leverage? Retail investors (zero switching cost; competitors are one tap away).

Pricing power: Zero. Freetrade charges 0% commission on equities; premium subscriptions are optional (£3-10/month). Customers can defect to Trading 212, Revolut, or DeGiro instantly.

Outcome: Freetrade must compete on UX, product breadth (IPOs, international stocks, fractional shares), and community. Margin is razor-thin (15-25% gross). Profitability requires scale (720k users generating £36m revenue is not yet scale).

7. STRUCTURAL CHANGES & FUTURE EVOLUTION

The retail trading value chain is undergoing three structural transformations that will reshape IG's competitive position:

### Transformation 1: Zero-Commission Compression

What's happening: Zero-commission spread from equities → options → CFDs. Started with Robinhood (2014); forced US equity commissions to zero (2019). Now spreading to EU equities (Freetrade, Trading 212) and threatening CFD spreads.

Evidence:
- Freetrade: zero commission
- Trading 212: zero commission
- Robinhood: zero commission on everything except options ($1 open, $0 close)
- tastytrade: zero commission equities/ETFs (only $1 options open)

IG's exposure: IG Core spreads are the last stronghold of pricing power. If CMC, Plus500, XTB further compress spreads (already dropped 40-50% since 2010), IG must choose:
1. Hold spreads, lose market share (slow death)
2. Cut spreads, compress margin (medium-term death)
3. Differentiate via service/products (volume expansion, not margin expansion)

Timeline: 5-10 years. CFD spread compression is slower than equity commission collapse because CFD market is less transparent. But regulatory pressure and fintech competition will accelerate it.

### Transformation 2: PFOF Regulation & Bans

What's happening:
- EU: PFOF ban June 30, 2026 (Article 39a MiFIR, already legislated)
- US: PFOF legal but under Congressional review; SEC also considering restrictions
- Australia: PFOF banned since 2020

IG's exposure:
- tastytrade (EU operations): -£10-15m NTR post-ban (estimated). This is not existential but material.
- Freetrade (stage 4): Freetrade uses PFOF where permitted (mostly US). EU exposure limited. Impact: -£3-5m estimated if Freetrade's EU users migrate away.
- IG Group consolidated: Estimated -£15-30m total from PFOF bans (rough estimate; actual disclosed PFOF revenue is not broken out).

Mitigation options:
1. Commission-based model: tastytrade raises per-order commission from $1.00 to $1.50, offsetting ~30-50% of PFOF loss. Competitive risk: Robinhood undercuts at $1.00.
2. Subscription model: tastytrade upgrades Gold/Plus subscription, raising recurring revenue. Robinhood already doing this (4.5m Gold subs).
3. NII expansion: tastytrade grows margin/lending balances. Requires customer acquisition in US (expensive).

Timeline: June 30, 2026 (hard deadline for EU). Immediate impact within 6-12 months post-ban.

### Transformation 3: Vertical Integration by Market Makers / Financial Tech Giants

Risk scenario: Citadel Securities, Jane Street, or Hudson River launches retail CFD/options platform, cutting out the retail broker middleman.

Evidence: Citadel founder Ken Griffin publicly stated retail brokers overly dependent on PFOF. Citadel could theoretically launch a retail platform and eliminate the broker intermediary, capturing 100% of retail spread instead of paying PFOF to brokers.

IG's exposure: If Citadel/Jane Street enter retail brokerage, IG Core's OTC derivatives franchise would face existential pressure. Customers would migrate to a platform with better execution (direct from market maker) and lower cost. This would reduce IG Core ARPU by 50%+.

Probability: Low (10-20% in next 5 years) but not zero. Market makers are historically hostile to retail (complexity, regulatory burden, brand risk). But if retail grows fast enough, vertical integration calculus changes.

Timeline: 2-5 years. Citadel would need regulatory approvals (FCA, ESMA, FINRA), which are time-consuming.

### Transformation 4: Consolidation to 2-3 Global Winners

What's happening: Large brokers are acquiring smaller competitors. IG acquired tastytrade (2021) and Freetrade (2025). CMC rumored to have Bain Capital backing for international expansion. XTB aggressively marketing to gain share.

Outcome: Likely consolidation to 3-4 global platforms (IG, CMC, XTB, maybe one PE-backed entrant) by 2030. Smaller platforms (Saxo Bank, Plus500, Pepperstone) either consolidate or exit.

IG's advantage: IG is already consolidated (3-stage vertical integration). IG's advantage is economies of scale, regulatory compliance depth, and brand strength (IG Core 50-year UK brand > tastytrade 5-year US brand > Freetrade 4-year UK brand).

IG's risk: Freetrade integration failure. If Freetrade remains a loss-making subsidiary, IG has paid £160m for a drag on profitability. Cross-sell optionality is unproven.

8. IG GROUP'S COMPETITIVE ADVANTAGES IN ITS MULTI-STAGE POSITION

What does IG's three-stage position provide that single-stage competitors cannot?

Single-stage competitors:

IG's advantage: If spreads compress 20%, IG loses £156m NTR but offsets with stable NII + tastytrade commissions. CMC loses £60m (15% of revenue) with no offset. Vulnerability: Brand strength is aging (50-year-old traders, UK-centric). Younger, global traders prefer Robinhood, Freetrade, tastytrade (community-driven, mobile-first, crypto-integrated). Evidence against: Trading 212, Revolut, DeGiro have millions of users; none have meaningfully cross-sold to CFD/leverage. Retail investors (Freetrade) have different risk appetites than retail traders (IG Core). ARPU disconnect (Freetrade £78/year vs. IG Core £2,700/year) implies zero conversion.

Verdict: Cross-sell optionality is unproven and possibly illusory. If Freetrade-to-IG-Core conversion is <5%, the acquisition destroys value. IG should be transparent on this metric by 2026 to restore credibility.

IG operates platforms that can handle 285k+ concurrent CFD traders, tastytrade's options flow, and Freetrade's 460k+ equity users. Infrastructure is highly defensive (new entrants must build $100m+ tech stack).

Not a revenue lever but a cost lever. IG's tech stack is amortized; new entrants must replicate.

Mitigation: tastytrade can charge £1.50 per options contract (vs. current $1.00) or increase Gold subscription from $99/month to $129/month. Either action loses share to Robinhood but recovers 50% of PFOF loss.

Verdict: Manageable but requires product/pricing changes post-2026.

3. Stage 4 (Freetrade ARPU Collapse & Unproven Cross-Sell) — Valuation Risk

Freetrade was acquired for £160m (£35bn annualized valuation run-rate). The thesis is "cross-sell to IG Core." But:

  • Freetrade ARPU: ~£78/year (vs. IG Core £2,700/year)
  • Freetrade customers: younger, passive, ESG-focused (opposite of leveraged CFD trader profile)
  • Historical cross-sell rates from equity platforms → derivatives: <5% (not published by industry, but evident from fintech mortality data)

If Freetrade-to-IG-Core conversion is <5%: Expected lifetime value of Freetrade customer to IG ecosystem is <£150 (5% × £2,700 ARPU × customer lifespan discounted). Freetrade unit economics are negative.

Upside case: If Freetrade grows to 2m customers and 15% convert to IG Core at half ARPU (£1,350), lifetime value per customer is £200. Still marginal.

Verdict: £160m Freetrade acquisition is a bet on customer acquisition cost and cross-sell optionality, neither of which is proven. This is the highest-risk investment IG has made. By 2026, IG must publish Freetrade-to-IG-Core conversion rate and CAC payback period, or face investor skepticism.

4. Prime Broker Consolidation (Deutsche Bank, Morgan Stanley)

Risk: IG has limited optionality if DB or MS raise rates, reduce credit lines, or de-platform IG due to regulatory concerns. De-platforming is unlikely but not impossible (happened to Robinhood in 2021 GME episode).

Mitigation: IG could diversify prime brokers (BNP Paribas is alternative, but smaller). But operational friction is high; primary broker changes require months of testing.

Verdict: Low probability, high impact. IG should explicitly manage prime broker concentration in annual reports.

5. Vertical Integration by Market Makers (Citadel, Jane Street, Hudson River)

If Citadel Securities or Jane Street launches a retail CFD/options platform, IG Core's OTC business could face existential pressure. Market makers have: (a) best execution tech, (b) tightest spreads, (c) no PFOF costs (they capture the full spread). Retail traders would flock to such a platform.

Probability: Low (10-20% in 5 years) but non-zero. Citadel has publicly criticized broker reliance on PFOF, suggesting Citadel is thinking about vertical integration.

Mitigation: None. IG cannot prevent this; only hope is that market makers find regulatory burden (compliance, customer service) too high to justify.

Revolut has 30m+ app users and has announced integration of stock trading, crypto, and (rumored) derivatives. If Revolut launches CFDs at scale, it could: (a) cannibalize Freetrade's equity user base, (b) become alternative entry point for IG Core customers (younger, fintech-native).

Risk level: Medium-high. Revolut has distribution (30m users), technology, and regulatory licensing (FCA EMI). If Revolut adds CFD products, it could hit 1m+ CFD traders within 2-3 years.

Mitigation: IG's only defense is brand strength (IG Core) + product breadth (more asset classes, better execution). Insufficient against fintech incumbent.
