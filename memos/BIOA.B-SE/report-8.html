§1 — Industry Overview + Value Chain Economics (BB#2 TC3 + TC5 inputs)

BioArctic sits within the Alzheimer's disease therapeutics sub-sector of global biopharma — one of the largest, fastest-growing, and most scientifically complex disease areas. The value chain runs from target discovery through clinical development, regulatory approval, commercial manufacturing, and ultimately payer/patient access.

Industry verdict: The AD therapeutics market is structurally large and growing, with the margin pool heavily concentrated at the IP/royalty stage and commercial scale — BioArctic captures the IP/royalty layer, which is high-value but Eisai-mediated [#J]

  • Industry size: The global AD therapeutics market was estimated at approximately USD 7-9bn in 2024, with disease-modifying therapy segment (where Leqembi competes) still nascent but forecast to grow to USD 15-25bn by 2030 per multiple public industry analyses [#I]
  • Sub-bullet: The symptomatic market (acetylcholinesterase inhibitors, memantine generics) is essentially flat/declining in value; the growth is entirely in the DMT segment [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: Comparable neuroscience platform royalty situations (e.g. Prothena's prasinezumab deal with Roche) suggest royalty-stage participants can capture 5-12% of net sales from a successful partnership [#A]
  • Growth trajectory: DMT segment CAGR of 25-40% pa is plausible N3-5Y based on Leqembi launch trajectory and donanemab approvals; base market growth of ~6-8% pa for broader AD pharma is well-established by Evaluate Pharma and GlobalData public forecasts [#I]
  • Sub-bullet: Near-term growth is constrained by slow payer reimbursement uptake (CMS coverage with evidence development in the US, slow HTA in EU/Japan) [#A]
  • Structural driver: Primary structural demand driver is demographic — global population ageing; ~55 million AD patients globally, forecast to reach 78 million by 2030 (WHO/Alzheimer's Disease International public data) [#I]
  • Sub-bullet: Scientific legitimacy of the amyloid hypothesis (post-Leqembi FDA regular approval 2023) is a secondary structural driver unlocking commercial investment [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: Regulatory pathway de-risking (FDA surrogate endpoint acceptance for amyloid PET clearance) has structurally lowered development risk and attracted capital [#A]
  • Geographic split: US is ~55-60% of DMT market value; Japan is significant due to Eisai's home-market strength; EU is slower due to HTA hurdles (EMA vs national payers); China remains early-stage for premium DMTs [#I]
  • Sub-bullet: Japan Ministry of Health approved Leqembi in September 2023; EU EMA application ongoing as of early 2025 [#I]
  • Fragmentation: The DMT competitive space is currently oligopolistic at launch (Leqembi vs donanemab); symptomatic market is highly fragmented with generics dominant [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: Fragmentation in clinical-stage pipeline is high (100+ compounds in Phase 1-2 globally) but near-term commercial concentration is low (2 approved DMTs, 1 dominant) [#A]
  • Consolidation: Trend is toward consolidation among large pharma (Biogen/Eisai collaboration, Eli Lilly donanemab solo) with smaller biotechs supplying IP via licensing; BioArctic's model is consistent with this consolidation pattern [#A]

§3 — Barriers to Entry + Exit + Disruption Forces (BB#2 TC3 RA11)

Barriers verdict: The AD DMT space has extremely high barriers driven by scientific complexity, capital requirements, regulatory pathway difficulty, and decade-length development timelines — but these barriers protect incumbents imperfectly against paradigm-shift disruptions [#J]

  • Capital requirement: Phase 3 Alzheimer's trials cost USD 2-5bn per compound (based on Biogen ENGAGE/EMERGE ~USD 4bn, Lilly TRAILBLAZER ~USD 2bn public estimates); BioArctic could not independently prosecute a full Phase 3 programme [#I]
  • Sub-bullet: This capital barrier is the primary reason BioArctic's model is partnership-dependent; it is a structural feature, not a management choice [#A]
  • Regulatory barrier: FDA now accepts amyloid PET clearance as surrogate endpoint for accelerated approval but requires Phase 4 confirmatory trial for full approval; this pathway is de-risked but still requires extensive clinical infrastructure [#I]
  • Sub-bullet: EU CHMP requirements are more conservative; EMA review of Leqembi is ongoing as of early 2025 with outcome uncertain — a European rejection would materially impair BioArctic's royalty TAM [#A]
  • Technology barrier: Protofibril-selective antibody engineering (BioArctic's founding IP, originally pioneered with the Uppsala University spin-out) is scientifically distinctive; replicating the specific binding profile requires deep protein engineering expertise [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: However, donanemab (anti-N3pE truncated amyloid) proves that multiple anti-amyloid approaches can achieve regulatory success — the technology moat is real but not impenetrable [#A]
  • IP barrier: BioArctic's core patents on the lecanemab antibody and protofibril-selection methodology provide exclusivity; patent expiry timelines (primary patents ~2033-2036 estimated) provide a commercial window but not an indefinite one [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: Biosimilar entry risk post-patent expiry is material for biologics; Alzheimer's biosimilar economics are uncertain but represent a 10-year-horizon disruption risk [#A]
  • Scale economy: Manufacturing scale for biologic antibodies (Eisai/Samsung Biologics partnership) creates meaningful cost advantages; new entrants must replicate expensive CDMO relationships [#A]
  • Distribution barrier: Leqembi requires monthly IV infusion in certified infusion centres; the infusion infrastructure buildout is itself a barrier — Eisai/Biogen have invested in infusion centre partnerships; new approved entrants face the same infrastructure requirement [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: Subcutaneous formulation of lecanemab (in clinical development as of 2024) could lower this barrier further and expand the patient access funnel — positive for BioArctic royalties [#A]

Disruption forces (RA11, CQ50):

  • Disruption force — modality shift: Oral small-molecule approaches (BACE inhibitors largely failed; gamma-secretase modulators; GLP-1 crossover) represent an existential long-run threat if any achieve comparable efficacy with oral convenience; IV biologic moat could be disrupted by an oral DMT [#J]
  • Sub-bullet: GLP-1 signals in AD cognition (semaglutide; liraglutide) are preliminary but are being pursued by Novo Nordisk in Phase 3; if GLP-1 proves to be a cheaper, orally-available DMT, the IV antibody segment's premium could erode materially [#J]
  • Disruption force — tau/synaptic paradigm: If tau-targeting or synaptic-protection approaches prove superior in later-stage trials, the amyloid modality (Leqembi + donanemab) could face clinical obsolescence; this is a 5-10 year horizon but is non-trivial [#A]
  • Disruption force — diagnostics: Expanding blood-based amyloid testing (C2N PrecivityAD, Fujirebio Lumipulse plasma) increases diagnosis rates — net positive for BioArctic's royalty TAM — but also expands the addressable competitive space [#A]
  • Adjacencies entering (RA10, CQ46): Large oncology pharma (AstraZeneca, Roche) are not currently focusing capital on AD DMTs post-Roche's gantenerumab failure; GLP-1 companies (Novo, Lilly) are adjacent entrants via metabolic/neurological crossover [#A]
  • Substitutes (RA10, CQ49): Non-pharmacological interventions (lifestyle, cognitive training, hearing aid for dementia prevention) represent a substitute for prophylactic use cases but are not substitutes for DMT treatment in diagnosed MCI/AD populations [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: Generic symptomatic treatments (donepezil, memantine) remain the standard of care for mild-moderate AD due to payer cost containment; these are economic substitutes for payers even if clinically inferior [#A]
  • Vanilla new entrants (CQ47): Traditional pharma companies can enter with capital and development infrastructure; history shows consistent failure rate (>95% Phase 3 failure historically in AD); capital willingness is declining among non-specialist entrants post-Biogen ENGAGE failure [#A]
  • Innovative new entrants (CQ48): Gene therapy, RNA-based approaches, and next-generation precision medicine (APOE4-stratified treatment) represent innovative entrant pathways; all are early-stage and ≥7-10 years from potential commercial competition with BioArctic's core asset [#A]

Regulatory/political landscape (RA12, CQ51-52):

  • Regulator SWOT: CMS coverage decision (2023 CED policy) is the most important near-term regulatory dynamic; positive resolution of CED requirements would expand Medicare patient access dramatically [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: CMS finalised expanded traditional Medicare coverage for Leqembi and similar approved AD therapies in July 2024 — a significant positive catalyst; BioArctic royalties directly benefit [#I]
  • Political SWOT: Drug pricing legislation (IRA implications for biologics, 13-year small molecule / 9-year biologic protection) is a long-run risk; Leqembi's biologic status provides better pricing protection than small molecules [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: EU payer politics (national HTA divergence) is a nearer-term political risk — Germany, France, and UK NICE assessments could restrict reimbursement; EU is a lower-royalty contributor than US anyway [#A]

Sceptical bullet (§3): The high-barrier framing is vulnerable to the GLP-1 disruption scenario: if Novo Nordisk's semaglutide Phase 3 in AD delivers positive results (~2026-2027 read-out), the oral/convenient profile of a GLP-1 could rapidly shift prescriber and payer preferences away from monthly-IV anti-amyloid antibodies — the technology and IP barriers that protect BioArctic today would be largely irrelevant against a modality shift of this nature [#J]
