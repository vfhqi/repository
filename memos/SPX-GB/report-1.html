[W][C] Spirax Group plc (SPX.L) — Q1 IG Business Description

Date: 07-May-26 | Stage: IG | Source: [C] | Author: Watson (Claude-native)

BLUF — Key Findings

Verdict: Spirax Group is a high-quality specialty industrial compounder with a durable aftermarket moat, but faces a structural transition risk as its legacy steam franchise matures and ETS must prove it can replicate the STS margin profile at scale. De-rating from 40x+ to ~21x forward PE creates a potential Pattern 4 re-entry window — but only if ETS margin trajectory and Watson-Marlow biopharm recovery translate into sustained double-digit earnings growth by 2027. [#J]

  • Moat strength — above peer median, but steam is maturing: ~85% of group sales are recurring MRO/maintenance; STS aftermarket lock-in via installed base + proprietary steam traps is genuine wide-moat. Peer Roper Technologies earns similar recurring % but at higher incremental margins. [#J]
  • Pricing power — demonstrated, but tested: Group held adjusted operating margin at 20.1% through 2021-24 inflation cycle despite cost inflation; gross margins ~76% are top-decile vs specialty industrial peers. Watson-Marlow's gross margin is estimated 55-60% vs STS's ~70%+, indicating meaningful mix-shift risk as WM share grows. [#A]
  • ETS — strategic bet, not yet proven: ETS grew 11% organically in 2025 with medium-term margin target of 20%; current margin substantially below STS's 23.5% target; until ETS reaches mid-teens adjusted margin the division is structurally dilutive. [#J]
  • Watson-Marlow — biopharm recovery is the near-term earnings lever: Two-year destocking cycle (2022-2024) is definitively over; WM +6% organic in FY2025 with margins +160bps to 26.2%. Medium-term WM target of 30%+ is credible given pre-destocking margins. Biopharm ~50% of WM revenue = concentrated single-sector exposure. [#A]
  • CEO quality — positive signal, not yet conclusive: Nimesh Patel (CEO since Jan 2024) is a finance/M&A background leader (ex-De Beers CFO, Anglo American, JP Morgan/UBS) steering a complex engineering business; £40m restructuring completed on time; jury still out on whether his background is optimal for an engineering-driven moat business. [#J]
  • China/STS project risk — a known headwind, not existential: China is ~20% of STS revenue, with weakness in large project orders 2023-2024; MRO sales showed resilience; China large-project cycle is cyclical not secular, but adds volatility. [#A]
  • Valuation context: SPX trades at ~21-22x forward PE (May 2026); peak was 40x+ in 2021. De-rating reflects ETS dilution, WM destocking drag, and China weakness — all three headwinds are partially reversing. Analyst consensus target ~8,123p vs ~6,595p market price; 27 covering analysts. [#I]
  • Pattern 4 flag: Significant de-rating from quality peak valuations is a Viewforth buying signal when underlying business quality is intact. The question is whether the earnings recovery trajectory (FY2025: adj. OP margin +30bps organically; FY2026 guidance: continued organic growth + margin progress) supports a 25-30x re-rating anchor. [#J]

§2 Company Details

Verdict: Spirax's three-division structure is coherent strategically (steam→electric pivot + biopharm growth engine) but creates transitional earnings drag as ETS dilutes margins and WM recovers from a structural inventory correction. The business model's durability is not in question; the question is re-rating catalyst timing. [#J]

Revenue Model

  • STS recurring model: Steam traps fail at ~2-5% per year; customers must replace to avoid energy waste and safety risk; creates predictable annual MRO revenue stream from large installed base. [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: STS installed base spans >180 countries; 50+ years of Spirax-Sarco brand in industrial steam. Field engineers (~4,000+ globally) provide direct customer service = switching cost enhancement.
  • Sub-bullet: ~50% of STS revenue estimated to be MRO/aftermarket (management's ~85% "recurring" group figure includes small upgrade projects); large project orders are the volatile component.
  • WMFTS revenue model: Peristaltic pump consumables (tubing) + hardware; tubing replacement cycles of 6-18 months per installation; biopharm customers typically sole-source from WMFTS once validated due to regulatory validation costs. [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: FDA/EMA validation lock-in is one of the strongest aftermarket mechanisms in the industrial universe — a customer would spend 12-24 months re-validating an alternative supplier.
  • Sub-bullet: Pharma/biotech ~50% of WM revenue (down from ~60% in 2022 pre-destocking peak); food/beverage, water/wastewater, mining make up the balance.
  • ETS revenue model: Heat tracing systems (process heating, equipment heating, and heat trace cable); projects-led with aftermarket MRO on installed base developing; currently less recurring than STS. [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: Three sub-segments — Process Heating (industrial/oil & gas), Equipment Heating (semiconductor/nuclear/aerospace), Heat Trace (pipe freeze protection). Semicon demand driving near-term growth.
  • Sub-bullet: Datacentre OEM contract win in FY2025 signals ETS is accessing new adjacencies beyond traditional process industries.

Moat Assessment

  • Core moat type: Installed base + switching costs + regulatory lock-in + proprietary steam systems know-how . Not purely a network effect or IP moat — it is an operational integration moat. [#J]
  • STS moat durability: Steam will not disappear from industrial processes in <20 years; pharmaceutical sterilisation, food pasteurisation, and industrial heating remain steam-dependent. Electrification risk is real but decades-long transition. [#A]
  • WM moat durability: Regulatory validation lock-in is durable regardless of energy transition; biopharm CDMO growth is a structural tailwind. Risk: concentration in biopharm means cycle exposure (proven 2022-2024). [#A]
  • ETS moat emerging: Still early; nVent and Thermon are established; Spirax's STS channel (direct sales force, global footprint) is an ETS competitive advantage if STS salesforce cross-sells ETS effectively. [#J]

Recent Delivery (FY2024 / FY2025)

  • FY2025 headline: Revenue £1,702.9m (+2% reported, +5% organic); adj. OP margin +30bps organically to 20.0%; all three divisions delivered organic growth. [#I]
  • FY2024 headline: Revenue £1,665.2m (-1% reported vs FY2023); group margin ~20.1%; STS and ETS grew, WM flat as destocking resolved. [#I]
  • Restructuring complete: £40m annualised savings delivered from programme launched late 2024; ~half realised in FY2025; reinvested into salesforce, digital, and R&D. [#A]
  • Cash conversion: 89% in FY2025; net debt/EBITDA 1.5x — leverage trajectory is positive; supports dividend growth and organic reinvestment. [#I]

Internal Change (Patel Era)

  • Patel agenda: Three priorities — organisational fitness, operational excellence, commercial excellence. Restructuring was the organisational fitness phase; now into operational and commercial. [#A]
  • Group rename: Spirax-Sarco Engineering → Spirax Group (2022) signals intent to be a diversified industrial group, not solely a steam company. [#I]
  • ETS strategic elevation: ETS grown from ~15% of revenue (pre-2020) to ~24% (FY2024); management has set a medium-term ETS margin target of 20% vs STS 23.5%. [#A]

§3 Industry Context

Verdict: Spirax operates in three structurally attractive sub-markets (steam systems, precision fluid, industrial heat tracing) that benefit from defensive end-market mix (~50% non-cyclical) and decarbonisation tailwinds — but competitive intensity is rising in ETS and Watson-Marlow faces biopharm cycle risk. [#J]

Steam Thermal Solutions Industry

  • Market structure: Spirax-Sarco is global #1 in steam system solutions with no close pure-play listed peer; Armstrong International (private) is the only meaningful global competitor; most competition is regional/niche. [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: Near-monopoly positioning in steam trap supply and service gives Spirax pricing power that no listed peer in fluid/thermal can match on aftermarket.
  • Steam market outlook: Global steam heating market is large and durable; EU industrial steam demand remains significant in pharma/food/chemical/power. Energy efficiency regulation (EU ETS, CBAM) creates upgrade cycle demand. [#A]
  • Macro sensitivity: STS MRO is near-acyclical (maintenance must happen); large project orders (STS ~20-25% of STS revenue) correlate with industrial capex and are China-sensitive. [#I]

Watson-Marlow Industry

  • Market structure: WMFTS is the global leader in peristaltic pump technology; nearest peers are Masterflex (Avantor sub.), Watson-Marlow's own history, and Verder. No large-cap pure-play peer exists. [#A]
  • Biopharm CDMO growth: Global biopharmaceutical manufacturing capacity is expanding; CDMO sector growth ~8-10% pa structural tailwind; peristaltic pumps are the fluid handling standard in single-use bioreactor systems. [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: Single-use bioreactor penetration in biopharm manufacturing is still <50% globally; shift from stainless steel to single-use is a multi-year WM growth driver.
  • Competitive intensity: Watson-Marlow's regulatory validation lock-in creates very high switching costs; competition tends to emerge at the new installation stage, not in aftermarket. [#J]

Electric Thermal Solutions Industry

  • Market structure: Fragmented but consolidating; top 3 players are nVent Electric (~$10bn cap), Thermon Group (~$0.9bn), and Spirax ETS (~24% of £4.5bn group). Market growing at ~5-7% pa driven by decarbonisation and near-shoring of industrial infrastructure. [#A]
  • ETS competitive dynamic: nVent has scale and digital monitoring capabilities; Thermon is pure-play with OEM relationships; Spirax ETS differentiator is direct industrial sales channel and STS cross-sell. [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: ❌ ETS is currently the weakest positioned of the three divisions on margin (well below 20% target) and faces well-capitalised competitors in nVent who have already solved the scaling problem.
  • Decarbonisation tailwind: Industrial electrification is a regulatory mandate in EU (EU ETS carbon pricing); steam-to-electric conversion is real but slower than consensus assumed in 2020-2022. [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: Spirax's "raising steam electrically" proposition (replacing gas-fired steam boilers with electric boilers) is a legitimate large addressable market but customer payback periods remain challenged at current European electricity prices.

Macro / Non-Macro Change Factors

  • Non-macro (structural positive): EU industrial decarbonisation mandates; biopharm capacity expansion; near-shoring of manufacturing creating new industrial plant demand. [#A]
  • Macro (cyclical sensitivity): Global industrial production growth is SPX's primary macro driver; company guides to "well above IP" organic revenue growth; FY2025 IP was ~1.5-2%; SPX delivered 5% organic — beat sustained. [#A]
  • Iran/geopolitical context (Richard's current risk framing): SPX has limited MENA exposure; ~43% EMEA (including UK, Europe); food/pharma/water end markets are among the most resilient to geopolitical disruption. SPX is well-suited to current risk-off framework. [#J]

§4 BB#2 Foundation Quality

Verdict: SPX is a high-BB#2 business — above peer median on operator quality, moat durability, and value chain positioning — but ROIC compression (12-13% vs historical 17-18%) and ETS margin lag prevent a top-decile rating at this stage of transition. [#J]

  • Sub-bullet: Finance/M&A background implies strong capital allocation discipline and shareholder communication skills; weaker signal on engineering product innovation and R&D direction.
  • Sub-bullet: De Beers CFO experience = managing a natural resource business through commodity cycles and ESG transitions; relevant to Spirax's steam-to-electric narrative management.
  • Strategic direction: Three-priority framework (organisational fitness → operational excellence → commercial excellence) is logical sequencing for a post-growth-deceleration reset. [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: Restructuring delivered £40m annualised savings; reinvested in salesforce (+headcount), digital customer connectivity, and NPD — capital allocation is directionally correct.
  • Sub-bullet: Medium-term margin targets (STS 23.5%, ETS 20%, WM 30%+, group 22-23%) are credible but require 2-4 years to achieve; no short-term EPS catalyst from targets alone.

§4.2 Advantaged Business + SRCA (MOAT + GENERATE)

Verdict: Spirax has a genuine, durable moat in STS and WM — above peer median. The moat in ETS is nascent and unproven. ROIC is currently below historical peak but the trajectory (restructuring savings + margin expansion) supports recovery toward 15%+ by FY2027. [#J]

MOAT — Competitive Advantages

  • STS: Installed-base aftermarket moat (STRONG): ~85% of group revenues are recurring; STS steam trap installed base is the most geographically diverse in the industry; Spirax field engineers are embedded with customers. [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: Steam trap replacement is non-discretionary (regulatory/energy compliance); Spirax's ~180-country direct distribution is a structural barrier to any new entrant.
  • Sub-bullet: ⚡ RARE: STS is one of the few industrial businesses globally where the competitor (Armstrong International) has been private and non-aggressive for decades — near-monopoly in a £5-10bn addressable market. Exceptional competitive position relative to all listed peers.
  • WM: Regulatory validation lock-in (STRONG): FDA/EMA-validated peristaltic pump tubing cannot be substituted without ~12-24 months re-validation; creates multi-year aftermarket lock-in per installation. [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: This mechanism is more powerful than typical switching-cost moats because the lock-in is externally enforced (regulators), not just organisational inertia.
  • ETS: Channel + cross-sell advantage (EMERGING): Spirax ETS can leverage STS direct sales force (4,000+ field engineers) to cross-sell heat tracing to existing steam customers; no competitor has this channel. [#J]
  • Sub-bullet: This is an untested thesis — cross-sell execution requires salesforce training and incentive alignment that is still in progress.

Pricing Power (CQ-A2 explicit — see §4.6)

  • Evidence of pricing power: Group held adjusted operating margin at ~20-21% through 2022-2024 inflation cycle despite input cost inflation; organic drop-through supported by pricing discipline. [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: Gross margin ~76% is top-decile vs specialty industrials; sustained at this level through 2021-2024 cycle = positive pricing power signal.
  • Sub-bullet: STS pricing discipline is easier to maintain given near-monopoly structure; WM pricing is more competitive (biopharm procurement teams are sophisticated buyers).
  • Inflation cycle result: FY2022-FY2023 saw cost inflation pressures (energy, logistics, labour) absorbed without margin collapse; FY2024 adjusted margin ~20.1% is roughly flat vs FY2023 ~21.8% on lower revenues — acceptable but not exceptional preservation. [#A]

GENERATE — Returns Profile

  • ROIC trajectory: ROIC peaked ~17-18% pre-pandemic (FY2019-FY2020); declined to ~12.8% (FY2024) driven by WM destocking demand loss + ETS ramp investment + China project weakness. [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: FY2025 ROIC: ~12-13% (H1 2025 ROIC data: company guided ROIC improvement as restructuring savings flow through).
  • Sub-bullet: Medium-term ROIC recovery toward 15%+ is the core bull case; requires all three divisions to reach margin targets simultaneously.
  • Capital allocation discipline: £40m restructuring savings reinvested in growth (not returned); M&A focus is bolt-on (last major deal was Thermocoax acquisition ~2019); leverage target 1.0-2.0x EBITDA. [#A]
  • ❌ ROIC below WACC on some estimates: GuruFocus ROIC 9.55% vs WACC 10.75% ; this is a bear-case data point that warrants monitoring. Cause is ETS ramp drag + WM volume loss. [#A]

§4.3 Value Chain — Demand Side and Competition

Verdict: Demand side is structurally attractive — diversified, non-cyclical end markets, global installed base, and multiple independent growth drivers (biopharm, electrification, energy efficiency). Competition is benign in STS, moderate in WM, and intensifying in ETS. [#J]

Demand Side

  • End-market mix (group level): ~50% of revenues to non-cyclical sectors: food/beverage + pharma/biotech + healthcare + water/wastewater. ~50% to more cyclical: chemical, oil & gas, power, semiconductor, manufacturing. [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: Non-cyclical ~50% is above peer median for UK-listed specialty industrials; provides earnings floor in industrial downturns.
  • Geographic split: EMEA 43%, Americas 36%, Asia-Pacific 21%. Asia-Pacific driven by STS; Americas strength in ETS (Heat Trace). [#I]
  • Sub-bullet: ❌ China ~20% of STS revenue is a top-5 geographic dependency; China large project orders are lumpy and correlated with Chinese government industrial policy.
  • Customer concentration: No single customer >5% of revenue; customer base spans >200,000 industrial sites globally. No customer concentration risk. [#A]
  • Demand drivers by division:
  • STS: MRO (maintenance-driven, acyclical) + large projects (cyclical, China-sensitive) + energy efficiency upgrades (regulatory-driven)
  • WM: Biopharm capacity expansion + single-use bioreactor adoption + food safety regulations
  • ETS: Industrial electrification mandates + semiconductor capex + datacentre thermal management + nuclear/aerospace & defence

Competition

  • STS: Armstrong International (private, US-based) is the only meaningful global competitor; regional players exist but no large-cap peer. Spirax has >50% global share in steam trap supply and service. [#A]
  • WM: Masterflex (Avantor) and Verder Group are meaningful competitors; Watson-Marlow retains regulatory lock-in advantage in biopharm. Competition is at new-installation stage. [#A]
  • ETS: nVent Electric (strong, well-capitalised, digital-first), Thermon Group (pure-play, OEM relationships), BARTEC (private); Spirax is mid-sized in a more competitive field. [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: ❌ nVent's January 2025 Elexant 3500i launch and Thermon's AI-powered heat management system (Feb 2025) signal ETS peers are investing in digital monitoring — an area where Spirax ETS is behind.

§4.4 Industry Structure (Concentration)

Verdict: STS operates in a highly concentrated market (effective duopoly with private competitor); WM is a niche near-monopoly in biopharm peristaltics; ETS is more fragmented and competitive. Group-level structure is favourable but becoming more competitive at the margin as ETS grows as a share of revenue. [#J]

  • STS market structure: Near-monopoly in steam trap service + supply globally; Spirax has been the market leader for 60+ years. Industry concentration is HIGH = pricing power + moat durability. [#A]
  • WM market structure: Niche near-monopoly in peristaltic pumps for regulated markets; market is small enough (global peristaltic pump market ~£2-3bn) that large-cap entrants are not economically rational. [#A]
  • ETS market structure: Fragmented globally; top 3 players have <40% combined market share in industrial heat tracing; low concentration = moderate pricing power. [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: As ETS grows from 24% to potentially 30%+ of group revenue, the group's aggregate industry concentration advantage will dilute somewhat.
  • Sceptical: The long-term structural bull case (steam dominance) has a terminal question: what % of Spirax Group's revenue will come from steam-related activities in FY2035? If ETS + WM combined reach 60%, the STS moat argument becomes less load-bearing for group valuation. [#J]

§4.5 Long-Term Growth + Paradigm Fit

Verdict: Spirax's paradigm fit is above average — it benefits from decarbonisation, biopharmaceutical growth, and energy efficiency megatrends. The steam franchise's long-term growth will decelerate but not collapse. The group's strategic pivot is directionally correct but execution risk is non-trivial. [#J]

  • LT growth framework: Management guides to "well above IP" organic revenue growth; historical IP outperformance of 3-5ppts is the track record. FY2025 delivered 5% organic vs ~1.5-2% global IP = within historical band. [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: FY2026 guidance: continued organic growth and margin progress; no specific revenue growth quantum guided.
  • Paradigm fit — decarbonisation: ETS is directly aligned; EU carbon pricing creates economic case for electrifying steam heating; Spirax's "steam-to-electric" conversion narrative is real but timelines are longer than 2020-2022 consensus expected. [#A]
  • Paradigm fit — biopharm: WM is directly aligned with global biologics manufacturing expansion; GLP-1 drug manufacturing boom (Novo Nordisk, Eli Lilly) creates near-term incremental demand for peristaltic pumping in fill-and-finish operations. [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: ⚡ RARE: GLP-1 manufacturing capacity buildout (2024-2028) is one of the largest biopharmaceutical capex cycles in history; WM's peristaltic pumps are used in fill-and-finish; this is a multi-year volume tailwind not yet fully priced into consensus estimates.
  • Paradigm fit — energy efficiency: STS energy efficiency upgrades (smart steam traps, optimised steam systems) benefit from rising energy costs and EU industrial efficiency mandates. [#A]
  • Steam long-term decline risk: Steam remains the dominant process heating medium in food/pharma/chemical globally; EIA and IEA projections show industrial steam demand declining <1% pa through 2035 in developed markets — a manageable decline, not a cliff. [#A]

§4.6 Sub-Cohort A CQ Answers (Explicit)

CQ-A1: Aftermarket/service vs new equipment mix — trend over 5 years

  • Answer: ~85% of group revenues are described by management as "recurring maintenance and small improvements to existing customer systems." This is the broadest definition and includes MRO, small upgrade projects, and recurring consumables (WM tubing). [#I]
  • Sub-bullet: STS-specific: ~50% estimated as pure MRO/aftermarket (steam trap replacement, spare parts, service); large project orders are the balance. STS large projects are the most cyclically volatile revenue line.
  • Sub-bullet: WM-specific: tubing replacement ~40-50% of WM revenue (est.); hardware/equipment is higher at initial installation but recurring tubing is the structural recurring element.
  • Sub-bullet: ETS-specific: currently projects-heavy; aftermarket developing as installed base grows; less mature recurring model than STS or WM.
  • Trend: STS as % of group revenue declined from ~80% (10 years ago) to ~52% (FY2024); WM+ETS grew. Net effect: group-level aftermarket % has been broadly stable as WM's high-recurring consumables offset ETS's lower recurring profile.
  • Peer anchor: Roper Technologies ~90% recurring revenue; SPX's ~85% is strong but not top-decile. IMI plc has ~35-40% aftermarket — SPX is substantially superior. [#J]

CQ-A2: Pricing power — 2021-23 inflation cycle gross margin trajectory

  • Answer: SPX demonstrated pricing power through the 2021-24 inflation cycle; adjusted operating margins were broadly held. Gross margin ~76% is top-decile for specialty industrials. [#A]
  • Sub-bullet: FY2021 adj. OP margin ~25%+ (pre-WM destocking, pre-China weakness). FY2022-FY2023: margins compressed as cost inflation hit and WM destocking volume fell. FY2024: ~20.1%. FY2025: 20.0% (flat but organic +30bps).
  • Sub-bullet: The margin compression was primarily volume-driven (WM biopharm destocking lost volumes) + China large project loss, NOT pricing failure. STS maintained pricing discipline throughout; pricing offset cost inflation in STS.
  • Sub-bullet: WM pricing is harder to hold when volumes are falling — but management maintained WM margins better than a pure volume-leverage analysis would predict, suggesting some pricing stickiness.
  • Verdict: Pricing power in STS is strong (near-monopoly pricing); WM pricing is above average (regulatory lock-in); ETS pricing is moderate (more competitive market). Overall: positive pricing power signal, but not exceptional during this specific inflationary episode because volume loss confounds the margin picture. [#J]

CQ-A3: Electrification/decarbonisation risk to installed base + management response

  • Answer: The electrification risk to STS is real but long-duration; management's ETS pivot is the concrete response. [#J]
  • Sub-bullet: Risk: industrial customers could replace steam heating systems with direct electric heating over time; this would erode STS's installed base growth and eventually MRO. However, steam is embedded in food processing, pharmaceutical sterilisation, and district heating in ways that direct electric cannot easily substitute in <10-15 years.
  • Sub-bullet: Management response — ETS strategic build: ETS has grown from ~15% to ~24% of group revenue since 2018; medium-term target is 20% adjusted operating margin; Spirax is explicitly positioning ETS as the decarbonisation solution for its existing STS customers.
  • Sub-bullet: Concrete ETS actions: Vulcanic acquisition (electric process heating); datacentre OEM contract win (FY2025); semiconductor equipment heating growth; dedicated heat trace sales team restructured (FY2025).
  • Sub-bullet: The strategy is coherent — Spirax is trying to "own the transition" by being both the steam incumbent and the electric alternative. The risk is that this hedge is expensive (ETS is margin-dilutive) and that larger players (nVent) out-execute on electric at scale. [#J]

CQ-A4: CEO quality signal — Nimesh Patel

  • Answer: See §4.1 in full. Summary: above-average for this phase (restructuring + capital discipline), positive signals on delivery (£40m savings on time), uncertain signals on long-term product/engineering direction given finance background. Not a founder-type visionary; is a professional manager. [#J]
  • Sub-bullet: Patel became CEO January 2024 (not November 2021 as sometimes cited — he was CFO from 2020-2023; CEO from Jan 2024).
  • Sub-bullet: Analyst read is positive post-FY2025 results; 16 of 27 covering analysts submitting estimates; consensus target ~23% above market price.
  • Sub-bullet: Key question: will Patel pursue ETS-accretive M&A (building scale) or stay organic? His M&A/finance background suggests M&A is possible; SPX's leverage at 1.5x EBITDA gives capacity. [#J]

CQ-A5: Geographic and end-market concentration

  • Answer: Geographic mix is well-diversified; no single country >20% of revenue; largest dependency is China (~20% of STS). End-market mix is ~50% non-cyclical. [#A]

  • Sub-bullet: China exposure is a top-5 risk: weakness in Chinese large project orders drove STS revenue headwinds in 2023-2024; MRO showed resilience. China is correlated with Chinese government industrial capex policy.
