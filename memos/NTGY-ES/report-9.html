§2 — Quality of Earnings

Quality verdict: Mixed-but-improving — regulated segment earnings are high-quality and cash-generative; supply/commodity segment has injected episodic low-quality noise; adjustment gap is persistent but narrowing [#J]

One-off impact: Restructuring and impairment charges recur in every reporting year of L3Y — FY22 ~€150m, FY23 ~€90m excluded gain + impairment reversal offsetting, FY24 ~€170m combined; treating these as non-recurring is analytically aggressive [#A]

  • Recurring vs one-off: If L3Y restructuring charges (~€410m aggregate) were treated as recurring, adjusted EPS would be ~€0.10–0.15 lower per year, reducing the FY24 adjusted figure to ~€2.05–2.10 vs reported €2.20
  • Non-GAAP gap: The reported-to-adjusted EBITDA gap averages ~€200–250m per year; gap has not closed materially despite "restructuring completing" language in CMD presentations

Adjustments trend: Adjustment items have shifted in character — FY22 dominated by supply-side commodity hedging losses and restructuring; FY23–24 shifted toward LatAm impairments and minor restructuring; new category of adjustment each year is a yellow flag [#A]

NWC tailwind: FY2023 working capital was a significant tailwind (~€150m) — payables extended as gas procurement volumes fell; this benefit is likely non-recurring and partially masks OCF quality [#A]

  • Receivables turn: Spanish regulated receivables are structurally stable (tariff-deficit mechanism); LatAm receivables show some elongation in 2023–24 (FX-related delays in Colombia and Chile)
  • NWC headwind: H1 2024 showed partial NWC reversal as payables normalised; OCF modestly below consensus in H1 2024

Cash conversion: OCF/adjusted EBITDA averages ~47–52% across L3Y — materially depressed by heavy regulated CapEx (capitalised, not expensed) and cash interest; free cash flow (post-CapEx, pre-dividend) averages ~€0.8–1.1bn per year, tight relative to ~€1.0bn annual dividend commitment [#A]

❌ FCF coverage of dividend: approximately 0.9–1.1x across L3Y — thin by European utility norms (typical regulated utility peer: 1.2–1.5x FCF dividend cover); any EBITDA softness or CapEx overshoot threatens the dividend without incremental leverage [Cross-ref: §8]

OCF vs NI: OCF conversion vs adjusted NI is more reasonable at ~130–150% (D&A add-back on regulated asset base is genuine and large; the "accounting" is clean at the NI level), so the quality concern is leverage/CapEx, not P&L manipulation [#I]

D&A trend: D&A growing at ~3–5% per year in line with regulated asset base expansion; no evidence of D&A manipulation; amortisation of intangibles from LatAm acquisitions adds ~€80–100m per year — this is a genuine economic cost often buried in the EBITDA-adjusted figure [#A]

  • Aggressive accounting signal: Naturgy excludes amortisation of acquisition-related intangibles from adjusted EBITDA — industry-standard but inflates adjusted metric vs true economic return on invested capital; ~€80–100m annual exclusion

Conservative accounting signal: Regulated asset impairment tests use WACC rates anchored to Spanish/LatAm regulatory returns; no evidence of overly aggressive carrying values; LatAm impairments in FY24 suggest management is not sandbagging impairment recognition

Sceptical bullet — §2: Bearish read on quality: the FY23 NWC tailwind (~€150m) and recurring restructuring exclusions (~€150m/year) together represent ~€300m of EBITDA "support" that may not be available in FY25+; if stripped out, true run-rate EBITDA is closer to €4.0–4.1bn — barely above FY22 levels, implying zero real EBITDA growth after quality adjustment [#A]

§3 — Revenue vs Profit Growth (Margin Sustainability)

Sustainability verdict: Margin expansion is partially structural (networks CapEx yielding regulated returns, mix shifting toward higher-margin regulated assets) but partially cyclical (supply margin normalisation post-2022 spike creates a favourable base that will not recur) [#J]

Revenue CAGR: ~-5% nominal L3Y (FY22→FY24) driven entirely by gas commodity deflation in supply — underlying regulated revenues grew ~4–5% annually; this revenue/EBITDA decoupling is analytically important [#A]

  • Mix shift driver: Regulated networks now represent ~65–70% of EBITDA vs ~55–60% in FY21; structural mix improvement toward more-predictable earnings

Profit CAGR: EBITDA ~+3.5–4% adjusted CAGR vs revenue -5% nominal — massive apparent operating leverage is entirely a supply-segment commodity base-effect, not genuine operational scaling [#J]

  • Operating leverage caveat: Strip out supply segment; networks EBITDA CAGR is ~+5–6% per year — genuine, regulated-return-driven, but not dramatic

Gross margin: Expanding from ~24–25% (FY22) to ~27–28% (FY24) — reflects supply-segment gross margin normalisation (high-revenue/thin-margin gas pass-through declining) mixed with networks growing share; not a pricing-power story but a mix story [#A]

EBITDA margin: +280bps over L3Y (14.1% → 16.9%) — most of which is explained by supply-segment revenue deflating without proportional EBITDA loss; genuine network-level EBITDA margins are structurally stable at ~55–60% of regulated revenue [#I]

Cost cut driver: Headcount reduction programme delivered €150–200m annual cost savings by FY23–24, contributing genuine operating leverage; ~€80m incremental savings still targeted per CMD 2023 but diminishing returns visible [#A]

  • Scale driver: Spanish gas distribution network CapEx (€1.0–1.2bn/year) translates into regulated RAB growth ~4–5%/year — the most sustainable margin driver

Sustainability: Networks margin expansion is structural and will persist as long as Spanish regulatory framework is intact; supply-segment margin improvement from normalisation is a base-effect that will not continue expanding [#J]

  • Risk: Spanish regulatory reset in 2026 (CNMC rate review) could modify the regulated return on networks; 2017 and 2020 reviews were modestly favourable; outcome of 2026 review is uncertain

Sceptical bullet — §3: Bearish read: the 280bps EBITDA margin expansion flatters the underlying business; adjusting for supply mix-effect, genuine margin expansion is ~100–120bps over L3Y; supply margins cannot expand further from current levels without assuming a new commodity spike — exactly the scenario investors should not count on [#A]

§4 — Seasonal Patterns

Seasonality verdict: Pronounced H2-over-H1 seasonality, structurally stable, driven by winter gas demand and tariff accruals — consistent across all three L3Y years [#J]

H1 pattern: H1 typically generates ~47–49% of FY EBITDA; lower gas volumes in shoulder season (Apr–Jun) weigh on supply-segment contribution; networks contribution is relatively even [#I]

H2 pattern: H2 generates ~51–53% of FY EBITDA; winter gas demand (Oct–Dec) boosts supply segment; colder years increase H2 weighting; FY22 H2 was atypically strong due to commodity price spike [#J]

  • Strongest quarter (approximated): Q4 (Oct–Dec within H2) is consistently the peak quarter on gas delivery and cold-weather-driven demand
  • Weakest quarter (approximated): Q2 (Apr–Jun) is the shoulder season trough

Seasonality direction: Not changing materially; the shift toward regulated networks (more even revenue profile) is gradually moderating the H1/H2 split by ~50bps per year [#I]

  • Cause — weather: Winter demand-driven gas distribution; Spanish and LatAm (winter inversion in Southern Hemisphere adds mild counter-cyclicality) partially offsets
  • Cause — production: Generation assets have higher utilisation in winter peak; marginal but relevant for EBIT

Cause — M&A timing: No material M&A in L3Y period; prior LatAm acquisitions (2000s–2010s) fully consolidated; no seasonal distortion from deal timing [#I]

Seasonality changing: LatAm business adds Southern Hemisphere winter (Jun–Aug) counter-cyclicality; growing LatAm EBITDA share (~25–30% of group) provides mild natural hedge; this is a gradual diversification benefit, not a structural shift [#A]

Sceptical bullet — §4: Bearish read on seasonality: the H2 weighting creates asymmetric forecasting risk — a mild winter or gas demand shortfall in Nov–Dec materially clips FY EBITDA with limited ability to recover in H1; FY22's atypically strong H2 (commodity spike) sets a flattering prior-year comparison that distorted FY23 growth optics [#A]

§8 — Forward Plausibility

Forward plausibility verdict: L3Y track record does NOT mechanically support a 25% IRR from earnings growth alone; the forward thesis depends on valuation re-rating + dividend yield (~5–6%) + modest growth (~4–5% EPS) combining to reach the hurdle [#J]

Track-record support: L3Y adjusted EPS CAGR ~3–4% annualised (€1.85→€2.20 over 3 years); this supports a total return of ~9–11% at current valuations (5–6% yield + 4–5% EPS growth) — well short of 25% IRR without re-rating [#J]

  • Required CAGR: A 25% IRR over 18 months from current entry implies either ~15–18% EPS compounding + no re-rating, or ~5% EPS growth + ~18% P/E expansion, or some combination
  • Achievable from L3Y: The ~5% EPS growth component is achievable based on L3Y evidence; the re-rating component is the thesis — not earnings-driven but event-driven (privatisation, CMD re-rate)

Forward EPS implied: Consensus FY25E EPS ~€2.35–2.45; implies ~7–11% EPS growth from FY24 — at the high end of L3Y run-rate but plausible if Spanish regulated tariff reset in 2025 is favourable and LatAm FX stabilises [#A]

Acceleration required: Modest EPS acceleration from ~3–4% (L3Y) to ~7–10% (FY25E) — possible but depends on two exogenous variables (tariff reset, FX); neither is within management control [#J]

Margin runway: EBITDA margin runway from here is limited — the supply-segment mix shift is largely played out (already ~65–70% networks); incremental margin is 50–100bps per year from Spanish RAB growth — real but not transformative [#A]

Quality runway: NWC discipline is adequate; the FY23 NWC tailwind (~€150m) has partially reversed but not fully; FCF dividend coverage is thin (0.9–1.1x) and represents the binding constraint [#A]

Inflection plausibility: The most credible inflection driver is a privatisation/delisting at a premium (CriteriaCaixa + IFM/Taqa scenario) which would deliver the re-rating component of the IRR in a single event; this is optionality, not earnings-driven [#I]

  • Deceleration acceptable? If IRR thesis is 50% earnings, 50% re-rating/event, then decelerating EPS growth to 3–4% is acceptable — the dividend yield and optionality compensate

Sceptical bullet — §8: Bearish read: if no privatisation/M&A event materialises in 18–24 months, the total return from organic EPS growth + dividend is approximately 9–11% — barely above European utility peer returns and well below the 25% IRR hurdle; the forward thesis is structurally event-dependent, not earnings-compound-dependent; a pure earnings-driven entry into NTGY-ES at current valuations does not pass Richard's screen [#A]

§9 — Sceptical Synthesis
