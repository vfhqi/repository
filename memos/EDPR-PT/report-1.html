§1 — General (Description, Financials, Peer Comparison)

Net signal: EDPR is a large-cap European renewable IPP (~EUR 10bn market cap, ~EUR 20bn EV) with strong revenue predictability from contracted portfolio, but ROIC/ROCE structurally below framework thresholds and FCF suppressed by an aggressive growth capex cycle [#J]

Description: EDPR is a global pure-play renewable energy Independent Power Producer *, generating and selling electricity from wind (onshore + offshore), solar PV, and hydro assets, operating across 4 continents [#I]
- Sub: Geographies include EU (Spain, Portugal, France, Romania, Poland, Italy, Germany), US, Brazil, Canada, Australia, and selected emerging market positions
- Sub: Business model is project-development + own-operate-for-contracted-life; asset rotation (sell minority stakes, crystallise value) is a deliberate secondary revenue stream**

Strategic objective: EDPR's stated goal is to reach ~50 GW of installed capacity by 2030 from ~28 GW in 2024, funded through a combination of internal FCF, asset rotation proceeds, and EDP Group equity support [#I]
- Sub: Priorities ordered: build-out of onshore wind and solar in core markets; selective offshore wind development (offshore materially de-risked vs Orsted post-2022 sector shocks); battery storage bolt-on
- Sub: Asset rotation target of EUR 0.9-1.2bn pa in capital gains is embedded in the plan — without it, EBITDA and EPS guidance would be unachievable at disclosed capex levels

Organisation: Structured as a holding company with regional operating subsidiaries — EDPR North America, EDPR Europe, EDPR South America, and EDPR Asia Pacific [#I]
- Sub: Each region has its own development pipeline, O&M function, and PPA origination capability; corporate centre holds shared procurement, treasury, and ESG functions
- Sub: Parent EDP controls 71.73% of shares; China Three Gorges holds ~22% of EDP, meaning through-chain, CTG has material indirect influence on EDPR capital allocation

Listing: Euronext Lisbon (primary); Euronext Madrid (secondary) [#I]
- Sub: Reporting currency EUR; functional currencies include USD (North America), BRL (Brazil), PLN (Poland), adding meaningful translation FX noise to reported figures
- Sub: EDPR market cap ~EUR 8-11bn depending on period (2024 range); EV ~EUR 18-22bn when including net project-level and corporate debt

§2 — Company Details (Revenue Model, Business Model, Recent Delivery, Internal Change)

Net signal: Revenue model is high-quality contracted with long-duration visibility, but moat is infrastructure-type rather than franchise-type — limited pricing power, no recurring software-style loyalty, and execution risk sits in development pipeline conversion rates [#J]

Revenue source: Electricity generation revenues (tariff/PPA/merchant price × MWh produced) are ~85-90% of total; asset rotation gains contribute ~10-15% of EBITDA in most years [#I]
- Sub: PPA/regulated/CfD revenues ~70-75% of electricity revenues — weighted average contract life ~13-15 years per management (CMD 2023 disclosure)
- Sub: Merchant exposure ~25-30% of electricity revenues — primarily Spain spot market and US merchant tail exposure; this drove the FY2022 spike and subsequent FY2023 reversal

Revenue mix — geographic: North America ~45% of EBITDA; Europe ~45%; South America/other ~10% — US is the single largest geography by EBITDA contribution [#I]
- Sub: US exposure (primarily wind and solar in Texas, Midwest, Northeast) creates structural USD functional currency — EDPR reports in EUR, so USD/EUR moves are directly translated; 2022 dollar strength added ~5% tailwind to reported EBITDA
- Sub: European footprint: Spain (~20% of group EBITDA), Portugal (~5%), France (~5%), Poland/Romania/other (~15%) — eastern Europe is a growth priority

End-market: Contracted offtakers are utilities, industrial corporates, hyperscalers (data centres), and government/regulated entities — diversified by type but all electricity buyers [#I]
- Sub: US corporate PPA market is the key growth engine: tech companies (Microsoft, Amazon, Google, Meta) have signed multi-GW commitments to renewable energy, and EDPR has a pipeline of contracted capacity to these counterparties
- Sub: Data centre electricity demand is a structural tailwind with no credible substitute for renewables where ESG commitments are binding — this is the most durable demand driver over N36M

Predictability: Revenue predictability is structurally high for contracted portion, low for merchant tail [#A]
- Sub: Wind/solar generation volume is statistically predictable at portfolio level (P50 vs actuals within ~5-8% in most years) — single-year weather variance is real but multi-year averages out
- Sub: Price predictability degrades rapidly in the merchant tail — Spanish market power prices swung from EUR 170/MWh peak in 2022 to EUR 60-80/MWh in 2023-2024

Competitive advantage — contracted revenue lock-in: Long-dated PPAs (10-20 year contracts) create strong revenue lock-in once signed — counterparty substitution effectively impossible without breaking contract [#A]
- Sub: Switching costs for offtakers are low (they could theoretically buy spot), but given ESG mandates and price certainty benefits, corporates increasingly prefer long PPAs — making initial signing a quasi-lock-in
- Sub: EDPR benefits from being a counterparty with EDP Group balance sheet support — larger corporates prefer to sign with investment-grade entities; smaller developers compete less effectively on credit quality

Scale moat — procurement: EDPR's scale gives it top-tier procurement leverage with turbine OEMs (Vestas, Siemens Gamesa, GE Vernova) — multi-year, multi-GW framework agreements result in estimated 10-15% unit cost savings vs smaller developers [#A]
- Sub: This procurement advantage has become more critical post-2021 as supply chains tightened; EDPR secured long-term turbine supply agreements that smaller players couldn't access
- Sub: Solar PV panel procurement similarly benefits from scale — though the commodity nature of panels limits the advantage relative to wind turbine procurement

Cyclicality: Revenue is not economically cyclical in the traditional sense — electricity consumption is relatively GDP-inelastic, and contracted revenues are insulated from spot price cycles [#A]
- Sub: The key cyclicality is interest rate sensitivity: project NPVs are inversely correlated with risk-free rates; the 2022-2024 rate cycle materially compressed the value of long-dated contracted cash flows, driving EDPR's 50%+ share price decline from 2021 peak
- Sub: Capex cycle timing is also a source of cyclicality — EPC cost inflation (2021-2024) compressed project IRRs from ~8-9% to ~6-7% before recovering modestly

Recent delivery — FY2021-FY2023: EDPR delivered strong capacity additions — grew installed from ~12 GW (2019) to ~28 GW (2024), broadly on track with its prior strategic plan [#A]
- Sub: FY2021-FY2022 benefited from exceptional energy prices, generating asset rotation gains well above plan; management used this window to accelerate pipeline development
- Sub: FY2023 was operationally solid (capacity additions ~3.5 GW) but financially disappointing vs FY2022 on reported EBITDA due to energy price normalisation — causing SS downward revisions

Recent delivery — execution risks: Two material execution misses: onshore wind permitting delays in France and Poland (2022-2023), and offshore wind programme scale-back (EDPR exited several offshore pre-development agreements in line with sector-wide pullback) [#A]
- Sub: France onshore permitting backlogs remain a sector-wide issue — EDPR's France EBITDA growth has underperformed internal plan by ~15-20%
- Sub: Offshore retrenchment (cancellation of several North Sea and US Atlantic Coast pre-development positions) was prudent relative to Orsted's more aggressive commitments, but signals EDPR's risk appetite has moderated

Internal change — leadership: Miguel Stilwell d'Andrade became CEO of both EDP and EDPR in August 2020, replacing António Mexia [#I]
- Sub: Stilwell is a finance-track executive (MIT-trained, ex-McKinsey, 20+ years within EDP Group) — technically strong but less of the "animal CEO" archetype and more of a capital allocator/operator profile
- Sub: No externally hired C-suite since his tenure began — team is largely internal EDP veterans, which ensures execution continuity but limits external strategic challenge

Internal change — strategy shift: CMD 2023 (November 2023) was the key strategic reset — reduced offshore ambition, increased solar emphasis, maintained onshore wind focus [#I]
- Sub: Solar target raised to ~15 GW by 2030 (from prior ~10 GW) — solar has faster build cycles, lower capital intensity per MW, and currently higher project IRRs than offshore wind
- Sub: Asset rotation target maintained at EUR 0.9-1.2bn pa — this is non-negotiable for plan delivery; if infrastructure fund appetite for renewable assets softens, guidance will be missed

Sceptical counter-read: The apparent moat — long PPAs, scale procurement, contracted revenues — is an infrastructure moat, not a franchise moat [#J]
- Sub: EDPR has zero pricing power on contracted revenues (PPAs are fixed price) and limited ability to raise prices on renewals; when long contracts expire, the electricity price they can negotiate will be set by the prevailing market/renewable energy market structure
- Sub: The scale procurement advantage erodes if turbine OEMs consolidate or if smaller developers achieve similar scale through consolidation — and the track record of beating plan on permitting/development delivery has been inconsistent

- Sub: Spain's retroactive regulatory interventions (2013-era tariff cuts) remain a historical risk factor; no similar retroactive intervention has occurred since, but Spain is ~20% of EDPR EBITDA and this risk is not zero

Regulatory shift — US: US IRA (Inflation Reduction Act, August 2022) is the most significant policy tailwind for EDPR's North American business [#A]

- Sub: IRA domestic content bonus (additional 10 percentage points for US-manufactured equipment) creates a modest headwind given EDPR's predominantly non-US turbine supply chain — but the base PTC benefit far outweighs this

Political shift risk — US: The 2025 Republican administration has created uncertainty around IRA implementation — potential rollback of clean energy subsidies is a medium-term political risk [#A]
- Sub: Most SS analysts (Barclays, Citi, JP Morgan, Berenberg) have assessed that full IRA repeal is unlikely (too many Republican-state construction jobs at stake), but partial modification (e.g. offshore wind exclusion) is plausible
- Sub: EDPR's US portfolio is predominantly onshore wind and solar — better positioned than offshore-heavy Orsted in the current political environment

Thematic trend — data centre demand: Hyperscaler electricity demand (Microsoft, Amazon, Google, Meta) is creating a structural new source of long-duration PPA demand across EDPR's core markets [#A]
- Sub: Microsoft's 2023-2024 renewable energy procurement pace accelerated to ~8 GW globally per year — EDPR has existing relationships with hyperscalers and active PPA negotiations in US and Spain
- Sub: This demand is particularly valuable for EDPR because hyperscalers want 24/7 dispatchable or firmed renewable supply — increasingly bundling wind/solar/storage, which favours larger developers with cross-technology portfolios

Sceptical counter-read: The regulatory tailwinds (IRA, RED III) are priced in by the market and may prove insufficient to offset the structural challenge of rising cost of capital [#J]
- Sub: The deeper structural risk is that the global renewable build-out, by design, drives down electricity prices over time (merit order effect) — EDPR's long PPAs protect current portfolio but new MW added in a low-price electricity environment will earn lower contracted prices, compressing per-MW EBITDA
- Sub: Offshore wind's commercial viability crisis (Orsted, Vattenfall contract terminations) signals the entire sector's IRR assumptions from 2018-2021 were too optimistic — this lesson likely applies to some of EDPR's own pipeline assumptions

§4.1 — Operator (BB#2 TC1: Great Operator)

Strategic ambition: EDPR's stated ambition — 50 GW by 2030, net-zero operations, EUR 3-4bn annual EBITDA by 2030 — is growth-oriented and concrete, with specific targets tied to capital deployment and asset rotation [#I]
- Sub: The ambition is congruent with the trend environment (energy transition, policy support, data centre demand) but dependent on execution of a EUR 25-30bn gross investment programme — large relative to the company's equity base
- Sub: Strategy fit is reasonable across most plausible environments (mild rate cuts support NPVs; further supply chain deflation improves IRRs) but fails in a scenario of sustained high rates + prolonged permitting delays

Strategy congruence: CMD 2023 strategy shifted meaningfully toward solar and away from offshore wind — this is a positive signal of strategic flexibility, not rigidity [#J]
- Sub: The shift tracked market signals appropriately — offshore wind IRRs collapsed 2022-2024, and EDPR moved capital toward solar (faster build, lower risk, better current IRRs); this is responsive not reckless
- Sub: The asset rotation rate (EUR 0.9-1.2bn pa target) is structurally integral to the strategy — this is not optionality but a necessity, which introduces dependency risk if infrastructure fund appetite reverses

CEO: Miguel Stilwell d'Andrade, CEO since August 2020, age ~50 [#I]
- Sub: MIT engineering + MBA background; career within EDP Group (joined ~2005); transitioned from CFO of EDP to CEO of both EDP and EDPR — capital allocation oriented, not a technical operator
- Sub: His tenure's defining action has been the strategic reorientation at CMD 2023 (offshore retreat, solar acceleration) — a defensible call with benefit of hindsight; the bear case is that this is reactive not proactive

Leadership team: Leadership team is uniformly internal EDP Group appointments — no external hires at C-suite in the 2020-2024 period [#A]
- Sub: Positive: deep technical and regulatory knowledge of renewable project development globally; continuity of execution culture
- Sub: Negative: limited external perspective; no evidence of the "hiring machine" culture (Ryanair/Lifco archetype) that characterises the best operator profiles

Culture: EDPR's organisational culture is project-execution driven — measured against MW commissioned, capacity factors, and development pipeline conversion [#A]
- Sub: Culture metrics: EDPR has consistently achieved ~90-95% of its annual capacity addition targets — above the sector median execution rate for comparable developers (~80-85% per SS analysis)
- Sub: ESG culture is genuinely embedded (EDPR is MSCI AAA-rated on ESG) but this is table stakes in the sector, not a differentiator

Guidance posture: EDPR's guidance has been directionally accurate but quantitatively optimistic in the 2021-2024 period [#A]
- Sub: CMD 2021 targets (set pre-rate cycle) were not fully achieved — EBITDA guidance missed by ~5-10% in FY2023 primarily due to energy price normalisation and offshore write-downs
- Sub: Most recent CMD 2023 guidance revision was conservative (offshore targets reduced, asset rotation targets maintained) — current guidance is more achievable than prior cycle

Track record — capital allocation: EDPR's capex allocation follows a disciplined minimum-IRR threshold (management cites 7-8% unlevered project IRR hurdle) — refusing projects below this hurdle is verifiable through the offshore exit decisions [#A]
- Sub: The offshore pullback (walking away from pre-developed positions rather than building at negative NPV) is a credible demonstration of capital discipline, contrasting with Orsted's overly committed posture
- Sub: Asset rotation has consistently generated stated targeted returns — proceeds from stake sales have exceeded book value in most transactions, implying EDPR is a price-disciplined seller

Shareholder alignment: Major shareholders (EDP 71.73%) and management have interests broadly aligned on growth but not necessarily on return of capital to minorities [#A] ❌
- Sub: Minority shareholders receive dividends but no buybacks; the growth programme benefits EDP Group (increasing EDPR's EBITDA as EDP's primary renewable vehicle) and potentially disadvantages minorities who fund dilutive equity issuance without buyback offset
- Sub: Share issuance in 2021-2022 was used to fund part of the growth programme — dilutive to existing minority shareholders in a period when the share price was elevated

Governance: Standard Euronext/CNMV governance — no material governance concerns flagged by sell-side or proxy advisors; board has independent directors and adequate audit/remuneration committees [#I]
- Sub: The 71.73% parent ownership structurally limits governance independence — related-party transaction risk (e.g. inter-company financing, procurement agreements with EDP Group entities) is real but appears within market norms

§4.2 — Advantaged Business + SRCA (BB#2 TC2)

Customer moat: Long-duration PPA contracts (average ~13-15 years remaining) provide strong contractual lock-in once signed — but zero pricing power within contract, and renewal is competitive [#A]
- Sub: Corporate PPA counterparties are increasingly sophisticated — Amazon, Microsoft, Google negotiate hard on price; EDPR's advantage is balance sheet quality and project execution certainty, not pricing leverage
- Sub: CfD structures (UK, France, Poland) provide ultimate lock-in against merchant risk — but CfDs are government-set, limiting upside pricing optionality

Production moat: EDPR has a best-in-class wind resource portfolio in Iberia and North America — land positions in Class 1 wind zones (capacity factor 35-45% onshore) are difficult to replicate as prime sites become scarce [#A]
- Sub: Portfolio average capacity factor ~30-32% (wind + solar blended) — above the global median of ~25-27% for comparable mixed renewable developers
- Sub: New entrants face significantly degraded wind resource availability in core markets — the best land has been secured by incumbents; this is a silent land bank moat

Scale moat: EDPR's global scale (~28 GW) delivers demonstrable procurement advantages: multi-GW turbine framework agreements, volume-based O&M contracts, and tax equity structuring scale [#A]
- Sub: US tax equity market requires scale — EDPR's ability to use IRA PTCs/ITCs via tax equity partnerships provides an advantage unavailable to sub-1 GW developers
- Sub: Procurement savings estimated 10-15% per MW vs smaller developers (Barclays and JP Morgan estimates) — translates directly to higher project IRRs

Regulatory moat: Permitted and consented pipeline (~10-12 GW "ready to build" or "advanced") represents a multi-year time-lag advantage over any new entrant who must start the permitting clock fresh [#A]
- Sub: Average permitting timelines in EU: 3-7 years for onshore wind; EDPR's pipeline advantage represents 3-5 years of compressed lead time for an entrant trying to replicate
- Sub: US federal/state permits, grid interconnection agreements, and transmission upgrade agreements similarly take 2-5 years to acquire — EDPR's secured queue positions are valuable

Relative scale: EDPR is #3 by installed GW among listed or separable renewable developers globally — behind Enel Green Power (within Enel, ~60 GW) and Iberdrola Renewables (~40 GW) but ahead of Orsted (~16 GW), Neoen (7 GW, now private), and most pure-play peers [#A]
- Sub: Within Europe exclusively, EDPR is top-3; in North America among European-origin developers, EDPR is top-2 (alongside Iberdrola/Avangrid)

TAM: Global renewable capacity additions target ~600-800 GW per year by 2030 (IEA Net Zero Scenario) vs ~300 GW in 2023 — the TAM is large and expanding at 2x current pace [#A]
- Sub: EDPR's 50 GW 2030 target captures <10% of even the most conservative scenario for global additions — the TAM constraint is not market size but EDPR's own execution capacity and capital availability
