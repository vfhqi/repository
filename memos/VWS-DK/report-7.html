§2 — Key Driver #1: EBIT Margin Recovery (Manufacturing)

VWS-DK's manufacturing segment EBIT margin is the single most important driver — the company has run deeply negative manufacturing margins in FY22-FY24 and the entire investment case hinges on recovery to 8-10% [#J]

  • Driver definition: Power solutions (turbine manufacturing/installation) EBIT margin; Vestas guides 8-10% EBIT margin group target for "normalised" operations [#J]

  • Sub: This segment ran at approximately -5% to -8% EBIT margin in FY22-FY24 on a cumulative basis, representing billions of euros in losses [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: 1pp EBIT margin improvement on ~€17bn revenue = ~€170m pre-tax profit; at ~900m shares diluted, ~€0.19 EPS per pp — highly convex from near-zero base [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Market will re-rate VWS-DK on forward EBIT % trajectory; a confirmed 5% EBIT exit rate would likely trigger a 20-30% share price re-rating given current near-zero earnings [#J]

  • Company guide: Management guided FY25 EBIT margin (before special items) of 4-7%, with FCF positive; longer-term 8-10% target reiterated at CMD [#A]

  • Sub: The 4-7% FY25 band is exceptionally wide — management has not narrowed guidance, which itself signals execution uncertainty [#J]

  • Management commentary: CEO Henrik Andersen has repeatedly stated the margin recovery is "on track" but has declined to narrow the range; language remains cautious [#A]

  • Track record: Management has missed margin guidance in 4 of the last 5 reporting periods; the pattern of underestimating cost headwinds (logistics, steel, warranties) is established [#J]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Guidance skews optimistic relative to delivery history; the 8-10% long-term target has been the stated goal since 2022 and has not been achieved [#J]

  • SS consensus: Sell-side median for FY25 EBIT margin is approximately 5-6%, implying consensus expects midpoint-of-range delivery; FY26 consensus is 7-8% [#A]

  • Sub: Goldman Sachs and Barclays have flagged the FY25 path as the key uncertainty; both model EBIT margin entering FY25 below the midpoint before recovering [#A]

  • Sub: Morgan Stanley and Deutsche Bank are more constructive, modelling faster margin normalisation on the assumption of lower steel/logistics costs [#A]

  • Consensus changes L12M: Modest upgrade in FY26 estimates, flat-to-down for FY25 as warranty charges have persisted longer than consensus expected [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Delivery at the top of the 8-10% band by FY26 implies EPS of approximately €1.50-1.80 vs near-zero currently — roughly 6-8x earnings uplift if fully delivered [#A]

  • Recent delivery: FY24 full-year EBIT margin improved marginally from FY23 trough, but H2 FY24 showed the first sequential improvement; this is the earliest green shoot in the recovery [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: FY22: approximately -2% group EBIT; FY23: approximately -4%; FY24: approximately -2% to 0% — the trough appears to be FY23 [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: No direct listed peer shares this depth of loss cycle; Siemens Gamesa (now private under Siemens Energy) experienced a similar but even deeper margin collapse; GE Vernova has guided positive wind margins for FY25 at low single digits — ahead of VWS-DK on recovery path [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: Steel and logistics cost normalisation since peak 2022 is a meaningful structural tailwind; spot steel prices ~40% below 2022 peaks provide cost input relief [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Residual supply chain complexity, warranty exposure on legacy contracts (pre-2022 fixed-price), and potential trade policy disruption (US tariffs on European goods) remain live risks [#J]

  • Visibility: Low-medium; the warranty provision risk is the least visible cost line — one large legacy project warranty event can swing EBIT by €100-200m [#J]

  • Bear case: FY25 warranty charge of €200-400m (consistent with FY22-FY24 pattern) pushes EBIT back negative; management credibility collapses; re-rating to trough multiple [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Legacy fixed-price contracts (2020-2022 vintage) contain unquantified remaining warranty exposure; if project commissioning defects emerge at scale, provision building repeats [#J]

  • Miss magnitude: A €300m warranty charge in FY25 would eliminate most of the guided EBIT improvement and push EPS to near-zero again; the share price downside from such an event is 30-40% given current pricing of partial recovery [#J]

  • Red flags: Company has not disclosed the remaining exposure on legacy fixed-price contracts with precision; this information gap is a yellow flag [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: What if the stated 8-10% EBIT target is structurally unachievable given VWS-DK's cost base and competitive pricing dynamics? The company has never actually achieved 8-10% EBIT in recent history; the market may be pricing a target that is aspirational rather than demonstrated [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — achievable by FY26-27 if legacy warranty exposure is contained, but the delivery track record is poor and the range is wide

§3 — Key Driver #2: Order Backlog ASP and Quality Trajectory

The average selling price embedded in the order backlog is the leading indicator for future manufacturing margins — improving ASP is the thesis-critical forward signal [#J]

  • Driver definition: Average selling price per MW in new turbine orders; VWS-DK reports order intake in MW and €m, allowing ASP calculation [#J]

  • Sub: ASP trough was approximately €0.8m/MW in 2021-2022; management has guided recovery toward €1.0-1.1m/MW in more recent vintages [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Higher ASP on new orders flows into revenue recognition 18-36 months forward; ASP improvement of €100k/MW on 10GW annual install = €1bn incremental revenue at constant volume [#A]

  • Sub: Revenue uplift at 8% EBIT margin = €80m EBIT; at current shares = ~€0.09 EPS contribution [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Market will anticipate the margin impact of higher-ASP orders entering the revenue recognition cycle; the "backlog quality" narrative is a key sell-side monitoring variable [#J]

  • Company guide: Management has confirmed ASP recovery is "ongoing" and that newer order vintages carry "improved economics"; no explicit ASP per-MW target published [#A]

  • Management commentary: Management emphasises that order selectivity has increased — VWS-DK has stated it will decline lower-margin orders; this is a behavioural change from 2020-2022 volume-maximisation [#A]

  • Track record: The volume-first strategy of 2020-2022 demonstrably destroyed margin; the stated pivot to "profitable growth" since mid-2022 is partially evidenced by the ASP improvement in reported orders [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Management's order selectivity narrative is plausible but hard to audit from external data; the actual margin per contract is not disclosed [#J]

  • SS consensus: Barclays and Berenberg have published ASP-focused notes arguing the backlog improvement is real and that €1.0-1.1m/MW is sustainable; Goldman Sachs models ASP as a key upside sensitivity [#A]

  • Sub: Sceptical brokers (including some Nordic coverage) note that competition on offshore and large onshore projects from Siemens Gamesa (Siemens Energy) and GE Vernova constrains pricing power [#A]

  • Estimated revenue impact: Order intake in FY24 was approximately 15-17GW; at €1.05m/MW vs €0.85m/MW, this represents €3bn in additional forward revenue on an annual cohort [#A]

  • Recent delivery: Q3/Q4 FY24 ASP data points appeared consistent with the €1.0-1.1m/MW range — the first multi-quarter sequence without ASP deterioration since FY22 [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: FY22: ~€0.85m/MW; FY23: ~€0.92m/MW; FY24: ~€1.0-1.05m/MW — clear upward trajectory [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: No clean listed peer for direct ASP comparison; GE Vernova reports US onshore ASP metrics that suggest pricing has also improved in the US market, providing macro corroboration for the global repricing thesis [#A]

  • Sub: Siemens Energy's CMD commentary indicated similar ASP recovery dynamics across wind contracts, lending credibility to VWS-DK's stated trajectory [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: Supply-side capacity constraint (Siemens Gamesa exits certain markets, GE Vernova focuses on US) has reduced competitive intensity in European onshore — structurally supportive of sustained ASP [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Potential re-entry of fully verticalised Chinese OEMs (CSSC Haizhuang, Goldwind, Ming Yang) into European markets via local manufacturing could reintroduce price pressure in the 2026-2028 window [#J]

  • Visibility: Medium-high for the near-term (FY25-26 order book already partially visible); lower for FY27+ where Chinese competition and interest rate dynamics become relevant [#J]

  • Bear case: ASP stabilises at €1.0-1.05m/MW rather than continuing to €1.15-1.20m/MW; the margin recovery is real but less powerful than bulls model [#J]

  • Miss scenario: A major European utility (Ørsted, RWE, Vattenfall) uses competitive tension between OEMs to push ASP back toward €0.95m/MW on a large multi-GW frame agreement [#J]

  • Miss magnitude: A €50k/MW ASP miss on 15GW of annual orders = €750m lower forward revenue; at 8% EBIT = €60m EBIT = ~€0.07 EPS — meaningful but not thesis-destroying alone [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: What if ASP improvement is concentrated in a few large European projects that are not representative of the broader order book? Headline ASP can be lifted by selective contract selection that simultaneously compresses volume growth [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — ASP recovery is evidenced by L3Y trend but ceiling at €1.1m/MW is uncertain given competitive dynamics and Chinese OEM risk

§4 — Key Driver #3: Service Segment Compounding

The service/aftermarket segment is VWS-DK's only high-quality recurring earnings stream and its compounding at ~24% EBIT margin provides the stability that makes the manufacturing recovery wait tolerable [#J]

  • Driver definition: Vestas service segment — multi-year service agreements (MSAs), parts supply, upgrades; ~€2.0-2.2bn revenue, ~24% EBIT margin [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Service revenue grows with installed base (~170GW under service globally); margin stability means each €100m of service revenue adds ~€24m EBIT = ~€0.027 EPS [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Service acts as a floor valuation; analysts apply a "service stub" valuation of €3-5bn standalone to anchor VWS-DK's sum-of-the-parts [#J]

  • Company guide: Management guided service revenue growth of mid-to-high single digit % CAGR; margin stable-to-improving toward 25%+ longer-term [#A]

  • Management commentary: Service is consistently described as the "cornerstone" of the investment case; management has invested in multi-year service contract extensions and digital performance management [#A]

  • Track record: Service has delivered consistent margin in the 22-25% band across FY21-FY24 — the one driver where historical delivery matches guidance [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Service guidance is credibly conservative; the installed base is known, contract lengths are 5-15 years, and churn is structurally low [#A]

  • SS consensus: Near-universal sell-side agreement on service quality; brokers including Berenberg, JP Morgan, and Deutsche Bank use service as the valuation anchor in SOTP models [#A]

  • Sub: Consensus service revenue for FY25 is approximately €2.1-2.3bn; FY26 approximately €2.3-2.5bn [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Service at €2.4bn / 25% margin = €600m EBIT; at ~30% tax / ~900m shares = ~€0.47 EPS from service alone — a meaningful base even before manufacturing recovery [#A]

  • Recent delivery: Service margin held above 22% through the entire FY22-FY24 manufacturing loss cycle — this is the single most impressive execution fact in VWS-DK's recent history [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: Service revenue: FY22 ~€1.8bn, FY23 ~€1.95bn, FY24 ~€2.1bn — steady 7-9% CAGR [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: Service margin of 22-25% compares very favourably vs wind peers; GE Vernova's services business runs at lower absolute margins; Siemens Energy's industrial services benchmark is similar [#A]

  • Sub: In the broader industrial aftermarket context (Atlas Copco, Alfa Laval service segments ~25-30%), VWS-DK's service margin is competitive [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: Growing global installed base (170GW under service vs ~100GW five years ago); increasing proportion of turbines entering mid-life requiring intensive maintenance [#A]

  • Macro headwind: OEM competition in service — turbine owners increasingly seek multi-vendor service contracts; VWS-DK's advantage is largest on its own proprietary turbines but third-party competition exists [#J]

  • Visibility: Very high — multi-year MSAs provide 3-5 year forward visibility on the majority of service revenue; churn rate historically <5% annually [#A]

  • Bear case: Turbine owner consolidation (Ørsted, RWE restructuring) leads to contract renegotiations at lower ASP for service; OEM commoditisation of service reduces pricing power [#J]

  • Miss scenario: A significant turbine fault on a high-penetration model (V236 Prototype issues) creates warranty obligations that reclassify service revenue as warranty cost [#J]

  • Miss magnitude: A service margin contraction from 24% to 20% on €2.2bn revenue = €88m EBIT reduction = ~€0.07 EPS — contained relative to manufacturing upside [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: What if the service margin is structurally declining as VWS-DK's fleet ages and owners use multi-vendor competition to compress prices? This risk is currently priced as negligible by consensus but could become visible in 2026-2028 [#J]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — service compounding is the most credible driver given track record, installed base visibility, and contract structure

§5 — Key Driver #4: Volume Delivery and Installation Execution

Revenue recovery requires VWS-DK to scale turbine installations from depressed FY22-FY23 levels back toward 15-18GW annually without triggering further cost overruns [#J]

  • Driver definition: Annual MW installed/commissioned; FY24 approximately 14-15GW installed; management targeting recovery toward 16-18GW by FY26 [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Volume growth amplifies the margin recovery; 1GW of incremental annual installations at 8% EBIT margin and €1.05m/MW ASP = €1.05bn revenue = €84m EBIT = ~€0.06 EPS [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Volume recovery narrative supports the "normalisation" re-rating; market will price higher confidence in target-year EPS as volume ramps [#J]

  • Company guide: FY25 deliveries guided at approximately 14-16GW; FY26 step-up implied by order backlog of ~26GW [#A]

  • Management commentary: Management has acknowledged installation bottlenecks in Germany (permitting delays) and in offshore (specialist vessel availability); these are structural constraints not immediately within VWS-DK's control [#A]

  • Track record: Volume delivery has underperformed guidance in 3 of the last 4 years; supply chain disruptions, project delays, and commissioning issues have repeatedly pushed deliveries to the back half of the year [#J]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Volume guidance tends to be back-half loaded, creating recurring H1 disappointment and H2 catch-up risk [#J]

  • SS consensus: Consensus models approximately 15-16GW for FY25 and 16-18GW for FY26; the range is wide, reflecting project execution uncertainty [#A]

  • Estimated revenue impact: FY26 at 17GW vs FY24 ~14GW = 3GW incremental; at €1.05m/MW = €3.15bn incremental revenue; at 8% EBIT = €252m incremental EBIT [#A]

  • Recent delivery: FY24 delivery was approximately in line with the lowered guidance range; this represents a stabilisation but not acceleration — the worst back-loading patterns from FY22-FY23 appear reduced [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: FY22: ~14GW, FY23: ~15GW, FY24: ~14-15GW — flat trend, not the recovery trajectory the bull case requires [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: GE Vernova guided ~8GW onshore wind for FY25 and is ahead of schedule on delivery; Siemens Gamesa's production recovery under Siemens Energy has been slower than VWS-DK [#A]

  • Sub: The industry context is mixed — GE's US-focused model avoids European permitting delays; VWS-DK's European concentration exposes it to the most permitting-constrained markets [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: European permitting reform (EU Emergency Regulation, national fast-track programmes in Germany, Denmark) should reduce 2-4 year permitting timelines over a 3-5 year horizon [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Near-term (FY25-FY26) permitting pipeline remains constrained; the permitting reform benefit is predominantly a 2027+ story [#J]

  • Visibility: Medium; order backlog provides volume guidance 18-36 months forward, but project timing remains uncertain within that window [#J]

  • Bear case: Permitting delays in Germany push 2-3GW of FY25 deliveries into FY26; FY25 volume comes in at 13-14GW; revenue guidance misses; EBIT margin recovery stalls [#J]

  • Miss scenario: A key market (Germany, UK) experiences a regulatory delay on approvals for 1-2GW of projects; revenue recognition slips; H2 FY25 weaker than expected [#J]

  • Miss magnitude: 1GW delivery miss = ~€1bn revenue; at 6% EBIT margin = €60m EBIT = ~€0.04 EPS — modest in isolation but damaging if coincident with cost overruns [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: What if the volume flat-line of FY22-FY24 is structural rather than cyclical — reflecting the saturation of VWS-DK's core European onshore market and insufficient offshore scale to replace it? This structural volume ceiling scenario is underpriced by consensus [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — volume recovery is in the backlog but execution on permitting and installation sequencing has been the persistent weak link

§6 — Key Driver #5: FCF Inflection and Balance Sheet Repair

The transition from materially negative FCF (FY22-FY24) to positive FCF (guided FY25) is a critical re-rating trigger — balance sheet risk remains elevated until FCF positive is confirmed [#J]

  • Driver definition: Free cash flow generation; VWS-DK consumed approximately €1-2bn of cash in FY22-FY23 on a cumulative basis; FY24 FCF was approximately -€0.5bn to breakeven [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: FCF inflection is not directly EPS-accretive but eliminates the risk premium the market embeds for equity dilution or distressed balance sheet; re-rating can be significant [#J]

  • Mechanism to SP: A clean FCF-positive FY25 would likely remove the last structural bear argument about balance sheet sustainability; multiple expansion of 2-4x EV/EBITDA could follow [#J]

  • Company guide: Management explicitly guided positive FCF for FY25 at the FY24 annual results; this is a firm commitment, not an aspiration [#A]

  • Management commentary: CFO has highlighted working capital release from the backlog conversion cycle as the primary driver of FCF inflection; advance payment terms on new high-ASP orders are also FCF-positive [#A]

  • Track record: FCF has been negative for 3 consecutive years; management made similar positive FCF commitments in prior years that were subsequently missed due to cost overruns [#J]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: The FCF guide for FY25 is optimistic relative to track record; however, the improvement in order economics (higher ASP, better advance payment terms) provides a more credible structural underpinning than in prior years [#J]

  • SS consensus: Consensus FCF for FY25 is approximately €0-500m positive; a wide range reflecting uncertainty on working capital timing and warranty cash outflows [#A]

  • Sub: Barclays and Goldman Sachs have flagged the FCF guide as the "key credibility test" for FY25 — if missed again, multiple compression is likely [#A]

  • Estimated re-rating impact: Clean positive FCF would likely drive a 15-25% share price re-rating independent of EBIT; the discount for cash burn uncertainty has been a persistent drag [#J]

  • Recent delivery: FY24 FCF improved materially from FY23 — the trajectory is right but the magnitude of improvement needs to accelerate in FY25 [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: FY22 FCF: approximately -€1.2bn; FY23: approximately -€1.5bn; FY24: approximately -€0.3-0.5bn — clear improvement but not yet positive [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: GE Vernova guided positive FCF from wind segment in FY25 and has a stronger balance sheet entering the recovery; VWS-DK is one cycle behind on FCF normalisation vs its closest recovery peer [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: Working capital release from the accumulated backlog of high-advance-payment new orders; supply chain cost normalisation reduces cash outflow on project execution [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Any warranty charge in FY25 is immediately FCF-negative; advance payments from customers can be clawed back if projects are delayed [#J]

  • Visibility: Low-medium; working capital movements are inherently volatile; warranty cash outflows are opaque from outside [#J]

  • Bear case: A €300-400m warranty cash outflow in FY25 reverses FCF positive; management misses the FCF commitment for the fourth consecutive year; the market re-prices VWS-DK as a structurally cash-consuming business [#J]

  • Miss scenario: H1 FY25 FCF negative on project cost overruns; company maintains full-year guidance but market sell-off on "miss the guide again" narrative [#J]

  • Miss magnitude: Missing the FCF positive guide is primarily a multiple event; at current market cap (~€14-16bn), a return to the "balance sheet risk" narrative could de-rate by 15-20% [#J]

  • Red flags: The FCF guide history is poor; management's track record on FCF timing is the weakest on any guided financial metric [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: What if the working capital improvement is a one-cycle timing effect — advance payments pulled forward on new orders — rather than a structural normalisation? The FCF inflection could be a one-year event before normalising back toward breakeven as new orders cycle through [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — FCF guide is the firmest management commitment in two years but track record of missing this metric specifically is the worst in the driver set

§7 — Key Driver #6: European Policy and Macro Tailwind

The structural demand floor for European wind is exceptionally strong — REPowerEU, national net-zero targets, and energy security mandates provide a multi-decade demand mandate that is VWS-DK's most durable macro tailwind [#J]

  • Driver definition: European energy policy (REPowerEU target: 510GW wind by 2030, up from ~220GW in 2022), national auction programmes, grid expansion investment [#J]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Policy tailwind ensures demand for 50-60GW annual European wind additions through 2030; VWS-DK with ~30-35% European onshore market share captures structural volume floor [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Demand certainty reduces discount rate on VWS-DK's future cash flows; the policy underpinning allows analysts to apply higher confidence to 2027-2030 earnings visibility [#J]

  • Company guide: Management does not explicitly guide on policy; however, forward order guidance implicitly assumes continued European policy support — the backlog of ~26GW confirms this demand floor is already partially monetised [#A]

  • Management commentary: CEO Andersen has consistently cited REPowerEU and national energy security as structural tailwinds; the public positioning has shifted from "climate transition" to "energy security" — a politically more durable framing [#A]

  • Track record: European wind installation growth has consistently outperformed IEA/BloombergNEF base-case forecasts for the past 3 years; policy over-delivery is the norm, not under-delivery [#A]

  • SS consensus: Near-universal sell-side agreement that the demand backdrop is structurally supportive; differentiation is on near-term vs long-term: bear cases focus on 2025-2026 permitting constraints, not on 2028+ demand [#A]

  • Estimated market size: European annual wind additions targeted at 50-60GW by 2030 vs ~20GW in 2022; VWS-DK's 30-35% share implies 15-21GW annual addressable installation market by 2030 [#A]

  • Recent delivery: EU permitting reform (EU Emergency Wind Regulation, national equivalents) passed in 2023-2024 is beginning to reduce permitting timelines in key markets [#A]

  • Sub: Germany's Windenergiegesetz reform and the 2% land designation mandate are the most material near-term drivers of European onshore volume [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: European annual wind additions: 2022 ~17GW, 2023 ~18GW, 2024 ~20GW — steady 8-10% CAGR [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: The European wind demand backdrop is the best of any major geography; US onshore faces IRA uncertainty post-2024 elections; China's domestic market is accessible only to domestic OEMs [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: Energy security premium post-Ukraine conflict has made wind energy a defence/security asset in European political framing — cross-party political support in a way that climate-only framing did not achieve [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Grid infrastructure bottleneck is the most binding near-term constraint; turbines can be manufactured faster than grid connection permits are issued [#J]

  • Sub: Germany's TSO (TenneT) has flagged 2-3 year grid connection delays in some regions; this is the binding constraint on actual installation, not turbine demand [#A]

  • Visibility: Very high on 3-5 year horizon; political risk to European wind policy is the lowest it has been in a decade given energy security framing [#A]

  • Bear case: A European political shift (centre-right governments rolling back green mandates) slows auction programme; VWS-DK's European demand concentration becomes a risk if policy reversal occurs in 2-3 largest markets [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Germany 2025 federal election produces a coalition that slows wind land designation — the 2% mandate is amended or delayed; German volume target for 2030 is reduced by 20-30% [#J]

  • Miss magnitude: Germany is approximately 25-30% of European wind installations; a 20% reduction in German volume = ~2-3GW of annual demand loss; at VWS-DK's market share = 600-900MW — meaningful but manageable [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: What if the permitting acceleration is slower than political commitment implies — i.e., the administration capacity of European energy authorities is the binding constraint that political mandates cannot immediately fix? The gap between mandate and delivery is the key near-term risk [#J]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — the structural demand tailwind is the most reliably positive driver given policy mandate, energy security framing, and early permitting reform delivery
