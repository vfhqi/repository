§3 — Barriers to Entry + Exit + Disruption Forces

  • Barriers verdict: Extreme capital requirements + regulatory licensing + grid access constraints create near-impenetrable barriers in regulated networks; renewable development barriers are lower but still material; disruption forces are technology-driven and long-dated, not near-term [#J]

  • Capital requirement: Single offshore wind project costs €2-5bn; full distribution network franchise requires €10bn+ RAB investment; US state-regulated utility acquisition requires multi-billion regulated asset base plus regulatory approval across multiple states [#I]

  • Iberdrola's ScottishPower Transmission RAB: ~£7bn+ (Ofgem RIIO-T2); Avangrid regulated asset base: ~$20bn+

  • Capital requirement alone excludes all but sovereign-backed or global investment-grade operators

  • Scale economy: Levelised cost of energy for offshore wind declines ~15-20% for operators >3 GW installed (industry data); procurement power (turbine supply chain, vessels, installation) rewards scale; Iberdrola's turbine supply agreements with Siemens Gamesa / Vestas are scale-advantaged vs smaller developers [#A]

  • Regulatory barrier: Regulated distribution and transmission require government licence and ongoing regulatory compliance; entry requires legislative change — not achievable by private actors unilaterally; barriers are sovereign-backed [#J]

  • US FERC and state PUC regulatory approval for new transmission entrants takes 5-10 years; existing franchise areas effectively prevent competitive entry

  • Distribution barrier: Iberdrola's retail customer base in Spain (~4mn) has relationships and billing infrastructure built over decades; network ownership creates a natural bundling opportunity for adjacent energy services [#A]

  • Technology barrier (renewables): Project development expertise — offshore metocean analysis, turbine site optimisation, grid connection design, permitting across multiple jurisdictions — is difficult to replicate quickly; Iberdrola's offshore wind development track record spans 20+ years [#A]

  • Structural exits: European thermal generation is exiting (coal phase-out, gas plant mothballing) — this removes merchant capacity and improves market pricing for remaining dispatchable capacity; Iberdrola's low thermal exposure benefits as thermal exits remove competition [#J]

  • Disruption force — technology: Battery storage + demand response could reduce need for grid upgrades in some geographies; grid-edge technologies (virtual power plants, behind-the-meter solar + storage) could reduce throughput volumes on distribution networks [#J]

  • Timescale: material impact by 2035+, not 2026-2028 ESA horizon

  • Iberdrola's storage investment ($3-4bn committed) hedges against this disruption

  • Innovative entrants: Tesla Energy (Megapack), Stem Inc., and technology-native aggregators are building grid-edge positions; these are adjacent, not direct competitive threats to regulated networks on ESA horizon [#A]

  • Vanilla entrants: New regulated utility entrants: structurally impossible without government franchise award; in renewable generation, new entrants are abundant but face capex/permitting barriers that limit scale [#I]

  • Adjacency entry: Oil majors (BP, Shell, TotalEnergies) entering renewable generation with sovereign-backed capital — genuine competitive pressure in offshore wind auction processes; Shell's UK offshore wind bid at Hornsea impacted project IRRs for incumbents in 2023 [#J]

  • This is the most material near-term competitive disruption for Iberdrola's renewables development business

  • Substitutes: In power supply: no near-term substitutes for grid electricity in residential/commercial use; EV charging could create distributed alternatives but requires Iberdrola's grid anyway; demand-side response is a complement, not substitute [#A]

  • Disintermediation: Digital energy platforms (Octopus Energy, Flexitricity) are building between network and customer — potential long-term threat to regulated retail; not material in regulated transmission/distribution economics [#J]

  • Disruption force — policy: Retroactive regulatory intervention (Spain 2013) is the highest-probability disruption force; windfall tax risk in high-electricity-price environments; political pressure on utility profitability is observable across EU [#J]

  • Sceptical read — §3: The moat characterisation relies heavily on regulatory franchise protection — but regulatory frameworks are rewritten every 5-8 years (RIIO-T3 determination will set UK returns post-2026; CNMC Spain resets periodically); oil major entry into offshore wind is accelerating, not decelerating; the capital barrier moat is weakening as sovereign wealth funds and pension capital compress renewable development returns globally [#J]
