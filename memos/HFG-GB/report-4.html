1. Revenue Growth Trajectory

1.1 Historical (L3Y)

Hilton Food Group has grown revenue from GBP 3,846m in FY2022 to GBP 4,215m in FY2025 (continuing operations), a CAGR of approximately 3.1%. However, the growth profile is lumpy and heavily influenced by raw material pass-through pricing.

FY2022 (52 weeks to 1 January 2023): Revenue of GBP 3,990m. This represented a significant step-up from FY2021 (GBP 3,455m), driven by raw material inflation pass-through across protein categories and the full-year effect of the Fairfax Meadow and Dalco acquisitions completed in 2021. Volume growth of 4.1% was supported by newly acquired businesses and convenience growth in Central Europe.

FY2023 (52 weeks to 31 December 2023): Revenue of GBP 3,988m, essentially flat on FY2022. On a constant currency basis, revenue grew modestly. The year was characterised by raw material deflation in APAC offsetting inflation in Europe and the UK. Volumes grew 0.7% at group level, though APAC delivered strong 7.2% volume growth. Adjusted operating profit increased 33.5% to GBP 95.0m, reflecting a significant profit recovery as seafood returned to operating profitability after a poor FY2022.

FY2024 (52 weeks to 29 December 2024): Revenue of GBP 3,988m on a reported basis, up 1.9% on a constant currency basis. APAC raw material deflation continued to weigh on reported numbers. Volume growth was a solid 4.4% across all regions, with UK & Ireland delivering 9.1% volume growth and revenue up 10.6% in constant currency. UK & Ireland revenue reached GBP 1,460m (approximately 37% of group). Europe volumes grew 1.6% with revenue up 3.2% in constant currency. Adjusted operating profit rose 10.2% to GBP 104.7m (11.9% in constant currency). Adjusted PBT reached GBP 76.1m, up 15.3% -- the peak of the recent cycle.

FY2025 (52 weeks to 28 December 2025): Revenue from continuing operations rose 10.3% to GBP 4,215m. However, this was almost entirely a function of raw material inflation across all markets rather than volume. Volumes from continuing operations grew just 0.2%. Adjusted PBT from continuing operations was GBP 69.0m, with total adjusted PBT (including discontinued ops) at GBP 73.2m. Constant currency adjusted PBT from continuing ops was down 1.0%. The core meat business remained resilient, but Seachill volumes fell ~7% and the seafood/vegan businesses dragged overall profitability.

The key observation is that HFG's revenue trajectory is not a clean growth story. Revenue bounces around the GBP 4.0bn level, driven more by commodity price movements (which are passed through to retail customers) than by underlying volume growth. True organic growth -- measured by volumes -- has averaged roughly 2-4% per annum over this period, with FY2025 representing a near-zero year.

1.2 Consensus Forward (N2Y)

FY2026E: Management has guided adjusted PBT of GBP 60-65m, well below the prior consensus of GBP 68.6m. This implies a further 11-16% decline in profitability from FY2025 levels. The company cited continued challenges in seafood (Seachill, Foppen), the vegan business (Dalco), and inflationary pressures in beef and white fish. Revenue is expected to remain broadly around the GBP 4.2bn level or higher, as raw material inflation continues to push through the top line. However, margins will compress further given the ongoing drag from non-core businesses.

FY2027E: The outlook is more constructive. The Saudi Arabia joint venture (with NADEC) is expected to begin operations in early 2027, adding a new geography. The Canada facility buildout continues with GBP 48.7m of capex already deployed by end-2025. The strategic review refocusing on core meat should yield benefits by removing management distraction and capital misallocation toward underperforming seafood/vegan units. If Seachill/Foppen/Dalco can be stabilised or exited, the core meat business should resume its trajectory of modest volume growth (2-4%) and stable-to-improving margins. No specific FY2027 consensus figures were available in the search data, but the direction of travel post-2026 should be upward assuming the strategic pivot executes.

The critical question for the forward view is whether FY2026 is genuinely the trough or whether the seafood drag persists into FY2027. Management language suggests improvement plans are underway but has not committed to a specific timeline for these businesses to break even.

2. Margin Analysis

2.1 Gross Profit Margin

HFG's gross profit margin data requires careful interpretation. The business model is fundamentally a cost-plus processor: HFG buys raw protein on behalf of its retail customers, processes it in dedicated facilities, and charges a conversion margin. This means the gross margin is structurally low and highly sensitive to the accounting treatment of raw material costs.

Available data suggests the gross margin has fluctuated between approximately 10-11% in recent years. In periods of raw material inflation, the GP margin tends to compress mechanically because the numerator (gross profit) grows more slowly than the denominator (revenue inflated by commodity pass-through). This is an optical effect rather than a real deterioration in economics -- the absolute gross profit in pounds may be stable or growing.

The more meaningful metric is the absolute gross profit per tonne processed, which the company does not explicitly disclose but which can be inferred from the volume and margin data. With volumes growing 4.4% in FY2024 and adjusted operating profit up 10.2%, the implication is that per-unit economics improved meaningfully in that year. Conversely, FY2025's near-zero volume growth and declining adjusted operating profit suggest per-unit economics came under pressure.

2.2 EBITDA Margin

Adjusted EBITDA has followed a recovery trajectory from FY2022 through FY2024:

  • FY2022: Adjusted EBITDA of GBP 119.9m (margin ~3.0% on GBP 3,990m revenue)
  • FY2023: Adjusted EBITDA of approximately GBP 144m (margin ~3.6% on GBP 3,988m revenue)
  • FY2024: Adjusted EBITDA of approximately GBP 153m (margin ~3.8% on GBP 3,988m revenue)
  • FY2025: EBITDA margin likely compressed to approximately 3.3-3.5% given the revenue inflation and operating profit decline

The FY2022-to-FY2024 trajectory showed strong EBITDA margin recovery, driven by the seafood turnaround in FY2023 and operational leverage in the core meat business in FY2024. FY2025 reversed this trend as Seachill moved back into losses and Foppen incurred exceptional costs.

The EBITDA margin for this business will always look thin (3-4%) because of the high raw material pass-through revenue base. What matters more is the absolute EBITDA per facility and per tonne, and the trend in adjusted operating profit.

2.3 Operating Margin

Adjusted operating profit and margins over the L3Y:

  • FY2022: Adjusted operating profit approximately GBP 71m; statutory operating profit GBP 64.7m. Operating margin approximately 1.6% (statutory) to 1.8% (adjusted).
  • FY2023: Adjusted operating profit GBP 95.0m; statutory operating profit GBP 86.1m (up 59.4%). Adjusted operating margin approximately 2.4%. The dramatic improvement was driven by the seafood recovery and strong meat performance.
  • FY2024: Adjusted operating profit GBP 104.7m (up 10.2%). Adjusted operating margin approximately 2.6%. This represented the peak margin year.
  • FY2025: Reported operating margin approximately 2.3%. Constant currency adjusted operating profit fell 4.4%. The decline was driven almost entirely by Seachill (loss-making), Foppen (exceptional costs), and Dalco (still loss-making).

Underlying vs reported: The gap between adjusted and statutory operating profit is driven by acquisition intangible amortisation, depreciation of fair value adjustments to PP&E, exceptional items, and IFRS 16 lease adjustments. In FY2024, the gap was approximately GBP 6m (GBP 104.7m adjusted vs approximately GBP 98.4m statutory). In FY2025, exceptional items widened the gap significantly due to GBP 9.2m of Foppen relocation costs.

The operating margin trajectory tells a clear story: FY2022 was the trough (seafood problems), FY2023-FY2024 was the recovery, and FY2025-FY2026 represents a second downturn driven by the same seafood/vegan businesses that caused FY2022's problems. This is a red flag -- the non-core businesses appear to be structurally challenged, not merely cyclically weak.

3. Volume vs Price Dynamics

HFG's revenue growth decomposition reveals a critical feature of the business model: the vast majority of revenue "growth" in any given year is driven by raw material price pass-through, not by genuine volume expansion.

FY2024: Volumes grew 4.4% across all regions. Revenue grew 1.9% in constant currency. The apparent contradiction (higher volume growth than revenue growth) reflects raw material deflation in APAC, where commodity prices fell while volumes rose. In the UK & Ireland, volumes grew 9.1% and revenue grew 10.6% in constant currency -- here, modest pricing inflation amplified volume-driven growth.

FY2025 H1: Volumes grew 2.5% but revenue rose 10.4% in constant currency. This indicates significant raw material inflation across all markets, with pricing contributing roughly 8 percentage points of revenue growth. In other words, approximately 75% of H1 2025 revenue growth was pure price pass-through.

FY2025 Full Year: Volumes from continuing operations grew just 0.2% while revenue rose 10.3%. Nearly all of the revenue growth was raw material inflation pass-through. This is the worst volume year in the recent data set.

Implication for earnings quality: In a cost-plus model, raw material inflation increases revenue without proportionally increasing profit. HFG earns a conversion margin (pence per kilo), not a percentage of revenue. Therefore, headline revenue growth driven by commodity inflation is misleading as a proxy for earnings growth. The investor should focus on volume growth (which drives absolute profit growth) and conversion margin per unit (which drives margin expansion). By both measures, FY2025 was poor.

Mix effects: The shift toward higher-value segments (premium steaks, convenience, fresh prepared foods) is a positive structural trend. UK Christmas trading in FY2024 set volume records for meat and seafood, with growth in premium tier steaks. This mix improvement supports per-unit margin expansion even when overall volume growth is modest.

4. Geographic Mix Evolution

HFG operates through three geographic segments plus central costs:

UK & Ireland (~37-38% of group revenue): This is the growth engine. Revenue of approximately GBP 1,460m in FY2024, up 10.6% in constant currency. Volume growth of 9.1% in FY2024 was the strongest of any region. The segment benefits from the deep Tesco partnership and is expanding into fresh prepared foods and convenience. Includes the Seachill seafood business in Grimsby and the Fairfax Meadow food service operation (acquired 2021). The UK is also where margin pressure from Seachill is concentrated.

Europe (~35-37% of group revenue): Operations in the Netherlands (Foppen smoked salmon, meat processing), Sweden, Denmark, Central Europe (Poland, Czech Republic), and Portugal. Revenue of approximately GBP 1,400-1,500m. Volume growth of 1.6% in FY2024 was the weakest of the three regions, though revenue grew 3.2% in constant currency. The region houses Foppen (smoked salmon, regulatory issues in Greece) and Dalco (vegan/vegetarian, loss-making). Excluding these problem businesses, the core European meat operations appear stable.

APAC (~25-27% of group revenue): Operations in Australia and New Zealand, primarily serving Woolworths. Revenue of approximately GBP 1,000m. Volume growth of 7.2% in FY2023 was exceptional, though this normalised in FY2024. APAC has been affected by raw material deflation, which suppresses reported revenue growth even when volumes are healthy. The region is a steady contributor but unlikely to be a major growth driver given the mature Australian retail market.

North America / Middle East (emerging): Canada facility under construction (GBP 48.7m capex deployed by end-2025), expected to launch in early 2027. This represents a meaningful new market entry, likely serving Walmart Canada based on the existing Walmart relationship. Saudi Arabia joint venture with NADEC announced March 2025, also targeting early 2027 operations. These are the most significant geographic expansion moves in HFG's history and represent the key medium-term growth catalysts.

Mix shift observation: The UK & Ireland segment has been gaining share of group revenue, driven by superior volume growth. Europe has been relatively stagnant, weighed down by the non-core businesses. APAC has been a swing factor depending on commodity prices. The entry into North America and the Middle East will diversify the geographic base meaningfully from FY2027 onward, but until then, the UK is doing the heavy lifting.

6. Sector Trends

UK food processing sector context: The broader UK food and drink manufacturing sector is in reasonable health. A BDO survey of 100 UK food and drink manufacturers found 95% of business leaders feel positive about prospects, up from 70% in the prior survey. Revenue growth across the sector is running at approximately 2.6% per annum.

Protein demand: UK meat consumption has been broadly stable, with volume growth driven by population growth (net migration) and premiumisation. The cost-of-living crisis peaked in 2023-2024 and has been easing, supporting a modest recovery in protein volumes. HFG's 9.1% UK volume growth in FY2024 significantly outperformed the market, suggesting share gains from the Tesco relationship.

Outsourcing trend: The secular trend toward outsourced protein processing favours HFG. Retailers continue to prefer dedicating their capital to store formats and digital rather than manufacturing infrastructure. HFG's model -- building and operating dedicated facilities on behalf of a single retailer -- offers retailers variable cost structures and operational expertise. The Walmart partnership (now expanding to Canada) validates this model for large-format international retailers.

Input cost inflation: Beef and white fish prices have been rising through 2025-2026, creating a headwind for HFG. While raw material costs are passed through to retail customers, inflation compresses margins in the short term (HFG's conversion fee is negotiated periodically, with a lag in pass-through of operating cost inflation -- labour, energy, packaging). This is a key driver of the FY2026 profit warning.

Labour market: UK food manufacturing faces structural labour shortages post-Brexit. Automation and Industry 4.0 investment is accelerating across the sector. HFG has been investing in automation in its facilities, which should support conversion margin over the medium term.

Trade: UK accession to CPTPP (December 2024) is expected to boost meat exports to countries including Canada, Japan, and Australia. This is marginally positive for HFG's APAC and future North American operations.

Plant-based headwinds: The plant-based protein category has seen significant demand softening after the initial hype cycle. This is directly relevant to HFG's Dalco business (vegan/vegetarian, Netherlands-based), which remains loss-making. The broader sector trend validates management's decision to deprioritise this segment.

7. Capital Allocation & Returns

Capital expenditure: HFG is in an elevated capex cycle driven by the Canada and Saudi Arabia expansion projects. Total capex was GBP 48.7m in FY2025, of which GBP 15.1m (in H1 alone) related to the Canadian project. Full-year FY2024 capex was also elevated. The Canada facility total spend is expected to complete in 2026. This is growth capex that will not generate returns until FY2027 at the earliest, creating a near-term drag on free cash flow and ROIC.

Maintenance capex for the existing business is estimated at approximately GBP 30-35m per annum (roughly in line with depreciation of the processing facilities). The cost-plus model means much of the facility investment is underwritten by the retail customer, reducing HFG's balance sheet risk.

ROIC: Return on capital employed was 21.7% in FY2024 (full year) and 20.8% in H1 2025, reflecting the asset-light (for HFG's portion) nature of the business model. The TTM ROIC of approximately 10% (based on a broader invested capital base including goodwill from acquisitions) is less flattering but still adequate. The Seachill, Foppen, and Dalco acquisitions appear to have been value-destructive -- the goodwill associated with these businesses is generating minimal or negative returns.

Dividend policy: Progressive dividend maintained. Total dividend of 35.0p per share for FY2025 (FY2024: 34.5p). The payout ratio is approximately 74%, which is high given the current earnings decline trajectory. If FY2026 adjusted PBT comes in at the low end of GBP 60-65m guidance, the payout ratio will stretch further. However, management has signalled commitment to the progressive policy.

Balance sheet: Net debt/EBITDA at 0.9x at FY2025 year-end, down from 1.3x at H1 2025. The H1 spike reflected seasonal working capital (stock build ahead of Christmas) and elevated capex. The year-end deleveraging to 0.9x demonstrates the business's cash-generative characteristics when working capital normalises. Net bank debt was GBP 126.7m at year-end. Covenant headroom is ample. The balance sheet is not a concern.

Acquisition strategy: The FY2021 acquisition spree (Fairfax Meadow, Dalco, Foppen, Foods Connected stake increase) has been a mixed bag. Fairfax Meadow (food service) has been integrated successfully. Foppen and Dalco have been disappointments, prompting the FY2025 strategic review. Management appears to have learned the lesson -- the focus is now on organic geographic expansion (Canada, Saudi Arabia) rather than acquisitions into adjacent categories.

8. Earnings Quality Assessment

Cash conversion: HFG's cash conversion is typically strong in H2 due to working capital unwind post-Christmas. H1 cash flow is seasonally weak due to stock build. FY2025 H1 saw a significant free cash outflow, with management guiding that working capital would unwind in H2 and into early 2026. The seasonal pattern is predictable and well-understood. Adjusted free cash flow for a normalised year should convert at 70-80% of adjusted operating profit after maintenance capex, though the current elevated growth capex cycle temporarily depresses this ratio.

Recurring vs one-off: The core meat processing business generates highly recurring revenue -- these are multi-year dedicated facility contracts with major retailers. Revenue visibility is high. The problem businesses (Seachill, Foppen, Dalco) generate less predictable earnings with a higher incidence of one-off costs. In FY2025, Foppen incurred GBP 9.2m of exceptional costs for Greek facility relocation and air freight. These are classified as non-underlying but have recurred across multiple years (Foppen has been a serial source of one-offs), which raises questions about whether they should be treated as truly "exceptional."

Accounting considerations: HFG's adjusted results exclude acquisition intangible amortisation, depreciation of fair value adjustments to PP&E, exceptional items, and IFRS 16 lease adjustments. The gap between adjusted and statutory is not excessive for the core business but has widened as the Foppen/Seachill exceptional items have accumulated. Investors should monitor the cumulative quantum of "exceptional" items in the seafood/vegan businesses -- if these persist beyond FY2026, it suggests the strategic review has not resolved the structural issues.

Revenue recognition: The cost-plus model means revenue includes the pass-through of raw material costs. This inflates the revenue base without proportionally inflating profit. HFG's "revenue" is not comparable to a conventional manufacturer's revenue. A more representative measure of economic activity is either volumes (tonnes processed) or gross profit. Investors focusing on revenue multiples will systematically undervalue HFG relative to peers with less pass-through revenue.

Customer concentration: HFG's business model creates inherent customer concentration risk. Each facility is typically dedicated to a single retailer (Tesco, Ahold Delhaize, Woolworths, Walmart). The loss of a major customer relationship would be material. However, the dedicated facility model creates switching costs on both sides -- the retailer has invested in the partnership and would face significant supply chain disruption from switching. No customer losses have occurred in HFG's history as a public company. This is a latent risk rather than an active concern, but it deserves monitoring.

Earnings trajectory summary: FY2026 is set to be a trough year (adjusted PBT GBP 60-65m vs FY2024 peak of GBP 76.1m). The core meat business is growing modestly and delivering stable returns. The drag is entirely from the non-core seafood/vegan businesses acquired in 2021. The strategic review provides a path to resolution, but execution risk remains. The key question is whether FY2027 can deliver a meaningful earnings recovery -- this requires Seachill to stop losing money, Foppen exceptional costs to cease, and the Canada/Saudi Arabia facilities to begin contributing. All three are achievable but none is guaranteed.
