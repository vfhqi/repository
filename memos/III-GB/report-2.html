Reporting cadence reference: FY26 = year to 31 March 2026, results announced 14-May-2026

BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front)

  • Judgement: 3i is a one-asset case study now disguised as a diversified PE platform — Action is c.74% of NAV at March 2026 (£23.7bn of £32bn portfolio) and the next 12-24 months will be dictated almost entirely by Action's LFL trajectory, not by 3i Group decisions.
  • Judgement: The May-2026 share-price reset (-19% to £19.71) re-rated 3i from a structural premium-to-NAV stock into an Action-tracker; the £750m buyback is a defensive premium-defence tool, not capital-allocation conviction.
  • Judgement: Action's structural compounding (3,302 stores, +384 in 2025, €16bn sales, 14% Q1-26 growth) remains intact, but LFL deceleration from 6.8% to 2.4% inside three months exposes how fragile premium ratings are when the single asset wobbles.
  • Judgement: Versus peers (BPT €94bn AUM fee-machine; EQT €4.5bn evergreen NAV; ICG $87bn fee-earning AUM), 3i is structurally not a fund-management business — change forces favouring peers (fee compression, permanent capital, third-party fundraising) are largely irrelevant to 3i and that is both insulation and a ceiling.
  • Analysis: Five exogenous changes — sterling/euro path, ECB rates, Middle East consumer drag, US tariff posture, Action US-entry capex (€350-400m 2026-30) — define the next 24 months more than anything endogenous.
  • Analysis: Action exit timing is the single biggest latent change force, but Borrows has explicitly said current private arrangement is preferred; no IPO is signposted for FY27 or FY28.
  • Analysis: Borrows is 14 years into CEO seat (joined Oct-2011, CEO May-2012); no successor disclosed — succession itself is now a meaningful endogenous change force inside 24 months.
  • Information: Dividend stepped up 84.5p (FY26) vs 73.0p (FY25), +15.8%; 3iN comparison 13.45p paid, 14.30p targeted FY27 (+6.3%) — 3i dividend growth structurally higher because variable.
  • Information: Insider activity is supportive — 7 director purchases trailing 90 days for £1.58m, no sales; Borrows has historically been a sizeable buyer.

SECTION 1 — ENDOGENOUS CHANGE FORCES INSIDE 3i

1.1 Action exit thesis (IPO vs secondary sale vs hold-and-distribute)

  • Judgement: Hold-and-distribute remains the explicit default; Borrows has stated current private arrangement preferred for control, narrative and dividend extraction without transparency obligations.
  • Judgement: An IPO is the most logical eventual exit given Action's scale (€16bn sales, €2.65bn LTM EBITDA, 18.5x multiple) but probability is low inside the 12-24 month window — no banker mandate signposted, no listing-venue work disclosed.
  • Analysis: A secondary sale to mega-cap PE (KKR, CVC, EQT) would face anti-trust complexity and would be size-constrained — Action's €49bn enterprise value at current 18.5x exceeds typical single-PE bite size.
  • Analysis: Hellman & Friedman bought the Partners Group minority in 2019 at lower valuation — 3i has never been forced to monetise and there is no LP-clock pressure (proprietary capital).
  • Analysis: A partial monetisation (sell 5-15% stake) is the highest-probability change event in 24 months — would crystallise mark, return cash, and let 3i hold residual stake; pattern matches infrastructure exits where they take partial profits.
  • Analysis: Action's US-entry capex commitment (€350-400m 2026-30) actually anchors hold-and-distribute — it would be a strange thing to launch a US store programme and then sell the asset midway.
  • Information: 3i stake moved from 57.9% (Mar-25) to 65.4% (Mar-26) — direction of travel is increasing not decreasing exposure (buying out co-investors), inconsistent with imminent exit.
  • Information: Peer anchor — 3iN sold TCR for €1.14bn at 50% premium (FY26); this is the partial-monetisation pattern 3i could replicate at Action.
  • Sceptic bullet: If LFL keeps decelerating into FY27 — Middle East drag persists, French/German confidence fails to recover — 3i could be forced into a partial monetisation at a lower multiple, reflexive against the thesis.

1.2 Capital allocation rebalance toward third-party fund management

  • Judgement: 3i is structurally not pivoting to fund management at scale; only 3iN (which is itself listed) and select PE co-investment carry third-party capital — this is unlikely to change inside 24 months.
  • Analysis: Peer anchor — ICG-GB grew fee-earning AUM 11% to $87bn FY26 with FRE +23% to £350m; BPT-GB AUM +24.5% to $94.1bn with FRE +20.7%; EQT BPEA IX closed €12.7bn FAUM. 3i's third-party FAUM is a rounding error against these.
  • Analysis: The 3i model — proprietary capital, hold winners, compound NAV, pay growing dividend — is structurally incompatible with the fund-factory model; trying to bolt on a fund business would dilute the model.
  • Information: 3i Infrastructure (3IN) is the only meaningful third-party fund management vehicle; FY26 NAV 405.2p vs 379.9p prior year, total return 8.5%, within 8-10% target range.
  • Information: 3i does take co-investor capital on selected PE assets but does not market discrete funds in the way EQT/BPT/ICG do.
  • Sceptic bullet: If Borrows is replaced by an external CEO with asset-management background, the strategy could pivot; the 3iN listing premium and Royal Sanders co-investment template would be the seed assets.

1.3 Simon Borrows succession

  • Judgement: Borrows is 14 years into CEO seat (since May-2012); no successor has been disclosed; succession is now itself a 12-24 month change force that the market has not priced.
  • Analysis: Borrows personally executed the original £114m Action acquisition in Dec-2011 and chairs Peer Holding I B.V. (Action's Dutch holding company) — his departure would create real continuity risk on the single largest portfolio decision in 3i's history.
  • Analysis: Borrows continues to buy stock (he and family trusts bought 350,147 shares in earlier transaction window) — signals no near-term retirement intent but tenure averages in PE CEO roles are 8-10 years; he is materially over-tenured.
  • Information: Internal candidates would include James Hatchley, Peter McKellar, Jasi Halai — all have recently bought stock in own names; no external front-runner has been signposted in press.
  • Information: Peer anchor — Bridgepoint's William Jackson, EQT's Christian Sinding remain entrenched; ICG's Benoit Durteste also long-tenured; CEO continuity is the listed-PE norm not the exception.
  • Sceptic bullet: Borrows succession could be a negative change force if poorly managed — Action chair seat at Peer Holding I B.V. needs careful handover; loss of Borrows could re-rate the stock lower not higher.

1.4 Dividend policy evolution

  • Judgement: Dividend has stepped meaningfully higher (84.5p FY26 vs 73.0p FY25, +15.8%); policy is variable not progressive; the next 24 months will see continued growth conditional on Action cashflow remittances, but the rate of growth will decelerate if Action LFL weakens.
  • Analysis: Second FY26 dividend of 48.0p (payable July-2026 subject to shareholder approval) is the largest single payment in the company's history.
  • Analysis: £750m buyback (announced 14-May-2026, completing 31-Dec-2026) signals capital-return preference for retained cash over dividend step-change — buyback is more flexible if Action cash deteriorates.
  • Information: Peer anchor — ICGT preliminary FY26 dividend +8% to 39p; 3iN final 6.725p, full year 13.45p, FY27 target 14.30p (+6.3%); 3i's 15.8% growth is structurally higher because variable, not progressive.
  • Information: Dividend coverage is comfortable while Action distributes — Action distributed strong cash in FY26; if LFL drops materially, dividend coverage could compress inside 24 months.
  • Sceptic bullet: A formalisation of dividend policy (commitment to progressive, capping at variable level) would be a meaningful re-rating trigger but has not been signalled.

1.5 Hurdle-rate dynamics

  • Judgement: Hurdle rates inside PE are mechanically rising as risk-free rates remain elevated; 3i's internal IRR requirements on new investment are likely 18-20%+ vs c.15% in the 2018-2021 era — this constrains new commitment pace.
  • Analysis: 3i's £394m new and follow-on at 3iN level suggests selective deployment; on Group level, recent activity (Royal Sanders +£56m December 2025) is bolt-on not platform.
  • Analysis: Higher hurdle rates favour existing winners over new platform deals — explains why 3i is buying out Action co-investors rather than seeding new positions.
  • Information: Peer anchor — EQT deployment was €7.8bn (Bridgepoint number) vs returns of €8.1bn; net capital deployment is slowing across the listed PE complex.
  • Sceptic bullet: If ECB cuts faster than priced (sub-2% terminal), hurdle rates compress and 3i loses its concentration premium — could be forced to deploy faster into lower-quality assets.

SECTION 2 — EXOGENOUS MACRO CHANGE FORCES

2.1 Interest rate path and private market valuations

  • Judgement: ECB rate path is the single biggest exogenous force on 3i's NAV — Action is valued at 18.5x LTM EBITDA, and that multiple is mechanically sensitive to discount rates; a 50bps cut would justify multiple expansion, a 50bps surprise hike would force multiple compression.
  • Analysis: 3i's hold valuations are reviewed semi-annually; in a falling-rates environment, exit multiples expand and 3i would mark up; in a stable/rising environment, the cushion to Action's elevated multiple narrows.
  • Analysis: Peer anchor — EQT, Bridgepoint, ICG all benefit from rate cuts via exit volumes (more transactions); 3i benefits via mark-to-market on Action alone — different transmission mechanism.
  • Information: 58% of PE professionals anticipate positive exit performance in 2026; 49% expect exit volumes to rise — broad re-opening is supportive backdrop.
  • Information: Roland Berger/EY data: small- and mid-cap segments forecast strongest growth in 2026 (64% of respondents), large-cap easing — favours 3i's portfolio orientation.
  • Sceptic bullet: Rates have re-tightened on Middle East shock — if oil shock persists, central banks delay cuts and 3i's multiple cushion narrows further.

2.2 Sterling strength/weakness on overseas NAV

  • Judgement: 3i's NAV is c.85%+ euro-denominated (Action is Dutch-listed entity reporting in EUR); sterling moves are a structural P&L lever that the market under-weights.
  • Analysis: A 5% GBP/EUR weakening adds c.£1.2bn to reported NAV mechanically — sterling soft phase has been a tailwind into FY26 results.
  • Analysis: If sterling rallies on Bank of England hold-vs-ECB-cut, 3i NAV faces 5-10% mechanical headwind that has nothing to do with operating performance.
  • Information: No formal hedging programme disclosed; 3i runs unhedged FX exposure as a deliberate policy reflecting long-hold horizon.
  • Information: Peer anchor — 3iN runs more diversified currency mix; ICG, BPT have FX exposure offset by sterling cost base; 3i's translation impact is the most direct.
  • Sceptic bullet: If sterling appreciates 10% on UK fiscal stabilisation, 3i's headline NAV growth could be flat-to-negative in FY27 even with Action growing — pure optics risk.

2.3 Global consumer spend cycle and Action

  • Judgement: Action is non-discretionary discount — supposed to be counter-cyclical, but Q1-26 data shows France and Germany LFL went flat as consumer confidence cracked on Middle East shock; the model is not bulletproof against confidence shocks even at low ticket prices.
  • Analysis: Action's value proposition strengthens in cost-of-living pressure (consumers trade down), but traffic correlates with confidence — fewer trips even when basket value is lower.
  • Analysis: Peer anchor — UK discounters (B&M, Poundland) saw similar pattern in 2022-23; the trade-down benefit and confidence-drag work in opposite directions.
  • Information: Action Q1-26: net sales +14% to €4bn, LFL +3.6%; current run-rate LFL decelerated to 2.4% with France/Germany flat.
  • Information: Action 22.2m weekly customers (Q1-26) — traffic is the swing variable, not basket.
  • Sceptic bullet: If Middle East shock evolves into broader European recession, even discount retail faces aggregate spend contraction — Action's first sub-3% LFL year since 2020 would re-rate the stake.

4.2 Action change forces — the central weight

  • Judgement: Action is the entire 3i thesis in the 12-24 month window — its LFL trajectory, store rollout pace, US-entry execution, and gross margin trajectory will define 3i's NAV change more than all other factors combined.

4.2.1 LFL sales 2026 trajectory

  • Judgement: LFL is the single most important number on the 3i investment case; current 2.4% YTD vs 3.6% Q1-26 vs 6.8% prior year shows a clear deceleration that needs to bottom and re-accelerate inside 24 months to defend the 18.5x multiple.
  • Analysis: Flat LFL in France and Germany is the swing risk — these are mature, high-density Action markets where the LFL slowdown is most material.
  • Analysis: Netherlands, Belgium, Southern Europe LFL "in line with expectations" — bifurcation suggests confidence-driven not pricing-driven.
  • Information: 2025 full year LFL: 4.9%; Q1-26 LFL: 3.6%; YTD-26 LFL: 2.4% — three-step deceleration.
  • Sceptic bullet: If LFL prints sub-2% for FY27, the 18.5x multiple is unsustainable — 3i would have to mark Action lower, hitting NAV by 5-10%.

4.2.2 Country expansion

  • Information: Peer anchor — Five Below opens c.200 stores/year in US; Dollar General c.1,000; Action's 100-store target by 2030 is modest entry vs incumbents.

OVERALL SCEPTIC SECTION

  • 3i has been the structural premium-stock in listed PE for a decade — the very fact that this memo is being written suggests the consensus has already shifted; the re-rating may have largely happened.
  • The £750m buyback is finite and discount-defence is a losing game if LFL doesn't recover — past buybacks by single-asset holdcos rarely defend ratings indefinitely.
  • All five force categories ultimately reduce to Action — if Action LFL re-accelerates to 4%+ by H2-26, every other concern fades; if it doesn't, none of the endogenous tools (buyback, dividend, succession messaging) defend the rating.
  • The most under-discussed force is succession — Borrows is the architect of the entire current 3i model; his departure inside 24 months would be the biggest structural change force, but the market is treating it as a tail risk not a base case.

CONCLUSION

3i is no longer a diversified PE compounder — it is, in the next 12-24 months, an Action-tracker with a defensive overlay (buyback, dividend, succession continuity). Five questions will define the case: (1) does LFL re-accelerate to 4%+ by H2-26; (2) does the 18.5x EBITDA mark hold; (3) does Borrows announce a successor or stay; (4) does sterling stay soft; (5) does the discount-to-NAV close, hold, or widen. Every other change force is downstream of those five. The market has correctly identified the concentration risk in May-2026; what is mispriced is the succession risk, the FX optionality, and the partial-monetisation optionality on Action.
