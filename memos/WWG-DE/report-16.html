§4 — Board Composition + Independence

J-front verdict: Structurally constrained — two-tier German AG board with Aufsichtsrat majority held by public-sector shareholders; independence in the Anglo-Saxon sense is limited by design.

  • Board size: German AG two-tier structure — Vorstand (management board, 3-4 members) + Aufsichtsrat (supervisory board, typically 12-16 members for a company of Gelsenwasser's size, with co-determination requiring employee representation)

  • Independent directors: Under German co-determination law (Mitbestimmungsgesetz), typically 50% of Aufsichtsrat seats are employee representatives; of the shareholder-side seats, independence is further constrained by Stadtwerke Bochum (~70% shareholder) and RWE (~25% shareholder) each nominating representatives; the concept of "independent" in the Anglo-Saxon proxy-advisory sense is structurally limited — virtually all shareholder-side Aufsichtsrat members are nominees of the two dominant shareholders

  • Insider directors: By definition, the Vorstand members are not on the Aufsichtsrat (German two-tier law prohibits this); however, Aufsichtsrat shareholder nominees are effectively "insider" to the shareholder bloc's interests even if not employees of Gelsenwasser; Stadtwerke Bochum-nominated members may have their primary duty to the City of Bochum as employer rather than to Gelsenwasser minority shareholders

  • Independence ratio: Effectively 0% truly independent (shareholder-nominated members represent controlling shareholders; employee-nominated members represent labour interests); this is structurally normal for German co-determined utilities but is a governance constraint relative to Anglo-Saxon best practice

  • Director tenure pattern: German Aufsichtsrat members are typically appointed for 4-5 year renewable terms; long-serving members at utilities are common; full tenure data is not available in English-language sources

  • Recent board change: No evidence of disruptive Aufsichtsrat composition change L24M; RWE's ongoing strategic repositioning (towards renewables) could potentially affect its engagement with Gelsenwasser stake in the medium term — this is a governance watch item

  • Audit committee composition: German AG annual reports include an audit committee (Prüfungsausschuss) within the Aufsichtsrat; composition is drawn from the same constrained independence pool; financial expertise requirement is met under German law; specific member names and qualifications not confirmed from available English-language sources

  • Comp committee composition: Compensation for Vorstand is approved by the full Aufsichtsrat under German AG law (§87 AktG); a dedicated compensation committee may or may not exist separately; Vorstand compensation determination is therefore subject to the same structural constraints as above

  • Nomination committee: Nomination of Vorstand members falls to the Aufsichtsrat; dominant shareholder influence over nominations is structurally embedded

  • Director skills mix: Likely includes: utility/energy sector expertise (shareholder nominees from Stadtwerke Bochum + RWE); municipal/public administration expertise; labour/workforce expertise (employee side); financial expertise (requirement for audit committee); technical/engineering expertise (utility operational norms); specific skills matrix not publicly available

  • Director equity ownership: Directors (Aufsichtsrat members) almost certainly hold no or de minimis personal equity in Gelsenwasser given thin free-float and absence of equity-based director compensation; alignment is through institutional interest of the nominating shareholders, not personal equity

  • Diversity profile: German co-determination creates structural gender diversity on employee side (German listed-company gender quotas apply to supervisory boards); likely meeting German legal requirements of 30% women on Aufsichtsrat; specific composition not confirmed
