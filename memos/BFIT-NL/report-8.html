European Low-Cost Fitness Industry Structure & Value Chain

Macroeconomic Growth Drivers

The European ﬁtness market is experiencing robust structural growth, largely driven by increasing health
consciousness and an ongoing shift toward preventative wellness. Megatrends such as an aging
demographic, rising rates of lifestyle diseases, and growing urbanization are fundamentally expanding the
addressable market for ﬁtness operators. Furthermore, younger demographics, particularly Generation Z, are
prioritizing ﬁtness within their discretionary spending, treating it as an essential lifestyle component rather than a
luxury. The overall UK gym market penetration alone increased to 16.6% of the population in 2025,
demonstrating the continued resilience and expansion of the sector.

Despite this momentum, the European market remains signiﬁcantly underpenetrated compared to the United
States. While the US boasts a ﬁtness penetration rate of approximately 23.4%, core European markets like
France, Belgium, and Germany continue to hover between 10% and 13%. Even the Netherlands, which leads
continental Europe at roughly 19% penetration, still possesses considerable runway for expansion. Operators
project that converging toward US penetration levels could unlock a "white space" of over 14.3 million new
memberships across key European regions over the next decade.

Value Chain and Operational Eﬃciency

The value creation model of the European low-cost sector is predicated on stripped-down, highly automated
operations that maximize ﬂoor space and minimize variable costs. Unlike traditional gyms, HVLP operators
deliberately eliminate expensive "wet facilities" such as swimming pools, saunas, and towel services. Instead,
they dedicate up to 90% of their square footage directly to core ﬁtness activities, focusing heavily on
cardiovascular machines, free weights, and functional strength training. Technology acts as the linchpin of
this value chain, enabling scalable eﬃciencies across real estate portfolios. Digital applications, remote
monitoring, and automated entry gates allow clubs to operate with minimal on-site personnel.

signiﬁcantly reduces labor costs during off-peak hours and weekends. For example, Basic-Fit's recent

than independent competitors, while securing extended warranties and priority service agreements.

Market Fragmentation and the Ongoing Consolidation Trend

While the HVLP model is highly scalable, the European ﬁtness landscape remains deeply fragmented, creating a
ripe environment for consolidation. In large markets like Germany, the sector is dominated by small, owner-led
regional chains that operate between ﬁve and thirty locations in localized metropolitan areas. This
fragmentation presents a substantial barrier to organic entry for new players, but offers major inorganic growth
opportunities for well-capitalized incumbents seeking rapid expansion.

Competitive Dynamics

The European high-value, low-price ﬁtness segment is ﬁercely competitive, dominated by large pan-

Unit Economics and Margin Proﬁles

The HVLP ﬁtness model is predicated on achieving high membership volumes to eﬃciently leverage ﬁxed
property and operational costs. A standard European low-cost facility typically requires between 1,000 and
1,500 active members just to reach breakeven proﬁtability. Basic-Fit's highly automated clubs, for example,
achieve a cash-ﬂow positive state at the club level with approximately 1,400 to 1,500 memberships. Once a
facility reaches maturity—generally deﬁned as being operational for at least 24 months—the unit economics
become highly compelling. Best-in-class operators target a Return on Invested Capital of 30% to 40% at
maturity. In 2024, Basic-Fit's mature clubs reported an average underlying club EBITDA of €399,000 per site,
yielding a 34% ROIC. Similarly, The Gym Group achieved a mature site ROIC of 27% in 2025, supported by
disciplined cost management and yield optimization. The initial capital expenditure to build a new big-box gym
typically ranges from £70 to £90 per square foot, or roughly €1.2 million to €1.5 million per club. Once ramped,
these mature clubs consistently generate strong gym-level EBITDA margins ranging from 30% to 40%. While
utility and staﬃng expenses remain the primary variable costs, leading operators actively mitigate these
headwinds through ﬁxed-price energy contracts and the rapid deployment of staﬄess, 24/7 operating models.

Barriers to Entry

While launching a single independent gym carries relatively low hurdles, successfully scaling a competitive HVLP
chain involves formidable barriers to entry. Capital intensity is a primary obstacle, as outﬁtting a modern
15,000 to 20,000 square foot facility demands signiﬁcant upfront investment. Access to this level of capital is

severely restricted for new entrants, whereas established operators fund rapid expansion through internal free

advantage over smaller rivals. Large operators leverage their immense purchasing power to secure ﬁtness
equipment at steep discounts, making their ﬁt-out costs hundreds of thousands of euros cheaper per site

Barriers to Exit

Exiting the European ﬁtness market is complex and highly costly due to the structural characteristics of the
commercial real estate portfolio. Gym operators typically sign long-term lease agreements, often exceeding
ﬁve to ten years, to secure prime high-traﬃc locations. Prematurely terminating these contracts incurs severe
ﬁnancial penalties and massive lease break fees that can cripple a struggling business. Additionally, the
physical build-out of a high-capacity ﬁtness facility involves extensive and specialized architectural modiﬁcations,
including reinforced ﬂooring, advanced HVAC systems, and heavy plumbing for locker rooms. These highly
specialized modiﬁcations are notoriously diﬃcult to repurpose for standard retail or oﬃce tenants.
Consequently, it is challenging for landlords and departing gym operators to sublease the space eﬃciently,
meaning underperforming mid-market operators are often forced to absorb losses rather than face crippling exit
liabilities.

Key Success Factors

Winning in the highly consolidated European low-cost ﬁtness landscape requires operators to master several
speciﬁc operational pillars. Unrelenting cost discipline is the most critical factor, achieved by stripping out
expensive non-essential amenities like swimming pools and leveraging technology to minimize in-club
headcounts. Operators must achieve immense geographic scale to dictate favorable terms to equipment
suppliers and landlords, thereby ensuring a permanent structural cost advantage over local insurgents.
Strategic real estate selection, driven by advanced data analytics, is required to ensure new clubs are situated in
highly populated catchments that allow them to reach their breakeven threshold rapidly. Finally, offering
ﬂexible, contract-free base memberships alongside premium add-ons allows operators to continuously optimize
revenue yield while maintaining an overarching value proposition.

1. Industry Overview

The European health and fitness market generated €36bn in revenue in 2024, reaching 71.4 million members. In 2025 this expanded to €39.1bn and 75.5 million members — a 9% revenue and 6% membership gain in a single year (EuropeActive/Deloitte 2025). Net new clubs added in 2025 were 1,965, taking the total to 67,515.

Penetration of the total European population was 8.9% in 2024, rising to 9.3% in 2025. The equivalent US figure is approximately 24-25%. This gap is the structural long-term growth thesis: Europe has substantial headroom before approaching US penetration rates.

The low-cost segment (gyms charging below ~€30/month) has been the primary growth engine post-pandemic. Chain operators average €40.54/month vs independents at €59.24/month, reflecting the weight of budget brands in the volume mix. Low-cost clubs now account for approximately 41% of all private-sector members in the UK; the European proportion is lower but growing.

Key drivers (L3Y and N2Y):
- Post-pandemic normalisation of gym attendance, with visits now exceeding pre-2020 levels
- Consumer preference for value — economic pressure steering members from mid-market to low-cost
- Landlord demand for gym operators as anchor tenants in retail parks and mixed-use schemes
- Demographic shift — younger cohorts more fitness-conscious; health awareness rising post-COVID
- 24/7 staffless model expansion reducing operating leverage risk

Structural consolidation: 30 M&A transactions in 2024 transferred 669 clubs across 14 European countries. The German market saw Basic-Fit absorb former clever fit clubs. Market fragmentation remains high — thousands of independent operators — but the branded low-cost chains are gaining share consistently.

2. Value Chain & Economics

Member acquisition — predominantly digital: direct website, app, and social. Low cost of acquisition vs traditional clubs (no sales staff model). Marketing as % of revenue is structurally low at scale. Churn management via app engagement and freeze/pause functionality. Basic-Fit acquires the majority of members through its own website and app.

Club operations — the key cost line. Staffing: basic-fit clubs operate with 1-3 staff on peak hours, zero overnight. Automated check-in, QR access, camera surveillance. Energy costs are the second largest operational expense. Rent is fixed and long-term (leases typically 10-15 years in Benelux and France).

Equipment suppliers — Basic-Fit standardises equipment sourcing, purchasing at volume discount. Equipment refresh cycle typically 5-7 years. Key suppliers include Life Fitness, Technogym, Precor. Pricing power sits with the gyms at scale: Basic-Fit is one of the largest buyers in Europe.

Landlords — the key external counterparty. Long-term leases are fixed costs; rent is the largest off-P&L liability under IFRS 16. New club rent negotiations have become more competitive as landlords — especially in retail parks — actively seek gym tenants to drive footfall.

Digital/tech — Basic-Fit operates its own proprietary app (in-house developed). App handles member onboarding, access, class booking, and tiered membership management. The app is a competitive moat in-making: proprietary data on member behaviour, and low incremental cost to deliver premium digital tier.

Where margins accrue: The mature club model generates ~€399k EBITDA per club at 34% ROIC. Margins are highest at maturity. The main P&L drag is (a) the ramp period (typically 24-36 months to maturity) and (b) corporate overhead on a still-expanding network. Pricing power sits with the chain operators, not landlords or suppliers, at scale.

4. Barriers to Entry & Exit

Entry barriers:
- Capital: Opening a low-cost gym requires €1.2-1.5m per club (Basic-Fit average: €1.30m capex per club in 2024). A credible scale network requires hundreds of clubs and €500m+ of capital commitment.
- Scale economics: Equipment purchasing, energy procurement, technology, and central marketing costs all benefit from scale. Basic-Fit at 1,575 clubs can source equipment 15-20% cheaper than a 50-club operator.
- Real estate: Premium locations (retail parks, high-footfall strips) are contested. Landlord relationships are relationship-driven; established operators get first refusal on new developments. Basic-Fit has a dedicated property pipeline team.
- Brand: Low-cost gyms have lower brand stickiness than premium clubs, but awareness and member familiarity with an operator's app ecosystem creates mild switching friction.
- Technology/app: Basic-Fit's in-house app took years to build. Smaller operators rely on white-label platforms at higher per-member cost.
- Regulatory: Planning and construction permits can be slow; no industry-specific regulatory moat but real estate zoning is a practical constraint.

Exit barriers:
- Long leases (10-15 years) with limited break clauses create exit costs.
- Equipment has residual value but is depreciating.
- Staff redundancy obligations (where staff exist) are manageable but real.
- Exit is structurally difficult: operators tend to sell club estates rather than wind down. This locks capital into the sector and sustains competitive density.

7. Business Model Mechanics

Revenue drivers:
1. Net club additions × mature club yield
2. Member growth per existing club (fill rate)
3. ARPM growth (pricing, tier mix)

Cost structure:
- Club-level costs: rent, energy, maintenance capex (~€58k/club/year), minimal staff
- Corporate: IT, marketing, management
- CapEx: €1.30m per new club (2024 average); maintenance €58k/year

Capital intensity: Moderate. €1.30m/club initial outlay against ~€399k mature club EBITDA = 3.3x payback (before rent). Including rent, the payback is longer but ROIC still 34% at maturity. CapEx is front-loaded; FCF materialises at maturity.

NWC dynamics: Favourable. Members pay monthly in advance by direct debit. Suppliers paid on 30-60 day terms. NWC is structurally negative at scale — a cash-generative feature.

Cash conversion: High at maturity. The drag is the immature club estate — clubs ramp for 24-36 months before hitting steady-state EBITDA. With 585 immature clubs at year-end 2024, the drag on reported FCF is substantial but time-limited.

Operating leverage: Significant. Club-level incremental margin on each new member above breakeven is 90%+. Revenue growth flows disproportionately to EBITDA as the fixed cost base (rent, staff, overhead) is covered.

What breaks the model:
- Sustained consumer downturn pressuring membership price elasticity
- Energy cost spike with no pass-through mechanism
- Real estate market tightening — new sites become uneconomic at inflated rents
- Leverage remains elevated if EBITDA growth disappoints, restricting FCF flexibility

8. Key Success Factors

Scale is the primary determinant of competitive success. Operators below 200 clubs struggle to achieve the purchasing scale, technology investment, and brand awareness needed to compete on cost while generating acceptable returns.

Density matters within geography. A network of 50 clubs in one city is worth more than 50 scattered clubs — shared marketing, logistics, real estate knowledge, and member referral compound.

Maturation cadence is everything. New clubs drag returns for 24-36 months. Operators that open faster than their maturation capacity face prolonged earnings compression.

Technology ownership separates leaders from followers. Proprietary apps, automated member onboarding, and data-driven churn prediction are now table stakes for top-quartile operators.

Sustainable margin levels: EBITDA margin (pre-IFRS 16) of 20-30% for leading operators at scale. Below 15% suggests either sub-scale, over-levered, or premium-cost real estate decisions.

Achievable growth rates: Revenue growth of 10-20% for scale operators in growing markets (France, Spain). Maturing markets (Benelux) grow at 5-8%. Member penetration growth plus ARPM mix-shift are both incremental drivers.

9. Industry Evolution

Consolidation: Accelerating. 30 M&A transactions in 2024 alone, 669 clubs changing hands. The independent sector is under structural pressure. Scale chain operators are the natural acquirers.

Digital disruption: Mixed picture. Digital-only fitness (Peloton, Apple Fitness+) benefited briefly during COVID but proved complementary rather than substitutional. Gym visits have recovered and exceeded pre-pandemic levels. The digital risk to the physical gym model is lower than feared.

Vertical integration: Basic-Fit and others integrating app-based PT, nutrition, and wellness content into premium membership tiers. Monetising data and engagement beyond the gym floor is a nascent opportunity.

24/7 staffless expansion: Already widespread in Benelux, spreading rapidly in France and Spain. Reduces the labour cost line permanently and opens sub-economic locations (smaller towns, non-retail-park sites).

Pricing power: Trending towards operators. Member price elasticity is lower than assumed — The Gym Group raised ARPM 7% in 2024 with no material membership loss. European operators have room to take real-price increases above inflation over the medium term.

Regulatory change: Low risk in most markets. Planning restrictions are the practical constraint, not regulatory.

Where pricing power is moving: To scale chain operators. Landlords are competing for gym tenants, not the reverse. Equipment suppliers face volume buyer pressure. Members face limited competition in dense urban clusters where one or two chain operators dominate.

10. Sceptical View

Bull case: European penetration gap (8.9% vs US 24%) means decades of runway.
Bear case: US penetration is structurally higher because of cultural, climate, and suburban layout differences. European markets may normalise at 12-15%, not 24%. The catchup narrative overstates addressable demand.

Bull case: 34% mature-club ROIC justifies aggressive expansion.
Bear case: 34% ROIC is backward-looking on clubs that opened in a lower-cost environment. Newer clubs face higher rent and construction costs, inflated equipment prices, and an energy cost base that was not modelled at opening. Future cohort ROIC may normalise to 20-25%.

Bull case: France and Spain are vast underpenetrated markets.
Bear case: Fitness Park has 280+ franchised clubs in France and is not standing still. Spain has local operators (Altafit, Anytime Fitness) with cost advantages via lower labour cost and franchise structures. Basic-Fit is opening new clubs into rising competition, not a vacuum.

Bull case: Three-tier membership drives ARPM structurally higher.
Bear case: A 3% ARPM uplift on one repricing event is not a flywheel. Without adding genuine product value (group classes, pools, PT content), there is limited capacity to take tier pricing meaningfully above €30/month without driving churn.

Bull case: Deleveraging to sub-2x by 2026 is on track.
Bear case: Leverage is contingent on EBITDA guidance being met and club opening capex reducing. If the 50-club 2026 target proves insufficient to drive members/revenue growth, EBITDA misses against guidance and leverage stalls above 2x, extending the equity de-rating.

Structural competitive position: GREEN. Basic-Fit holds the best structural position of any pure-play listed European low-cost gym operator. Scale, technology, geography, and unit economics are all ahead of peers. The near-term risks (leverage, immaturity tail, France competition) are execution risks on a structurally sound model, not thesis-breakers.
