§2 — Key Driver #1: BECCS/Biomass Contracted Revenue Durability

J-front verdict: The contracted biomass revenue stack is the investment case's foundation — high visibility, but multi-decade policy dependency means "high plausibility" is conditional, not structural [#J]

  • Driver definition: Drax operates Europe's largest biomass power station (3.9GW capacity at Drax Power Station, North Yorkshire) generating electricity under legacy Renewables Obligation Certificates (ROCs) and newer CfDs; contracted revenues provide near-utility EBITDA visibility through ~2027 for ROCs and longer for CfD tranches [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Contracted revenues flow directly to EBITDA with limited volume risk; ROC + CfD stack covers ~65-70% of group generation EBITDA by management's own disclosures; EPS is essentially a function of contracted spread vs. biomass pellet input cost [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Revenue visibility supports a near-utility multiple; any contraction in contracted revenue tenure or price resets the multiple ceiling sharply downward; conversely, contract extensions or new CfDs are immediate re-rating catalysts [#J]

  • Company guide: Management has guided to stable EBITDA of approximately £900M-£1.0bn for FY2025, underpinned by the existing contracted stack; no explicit guidance on post-2027 revenue beyond BECCS CfD aspirations [#A]

  • Sub: ROC expiry from 2027 onward creates a revenue cliff that management frames as "bridged by BECCS CfD" — this assumption is the key embedded risk in all consensus models [#I]

  • Management commentary: FY2024 results presentation emphasised "strong contracted position" and "multi-year revenue visibility"; language around BECCS commercialisation has been consistently optimistic but timelines have slipped [#A]

  • Track record: Drax has delivered on contracted revenue targets consistently over L3Y; EBITDA of ~£1.0-1.1bn in FY2022-23 exceeded guidance in power-price-elevated environment; FY2024 modestly below elevated FY2023 as power prices normalised [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Management guidance on contracted revenues is credible and conservative; the aggressive assumption is the ROC-to-BECCS-CfD bridge — this is speculative, not contracted [#J]

  • SS consensus: Consensus EBITDA estimates for FY2025-26 cluster around £850M-£950M, implying modest normalisation from FY2023 peak; EPS consensus approximately 150-175p for FY2025 based on public estimate summaries [#A]

  • Consensus changes L12M: Modest downward revision (5-8%) on biomass cost normalisation and lower power price assumptions; no material consensus breaks [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Loss of contracted revenue visibility (e.g., regulatory intervention) could reduce EPS by 30-50p (20-30% hit) immediately via multiple compression even before earnings impact [#A]

  • Recent delivery: FY2024 results (reported early 2025) showed EBITDA in line with updated guidance; generation volumes broadly stable; no material contract impairments [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: EBITDA of ~£1.0bn in FY2022, ~£1.1bn in FY2023 (power price tailwind), ~£850-900M FY2024 (normalisation); trajectory is downward from peak but above pre-2022 baseline [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: No cohort peers available; sector context — UK utility contracted EBITDA visibility of 3-5 years is broadly comparable to network operators but short vs. regulated assets with 20Y+ visibility; Drax's 2-4Y contracted runway is below sector best practice [#I]

  • quality_flag: thin_peer_context — no cohort peer available for direct benchmark

  • Macro tailwind: UK energy security agenda supports biomass as transition fuel; government rhetoric favours maintaining existing contracted positions through transition [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Normalising UK wholesale power prices (from 2022-23 spike) reduce uncontracted upside; rising interest rates have compressed utility multiples sector-wide [#A]

  • Visibility: High for existing contracted revenues through 2026-27; low for post-2027 without BECCS CfD confirmation [#J]

  • Bear case: ROC-period revenues are government-mandated payments ultimately funded by consumer bills; political pressure on energy costs could trigger a "ROC windfall" review; any clawback or cap mechanism would be severely earnings-dilutive [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Biomass sustainability reclassification (EU or UK taxonomy) disrupts ROC eligibility; probability low but non-zero given evolving bioenergy accounting standards [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: A 20% ROC revenue reduction would cut group EBITDA by ~£150-180M (~15-20% impact), compressing EPS by 25-35p [#A]

  • Red flags: Parliamentary scrutiny of biomass subsidies has intensified since 2023; Friends of the Earth and competing NGOs have maintained pressure on DESNZ to review ROC treatment for biomass — not imminent but a slow-burn risk [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet: If UK wholesale power prices fall another 15-20% from current levels, the uncontracted margin "buffer" above biomass pellet cost narrows materially — management's £850M+ EBITDA floor may prove optimistic without the power price tailwind the model assumes [#J]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — contracted revenue delivery to 2026-27 is robust given existing legal frameworks; post-2027 bridge to BECCS is the unresolved assumption [#J]

§3 — Key Driver #2: BECCS CfD Award and Capital Deployment

J-front verdict: BECCS represents the single largest source of 24M share price optionality, but timeline slippage and capital commitment risk make this medium plausibility at best [#J]

  • Driver definition: Bioenergy with Carbon Capture and Storage — Drax's plan to retrofit carbon capture to the Drax Power Station biomass units, generating negative-emission electricity eligible for a long-term CfD at elevated strike prices; management target is FP investment decision by mid-2020s [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: A confirmed BECCS CfD would extend Drax's revenue visibility well beyond 2027, potentially with 20-25 year contract terms; consensus models imply a 20-40% re-rating on CfD confirmation given the de-risking of the post-ROC revenue cliff [#A]

  • Sub: Estimated BECCS CfD strike price in ranges of £200-250/MWh have been cited in public policy consultations; at 2.6GW eligible capacity, revenues could add £1.0-2.0bn per annum at full build-out [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: BECCS CfD confirmation would (a) remove post-2027 cliff risk, (b) justify significant re-rating toward infrastructure/long-term contracted peer multiples, (c) potentially trigger index re-classification [#J]

  • Company guide: Management has described BECCS as "the most important project for UK energy security and net zero"; FID timing has slipped from 2023 guide to "mid-2020s"; capital cost estimates of ~£2.0bn for Phase 1 have been cited publicly [#A]

  • Management commentary: CEO Will Gardiner has consistently framed BECCS as strategically essential; tone at Capital Markets Day (2023) was bullish; subsequent quarterly commentary has been more measured as policy clarity lagged [#A]

  • Track record: Management has not previously delivered a project of BECCS scale; analogous CCS projects globally have experienced cost overruns of 50-150%; this is a first-of-kind execution risk [#J]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Management is clearly optimistic on BECCS timeline and capital cost; independent engineering reviews and CCS project databases suggest significant downside risk to both [#J]

  • SS consensus: Sell-side broadly ascribes BECCS option value of £100-200p per share in sum-of-the-parts models, but the range is very wide (some brokers at zero, some at £300p+) reflecting genuine uncertainty [#A]

  • quality_flag: thin_peer_context — no named broker pool available; public commentary only

  • Consensus changes L12M: BECCS option value has likely been trimmed in L12M models as policy timeline extended; no broker has moved to zero option value [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Full BECCS CfD confirmation could add 50-100p to SOTP value directly via multiple re-rating; EPS impact only materialises post-construction (~2030+) [#A]

  • Recent delivery: No BECCS CfD has been awarded to date; Drax received "Track 1" status in the UK CCUS cluster sequencing but BECCS-specific CfD design remains under consultation [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: BECCS timeline has slipped consistently — each year's management commentary has pushed FID 12-18M to the right [#I]

  • Macro tailwind: UK government's North Sea Transition Deal, Powering Up Britain, and net zero commitments structurally favour BECCS; no policy has withdrawn BECCS support [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Capital cost inflation (steel, engineering, CO₂ transport infrastructure) has materially increased BECCS build cost estimates; government fiscal constraints post-2023 budget cycles limit subsidy headroom [#A]

  • Visibility: Low — BECCS CfD is not a contracted position; it is a policy aspiration with government support but no binding commitment [#J]

  • Bear case: UK government deprioritises BECCS in favour of offshore wind and nuclear; CfD strike price offered is below the levelised cost of BECCS; Drax declines to FID; post-2027 revenue cliff is unhedged and EPS collapses 40-50% [#J]

  • Miss scenario: CfD design process extends to 2027-28; ROC revenue stream begins winding down before BECCS revenues start; 2-3 year earnings trough with high leverage amplifying downside [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: Worst-case (no BECCS CfD, no contract extension) would reduce EBITDA from ~£900M to ~£400-500M post-2027, implying 50%+ EPS decline and severe multiple de-rating [#A]

  • Red flags: ⚡ RARE: The gap between management's BECCS timeline confidence and the actual policy/regulatory progress is one of the widest management-vs-reality divergences in the UK utility sector — timeline has slipped 3+ years from original 2025 FID guide [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: The BECCS option value embedded in consensus models may be double-counting — government is unlikely to offer both a high CfD strike price AND maintain ROC-equivalent subsidy generosity; the "subsidy budget" is finite and investors may be pricing both [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — government strategic support is genuine but FID timing, capital cost certainty, and CfD strike pricing are all unresolved; 24M catalyst unlikely, 36-48M plausible [#J]

§4 — Key Driver #3: Biomass Input Cost and Supply Chain Resilience

J-front verdict: Biomass pellet cost is the key margin lever in the near term — improving from 2022-23 peaks but structurally elevated vs. pre-2021 and subject to supply concentration risk [#J]

  • Driver definition: Drax sources ~13-14M tonnes of compressed wood pellets annually, primarily from North America (US South, Canada) and increasingly from other regions; pellet cost is the largest single operating cost and the primary margin variable within the contracted revenue envelope [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Pellet cost × volume = dominant COGS line; a £10/tonne move on 13M tonnes = ~£130M EBITDA impact; in recent years, pellet costs have moved £50-100/tonne in either direction — this is a ±£650M-£1.3bn EBITDA swing factor, well in excess of consensus EBITDA [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Cost beats vs. consensus translate directly to EPS beats; Drax's 2022-23 earnings outperformance was partly a power-price story but also a cost-management story; ongoing cost discipline is required to maintain £850M+ EBITDA floor [#J]

  • Company guide: Management guided to "improving biomass cost trajectory" in FY2024 results; specific pellet cost per tonne guidance is not public but directional commentary is optimistic; long-term supply agreements (LTAs) with Canadian/US suppliers provide partial cost lock [#A]

  • Management commentary: Drax has emphasised supply chain diversification and LTA coverage; investment in pellet plants (Drax owns manufacturing capacity) partially hedges external pellet market volatility [#A]

  • Track record: Drax managed biomass costs well in 2019-2021; 2022-23 spike was partly pass-through under CfD/ROC mechanisms but exposed unhedged volumes to spot market pressures; FY2024 showed improvement [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Management guidance on pellet cost improvement appears credible given market conditions; the key risk is structural (shipping, logistics, forest certification tightening) not cyclical [#J]

  • SS consensus: Consensus models assume gradual pellet cost normalisation through 2025-26; estimates embed a £10-20/tonne reduction from 2023 peak; this appears reasonable but not conservative given structural supply tightness [#A]

  • Consensus changes L12M: Biomass cost assumptions have been revised modestly lower (more optimistic) in L12M; risk is consensus is too optimistic on pace of normalisation [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: A £15/tonne better-than-consensus pellet cost would add ~£195M to EBITDA, implying ~30-40p EPS upside; conversely, a £15/tonne cost overshoot is ~30-40p headwind [#A]

  • Recent delivery: FY2024 results indicated pellet cost improvement vs. FY2023 peak; no specific quantum disclosed publicly but EBITDA trajectory supports the direction [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: Pellet costs rose sharply in 2022 (+30-40% from pre-pandemic) driven by freight, energy, and raw material inflation; partial recovery in 2024; full normalisation to pre-2021 levels is unlikely given structural cost floor increases [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: No cohort peer available; sector context — biomass-dependent generators (Ørsted, smaller European operators) have all flagged similar cost inflation; Drax's vertical integration (pellet manufacturing) is a partial differentiator [#I]

  • quality_flag: thin_peer_context

  • Macro tailwind: Freight cost normalisation since 2023 peak (Baltic Dry Index down ~60% from 2021 highs); energy input costs for pellet manufacturing declining [#A]

  • Macro headwind: EU sustainability certification tightening (RED III) could raise sourcing costs and restrict supplier pool; North American wildfire disruptions remain a tail risk to Canadian supply [#A]

  • Visibility: Medium — LTA coverage provides partial visibility; spot market exposure and sustainability certification risk create meaningful uncertainty [#J]

  • Bear case: RED III implementation and UK equivalent creates certification rejection of current supplier base; Drax must renegotiate supply at higher cost or reduce generation; worst case is a generation reduction/curtailment event [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Pellet costs stabilise at £10-15/tonne above consensus assumptions; EBITDA misses by £130-195M vs. consensus; EPS disappointment drives re-rating from contracted to uncontracted utility multiple [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: A sustained £20/tonne cost overshoot reduces FY2026E EBITDA by ~£260M (~25-30%); EPS impact ~35-50p; significant multiple compression likely [#A]

  • Red flags: UK's own biomass sustainability criteria review (DESNZ consultation 2024) has not resolved; any tightening could retroactively affect ROC eligibility for specific supplier streams [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet: Drax's vertical integration in pellet manufacturing is often cited as a hedge, but owned capacity covers only ~30-35% of total pellet demand — the majority of the portfolio remains exposed to third-party suppliers and their certification risk [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — near-term cost improvement is underway and credible; structural floor is higher than pre-2021, and sustainability/certification risk is a non-trivial tail [#J]

§5 — Key Driver #4: UK Wholesale Power Price Environment

J-front verdict: Power prices are an amplifier, not a driver — the contracted structure caps upside but uncontracted exposure (~30-35% of generation) means power prices materially affect EPS in either direction [#J]

  • Driver definition: Drax generates electricity primarily from biomass; contracted revenues (CfD, ROCs) reference strike prices with limited direct wholesale exposure, but uncontracted generation and the "floor" below strike prices both reference UK day-ahead/forward markets [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Uncontracted generation (approximately 1.0-1.4GW equivalent capacity hours at spot prices); higher spot prices directly add to EBITDA above contracted floor; 2022-23 windfall was partially this mechanism [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Power price narrative affects investor sentiment disproportionately to fundamental EPS impact; market tends to re-rate UK power generators on power price direction as a proxy for energy sector health [#J]

  • Company guide: Drax does not provide explicit power price guidance; management notes hedging policies reduce but do not eliminate price exposure; "contracted position" is the consistent framing [#A]

  • Management commentary: Management has been explicit that CfD/ROC revenues provide the floor; incremental upside from higher power prices is acknowledged but not modelled into guidance [#A]

  • Track record: FY2022-23 EPS benefited materially from elevated power prices; FY2024 showed normalisation as power prices retreated from spikes; the business has demonstrated both directions of this sensitivity [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Management guidance appropriately frames power prices as exogenous; consensus models embed UK baseload power prices of approximately £80-100/MWh for 2025-26, consistent with forward market curves [#A]

  • SS consensus: UK power price consensus for 2025-26 is approximately £80-95/MWh (public forward curves); a £10/MWh move on 1.2GW uncontracted capacity for ~7,000 hours = ~£84M EBITDA impact (~8-10% of group) [#A]

  • Consensus changes L12M: Power price assumptions in consensus have moderated from 2023 elevated levels; current consensus appears broadly aligned with forward curves [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Each £10/MWh power price change = ~£84M EBITDA = ~15-20p EPS (rough estimate); this is meaningful but not thesis-breaking in either direction [#A]

  • Recent delivery: UK day-ahead power prices averaged approximately £70-80/MWh in H2 2024, below FY2023 peak (~£120+/MWh) but above pre-2021 norms (~£50-60/MWh) [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: Power prices elevated 2021-2023 (energy crisis); normalising in 2024-25; forward curves imply gradual decline toward £70-80/MWh by 2026-27 [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: UK's accelerating offshore wind build-out will increase renewable capacity and structurally push baseload prices lower — this is a net headwind for uncontracted biomass generation [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Wind intermittency means Drax's dispatchable biomass is a "peaker" in the new grid — it earns premium prices when wind is low, but average prices will decline as wind capacity grows [#J]

  • Visibility: Low for uncontracted exposure; high for contracted floor [#J]

  • Bear case: UK wholesale power prices fall to £60/MWh on aggressive renewables buildout; uncontracted EBITDA contribution halves from current levels; net impact ~£80-100M EBITDA reduction [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Power price volatility spikes again (cold winter, gas supply disruption) — this would be an EPS positive; the miss scenario is actually sustained low prices, which is the base case under energy transition [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: A sustained £20/MWh power price reduction from consensus = ~£170M EBITDA headwind = ~25-30p EPS reduction [#A]

  • Red flags: UK government's electricity market reform (Review of Electricity Market Arrangements — REMA) could restructure how dispatchable generators like Drax earn revenues, introducing significant uncertainty even for contracted positions [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bull case for Drax from power price upside is bounded by the CfD structure (CfDs cap upside via two-way contract); the bear case from power price decline is NOT bounded — uncontracted exposure is asymmetrically risky to the downside [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — power price normalisation is the consensus scenario and the contracted floor is real; but REMA reform risk and asymmetric downside on uncontracted exposure add meaningful uncertainty [#J]

§6 — Key Driver #5: Flexible Generation and Pumped Hydro Optionality

J-front verdict: Drax's flexible generation assets and pumped storage aspirations are long-dated options, not near-term EPS contributors — relevant to 36-60M timeframe, not 24M [#J]

  • Driver definition: Beyond Drax Power Station, the group owns gas peaking plants (~1.5GW open-cycle gas turbine capacity, Cruachan pumped storage hydro in Scotland ~0.44GW); management has outlined expansion of Cruachan to ~1.04GW and development of further flexible capacity [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Flexible assets earn revenues from capacity market auctions, balancing mechanism dispatches, and ancillary services; as UK grid becomes more renewable-heavy, flexible capacity premiums should increase [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Pumped storage expansion (Cruachan+) is a long-dated infrastructure story; consensus currently assigns limited value but successful permitting/development could add £100-200M to SOTP [#A]

  • Company guide: Management has guided to Cruachan expansion as a "significant long-term value creation opportunity"; capital cost estimates of ~£500-700M; planning permitting ongoing [#A]

  • Management commentary: Cruachan expansion is consistently cited in capital allocation discussions; Drax management frames it as a "strategic fit" with the flexible generation business [#A]

  • Track record: Cruachan has operated reliably since 1965; expansion project is at early permitting stage — no delivery track record on new build hydro [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Management is optimistic on flexible generation revenue potential; independent analysis suggests UK capacity market prices remain subdued relative to what would be needed to incentivise new pumped storage [#J]

  • SS consensus: Most sell-side models treat flexible generation as a stable but modest contributor (~£100-150M EBITDA); Cruachan expansion is SOTP optionality, not in base-case EPS [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Current flexible/hydro EBITDA contribution ~£100-150M; Cruachan expansion adds incrementally but 10+ years to full contribution; 24M share price impact is driven by permitting milestones, not earnings [#A]

  • Recent delivery: Capacity market auction results in FY2024 broadly in line with prior year; no step-change in flexible generation earnings; Cruachan permitting progressing but no major milestone in L12M [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: UK grid flexibility need is growing rapidly as wind penetration increases; government has published flexible storage ambition (24GW by 2030 from ~5GW today) — structural tailwind [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Capacity market auction designs have historically underpriced flexibility; regulatory uncertainty on how pumped storage is compensated in a reformed electricity market limits investor confidence [#A]

  • Visibility: Low for Cruachan expansion value realisation within 24M; medium for existing flexible generation capacity market revenues [#J]

  • Bear case: REMA reform disadvantages incumbents; capacity market auction clearing prices decline; Cruachan expansion faces planning refusal or cost overruns; net contribution from flexible generation segment stagnates [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Capacity market clearing prices decline 15-20% from current levels; flexible generation EBITDA falls £15-25M; modest but not thesis-breaking [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: Flexible generation miss is a £15-30M EBITDA headwind in isolation — not case-breaking, but adds to multi-driver squeeze if occurring simultaneously with other misses [#A]

  • Red flags: Cruachan permitting timeline has already extended; pumped storage projects globally have a poor track record of on-time/on-budget delivery [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet: The flexible generation and pumped storage story is intellectually compelling as a grid-balancing play, but within a 24M investment horizon it is essentially option value with no near-term cash payoff — Richard's 18M-3Y EPS focus means this driver is likely a distraction [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Low-medium plausibility within 24M — the structural case is genuine but the earnings delivery timeline extends well beyond Richard's horizon; permitting and capital allocation risk are both real [#J]

§7 — Key Driver #6: Political and Subsidy Regime Risk

J-front verdict: Political risk is a binary downside driver, not a gradual headwind — the probability is low but the magnitude is case-destroying; it deserves explicit driver status rather than being buried as a footnote risk [#J]

  • Driver definition: Drax's entire earnings model depends on UK government subsidy frameworks (ROCs, CfDs, capacity market); a material change to any of these — including retrospective amendment, non-renewal, or redesign — would impair EPS permanently [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Direct: ROC clawback or cap reduces contracted revenue by up to £500M+ pa; CfD redesign changes strike prices; capacity market reform affects flexible generation revenues; any one of these would be materially dilutive [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Policy risk is a permanent de-rating mechanism — once investors price a subsidy review, the contracted utility multiple is gone; re-rating back to contracted multiple requires policy certainty restoration, which can take years [#J]

  • Company guide: Management does not explicitly guide on political risk; they consistently frame the regulatory environment as "supportive of the energy transition" — an optimistic framing of a genuine binary risk [#A]

  • Management commentary: CEO Will Gardiner has engaged extensively with DESNZ and parliamentary committees; Drax has made public arguments for BECCS strategic importance; lobbying is intensive but not a guarantee [#A]

  • Track record: Drax has navigated multiple rounds of policy change successfully (ROC allocation, CfD early implementation); the company has political capital but also political vulnerability (largest single recipient of UK renewable energy subsidies) [#J]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Management and consensus consistently underweight political risk — this is structurally true of regulated/contracted utilities until a review actually happens, at which point valuation gaps are large [#J]

  • SS consensus: Sell-side models embed minimal political risk premium; most brokers note it as a risk factor but do not explicitly discount EPS for probability of adverse policy change [#A]

  • Consensus changes L12M: Political risk has not meaningfully entered consensus valuation despite heightened parliamentary scrutiny; this is a potential mispricing [#J]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Full ROC clawback or equivalent (low probability, high impact) = 40-60% EPS reduction; partial ROC review (medium probability) = 15-25% EPS reduction [#A]

  • Recent delivery: No adverse policy change has been implemented; however, DESNZ biomass sustainability consultation (2024) is ongoing and outcome is uncertain [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: Since 2022, parliamentary scrutiny has intensified (Energy Security and Net Zero Select Committee, NAO reports on ROC costs); media criticism of Drax subsidies has increased; risk is elevated vs. 2019-21 baseline [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: UK energy security agenda protects large-scale baseload renewable generators from near-term policy attack; government has explicitly stated intention to maintain existing contracted positions [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Cost-of-living crisis increases political sensitivity of energy subsidies; Drax is the highest-profile single recipient of ROC payments (~£700M+ pa); this creates adverse political optics [#A]

  • Visibility: Very low — political risk is by definition an exogenous, binary, poorly-visible risk [#J]

  • Bear case: UK government initiates ROC review post-2025 election; Drax stock de-rates 30-40% on announcement before any earnings impact; parallel BECCS CfD support withdrawn; thesis destroyed [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Biomass sustainability consultation results in tighter criteria that disqualify a portion of Drax's supply; ROC revenues reduced 10-15%; EBITDA hit £70-100M [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: Severe political scenario (ROC windfall review) = 40-60% EPS destruction and permanent multiple de-rating; moderate scenario (tighter sustainability criteria) = 10-20% EPS reduction [#A]
