Section 3 – What the company does 12 findings

Section summary
  1. The business utilizes an asset-light, formulation-led specialty distribution model characterized by high customer stickiness and a massive consolidation runway in a highly fragmented global market.

Segment Operations and Value Chain Position

1.
Life Sciences dominance: The company distributes specialty chemical inputs across two distinct divisions, with Life Sciences generating 63.00% and Industrial Chemicals generating 37.00% of FY25 revenue. Life Sciences serves highly defensive end markets, including Pharmaceuticals, Food & Nutrition, Personal Care, and Agriscience
2.
Industrial Chemicals exposure: Industrial Chemicals operates in more cyclical end markets, covering Coatings, Adhesives, Sealants, Elastomers, and Electronics. Volume performance in these segments remains subdued, reflecting broader global industrial production trends
3.
Outsourced asset-light footprint: Azelis operates an asset-light model by leasing almost all of its offices, blending sites, and formulation laboratories. This setup minimizes annual capital expenditure to less than 0.50% of revenue, supporting robust cash conversion
4.
Value chain role: Azelis acts as a catalyst in the supply chain, handling the complex distribution of mandated products for large chemical principals. It distributes these specialty chemicals to small-and-medium-sized accounts that principals cannot reach cost-effectively
5.
Fragmented customer base: The group distributes complementary products to over 65,000 customers globally, supported by more than 2,800 principal relationships. This extreme fragmentation ensures that Azelis faces minimal customer concentration risk

Technical Differentiation and Competitive Moats

6.
Application lab differentiation: Azelis operates an extensive network of over 70 specialized formulation laboratories globally. These labs proactively co-develop custom formulations with customers, driving high commercial loyalty
7.
Formulation switching costs: Specialty chemical inputs represent a tiny fraction (~15.00% to 20.00%) of final end-product costs but determine performance. Consequently, customers face high technical switching costs and are highly unwilling to risk re-engineering existing products
8.
Digital scale benefits: The group has implemented a unified, cloud-based Microsoft D365 ERP and CRM digital backbone. This digital ecosystem features over 140 customer portals and virtual application e-Labs, driving cross-selling efficiency
9.
Consolidation market runway: The global specialty distribution market is valued at over EUR200bn and remains highly fragmented. The top 10 players control less than 15.00% of the market, offering a vast runway for M&A consolidation
10.
Outsourcing secular tailwind: Chemical principals are continuously seeking to rationalize direct sales networks and outsource distribution to global platforms. This trend supports a mid-term market growth rate of approximately 3.50% to 4.00% per annum
11.
Other side of the story: In severe economic downturns, chemical principals may resort to direct sales strategies to defend their own direct margins. This direct encroachment would temporarily bypass Azelis, eroding its volume and organic margin progression

2.

M&A-led top-line growth: Multi-year top-line expansion has been heavily driven by M&A, with the group completing eight acquisitions representing EUR140m in revenues during FY24. 2019-2025 gross profit CAGR stood at 13.90%
5.

Subsidiary turnaround support: The successful resolution of past operational issues at its Vigon subsidiary will support earnings. Management confirmed the situation was fully resolved in the summer of 2025

Section 5 – The possible investment case 10 findings

Section summary
  1. The investment case relies on a cyclical rebound in specialty volumes and raw material price pass-through, alongside deleveraging to unlock the historical M&A roll-up model.

Core Forward Drivers and Plausibility

1.
Secular outsourcing trend: The outsourcing trend from chemical principals continues as manufacturers seek to simplify operations. This driver has high plausibility, as cost-pressured principals shut down in-house salesforces
2.
Deleveraging to reactivate M&A: Rebuilding balance sheet headroom by reducing net leverage below 3.00x is a management priority. This high-plausibility driver will allow the group to reactivate its highly accretive bolt-on M&A engine
3.
Raw material cost pass-through: shipping-related disruptions in the Middle East have triggered portfolio price increases of up to 40.00%
4.
Raw material cost pass-through (cont.): This has medium plausibility, as pricing power is contingent on underlying customer volume support
5.
Chinese competition normalization: Competition from Chinese suppliers in semi-specialties is easing as volumes are redirected to China. This medium-plausibility driver should stabilize gross profit margins in Southeast Asia
6.
Reformulation secular demand: Clean-label consumer shifts and regulatory updates are driving constant demand for natural reformulations. This high-plausibility driver leverages the group's network of over 70 laboratories

Synthesis of Bull Case Bottlenecks

7.
Cumulative case bottlenecks: For the bull case of mid-single-digit organic growth to work, independent drivers do not need to compound simultaneously
8.
Cumulative case bottlenecks (cont.): Instead, recovering underlying customer demand visibility is the primary bottleneck
9.
Other side of the story: If underlying demand remains soggy, any near-term pricing or pre-buying uplift will be moderate and short-lived. This would fail to trigger the operating leverage required for meaningful EBITA margin expansion

Section 7 – Key risks and the other side 9 findings

Section summary
  1. Geopolitical supply chain disruptions and debt-related M&A growth constraints represent credible threats to earnings that require rigorous credit and operational monitoring.

Structural and Operational Earnings Risks

1.
Digital platform disintermediation: The rise of digital distribution platforms threatens to reduce information asymmetry, introducing structural disintermediation risk. This risk is particularly acute for the group's highly fragmented smaller customers
2.
Prolonged industrial slowdown: Continued cyclical weakness in the CASE and metalworking segments could drag EMEA operating leverage down further. Industrial Chemicals segment recovery remains slow and uneven
3.
Credit rating risk: EBITDA net leverage of 3.70x (Fitch-adjusted) sits above Fitch's negative rating sensitivity of 3.50x. A sustained breach risks a downgrade of the BB+ credit rating, increasing borrowing costs
4.
Emerging market mix dilution: Rapid expansion into Latin America and emerging EMEA markets has diluted gross margins. These regions structurally carry lower gross profit profiles
5.
Refinancing maturity concentration: Although near-term maturities are cleared, Azelis faces a concentration of EUR1.20 billion in maturities in 2029. This requires proactive capital restructuring over the medium term

Monitoring Signals and Protective Moats

6.
Cash flow false friend: A high cash conversion ratio (e.g. 106.00% in FY25) can act as a false friend. This cash performance is driven counter-cyclically by inventory liquidation rather than business growth
7.
Early warning signal: Investors must monitor monthly order size and order frequency as a key early-warning signal. High order frequency with tiny order sizes indicates persistent lack of customer demand visibility
8.
Other side of the story: Risks are largely mitigated because specialty formulations are deeply embedded in customer product claims. Customers are highly unwilling to risk expensive product failures to achieve minor input cost savings
