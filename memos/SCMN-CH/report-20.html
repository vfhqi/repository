3. THEME SECTIONS

§2 — Theme 1: Revenue Drivers + Visibility

J-front verdict: Swiss revenue is ex-growth in volume terms; pricing power is the only variable — Fastweb Italy volume growth is real but execution-dependent, and Italy competitive intensity is rising

Signpost — Volume vs price split:

Q1. In Swiss residential, what percentage of 2024 ARPU growth came from price increases vs upsell to higher-tier plans — and is that ratio sustainable through 2026?

  • Expected answer: Management will blend the two, citing convergence upsell as a structural driver, without cleanly splitting price vs volume
  • Good answer signature: Clean 60/40 or similar split, with named plan-tier migration data and churn-by-segment quantification
  • Bad answer signature: Vague "value over volume" framing with no underlying metric; or heavy reliance on one-off price indexation
  • Follow-up probe: "If you stripped out the March 2024 price adjustment, what is the underlying organic ARPU trend in the base?"
  • Diagnostic note: Bull hypothesis — Swisscom has structural pricing power in a high-willingness-to-pay Swiss market; Bear hypothesis — ARPU is a statistical artefact of churn of low-ARPU customers, not genuine pricing power; Fluent answer discriminates bull; deflection discriminates bear

Signpost — Churn / retention:

Q2. What is churn by product segment — broadband-only vs converged bundle vs mobile-only — and what is the churn delta when a customer takes a converged product?

  • Expected answer: Overall residential churn cited (likely sub-10%), bundle benefit cited qualitatively
  • Good answer signature: Specific churn differential of ≥2-3pp for converged vs single-product; named retention program
  • Bad answer signature: Overall blended churn only; no segment disaggregation; or "bundled customers are more sticky" without data
  • Follow-up probe: "What share of your Swiss residential base is currently in a fully converged bundle, and what is the 3-year target?"
  • Diagnostic note: If converged penetration is already high (>50%), the upsell runway is limited; if low (<30%), the upside is real but so is competitive vulnerability in the transition

Signpost — End-market segmentation (Italy):

Q3. For Fastweb in Italy, break down 2024 revenue growth between Consumer, SME, and Enterprise — and which segment has the highest gross margin and why?

  • Expected answer: Enterprise/B2B cited as higher-margin anchor; Consumer growth cited as volume-driven
  • Good answer signature: Specific revenue split and margin differentials by segment; coherent explanation of why Fastweb competes differently vs TIM in each
  • Bad answer signature: "Balanced growth across segments" with no specifics; or emphasis on Consumer volume without margin disclosure
  • Follow-up probe: "Is the Enterprise margin premium structural — i.e. contract terms and switching costs — or is it currently subsidised by below-market infrastructure pricing from Open Fiber?"
  • Diagnostic note: If Enterprise margin depends on below-cost access pricing, it is regulatory-duration-dependent, not structural; this discriminates bull (durable moat) from false-friend (regulatory arbitrage)

Signpost — Forward order book (Italy fiber):

Q4. What is Fastweb's FTTH activated home count today versus the contractual homes-passed target under the Open Fiber co-investment agreement — and what is the activation conversion rate?

  • Expected answer: Homes-passed number cited (public); activation rate likely not disclosed in detail
  • Good answer signature: Specific activation rate (ideally >30%), with cohort data on how conversion improves with tenure of the FTTH footprint
  • Bad answer signature: Homes-passed emphasized; activation rate avoided or cited at blended level that obscures early-cohort drag
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the average monthly ARPU on a newly activated FTTH customer vs legacy ADSL — and what is the payback period at that delta?"
  • Diagnostic note: Low activation rates make the Italian fiber story a capex-heavy option value, not a near-term EPS contributor; critical for the 18-24M EPS trajectory the reader cares about

Sceptical bullet — §2: A management team that answers Q1-Q4 fluently is not necessarily a comfort signal — Swiss telecom pricing and bundle mechanics are well-rehearsed with analysts; rehearsed fluency on Italy fiber may indicate investor-relations polish rather than genuine operational confidence; watch for whether the CFO defers Italy specifics to a future Fastweb CMD

§3 — Theme 2: Margin Trajectory + Levers

J-front verdict: Swiss EBITDA margin is near-peak; Fastweb dilutes Group margin near-term; the question is whether Italian operating leverage is real and when it arrives

Signpost — Op leverage (Italy):

Q5. At what Fastweb revenue run-rate does Italian EBITDA margin converge to Swiss domestic levels — and what are the three specific cost levers that drive that convergence?

  • Expected answer: Management will cite "mid-term" convergence without a specific number; three levers will include network cost reduction, IT integration, and headcount rationalisation
  • Good answer signature: Named revenue threshold (e.g. "above €2bn revenue, network cost per GB drops below X"); specific FTE reduction timeline with euros attached
  • Bad answer signature: "Structural improvement ongoing" with no bridge; margin convergence framed as a narrative goal rather than a calculable outcome
  • Follow-up probe: "Of the €X synergy target you've announced, how much has been realised in the income statement vs still in the pipeline — and is the pipeline risk-weighted?"
  • Diagnostic note: Margin convergence is the key earnings catalyst for 2026-2028; if management can't articulate the bridge, EPS upgrades are not near-term

Signpost — Capex pacing:

Q6. What is the peak year for Swisscom Group capex — and when does capex intensity (capex/revenue) begin a structural decline?

  • Expected answer: CFO will likely cite 2025-2026 as the peak investment period; declining thereafter
  • Good answer signature: Specific capex/revenue trajectory; named projects whose completion triggers the decline (e.g. Swiss FTTH rollout completion, Fastweb core network integration)
  • Bad answer signature: "Investment cycle" framing without commitment; or capex described as "flexible and demand-driven" (a hedge that signals no firm commitment to decline)
  • Follow-up probe: "If the Italian regulatory environment forces accelerated Open Fiber co-investment contributions, what is the incremental capex exposure — and does that come from the Swiss program or is it additive?"
  • Diagnostic note: Peak capex timing is directly linked to FCF generation and dividend sustainability — the most financially material question in this theme

Signpost — Pricing-cost spread:

Q7. Energy costs are a significant and variable input for network operators — what percentage of Swiss and Italian operating costs are energy-linked, and what is your hedging horizon?

  • Expected answer: Energy flagged as a headwind in 2022-2023 but partially hedged; hedging horizon probably 12-18 months
  • Good answer signature: Specific energy cost as % of opex; hedge coverage disclosed with tenor; quantification of residual exposure
  • Bad answer signature: "We monitor energy costs closely" without disclosure; or bundled into generic cost-efficiency language
  • Follow-up probe: "Are Swiss network energy costs fixed in the regulatory framework — i.e. can you pass through energy inflation in wholesale pricing — or is the exposure entirely on your P&L?"
  • Diagnostic note: Energy passthrough asymmetry is a hidden margin risk in bear scenarios involving energy re-inflation; consensus models likely assume normalised energy costs

Signpost — R&D adequacy:

Q8. What share of your IT/transformation budget is genuinely discretionary vs committed — and if you faced a 15% FCF shortfall, which transformation projects would you defer first?

  • Expected answer: Management will resist the "15% shortfall" framing; likely to distinguish between "growth capex" and "maintenance capex" without being specific about what is genuinely deferrable
  • Good answer signature: Named project with specific cost and deferral consequence; clear internal framework for triage
  • Bad answer signature: "All investment is essential to our strategy" — a classic deflection that signals management views the question as adversarial
  • Follow-up probe: "In 2023-2024, did you defer any planned IT transformation spend — and if so, what was the business consequence?"
  • Diagnostic note: Willingness to answer the deferral question honestly is a culture-quality probe; it also discriminates between bull (genuine operating leverage runway) and bear (capex already structurally elevated)

Sceptical bullet — §3: Swiss EBITDA margin is well-modelled by sell-side; the real margin uncertainty lives in Italy, where Fastweb is a newer story and analyst forecasts rely heavily on management guidance — management fluency on Swiss margin is expected and unremarkable; diagnostic signal lives in whether the CFO can give a granular Fastweb EBITDA bridge

§4 — Theme 3: Capital Allocation + Balance Sheet

J-front verdict: Leverage is at the upper end of stated target; dividend commitment is political as well as financial given Confederation ownership; the diagnostic question is what management would actually do if FCF fell 20%

Signpost — Dividend policy:

Q9. What is the minimum FCF cover ratio at which the Board would recommend maintaining the current dividend — and has that threshold been explicitly discussed at Board level?

  • Expected answer: Management will restate the dividend policy commitment without disclosing a specific FCF cover floor; likely to cite "stable and reliable dividend" framing
  • Good answer signature: Specific cover ratio (e.g. "we would not recommend a cut unless FCF cover fell below 1.0x") plus acknowledgement that the Confederation's income dependency creates a constraint
  • Bad answer signature: Dividend "commitment" restated without any stress scenario; or the question deflected to "we don't comment on hypothetical scenarios"
  • Follow-up probe: "In 2025-2026 if Italian capex runs above plan, would the Board consider suspending the SBB programme before cutting the dividend — what is the explicit sequencing?"
  • Diagnostic note: If management cannot articulate the sequencing between dividend, SBB, and capex in a stress scenario, the capital allocation framework is politically driven rather than financially disciplined — a false-friend signal

Signpost — Leverage target:

Q10. Your stated net debt/EBITDA range is 1.5-2.0x — you are currently near the top of that range. What would cause you to operate above 2.0x temporarily, and what is the return-to-range timeline?

  • Expected answer: Italian capex cited as the reason for current elevation; return to mid-range by 2026-2027
  • Good answer signature: Specific FCF-to-debt-reduction pathway; named project completions that trigger deleveraging; acknowledged upper bound above which refinancing cost rises
  • Bad answer signature: "Comfortable within our range" without acknowledgement of current top-of-range positioning; or vague "medium-term" normalisation
  • Follow-up probe: "If interest rates re-rise materially from current levels before your next refinancing window, what is the incremental annual interest cost — and does that change the leverage comfort zone?"
  • Diagnostic note: Credit cost sensitivity matters because Swisscom refinances in the EUR market — a rate reset would directly affect interest cover and dividend headroom; not fully priced in consensus

Signpost — M&A philosophy:

Q11. Is there any material M&A contemplated in Italy beyond organic Fastweb build — and how do you think about the Vodafone Italy / WINDTRE competitive dynamic as a potential structural consolidation opportunity?

  • Expected answer: Management will be guarded; likely to cite organic-first strategy; will not comment on specific targets
  • Good answer signature: Clear statement of what would trigger an M&A appetite (e.g. "if a network-sharing deal with a third party creates a 15% network cost reduction, we'd consider it"); honest acknowledgement of balance sheet constraint
  • Bad answer signature: "We don't comment on M&A speculation" — this is non-informative but watch for body language / tone; any divergence from prior public statements on M&A stance is signal
  • Follow-up probe: "The Italian market has historically under-consolidated relative to other European markets — do you believe four full mobile operators is sustainable in Italy for the next five years, or does structural consolidation become a necessity?"
  • Diagnostic note: ⚡ — See §8 for the unconventional consolidation probe

Sceptical bullet — §4: Swisscom's capital allocation is partially exogenous — the Confederation's ~51% stake means dividend decisions are not purely financial; management fluency on capital allocation may reflect Confederation expectations rather than intrinsic financial discipline; this is a structural limitation on the diagnostic value of Q9-Q11

§5 — Theme 4: Strategy + Competitive Position

J-front verdict: Swiss strategic moat is durable but mature; Italy is the growth vector but faces a more competitive market structure; the diagnostic question is whether Fastweb's differentiation is sustainable or is a second-mover story running out of room

Signpost — Moat trajectory (Switzerland):

Q12. Sunrise has been aggressive in converged bundle pricing since its re-listing — have you seen any measurable share loss in any Swiss residential segment, and what is the specific competitive response mechanism?

  • Expected answer: Management will acknowledge competitive pressure but cite retention metrics to argue share is stable; they will not volunteer segment-level share loss
  • Good answer signature: Specific segment data (e.g. "We have seen modest share pressure in the 25-35 age urban cohort, which we are addressing with X") — honesty about segment-specific vulnerability is a management quality signal
  • Bad answer signature: "Market share broadly stable" with no segment granularity; or framing Sunrise as non-threatening
  • Follow-up probe: "In the last 12 months, has the cost of retaining a at-risk customer (retention incentive value) increased, decreased, or stayed flat — and what does the trend say about competitive intensity?"
  • Diagnostic note: Retention cost inflation is an early-warning signal for ARPU compression before it shows in headline numbers; not visible in public filings

Signpost — Competitive response (Italy):

Q13. In Italian Enterprise/B2B, who is your primary competitive threat — TIM, Vodafone Business, or new digital-native entrants — and on what basis do you win or lose a competitive tender?

  • Expected answer: TIM cited as primary competitor; win basis cited as price-performance and service quality
  • Good answer signature: Specific win-rate data on tenders; named customer verticals where Fastweb consistently wins and why; acknowledgement of where Fastweb is price-competitive rather than quality-differentiated
  • Bad answer signature: Generic "quality of service and customer experience" framing without any win-rate or competitive displacement data
  • Follow-up probe: "What percentage of Fastweb Enterprise revenue is under contract with more than 18 months remaining — and what is the average contract length in SME vs large Enterprise?"
  • Diagnostic note: Contract duration is the primary visibility metric for Italian B2B revenue; short contracts in a competitive market = false-friend scenario

Signpost — Disruption risk:

Q14. What is your assessment of Starlink and LEO satellite as a competitive threat to Swiss broadband penetration in rural or enterprise backup use cases — and have you seen any evidence of substitution in the last 12 months?

  • Expected answer: LEO cited as a niche/rural product; not seen as a threat to core FTTH proposition
  • Good answer signature: Specific data on rural broadband churn patterns; honest acknowledgement of the backup/enterprise edge case where LEO is gaining; a named mitigation strategy
  • Bad answer signature: "Satellite is complementary, not competitive" — this is the standard industry deflection and should be challenged
  • Follow-up probe: "If Starlink achieves 50ms latency and 500Mbps in Swiss rural markets within 3 years — which is technically plausible — what percentage of your rural FTTH investment would be at risk of being stranded?"
  • Diagnostic note: Stranded asset risk on Swiss FTTH is a long-duration bear scenario; management's comfort with the question tests whether they've genuinely stress-tested their infrastructure strategy

Sceptical bullet — §5: Strategy Q&A is the section most susceptible to polished non-answers — CEOs are skilled at competitive positioning language that sounds specific but conveys no information; the follow-up probes in Q12-Q14 are designed to break through the first-order answer

§6 — Theme 5: Management Quality + Operator Track Record

J-front verdict: Swisscom's management is SOE-constrained, which limits both upside ambition and downside recklessness; the diagnostic question is whether the CEO thinks like an owner or an administrator

Signpost — Power of incentives:

Q15. Can you walk me through the specific LTI vesting conditions — which metrics, what weighting, and what is the threshold vs maximum payout — and how closely does that align with equity FCF per share?

  • Expected answer: LTI metrics likely include relative TSR, EBITDA, and a sustainability/ESG component; FCF per share may not be a primary metric
  • Good answer signature: Named metrics with explicit weightings; acknowledgement of any tension between TSR and FCF per share; CEO demonstrates they actively think about how their incentives align with minority shareholders
  • Bad answer signature: "Our compensation is disclosed in the Annual Report" — a deflection that signals discomfort with the question; or over-reliance on TSR without FCF correlation
  • Follow-up probe: "If EBITDA grows but FCF per share is flat or falling — for example because capex inflates — is that a vesting success or a failure under your current LTI framework?"
  • Diagnostic note (Concept A — Power of incentives): If LTI vests on EBITDA without FCF discipline, management is incentivised to invest capital without adequate return discipline — the Confederation's SOE context amplifies this risk; this is a direct bull/false-friend discriminator

Signpost — Sandbagging discipline:

Q16. Looking at your last three annual guidance sets, have you systematically met or beaten them — and can you point to an instance where you deliberately set guidance conservatively and why?

  • Expected answer: Track record will be cited selectively; management unlikely to admit sandbagging explicitly
  • Good answer signature: Honest acknowledgement of at least one instance where guidance was set below internal expectation; explanation of why conservatism serves shareholders (avoids momentum-chasing)
  • Bad answer signature: "We set guidance to be achievable" without specifics; or defensiveness about missed guidance years without candid explanation
  • Follow-up probe: "For 2025-2026, is your current guidance set with a buffer — i.e. do you have a scenario where you beat it by more than 5%, and what would trigger that?"
  • Diagnostic note: Sandbagging discipline is a quality-of-earnings signal; a CEO who cannot distinguish between their sandbagging and stretch targets is either not tracking the difference (operator quality concern) or managing the analyst relationship (credibility concern)

Signpost — Operator track record (Fastweb):

Q17. Since the Fastweb acquisition was agreed, name one decision you made in the integration that you would make differently — and what did you learn from it?

  • Expected answer: Management will cite a minor process issue or a positive "learning" framing; unlikely to volunteer a materially costly mistake
  • Good answer signature: Named decision with a quantifiable cost or delay consequence; demonstrates genuine retrospective learning rather than PR-managed humility
  • Bad answer signature: "The integration has been running ahead of plan" — a complete deflection; or a "learning" so minor it is not credible
  • Follow-up probe: "Has the cultural integration between Swisscom's Swiss operating culture and Fastweb's Italian entrepreneurial culture created any operational tensions — and how are you managing them?"
  • Diagnostic note: The quality of the answer to Q17 is the single best management quality probe in this memo — it tests candour, self-awareness, and whether management has processed the reality of a cross-border integration

Signpost — Crisis playbook:

Q18. If Swisscom experienced a major network outage lasting 48+ hours in Switzerland — comparable to the 2023 outage — what is the specific internal improvement that has been made since, and how would the customer compensation obligation affect the P&L?

  • Expected answer: Infrastructure investments cited; regulatory obligations on compensation noted
  • Good answer signature: Named redundancy investment with a cost attached; specific quantification of compensation liability under Swiss regulatory framework; honest assessment of residual tail risk
  • Bad answer signature: "Our network resilience has been significantly improved" without specifics; deflection to network statistics without P&L impact
  • Follow-up probe: "Is there a scenario where a major outage would trigger a regulatory review of Swisscom's network investment obligations — and how is that risk modelled in your capex plan?"
  • Diagnostic note: Regulatory reputational risk in Switzerland is a tail item but a real one; management's comfort with the P&L consequences tests whether this is genuinely stress-tested or treated as boilerplate risk disclosure

Sceptical bullet — §6: SOE-constrained management teams are disproportionately skilled at presenting stability rather than upside potential — the CEO who is a genuinely good operator may be indistinguishable in a management Q&A from the CEO who is a talented administrator; Q17's "what would you do differently" is the most diagnostic quality test because it punishes the administrator more than the operator

§7 — Theme 6: Risk + Downside (Pre-mortem Cross-ref)

J-front verdict: The highest-plausibility downside scenarios are Italian execution risk and Swiss pricing erosion under converged bundle competition — the questions below test whether management has genuinely stress-tested these

Signpost — Bear-case probe (Italy regulatory):

Q19. The Open Fiber co-investment agreement is governed by AGCOM rules that have evolved and may evolve further — what is the specific regulatory scenario that would most damage Fastweb's Italian fiber economics, and how have you hedged against it?

  • Expected answer: Regulatory risk acknowledged; management will cite AGCOM engagement and note that the framework is settled; unlikely to volunteer a specific adverse scenario
  • Good answer signature: Named AGCOM decision risk (e.g. access pricing reset, co-investment contribution formula change); specific financial impact disclosed; hedging mechanism named
  • Bad answer signature: "We work constructively with Italian regulators" — a standard non-answer; or framing the regulatory environment as entirely benign
  • Follow-up probe: "If AGCOM mandated a significant reduction in wholesale access prices in Italy, what is the Fastweb revenue impact — and is the Italian Enterprise/B2B business exposed to the same regulatory repricing?"
  • Diagnostic note (Q19 ACH cross-ref): Italian regulatory failure is the highest-plausibility bear scenario that is under-covered in sell-side consensus; this question is directly diagnostic of whether the Italian growth story is durable or regulatory-duration-dependent

Signpost — False-friend probe:

Q20. Swisscom trades at a premium to European telecom peers on EV/EBITDA — what do you believe justifies that premium, and what is the single factor that, if it changed, would cause you to believe the premium is not deserved?

  • Expected answer: Management will cite network quality, dividend stability, and Swiss market position as premium justification; unlikely to offer a genuine downside scenario
  • Good answer signature: Honest acknowledgement of the premium's dependency on one or two specific factors (e.g. Swiss ARPU stability; Fastweb growth delivery); willingness to name the condition under which the premium is not defensible
  • Bad answer signature: "We believe our premium is fully justified by our fundamentals" — a tautology; or deflection to ESG/sustainability premium rationale
  • Follow-up probe: "At what Italian EBITDA delivery level does the Group growth story become credible enough to sustain the premium — i.e. is there a number you are targeting?"
  • Diagnostic note: The false-friend scenario is that Swisscom's premium is a legacy of Swiss defensive-capital status and will compress as Italy integration risk is recognised; this question tests whether management is aware of that dynamic or is complacent about the premium

Signpost — Pricing fragility:

Q21. In Swiss SME/B2B, have you seen any evidence of pricing pressure from digital-native competitors — Teleclinic, cloud PBX providers, UCaaS entrants — eroding the traditional voice and data contract base?

  • Expected answer: Management will acknowledge cloud migration trend but cite managed migration strategy
  • Good answer signature: Specific revenue at risk quantified; named migration playbook with ARPU impact modelled; honest assessment of whether Swisscom is gaining or losing in cloud-native SME
  • Bad answer signature: "Our SME offering has been modernised" without revenue-at-risk disclosure; or framing digital substitution as a non-issue
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the average ARPU of an SME customer who migrates from a legacy voice/data contract to a cloud-first package — is it higher or lower, and what is the trend?"
  • Diagnostic note: B2B digital substitution is a slow-moving but structural revenue risk; if ARPU on cloud migration is lower, it is a hidden revenue headwind not captured in consensus

Sceptical bullet — §7 (ACH gap disclosure): Q19 ACH-matrix items NOT covered by any question in this memo include: (a) Swiss residential market structural saturation leading to SIM-only substitution gaining share, (b) FX volatility impact on CHF/EUR consolidation (Fastweb reports in EUR; Swisscom consolidates in CHF), (c) specific Swiss regulatory decisions on wholesale fibre access pricing — these items are flagged as open gaps to be addressed in post-meeting back-brief or in a Q19 ACH update

§8 — Cross-Cutting Meta + Non-Obvious Questions

J-front verdict: The most diagnostic questions are not the revenue or margin questions — they are the incentive structure probe and the "what would change your mind" question; management's handling of those two reveals more than ten conventional questions

Signpost — Non-obvious angle:

Q22 ⚡ — WH Smith probe. "If you were a new entrant to the Italian telecom market with CHF 3bn of capital, would you build what Fastweb has built — or would you build something structurally different? What would you build?"

  • Diagnostic note: This forces management to evaluate Fastweb's design from first principles rather than defending a prior decision; a CEO who thinks like an owner will engage with the counterfactual; an administrator will deflect or re-narrate the acquisition rationale
  • Expected answer: Deflection to "Fastweb is an excellent asset" framing
  • Good answer signature: Genuine engagement with the counterfactual — "actually we'd build the Enterprise segment first because the Consumer economics are tougher than expected"
  • Bad answer signature: "We're proud of what Fastweb has achieved" — complete avoidance of the premise

Signpost — Edge-detection probe:

Q23 ⚡ — What single metric, if it moved against you in the next 12 months, would most concern you — and what is the current trend on that metric?

  • Diagnostic note (Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 3): This is a "multiple-truths" question — it invites management to simultaneously acknowledge the bull case (current performance) and reveal the bear-case vulnerability; the metric they name is the most important forward indicator to track
  • Expected answer: A safe metric cited — e.g. "customer satisfaction scores" or "network quality metrics"
  • Good answer signature: A financially material metric named (e.g. "Italian FTTH activation rate" or "Swiss converged bundle net adds") with a candid current-trend disclosure
  • Bad answer signature: ESG or employee engagement metric cited — a deflection into non-financial territory

Signpost — Incentive probe:

Q24 — Does the Confederation's role as majority shareholder constrain any capital allocation decision you would otherwise make — and is there any investment you have NOT made because of that constraint?

  • Diagnostic note (Concept A): The Confederation creates a unique principal-agent dynamic — dividend income is a Swiss federal budget line item, which means dividend cuts are politically as well as financially costly; this question surfaces whether management is making decisions in the interest of all shareholders or primarily the controlling shareholder
  • Expected answer: "The Confederation is a supportive shareholder and we operate independently" — standard SOE framing
  • Good answer signature: Honest acknowledgement of at least one instance where Confederation dynamics influenced a decision (e.g. "we wouldn't pursue a transformative M&A that required a rights issue because we know the Confederation's fiscal position limits its ability to follow")
  • Bad answer signature: Complete denial of any principal constraint — implausible given SOE structure

Signpost — Multiple-truths probe:

Q25 — Swisscom is often cited as a best-in-class European telco operator — I accept that case, AND I want to understand the bear case from your perspective. What would a well-informed bear say about Swisscom that you think is genuinely partly correct?

  • Diagnostic note (Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 3): This is the explicit "and yet" question — it prevents management from retreating into either bull or bear framing; a CEO who engages with the premise demonstrates intellectual honesty; the content of the bear case they offer is more valuable than any investor presentation
  • Expected answer: Management will offer a bear case they have already pre-refuted — "bears say Swiss market is saturated, but we have Fastweb growth"
  • Good answer signature: A genuine vulnerability acknowledged — "bears are right that our capex intensity is elevated and FCF yield is under pressure; we are betting that Fastweb operating leverage arrives on time"
  • Bad answer signature: The question deflected entirely; or a bear case offered that is so mild it reveals no genuine self-awareness

Signpost — Diagnostic crystalliser:

Q26 — If Fastweb's EBITDA margin has not improved by at least 300bps by end-2026, what is the cause most likely to be — and is that cause within your control?

  • Diagnostic note: This question asks management to pre-commit to the failure mode — it forces a clear articulation of the dependency structure; it also tests whether management has genuine control over the most important earnings lever in the Group
  • Expected answer: Management will express confidence in the 2026 timeline; failure scenario avoided
  • Good answer signature: Named failure modes (regulatory, competitive, integration delay) with probability assessment; honest acknowledgement of which are within and outside control
  • Bad answer signature: "We are confident in our guidance trajectory" — a non-answer

Signpost — Stewing trigger:

Q27 — In the 12 months since we last met [or since the Fastweb deal closed], what has surprised you most — positively and negatively — about the Italian business?

  • Diagnostic note: The negative surprise is the load-bearing answer — what management volunteers as a genuine surprise reveals where their pre-acquisition modelling was wrong; this is the single best integration quality probe after Q17
  • Expected answer: Positive surprise emphasised; negative surprise will be minor or absent
  • Good answer signature: A specific negative surprise named — "Italian enterprise sales cycles are longer than we expected, which has pushed some revenue recognition into 2025 from 2024"
  • Bad answer signature: "It has broadly tracked our expectations" — implausible for a cross-border integration

Sceptical bullet — §8 (Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 1): Questions the analyst has NOT included because asking them would be uncomfortable — (a) "Did Swisscom overpay for Fastweb, and how would you know if you had?" — this directly questions management's capital allocation discipline in a way that is adversarial; (b) "What is the Confederation's position on an eventual privatisation, and has that been discussed at Board level?" — this surfaces the principal-agent question most directly and most uncomfortably; (c) "Is there a scenario in which Fastweb is better owned by a different parent — and have you been approached?" — tests whether management has considered strategic alternatives. All three are omitted from the main flow because they would likely terminate productive dialogue; they should be held for the end of the meeting as optional probes if rapport is high

4. CROSS-CUTTING META

J-front verdict: The most diagnostic questions surface management's incentive structure (Q15), integration candour (Q17, Q27), and Italian regulatory vulnerability (Q19) — these three together discriminate all three hypotheses
