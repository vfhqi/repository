3. PART A — FAILURE NARRATIVES

§2 — Failure Narrative 1: Organic Growth Deceleration Hidden by Acquisition Noise

Narrative: Addtech's revenue growth has historically run at high-single to low-double-digit rates, but a material share has come from bolt-on acquisitions typically contributing 5–8% pa inorganically. If organic growth in legacy units decelerates to 0–2% (consistent with late-cycle industrial demand softening), headline numbers may still show 8–10% growth for 12–18 months before the acquisition pipeline thins or is paused. The EPS transmission mechanism: markets price Addtech on an organic-growth premium multiple; when organic growth is revealed at an analyst day or through slowing Q-o-Q, the re-rating is abrupt and disproportionate to the actual earnings miss.

Early warning signals:
- Organic growth disclosure in quarterly reports drops below 3% for two consecutive quarters
- Order intake growth (where disclosed by division) diverges from revenue growth by >300bps
- Average revenue per acquired business in year 2 post-acquisition underperforms year-1 exit rate
- Management commentary shifts from "strong underlying demand" to "solid" or "stable"
- Gross margin at the divisional level (not group) begins to compress despite positive mix narrative

Plausibility verbal verdict: High plausibility. Swedish industrial distributor/niche technology peers (broadly, conglomerate-style acquirers across Nordic and DACH industrials) have shown this pattern repeatedly in 2015–16 and 2022–23 cycles — organic deceleration trails headline deceleration by 2–4 quarters when acquisition pace is elevated.

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: Addtech has the acquisition pipeline and balance sheet capacity to sustain inorganic revenue growth while organic slows — ✓
- Motive: management compensation is partly tied to EPS growth; maintaining headline growth trajectory has value — ✓ (partial)
- Opportunity: quarterly disclosure aggregates organic and acquired revenue with limited granularity at sub-segment level — ✓
- Verdict: All three legs present — full failure mode, not watch-only

Disconfirming evidence: If Addtech provides granular organic growth disclosure by division (Energy, Industrial Solutions, Life Science etc.) showing consistent 4%+ organic rates, and if order books corroborate, this hypothesis is substantially weakened.

Monitoring checklist:
- Organic growth rate per division (quarterly)
- Order book/backlog commentary per division
- Revenue per acquired entity (year 1 vs year 2)
- Days sales outstanding trend at group level
- Management guidance language shift analysis

Interaction with other failure modes: Directly correlated with FM3 (margin compression from operating-leverage failure) and FM8 (false-friend: earnings beat from unsustainable acquisition timing). Sequential trigger for FM5 (multiple compression on growth narrative break).

§3 — Failure Narrative 2: Customer Concentration Within Acquired Niches

Narrative: Addtech's decentralised model acquires businesses that are frequently leaders in narrow niches — which often means 2–4 customers represent 40–60% of an acquired unit's revenue at point of acquisition. If a major OEM customer in, say, the electrification or automation niche shifts sourcing, vertically integrates a component, or is acquired by a consolidator that renegotiates terms, a single acquired unit can lose 15–30% of its revenue within 12 months. The transmission mechanism: single-unit revenue loss flows directly to EBITA given high operating leverage in small niche businesses; group-level EBITA impact is diluted but cumulative across multiple units produces a step-down.

Early warning signals:
- Specific disclosure of customer churn at business-unit level (rare but occasionally surfaced in capital market days)
- Key customer publicly announcing vertical integration or supply-chain restructuring in Addtech's end-markets
- Unusual decline in a divisional segment's organic revenue in a quarter where the broader market is stable
- Gross margin decline at segment level without explanation from management
- Acquired business appearing in impairment testing disclosure earlier than typical 5-year horizon

Plausibility verbal verdict: Medium plausibility. Addtech's diversification across 130+ operating companies structurally mitigates this, but the risk is non-zero and has historically been disclosed at capital market days when individual units underperformed expectations post-acquisition.

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: concentrated customers in niche units have the scale to shift sourcing — ✓
- Motive: OEM cost reduction and supply chain consolidation are secular trends — ✓
- Opportunity: Addtech's disclosure granularity makes early detection by external investors very difficult — ✓
- Verdict: All three legs — full failure mode

Disconfirming evidence: If Addtech discloses at CMD or in annual reports that no acquired business derives >30% of revenue from a single customer, and post-acquisition revenue retention rates are disclosed at >90% in year 2, this hypothesis is weakened.

Monitoring checklist:
- Customer concentration disclosures at CMD
- Post-acquisition revenue retention (disclosed or derivable)
- Segment organic revenue variance vs market
- Impairment references in annual report notes
- OEM procurement announcements in key end-markets

Interaction with other failure modes: Moderate correlation with FM1 (organic deceleration) and FM7 (competitive/structural disruption in specific niches). Low interaction with FM4 (cost inflation) — independent mechanisms.

§4 — Failure Narrative 3: Margin Compression from Operating-Leverage Failure

Narrative: Addtech's EBITA margins have expanded from ~10% toward 14–16% over the past decade, driven by portfolio mix-shift toward higher-value technology distribution and niche manufacturing. If top-line growth stalls at or below cost-base growth — particularly given the people-intensive nature of Addtech's model (technical sales specialists, product engineers) — operating leverage reverses sharply. The transmission mechanism: Addtech's cost base is 65–70% people-costs with limited variability at the business-unit level; a 2–3% organic revenue shortfall against expectations translates to 100–150bps EBITA margin contraction with a 2-quarter lag.

Early warning signals:
- Staff-cost-to-revenue ratio rising on a rolling 12-month basis across multiple divisions
- Headcount growth continuing into a quarter of organic revenue deceleration (management slow to react)
- SG&A as percentage of revenue inflecting upward in financial statements
- EBITA margin guidance revised down mid-year
- Employee satisfaction/retention indicators (Glassdoor, Swedish labour market data) showing churn in technical roles

Plausibility verbal verdict: Medium plausibility. The structural mix-shift to higher-margin niches provides a durable tailwind, but the base rate for niche industrial distributors facing a revenue inflection point showing 100–200bps margin contraction in the following year is well-established across the European SMID industrial universe.

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: Addtech's cost base is structurally semi-fixed — ✓
- Motive: none required — this is a structural mechanics risk, not behavioural — N/A
- Opportunity: operating leverage failure is latent in any company with high people-cost intensity — ✓
- Verdict: Two of three (motive N/A for structural risk) — watch-only until revenue inflection confirmed

Disconfirming evidence: If Addtech demonstrates variable cost management through a prior downturn cycle (2020 COVID data shows whether headcount was flexed quickly) with margin defending within 50bps, this hypothesis is substantially weakened.

Monitoring checklist:
- Quarterly EBITA margin by segment
- Headcount per revenue unit (annual report)
- Staff costs as % of revenue
- Revenue guidance vs actual by half
- CEO/CFO commentary on cost flexibility

Interaction with other failure modes: Directly triggered by FM1 (organic deceleration); correlated with FM6 (key executive departure slowing cost response); amplified by FM9 (FALSE FRIEND margin expansion masking underlying cost creep).

§5 — Failure Narrative 4: Input Cost and Supply Chain Inflation Outpacing Pricing Power

Narrative: Addtech distributes and sometimes lightly manufactures technology components where input cost is partly a function of global commodity and electronics supply chains. If freight, component, or energy costs re-inflate (a plausible scenario given 2025–26 geopolitical disruption to shipping lanes and semiconductor supply), Addtech's ability to pass costs through depends on the pricing power of each individual operating company. The transmission mechanism: gross margins compress at the unit level; given the multi-tier portfolio, aggregate gross margin declines 50–100bps before management detects systemic pattern; EBITA follows with a 1-quarter lag.

Early warning signals:
- Gross margin per segment declining in a quarter where revenue is still growing
- Management commentary on "pricing environment" shifting from "strong" to "selective" or "under pressure"
- Baltic Dry Index or container freight rate index spiking >30% over 3-month period
- Component lead-time extension signals from major suppliers in Addtech's end-markets
- Peer-company gross margin disclosures showing sector-wide compression

Plausibility verbal verdict: Medium plausibility. Addtech navigated the 2021–22 inflationary period with margin intact, suggesting decent pricing power at the unit level. However, the 2021–22 episode was also characterised by unusually strong demand, which naturally supports pricing — a stagflationary environment (cost inflation + demand softness) would be materially harder.

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: global supply chains can re-inflate regardless of Addtech's actions — ✓
- Motive: none required (external structural risk) — N/A
- Opportunity: distributed model makes early detection slower than centralised procurement — ✓
- Verdict: Two of three — watch-only unless external inflation signal confirmed

Disconfirming evidence: If Addtech's annual report and CMD disclosures confirm long-term supply agreements with cost pass-through clauses for >60% of revenue, this hypothesis is weakened.

Monitoring checklist:
- Gross margin per segment quarterly
- Management commentary on cost pass-through
- Freight/component cost indices
- Supplier lead-time data from end-market trackers
- Peer gross margin disclosures (Lagercrantz, Indutrade)

Interaction with other failure modes: Moderate correlation with FM3 (operating leverage failure); low-to-null interaction with FM7 (competitive disruption) and FM10 (M&A integration failure).

The departure of 2+ of these individuals — to private equity, a competitor, or retirement — would not immediately appear in any financial metric but would degrade acquisition quality and organic performance over 18–36 months. The transmission mechanism: degraded acquisition quality → higher proportion of underperforming acquired units → EBITA misses at the divisional level → group miss.

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:

In the electrification/power niche specifically, large OEM players (ABB, Schneider, Siemens) have been systematically expanding their direct-sales and digital-platform channels, compressing the role of specialist distributors and intermediaries. If Addtech's niche businesses face direct-channel encroachment by original equipment manufacturers, the premium gross margins that justify the acquisition multiple erode over 3–5 years. The transmission mechanism: gross margin pressure → EBITA margin compression → acquisition multiple justification declines → valuation de-rates on both EPS and multiple.

Early warning signals:
- OEM direct-sales announcements in Addtech's core verticals (press releases, investor days)
- Digital procurement platform launches by key OEM suppliers that bypass Addtech's value-add
- Gross margin per unit declining without volume explanation
- Addtech's management commentary at CMD acknowledging increased competition in specific niches
- Competitor (Lagercrantz, Indutrade) disclosing similar margin pressure in overlapping segments

Plausibility verbal verdict: Medium plausibility. The OEM direct-channel threat has been present for 10+ years in industrial distribution and Addtech has historically out-positioned it through deep technical service capability. However, the pace of digital procurement platform adoption in 2024–26 is materially higher than prior cycles.

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:

- Motive: OEM margin improvement from disintermediating distributors is well-documented — ✓
- Opportunity: Addtech's technical-service moat is harder to replicate for standardised/commodity products than for highly engineered niches — partial ✓
- Verdict: All three legs, but opportunity is partial — medium priority

Disconfirming evidence: If Addtech's CMD presentations show consistent gross margin stability or improvement in the most at-risk segments (standardised electronics distribution), and if technical service attach-rate per sale is rising, this hypothesis is weakened.

Monitoring checklist:

§9 — Failure Narrative 8 (FALSE FRIEND): Earnings Beat from Unsustainable Acquisition Timing

Narrative (FALSE FRIEND): Addtech reports strong EPS growth and EBITA margin expansion. However, 60–70% of the apparent EPS growth is attributable to acquisitions made in the prior 18 months that are in their "honeymoon period" — costs have been stripped, culture has been aligned, and revenue is being recognised on the acquired book. This produces earnings beats that confirm the thesis while the underlying organic franchise is deteriorating. The EPS path looks exactly as modelled — until the acquisition pipeline slows (balance sheet constraint, valuation discipline, or market competition for targets), at which point the masking effect ends and the true organic growth rate is revealed. The transmission mechanism: acquisition contribution recedes → organic deceleration unmasks → consensus scrambles to revise organic growth assumptions → 15–25% de-rating without any single large negative event.

Early warning signals:
- Acquisition pace (SEK deployed) slowing for two or more consecutive halves
- Multiple expansion in acquisition target pricing (EV/EBITA paid rising from 7–9x toward 10–12x)
- Management commentary shifting to "disciplined" on M&A from "active"
- Net debt / EBITDA trending toward 2.5x+ (balance sheet constraint signal)
- Organic growth ex-acquisition declining while total growth still looks healthy

Plausibility verbal verdict: High plausibility. This is the archetypical false-friend failure mode for acquisition compounders and has materialised in analogous platforms (Lifco's 2022 period; Diploma's 2019 wobble; Halma's occasional organic de-rating episodes) when acquisition pacing moderated.

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: Addtech's acquisition machine can structurally produce this masking effect — ✓
- Motive: management has strong incentive to maintain the acquisition pace narrative — ✓
- Opportunity: disclosure aggregation prevents external detection until the masking ends — ✓
- Verdict: All three legs — high-priority false-friend failure mode

Disconfirming evidence: If Addtech separately reports organic growth rates by segment with independent auditor attestation, and organic growth consistently runs at 4%+ across legacy units, the masking hypothesis is weakened. If SEK deployed per acquisition is stable and EV/EBITA paid has not inflated, pipeline quality is not degrading.

Monitoring checklist:
- Organic growth rate per segment (quarterly)
- EV/EBITA paid per disclosed acquisition
- SEK deployed per half-year period
- Net debt / EBITDA trajectory
- Number of acquisitions per year vs historical baseline

Interaction with other failure modes: This is the central false-friend scenario — it interacts with FM1 (organic deceleration is the thing being masked), FM5 (multiple compression is the eventual price mechanism), and FM6 (integration quality may be declining even as reported numbers look good).

§10 — Failure Narrative 9 (FALSE FRIEND): Margin Expansion from Capex Deferral / R&D Under-Investment

Narrative (FALSE FRIEND): Addtech's reported EBITA margins have expanded and the narrative attributes this to portfolio mix-shift toward higher-value niches. However, a portion of the margin expansion may be attributable to R&D and capex under-investment at the operating company level — acquired businesses in their first 3–5 years may defer product development investment and infrastructure upgrades, boosting near-term EBITA at the cost of medium-term competitive position. Because Addtech's model delegates operational decisions to business unit managers who are evaluated on EBITA, there is a structural incentive to defer long-cycle investments that won't payoff within a manager's evaluation horizon. The transmission mechanism: capex/R&D deferral → short-term EBITA margin expansion → medium-term product obsolescence or customer defection → EBITA miss 3–5 years post-acquisition.

Early warning signals:
- Capex as a percentage of revenue declining below sector norms for niche industrial businesses (typically 2–4%)
- R&D expense (where capitalised) declining as % of revenue at the segment level
- Management commentary at CMD shifting from "investing in the portfolio" to "optimising the portfolio"
- Customer satisfaction or NPS data (where available for key Addtech units)
- Product introduction frequency declining in key end-market verticals

Plausibility verbal verdict: Medium plausibility. The capex-deferral false-friend is structurally present in any decentralised EBITA-evaluated acquisition company. Addtech's track record suggests disciplined capital allocation, but the incentive structure creates a persistent latent risk, particularly during high-acquisition-pace periods when management bandwidth is stretched.

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: business unit managers control capex/R&D decisions — ✓
- Motive: EBITA-linked evaluation creates deferral incentive — ✓
- Opportunity: Addtech's group-level disclosure does not surface unit-level capex/R&D trends — ✓
- Verdict: All three legs — full failure mode

Disconfirming evidence: If Addtech's annual report and CMD presentations show stable capex/revenue ratios across the portfolio at 2–4% and management explicitly ties capex investment to strategic KPIs at the business-unit level, the deferral hypothesis is weakened.

Monitoring checklist:
- Group capex as % of revenue (annual)
- R&D expense capitalisation policy and trend
- CMD language on "investment vs optimisation"
- Post-acquisition EBITA trajectory at year 3–5 (where derivable)
- Patent filing or new product launch frequency in core niches

Interaction with other failure modes: Correlated with FM7 (competitive disruption becomes more likely if R&D investment is deferred); amplifies FM2 (customer concentration risk rises if product competitiveness declines); sequential trigger for FM1 (organic deceleration follows product obsolescence).

§11 — Failure Narrative 10: M&A Integration Failure / Goodwill Write-Down Cascade

Narrative: Addtech has accumulated a portfolio of 130+ operating companies over multiple decades, with goodwill on the balance sheet representing a significant portion of net assets. If macroeconomic deterioration (recession, industrial demand collapse) or a strategic error in a large acquisition causes one or more units to require impairment, the resulting goodwill write-down flows through the P&L and triggers a reassessment of the quality of the entire acquisition portfolio. The transmission mechanism: a single large impairment signals potential systemic overvaluation of goodwill across the portfolio → audit-driven review of other units → additional write-downs → EPS impact AND re-rating on P/BV and P/E multiples simultaneously.

Early warning signals:
- First disclosed goodwill impairment at any significant operating company (>SEK 100m)
- Management disclosing that a specific acquisition is "being reviewed" or "restructuring"
- Auditor qualification or emphasis-of-matter paragraph related to goodwill assumptions
- Industrial PMI in Addtech's primary end-markets falling below 47 for three consecutive months (recession signal)
- Peer companies (Lifco, Indutrade) disclosing impairments in overlapping sectors

Plausibility verbal verdict: Low plausibility in the near-term, rising to medium plausibility in a prolonged industrial recession scenario. Addtech's track record of zero significant goodwill impairments over 20+ years is strong disconfirming evidence, but tail risk is non-zero and the portfolio has grown materially in scale.

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: goodwill write-downs are accounting responses to real deterioration — ✓
- Motive: none (management disincentivised to write down) — inverse motive ✗
- Opportunity: large goodwill balance on balance sheet — ✓
- Verdict: Two of three — watch-only, but severity if triggered is high

Disconfirming evidence: Addtech's 20+ year impairment-free track record is itself strong disconfirming evidence for this scenario. If the portfolio demonstrates consistent ROCE above WACC across acquired units in annual reporting, the goodwill-cascade scenario is weakened.

Monitoring checklist:
- Goodwill impairment disclosures (annual report)
- ROCE by segment vs WACC estimate
- Industrial PMI in Sweden/DACH (primary Addtech markets)
- Auditor notes on goodwill assumptions
- Peer impairment disclosures

Interaction with other failure modes: Triggered by severe version of FM1, FM3, or FM7; amplified by FM5 (valuation de-rating); relatively independent of FM6 (management departure) in the near-term.

4. PART B — META-ANALYSIS

Net signal: Failure modes cluster into two correlated families with organic deceleration as the shared upstream driver; false-friend density is elevated, making this case more dangerous than direct-bearish signals alone would suggest.

Scenario cluster — Family 1 (Organic/Growth): FM1 (organic deceleration), FM3 (operating leverage failure), FM5 (multiple compression), FM8 (FALSE FRIEND — acquisition masking), FM11 (balance sheet constraint) form a tightly correlated cluster. All five scenarios share the trigger of organic growth falling below the market's priced expectation. If FM1 is live, the conditional probability of FM3, FM5, and FM8 all materialising simultaneously rises from independent to 60–70% correlated.

Scenario cluster — Family 2 (Portfolio/Quality): FM2 (customer concentration), FM6 (key integrator departure), FM7 (competitive disruption), FM9 (FALSE FRIEND — capex deferral), FM10 (goodwill write-down) form a looser quality-deterioration cluster. These scenarios unfold over longer time horizons (3–5 years) and are less correlated with each other, but each degrades the fundamental quality of the portfolio quietly before headline numbers reflect it.

Joint probability assessment: If each of the 11 scenarios is independently rated at 10–25% probability over an 18-month horizon, the joint probability of zero failures is low (roughly 10–20%). The probability of 1+ scenario materialising in some form is the modal outcome. However, most single-scenario outcomes produce only partial EPS impact — the severity threshold for thesis-breaking failure (>20% EPS miss) requires either FM1+FM5 together or a Family 2 scenario reaching critical mass.

Compound monitorables — appearing across ≥3 scenarios:
- Organic growth rate per division: FM1, FM3, FM5, FM8, FM11 — the highest-frequency compound monitorable
- Acquisition pace (SEK deployed, deal count): FM8, FM11, FM6 (indirectly), FM9
- Gross margin per segment: FM2, FM4, FM7, FM9
- Management language shift at CMD/earnings calls: FM1, FM3, FM6, FM8, FM9

⚡ False-friend density is unusually high for this case: four of eleven scenarios (FM8, FM9, and partial FM1 + FM2) are false-friend in character — positive signals (earnings beats, margin expansion, stable reported numbers) actively mislead during the deterioration window. This is notably higher than typical direct-bearish industrials pre-mortem profiles, where 1–2 false-friend scenarios are standard. High false-friend density means the case is most dangerous precisely when it appears to be confirming.

Leading indicators meta — most-monitored vs least-monitored:
- Most-monitored: total revenue growth (high visibility, quarterly), EBITA margin (quarterly), net debt/EBITDA (semi-annual)
- Least-monitored: organic growth by division (disclosed infrequently, not audited separately), capex/R&D intensity at business-unit level (annual, aggregated), senior management roster continuity (no formal disclosure mechanism)

False-friend warning class: The Family 1 cluster operating through FM8 (acquisition masking) represents a Class 1 false-friend risk — it produces confirming signals at the precisely the moment of maximum risk, and the unmasking event is typically non-linear (a slow build followed by a sharp single-quarter reveal).
