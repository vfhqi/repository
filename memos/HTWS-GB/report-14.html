Net leverage reached 4.0x at end-2024 and 3.4x at end-2025, within management's own medium-term 2.5x-3.5x target range.   • KD4 (MNO Capex) headwinds have partially resolved. Airtel Africa/Vodacom infrastructure sharing agreement in Tanzania and DRC (August 2025) signals network densification intent.   • KD5 (Tanzania Regulatory Risk) remains a monitoring item — no enacted legislation. TCRA's focus appears to be quality-of-service enforcement against MNOs rather than rate compression for towercos. Regulatory environment was actually supportive in 2024 (removal of excise levies on mobile money). Tanzania's ~2.46x tenancy ratio and grid connectivity rollout make it Helios's most productive market.
  • The setup has evolved from Leverage Turnaround (distressed/conditional) to Leverage Turnaround (executing/re-rating). The question for DD is no longer "will KD3 gate the whole thesis?" but "at what EBITDA/FCF multiple does the market appropriately re-rate a de-risked African towerco?"
  • Residual risks are FX and country concentration, not leverage per se. At 3.4x net leverage with $66m FCF and a refinancing that has extended maturity to 4-year average at 6.7% cost, the balance sheet is no longer fragile. The primary risks now are currency depreciation in DRC/Tanzania compressing USD EBITDA, and DRC/Tanzania market concentration.
  • Watson's overall conviction upgrades from ORANGE to YELLOW on the back of FY2024 and FY2025 execution. The case has delivered materially against every key driver. DD priorities shift from risk validation to valuation and re-rating mechanics.

Executive Summary

At the time of the APM's Pass 1 KD analysis, the prevailing framing was: KD3 (Leverage) is the gating condition, KD1 (Tenancy) is the upside engine, and the overall setup requires a conditional Leverage Turnaround thesis to work before KD1 and KD2 can generate the required returns. The primary fear was that Helios would repeat its 2022-23 experience — where management guided deleveraging but net debt rose, triggering a share price collapse from ~180p to ~70p.

The research conducted for this report materially updates that framing. Two full-year results cycles (FY2024 and FY2025) have since been reported, and they show consistent, on-track execution across all five key drivers. Net leverage has declined from a peak of approximately 5.1x (2022) to 4.4x (2023), 4.0x (2024), and 3.4x (2025) — a 1.7x reduction over three years. The tenancy ratio, which the APM pegged at ~2.0x with a 2.3x target, has already reached 2.17x at end-2025, ahead of management's own 2.2x target for end-2026. FCF, which was deep negative as recently as 2023, turned positive in 2024 ($19m) and accelerated sharply in 2025 ($66m reported FCF, $208m recurring FCF). A March 2026 bond refinancing has reduced cost of debt by 0.4% to 6.7% and extended average maturity to four years.

This does not mean the case has been "solved" — significant risks remain. FX mismatch between USD/EUR debt and local currency revenues (Tanzanian shilling, Congolese franc) persists structurally. While 64% of revenues and 71% of EBITDA are in hard currencies, the remaining 29-36% in local currencies creates amplification risk in depreciation scenarios. Tanzania and DRC represent a disproportionate share of the portfolio, meaning idiosyncratic regulatory or political risk in these markets retains outsized impact potential. The new IMPACT 2030 strategy is ambitious (9%+ EBITDA CAGR, 2.5x+ tenancy ratio, $400m+ shareholder returns) and requires continued execution.

The fulcrum driver classification requires updating. KD3 remains conceptually the primary structural driver, but the thesis has now moved past the point where KD3 is a binary gating condition. The gating risk has largely resolved; the remaining question is the pace and sustainability of re-rating. KD1 (Tenancy) has become the primary forward-looking upside driver, with 2.5x as the next waypoint. KD2 (Solar) is real but smaller in EBITDA contribution than originally framed. KD4 (MNO Capex) has been less of a headwind than feared. KD5 (Tanzania regulatory) has not materialised.

At ESA stage, the appropriate verdict is: the case has materially de-risked from the APM Pass 1 framing. The setup confirmation is a Leverage Turnaround moving into re-rating phase. DD priorities are now valuation (what multiple should a de-risked African towerco trade at?), FCF modelling (stress-test the IMPACT 2030 targets), and country concentration risk analysis. Overall Watson conviction upgrades to YELLOW from the APM's ORANGE, with the key residual risk being FX and whether the market will close the valuation gap given persistent SSA discount.

KD1: Tenancy Ratio Expansion — Full Assessment

1. Define the Driver Precisely

The thesis required the portfolio average tenancy ratio to expand from approximately 2.0x (APM Pass 1 framing) to 2.3x minimum, with each incremental tenancy adding approximately 75-80% incremental EBITDA margin given the fixed-cost tower infrastructure. The APM framed this as requiring +0.1x per 12-15 months and identified it as the primary organic growth driver. Evidence gaps included Tanzania-specific tenancy data and the pace of MNO co-location demand.

2. Bull Case Evidence

The bull case has materially outperformed expectations.

Tenancy ratio trajectory (confirmed):
- FY2023: 1.91x
- FY2024: 2.05x (increase of +0.14x year-on-year; 2,481 new tenancies added, a 9% increase)
- FY2025: 2.17x (increase of +0.12x year-on-year; 2,538 new tenancies added, record for the company)
- New IMPACT 2030 target: 2.5x+ by 2030, implying continued +0.06-0.08x per year through the period.

The APM's original assumption of ~2.0x was conservative relative to the realised trajectory. The 2.3x minimum target is now firmly within a 2-3 year delivery window at the current pace.

Tanzania (the critical market — APM evidence gap filled):
Tanzania's tenancy ratio at Q3 2024 was 2.46x — the highest in the portfolio, not the laggard the APM framing implied. This is a substantial finding. Tanzania is Helios's largest market by sites (~4,000+), and its above-average tenancy ratio means it is the portfolio's strongest contributor per tower. Revenue growth from Tanzania in 2024 was cited as the primary organic driver of group results.

Structural demand support:
GSMA data confirms mobile data traffic in SSA will quadruple over the next five years from the 2025 base. Mobile internet penetration is 27% (2023) rising to a forecast 37% by 2030. Subscriber growth is running at ~5% per annum. These are powerful structural underpins for co-location demand: MNOs adding capacity must either build new towers (expensive) or co-locate on existing Helios towers (the rational choice given take-or-pay master lease agreements already in place).

Competitive context:
Helios's 2.17x tenancy ratio compares favourably with IHS Towers' 1.49-1.51x, illustrating the structural advantage of Helios's more concentrated, operationally mature portfolio in its core markets.

3. Bear Case Evidence

Airtel Africa capex reduction:
Airtel Africa's announced ~30% group capex reduction was primarily Nigeria-centric. The actual impact on Helios's core markets of Tanzania and DRC has been limited, as demonstrated by the 2,481 and 2,538 tenancy additions in 2024 and 2025 respectively. However, the August 2025 Airtel/Vodacom infrastructure sharing agreement in Tanzania and DRC introduces a second-order question: if the two MNOs are sharing towers and fibre rather than independently co-locating on Helios towers, does this reduce net new co-location demand?

Infrastructure sharing dynamics:
The Airtel Africa/Vodacom Tanzania and DRC infrastructure sharing agreement (August 2025) is notable. Infrastructure sharing between MNOs can reduce the total pool of new co-location demand by substituting inter-operator sharing for independent tower expansion. This is a monitoring risk, though the current trend data (2025 tenancy additions at record levels) suggests it has not yet impacted momentum.

Macro headwinds in DRC:
The DRC faces ongoing political and security instability in eastern provinces. This creates operational uncertainty, although Helios's tower infrastructure as critical national connectivity is unlikely to be a primary target for disruption.

4. Forward Trajectory Assessment

The current trend is unambiguously positive. The 2.2x target was achieved one year ahead of schedule. The IMPACT 2030 target of 2.5x+ by 2030 requires approximately +0.3x over five years — roughly +0.06x per year, below the +0.12-0.14x achieved in 2024-2025. The forward trajectory is highly plausible unless MNO capex collapses across multiple Helios markets simultaneously, or infrastructure sharing materially cannibalises co-location demand. Neither scenario has materialised to date.

Key dependencies: MNO capex stability in Tanzania, DRC, and Ghana; continued absence of infrastructure sharing cannibalisation.

Downside scenario: If tenancy additions slow to ~1,500/year, it would take until 2029+ to reach 2.3x — not catastrophic but would delay the FCF/re-rating timeline by 18-24M.

5. Evidence Gaps (Updated)

The Tanzania tenancy ratio evidence gap identified by the APM has been substantially resolved — Tanzania is at 2.46x, above portfolio average. Remaining gaps:
- DRC-specific tenancy ratio (not separately disclosed)
- Quantification of the impact of Airtel/Vodacom infrastructure sharing on forward co-location pipeline

6. Watson Rating: YELLOW-GREEN

Tenancy expansion is executing materially ahead of the APM's ESA-stage case. The driver is confirmed as real, with empirical evidence of +0.14x and +0.12x annual progression in 2024 and 2025. Tanzania outperformance removes the largest single evidence gap. Rating upgraded from APM's YELLOW to YELLOW-GREEN.

7. Fulcrum Assessment

APM said YES (primary organic growth driver). Confirmed as secondary fulcrum / upside engine. The primary fulcrum designation (KD3) now requires requalification given KD3's execution progress. KD1 is the forward value driver; KD3 is the resolved gating condition. The two should be reordered in a DD-stage analysis: KD1 first (upside sustainability), KD3 second (confirmed resolving, pace of re-rating).

KD2: Solar / Power Programme — Full Assessment

1. Define the Driver Precisely

The solar programme (Project 100) targets near-universal hybrid/solar coverage to displace diesel opex, with the APM estimating $30-50m annual opex reduction. Solar represents an entirely within-management-control EBITDA lever. The APM estimated ~40-50% solar penetration as of 2024 and flagged evidence gaps around actual delivery.

2. Bull Case Evidence

Project 100 investment:
Helios has invested $44m in grid, solar, and battery solutions since 2022 (through 2025). In 2025 alone, $11m was invested. The target is $100m total between 2022 and 2030.

Power uptime:
Power uptime reached 99.99% in 2025 despite weak grid infrastructure in several markets. This is an indirect but significant indicator that solar/hybrid investment is delivering operational improvement, reducing generator dependency.

Opex and margin impact:
Adjusted EBITDA margin expanded from 51% (FY2023) to 53% (FY2024) to 55% (FY2025), a 4 percentage point improvement over two years. Solar/power efficiency is cited as a contributing factor alongside tenancy expansion. This is consistent with genuine cost reduction.

Tanzania grid:
In Tanzania, approximately 80% of towers are on-grid with grid availability of ~20 hours/day. This dramatically reduces diesel dependency in Helios's largest market, structurally lowering power opex without requiring solar capex investment at those sites.

3. Bear Case Evidence

Power mix complexity:
H2 2025 earnings call data reveals the actual power mix: in a typical 24-hour period, approximately 17 hours comes from grid, with the remaining 7 hours split roughly equally between diesel and solar/hybrid batteries. Solar/hybrid batteries account for approximately 3.5 hours or ~14-15% of total power supply — lower than "80% site coverage with hybrid/solar" might imply.

Customer pass-through:
H2 2025 earnings commentary noted a "small decrease in power-related revenues due to decreasing fuel prices in DRC and Tanzania, passed on to customers." Helios's take-or-pay contracts include power cost pass-through provisions. Diesel savings are partially transmitted back to MNOs through reduced power charges. The direct EBITDA uplift from solar is therefore smaller than the gross diesel saving — the mechanism is margin protection and gradual improvement rather than transformative step-change.

Capex drag on a leveraged balance sheet:
$100m capex over 2022-2030 for Project 100, while modest versus the ~$1.7bn net debt base, still represents ongoing cash commitment. The solar programme is self-funding at the site level (payback measured in months given diesel costs) but aggregate timeline means steady-state investment continues.

4. Forward Trajectory Assessment

Solar/power is the best-evidenced driver and entirely within management control. The direct EBITDA contribution is real but somewhat more modest than the APM's $30-50m annual framing would suggest, given the customer pass-through structure and the dominance of grid power in Tanzania. The more precise framing is: solar/hybrid investment protects EBITDA from diesel price spikes, provides margin floor insurance, and gradually reduces power-related opex intensity as grid coverage improves — but it is not a step-change EBITDA accelerator. The margin expansion trajectory (51%→53%→55% over 2023-2025) is consistent with this interpretation: steady-state margin improvement driven by a combination of tenancy leverage and power efficiency.

5. Evidence Gaps (Updated)

  • Exact diesel cost per tower pre- and post-Project 100 (not publicly disclosed with sufficient granularity)
  • DRC-specific solar/grid breakdown
  • Proportion of power revenue reduction attributable to pass-through vs genuine EBITDA improvement

6. Watson Rating: YELLOW-GREEN

Solar is executing and contributing to margin expansion. The programme is credible, within management control, and consistent with the disclosed results. Rating maintained at YELLOW-GREEN with a caveat that the direct P&L contribution is more modest than the APM's framing suggested — the $30-50m annual opex reduction figure should be treated with caution given the customer pass-through mechanism. The driver is real but its contribution is best characterised as "steady margin protection and gradual improvement."

7. Fulcrum Assessment

APM said YES (only driver fully within management control). Confirmed as a supporting driver, but the fulcrum designation at ESA stage was somewhat overstated. Better classified as a margin-defence and quality-of-earnings driver than an independent fulcrum. Its contribution matters most in scenarios where tenancy expansion disappoints — it provides a floor rather than an independent upside engine. Rating: supporting driver, not co-fulcrum.

KD3: FX / Leverage Resolution — Full Assessment

1. Define the Driver Precisely

The APM identified this as the primary fulcrum — the gating condition for the thesis. Net debt was framed at ~$1.7-1.9bn, approximately 5-6x ND/EBITDA, with the 2024 refinancing at ~9-10% weighted average interest rate creating elevated annual interest costs. The thesis required ND/EBITDA below 4x by 2026-27 and FCF to turn positive as the precondition for investment.

2. Bull Case Evidence

Deleveraging trajectory (substantially delivered):
- FY2022: net leverage ~5.1x (peak, post-Oman acquisition)
- FY2023: 4.4x (improvement of 0.7x)
- FY2024: 4.0x (improvement of 0.4x; net debt $1,735.5m vs $1,783.1m in 2023)
- FY2025: 3.4x (improvement of 0.6x; within management's own medium-term 2.5x-3.5x target range)
- Total leverage reduction of 1.7x since FY2022 peak, in three years.

The APM's bear concern (2022-23 precedent of failed deleveraging) has been fully superseded by three consecutive years of leverage reduction.

FCF inflection (confirmed and accelerating):
- FY2023: FCF deeply negative (approximately -$81m based on $100m improvement to get to $19m)
- FY2024: FCF +$19m (first positive year; $100m improvement year-on-year)
- FY2025: FCF +$66m (more than tripled); Recurring FCF $208m (up 40%)
- FY2026 guidance: Recurring FCF $210-225m

The FCF inflection that the entire thesis depended upon has occurred and is accelerating.

March 2026 refinancing:
HTA Group (Helios subsidiary) priced a $500m note issue at 6.8% interest, using proceeds to prepay $445m of term facilities. This: (a) reduces cost of debt from ~7.1% to 6.7% (0.4% reduction), (b) extends average debt maturity by one year to four years, and (c) locks in fixed-rate financing for the majority of the debt stack. The risk of a distressed refinancing has been removed.

Credit rating trajectory:
S&P upgraded Helios twice within a year, reaching BB- (Stable). This reflects external validation of the deleveraging trajectory and reduces future refinancing risk.

Hard currency revenue:
64% of revenues and 71% of EBITDA are in hard currencies (USD/EUR). This materially mitigates the FX mismatch risk — the local currency exposure on the EBITDA side is approximately 29%, not 100%.

3. Bear Case Evidence

Net debt remains large in absolute terms:
At end-2025, net debt was approximately $1,600-1,700m (implied by 3.4x leverage against $471m EBITDA). This is a meaningful absolute debt burden; any significant EBITDA miss (FX depreciation, MNO churn, regulatory action in Tanzania) could reverse leverage progress.

Interest cost elevated versus prior cycle:
At 6.7-6.8% cost of debt, the annual interest bill on approximately $1.65bn of debt is approximately $110m. This compares to EBITDA of $471m (FY2025), implying gross interest coverage of approximately 4.3x — adequate but not comfortable, particularly given the African operating environment.

FX mismatch residual:
The 29% of EBITDA in local currencies (TZS, CDF, etc.) remains exposed. The Tanzanian shilling and Congolese franc have historically depreciated against USD. If 2026-2030 sees sustained local currency depreciation, the USD-equivalent EBITDA contribution from local-currency markets will compress, slowing deleveraging progress.

DRC concentration:
DRC is a growing share of Helios's portfolio (new sites primarily in DRC and Tanzania in 2024). DRC is a higher-risk environment (political instability, eastern conflict zones, weaker grid). Concentration growth in DRC raises country risk even as it supports tenancy ratio growth.

2022 precedent is historical, not theoretical:
The 2022-23 period demonstrated that leverage can rise sharply in an environment of acquisition activity combined with FX headwinds. The 2022 Oman acquisition for ~$500m added substantial debt during a period of rising interest rates. The IMPACT 2030 strategy includes over $500m in discretionary capex 2026-2030 — if any of this is debt-funded, leverage dynamics could reverse.

4. Forward Trajectory Assessment

The deleveraging trajectory is clearly established and the FCF inflection has occurred. The primary risk scenario is no longer "will KD3 gate the thesis?" but "will FX depreciation and continued capex requirements prevent leverage from reaching management's 2.5x target?" The IMPACT 2030 framework implies leverage continues to fall toward the lower end of the 2.5-3.5x range over 2026-2030. FCF guidance of $210-225m for 2026 provides strong internal funding capacity.

The forward trajectory is YELLOW (plausible, executing, with residual FX risk) rather than ORANGE as the APM framed it.

5. Evidence Gaps (Updated)

  • Covenant headroom on current bonds (not publicly disclosed in detail)
  • FCF sensitivity analysis to a 10-15% TZS and CDF depreciation scenario
  • DRC-specific EBITDA contribution and margin profile
  • Exact net debt figure at end-2025 (extrapolated from leverage ratio and EBITDA rather than directly confirmed)

6. Watson Rating: YELLOW (upgraded from APM's ORANGE)

The gating condition has substantially resolved. Leverage at 3.4x is within management's own target range, FCF is positive and accelerating, and a March 2026 refinancing has locked in fixed-rate financing at 6.7%. The primary residual risk is FX mismatch on the ~29% of EBITDA in local currencies, and the absolute debt burden remains large in a scenario of EBITDA stagnation. Rating upgraded from ORANGE to YELLOW.

7. Fulcrum Assessment

APM said PRIMARY FULCRUM (gating condition). Confirmed as primary structural driver — but the thesis has now moved past the point where this is a binary gate. The relevant question for DD is no longer "does KD3 allow the thesis to proceed?" but "what is the pace of further deleveraging and re-rating?" The balance sheet is no longer fragile; it is in a managed deleveraging-to-returns allocation phase. The fulcrum has been crossed.

KD4: MNO Capex Cycle — Full Assessment

1. Define the Driver Precisely

MNO network investment is the indirect driver of co-location demand: MNOs adding capacity must pay for new tenancies on Helios towers. The APM identified Airtel Africa's ~30% group capex reduction as a negative signal, primarily Nigeria-focused but representing macro MNO caution. The target is MNO capex stabilisation enabling KD1 expansion.

2. Bull Case Evidence

Airtel Africa Nigeria-specificity confirmed:
The Airtel Africa group capex reduction was heavily weighted toward Nigeria, where Airtel faces specific balance sheet stress and a challenging macroeconomic environment (naira devaluation). Helios does not operate in Nigeria. The impact on Helios's core markets of Tanzania, DRC, Ghana, and Senegal has been demonstrably limited — 2,481 tenancy additions in FY2024 and a record 2,538 in FY2025 confirm this.

Airtel Africa capex recovery:
Airtel Africa has subsequently guided for capex of $725-750m (excluding licence renewals and spectrum), representing a recovery toward more normalised levels. In DRC specifically, Airtel added 251 new sites in the year, indicating continued network investment.

Vodacom/Orange DRC solar base station deployment:
January 2025: Vodacom and Orange partnered in DRC to deploy up to 2,000 solar-powered base stations over six years. This is a direct co-location demand signal for Helios's DRC portfolio.

Structural data demand:
GSMA's forecast of 4x data traffic growth in SSA over five years is the structural underpinning. Even in capex-constrained environments, MNOs must eventually densify to manage network congestion.

5G and AI catalyst:
Helios management commentary from early 2025 highlighted 5G and AI as emerging demand drivers. Africa's 5G rollout, while nascent, creates a new layer of densification requirement. This is a medium-to-long term driver (2-5 year horizon) rather than near-term.

3. Bear Case Evidence

African currency depreciation:
USD-denominated capex becomes more expensive for MNOs paying in local currencies. Multiple SSA currencies have depreciated materially against USD since 2022. This directly reduces the effective capex budget for MNOs investing in networks, which can compress tower co-location demand.

Infrastructure sharing substitution risk:
The Airtel/Vodacom sharing agreement in Tanzania and DRC (August 2025) could displace independent co-location demand. This is the most concrete near-term monitoring risk for KD4.

Multiple SSA economies under macro pressure:
Ghana completed an IMF-supported debt restructuring programme. DRC faces fiscal constraints. These pressures can delay MNO network investment decisions, even where structural data demand exists.

4. Forward Trajectory Assessment

KD4 has been less of a headwind than the APM framing implied. Tenancy additions have been at or above the trajectory required for the thesis in both 2024 and 2025. The structural data demand underpinning is intact. The monitoring risk is the Airtel/Vodacom infrastructure sharing dynamic. The overall assessment is YELLOW — supportive rather than risk, with infrastructure sharing as the key forward-looking monitoring item.

5. Evidence Gaps (Updated)

  • Clarification on whether the Airtel/Vodacom infrastructure sharing agreement in Tanzania and DRC affects Helios-owned towers directly
  • Tanzania and DRC-specific MNO capex guidance beyond the group-level figures
  • Pace of Vodacom/Orange DRC solar base station deployment and impact on Helios co-location pipeline

6. Watson Rating: YELLOW (in line with APM assessment)

7. Fulcrum Assessment

APM said MEDIUM (supports KD1 but not itself fulcrum). Confirmed. KD4 is a KD1 gate but not an independent value driver. The evidence base has improved since Pass 1: actual tenancy additions have exceeded cautious expectations despite the Airtel capex cut headlines.

KD5: Tanzania Regulatory Risk — Full Assessment

1. Define the Driver Precisely

Tanzania represents approximately 35-40% of Helios's revenue (the largest single market by sites at ~4,000+ of 14,325 total). The TCRA regulatory risk was framed as a potential force to reduce tower rental rates for small/local MNOs. No enacted legislation existed as of the APM Pass 1 analysis.

2. Bull Case Evidence

No enacted legislation:
As of April 2026, there is no enacted legislation mandating tower rate reductions for small/local MNOs.

TCRA focus is MNO quality, not tower rates:
TCRA's regulatory focus in 2024-2025 has been on quality-of-service enforcement against MNOs (demanding Vodacom Tanzania improve network quality under threat of penalties), not on tower rate compression. TCRA also directed that penalties shift toward mandatory network investment orders, which is actually positive for tower demand.

Supportive regulatory environment:
Tanzania removed excise levies on airtime and mobile money transfers in 2024 — a pro-investment regulatory action that improves MNO unit economics and indirectly supports network investment willingness.

Government alignment:
Tanzania's government has a stated interest in expanding connectivity. Tower company investment creates employment and accelerates digital inclusion targets. Adversarial regulation toward foreign tower operators would contradict this stated objective.

Tanzania tenancy ratio at 2.46x:
If Tanzania were experiencing regulatory headwinds, tenancy ratios would be falling. The fact that Tanzania has the highest tenancy ratio in the portfolio and was cited as the primary organic growth driver in FY2024 is strong evidence against regulatory impairment.

3. Bear Case Evidence

TCRA has a history of assertive regulation:
The TCRA has historically been willing to intervene in the telecommunications sector. The original concern about TCRA directing reduced rates for small MNOs is not without precedent in the broader SSA regulatory landscape.

Revenue concentration risk:
If Tanzania represents ~35-40% of Helios revenue, any material regulatory action — even one limited to small/local MNOs initially — creates risk to a disproportionate share of the group revenue base.

African political risk premium:
Tanzania, while more stable than DRC, has a history of regulatory interventions against foreign corporations. The Magufuli era (ended 2021) saw multiple confrontational interventions with foreign businesses. The Samia administration has been more business-friendly, but the institutional memory of assertive regulation remains.

Medium-term escalation risk:
Even if near-term risk is low, TCRA's regulatory direction (if it continues to push toward reduced tower access costs for smaller operators) could escalate over the 2026-2030 horizon.

4. Forward Trajectory Assessment

Near-term: Low probability of material impact. No enacted legislation, supportive regulatory environment in 2024, Tanzania is the portfolio's top-performing market. Medium-term (2027-2030): Monitoring required. The TCRA regulatory direction is not fully benign, and Tanzania's revenue concentration means any deterioration would be asymmetric.

5. Evidence Gaps (Updated)

  • TCRA directive exact text and current status (requires accessing TCRA regulatory publications directly)
  • Tanzania revenue as a percentage of Helios group revenue (management does not separately disclose; estimate required)
  • Helios contract terms regarding regulatory force majeure and rate adjustment provisions

6. Watson Rating: YELLOW (upgraded from APM's YELLOW-ORANGE)

The weight of evidence from the research period is positive: no enacted legislation, TCRA focused on MNO quality enforcement (positive for tower demand), Tanzania excise levy removal (positive), and Tanzania's record-high tenancy ratio. The monitoring requirement remains but the immediate risk is lower than the APM framing suggested.

7. Fulcrum Assessment

APM said NO (conditional). Confirmed as monitoring item. Not current fulcrum. The condition for upgrade (TCRA enacting binding rate legislation) has not been met and the direction of travel in 2024-2025 has been away from this risk.

Cross-Driver Analysis

Driver Interactions

KD1 × KD4 (Tenancy × MNO Capex): The relationship is positive and confirmed. Record tenancy additions in 2024-2025 were delivered despite the Airtel Africa group capex reduction headlines, demonstrating that Helios's markets have been insulated from the Nigeria-specific capex compression. The Airtel/Vodacom infrastructure sharing agreement introduces a new interaction to monitor — sharing between MNOs could either increase Helios's tenant count (both operators on one Helios tower) or reduce incremental co-location demand (MNOs sharing non-Helios assets).

KD2 × KD3 (Solar × Leverage): The solar programme requires ongoing capex ($11m in 2025, $100m target through 2030) but this is modest relative to FCF generation ($66m in 2025, $208m recurring FCF).
