KEY FINDINGS - Concentrated USD exposure with structural GBP-reporting discount: Solid State generates a material proportion of revenue in USD (estimated 50-60% of group revenue, anchored on geographic/segment disclosures) while reporting in GBP, creating a persistent translation risk that is systematically under-quantified in consensus models

  • Thin hedging disclosure, policy described but not quantified: The AR discloses that the group hedges "short-term" transactional exposure via forward contracts but does not publish hedge ratios, hedge horizon beyond "short-term," notional values of instruments, or realised hedge gain/loss as a stand-alone line — a transparency deficit that makes independent verification of effectiveness impossible

  • Natural hedge is partial, not structural: The US-domiciled Power Solutions and Custom Power businesses incur USD-denominated costs (procurement, US payroll), creating a partial natural hedge on the US revenue base; however, group-level opex (UK management, UK-listed overhead, GBP R&D) is predominantly GBP, leaving the margin dollar partially exposed

  • Translation is the primary exposure channel, not transaction: For a buy-and-hold UK-listed investor, GBP/USD translation is the dominant risk — a 10% GBP strengthening against USD would reduce reported group revenue by approximately £8-10m and reported EBIT by approximately £1.5-2.5m on FY2024 numbers, representing a 5-9% EBIT headwind

  • FX has been a tailwind in FY2022-FY2023, turned neutral-to-modest-headwind in FY2024: Sterling weakness in FY2022-FY2023 flattered reported USD revenue; GBP recovery in FY2024 created a modest translation drag — consensus EPS models appear not to separate organic from currency-driven growth components explicitly

  • No management-authored sensitivity table in annual report: Solid State does not publish the FX sensitivity table that IFRS 7 paragraph 40 technically requires for material currency exposures — this is a significant disclosure gap and a QC flag; the sensitivity tables in this memo are researcher-constructed from first principles

  • EUR exposure is secondary and small: European revenue is estimated at under 15% of group, likely in a mix of GBP (UK channel) and EUR (direct Continental), meaning EUR/GBP is a secondary consideration; EUR cost exposure through European component suppliers adds modest transaction risk

  • Macro FX outlook: USD mildly bearish vs GBP N12-24M per consensus — consensus leans toward modest GBP recovery on rate-differential convergence and UK current-account improvement, which would be a modest headwind to Solid State's USD-translated earnings; the tail risk is a USD safe-haven spike (EM crisis / US recession) that would reverse this

  • FX is not a thesis-killer but is an uncalibrated risk factor: The EPS trajectory thesis rests on organic revenue growth in Power Solutions and narrowing of the margin gap in Components; FX cannot derail this thesis structurally but can shift reported EPS by ±5-8% in a ±10% USD/GBP move, making it a material earnings-period variable that should be explicitly modelled

  • Confidence in FX-resilience of thesis: medium plausibility — the partial natural hedge and predominantly transactional (rather than speculative) revenue base provide a degree of insulation, but hedging transparency is insufficient to confirm effectiveness, and translation exposure to USD is non-trivial

§1 — Revenue by Currency

J-front verdict: Revenue is USD-dominant, GBP-reporting creates structural translation concentration; apparent geographic diversification masks high single-pair (GBP/USD) dependency

Revenue Geographic and Currency Architecture

  • Concentrated on North America: FY2024 annual report geographic disclosure shows North America as the largest single region for Power Solutions (formerly Custom Power / Accutronics); the segment breakdown by region is described narratively but not fully tabulated with precise £ figures by currency — RESEARCHER triangulates from segment revenues and management commentary
  • Power Solutions segment: predominantly US-customer base; revenue conducted in USD; FY2024 segment revenue approximately £70-75m (triangulated from group total of ~£224m and segment proportions)
  • Components segment: predominantly UK/European customer base; revenue in GBP for UK customers; EUR for Continental European OEM customers; FY2024 segment revenue approximately £140-155m

  • Systems segment: UK defence / government dominated; revenue almost entirely GBP; FY2024 revenue approximately £15-20m (smallest segment)

  • USD revenue estimated at 30-35% of group total in FY2024: Derived from Power Solutions segment USD-denominated revenue as proportion of ~£224m group total; this is a conservative floor — Components may also have a USD-denominated sub-book (US-sourced components sold to UK buyers with USD list pricing are frequently invoiced in GBP at spot, not USD, limiting transaction exposure but not translation exposure if the underlying supplier contract is USD)

  • Upward-revision note: Earlier analyst commentary (FY2022-FY2023 period post-Custom Power acquisition) implied North American revenues were closer to 35-40% of group, suggesting the USD proportion was higher pre-FY2024 revenue mix normalisation; the Components acquisition strategy (RF Industries / SL Power integration) may have shifted the mix marginally

  • GBP revenue estimated at 55-65% of group: UK Components and Systems segments both predominantly GBP; this limits translation exposure from GBP/EUR but concentrates reporting-currency risk in GBP/USD

  • EUR revenue estimated at 5-10% of group: Continental European OEM customers in Components; small absolute exposure; EUR/GBP sensitivity is secondary

  • Mix trend L3Y — shifting toward USD, then back: Custom Power acquisition in FY2021 materially increased USD revenue share; subsequent organic growth in UK Components and Systems has partially diluted USD share as a proportion of group; net trend over L3Y is approximately flat USD % share, with mix stability masking compositional change (acquired USD revenue base vs organic GBP growth)

  • FX-driven vs organic contribution: FY2022 GBP/USD average approximately 1.30; FY2023 average approximately 1.21; FY2024 average approximately 1.27 — the FY2022-FY2023 GBP weakening generated a translation tailwind of approximately 7% on USD-denominated revenues (approximately £4-6m boost to reported revenue on a £70m USD base); FY2024 partial GBP recovery reversed approximately 5% of this (approximately £3-4m drag); these are not separately disclosed by management

  • Constant-currency growth: Management does not routinely publish constant-currency revenue growth — a notable gap for a business with material USD exposure; Richard cannot directly separate organic from currency-driven growth from the disclosures alone

§2 — Cost by Currency

J-front verdict: Cost currency is GBP-dominant at the opex level, creating a margin mismatch with USD revenue; COGS has a partial USD natural hedge through US-sourced components procurement

COGS Currency Architecture

  • COGS — partial USD natural hedge in Power Solutions: The US-based Power Solutions segment sources components (cells, battery management systems, enclosures) substantially in USD from US and Asian suppliers; USD procurement partially offsets USD revenue at the gross-margin level; the natural hedge ratio at gross-margin level for this segment is estimated at 60-70% (i.e. for every $1 of USD revenue, approximately $0.60-$0.70 of direct cost is also USD-denominated)

  • Implication: Power Solutions gross margin is partially insulated from GBP/USD at the transaction level, but the hedge is not complete; US-domiciled payroll (manufacturing, engineering) adds USD cost that further improves the natural hedge

  • COGS — Components segment: USD input cost with GBP billing creates transaction exposure: The Components distribution business sources products from global manufacturers (many USD-denominated global list prices — RF connectors, antennas, power conversion) and sells predominantly in GBP to UK customers; this is a classic distribution transaction exposure — input cost in USD/EUR, selling price in GBP; the company must pass through currency moves or absorb margin compression

  • Opex — predominantly GBP: Group-level SG&A (UK headquarters, UK-listed company costs, UK-domiciled senior management), UK R&D, and UK engineering resource is GBP-denominated; estimated 80-85% of group opex is GBP-denominated

  • This creates a structural margin asymmetry: if USD weakens vs GBP, USD-translated revenue falls while GBP opex is unchanged — EBIT margin compresses; the USD revenue decline flows through to EBIT with limited opex offset

  • Capex — predominantly GBP: UK facilities, UK IT, and UK manufacturing capex is GBP; US capex for Power Solutions facilities is USD but is small relative to group capex spend; overall capex currency mix estimated at 70-80% GBP

  • Natural hedge ratio (group level): At group level, the natural hedge ratio between USD revenue and USD cost is approximately 40-50% — i.e. roughly half of USD revenue is offset by USD cost (Power Solutions direct cost + US payroll); the remaining USD revenue translates without a natural offset, creating net long-USD economic exposure at the group P&L level

  • EUR cost exposure: European component suppliers (some Continental European manufacturers invoice in EUR); this creates a secondary EUR/GBP transaction exposure on Components COGS; estimated EUR COGS is under 5% of group COGS — manageable, secondary

§3 — Translation Exposure

J-front verdict: Translation is the dominant FX mechanism for Solid State; L3Y average impact is an estimated ±3-5% on group revenue and ±4-7% on reported EBIT; FY2023 was a translation tailwind year; FY2024 modest reversal

Translation Architecture and L3Y Record

  • Primary translation pair is GBP/USD: Power Solutions segment USD revenue translates at average GBP/USD rate for the year; a weaker GBP inflates reported revenue in GBP terms without any operational change; Solid State does not separately disclose the FX translation impact on revenue or EBIT — RESEARCHER constructs this from average exchange rates and estimated USD revenue base

  • FY2022 translation impact (estimated): Average GBP/USD ~1.30; USD revenue base ~£70m equivalent; at constant FY2021 rate (approximately 1.37), reported revenue would have been approximately £3-4m lower — FY2022 was a modest translation tailwind (GBP weakening vs USD); estimated translation contribution to group revenue growth: +1.5-2.0pp

  • FY2023 translation impact (estimated): Average GBP/USD ~1.21 (significant GBP weakness post-Liz Truss fiscal event); USD revenue base ~£80m equivalent at reported rate; at constant FY2022 rate (1.30), reported revenue would have been approximately £5-6m lower — FY2023 was a meaningful translation tailwind; estimated translation contribution to group revenue growth: +2.5-3.5pp

  • FY2024 translation impact (estimated): Average GBP/USD ~1.27 (partial GBP recovery); at constant FY2023 rate (1.21), reported revenue was approximately £3-4m lower than it would have been at FY2023 rates — FY2024 was a modest translation headwind; estimated drag on group revenue growth: -1.5-2.0pp

  • Translation impact on EBIT is amplified vs revenue: Because group opex is predominantly GBP (fixed in GBP terms), USD revenue translation moves flow through to EBIT with limited opex offset; on an estimated £12-15m EBIT base (FY2024 reported), a £3-4m translation revenue swing equals a 20-30% EBIT swing — translation is materially more powerful at the EBIT level than at the revenue level; EBIT margin % compresses in GBP-strengthening years

  • Translation on balance sheet / net assets: The US-domiciled Power Solutions entity has USD-denominated net assets (goodwill, PP&E, working capital) that translate at closing rates; balance sheet translation moves are taken through other comprehensive income (not P&L) per IFRS; no P&L impact but equity volatility is real; disclosed in the "translation reserve" in OCI — FY2024 AR shows a small negative OCI movement on retranslation, consistent with GBP recovery vs USD

  • L3Y average translation impact on revenue: +1.0 to +2.0pp per year tailwind (average across FY2022-FY2024, net of FY2024 partial reversal); consensus models that assume GBP/USD stability or use trailing rates will systematically mis-state organic growth contribution

  • L3Y average translation impact on EBIT: more variable, estimated ±5-8% in a given year — this is the number that matters for EPS modelling; in a year of 10% EPS growth, translation can be contributing or costing 3-5pp invisibly

Sceptical Bullet

  • Bearish read — translation stability masking binary-event risk: The L3Y average looks modest because GBP/USD volatility was directional (sterling weakening then partially recovering); the binary risk is a sharp GBP strengthening (e.g. UK rate convergence overshoot, post-election fiscal credibility premium) coinciding with a cyclical softening in Power Solutions demand — translation headwind compounds organic softness; historical analogue is the 2014-2015 period when GBP-reporting companies with USD revenues faced simultaneous translation drag and demand deceleration; the static L3Y average understates the single-year event risk

§4 — Transaction Exposure

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is concentrated in Components (USD-priced inputs sold in GBP); secondary Power Solutions transaction exposure is partially naturally hedged; economic magnitude is smaller than translation but operationally disruptive to margin

Transaction Exposure Architecture

  • Transaction exposure — Components segment (primary): The distributor buys components at USD-denominated global list prices (paid in GBP at spot at invoice date, or in USD if the supplier requires USD settlement) and sells to UK customers in GBP under annual price agreements; the mismatch creates a transaction exposure window of 6-18 months (time between price agreement and component purchase/delivery)

  • Magnitude: if 30% of Components COGS (~£42-46m on ~£145m COGS) is USD-priced, a 10% USD strengthening that cannot be passed through increases Components COGS by approximately £4-5m — a 100-150bps Components gross-margin compression; this is the economically significant transaction exposure

  • Transaction exposure — Power Solutions (partial): Where Power Solutions buys from non-US (Asian) suppliers in USD and collects revenue in USD, there is a matched transaction; however, where Asian component costs are USD and US customer contracts are fixed USD, there is no transaction exposure on the revenue-cost match — both sides move together

  • Transaction exposure — timing lag: Annual pricing agreements with UK OEM customers create a structural lag between FX move and pricing adjustment; Solid State cannot reprice immediately; this creates a period of unhedged economic exposure until the next price negotiation cycle

  • Pricing pass-through assessment: Based on the mix of customer types (UK defence primes, industrial OEMs), pricing power to pass through FX-driven cost increases is moderate in Components (12-24 month lag), higher in Systems (government contracts with cost escalation clauses), and limited in Power Solutions where customer relationships are long-term and re-pricing is annual at best; overall group pricing pass-through is assessed as partial (50-60%) on a 12-month horizon

  • Cost pass-through from suppliers: Solid State as distributor has limited ability to renegotiate supplier list prices mid-year (global manufacturers set annual price books); the company absorbs USD/GBP moves in Components margin until the annual price book refresh; this asymmetry (cannot pass through to customers immediately; cannot renegotiate suppliers immediately) is the core transaction-exposure economic risk

  • Transaction vs translation magnitude: Transaction exposure economic impact is estimated at £2-5m annual P&L sensitivity in a ±10% GBP/USD move (primarily Components gross margin); translation exposure is estimated at £3-6m annual P&L sensitivity for the same move; the two exposures are additive but partly offsetting in direction (GBP strengthening = translation headwind AND transaction relief for Components importers; GBP weakening = translation tailwind AND transaction cost pressure for Components importers)

  • Cash-flow currency mismatch — net USD position: At the group level, USD inflows (Power Solutions revenue) likely exceed USD outflows (USD-denominated procurement payments) — Solid State is a net USD earner at the group level; this net USD cash position must be repatriated to GBP (to fund UK-level SG&A, dividends, capex), creating a regular conversion requirement that is the core transaction event

Sceptical Bullet

  • Bearish read — pass-through failure in stress: The 50-60% pricing pass-through estimate assumes customers accept cost escalation clauses in a moderate FX environment; in a stress scenario (e.g. GBP strengthens 20% over 18 months, UK manufacturing demand softens simultaneously), UK OEM customers facing their own margin pressures will resist FX-driven price increases, and Solid State's Components margin is squeezed from both sides — supplier costs in USD (already locked) and customer resistance to GBP re-pricing; the "partial natural hedge" of UK-priced sales disappears and transaction exposure materialises in full; the pricing-pass-through assumption is the single most fragile input in the transaction-exposure model

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Hedging policy is disclosed at a principle level (short-term forward contracts on transactional exposure) but is operationally opaque — no hedge ratio, no horizon beyond "short-term," no realised gain/loss disclosure; hedging discipline cannot be independently verified

Hedging Policy Disclosure

  • Mandate — stated policy: Solid State's annual report states the group uses forward currency contracts to hedge "known" or "highly probable" transactional FX exposures; this is consistent with IFRS 9 cash-flow hedge accounting treatment; the policy covers transaction exposure, not translation exposure (standard practice — translation hedging is rarely economic for small-cap UK industrials)

  • Translation hedging — explicitly not done: Management does not hedge translation exposure; this is rational for a company of Solid State's size (translation hedging costs are non-trivial; the instrument is a structural drag on earnings in normal years and only pays in stress scenarios); consistent with sector norms for sub-£300m market-cap UK industrials

  • Hedge horizon — "short-term," unquantified: The AR does not define "short-term" — RESEARCHER infers this means 3-6 months based on standard small-cap industrial hedging practice; this is a materially shorter horizon than the 12-18 month gold-standard for companies with structural USD exposure; the short horizon means that sustained GBP/USD moves (lasting 6+ months) will flow through to reported results after the hedge book rolls off

  • Hedge ratio — not disclosed: No notional value of outstanding forward contracts is published in the AR notes; IFRS 7 paragraph 40 requires disclosure of quantitative information about market risk including sensitivity analysis for each type of market risk to which the entity is exposed — Solid State's FX sensitivity disclosure is qualitative ("a significant movement in exchange rates would have a material impact"), which appears to fall short of the IFRS 7 paragraph 40 quantitative requirement; RESEARCHER flags this as a hard disclosure gap

  • Hedge instruments — forward contracts only: No mention of options, collars, or structured instruments; this is consistent with small-cap hedging practice (forwards are cheaper and simpler) but provides no asymmetric protection; if GBP moves favourably (USD strengthens), forwards lock in the company at the forward rate and forfeit upside — however, for a net-USD-earner, USD strengthening is favourable, so this asymmetry is actually less costly than it sounds

  • Counterparty — not disclosed: No disclosure of hedging counterparty bank(s); for a company of this size, hedging is likely with its primary banking relationship (likely Lloyds / HSBC / Barclays); counterparty concentration risk is non-zero but not flagged as elevated

  • Hedge effectiveness — not quantified: No realised gain/loss on derivative instruments is separately disclosed as a line item; it is either immaterial (consistent with small hedge book) or has been netted into COGS/opex without separate identification; RESEARCHER cannot verify whether disclosed hedging policy generates meaningful P&L protection in practice

  • FRS 102 vs IFRS — applicable standard: Solid State reports under IFRS (confirmed post-AIM listing compliance requirements); IFRS 7 and IFRS 9 apply; the disclosure gap on quantitative FX sensitivity is therefore a potential compliance concern, not merely a best-practice gap

  • Effective hedging assessment — LOW confidence, not LOW effectiveness: The inability to verify effectiveness does not confirm ineffectiveness — the company's transaction exposure (estimated £2-5m annual sensitivity) is small enough that even a modest, informal forward programme could substantially reduce it; the concern is that Richard cannot independently confirm this

  • Hedge cost — estimated immaterial: On a notional hedge book covering perhaps £5-10m of USD transaction flow per quarter (rough estimate), forward premiums at current GBP/USD are sub-1% annualised — total hedge cost likely under £200k per year; this is not a meaningful P&L drag

  • Comparison to sector-general principles:

  • Duration: below gold standard (3-6M vs 12-18M recommended)
  • Ratio: unknown
  • Cyclicality: unknown (no data to assess counter vs pro-cyclical behaviour)
  • Instrument mix: forwards only (adequate but not asymmetrically protective)
  • Natural vs financial: partial natural hedge (positive); financial hedge as supplement
  • Transparency: poor (below peer median for companies of equivalent size with equivalent FX exposure)

Sceptical Bullet

  • Bearish read — "short-term" hedge expires into a sustained GBP-strengthening cycle: The bear mechanism: GBP strengthens materially over 12 months (driven by UK rate differential or fiscal credibility event); Solid State's 3-6 month forward book provides protection for Q1 of the move; at roll-off, the company re-hedges at the new (worse-for-them) forward rate and absorbs the full translation impact on unhedged USD revenue; because translation is not hedged at all, the full GBP/USD move flows through to reported P&L; the company's "hedging policy" covers only the transactional sub-component and provides no protection against the larger translation exposure; Richard's forecast should assume translation exposure is fully unhedged for planning purposes

Second-Order and Non-Linear Effects

  • Pricing-pass-through ceiling: In Components, annual OEM price negotiations are the repricing mechanism; in a sustained GBP-strengthening environment, UK OEM customers will resist price increases; the pricing-pass-through ceiling is approximately 50-70% of the FX move on a 12-24 month horizon, meaning 30-50% of the transaction impact is absorbed as permanent margin compression until the next product cycle

  • Supplier-renegotiation trigger: At USD strengthening >20%, Solid State may be able to renegotiate GBP list prices with US/Asian suppliers (arguing GBP strength makes UK a more expensive market); this is a positive non-linearity in USD-weakness scenarios; however, it is negotiating leverage, not a guaranteed offset

  • Demand second-order effect: A sharp GBP strengthening that damages UK manufacturing competitiveness (the primary customer base for Components) would simultaneously compress FX margins AND reduce customer demand — a double negative; this is the compound tail risk
