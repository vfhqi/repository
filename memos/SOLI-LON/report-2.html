§7 — Value Chain — Competition

J-front verdict: Competitive intensity in defence electronics distribution is structurally lower than in general distribution, but scale disadvantage vs global peers is a persistent constraint [#J]

  • Industry structure: Defence electronics supply chain is characterised by approved-vendor lists, security clearances, and long qualification cycles — these are genuine barriers to entry that benefit incumbents like Solid State [#A]

  • Market structure: The UK specialist defence electronics distribution market is fragmented; Solid State is one of the larger independent players but is tiny relative to global defence primes (BAE Systems supply chain spends dwarf Solid State's revenues) [#I]

  • New competitor: US-based defence electronics distributors (e.g. Richardson RFPD, TTI Inc) have UK presences and could intensify competition if UK defence spend grows materially — scale and component access would be advantages [#A]

  • Intensity: In industrial distribution, competition from RS Group, Farnell, and Mouser/Digi-Key is intense and scale-driven; Solid State cannot compete on breadth or price in this arena [#A] ❌

  • BtE: Approved-vendor status and security clearances are meaningful barriers to entry in defence; replicating these takes 2–4 years typically [#A]

  • Solid State holds relevant security clearances; this is a real, if modest, moat

  • Disintermediation: The structural threat of OEM-direct models (component manufacturers selling direct to end-customers, bypassing distributors) is a secular headwind for the distribution segment [#A] ❌

  • Competitor returns: Diploma plc (comparable UK specialist distribution) earns ROIC of ~15–20%; Solid State's implied ROIC is likely in the 8–12% range currently, reflecting integration drag [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: Approved-vendor moats in defence can erode if primes consolidate supply chains around fewer, larger partners — a mega-prime like BAE Systems rationalising its supply base could remove Solid State from approved lists regardless of relationship history [#J]

§8 — Value Chain — Disruption

J-front verdict: Disruption risk is moderate and concentrated in the distribution segment; the manufacturing/systems segment has more durable positioning, but AI procurement tools are an emerging threat [#J]

  • Technology disruption: AI-powered procurement platforms (Sourceability, Octopart/Altium, Nexar) are reducing the information asymmetry that traditional distributors relied on for margin [#A] ❌
  • These platforms aggregate component data, pricing, and availability across thousands of sources — reducing the need for a specialist distributor's market knowledge

  • Disruption pace: early-stage but accelerating; not yet a P&L event for Solid State but a 3–5 year structural concern

  • New entrant — innovative: Digital-native electronics distributors (e.g. Octopart, FindChips) are building procurement infrastructure that bypasses traditional distribution relationships [#A]

  • New entrant — traditional: Global broadliners (Arrow, Avnet, TTI) are investing in digital interfaces and value-added services that overlap with Solid State's specialist positioning [#A]

  • Incumbent response: Solid State's stated response is to move up the value chain into manufacturing and systems integration — this is the correct directional response but execution is multiyear [#A]

  • Disruption cost: If Solid State's distribution segment sees structural margin erosion of 100–200bps from digital disintermediation over 3–5 years, the group EBIT impact is approximately £1–2m on current revenues — material for a company with £6–8m EBIT [#J]

  • Adoption curve: Defence procurement is slower to adopt digital procurement platforms due to security and compliance requirements — this provides Solid State with a time buffer [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: Solid State's move into manufacturing via acquisition does not fully protect it from distribution disruption — the distribution segment still contributes meaningful revenue and, if disrupted faster than the manufacturing build-out matures, the transition period could be financially painful [#J]

§11 — Revenue/Demand Cycle

J-front verdict: Solid State sits at an awkward cycle junction — defence is mid-cycle uplift, industrial distribution is trough/early-recovery; group revenues are not cleanly positioned either way [#J]

  • Cyclicality: Defence electronics revenues are less cyclical than industrial distribution but are subject to lumpy programme timing; industrial distribution is highly cyclical and currently in a down-cycle [#A]

  • Cycle stage: Industrial electronics distribution sector is in a late inventory-digestion phase (FY23–FY25) — consistent with RS Group, Arrow, Avnet all reporting weak organic trends [#I]

  • Recovery in industrial distribution typically lags by 6–12 months post inventory normalisation; FY25–FY26 is the plausible recovery window

  • Inventory cycle: Post-COVID component excess inventory in industrial electronics is being worked through; Solid State's distribution customers are buying at lower volumes than underlying consumption [#I]

  • Capex cycle: UK defence capex cycle is in a genuine multi-year upcycle; this is more durable than an inventory cycle and more supportive for Solid State's systems/manufacturing revenues [#A]

  • Through-cycle profile: Solid State has not been tested through a genuine defence spend downcycle in its current form; the FY21–FY24 period has been broadly favourable [#A]

  • Predictability: Order book disclosure is limited; management does not regularly disclose book-to-bill or order intake metrics, reducing revenue predictability for external analysts [#A] ❌

  • Sceptical bullet: The risk is that Solid State's current revenue run-rate reflects a confluence of (1) defence catch-up pulse, (2) post-acquisition revenue consolidation, and (3) favourable FX on US acquisition — all of which could partially reverse simultaneously, creating a group-level revenue air pocket in FY25–FY26 [#J]
