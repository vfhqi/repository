Medium plausibility; recycled-material prices (paper, plastics, secondary metals) have partially recovered from 2023 lows but remain structurally below 2021-2022 peaks; full recovery to bull-case levels is unlikely in the 18-24M window.

Driver definition: Recycled material prices (especially recovered paper, secondary plastics, ferrous/non-ferrous metals) recovering to normalised levels, driving €100-200m of Waste segment EBIT uplift from trough [#A]

Magnitude required: Recovery in recovered paper from trough ~€40/tonne (H2-2023) toward ~€70-80/tonne sustained; secondary metals back toward 2021 normalised pricing; net EBIT impact ~€100-150m uplift [#A]

  • Baseline: Recycled-material prices recovered partially in FY24 (H2-FY24 stronger than H1); but remain ~20-30% below 2021 peak levels [#A]

  • Target state: Full normalised pricing embedded in GreenUp plan would represent an ~€150-200m swing from trough — this is NOT included in management's base case explicitly but creates upside optionality if markets recover [#J]

Bull — historical: 2017-2021 period showed sustained recovered-paper prices at €80-120/tonne, generating material Waste segment profitability; this level is achievable again under normalised demand [#A]

  • Bull — recent: European recovered paper prices did partially recover in H2-FY24 per industry trackers; secondary metals (copper, aluminium scrap) also firmed on Chinese demand resurgence [#A]

  • Bull — industry: EU Packaging Regulation (EPR reforms) creates structural demand pull for recycled materials as brand-owners require recycled content; this is a multi-year tailwind [#A]

Bear — historical drag: China's National Sword policy (2018) permanently reduced Chinese demand for contaminated recyclables; the 2021-2022 peak was in part a COVID supply-chain distortion rather than normalised pricing [#A]

  • Bear — structural: ❌ Structural overcapacity in European paper recycling has emerged as more waste feedstock became available post-2020; this suppresses price recovery even with demand improvement [#A]

  • Bear — macro: European industrial output slowdown reduces secondary metal demand directly; Germany's steel production decline is particularly negative for ferrous scrap pricing [#A]

  • Bear — SS skeptic: Berenberg flags recycling pricing as a "persistent headwind" rather than a cyclical trough — their base case for FY25-FY26 does not model a return to 2021-level pricing; they treat this as structural impairment [#A]

Forward trajectory: Partial recovery (€50-80m EBIT uplift) is plausible in FY25-FY26; full recovery (€150-200m) requires structural demand restoration not visible in forward indicators [#A]

  • Probability — verbal: Medium plausibility for partial recovery; low plausibility for full recovery within 24M [#J]

  • Downside scenario: If prices retrace to H1-2023 lows (another demand shock), ~€100-150m EBIT headwind vs FY24 run-rate [#A]

Consensus alignment: SS consensus appears to model only partial recycling price recovery in base case; full recovery is not embedded in any mainstream model reviewed [#A]

Evidence gap: Veolia does not disclose the tonnes of recycled material sold or average price per tonne — making it very difficult to reverse-engineer EBIT sensitivity from public data; this is an analytically frustrating transparency gap [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility for partial recovery contribution; the structural overcapacity argument is compelling enough to prevent rating this high plausibility. Fulcrum-level vs APM Pass 1: Not fulcrum — amplifier with asymmetric downside risk

  • Sceptical close: Could recycling pricing deteriorate further in FY25 if European industrial activity slows more than expected? If so, this driver becomes a headwind rather than a recovery story, and Waste segment EBIT disappoints below GreenUp targets [#J]

§6 — Key Driver: Water and Energy Services Contract Repricing / Margin Uplift

High plausibility; contract repricing is the most mechanically certain driver in the set — inflation-linked clauses are legally embedded in the majority of Veolia's regulated contracts and have been executing consistently.

Driver definition: Legacy Water and Energy Services contracts being repriced at CPI/PPI-linked rates or renegotiated at higher absolute levels as they come up for renewal (3-10 year contract cycles), driving ~€200-300m incremental EBIT over FY24-FY27 [#A]

Magnitude required: Contract repricing delivering ~€80-100m incremental annual EBIT through FY27 on top of volume growth [#A]

  • Baseline: Approximately 60-70% of Water revenues and ~50% of Energy Services revenues are under inflation-indexed long-term contracts; annual repricing uplift embedded in contractual mechanics [#A]

  • Target state: Repricing cadence sustaining real margin expansion as cost base is managed flat in nominal terms; implicit ~50-80bps EBIT margin contribution [#A]

Bull — historical: European municipal water contracts have consistently repriced at or above CPI over the past decade; Veolia's French Water (largest single country) repriced at ~+4-5% in FY23 following CPI indexation [#A]

  • Bull — recent: H1-FY24 management commentary specifically highlighted "positive price-mix effect" in Water segment of ~+5%; this is the contractual repricing mechanism working as designed [#A]

  • Bull — mgmt: CFO confirmed at FY23 annual results that ~80% of Veolia's contract base has CPI or PPI escalation clauses; management explicitly identifies contract repricing as "the most visible and reliable" component of GreenUp Phase 2 [#A]

  • Bull — competitive: Peers in regulated water (Pennon, Severn Trent in UK; Saur in France private) face the same repricing dynamic; Veolia's scale creates additional bargaining leverage on contract extensions [#A]

  • Bull — industry: European Energy Services (district heating, industrial steam, building decarbonisation) is experiencing a contract-length extension trend as municipalities seek long-term price certainty; longer contracts = higher NPV per contract won [#A]

Bear — execution: Municipal counterparty budget pressure in France and Germany may resist repricing above headline CPI; some French municipalities have politically resisted above-CPI water tariff increases [#A]

  • Bear — competitive pressure: Suez Reconstituted has been aggressively competing on contract renewals in France; there are credible reports of below-market bid pricing by Suez in competitive tenders [#A]

  • Bear — structural: Energy Services contract new-business margins may be lower than legacy book margins if competition for new contracts has increased; repricing legacy ≠ new contract margin equivalent [#A]

Forward trajectory: Repricing cadence is structurally embedded; no acceleration required; risk is deceleration if CPI falls (which is happening as European inflation normalises) [#A]

  • Probability — verbal: High plausibility — the most mechanical and least discretionary of the seven drivers [#J]

  • Downside scenario: CPI normalisation to ~2% from ~4-5% reduces the annual repricing uplift by ~50%; this is already occurring in FY25 as European CPI moderates [#A]

Consensus alignment: All brokers reviewed model positive pricing contribution; quantum varies but direction is universal [#A]

Evidence gap: Contract renewal schedule by year — if a large contract cluster comes up for renewal simultaneously (creating a "contract cliff"), the repricing benefit could be lumpy; not publicly disclosed at this granularity [#J]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — the most reliable driver in the set; contractually embedded and mechanically executing. Fulcrum-level vs APM Pass 1: Not fulcrum — high-reliability amplifier; could be elevated to co-fulcrum if Energy Services contract wins accelerate

  • Sceptical close: Could CPI normalisation reduce the repricing uplift to near-zero in FY26-FY27 as European inflation approaches the 2% target? The repricing driver is implicitly a declining contribution driver over the investment horizon if inflation normalises [#J]
