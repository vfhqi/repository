§1 — General

PPC (Δημόσια Επιχείρηση Ηλεκτρισμού, or ΔΕΗ) is Greece's vertically integrated incumbent electricity utility, majority state-owned (~34% Greek state post-partial privatisation), listed on the Athens Stock Exchange.

Description: PPC is an integrated electricity utility operating across generation, transmission (via independent operator ADMIE/IPTO — PPC divested its transmission stake), distribution (HEDNO/DEDDIE subsidiary), and retail supply — the full electricity value stack minus transmission [#I]

  • Sub: Generation mix: lignite (now largely exited), natural gas combined-cycle , large hydro, and rapidly growing wind/solar RES portfolio
  • Sub: Supply: serves ~5.5 million residential and commercial customers in Greece plus expanding Romanian operations (via Enel Romania acquisition, 2023)

Strategic objective: "Green Transition" corporate strategy targeting 70%+ RES generation mix by 2030, net debt reduction to below 2.5x EBITDA by 2025, and expansion of customer base in SE Europe [#I]

  • Sub: Romania acquisition (Enel Romania, closed late 2023, ~3.1 million customers) is the most significant strategic pivot — doubles addressable customer base
  • Sub: Stated RES pipeline: 7GW+ renewable capacity additions by 2030 vs ~2.4GW installed end-2023

Organisation: Three reportable segments post-restructuring: (1) Generation & Supply Greece, (2) Networks Greece (HEDNO regulated distribution), (3) Romania (post-acquisition) [#I]

  • Sub: Networks segment is the most regulated and cash-stable, underpinned by RAB-based tariffs reviewed periodically by RAE (Greek energy regulator)
  • Sub: Generation & Supply carries merchant commodity price exposure — dominant earnings swing factor

Listing: Athens Stock Exchange (ATHEX: PPC), reporting currency EUR, share price approximately €10-12 range during 2024-early 2026; market cap approximately €7.0-8.0bn at €11/share with ~700m shares in issue [#I]

§2 — Company Details

Net signal: Revenue model is ~40% regulated/quasi-regulated (network tariff + capacity payments), ~60% merchant/competitive (generation, retail supply) — partially moated but heavily exposed to wholesale power price and Greek macro conditions [#J]

Revenue source: Three primary revenue streams across the restructured business [#I]

  • Sub: Networks (HEDNO): RAB-regulated distribution tariffs — approximately €800-900m revenue, ~€500m EBITDA, highly stable, ROE governed by RAE
  • Sub: Generation & Supply Greece: wholesale/retail power sales — most volatile; heavily impacted by wholesale system marginal price , fuel costs, and retail bad-debt levels
  • Sub: Romania (post-2023): retail supply to ~3.1 million customers plus distribution assets — adds geographic diversification but near-term integration risk

Revenue mix: Approximate 2024E split: Networks Greece ~18%, Generation/Supply Greece ~62%, Romania ~20% [#A]

  • Sub: Romania's revenue contribution is high but EBITDA margin lower than Greece (integration phase, market conditions) — EBITDA split approximately Networks ~35%, Greece G&S ~50%, Romania ~15%
  • Sub: Networks segment has the highest earnings quality per unit revenue — contributes ~35% of EBITDA on ~18% of revenue

Geography: Predominantly Greece (GDP ~€220bn, population ~10.5m), with meaningful Romania exposure (~€320bn GDP, population ~19m) [#I]

  • Sub: Greece electricity demand growth approximately 1-2% pa structural; Romania slightly higher at 2-3% pa; both markets below Western European growth rates
  • Sub: Romanian electricity market more competitive than Greek — Enel (acquired by PPC), E.ON, CEZ present — PPC inheriting a subscale regional player relative to market leader E.ON

Cyclicality: High exposure to commodity price cycles (wholesale power price is ~60% revenue driver for unregulated segment); Greek macro sensitivity (retail bad debt spikes in downturns) [#A] ❌

  • Sub: 2010-2015 financial crisis: PPC EBITDA collapsed from €900m to <€500m, bad debt provisions exceeded €500m/year at peak — base rate for macro sensitivity exists and is severe
  • Sub: Current environment (2024-2026): normalising wholesale power prices post-2022 spike + improving Greek macro = benign, but reversal risk exists

Predictability: Low-to-medium vs European regulated utility peers — regulated networks segment is predictable; generation/supply segment is not [#A]

  • Sub: Regulated networks RAB tariff is reviewed every 4 years (current review period 2024-2027) — creates regulatory reset risk but generally predictable within period
  • Sub: Sell-side EPS estimate dispersion for 2025E approximately ±20-25% (range across brokers), which is wide vs sector median ±10-15% — reflects merchant exposure uncertainty [#A] ❌

Competitive advantage: Dominant retail market position (~55% residential market share Greece) sustained by brand, incumbent billing infrastructure, and customer inertia [#J]

  • Sub: Switching rates in Greek retail electricity approximately 5-8% pa — below pan-European average ~15% pa — indicating meaningful customer stickiness, partly structural (single incumbent for decades)
  • Sub: Networks monopoly (HEDNO) is a regulatory moat — no competition in distribution by definition; only tariff level and allowed return are contested

Secular trend: Three concurrent tailwinds: (1) European decarbonisation/RES buildout, (2) grid investment supercycle (EV charging, heat pumps), (3) re-industrialisation of SE Europe [#J]

  • Sub: Greek government targets 80% RES electricity share by 2030 (vs ~50% current including hydro); PPC as dominant operator is primary beneficiary of licencing pipeline
  • Sub: Data centre demand in SE Europe nascent but growing — early indication of industrial electricity demand uplift beyond GDP-linked baseline

Customer behaviour: Residential customer base is captive-adjacent due to inertia; commercial/industrial customers are more price-sensitive and switching-active [#A]

  • Sub: Post-liberalisation (2011 onwards) competition eroded PPC's share from ~100% to ~55% residential — pace of erosion slowed significantly by 2020-2023
  • Sub: Competitive loss to Mytilineos, Volterra, Elpedison in commercial segment is the structural negative — margin dilution in commercial supply ongoing ❌

Scale economy: PPC's scale (>5.5m customers, dominant generation fleet) provides purchasing power, grid balancing advantage, and regulatory political weight [#A]

  • Sub: Smaller Greek retailers (Mytilineos Energy, Elpedison) lack PPC's capacity to self-supply from owned generation — they buy wholesale and resell, making them structurally more volatile in price spikes
  • Sub: Romania integration adds scale to SE European operations but remains sub-critical for cost sharing with Greek operations — IT/back-office synergies only at this stage

Recent delivery (2021-2024): Three years of transformational execution — decarbonisation pivot executed faster than committed, financial rehabilitation completed, Romania M&A closed [#J]

  • Sub: Lignite capacity: from ~3.8GW in 2019 to effectively zero by end-2023 — ahead of EU coal phase-out obligations; cost structure reset accordingly
  • Sub: Rights issue (May 2021, €1.35bn) successfully placed at premium to book; strengthened balance sheet enabled RES investment acceleration and Romania bid
  • Sub: Bad debt provision normalisation: peak provisions ~€500m/year during crisis; by 2022-2023 approximately €150-200m/year — structural improvement in collections, supported by smart meter rollout and prepayment options

Internal change: CEO Giorgos Stassis appointed January 2019 — the single most important internal change marker [#J]

  • Sub: Stassis background: private sector energy (formerly at Energean and previously consulting) — unusual for a previously state-captured SOE; brought commercial discipline absent from predecessors
  • Sub: Management team renewal: CFO, commercial director, networks CEO all refreshed 2019-2022 — top-down cultural shift from civil-service ethos toward performance orientation
  • Sub: Board: Greek state retains nominating influence (~34% shareholding) but reduced majority; international institutional investors (post-rights issue) now ~40%+ free float — governance improvement ongoing

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on PPC's revenue model is that ~55% retail market share disguises accelerating structural erosion to leaner competitors who operate without PPC's legacy cost base; the Romania acquisition added complexity and leverage before Greek deleveraging was complete; and the "transformation" narrative relies on commodity tailwind (2021-2022 power prices) obscuring whether the operational cost restructuring alone would have generated acceptable returns — if wholesale power prices revert to 2018-2019 levels (~€45-55/MWh vs ~€100+ in 2022), EBITDA could revisit €800-900m, making the current leverage ratio structurally dangerous [#J]

§3 — Industry Context

Net signal: Greek electricity market is a structurally concentrated, regulator-mediated oligopoly with natural monopoly distribution and a transitioning competitive generation/supply segment — broadly supportive of dominant incumbent but with regulatory override risk and commodity price volatility [#J]

Market structure: Greek electricity generation highly concentrated — PPC ~40-45% of installed capacity (post-lignite exit), Mytilineos ~10-12%, Enel/PPC Romania ~8%, RES independents fragmenting the long tail [#I]

  • Sub: Top 3 players (PPC, Mytilineos, HERON/ELPEDISON) account for approximately 60-65% of total generation capacity — concentrated by European standards vs UK/Germany ~30-35% top-3 share
  • Sub: Retail supply: PPC ~55%, Mytilineos ~10%, Volterra ~8%, others fragmented — distribution curve steeply front-loaded in PPC's favour

Competitive intensity: Competition in retail supply intensified post-2011 liberalisation but is structurally limited by PPC's scale, brand, and billing infrastructure moat [#A]

  • Sub: New retail entrants face high customer acquisition cost vs PPC's installed base — economics favour incumbency; churn rates of 5-8% pa are low by European standards
  • Sub: Generation competition is more intense — CCGT peakers from Mytilineos, HERON compete for dispatch merit order; PPC's advantaged position is hydro (zero fuel cost) and growing RES (subsidised CfD-adjacent returns) ❌ partially offset

Regulatory shift: Energy regulator RAE has become more active post-crisis, setting RAB returns, overseeing capacity market mechanisms (HMEEP), and implementing EU energy market directives [#A]

  • Sub: 2022-2023 windfall profit tax (HMEEP mechanism) imposed on generators benefiting from elevated SMP — PPC subject to this; partially offset elevated commodity revenues but demonstrated regulatory override risk
  • Sub: Current regulatory review (2024-2027 RAB period for HEDNO networks) is setting allowed returns — RAE signals approximately 7% allowed return on RAB, broadly in line with prior period

Political shift: Greece's energy policy is strongly aligned with EU Green Deal — government has been a faster-mover on coal exit than most Central/Eastern European peers [#J]

  • Sub: PPC's decarbonisation was substantially government-directed — lignite exit accelerated by policy pressure as well as economic rationale; this is simultaneously a moat (policy tailwind for RES licences) and a risk (policy can be reversed)
  • Sub: Greek state as ~34% shareholder creates dual role — strategic principal and commercial principal — potential for value-destructive political interventions (price caps, employment obligations) remains a latent risk ❌

Macro shift: Greek GDP has recovered strongly from COVID trough — 2023 GDP growth ~2.0%, 2024E ~2.3% — above Eurozone average ~0.5-1.0%, supporting electricity demand and bad debt normalisation [#A]

  • Sub: Tourism-driven GDP model (>20% tourism contribution) creates seasonal electricity demand — hotels, air conditioning, summer peak — different from Northern European utility demand profiles
  • Sub: Energy poverty risk: high retail electricity prices (Greece among highest in EU on purchasing power parity basis) creates political pressure for state intervention on retail tariffs — recurring risk ❌

Thematic trend: European grid investment supercycle — ~€500bn grid capex required across EU 2025-2030 (per European Commission estimates) — directly benefits HEDNO network capex programme [#J]

  • Sub: EU funding (RRF, structural funds) available for grid modernisation — PPC/HEDNO is eligible beneficiary; partially de-risks network capex funding
  • Sub: RES integration requires grid reinforcement — PPC's network monopoly position means it is the mandatory intermediary for all new solar/wind connections — volume demand structurally underpinned

Disintermediation: No material disintermediation threat to distribution monopoly; retail supply more exposed to B2B direct sourcing (PPAs) from large industrials bypassing PPC supply business [#A]

  • Sub: Corporate PPA market growing — large energy consumers (aluminium smelters, data centres) signing direct offtake agreements with RES developers, bypassing retail suppliers including PPC
  • Sub: PPC response: developing own PPA offering for C&I segment — logical but requires competitive commercial team distinct from legacy retail culture

New entrant: RES-specialist pure plays (Terna Energy, Enel Green Power, RES independents) are growing capacity share but lack PPC's supply and network integration — they are suppliers to PPC's system, not direct competitors in retail [#I]

  • Sub: Terna Energy (TENERGY-GR) is the closest domestic RES pure-play comparator — smaller, higher margin, pure RES — growing faster than PPC's RES segment but from smaller base; market cap ~€2.5bn vs PPC ~€7.5bn

Peer divergence: No cohort or sector peers were available for direct comparison via D-BATCH-25; European integrated utility analogues (Enel Italy, EDP Portugal, CEZ Czech Republic) suggest PPC's transformation trajectory broadly follows the Eastern/Southern European privatisation playbook — 5-10 year earnings rehabilitation — but PPC's pace has been faster [#A]

  • Sub: CEZ (Czech state-controlled utility) underwent similar coal exit + RES pivot; achieved EBITDA margin expansion from ~18% to ~26% over 2015-2022 — directionally comparable but PPC's starting margin was lower and improvement steeper
  • Sub: EDP (Portugal) post-privatisation journey: 15+ years from state utility to investment-grade ESG-rated RES champion — PPC is approximately 5-7 years into an analogous journey

Sceptical bullet: The bearish cross-cutting read on industry context is that Greek wholesale power price normalisation (SMP falling from €200+/MWh in 2022 to €80-100/MWh in 2024) removes the commodity tailwind that drove most of 2021-2023 EBITDA improvement; simultaneously, increased RES penetration from independents will compress system marginal prices structurally over time, squeezing PPC's merchant margin; and the Greek state's dual shareholder/regulator role means any political shock (election, recession, energy poverty crisis) could result in tariff caps or employment interventions that destroy value faster than the transformation narrative implies [#J]

§4 — BB#2 Foundation Quality

Net signal: PPC presents a medium-quality foundation — credible operator transformation, genuine regulatory moat in networks, but GENERATE metrics below BB#2 thresholds on returns and FCF conversion, and MOAT breadth constrained by commodity exposure and competitive retail erosion [#J]

§4.1 — Operator (BB#2 TC1: Great Operator)

Operator: Giorgos Stassis (CEO since January 2019) is a demonstrably effective transformation CEO — delivered financial rehabilitation, decarbonisation acceleration, successful capital raise, and M&A execution within 5 years of appointment [#J]

  • Sub: Private sector background (Energean, consulting) is differentiated vs typical SOE CEO archetype — signals commercial orientation over political patronage
  • Sub: ⚡ RARE: Non-traditional SOE CEO — Stassis background in private energy sector is genuinely unusual for a dominant-state-owned utility with Greece's institutional history of political appointment; the Ryanair/Lifco analogue is imperfect but the operating culture shift is directionally similar [Cross-ref: §2 Internal change, §4.1]

Strategic ambition: "Green Transition" strategy is high-ambition (70% RES by 2030, SE European expansion, leverage <2.5x) — ambition is congruent with PPC's asset base and policy tailwinds, not aspirational without foundation [#J]

  • Sub: RES buildout target (7GW+ by 2030) is achievable given current pipeline and licencing position, though execution requires sustained capex and grid connection approvals
  • Sub: Romania acquisition as SE European platform — ambition to add Bulgaria or other Balkans market longer-term is signalled but not committed — optionality exists

Strategy fit: Strategy is congruent with the ambition: incremental RES capacity additions generate contracted/subsidised cashflows that reduce merchant revenue volatility; networks investment is RAB-compounding; Romania is geographic diversification with demographic growth [#A]

  • Sub: Mid-term moat-widening logic: more RES capacity → lower cost of supply → ability to offer competitive retail tariffs → defend/grow market share; virtuous loop if executed
  • Sub: Dynamic strategy across environments: management demonstrated strategic pivot capacity (lignite exit faster than plan, rights issue timed well, Romania bid post-COVID) — not a static playbook

Leadership team: Management renewal 2019-2022 was substantive — CFO, commercial director, networks CEO refreshed [#I]

  • Sub: CFO appointment of commercially-oriented executive improved investor relations quality — PPC's communication with capital markets improved markedly 2020-2023, per analyst commentary [AS-Eurobank Equities]
  • Sub: Resourcing: investment in renewables development team (licence acquisition, grid connection, EPC management) built from near-zero 2019 to ~200+ staff — critical capability addition

Culture: Culture shift from state utility toward performance-oriented business is acknowledged by all covering analysts but incomplete — legacy employment obligations, union agreements, and civil service grade structures persist [#A]

  • Sub: Headcount reduction: ~8,500 FTEs (2019) toward ~6,000 target (2026) — voluntary retirement scheme supported by Greek government; meaningful cost reduction but slow by private-sector standards
  • Sub: Stassis described by [AS-Piraeus Securities] as "the right CEO for this phase" — implying capital market awareness that CEO tenure + transformation phase match may not hold beyond 2027

Guidance posture: Guidance has been directionally conservative relative to delivered outcomes on EBITDA for 2021-2023 — company consistently met or slightly exceeded EBITDA guidance while missing FCF-to-equity targets due to capex slippage [#A]

  • Sub: 2025 EBITDA guidance (€1.5-1.6bn) vs consensus (~€1.55bn) — tight range signals management confidence; however leverage target (<2.5x by end-2025) is widely viewed by sell-side as aspirational given capex trajectory

Capital allocation: High-RONIC signalling: RES investments claim ~12-15% unlevered IRR; networks RAB investment claims ~8% regulated return — allocation tilted toward RES growth, which is the value-maximising decision [#A]

  • Sub: Share buybacks: zero — leverage precludes; not a capital allocator story yet; equity dilution (2021 rights issue) was value-accretive given subsequent performance, but dilution risk remains if capex overruns
  • Sub: Dividend reinstatement (2023 results) at modest €0.30/share (~2.7% yield at €11/share) — signals financial rehabilitation complete but not excess capital return

Track record vs environments: 2020-2024 delivery through COVID, energy price spike, and post-spike normalisation is a positive 4-year track record across a full cycle of environments [#J]

  • Sub: Performance during COVID (2020): EBITDA maintained at ~€495m despite demand fall — cost discipline and collections management; contrast with Greek utility peers who were more impacted
  • Sub: Energy price spike (2021-2022): captured upside via unhedged merchant generation; management did not over-hedge into the spike — correct call with hindsight ❌ (or lucky; hard to distinguish at IG stage)

Shareholders-first orientation: Partial — Greek state interest and employment obligations create competing principal concerns; however post-rights issue institutional investor influence has raised commercial disciplines [#A]

  • Sub: PPC has not engaged in value-destructive acquisitions (common SOE pathology) post-2019 — Romania is debatable but was strategically coherent; prior management legacy acquisitions in Balkans were written off pre-2019
  • Sub: No excessive executive compensation issues surfaced in analyst coverage — governance baseline adequate if not exemplary

§4.2 — Advantaged Business + SRCA (BB#2 TC2)

MOAT — Customer moats: Retail market share retention (~55% residential vs 100% pre-liberalisation) sustained over 13 years of competition — meaningful inertia moat despite structural erosion [#A] [CQ16]

  • Sub: Customer switching in Greece approximately 5-8% pa — structural inertia supported by billing infrastructure complexity, PPC's e-bill penetration, and multi-product bundling (electricity + gas + broadband pilots)
  • Sub: Network distribution monopoly (HEDNO) is the deepest customer moat — every electricity consumer in mainland Greece and most islands is a HEDNO customer by regulation; zero churn possible

MOAT — Production moats: Hydro fleet (~3.2GW installed, largest in Greece) generates at near-zero marginal cost — structural production cost advantage vs CCGT and RES competitors in hydro-optimal years [#J] [CQ17]

  • Sub: In wet hydrological years, hydro dispatch sets PPC's marginal cost below €5-10/MWh vs CCGT marginal cost €80-120/MWh — significant merchant margin advantage
  • Sub: RES fleet (growing to 7GW+ by 2030) adds to zero-marginal-cost production base — but RES is being replicated by competitors; less durable moat than hydro

MOAT — Scale advantage moats: ~5.5m customer accounts, dominant generation capacity, and integrated value chain create scale advantages in purchasing, regulatory engagement, and system balancing [#A] [CQ18]

  • Sub: Scale in balancing services: PPC as dominant generator has privileged position in ancillary services market — smaller generators cannot provide the same depth of balancing capability
  • Sub: IT and billing infrastructure: PPC's billing platform for 5.5m+ customers is a genuinely difficult-to-replicate asset; competitors building CRM systems from scratch face 3-5 year gap

MOAT — Regulatory moats: HEDNO distribution monopoly is a permanent regulatory moat — no competitor entry by statutory design; RAB grows as capex is invested, compounding the regulated asset base [#J] [CQ19]

  • Sub: PPC has strong licencing pipeline for RES — government preference for PPC as anchor RES developer (political economy of employment, investment) gives informal regulatory advantage in planning consent
  • Sub: Capacity market (HMEEP) design: PPC's thermal capacity (hydro + CCGT) receives capacity payments — small RES players without firm capacity cannot access this revenue stream

MOAT — Culture moats: Weak — transformation is ongoing; culture is the least developed moat [#A] [CQ20]

  • Sub: Commercial culture shift is acknowledged but incomplete — legacy headcount, union agreements, and civil service grade structures remain structural constraints
  • Sub: [AS-Alpha Finance] notes PPC's customer satisfaction scores below pan-European utility average — not a culture moat currently; neutral at best

MOAT — Relative scale: PPC is the largest listed Greek utility by revenue, asset base, and customer count — dominant relative scale in its home market [#I] [CQ21]

  • Sub: Revenue scale: ~€5.5-7bn (Greece + Romania) vs Mytilineos Energy ~€1.5bn, TERNA Energy ~€800m — 3-5x largest domestic competitor
  • Sub: Generation capacity: ~8GW installed (post-lignite + RES growth) vs domestic competitors ~2-3GW each — system-level scale advantage in grid balancing

MOAT — TAM: Greek + Romanian electricity markets combined addressable revenue approximately €15-20bn at retail level — PPC captures ~40% already; incremental TAM is SE European expansion (Bulgaria, North Macedonia signalled) [#A] [CQ22]

  • Sub: SE European electricity retail + distribution TAM approximately €40-50bn — PPC at early stage of realising SE European ambition; adjacency optionality exists
  • Sub: Green hydrogen, EV charging infrastructure, energy communities — nascent adjacent TAMs totalling €1-2bn addressable in 10-year horizon; not priced in current estimates

OPERATE (RA5) — high-level: SCU (System Cost per Unit) advantage exists via hydro/RES zero-marginal-cost generation; RSCU advantage less clear in retail supply segment where costs are higher than lean competitors; pricing power in distribution strong, in retail supply moderate and eroding [#A] [CQ23-26] — ESA Q8 to deepen

GENERATE — EPS and revenue growth: EPS CAGR approximately 40%+ over 2020-2023 (from near-zero to ~€0.70/share) — exceptional absolute pace but from a distressed base, not from a high-quality baseline [#J] [CQ27]

  • Sub: Forward consensus (2024-2026) EPS CAGR approximately 8-12% — decelerating toward GDP+ range as transformation matures; vs European utility sector median forward EPS growth ~5-8%
  • Sub: Revenue growth: 2024E ~€6-7bn (including Romania) vs 2019 €4.4bn — CAGR ~8-10% but heavily influenced by M&A and commodity prices, not organic volume

GENERATE — Deployable capex at high RONIC: €6.5bn capex 2023-2026 claiming 12-15% unlevered IRR on RES, 8% on regulated networks — if IRRs are achieved, this is value-creating deployment at scale [#A] [CQ28]

  • Sub: Current consensus assumes SMP ~€90-100/MWh for 2024-2026 — above historical average; execution risk if carbon pricing fails to support this floor
  • Sub: PPC's hedging policy for merchant generation is limited — deliberately exposed to capture upside; creates asymmetric downside if price falls faster than expected

  • Sub: Precedent: 2022-2023 windfall tax (HMEEP) extracted approximately €300-400m from PPC's commodity upside — shareholders bore the downside while state captured windfall; mechanism could recur
