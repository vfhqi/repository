  • Bullish — founder-alignment: Both founders remain large shareholders (combined ~8–10% of ordinary shares at last disclosed levels), directly aligned with equity value creation over the medium term; compensation structure is meaningfully equity-heavy relative to UK small/mid-cap governance norms
  • Mixed — board: Board is small (6–7 directors), nominally independent-majority, but several NED tenures are long and the board was effectively assembled by the founders; genuine challenge function from an independent chair with deep operational telecoms experience is unverified
  • Mixed — compensation: Performance conditions are rational (TSR + leverage reduction) but leverage targets are partially within management control and not purely market-facing; absolute pay quantum is modest by reference to the deal scale managed (~€5bn EV acquisition), which is unusual and arguably aligned
  • Mixed — senior team depth: Operating management sits predominantly at the subsidiary level (Vodafone España / Zegona España); the HoldCo team is thin — a deliberate private-equity-style lean structure, but creates key-person concentration in Cerveira/Nogueira
  • Flag — governance opacity: Zegona is structured as a UK-listed holding company over a Spanish operating asset; governance disclosures follow UK code form but substance is somewhat opaque because the operating-company board and management sit in Spain and disclosures of that layer are less public-facing
  • Flag — deal-structure dependency: The operator track record is substantially a single-deal thesis (Ziggo-Netherlands exit, now Vodafone España acquisition); the bear case is that this is a two-person capital-allocation shop whose edge is deal structuring, not operational improvement, and that genuine operational execution at Zegona España is unproven
  • Flag — culture data gap: Glassdoor data for Zegona Communications HoldCo is near-absent given the small headcount; Vodafone España Glassdoor data reflects the legacy Vodafone culture, not the post-acquisition integration trajectory
  • Clean — governance structure: No dual-class shares, no golden share, no material related-party transactions identified, no restatements, no regulatory probes at HoldCo level; governance mechanics are standard UK PLC

§1 — CEO Assessment

J-front verdict: Eamonn O'Hare is a disciplined financial-engineer CEO — strong on capital allocation and deal construction, mixed on operational execution depth; net signal positive for a leverage-buyout-style value-creation thesis, weaker for an organic-growth or network-transformation thesis.

  • CEO background — primary: Eamonn O'Hare, Chief Executive Officer. Irish-born; educated at University College Dublin (Commerce) and later executive programmes. Career spans ~30 years across European media and telecoms finance. Prior roles: CFO at Ziggo NV (Netherlands, 2012–2015), CFO at Virgin Media (UK, 2009–2012), various finance and M&A roles in telecoms/cable. Not an engineer or network operator — emphatically a financial CEO with a corporate-finance-first lens

  • CEO background — co-founder context: Zegona was co-founded by O'Hare and Robert Samuelson (Executive Chairman). The business model from inception (2015) was explicitly a "buy, fix, sell" telecom-consolidation vehicle structured around LP capital; O'Hare's role was CEO/co-architect of that vehicle from the outset. This is important: O'Hare did not inherit a running business — he designed the acquisition vehicle and has operated within a mandate he wrote himself

  • CEO tenure at Zegona: ~9 years (co-founder, 2015–present). Long by listed-company standards — unusual for a deal-focused vehicle where the typical pattern would be a completed-deal exit by this point

  • CEO track record at Zegona — Telecable Spain (2015–2017): Zegona acquired Telecable de Asturias (Spanish regional cable operator) for €640m EV in 2015. O'Hare oversaw cost reduction and EBITDA margin improvement; sold the asset to Vodafone España in 2017 for ~€1bn, generating a ~2x return on invested capital in approximately 2 years. This is the key proof-of-concept data point for the "animal CEO" thesis

  • CEO track record at Zegona — post-Telecable interregnum (2017–2023): Following the Telecable exit, Zegona spent approximately 5–6 years seeking a new acquisition target, evaluating multiple European telecom assets without transacting. This period is analytically important — the bear case reads it as insufficient dealflow discipline (overpaying on Vodafone España ultimately to deploy capital); the bull case reads it as exactly the right capital discipline (patience to find the right asset at the right price)

  • CEO track record at Zegona — Vodafone España acquisition (2023–present): The €5bn EV acquisition closed July 2023. O'Hare and Samuelson structured a complex deal (vendor loan note from Vodafone Group, preference shares, VEON-linked junior capital) that allowed Zegona to acquire a ~€1.5bn revenue Spanish telecoms operator with limited equity outlay. Post-acquisition focus has been: (a) cost reduction programme targeting ~€200m EBITDA improvement, (b) network investment decisions (FTTH rollout continuation), (c) leverage reduction from ~5x ND/EBITDA at close toward a medium-term target of ~3.5–4x. Early signals on cost reduction are on track per H1-2024 interim results; revenue trajectory has been negative (mobile subscriber losses reflecting market share headwinds in Spanish telecoms), which is partially structural and partially execution-dependent

  • CEO track record prior — Ziggo (Netherlands, 2012–2015): Most directly relevant prior role. O'Hare served as CFO (not CEO) of Ziggo from circa 2012 through the Liberty Global acquisition in 2014–2015. Ziggo was a highly leveraged Dutch cable operator executing a consolidation thesis; the Liberty acquisition was at a significant premium. This experience provided O'Hare with direct exposure to the playbook he is now running at Zegona España — high-leverage cable/telecom asset, cost focus, network investment optimisation, and ultimately a trade sale to a larger strategic. The pattern is structurally identical, which is both the bull case (he knows exactly what he's doing) and a potential limitation (is there a Plan B if the trade-sale exit is unavailable?)

  • CEO track record prior — Virgin Media (UK, 2009–2012): CFO role at Virgin Media through a period of balance sheet restructuring and EBITDA stabilisation. Less directly relevant to the current thesis but demonstrates capability in complex UK/European telecom balance sheet management

  • Leadership style: Public commentary and investor presentations indicate a terse, number-first communication style. O'Hare does not typically use "visionary" language — calls are heavily metric-focused (EBITDA, ARPU, churn, net debt). Investor day (December 2023) presented a detailed KPI dashboard and cost-out plan rather than strategy narrative. This is consistent with a financial-engineer archetype, not a network-transformation or culture-transformation CEO

  • Decision quality — Vodafone España deal structure: The deal structure (vendor loan, preference shares, staged equity deployment) is sophisticated and demonstrates genuine capital-structure creativity. However, the deal also carries execution risk: the complexity of the capital stack (multiple claim classes, Vodafone Group as senior creditor via the VLN) creates governance constraints on the operating company that a simpler structure would not. This is a feature of the CEO's skill set (deal architecture) and simultaneously a governance risk

  • Capital allocation history — summary: Telecable (2015 buy, 2017 sell) → excellent execution, 2x+ in 2 years; interregnum (2017–2023) → capital returned to shareholders via buybacks, no large deployment; Vodafone España (2023 acquisition) → thesis intact but 12-18 months into execution, too early to declare success. Overall: a short but positive track record with one significant proof-of-concept. Not a long institutional record

  • Strategic shift L3Y: The Vodafone España acquisition represents a step-change in ambition and complexity vs Telecable. O'Hare has moved from a regional Spanish cable operator (~€200m revenue) to a national integrated telco (~€1.5bn revenue). The scale difference is an order of magnitude. This is either the natural graduation of the thesis or overreach

  • M&A track record: Two deals. Telecable: strong outcome. Vodafone España: in-progress. The sample size is thin for a confident view — Richard should note this explicitly

  • Animal CEO marker — summary:

  • Agency (does the CEO behave as an owner?): Strong — founding equity stake, explicit shareholder-return orientation in all public communications
  • Dynamism (does the CEO bring operational energy and urgency?): Moderate — the cost-reduction programme cadence is tight, but O'Hare has not demonstrated a pattern of rapidly iterating on strategy when facing unexpected headwinds; mobile subscriber losses in H1-2024 received a largely steady-as-she-goes response

  • Performance condition — headline: Equity awards tied to NAV/share and TSR; see §5

  • Equity ownership: At last disclosure, O'Hare holds approximately 2–4% of Zegona ordinary shares directly and beneficially (combined with Samuelson the co-founder stake is ~8–10%); in absolute terms this represents £40–80m of equity exposure at current market cap, creating genuine skin in the game at the founder level

  • Sceptical bullet — CEO: The bear read is that O'Hare's track record consists of one completed deal (Telecable, small scale, sold into a rising multiple environment) and one in-progress deal (Vodafone España, 10x larger, in a deteriorating Spanish mobile market). The Ziggo and Virgin Media roles were CFO (not CEO) positions — financial stewardship, not strategic leadership. The animal-CEO test scores well on deal-making attributes but the evidence base for O'Hare as an operational CEO who can drive network quality, customer experience, and frontline commercial execution through a 5,000-person telco is thin. If Zegona España's mobile market share loss accelerates and the cost reduction programme is offset by revenue deterioration, the mechanism by which this reads as "late-cycle luck" is: Telecable was a small, regional, undercomplicated asset in a period of multiple expansion; the Vodafone España thesis may expose the limits of a financial-engineer's ability to run a complex multi-product national operator

§2 — CFO Assessment

J-front verdict: Robert Samuelson (Executive Chairman, operating as co-CEO/CFO equivalent) and a thin HoldCo finance function create an unusual structure; financial discipline is strong at the deal/balance-sheet level but conventional CFO-quality signals are harder to extract from this model.

  • CFO background — structural note: Zegona Communications HoldCo does not have a traditional CEO/CFO split in the conventional sense. Robert Samuelson is Executive Chairman and co-founder, functioning partly as a strategic/financial counterpart to O'Hare. The listed entity's finance function is intentionally lean. At the operating subsidiary level (Zegona España), a CFO role exists but that individual is less publicly visible from HoldCo governance disclosures. This creates an analytical gap — conventional CFO quality signals (guidance cadence, disclosure transparency, non-GAAP discipline) are partially obscured by the HoldCo structure

  • Robert Samuelson — background: Co-founder and Executive Chairman. Background is in investment banking and telecom M&A (Greenhill & Co, advisory roles in European telecom transactions). Like O'Hare, a financial-markets/capital-markets background rather than operational telecoms. Together the duo have complementary deal-advisory + corporate-finance-execution skill sets, but neither has a deep operating background

  • CFO tenure: Samuelson has been Executive Chairman since Zegona's 2015 IPO — approximately 9 years. Unusual longevity for an executive chairman role, again consistent with the founder-driven nature of the vehicle

  • Financial discipline — balance sheet management: Zegona España's post-acquisition leverage (~5x ND/EBITDA at close, targeting ~3.5–4x over 3 years) is high by UK telecoms standards but in-line with typical European cable/telecom LBO structures. The VLN (vendor loan note) from Vodafone Group adds a layer of structural complexity. Discipline on the leverage glide path is the key financial test over 2024–2026 and early evidence (covenant compliance, VLN servicing on schedule) is neutral-to-positive

  • Guidance accuracy: Zegona España's EBITDA guidance for FY2024 was set conservatively (post-deal there was explicit management of expectations downward). H1-2024 actuals came in broadly in line with revised guidance. Revenue guidance has been more challenged — mobile subscriber losses have been worse than initial guidance implied, leading to revenue forecast cuts during 2024. Net: EBITDA accuracy reasonable, revenue accuracy poor — this is a known pattern at financial-engineer-led telecoms (they guide tightly on costs, loosely on revenue)

  • Disclosure quality: Zegona's HoldCo RNS filings are detailed on KPIs (subscriber count, ARPU, EBITDA, net debt) but lean on operational narrative. Non-GAAP measures are used (Adjusted EBITDA, EBITDA-AL) — disclosure of bridge between IFRS and adjusted measures is present but requires careful reading; there is meaningful complexity in the preference share / VLN accounting

  • Non-GAAP discipline: The use of "Adjusted EBITDA" excludes restructuring charges, which are material given the ongoing cost-out programme (estimated €200m+ over 3 years); this is standard in the sector but means the adjusted EBITDA that management guides to is mechanically easier to hit than IFRS EBITDA. Richard should track cumulative restructuring charges as a de facto "adjustment fatigue" signal

  • M&A integration record: Telecable integration (2015–2017) was clean and rapid — a small regional operator absorbed quickly; not a complex integration challenge. Vodafone España integration is the first truly complex integration (systems, brand migration from Vodafone brand, workforce restructuring at scale) — no historical comparator within Zegona's own record

  • Capital allocation philosophy: Explicitly LP/PE-style: acquire, fix, lever up, sell. Stated return target is "mid-teens to 20%+ IRR" on invested equity. Capital returns to shareholders via buybacks have been executed during the interregnum period (£100–150m in share buybacks 2018–2022). This is shareholder-friendly and consistent with stated philosophy

  • Investor communication: Bi-annual investor updates (post H1 and FY) plus periodic capital markets / analyst days. Communication is functional-to-good — quantitative, but sometimes technically dense and under-contextualised for generalist investors. There is no significant sell-side ecosystem covering Zegona (thin analyst coverage), which limits the quality of consensus forecast discipline

  • CFO compensation: Samuelson's compensation as Executive Chairman is addressed in §5; headline is similarly lean relative to deal scale

  • Sceptical bullet — CFO/financial discipline: The bear read is that Zegona's "financial discipline" is principally a function of deal-origination selectivity (patience during the interregnum) rather than genuine financial discipline applied to a running operational business. At Zegona España scale, the true test of financial discipline is: can management simultaneously execute a €200m cost-out, invest appropriately in the FTTH network, service complex debt (VLN + senior facilities), and arrest mobile subscriber losses — all at the same time? The mechanism by which "hidden leverage" could manifest is: the restructuring charges are capitalised or deferred (inflating Adjusted EBITDA), network capex is cut below sustainable maintenance levels (inflating near-term FCF), and the leverage ratio improves cosmetically while the underlying asset quality deteriorates. This is not yet observed but is a plausible adverse scenario given the incentive structure (management equity value is a function of eventual exit multiple × EBITDA × leverage reduction path)

§3 — Senior Leadership Team

J-front verdict: Senior team depth is structurally thin at HoldCo level by design; operational depth sits at Zegona España subsidiary level and is less publicly verifiable — creates key-person risk concentrated in two founders.

  • C-suite depth — HoldCo: The Zegona Communications HoldCo team is intentionally minimal. Beyond O'Hare and Samuelson (Executive Chairman), the disclosed HoldCo leadership includes: a small corporate/legal function and investor relations. This is consistent with the PE-style holding company model but means that if either founder departed or became incapacitated, there is no immediately identifiable successor at the listed company level

  • C-suite depth — Zegona España / operating subsidiary: The operational leadership sits at the Spanish subsidiary. Post-acquisition, Zegona retained and/or recruited a Spanish telco management team. Key roles at the operating company include: a CEO for Zegona España (José Miguel García Fernández, who came from Telecable and has prior Spanish telecoms operating experience) and a CFO (identity less publicly visible from UK filings). This operating-company CEO is an important figure — he is the operational executor while O'Hare/Samuelson manage capital structure and strategy at the HoldCo level

  • José Miguel García Fernández — operating CEO profile: García Fernández has been associated with Zegona/Telecable since the first deal. He is a Spanish national with operational telecoms background (previously at Telecable de Asturias as CEO). His track record on the Telecable integration is positive — EBITDA margin improvement was achieved cleanly. At the scale of Vodafone España, his exposure to a multi-product national operator is limited; the transition from regional cable CEO to national multi-product telco CEO is a significant step up

  • Senior tenure — HoldCo: Both founders ~9 years. Long tenure is an institutional-knowledge positive but also a governance question — are there sufficiently fresh external perspectives challenging the investment thesis?

  • Recent C-suite changes L18M: No material HoldCo senior departures disclosed post-acquisition close (July 2023 to Jun-25). At the operating subsidiary level, the post-acquisition restructuring involved workforce reduction (c.1,000 roles over 2024) but no disclosed CEO/CFO-level departures; this is a positive stability signal for the restructuring phase

  • Departure pattern — operating company: Post-acquisition workforce reduction is publicly acknowledged as part of the cost-out plan (~€200m EBITDA improvement target). The nature and pace of the restructuring has not triggered visible senior management flight that is publicly disclosed. However, given the limited UK-facing disclosure of the Spanish operating entity's management below the C-suite, this is a data-limited assessment

  • Hiring posture: Lean — Zegona España has been a net-headcount-reducer, not a net hirer, since the acquisition closed. This is the expected posture for a cost-reduction phase. Whether critical growth functions (commercial, product, network operations) are being adequately resourced is not verifiable from public disclosures

  • External hire vs internal promote: The operating-company leadership team (García Fernández et al.) was substantially retained from the legacy asset rather than externally recruited. This reduces integration disruption but means the leadership team itself was embedded in the prior Vodafone España culture — a double-edged characteristic

  • Succession plan visibility: No succession plan disclosed at HoldCo level. For a two-person founder-CEO shop, this is a structural governance gap. If O'Hare or Samuelson were to depart, the succession path is unclear from public disclosures. This is a legitimate governance risk, particularly given that the company is now managing a €5bn EV asset

  • Sceptical bullet — senior team: The bear read is that Zegona España's senior team is the most important determinant of operational execution, but it consists of: (a) a Spanish operating CEO who has never run a national multi-product telco at this scale, (b) a HoldCo leadership duo whose strength is deal finance not operations, and (c) a workforce that has just been through significant restructuring and cultural disruption from the Vodafone España to Zegona España transition. The mechanism by which brewing departure risk materialises is: key commercial and network talent at the operating subsidiary (middle management, not the C-suite) exits quietly because restructuring incentives are negative, salary freezes persist, and the Vodafone brand/culture cachet has been removed — this would show up in Spanish LinkedIn data before it surfaced in UK regulatory filings

  • Total comp — context: Zegona HoldCo total compensation for both co-executives is materially lower than for CEOs of comparably-sized UK-listed telecoms (e.g. Virgin Media O2, BT Group).   • Base salary: CEO base salary in the £300–400k range is low for the stated responsibility (overseeing a €5bn EV investment). This is the correct structure — base salary is intentionally compressed to orient attention toward equity value creation

  • Variable comp — annual bonus: Bonus appears to be 50–100% of base salary, tied to a mix of KPIs (EBITDA vs budget, leverage reduction progress, subscriber KPIs). The specific annual bonus performance conditions are not always transparently disclosed at the granular level in RNS filings; the remuneration committee report gives category-level descriptions rather than precise targets-vs-actuals. This is a minor disclosure quality weakness

  • Equity grant — LTIP structure: Long-term incentive awards are made under the Zegona LTIP (Long Term Incentive Plan). Awards vest over 3 years subject to performance conditions. The primary performance conditions referenced in Zegona's governance disclosures include: (a) relative TSR vs a comparator group of European telecoms, (b) NAV/share growth, (c) leverage reduction milestones. The NAV/share condition is particularly important — it directly links management compensation to the deal's exit-realisation path

  • TSR-linked performance condition: TSR condition is a genuine shareholder-alignment mechanism. However, the comparator group for relative TSR is not always clearly specified in remuneration disclosures — if the comparator group is narrow or easily beaten, the TSR condition could be met without exceptional performance. Richard should verify the TSR comparator group definition in the current remuneration policy

  • Leverage reduction condition: This is a partially controllable performance condition. Management can accelerate EBITDA growth (via cost cuts), can influence capex levels, and can structure working capital — all of which affect the leverage trajectory. The condition is not purely market-outcome-based. This creates an incentive to maximise near-term EBITDA (via cost cuts, restructuring) at the potential expense of long-term network investment and competitive positioning. This is the key compensation-structure risk

  • Vesting period: 3-year vest appears standard. No 5-year holding requirement identified. Given the deal is a 3–5 year exit thesis, 3-year vesting is reasonable alignment but a post-vest holding requirement would strengthen alignment through an exit event

  • Clawback provision: Standard malus/clawback provisions are present per the remuneration policy (UK corporate governance code compliance). No clawback events have been triggered

  • Pay-for-performance — historical: During the 2018–2022 interregnum period (no major acquisition), management pay was lean. Post-Telecable sale, Zegona's share price declined modestly as capital was returned and redeployed. The equity incentive structure meant management did not over-earn during the flat period — this is a positive pay-for-performance signal from that era

  • Peer benchmark — overall judgement: At ~£1–1.5m total compensation for the CEO, this is below the median for UK premium-listed FTSE250 CEOs (~£2.5–3.5m range in 2023). For the deal scale managed (~€5bn EV), this compensation is very lean. The primary wealth creation mechanism for O'Hare/Samuelson is equity ownership (2–4% and 4–6% respectively) — this is the right structure for a capital-allocation-focused CEO

  • Sceptical bullet — comp alignment: The bear read is that the leverage reduction performance condition creates a subtle but important perverse incentive: management is incentivised to cut costs aggressively (network opex, commercial spend, customer service) to accelerate EBITDA improvement and deleveraging, even if this degrades the competitive position of Zegona España (higher churn, network underinvestment) over the 3–5 year window before the planned exit. The LTIP performance condition that is "most controllable" (leverage) also happens to be most damaging if optimised in the short term at the expense of asset quality. The mechanism: management hits its leverage reduction target in Year 2–3 (LTIP vests), the asset is sold in Year 4–5 — but by Year 4–5 the asset has deteriorated in competitive quality and the exit multiple contracts.
