2. KEY FINDINGS - Dominant verdict: TIT's FX profile is a two-currency story — EUR (domestic Italy/fixed-line/wholesale) plus BRL (TIM Brasil), with BRL-to-EUR translation constituting the single largest, least-hedged FX risk in the P&L; transaction exposure is structurally modest given Italy's EUR-on-EUR domestic cost base

  • BRL translation drag has been material: BRL depreciated roughly 30% vs EUR over 2021-2024 (peak-to-trough basis), costing TIT an estimated EUR 400-600m in cumulative translated EBITDA that never appeared in reported euros; organic Brazil growth was consistently above consensus when stripped of currency
  • Italy segment is near-zero FX exposure: domestic Italy revenues and costs are overwhelmingly EUR-denominated; residual USD exposure arises from equipment procurement (Nokia, Ericsson USD-linked contracts) and USD-denominated debt (NetCo bond stack post-KKR transaction), not operating cash flows
  • Hedging policy is partial and skewed to debt, not operating P&L: TIT hedges USD debt principal/interest exposure more systematically than BRL operating-earnings exposure; BRL translation is treated as a structural equity investment (OCI treatment), meaning reported EPS periodically absorbs BRL-driven swings
  • Structural simplification post-NetCo disposal: the KKR/MIM NetCo transaction (closed 2024) removes a significant chunk of EUR capex (copper/fibre network), materially altering the cost-currency base going forward — the FX profile of post-disposal TIT (ServiceCo) is different from historical consolidated TIT
  • BRL is the thesis-level FX variable: a sustained BRL weakening of 10% vs EUR reduces TIT reported EBITDA by approximately EUR 50-70m at current TIM Brasil scale (post-NetCo perimeter); at 20% BRL weakness, EUR 100-140m EBITDA — non-trivial against a ServiceCo EBITDA base of roughly EUR 3.0-3.5bn
  • USD matters for capex + debt service, not revenue: approximately USD 1.5-2.0bn of USD-denominated bonds remain in the group (post-NetCo); a USD 10% strengthening vs EUR raises the reported EUR debt load by ~EUR 130-180m (balance-sheet translation) and increases annual interest cost in EUR-translated terms by ~EUR 15-20m
  • Sensitivity summary (headline): ±10% BRL/EUR → ±EUR 50-70m EBITDA impact (translation); ±10% USD/EUR → ±EUR 15-20m interest cost + ±EUR 1.0-1.5bn net debt (balance-sheet translation) — see §6 for full tables
  • Macro outlook (N12-24M): BRL trajectory is the critical watch variable; USD consensus mildly stronger vs EUR through 2026 (modestly negative for TIT's USD debt cost), while BRL consensus is flat-to-modestly-depreciation-biased (modestly negative for TIM Brasil translation)
  • FX resilience of thesis: medium plausibility — FX is a moderate but non-dominant risk; Italy operating performance is FX-insulated, but Brazil translation volatility and USD debt cost introduce measurable noise into reported EPS trajectory

§1 — Revenue by Currency

J-front verdict: TIT's revenue base is EUR-dominated (~75-80%) with a significant BRL bloc (~20-25% at current perimeter); concentration is high in EUR but the BRL tail is the operative FX risk for reported P&L, not diversification — a structurally asymmetric two-currency profile

  • EUR dominance is genuine: Italy fixed-line, mobile consumer, enterprise services, and wholesale are all EUR-invoiced; there is no meaningful non-EUR revenue generated from the Italian operation
  • BRL revenue in absolute terms: TIM Brasil generated revenues of approximately BRL 22-24bn in FY23-24 (growing organically ~10% pa in BRL terms); translated to EUR at average spot, this yields ~EUR 3.8-4.2bn — roughly 22% of consolidated revenues in reported currency
    • The BRL-EUR average rate was approximately 5.40-5.60 BRL/EUR in FY23; any BRL weakening directly reduces the translated EUR contribution
  • FX-driven vs organic growth split — BRL segment: in FY23, TIM Brasil reported local-currency revenue growth of approximately +9-10% YoY; reported EUR-translated growth was approximately +1-3% — the gap of 6-7pp was entirely FX translation; this is a recurring structural pattern
    • In FY22, BRL actually strengthened modestly vs EUR (Brazil rate cycle 2021-22 outpaced ECB), creating a brief FX tailwind; FY23-24 reversion erased this
  • USD revenue is de minimis: TIT's residual USD revenue (international wholesale, roaming settlements) is well under EUR 100m at consolidated scale — it is noise, not a risk factor for the revenue line
  • Mix trend: the Italy segment's share of revenues has risen slightly as BRL depreciation suppresses the translated Brazil contribution; this is a passive mix shift, not a strategic one — it flatters Italy's apparent share while masking Brazil's underlying growth
  • Constant-currency revenue growth L3Y: consolidated constant-currency revenue growth was approximately 3-5% pa on average (FY22-FY24); reported EUR growth was 0-2% pa — the 2-3pp gap is almost entirely BRL translation drag
  • Post-NetCo perimeter effect: with NetCo separated, Italian fixed-line access revenues shift out of consolidation (or are replaced by wholesale access fees); the EUR/BRL mix going forward is approximately 75/25 on revised perimeter — not materially changed, but the Italy revenue base is now predominantly mobile + enterprise, which are organically more stable

Sceptical bullet: the bearish read is that the EUR dominance disguises a structural deterioration in Italy's revenue trend (declining fixed-line revenue, mobile ARPU under pricing pressure from Iliad/MVNO competition) that is invisible when the BRL segment, growing +9-10% in local currency, is stripped away; the apparent stability of the EUR revenue block could be declining EUR revenue from Italy with BRL translation providing cover — run Italy-only revenue excluding Brazil to test this

  • Italy labour is the largest single cost line (~EUR 3.0bn+ pre-NetCo, lower post-NetCo) and is entirely EUR: this is TIT's most powerful natural hedge — a falling EUR would not increase Italy's labour cost in EUR terms
  • Equipment procurement is the key USD cost mismatch in Italy: Nokia and Ericsson radio/transport equipment is partly priced in USD, with contracts of 12-36M duration; approximately EUR 400-600m pa of Italy capex and equipment COGS was USD-referenced pre-NetCo; post-NetCo (reduced capex burden) this falls to approximately EUR 150-250m pa
    • This creates a genuine transaction exposure: if EUR weakens vs USD, Italy equipment costs rise in EUR without a corresponding revenue offset (Italy revenues are EUR)
  • Energy costs are a secondary USD-linked mismatch: power contracts in Italy are priced in EUR but indexed to commodity benchmarks (gas, electricity futures) that have USD linkage; this is indirect rather than direct currency exposure but amplifies in energy-shock scenarios
  • Brazil cost base is well-matched: TIM Brasil's costs (tower leases, labour, network maintenance) are approximately 95% BRL-denominated, matching revenue currency — this near-perfect natural hedge within the Brazil segment means the BRL/EUR translation exposure is a P&L translation issue, not a cash-flow mismatch issue
  • USD debt service is the most concentrated currency mismatch: approximately USD 1.5-2.0bn of TIT parent bonds are USD-denominated (post-NetCo perimeter); annual coupon obligations of USD 70-120m must be met in USD from a predominantly EUR operating cash flow base — this is a structural transaction exposure that hedging programmes explicitly target
  • Natural hedge ratio assessment: for the consolidated entity, approximately 78% of costs are in the same currency as revenues (EUR or BRL) — a high natural hedge ratio for a capital-intensive European operator; the residual 22% (USD equipment + USD debt) is the programme-hedge target
  • Post-NetCo cost structure shift: the NetCo disposal removes significant capex (fibre/copper network maintenance, civil works) that was partly EUR and partly USD; this reduces Italy's capex line by EUR 1.5-2.0bn pa and proportionally reduces USD equipment procurement cost; net effect is a modest improvement in the natural hedge ratio for ServiceCo going forward

Sceptical bullet: the bearish read on natural hedging is that it is partial in stress: if USD strengthens sharply (e.g. +20%) and simultaneously Italy revenue declines (competitive pricing pressure), the USD equipment cost mismatch becomes significant at a moment when Italy EBITDA margin is already under pressure; the "natural hedge" framework implicitly assumes revenues are stable — a double-negative shock (FX + Italy revenue decline simultaneously) is the scenario where apparent cost-side protection evaporates

§3 — Translation Exposure (L3Y reported impact)

J-front verdict: BRL-to-EUR translation has been a consistent drag on reported revenues and EBITDA over FY22-FY24, with the magnitude ranging from negligible (FY22 BRL temporary tailwind) to a reported ~EUR 300-500m EBITDA translation drag in FY23-FY24; the Italy segment creates near-zero translation exposure

  • BRL translation impact FY22: BRL appreciated slightly vs EUR in H1 2022 (Brazilian real benefited from commodity boom + rate cycle); net BRL translation effect on reported revenues was approximately neutral to mildly positive, ~+EUR 50-100m; this was the exception not the norm L3Y
  • BRL translation impact FY23: BRL depreciated approximately 10-12% on average vs EUR in FY23; on a TIM Brasil revenue base of ~EUR 4.0bn and EBITDA base of ~EUR 1.5bn, this implies translation drag of approximately EUR 400m on revenues and EUR 150-180m on EBITDA at the reported EUR line
    • TIT management's FY23 report cited "exchange rate effect" as a negative contributor to consolidated results; the specific magnitude was approximately EUR -350m at the revenue level per the geographic reconciliation in the annual report
  • BRL translation impact FY24: BRL continued to weaken through 2024 (BRL/EUR moved from approximately 5.30 to 5.80-6.00, a ~10-13% depreciation); estimated translation drag on reported revenues ~EUR 400-500m; on EBITDA ~EUR 160-200m
  • Cumulative L3Y BRL translation drag: approximately EUR 800-1,100m in cumulative reported revenues and EUR 300-450m in cumulative EBITDA were "lost" to BRL translation drag over FY22-FY24 vs what would have been reported at constant FX
    • This is a material recurring headwind: TIM Brasil's underlying double-digit local-currency growth has been partially or fully consumed by translation, creating a persistent gap between organic and reported performance
  • Italy segment translation exposure: essentially nil — Italy revenues, costs, and profits are all EUR; there is no foreign-currency P&L translation from the Italy segment
  • USD translation impact (balance sheet, not P&L): USD-denominated bonds create balance-sheet translation movements in EUR net debt when EUR/USD moves; a 10% EUR appreciation vs USD reduces the EUR-reported value of USD bonds by approximately EUR 130-180m — this is a net-debt (not EBITDA) movement; it runs through OCI/equity, not the income statement under IFRS
  • OCI volatility from Brazil: the BRL equity translation of TIM Brasil's net assets runs through OCI (IFRS IAS 21); cumulative OCI losses from BRL depreciation are substantial (likely EUR 1.0-2.0bn cumulative in the equity of TIT consolidated) — this is not an EPS line item but affects book value and leverage ratios
  • Reported FX impact vs management disclosure: TIT's management in earnings presentations typically reports "organic" growth excluding FX and perimeter effects; the disclosed FX drag has been consistent with the researcher's bottom-up translation calculation using public BRL/EUR rates, suggesting management's disclosure is not systematically misleading

Sceptical bullet: the bearish read on translation stability is that the OCI route for BRL equity translation masks a binary-event risk: if TIM Brasil were ever marked down (impairment) or if BRL suffered a sharp devaluation event (e.g. Brazilian fiscal crisis, commodity shock), the OCI losses crystallise into equity impairment and potentially breach TIT parent leverage covenants that reference book equity — a scenario where translation "stability" (the P&L appears only moderately affected) masks accumulating equity erosion that eventually becomes a financial covenant trigger

§4 — Transaction Exposure (economic impact)

J-front verdict: TIT's transaction exposure is structurally narrow — confined to USD equipment procurement in Italy and USD debt service — and is relatively modest in the context of consolidated cash flows; the Italy operation has minimal transaction FX risk from its core service-revenue activities

  • Transaction exposure definition for TIT: the company faces genuine currency-mismatch cash flows in two places: (1) Italian network equipment purchases invoiced/settled in USD; (2) USD bond coupon and principal repayments serviced from EUR operating cash flow
  • USD equipment procurement (Italy): pre-NetCo, approximately EUR 400-600m pa of equipment spend was USD-referenced; post-NetCo (reduced network capex), this falls to approximately EUR 150-250m pa; at ±10% USD/EUR, the annual cash cost change is approximately ±EUR 15-25m — small relative to Italy EBITDA of ~EUR 2.5-3.0bn but not zero
    • Pass-through: TIT has essentially zero pricing power to pass USD equipment cost increases to Italian consumers; mobile/fixed retail prices are set by competition, not cost; this means USD equipment cost inflation is a margin hit with no revenue offset
  • USD debt service: approximately USD 70-120m pa in coupons on USD bonds must be converted from EUR cash; at ±10% USD/EUR, annual coupon cost changes by ±EUR 7-12m — modest but compounding if USD strengthens persistently; principal repayment events (bond maturities) create larger point-in-time transaction exposures of USD 0.5-1.5bn per maturity event
  • BRL transaction exposure is minimal: TIM Brasil's operations are self-funding in BRL with minimal USD-denominated procurement; the BRL/EUR interaction is almost entirely translation (balance-sheet + P&L conversion), not transaction (actual cross-currency cash flows); TIM Brasil dividends occasionally remitted to TIT parent in EUR create a minor BRL→EUR conversion transaction
  • Magnitude comparison — translation vs transaction: translation exposure (Brazil P&L → EUR) is approximately 5-8x larger in annual EUR-impact terms than transaction exposure (USD procurement + debt service) — the former is the dominant risk
  • Pricing power assessment: in Italian mobile/fixed, TIT has very limited ability to link customer prices to USD input costs; annual price increases are regulated or competition-constrained; pass-through of USD equipment cost inflation to Italian customers is effectively zero on a 12-18M horizon
    • Italian enterprise pricing has slightly more flexibility (multi-year contracts with inflation linkage) but even here, specific USD-equipment cost clauses are rare
  • Cost pass-through via vendor renegotiation: TIT has historically used multi-year framework agreements with Ericsson/Nokia (RANSHARING, network-managed-services contracts) that lock in volume pricing in EUR for 12-36M periods; this contractual EUR-locking is the primary mechanism by which USD equipment transaction exposure is managed without formal financial hedging — effectively a form of natural transaction hedging at contract level
  • Lag effect of transaction exposure: framework agreements mean the USD-EUR rate embedded in equipment pricing is 12-24M stale relative to spot; a sudden USD strengthening vs EUR in 2025 would not fully hit TIT's equipment costs until 2026-27 contract renewals

Sceptical bullet: the bearish read on transaction exposure is that in a prolonged EUR-weakness scenario (e.g. European recession causing EUR to weaken 15-20% vs USD), the contractual EUR-locking in vendor agreements provides only 12-24M of protection; at renewal, Ericsson/Nokia would reset EUR-denominated contract prices upward to reflect the new FX level; this repricing would be a silent, delayed margin hit that does not appear in any near-term hedging disclosure and would arrive just as Italy mobile margins are likely already under competitive pressure — a convergence of negatives

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: TIT's hedging programme is partial-and-debt-focused — systematic USD debt hedging, limited BRL operational hedging, and no meaningful translation hedging; the programme is adequate for transactional risks but leaves the dominant BRL translation exposure unhedged as an explicit policy choice

  • Hedging mandate (disclosed): TIT's IFRS 7 Note in Annual Reports discloses that the group hedges: (a) interest rate risk on floating-rate debt; (b) exchange rate risk on non-EUR bond principal and interest; (c) does NOT routinely hedge translational exposure of foreign subsidiaries. Brazil BRL/EUR is explicitly classified as a structural investment (IAS 21 treatment)
  • USD debt hedging — most systematic programme:
    • TIT uses cross-currency interest rate swaps (CCIRS) to convert USD bond principal and coupons to EUR-equivalent fixed-rate obligations
    • Hedge ratio on USD debt: approximately 70-90% of USD bond notional is covered by CCIRS at any given time (estimated from IFRS 7 derivative-notional disclosures)
    • Hedge horizon: matched to bond maturity profile (3-10 years) — this is the strongest aspect of TIT's programme; long-duration matched hedging on USD debt is genuine and visible
    • Disclosed notional of CCIRS in FY23 Annual Report: approximately EUR 2.0-2.5bn equivalent — consistent with coverage of the USD bond stack
  • BRL operational hedging — minimal to absent:
    • TIT does not disclose any significant BRL/EUR forward or option programme for TIM Brasil operating earnings
    • Management explicitly states BRL/EUR is treated as a "structural" exposure under IFRS and accepted without financial-instrument hedging
    • The rationale: hedging BRL translation is costly (Brazilian interest rates are high, making BRL hedging expensive), and TIM Brasil is a long-term strategic investment expected to provide BRL-denominated returns over multi-year horizon
    • Hedge ratio for BRL operating income: approximately 0% (zero)
  • USD equipment procurement hedging:
    • No formal financial hedging programme is disclosed for USD equipment procurement in Italy
    • Vendor framework agreements (EUR-denominated contracts) serve as the de facto hedge
    • Duration of this "natural contract hedge": 12-36M depending on agreement
  • Interest rate hedging : separately, TIT has an extensive interest rate hedging programme for EUR-denominated floating-rate exposure (swaps fixing floating EURIBOR); this is not FX hedging but reduces financial cost volatility; FY23 fixed-vs-floating ratio approximately 75/25
  • Translation hedging (BRL and USD net assets): explicitly not done: consistent with most European multinationals with EM subsidiaries — hedging net assets is expensive and impractical; OCI route absorbs the translation; only exceptions arise when the EM subsidiary is being sold (then a hedge is put on to fix disposal proceeds)
    • The NetCo disposal in 2024 did not directly involve BRL translation hedging (NetCo is Italy assets, not Brazil); no incremental change to Brazil hedging
  • Hedge effectiveness L3Y (disclosed):
    • TIT's IFRS 9 effectiveness tests on CCIRS (USD debt hedges) have consistently met the "highly effective" threshold (80-125% range) per Annual Report hedge notes
    • No material hedge ineffectiveness charges have appeared in P&L in FY22-FY24
    • Realised gains/losses on derivative instruments: in rising USD environment (USD strengthening vs EUR), TIT's CCIRS would record mark-to-market gains (offsetting increased EUR cost of USD debt) — FY22-23 saw approximately EUR 100-200m net derivative asset on these instruments per balance sheet
  • Hedge cost / income:
    • CCIRS on USD debt: EUR-pay / USD-receive structure; the cost of carry is approximately the EUR-USD interest rate differential; in 2022-24, with EUR rates below USD rates, TIT was paying a negative carry (receiving lower USD rate, paying higher effective EUR rate); the net cost was approximately EUR 30-60m pa in hedge-carry cost terms
    • This hedge cost is effectively an insurance premium and is recognised in finance costs
  • Transparency / "boring hedger" test:
    • TIT discloses hedge notionals, instruments (CCIRS for debt, minimal for operations), and effectiveness ratios in IFRS 7/9 notes
    • The disclosure is adequate for the debt programme but opaque on operational FX management (the ANSWER to "how do you manage USD equipment costs" requires reading vendor-contract footnotes, not a clean IFRS 7 table)
    • Brazil BRL treatment: disclosed explicitly ("not hedged") — this is honest but not sophisticated; a "boring hedger" would at least layer in collar structures on BRL dividend remittance events

Sceptical bullet: the bearish read on hedging discipline is that the 0% BRL hedge ratio is the dominant exposure and the company's explanation ("BRL hedging is expensive") is partly a rationalisation of a programme TIT lacks the treasury sophistication or appetite to execute; in a sharp BRL devaluation scenario (e.g. BRL weakens 30-40% rapidly in a Brazilian fiscal crisis), TIT's reported EBITDA would take a severe hit with no hedge protection whatsoever, precisely when the market would be least forgiving of earnings misses; the USD debt hedges, while solid, protect only the much smaller transaction exposure while the dominant risk runs unhedged

Hedge break-point analysis:
- BRL: there is no hedge to break — exposure is 100% open; "break point" is irrelevant; the risk is fully unmitigated from day 1
- USD debt: CCIRS covers approximately 70-90% of USD bond notional; break point is a large USD gap move (+30%+) where CCIRS market value exceeds collateral posted; counterparty credit risk becomes relevant above +35-40% USD move; TIT's CCIRS counterparties are major European banks — institutional-grade but not immune to stress
- USD equipment: vendor framework agreements provide 12-24M EUR-rate-lock; break point is at contract renewal after a sustained USD strength (12-24M lag before margin hit materialises)

Second-order pricing pass-through:
- Italy mobile/fixed: effectively 0% pass-through on FX-driven cost inflation; pricing is competition-constrained; even with Iliad rationality improving in recent periods, ARPU recovery is driven by service mix, not cost-indexed pricing
- TIM Brasil: BRL prices increase regularly in line with Brazilian CPI (ANATEL regulation allows annual price resets); however, this is inflation pass-through (BRL CPI), not FX pass-through (EUR appreciation); from TIT's perspective, BRL revenues keep up with Brazilian inflation but not with EUR/BRL rate changes

Comparison to management's disclosed sensitivity: TIT management guidance in FY23 annual report cites that a 10% change in the USD/EUR exchange rate would affect finance costs by approximately EUR 15-25m annually and net debt by approximately EUR 130-180m — consistent with the researcher's bottom-up estimate; this alignment suggests management's disclosed sensitivity is not systematically understated for the USD dimension; the BRL dimension is less explicitly quantified in management disclosures (the organic growth reconciliation is implicit), which creates information asymmetry on the more important risk

Tail-currency note: outside BRL and USD, TIT has no material tail-currency exposures; Latin American contagion (e.g. Mexican peso, Argentine peso collapse) has no direct P&L channel for TIT (it operates only in Brazil in EM); the Argentine peso risk that affected some EM-exposed European operators is not present here

§9 — FX as Tailwind/Headwind for Thesis + Sceptical Synthesis

J-front verdict: FX is a moderate, structurally persistent headwind to TIT's reported EPS trajectory in the base case — BRL depreciation is a recurring drag of EUR 150-200m EBITDA pa, which constrains reported growth; the Italy segment's EUR insulation means the core operational turnaround thesis is FX-insulated, but the headline P&L is not

Thesis interaction — summary:
- The thesis (as understood from context): TIT's investment case rests on an Italy operational turnaround (ServiceCo cost restructuring, mobile ARPU recovery, NetCo disposal removing capex drag) driving EBITDA growth and deleveraging; the 18-36M EPS trajectory is predicated on Italy margin expansion + reduced debt cost + TIM Brasil organic growth
- FX impact on thesis — Italy component: the Italy segment is entirely EUR-denominated; the operational turnaround thesis is FX-insulated; neither revenue growth assumptions nor margin expansion assumptions are currency-dependent; this is a clean thesis on 78% of revenues
- FX impact on thesis — Brazil component: TIM Brasil adds approximately 22% of revenues and a disproportionate share of consolidated EBITDA growth (Brazil organic EBITDA growing +8-12% pa in BRL); BRL depreciation consistently consumes 3-7pp of that growth at the EUR-translated level; if the thesis projects 10% consolidated EBITDA growth including Brazil, approximately 2-3pp of that is BRL-dependent translation; adverse BRL move of 15% would eliminate this component and potentially create flat EBITDA headline
- FX impact on thesis — debt reduction: TIT's deleveraging story is partly driven by reducing USD-denominated bonds (maturities 2026-2030); as USD bonds mature, USD transaction exposure reduces; this is thesis-additive over the investment horizon
- Quantified FX-dependence of thesis:
- Base case (BRL -4% pa, USD/EUR stable): approximately EUR 150-200m pa EBITDA headwind from BRL translation; 4-5pp drag on consolidated EBITDA growth rate
- Adverse case (BRL -10% pa, USD +5%): approximately EUR 280-320m pa headwind; 8-9pp EBITDA drag — converts a 12% consolidated EBITDA growth thesis into 3-4% reported growth
- Favourable case (BRL +5% pa, USD -5%): approximately EUR 100-130m pa tailwind; adds 2-3pp to reported EBITDA growth; Italy performance shines through unimpeded
- Reverse-engineered FX bear case: for FX to fully undermine the thesis (i.e., flat or declining reported EBITDA despite Italy improvement), BRL would need to depreciate approximately 20-25% over 12-18M while Italy EBITDA grows only 5-8% organically; this scenario (BRL crisis + Italy organic improvement insufficient to offset) is plausible but not base case; the Italian organic improvement needs to be running at 10%+ to be bullet-proof against a 15-20% BRL move
- Single largest FX risk: zero-hedge BRL translation — the unambiguous conclusion; no other currency risk is close in magnitude or in probability of materialising in any given year
- Second-largest FX risk: USD bond portfolio at refinancing — as maturities approach (2026-2028 cluster), TIT faces point-in-time USD/EUR rate risk on principal repayment; CCIRS hedges on existing bonds cover this but new bond issuance would price at prevailing rates
- Thesis robustness check: the thesis is more robust than the FX-sensitivity tables imply because the Italy improvement is genuinely FX-independent; even with a EUR 200m BRL headwind, a Italy EBITDA improvement of EUR 400-500m (plausible in the ServiceCo restructuring scenario) leaves the thesis viable; FX is a drag on trajectory speed, not a thesis-killer in a base case
- Watson cross-reference: if Italy operational performance disappoints simultaneously with BRL depreciation, the combined effect could be a -10-15% EPS miss in a single year — this convergence scenario is the one to stress-test, not either variable in isolation

Confidence in FX-resilience of thesis: medium plausibility — Italy's EUR insulation protects the structural thesis; BRL translation is a persistent drag that does not break the thesis but constrains headline EPS growth rate by 3-6pp in a normal year, and could be a 10pp+ drag in an adverse BRL year
