KEY FINDINGS (BLUF)

  • Verdict — trough confirmed, trajectory genuinely improving: Nokia's earnings passed a clear trough in 2023–H1 2024 and now show multi-dimensional improvement — revenue, gross margin, operating margin, EPS and free cash flow all moving in the right direction. [#J]
  • Margin gap vs Ericsson is the dominant overhang: Nokia's comparable operating margin ran ~10% in 2023, recovered to mid-teens in 2024; Ericsson's adjusted EBITA margin reached 18.1% in 2025. The gap is real and structural, not merely cyclical. [#J]
  • AI/Cloud optical pivot is the earnings change-force: AI & Cloud net sales grew 49% YoY in Q1 2026 and now account for 8% of group revenues; €1bn of AI/Cloud orders booked in a single quarter; addressable market CAGR raised to 27% (2025–2028). This is the most material near-term earnings driver. [#J]
  • Revenue trough was 2023 — deepest at Q4 2023 (−21% cc): Full-year 2023 net sales declined 8% in constant currency from a 2022 peak of ~€24.9bn. The recovery is underway but still below 2022 peak in absolute revenue terms. [#A]
  • Segment restructuring (Jan 2026) complicates L3Y comparisons: Nokia moved from four old segments (Mobile Networks, Network Infrastructure, Cloud and Network Services, Nokia Technologies) to two new ones (Network Infrastructure, Mobile Infrastructure). Recast data available for 2024–2025 only. [#A]
  • FCF conversion was severely impaired 2023, now normalising: 2023 FCF of ~€0.9bn (est. 20–50% conversion); 2024 FCF ~€2.3bn (147% YoY improvement); 2025 guidance 50–80% conversion of comparable operating profit. [#A]
  • Q1 2026 beat was material — EPS +31% vs consensus: Comparable EPS €0.05 vs forecast €0.034, stock surged ~7–12% in Helsinki on April 23 2026, reaching 16-year high. Operating profit beat ~€250m consensus at €281m (+54% YoY). [#J]
  • Nokia Technologies (IP licensing) is a quality-of-earnings wildcard: ~€1.4bn annual licensing run-rate, now folded into Mobile Infrastructure segment; one-off catch-up payments distorted 2024; target to grow run-rate to €1.5bn. Recurring but lumpy. [#A]
  • ❌ Mobile Networks structural drag: Mobile Networks revenue was flat FY2025 and down ~21% in FY2024; peer Ericsson grew organic mobile revenues 2%. Nokia's RAN share outside China has improved modestly in 2025, but the segment remains a margin and growth anchor. [#J]

§1 — Executive Summary

Nokia's earnings trajectory 2022–Q1 2026 follows a classic bust-recovery arc, distorted by a deeper and longer-than-expected capex downcycle in carrier RAN and compounded by Nokia's own execution mis-steps in cost management.

  • Direction: Clearly positive since H2 2024. Revenue, gross margin, comparable operating margin and FCF all improving simultaneously on a YoY basis entering 2026. [#J]
  • Magnitude: The recovery is meaningful but not yet at prior-peak levels; 2022 was the revenue high watermark (~€24.9bn); FY2025 net sales of ~€19.9bn still ~20% below that. [#A]
  • Momentum: Accelerating at Q1 2026 — guidance raised, AI/Cloud orders surging, stock at 16-year high post-results. [#J]
  • 2022 baseline: Comparable operating margin 15.5%; full-year revenue ~€24.9bn. A period of genuine strength following 5G ramp. [#I]
  • 2023 trough: Comparable operating margin compressed to 10.7%; revenue −8% cc; FCF conversion collapsed 20–50%; guidance cut mid-year; Nokia announced ~14,000 job reductions in October 2023. [#J]
  • 2024 recovery: Comparable operating profit €2.6bn, at mid-point of guided range; Q4 2024 comparable operating margin hit 19.1% (up 380bps YoY); FCF of ~€2.3bn, up 148% YoY. [#A]
  • 2025 consolidation: Full-year net sales €19.9bn (+3% reported); comparable operating profit ~€2.0bn; FCF ~€1.5bn (72% conversion); Network Infrastructure grew 7%, Mobile Networks flat. [#A]
  • Q1 2026 beat: Revenue €4.5bn (+4% constant currency); comparable operating margin 6.2% (Q1 seasonally weakest quarter, up 200bps YoY); EPS beat consensus by 31%; guidance for Network Infrastructure growth raised to 12–14% for FY2026. [#J]
  • Peer context: Ericsson's adjusted EBITA margin reached 18.1% in 2025 vs Nokia's mid-teens comparable operating margin. Nokia is recovering but structurally below its main peer on margins. [#A]
  • ❌ The structural gap: Nokia's revenue base has not returned to 2022 levels despite partial recovery; Ericsson's margin profile consistently outpaces Nokia's. Recovery does not mean parity. [#J]
  • Sceptical note: The current recovery narrative is being driven disproportionately by AI/Cloud optical orders — a segment Nokia did not own as a primary business four years ago. The durability and margin quality of this new revenue stream is unproven at scale. [#J]

§2 — Revenue Growth & Organic Dynamics

  • Verdict — post-trough recovery real but incomplete vs 2022 peak: FY2025 revenue of €19.9bn is still materially below 2022's €24.9bn peak; growth is accelerating selectively in Network Infrastructure while Mobile Networks remains structurally flat. [#J]
  • 2022 peak: Net sales ~€24.9bn; growth driven by 5G RAN buildout, particularly in North America and India. Comparable operating margin 15.5%. This represented the best year Nokia had seen since the Alcatel-Lucent integration. [#I]
  • 2023 bust: Net sales declined 8% in constant currency; Q4 2023 at −21% cc, the deepest single-quarter contraction. Cause: carrier capex digestion cycle, inventory correction, macro headwinds. Nokia reacted with 14,000 headcount reduction announced October 2023. [#A]
  • 2024 partial recovery: Net sales ~€19.5–20bn; the decline moderated and then reversed in H2 2024; Q4 2024 Network Infrastructure grew 17% (IP Networks +24%, Fixed Networks +16%); Mobile Networks down ~21% for FY2024. [#A]
  • 2025 consolidation (new segment basis): Network Infrastructure revenue €7.6bn (up from €6.3bn in 2024, +21%); Mobile Infrastructure revenue €11.4bn (down from €12.2bn in 2024, −6.5%). Full-year growth 3% reported. [#A]
  • Q1 2026 organic: +4% constant currency; AI & Cloud sub-segment +49% YoY and now 8% of group sales; Optical Networks +20%; Mobile Networks approximately flat again. [#A]
  • CQ-1 (RAN vs enterprise/private networks): Nokia sits meaningfully on both sides — Mobile Infrastructure (radio + core) is carrier-facing; Network Infrastructure (optical, IP, fixed) has growing hyperscaler/enterprise exposure. The AI/Cloud shift is moving the center of gravity toward the latter. [#J]
  • CQ-2 (cycle exposure): Nokia's revenue base is highly carrier-capex-correlated, as 2023 demonstrated. The IP/Optical/AI sub-segment provides a partial hedge against pure RAN cyclicality, but Nokia Technologies (IP licensing) at ~€1.4bn is the only genuinely through-cycle revenue stream. [#J]
  • ❌ Ericsson organic comparison: Ericsson organic sales grew 2% in FY2025 but had been shrinking previously; Nokia's organic revenue is recovering from a deeper trough with a slower-rebuilding mobile segment. Ericsson's North America exposure proved more resilient through the cycle. [#A]
  • Private wireless tailwind (partial offset): Private Wireless RAN revenues jumped 40% in 2024 globally, with Nokia among the top three players. This is a small but growing contributor, partially offsetting RAN carrier cyclicality. [#I]
  • Sceptical note: FY2025 total revenue of ~€19.9bn is still ~80% of the 2022 peak. Even with AI/Cloud growth at 49% YoY from a small base, Nokia needs years of above-market growth to recover its former revenue scale. The carrier RAN cycle remains the swing factor. [#J]

§3 — Margin Profile & Evolution

  • Verdict — material improvement underway, but structurally below Ericsson: Nokia's comparable operating margin troughed at 10.7% in 2023 and has recovered toward the mid-teens in 2024; Q4 2024 hit 19.1%, though this was aided by Nokia Technologies seasonality. FY2025 comparable operating margin was approximately 10% on a reported basis (€2.0bn / €19.9bn). [#J]
  • 2022 comparable operating margin: 15.5%. This was the high watermark post-Alcatel integration. Management had guided to 11.5–14.0% for 2023, implying expected deceleration before the actual shock. [#I]
  • 2023 compression: Comparable operating margin 10.7%. Revenue deleverage hit hard — Nokia's cost base has significant fixed components (R&D, integration/delivery). The 14,000-person headcount reduction was a direct response. [#A]
  • 2024 recovery: Full-year comparable operating profit €2.6bn; full-year net sales ~€19.5–20bn, implying comparable operating margin ~13%. Q4 2024 comparable margin 19.1% (benefiting from Nokia Technologies contribution and better mix). [#A]
  • 2025 reported: Comparable operating profit ~€2.0bn vs net sales €19.9bn = ~10% comparable operating margin. Note: this appears lower than 2024 because Nokia Technologies licensing revenue declined YoY (fewer one-off catch-up payments); the underlying run-rate improved. [#A]
  • Gross margin trend: Q1 2026 comparable gross margin 45.5% (+320bps YoY). Gross margin improvement is structural, driven by software mix, AI/Cloud product mix, and cost management. This is the most encouraging leading indicator. [#J]
  • CQ-4 (margin trajectory N3Y): Management guidance for FY2026 comparable operating profit: €2.0–2.5bn, with Nokia tracking "somewhat above mid-point" after Q1. On €19.9bn revenue, achieving the mid-point (€2.25bn) = ~11.3% margin; top-end (€2.5bn) = ~12.5%. The bar for reaching Ericsson's 18%+ remains distant. [#J]
  • ❌ Ericsson margin gap: Ericsson's adjusted EBITA margin reached 18.1% in FY2025 vs Nokia's comparable ~10% on reported basis. This ~800bps structural gap reflects Ericsson's higher software/services concentration in its managed services arm (Ericsson's cloud software and services is a larger % of revenue). Peer median for sector is approximately 14–15%. Nokia is below. [#J]
  • Network Infrastructure margin (new segment): Operating profit €770m on €7.6bn revenue = 10.1% margin. This segment is growing fastest (AI/Cloud tailwind) but has thinner margins than the Mobile Infrastructure segment. [#A]
  • Mobile Infrastructure margin (new segment): Operating profit €1,525m on €11.4bn revenue = 13.4% margin. This includes the IP licensing stream (~€1.4bn run-rate) without which the underlying hardware/software margin would be materially lower. [#A]
  • Operating leverage: The cost reduction programme (14,000 headcount from Oct 2023, implying ~€400–500m annualised savings) is flowing through. SG&A as a percentage of revenue has been declining. R&D spend maintained at ~20% of sales, reflecting Nokia's technology investment intensity. [#I]
  • Sceptical note: Nokia's FY2025 comparable margin of ~10% is not a new high — it approximates the 2023 trough comparable margin. The trajectory depends critically on whether AI/Cloud optical orders generate durable margin, or whether the surge is volume-driven at compressed margins reflecting competition from Ciena, Infinera, and Huawei. [#J]

§4 — EPS Trajectory & Delivery

  • Verdict — volatile EPS series driven by impairment charges and licensing timing; underlying earnings improving but not yet stable: The reported EPS track record includes significant non-recurring charges that make year-on-year comparison misleading without comparable-basis adjustment. On a comparable basis, the trend is clearly improving into 2025–2026. [#J]
  • 2022: Reported EPS approximately €0.29 (strong year, 5G revenue peak). [#I]
  • 2023: Reported annual EPS ~$0.13 (−84% vs 2022 on USD-denominated basis per MacroTrends). Large impairment charges and restructuring costs depressed reported figure significantly. [#A]
  • 2024: Reported annual EPS ~$0.25 (+92% YoY). Recovery was driven by better underlying operations and lower restructuring charges. [#A]
  • 2025: Reported annual EPS ~$0.14 (−45% YoY). This apparent decline was partly due to fewer one-off licensing catch-up payments and some FX effects. Comparable EPS was more stable. [#A]
  • Q1 2026 beat: Comparable diluted EPS €0.05 vs consensus forecast ~€0.034, a 31–67% beat depending on source. Reported diluted EPS €0.02 vs −€0.01 a year prior (improvement from loss to positive). [#J]
  • Beat/miss pattern (limited public data): Q4 2024 was characterised as "beat estimates on healthy top-line growth." Q1 2026 was a clear beat (+31% on EPS). Q4 2023 beat a low bar as Nokia pre-managed expectations sharply downward after the October 2023 profit warning. The pattern suggests Nokia tends to manage guidance conservatively, creating a beat-on-delivery dynamic. [#J]
  • Consensus direction 2026–2027: Consensus projects EPS growth of ~9.5% in 2026 and ~20.5% in 2027, reflecting expectations of operating leverage from AI/Cloud revenue scaling and cost programme payoff. [#A]
  • EPS quality concern: Nokia Technologies licensing income (~€1.4bn run-rate) is included in comparable EPS but has shown lumpiness (one-off catch-up payments in 2024 inflated that year, then partially reversed in 2025). This is a recurring but lumpy earnings component that makes clean comparisons difficult. [#J]
  • Sceptical note: The FY2025 reported EPS drop of ~45% YoY is striking and would alarm a naive screener. The comparable-basis explanation (fewer catch-up payments, restructuring charges) is legitimate but underscores that Nokia's EPS is not a clean, predictable series — it requires adjustment to interpret. [#J]

§5 — Guidance & Forecast Changes

  • Verdict — management guidance has been conservative in recent quarters, creating upside-on-delivery; but prior-cycle guidance was materially wrong in 2023: Nokia's guidance track record is mixed — accurate in upturns, significantly missed in the 2023 downturn. Current guidance regime appears more conservative and thus more reliable as a floor. [#J]
  • 2023 guidance failure: Nokia began 2023 guiding comparable operating margin 11.5–14.0%. By October 2023 they announced a major restructuring (14,000 jobs) and cut guidance sharply. This was a multi-step downward revision, not a single cut, indicating that management did not have timely visibility into the severity of the carrier capex pullback. [#J]
  • 2024 guidance delivery: Guidance of €2.3–2.9bn comparable operating profit; delivered €2.6bn, in-line at mid-point. Q4 beat on operating margin (+380bps). Guidance management improved significantly. [#A]
  • 2025 guidance delivery: Guided comparable operating profit €1.9–2.4bn; delivered ~€2.0bn. Technically in-range but at the low end, reflecting licensing revenue headwind. Free cash flow guided 50–80% conversion; delivered 72%. [#A]
  • 2026 initial guidance (Jan 2026): Comparable operating profit €2.0–2.5bn; Network Infrastructure growth 6–8%. [#I]
  • 2026 guidance upgrade (Q1 2026, April 23): Network Infrastructure growth raised to 12–14% (from 6–8%); Optical/IP combined raised to 18–20% (from 10–12%). Nokia tracking "somewhat above mid-point" of €2.0–2.5bn operating profit range after just one quarter. Addressable AI/Cloud market CAGR estimate raised to 27% (from 16%). [#J]
  • ⚡ RARE — guidance raise magnitude: Raising Network Infrastructure growth guidance from 6–8% to 12–14% after just one quarter is an unusually large single-quarter upward revision. This is either genuine demand acceleration or aggressive front-loading of orders (12–18 month lead-time in optical). Both interpretations warrant attention. [#J]
  • Analyst estimate direction: Post-Q1 2026, consensus EPS estimates revised upward. Bank of America upgraded to Buy at $12.40. Average 12-month consensus target ~$8.83 from 16 analysts (Moderate Buy). Revenue estimates for 2026 revised down 4.7% in the three months preceding Q1 (before the beat), but now revising up post-results. [#A]
  • Consensus EPS 2026–2027: ~9.5% and ~20.5% growth respectively per consensus. This implies the street expects operating leverage to materialise in 2027 as AI/Cloud revenues scale. [#I]
  • Sceptical note: The €2.0–2.5bn guidance range is wide (~25% spread). Nokia's track record of maintaining precise guidance through a cycle is poor. The guidance raise after one quarter of strong AI/Cloud orders could prove premature if hyperscaler capex shows any sign of deceleration in H2 2026. [#J]

§7 — Sector & Value Chain Context

  • Verdict — RAN market stable but no growth catalyst in sight; AI/Cloud optical is the genuine new demand signal; Nokia positioned better than consensus assumed 18 months ago: The carrier capex cycle is stabilising but not re-accelerating; the real upside is optical/IP infrastructure for AI data centres, which was not in Nokia's core thesis pre-2024. [#J]
  • RAN market size and status: The global RAN market stabilised at approximately $35bn in 2025 after significant contraction 2023–2024. Dell'Oro and Omdia both report stable but low-growth RAN market in 2026, with no near-term acceleration catalyst beyond India and emerging markets. [#I]
  • Carrier capex cycle (CQ-2): European and North American operators are in a post-5G-rollout digestion phase. Operators are not cutting capex further but neither are they investing aggressively. The incremental carrier capex uplift will require the arrival of 5.5G/6G or material AI-on-device adoption — neither near-term. [#J]
  • AI data centre optical demand (CQ-1): Hyperscaler capex expectations have been revised from $540bn to over $700bn for 2026 globally. Nokia's Optical Networks division is capturing a portion of the interconnect infrastructure spend within and between data centres. This is qualitatively different from RAN — shorter sales cycles, non-carrier customer, and growing. [#J]
  • ⚡ RARE — hyperscaler capex revision magnitude: The upward revision of hyperscaler global capex from $540bn to $700bn+ for 2026 is a material positive demand signal for optical interconnect suppliers. Nokia's €1bn single-quarter AI/Cloud order intake (Q1 2026) with 12–18 month lead times suggests this is real near-term demand, not speculative pipeline. [#J]
  • Huawei competitive context (CQ-5): Huawei remains #1 globally in RAN, dominant in China. Outside China, Huawei's share is constrained by Western security concerns. Nokia and Ericsson both benefit from Huawei exclusion in North America, UK, Australia, and most of Western Europe. Nokia gained ex-China RAN share in 2025. [#I]
  • Samsung competitive context: Samsung has invested heavily in O-RAN and US carrier relationships (AT&T, Verizon, DISH). Nokia competes more directly with Samsung in open-RAN deployments and private wireless. Samsung is not a material optical competitor. [#I]
  • Private wireless (B2B growth driver): Private Wireless RAN revenues grew 40% in 2024 globally; Nokia among top three players. This is a recurring-adjacent growth stream as enterprises build private LTE/5G networks. Nokia's NDAC (Nokia Digital Automation Cloud) and MX Industrial Edge are the platform plays here. [#I]
  • Sceptical note: The AI/Cloud optical narrative is creating a PE re-rating story for Nokia. However, optical networking is a commoditising market with strong competition from Ciena, Infinera (now part of II-VI/Coherent), and potentially Huawei. Nokia must demonstrate optical margin sustainability — not just revenue growth — to justify a structurally higher multiple. [#J]

§8 — Quality of Earnings & Accounting

  • Verdict — earnings quality has several identifiable distortions that require strip-out to understand the run-rate; underlying quality improving but not clean: Nokia Technologies licensing timing, restructuring charges, and segment restructuring comparability issues all create noise in the P&L. Cash conversion improving significantly provides offsetting quality signal. [#J]
  • Nokia Technologies / IP licensing distortion: ~€1.4bn annual licensing run-rate included in comparable operating profit. In 2024, one-off catch-up payments inflated this above run-rate; in 2025 the absence of those payments depressed comparable operating profit vs 2024. This is recurring but lumpy — not volatile operationally but variable in timing. [#A]
  • Restructuring charges: The October 2023 restructuring (14,000 headcount reduction) generated significant reported-vs-comparable divergence through 2023–2024. As restructuring charges normalise, reported and comparable margins converge. In Q4 2024, reported operating margin was 8.1% vs comparable 19.1% — a 1,100bps gap, still unusually large, driven by amortisation of acquired intangibles. [#A]
  • Revenue recognition (long-term contracts): Nokia's equipment and software revenue is recognised under percentage-of-completion or on delivery depending on contract type. Long-term network deployment contracts (e.g. for telecom operators) involve estimates of contract progress. No significant accounting concerns have been flagged in recent audit reports (20-F 2024 available but not reviewed in detail for this report). [#I]
  • Cash conversion improvement: FY2023 FCF ~€0.9bn (20–50% conversion); FY2024 FCF ~€2.3bn (significant improvement); FY2025 FCF €1.5bn (72% conversion). The 2024 FCF benefited from a partial prepayment of a licensing agreement in Q4 2023 (~€1.7bn cash in Q4 2023 alone). Normalised 2024–2025 FCF conversion of 50–80% is a credible range. [#A]
  • Working capital normalisation: Post-2023 inventory digestion, Nokia's working capital has normalised. H1 2024 saw continued NWC improvement; by FY2025 the position was stable. This underpins the improved FCF. [#A]
  • Net cash position: €3.8bn net cash as of Q1 2026. Balance sheet quality is not a concern. Nokia has been returning capital via dividends (reinstated) and buybacks. [#I]
  • Segment recast (Jan 2026): The move from four old segments to two new ones on 1 January 2026 means historical comparisons before 2024 require bridging from the old reporting structure. Nokia has published recast 2024–2025 data under the new structure, but 2022–2023 under old structure only. This is standard but creates analytical friction for L3Y trend analysis. [#A]
  • ❌ Reported vs comparable gap: The persistent gap between reported and comparable operating margins (typically 5–10 percentage points in recent years) is above the sector norm. Ericsson's equivalent gap is narrower. Nokia's high intangible amortisation from the Alcatel-Lucent acquisition (2016) continues to inflate the gap. This acquisition intangible drag has been running for nearly a decade and is now mostly amortised — a modest positive going forward. [#J]
  • Sceptical note: The combination of Nokia Technologies timing, large restructuring charges, and Alcatel-Lucent intangible amortisation has created a culture of "comparable vs reported" earnings management. This is not manipulation — the adjustments are disclosed and consistent — but it does mean Nokia's earnings quality requires more analytical effort to assess than a simpler peer like a pure-play optical vendor. [#J]

§9 — Sceptical Synthesis

  • Verdict — the bear case is: Nokia's recovery is built on a temporary AI/Cloud optical wave, structural mobile margins remain impaired, and the margin gap vs Ericsson reflects deep organisational differences that cannot be bridged by hardware volume growth alone: This is a recoverable position but not yet a proven one. [#J]
  • CQ-2 revisited — Nokia is still fundamentally a carrier capex play: Despite the AI/Cloud narrative, ~90% of Nokia's revenue remains tied to telecom operator or AI infrastructure capital expenditure. Both are lumpy. If hyperscaler capex pauses in H2 2026–2027 (DeepSeek-type efficiency shock scenario), Nokia's AI/Cloud growth story stalls with limited carrier RAN offset. [#J]
  • CQ-4 revisited — the margin N3Y path is shallow: Nokia's own guidance implies €2.0–2.5bn comparable operating profit on revenues growing to perhaps €21–23bn by 2027. That implies comparable operating margins of 9–11%, still well below Ericsson's 18%. For Nokia to reach even 14% (sector median) would require a combination of: (a) software/services mix shift, (b) full Nokia Technologies run-rate expansion to €1.5bn, (c) continued AI/Cloud volume growth. All three are plausible; none is certain. [#J]
  • Mobile Networks is structurally weaker than peers: Mobile Networks (Nokia's RAN segment, now part of Mobile Infrastructure) was flat FY2025 and −21% in FY2024. Ericsson organic mobile revenues grew 2% in 2025. Nokia has conceded some RAN market share in recent years and its competitive position vs Huawei (ex-China bans) and Samsung is under pressure in specific geographies. [#J]
  • AI/Cloud optical margin sustainability: Nokia's 49% AI/Cloud revenue growth and €1bn single-quarter order intake is compelling, but optical networking is not a high-margin stable business historically. Ciena's gross margins run ~46–48%; Nokia's are converging to similar levels. The question is whether optical pricing holds as competitors respond to the same hyperscaler capex wave. [#J]
  • IP licensing cliff risk: Nokia targets growing its licensing run-rate from €1.4bn to €1.5bn. However, licensing agreements are periodically renegotiated, and patent portfolios erode over time as standards evolve. A material licensing downgrade would reduce comparable operating profit by €100–200m directly. [#J]
  • ❌ FCF conversion still not best-in-class: Despite improvement, 72% FCF conversion in 2025 remains below Ericsson's 11.3% FCF/sales. Nokia's working capital intensity and capex needs (R&D ~20% of sales) leave less free cash flow per unit of operating profit. [#A]
  • Execution risk on restructuring benefits: The 14,000-person headcount reduction announced October 2023 aimed to deliver ~€400–500m annualised savings by 2026. The cost savings are flowing through (gross margin +320bps YoY at Q1 2026) but Nokia has a track record of offset from reinvestment in growth areas (R&D, enterprise go-to-market) that can absorb structural savings. [#J]
  • Verdict on sustainability: Nokia's earnings improvement trajectory is real and multi-metric-confirmed. The durability depends on: (i) AI/Cloud optical demand persisting through 2027, (ii) Mobile Networks not declining further, and (iii) Nokia Technologies licensing sustaining at €1.4–1.5bn. None of these is a fundamental structural certainty — they are near-term demand and contract factors. [#J]

AGGREGATE WEAK SIGNALS / DOWNSIDE (❌)

  • ❌ Margin gap vs Ericsson structural, ~800bps on adjusted EBIT basis (FY2025): Not explained by cycle — reflects different software/managed services mix. [§3, §6]
  • ❌ Mobile Networks revenue flat FY2025, −21% FY2024: Nokia's core RAN segment is a sustained drag vs peers; Ericsson grew organic mobile 2% in 2025. [§2, §6]
