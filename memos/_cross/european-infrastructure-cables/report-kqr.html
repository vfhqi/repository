Section 2 – Order Backlog Quality & Pricing Power 17 findings

Section summary
  1. Unprecedented high-voltage order books secure multi-year margin expansion across the oligopoly, while sophisticated raw material pass-throughs and emerging hyperscaler fibre contracts offer distinct pricing leverage.

Unprecedented backlog visibility and margin expansion

1.
Best-in-class profitability: Prysmian reached a record 20.1% margin in its Transmission segment during the first quarter of 2026, driven by meticulous project execution and a massive 16.8 billion euro order backlog heavily weighted toward lucrative subsea interconnections
2.
Accelerating margin trajectory: Morgan Stanley estimates that Prysmian's average backlog execution price will surge by roughly 80% to 1.45 million euros per kilometre by 2027, underpinning structural high-voltage margin improvements
3.
Record capacity reservations: NKT reported a record-high transmission order backlog of 13.5 billion euros, fortified by massive awards like the Eastern Green Link 3, securing earnings visibility deep into the decade
4.
Long-term multiple potential: DNB Carnegie projects that NKT's high-voltage revenue capacity will expand significantly as new lines ramp up, potentially driving group EBITDA margins toward an ambitious 25% by 2030
5.
Selective project acquisition: Nexans maintains an adjusted transmission backlog of 7.9 billion euros, deliberately narrowing its pipeline to prioritise high-margin, complex technological projects over volume
6.
Overhanging project risk: Jefferies cautions that while Nexans' grid execution remains strong, the temporary suspension of the high-margin Great Sea Interconnector project due to geopolitical risks creates a concerning capacity vacuum that could pressure near-term profitability

Raw material pass-through and tariff mechanics

7.
Structural inflation protection: The major European cable manufacturers neutralise the volatility of core metals like copper and aluminium through rigorous indexation formulas and back-to-back hedging strategies embedded in their long-term customer contracts
8.
Tariff-induced drag: Prysmian's Power Grid margins faced temporary compression dropping to 12.4% due to the delayed pass-through of surging Midwest aluminium premiums stemming from United States import tariffs
9.
Localised manufacturing advantage: Deutsche Bank Research notes that Prysmian's highly localised United States production footprint ultimately positions the company to capture market share from importers who are heavily penalized by the 50% tariff on imported copper and aluminium cables
10.
Vertical integration premium: Nexans benefits from unique vertical integration through its in-house metallurgy business, granting it direct access to copper cathodes from global mines and insulating it from acute raw material shortages
11.
Short-term spot exposure: While high-voltage projects are fully hedged, the low-voltage distribution market operates on spot pricing where rapid fluctuations in copper costs can temporarily pressure margins before catalogue prices are fully adjusted

Digital solutions and hyperscaler fibre edge

12.
Surging optical demand: Prysmian's Digital Solutions division is experiencing explosive growth driven by data centre expansion, propelling its adjusted EBITDA margin to 20.6% in the first quarter of 2026
13.
Transformative hyperscaler agreements: Deutsche Bank Research reports that Prysmian is finalizing multi-year purchase commitments with major United States hyperscalers, securing a potential 5 billion euro revenue stream and underwriting a massive 40-50% expansion of its optical fibre capacity
14.
Exploiting supply bottlenecks: Management at Prysmian confirmed they are actively renegotiating existing optical fibre contracts at higher prevailing market rates, capitalizing on chronic industry-wide fibre shortages to drive immediate pricing power enhancements
15.
Divergent segment trajectories: While high-voltage power remains the structural anchor, the sudden artificial intelligence-driven optical fibre boom provides Prysmian with a distinct, high-margin growth vector that pure-play power competitors like NKT cannot replicate
16.
Other side of the story: If the anticipated hyperscaler data centre build-out faces regulatory or power-constraint delays, Prysmian's aggressive billion-euro optical fibre capacity expansion could rapidly transform from a highly profitable pricing lever into a stranded asset, severely diluting the group's overall return on invested capital

Section 3 – Execution Capability & Capacity Expansion 17 findings

Section summary
  1. While the entire oligopoly is deploying record capital to relieve chronic high-voltage supply constraints, Prysmian commands a distinct execution premium through flawless asset delivery, leaving NKT to absorb near-term ramp-up costs and Nexans to navigate severe geopolitical project bottlenecks.

Prysmian's flawless execution and aggressive capacity scale-up

1.
Unrivalled capacity rollout: Prysmian has solidified its industry leadership by successfully executing a massive 850 million euro investment program since 2018, culminating in the on-time delivery of critical manufacturing and installation assets that directly accelerate near-term earnings
2.
Vertical manufacturing advantage: The company recently inaugurated a 185-metre vertical continuous vulcanisation tower at its Pikkala plant in Finland, a 200 million euro brownfield expansion that effectively doubles its production capacity for 525kV high-voltage direct current submarine cables to approximately one kilometre per day
3.
Superior installation fleet: Securing a profound competitive moat in project delivery, Prysmian launched the Monna Lisa cable-laying vessel in early 2025, featuring a 3,000-metre deep-water capability and unprecedented 17,000-tonne combined carousel capacity, ensuring the company does not have to rely on constrained third-party installation contractors
4.
Accelerating fleet expansion: Management confirmed plans to expand the proprietary installation fleet to eight vessels by 2028, with the Alessandro Volta and Marco Polo ships scheduled to come online shortly, granting Prysmian unmatched flexibility and execution security in complex turnkey offshore projects
5.
Consistent execution premium: Brokerage research from Morgan Stanley highlights that Prysmian's meticulous delivery of these assets, combined with a history of avoiding major project delays since the Western Link issues, warrants a clear execution premium over peers who are currently navigating riskier greenfield expansions

NKT's massive investment pipeline and short-term margin drags

6.
Aggressive greenfield commitments: NKT is currently executing a 2 billion euro capital expenditure pipeline stretching to 2028, highlighted by a massive 1.3 billion euro investment in its Karlskrona facility in Sweden to construct the world's largest offshore high-voltage cable factory
7.
Physical milestone achievements: The company successfully topped out its third extrusion tower in Karlskrona at 200 metres in late 2024 and has already launched the hull of its new cable-laying vessel, the Eleonora, keeping the physical assets on track for commercial operation in 2027

NKT's new cable-laying vessel, the Eleonora, following its recent hull launch.

8.
Margin dilution headwinds: DNB Carnegie notes that NKT is absorbing significant near-term margin pressure, with ramp-up costs and accelerated hiring for the new Karlskrona capacity expected to drag operational EBITDA margins down by 100 to 150 basis points throughout 2025 and 2026
9.
Capital expenditure overruns: The ambitious scale of the Karlskrona expansion has exposed the company to inflationary pressures, with management acknowledging a 300 million euro cost overrun compared to initial estimates, highlighting the inherent risks of executing massive infrastructure upgrades during a period of tight labour and supply chain constraints
10.
Asymmetric future profitability: Despite near-term transition pains, Jefferies analysts project that Karlskrona's extreme concentration and logistical advantages will ultimately yield the sector's highest high-voltage margins by 2028, creating a highly lucrative earnings inflection once the assets are fully scaled

Nexans' project bottlenecks and geopolitical execution risks

11.
Geopolitical project paralysis: Nexans' near-term execution visibility has been severely compromised by the suspension of the 1.43 billion euro Great Sea Interconnector project, a highly lucrative contract linking Greece and Cyprus that has stalled due to diplomatic tensions with Turkey and ongoing funding disputes
12.
Forced capacity underutilisation: Following the cessation of project down-payments, Nexans has been forced to reschedule its manufacturing activities, leaving a critical capacity vacuum in its high-voltage lines that management must rapidly backfill to protect 2026 and 2027 earnings trajectories
13.
Sub-optimal mitigation strategies: Bernstein cautions that Nexans' strategy to fill this idle capacity with short-term repair orders and spot contracts severely weakens its commercial bargaining power, forcing it into a price-taker dynamic that will likely dilute overall segment profitability compared to the original baseline
14.
Robust underlying footprint: Beyond the Great Sea Interconnector crisis, the company has successfully completed the expansion of its Halden submarine plant in Norway and is preparing to launch its third cable-laying vessel, the Nexans Electra, in mid-2026 to support its structural backlog
15.
Divergent capital allocation: While peers aggressively expand high-voltage capacity, Nexans has pivoted its capital deployment toward the United States low-voltage market, notably acquiring Republic Wire for roughly 520 million euros to establish a strategic platform in less complex, rapid-cycle electrification products
16.
Other side of the story: While Prysmian is universally praised for its on-time delivery of the Pikkala tower and Monna Lisa vessel, a sudden normalisation in hyperscaler optical fibre demand or unexpected delays in United States grid upgrades could leave the company with expensive, underutilised capacity, suddenly transforming its execution premium into an overhang of fixed-cost absorption

Section 4 – Thematic Tailwinds & Market Structure 17 findings

Section summary
  1. The structural deficit in high-voltage transmission capacity guarantees multi-year visibility for the European cable oligopoly, though permitting delays and emerging Asian competitors threaten to cap terminal market share.

Macro drivers and the 765kV super-cycle

1.
Surging grid investment: The European Commission estimates that 584 billion euros of grid investment will be required by 2030 to accommodate renewable energy integration and expanding electrification demands
2.
Data centre catalyst: Power consumption from artificial intelligence data centres is projected to double by 2030 to 945 terawatt-hours, forcing urgent upgrades to transmission networks
3.
Transmission efficiency imperative: Jefferies research highlights that the transition to 765kV extra-high-voltage lines represents a critical efficiency leap, as a single 765kV circuit carries four to six times the capacity of a standard 345kV line
4.
Cost and footprint advantages: Bernstein analysis demonstrates that 765kV transmission infrastructure is highly attractive to developers because it costs 50 to 60 percent less per mile and uses significantly less land right-of-way compared to equivalent lower-voltage bundles
5.
Offshore wind acceleration: European governments have reaffirmed their commitment to achieving 300 gigawatts of North Sea offshore wind capacity by 2050, directly underwriting long-term subsea cable demand
6.
Market undersupply: Deutsche Bank Research estimates that the European market for high-voltage transmission cables is currently 30 percent undersupplied, a dynamic that inherently protects manufacturer pricing power through the end of the decade

Oligopoly dominance and shifting market share

7.
Entrenched European oligopoly: The high-voltage subsea market operates as a strict oligopoly dominated by Prysmian, Nexans, and NKT, shielded by immense technological, capital, and vessel-capacity barriers to entry
8.
Current capacity share: Morgan Stanley estimates that Prysmian leads the global high-voltage subsea capacity market with a 38 percent share, followed by NKT at 23 percent and Nexans at 15 percent
9.
Regional supply chain preferences: Geopolitical tensions and the United States Inflation Reduction Act are driving a profound preference for domestic manufacturing, actively restricting Chinese cable suppliers like ZTT and Hengtong to their home markets
10.
Localised footprint advantage: Prysmian benefits disproportionately from this regionalisation trend, leveraging its established United States manufacturing footprint to capture market share from importers facing steep tariffs and logistical hurdles
11.
Emerging Asian challengers: Morgan Stanley warns that Asian manufacturers such as LS Cable and Sumitomo are aggressively expanding into Western markets, with LS Cable announcing a 923 million pound factory in the United Kingdom to capture European demand

Regulatory bottlenecks and edge risks

12.
Chronic permitting delays: Across major markets, permitting bottlenecks and lengthy grid interconnection queues are severely delaying renewable project timelines, with wait times for new grid connections in primary United States data centre markets exceeding four years
13.
Grid connection gridlock: Approximately 375 gigawatts of green energy capacity and storage projects worth 100 billion euros are currently stalled in European distribution grid connection queues
14.
Geopolitical project suspensions: Nexans has experienced firsthand how diplomatic tensions can paralyze infrastructure development, as evidenced by the suspension of the Great Sea Interconnector project which has temporarily disrupted its transmission capacity utilisation
15.
Supply chain constraints: The industry faces acute shortages of critical high-voltage direct current components, including converter stations and transformers, where lead times have extended drastically due to limited global manufacturing capacity
16.
Other side of the story: While the consensus views the current supply deficit as a permanent feature of the 2020s, the aggressive capacity expansions announced by both the European incumbents and ambitious Asian entrants like LS Cable could rapidly normalise the market by 2028, eroding the scarcity premium currently baked into manufacturer margins

Section 5 – Technology, Disruption & Edge Exposures 13 findings

Section summary
  1. The rapid transition to 800 VDC data centre architectures and the commercialisation of solid-state transformers threaten traditional copper cable volumes, while Nexans' pioneering push into high-temperature superconducting cables creates a formidable, non-consensus moat for ultra-dense power transmission.

The 800 VDC architecture shift

1.
Generational paradigm shift: Morgan Stanley research highlights that the artificial intelligence-driven transition to 800 VDC data centre architectures significantly reduces conversion stages and lowers overall distribution losses compared to traditional alternating current setups
2.
Copper volume risk: Rothschild & Co Redburn warns that operating at these higher voltages directly cuts copper cabling requirements, presenting a structural volume headwind for traditional low-voltage building wire suppliers as hyperscalers optimise their infrastructure
3.
Value over volume: Bernstein notes that Nexans is actively navigating this disruption through its metallurgy business, pivoting toward producing specialised busbars and advanced connection systems that easily replace the displaced traditional copper cable volumes in modern server racks
4.
Ecosystem readiness barrier: Despite the clear efficiency gains, Rothschild & Co Redburn cautions that the transition to pure 800 VDC will be delayed by a lack of operational standards, safety certifications, and fault containment maturity, likely resulting in hybrid deployments through 2027

Solid-State Transformers (SSTs) redefining grid constraints

5.
Miniaturised power conversion: Infineon Technologies reports that solid-state transformers, powered by silicon carbide semiconductors, are up to 14 times smaller and 40 times lighter than conventional iron-core transformers, enabling rapid deployment in space-constrained urban grids
6.
Bypassing legacy equipment: Bernstein analysis demonstrates that SSTs eliminate entire layers of legacy switchgear and rectifiers, compounding space savings and allowing data centres to drastically improve system-level power efficiency

7.
Cost premium friction: An industry expert serving as Director of Procurement at Rosendin Electric indicates that while SSTs offer superior power control, they currently cost three to five times more than traditional transformers, restricting their near-term adoption to highly specialised or owner-mandated projects

Superconducting cables as the ultimate edge exposure

8.
Ultra-dense transmission: Nexans investor materials reveal that a single high-temperature superconducting cable can carry up to 3.2 gigawatts of power in a 17-centimetre diameter, reducing the required spatial footprint in data halls by 24 times compared to conventional solutions
9.
Nexans' strategic moat: The company has officially inaugurated Stella Nova, a dedicated superconductivity centre in Germany, showcasing a world-first demonstration of low-voltage superconducting cables explicitly designed for hyperscale data centres
10.
Niche hyperscaler pricing: Bernstein points out that while superconducting technology is highly disruptive, it remains five to ten times more expensive than conventional cables, limiting its immediate total addressable market to elite hyperscalers who are entirely constrained by urban grid bottlenecks
11.
Offshore disruption potential: Nexans recently formed the SupraMarine consortium with Air Liquide to adapt superconducting technology for offshore wind farms, aiming to replace massive direct-current conversion platforms and potentially disrupt the traditional high-voltage subsea cable oligopoly
12.
Other side of the story: While the market focuses on the threat of 800 VDC and SSTs reducing overall copper cable demand, the sheer scale of the required gigawatt-class data centre capacity expansions may ultimately overwhelm any component-level volume savings, ensuring that all three major European cable manufacturers experience absolute revenue growth despite shrinking per-megawatt copper intensity
