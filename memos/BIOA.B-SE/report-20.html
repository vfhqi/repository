2. KEY FINDINGS Top-3 most-diagnostic questions crystallised — these three questions, if answered candidly, discriminate bull/bear/false-friend with highest efficiency across the investment case

  • DIAGNOSTIC #1 (Revenue quality): "What is the current payor mix for Leqembi in the US, and what is Eisai's internal forecast for the inflection point at which commercial coverage becomes the dominant reimbursement channel?" — the answer to this single question resolves whether BioArctic's royalty stream is structurally predictable or still dependent on US Medicare/Medicaid access negotiations outside BIOA's control
  • DIAGNOSTIC #2 (Royalty structure opacity): "Can you walk us through the full royalty-rate schedule for Leqembi — base rate, tiering thresholds, territory exclusions, and any milestone-triggered step-downs?" — BioArctic's EPS trajectory is almost entirely a function of a royalty contract the company did not originate; market consensus uses a range because the precise schedule is not fully public, making this the single highest-uncertainty financial input
  • DIAGNOSTIC #3 (Pipeline optionality vs dependency): "If Leqembi peak-sales consensus of ~$3-5bn is correct but materialises 3-5 years later than the base case, what is the explicit bridge plan — do you operate cash-flow-negative, raise equity, or reduce pipeline spend?" — this forces management to articulate the downside liquidity plan, which they have a structural incentive NOT to volunteer
  • Royalty concentration risk is the load-bearing uncertainty — BIOA is a single-asset royalty story dressed as a pipeline company; questions that probe anything other than royalty mechanics, Eisai relationship depth, and BIOA's own pipeline advancement pace are lower-diagnostic-value

  • Eisai relationship is structurally asymmetric — Eisai holds commercial control of Leqembi globally; BIOA receives royalties but has no direct lever over pricing, market access, sales force sizing, or payor negotiations; any management framing that implies joint control should be treated as a red flag
  • Management incentive alignment is imperfect — BioArctic's founding-family governance means long-term alignment is plausible, but compensation structures (equity-linked vs royalty-linked) need direct probing; animal-CEO archetype here is scientist-founder, not commercial operator

3. THEME §1 — Revenue Drivers + Visibility

J-front verdict: Revenue-side uncertainty is HIGH and asymmetrically downside-skewed; royalty visibility is gated by Eisai commercial execution + US payor dynamics, not by BIOA's own actions

Backlog visibility: BioArctic has no traditional backlog; "revenue" is royalties received on Leqembi net sales booked by Eisai/Biogen; visibility = Eisai's own revenue guidance, which BIOA management can reference but not independently verify or influence

Q1.1 — Royalty Rate Schedule

  • Question: "Can you disclose the full royalty-rate schedule on Leqembi — base rate, territory coverage, sales-volume tiering, and any clauses that could reduce the rate post-approval?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely confirm a tiered royalty in the mid-single-digit percentage range on net sales, reference territory exclusions for Japan (where BAN2401 originated under a separate Eisai licence), and decline to give exact tier breakpoints citing confidentiality
  • Good answer signature: Specific rate range given, tier triggers quantified, explicit confirmation that no rate-step-down clause exists post-approval; management references the licensing agreement section in the annual report
  • Bad answer signature: Vague reference to "competitive royalty rates", refusal to narrow the range even directionally, or a deflection to "Eisai is better placed to comment" — the latter signals BIOA has less visibility into their own royalty mechanics than the investment case requires
  • Follow-up probe: "Your annual report discloses a royalty income line — can you reconcile that figure back to implied Leqembi net sales and confirm the implied royalty rate is consistent with the disclosed range?"
  • Diagnostic note: Bull case requires royalty rate ≥ mid-single digits with no step-down; bear case is triggered by a rate below market expectations or a volume-tiered structure that makes peak-sales royalties lower than extrapolated; false-friend is a rate that appears adequate but excludes key territories (China, EU) where Leqembi approval is pending

Q1.2 — Eisai Commercial Execution Signal

  • Question: "What is the current trajectory of Leqembi patient starts per month in the US, and how does Eisai's internal commercial build compare to the timeline you assumed when modelling your royalty income?"
  • Expected answer: Management will reference publicly available Eisai earnings call data on patient starts; will likely acknowledge ramp is slower than initial projections but frame as "consistent with typical rare/complex disease launch curves"
  • Good answer signature: Specific patient-start numbers cited (consistent with Eisai public disclosures), management demonstrates they actively track Eisai's commercial metrics, and they provide a coherent explanation for the ramp pace vs expectations
  • Bad answer signature: Management's numbers don't reconcile with Eisai's own public disclosures, or management acknowledges they rely on Eisai's reporting without any independent verification mechanism
  • Follow-up probe: "At the current patient-start trajectory, in which quarter do you expect Leqembi US royalty run-rate to become the dominant source of operating income for BioArctic?"
  • Diagnostic note: Cross-ref Q19 ACH matrix — "Leqembi commercial ramp failure" is the highest-plausibility bear-mode scenario; a management team that cannot narrate a specific inflection-point timeline is implicitly validating the bear case

Q1.3 — Geography Revenue Sequencing

  • Question: "Which territory approval — EU, China, or Japan — do you believe represents the largest incremental royalty value, and what is the critical-path bottleneck for each?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely cite EU as highest near-term value (large market, CHMP re-review after initial negative opinion), Japan as complex (domestic Eisai-originated programme with different royalty terms), and China as longest-dated
  • Good answer signature: Management provides territory-specific royalty treatment (distinguishing Japan from ex-Japan territories), identifies specific regulatory milestones with timeline estimates, and acknowledges EU re-submission risk
  • Bad answer signature: Management treats all geographies as equivalent royalty contributors, or cannot articulate the difference between the Japan royalty treatment and the ex-Japan treatment — a fundamental financial architecture gap
  • Follow-up probe: "The EU CHMP issued a negative opinion in 2024 — has Eisai re-submitted, and what is BioArctic's read on the probability and timeline for an EU positive opinion?"
  • Diagnostic note: EU approval failure is a $500M+ NPV event given market size; management's EU confidence level is a direct read on whether they have privileged Eisai intelligence or are relying on public regulatory filings like the market

Q1.4 — Payor Mix and Coverage Inflection

  • Question: "What is the current commercial vs Medicare/Medicaid payor split for US Leqembi patients, and at what coverage-rate does the royalty stream become meaningfully predictable for modelling purposes?"
  • Expected answer: Management will acknowledge that broad commercial coverage is still developing, that CMS's traditional Medicare coverage through the NCD/LCD process is the dominant near-term payer, and that commercial insurer coverage is expanding but inconsistently
  • Good answer signature: Specific payor-mix data cited (sourced from Eisai commercial team), explicit acknowledgement that IRA pricing negotiation risk exists for Leqembi, and a stated threshold (e.g. "≥60% commercial coverage" = meaningful predictability)
  • Bad answer signature: Management conflates Medicare coverage with broad commercial coverage, or cannot quantify the payor mix at all — suggesting they are not getting granular commercial intelligence from Eisai
  • Follow-up probe: "Is Leqembi on the initial IRA drug negotiation list, and have you stress-tested your royalty model for the IRA price negotiation downside scenario?"
  • Diagnostic note: IRA negotiation risk is a systemic risk to all branded pharma royalties on Medicare-dominant drugs; BIOA's royalty income is structurally exposed if Leqembi's net price is negotiated downward

Sceptical bullet: Fluent answers on revenue drivers may reflect rehearsed investor-day narratives rather than genuine transparency; the diagnostic signal is whether management volunteers the limitations of their own visibility into Eisai's commercial operations — if they sound too confident about Leqembi's commercial trajectory, it likely means they are repeating Eisai's public statements rather than providing independent intelligence

4. THEME §2 — Margin Trajectory + Levers

J-front verdict: Margin trajectory is structurally unusual — BIOA is transitioning from a cash-burning R&D company to a royalty-receiver, but the opex base has not been rightsized for a pure royalty model; the transition path is the key uncertainty

Op leverage: Royalty income is near-100% gross margin; the question is whether BIOA's operating cost base scales sub-linearly with royalty growth, or whether pipeline ambition keeps opex elevated regardless of royalty income

Q2.1 — Fixed vs Variable Cost Architecture

  • Question: "As Leqembi royalties grow, what percentage of your operating cost base is genuinely variable versus fixed, and what does your 3-year operating leverage look like at various royalty scenarios?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely describe a relatively fixed R&D cost base (pipeline programmes have defined budgets), a small commercial/BD cost layer, and administrative costs that are largely fixed; they may suggest 60-70% of costs are fixed
  • Good answer signature: Management provides explicit royalty revenue scenario bridges (e.g. SEK 500M / SEK 1bn / SEK 2bn royalty income) with corresponding EBIT margin outcomes, demonstrating genuine financial modelling discipline
  • Bad answer signature: Management conflates total spend with variable spend, or cannot articulate the P&L impact of a specific royalty revenue scenario — suggesting the CFO has not stress-tested the model
  • Follow-up probe: "At what royalty income level does BioArctic reach EBIT breakeven on a sustainable basis, and what is the current cash burn runway at today's royalty run-rate?"
  • Diagnostic note: Bull case requires operating leverage that accelerates EPS disproportionately to royalty income growth; bear case is a cost base that doesn't bend, leaving EBIT thin even at optimistic royalty scenarios

Q2.2 — R&D Adequacy vs Capital Efficiency

  • Question: "How do you decide the right level of R&D spend on the internal pipeline, and what return hurdle do you apply to new programme commitments given the royalty income is already funding operations?"
  • Expected answer: Management will describe a science-driven allocation process, likely citing stage-gate decisions at IND-filing and Phase 1 readout; they may reference SEK 300-500M annual R&D range as appropriate for current pipeline stage
  • Good answer signature: Explicit IRR or NPV hurdle stated for new programme commitments, evidence of programmes that were stopped based on financial return logic (not just science), reference to opportunity cost of internal R&D vs out-licensing
  • Bad answer signature: R&D allocation described entirely in scientific terms with no financial discipline overlay — "we pursue the best science" is a red flag in a company where royalty concentration risk is already high and new programmes represent optional rather than mandatory spend
  • Follow-up probe: "Have you ever stopped a programme primarily on financial return grounds rather than scientific data — and if so, which one?"
  • Diagnostic note: Concept A (power of incentives) — a scientist-founder CEO is incentivised to maximise scientific ambition; the diagnostic question probes whether commercial discipline has genuinely been embedded or whether the CFO is the sole financial-discipline anchor

Q2.3 — FX Exposure and Royalty Reporting Currency

  • Question: "Leqembi net sales are reported in USD/JPY; your financials are in SEK — what is your natural hedge position, and have you implemented any systematic FX hedging on royalty income?"
  • Expected answer: Management will acknowledge USD/SEK and JPY/SEK exposure; SEK has been a relatively strong currency vs USD in recent years; they may describe partial hedging via treasury policy
  • Good answer signature: Explicit hedge ratio disclosed (e.g. "60% of 12-month forward royalty income hedged"), description of instruments used, acknowledgement of the remaining unhedged exposure in EPS terms
  • Bad answer signature: No systematic hedging programme, reliance on "natural hedge through USD-denominated costs" (BIOA's cost base is predominantly SEK, so this would be misleading), or inability to quantify the SEK EPS sensitivity per 10% USD move
  • Follow-up probe: "Can you walk me through the SEK EPS sensitivity to a 10% USD/SEK move at current royalty run-rate?"
  • Diagnostic note: FX is a low-frequency but potentially high-magnitude risk; a royalty company with unhedged USD income and SEK-denominated costs is implicitly making a currency call — management should be transparent about this

Q2.4 — Milestones vs Royalty as Income Mix

  • Question: "Beyond ongoing royalties, are there remaining regulatory or commercial milestone payments due from Eisai, and how do you distinguish one-off milestone income from recurring royalty income in your earnings communication?"
  • Expected answer: Management will confirm milestone payments have been received at key approval events (US FDA, Japan PMDA), confirm whether EU approval would trigger an additional milestone, and acknowledge they try to be transparent about the one-off vs recurring distinction
  • Good answer signature: Explicit milestone schedule provided (amounts, triggers, timing), confirmation that consensus models correctly strip out one-off milestones from recurring EPS, proactive disclosure of any remaining milestones not yet in public filings
  • Bad answer signature: Management conflates milestone income with royalty income in guidance language, or cannot confirm whether a specific milestone has already been received vs is still pending — a basic investor communication discipline failure
  • Follow-up probe: "Does your FY guidance include any milestone income, and if so, what is the probability-weighted milestone assumption embedded in that guidance?"

Sceptical bullet: Margin trajectory conversations carry a structural optimism bias — management will naturally present the operating leverage story (royalties grow, costs flat, EBIT inflects); the bear-case read of a fluent answer is that the company has convinced itself the royalty ramp is fast enough to fund the pipeline build, when the base case may require an equity raise within 24 months if Leqembi ramp disappoints

5. THEME §3 — Capital Allocation + Balance Sheet

J-front verdict: Capital allocation discipline is the underexplored pillar — BIOA is transitioning from biotech-burn to royalty-income and the governance framework for deploying incremental royalty cash is unclear

Investment runway: Current cash position and royalty run-rate must be assessed against pipeline capex commitments; if royalty ramp is slow, runway compression is a real risk within the 18-36M investment horizon

Q3.1 — Cash Runway and Equity-Raise Threshold

  • Question: "At the current cash burn and royalty run-rate, what is your cash runway, and at what royalty income level do you become operationally self-funding without further equity issuance?"
  • Expected answer: Management will give a runway figure (likely 18-30 months at current burn), confirm the self-funding threshold, and may pre-emptively reassure that no equity raise is planned — this pre-emptive reassurance is itself a red flag worth noting
  • Good answer signature: Specific breakeven royalty income stated (e.g. "SEK X million royalty run-rate"), runway figure consistent with published balance sheet and CFO guidance, honest acknowledgement of the scenario where equity issuance would be considered
  • Bad answer signature: "We have sufficient runway" without a number, or a runway figure that doesn't reconcile with the published cash balance minus annual opex
  • Follow-up probe: "If Leqembi US ramp is 18 months slower than your base case, does that change your equity issuance probability materially?"
  • Diagnostic note: Cross-ref Q19 ACH — equity dilution is a direct EPS-destroyer for the 18-36M horizon investor; management reluctance to model this scenario is a red flag

Q3.2 — Pipeline Spend Prioritisation and Optionality

  • Question: "If you had to cut R&D spend by 30% tomorrow, which programmes would you protect and which would you pause — and what does that tell us about your true conviction hierarchy?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely protect the most advanced programme (tau or alpha-synuclein, depending on current stage), and acknowledge earlier-stage programmes are more deferrable — this is the intellectually honest answer
  • Good answer signature: Clear programme hierarchy stated, explicit acknowledgement that some programmes are "nice to have" vs "must fund", evidence that the board has explicitly stress-tested a capital-constrained scenario
  • Bad answer signature: "We would protect all programmes" — this is the worst possible answer; it signals either lack of financial discipline or unwillingness to be transparent about the true conviction hierarchy
  • Follow-up probe: "Has the board ever formally approved a capital-constrained R&D budget scenario, and if so, what was the outcome?"
  • Diagnostic note: RONIC discipline — a royalty company that mechanically reinvests royalty income into marginal-RONIC pipeline programmes is value-destructive; the question probes whether management has genuinely applied capital discipline or is running the company as a science project

Q3.3 — Dividend and Return-of-Capital Framework

  • Question: "At what royalty income level would the board consider initiating a dividend or buyback programme, and is there a formal return-of-capital policy?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely indicate no near-term return-of-capital plan, citing pipeline investment priority; the founding-family governance may make buybacks structurally unlikely
  • Good answer signature: Explicit threshold stated (e.g. "when royalty income exceeds pipeline investment needs by SEK X million for two consecutive years"), evidence that the board has discussed the question, acknowledgement that capital returns become relevant as the royalty story matures
  • Bad answer signature: "Dividends are not relevant at our stage" without any threshold — this is a governance red flag if the company is approaching royalty-income breakeven
  • Follow-up probe: "Given the founding-family share structure, is there any governance constraint that makes buybacks structurally unattractive?"

6. THEME §4 — Strategy + Competitive Position

J-front verdict: Strategic positioning is premised on Leqembi being the dominant amyloid-targeting therapy AND BioArctic building independent pipeline optionality — both assumptions face material competition threats that management has structural incentives to downplay

Moat trajectory: BioArctic's "moat" is a licensor relationship with Eisai on a first-in-class antibody; this is a time-limited competitive advantage as Eli Lilly's donanemab and other anti-amyloid antibodies compete for the same patient population

Q4.1 — Competitive Differentiation vs Donanemab

  • Question: "How do you characterise the competitive positioning of Leqembi vs Eli Lilly's donanemab, and at what point does competition materially compress Leqembi's addressable market share?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite Leqembi's continuous-dosing differentiation (monthly vs quarterly), safety profile differences (ARIA rates), and the complementarity of the two drugs in reaching different patient populations; they may argue market is large enough for both
  • Good answer signature: Specific market-share modelling cited (e.g. "Eisai's commercial team models X% Leqembi share of treated patients by Year 5"), acknowledgement that pricing competition is a real risk, honest comparison of ARIA profiles
  • Bad answer signature: "The market is large enough for both" without any quantification — this is the classic management deflection on competitive risk; the diagnostic question is whether they have modelled a scenario where Leqembi loses share faster than expected
  • Follow-up probe: "If Lilly's donanemab achieves quarterly dosing approval and is reimbursed at parity, what is your 3-year market-share assumption for Leqembi?"
  • Diagnostic note: Bear case #2 in ACH — competitive displacement by donanemab reduces Leqembi peak-sales from $5bn to $2-3bn, compressing BIOA royalty income by 40-60%

Q4.2 — Next-Generation Alzheimer's Pipeline vs Leqembi Dependency

  • Question: "What is the realistic probability that any of your internal pipeline programmes — tau antibody, alpha-synuclein — reaches Phase 3 within 5 years, and how does that change BioArctic's financial profile?"
  • Expected answer: Management will be optimistic about Phase 2 readouts, likely cite tau as the most advanced internal programme, and frame the pipeline as a "next wave" option; they will be reluctant to give probability estimates
  • Good answer signature: Explicit Phase 2 timeline stated, probability range given (even if wide), acknowledgement that even a successful Phase 2 requires Eisai or another partner for Phase 3 funding — BIOA cannot fund a Phase 3 alone
  • Bad answer signature: Pipeline described in entirely scientific terms without Phase 3 funding path, or management implies BIOA could independently advance a Phase 3 programme without a partner — financially implausible given current cash position
  • Follow-up probe: "For the tau programme — do you intend to partner out at Phase 2 completion, or has any partner expressed interest already?"
  • Diagnostic note: False-friend scenario — BIOA appears to have pipeline diversification but actually requires a partner for any Phase 3, meaning the "pipeline optionality" story is really a "partnership optionality" story, which is lower-probability and less controllable

Q4.3 — Regulatory Positioning and ARIA Risk Management

  • Question: "ARIA remains the key safety concern for amyloid-targeting antibodies — how has the ARIA management protocol for Leqembi evolved since launch, and is there any regulatory risk of a label change that would restrict the patient population?"
  • Expected answer: Management will describe the current ARIA monitoring protocol (MRI monitoring schedule, dosing interruption rules), cite Leqembi's ARIA rate vs donanemab, and argue the protocol is manageable in practice
  • Good answer signature: Specific ARIA event-rate data cited (consistent with public clinical data), acknowledgement that real-world ARIA rates may differ from clinical trial rates, and an honest assessment of any ongoing FDA/EMA post-marketing requirement that could tighten the label
  • Bad answer signature: ARIA risk dismissed as "well-understood and manageable" without acknowledging the real-world protocol burden that may be limiting physician adoption — the compliance friction of monthly MRI requirements is a genuine commercial drag

7. THEME §5 — Management Quality + Operator Track Record

J-front verdict: Management quality is the hardest pillar to assess at BIOA — scientist-founder culture is strong on scientific conviction but the commercial-operator track record on royalty management, IR communication, and capital allocation is limited

Animal-CEO probe: BioArctic is a scientist-founder company (Gunnar Osswald, Lars Lannfelt heritage); the operative archetype is closer to "scientific missionary" than "capital-markets operator" — both creates alignment (genuine belief in the science) and risk (commercial blind spots)

Q5.1 — Power of Incentives: Compensation Structure

  • Question: "Can you walk me through the CEO and CFO's compensation structure — what proportion is equity-linked, what are the vesting conditions, and are any components directly linked to Leqembi royalty performance metrics?"
  • Expected answer: Management will describe a mix of fixed salary, short-term cash bonus (likely linked to operational milestones), and long-term equity (RSUs or options); will be reluctant to give specific amounts; may claim compensation is disclosed in the annual report
  • Good answer signature: Explicit acknowledgement that long-term equity vesting is tied to shareholder value creation (not just scientific milestones), disclosure that royalty income trajectory is a bonus metric, evidence that compensation has been reset to reflect the company's transition from pure R&D to royalty-income stage
  • Bad answer signature: Compensation predominantly linked to scientific programme milestones with no direct financial performance component — this means management is incentivised to advance science regardless of financial return, which is value-destructive in a royalty company
  • Follow-up probe: "If Leqembi royalty income disappoints vs internal budget by 30%, does that materially affect CEO/CFO compensation for the year?"
  • Diagnostic note: Concept A — compensation structure predicts behaviour; a CEO paid primarily on scientific milestones will allocate capital to science; a CEO paid on royalty income and EPS will allocate capital differently; the actual incentive structure is more predictive than any strategy presentation

Q5.2 — Sandbagging Discipline + Guidance Track Record

  • Question: "Looking at the last 3 years of guidance vs actuals on royalty income and operating cost — where have you been most accurate, where have you systematically over- or under-estimated?"
  • Expected answer: Management will acknowledge that early royalty income guidance was difficult given Leqembi's commercial launch uncertainty; may claim they have been conservative on royalty assumptions; cost guidance has likely been more accurate as it is internally controlled
  • Good answer signature: Specific guidance vs actual data volunteered (not just "we try to be conservative"), acknowledgement of where forecasting has been weakest, and a description of how forecasting processes have improved
  • Bad answer signature: "Our guidance has been consistent with our communication" — this deflects the question; the diagnostic is whether management has a genuine culture of sandbagging (conservative guidance + beat) or optimism bias (guidance that requires things to go right)
  • Follow-up probe: "Your FY2024 royalty income guidance — how does it compare to what Eisai's own Leqembi sales guidance implies for the corresponding royalty?"

Q5.3 — Crisis Playbook

  • Question: "If Eisai announced tomorrow that they were reducing investment in Leqembi's commercial infrastructure due to a strategic pivot — what is BioArctic's crisis playbook, and what contractual levers do you have to protect your interests?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely describe contractual minimum-effort clauses in the licensing agreement (if they exist), cite the partnership's mutual interest in Leqembi success, and acknowledge they have limited ability to force Eisai to maintain commercial investment
  • Good answer signature: Specific contractual protection mechanisms cited (minimum sales effort clauses, right-to-audit Eisai royalty calculations, change-of-control provisions), honest acknowledgement of the structural asymmetry
  • Bad answer signature: "Eisai is fully committed to Leqembi" without acknowledging the contractual question — this is evasive; the diagnostic is whether BIOA has negotiated real contractual protections or is relying entirely on Eisai's continued commercial interest
  • Follow-up probe: "Do you have audit rights over Eisai's royalty calculation methodology, and have you ever exercised them?"

8. THEME §6 — Risk + Downside (Pre-mortem Cross-ref)

J-front verdict: The highest-probability failure modes for the BIOA investment case are all exogenous (Leqembi commercial disappointment, IRA pricing, EU approval failure, competitive displacement) — BioArctic has very limited direct control over any of them

Bear-case probe: The pre-mortem question for BIOA is not "what could go wrong operationally" but "what would the world look like in which all the macro/competitive/regulatory variables moved against you simultaneously" — that scenario is not a tail event; it is a plausible 1-in-3 outcome given the binary nature of drug adoption curves

Q6.1 — IRA Pricing Risk Quantification

  • Question: "Has your team modelled the royalty income downside from IRA price negotiation for Leqembi — and what is the worst-case royalty income scenario if CMS negotiates a 40-60% net price reduction?"
  • Expected answer: Management will acknowledge IRA risk exists, may indicate Leqembi is not yet on the negotiation list for the current cycle, and will likely downplay the near-term risk while acknowledging it is a genuine medium-term concern
  • Good answer signature: Explicit modelling of IRA scenarios disclosed (e.g. "a 40% net price reduction would reduce royalty income by approximately SEK X million annually at peak sales"), evidence that the board has reviewed this scenario
  • Bad answer signature: "IRA risk is manageable and we are monitoring it" — this is the standard pharma-company deflection; the diagnostic is whether BIOA has quantified the royalty impact, which is a basic financial discipline test
  • Follow-up probe: "Does the royalty agreement specify whether royalties are calculated on gross or net sales — and does an IRA-negotiated price reduction flow through to net sales for royalty calculation purposes?"

Q6.2 — EU Approval Failure Scenario

  • Question: "The CHMP issued an initial negative opinion on Leqembi in 2024 — what is Eisai's re-submission status, and what is your base-case probability and timeline for EU approval?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely indicate Eisai has re-submitted or is preparing a re-submission, will be cautious about probability estimates, and will frame the EU as a meaningful but non-critical upside scenario (US is the primary market)
  • Good answer signature: Specific re-submission timeline stated, honest probability estimate given (even if wide), acknowledgement that EU failure would be a meaningful NPV reduction but not a company-threatening event given US approval is already in place
  • Bad answer signature: EU approval described as "very likely in the medium term" without acknowledging the CHMP negative opinion history — this is wishful thinking; the diagnostic is whether management has genuinely stress-tested the EU-failure scenario
  • Follow-up probe: "If EU approval is denied permanently, what is the NPV impact to BioArctic's royalty stream, and does your current share price already reflect some probability of EU failure?"

Q6.3 — False-Friend Probe: Pipeline as Optionality vs Distraction

  • Question: "Is it possible that BioArctic's internal pipeline is actually a distraction from optimising returns on the Leqembi royalty — and would shareholders be better served by a higher-return-of-capital model with no internal pipeline?"
  • Expected answer: Management will push back, asserting that the pipeline is the source of long-term value beyond Leqembi and that returning capital before building the next asset would be short-sighted; this is the expected answer from a scientist-founder
  • Good answer signature: Management engages with the question honestly, acknowledges the tension between pipeline investment and capital returns, and provides a specific NPV framework for why internal pipeline investment beats return-of-capital at current stage-gated probabilities
  • Bad answer signature: Dismissive pushback without engaging with the capital-allocation logic — "we are a science-driven company" is not a capital-allocation framework
  • Follow-up probe: ⚡ "Has any board member or major shareholder ever formally raised this question in a board meeting, and how was it resolved?"
  • Diagnostic note: False-friend scenario — BIOA is positioned as a pipeline company but the actual investment case is a royalty company; the pipeline spend may be reducing shareholder value relative to a pure-royalty + return-of-capital model

Q6.4 — Leqembi Safety / Post-Marketing Surveillance Risk

  • Question: "What is the current status of Leqembi's post-marketing safety surveillance requirements, and is there any safety signal in the real-world data that differs from the clinical trial profile?"
  • Expected answer: Management will describe the FDA-required REMS programme, cite real-world ARIA rates as "consistent with" clinical data, and note that Eisai is the primary reporter to FDA/EMA
  • Good answer signature: Specific real-world ARIA event-rate data cited (sourced from Eisai or independent registries), acknowledgement of any cases of severe ARIA outcomes that have occurred post-approval, honest assessment of whether real-world safety data could trigger a label restriction
  • Bad answer signature: "Real-world safety data is consistent with clinical trials" without any numbers — this is the standard reassurance that provides no diagnostic information; the real question is whether the ARIA monitoring burden is suppressing physician adoption

Sceptical bullet — Q19 ACH coverage gap: The following ACH diagnostic items are NOT covered by questions in this memo: (1) BioArctic's ability to negotiate improved royalty terms at contract renewal — the existing agreement may have a renewal or re-negotiation window that management has not publicly discussed; (2) Specific named competitor (beyond Lilly/donanemab) approaching Phase 3 with a differentiated mechanism — a third-generation amyloid-targeting antibody or non-amyloid approach could threaten the entire category; (3) Key-person risk on the science side — if the founding scientific leadership departed, what happens to the pipeline's credibility with partners?

9. CROSS-CUTTING META + NON-OBVIOUS QUESTIONS

J-front verdict: The most diagnostic non-obvious questions probe the Eisai relationship depth, the board's actual capital-allocation framework, and management's honest view of the probability distribution around Leqembi — not the bull case they are incentivised to present
