3. BEARISH SELL-SIDE VIEWS

The analyst consensus on BFIT is broadly positive. As of Q1-26, most covering brokers maintain Buy or Outperform ratings. Citigroup reiterated Buy in January 2026. However, the wide target price range — from €124 at the low end to €258 at the high end, with average around €192 — signals significant disagreement among analysts on the fair value.

Bears in the sell-side community have not published Sell or Underperform ratings, but several have flagged:

First, the operating leverage argument may be overstated. BFIT's FY25 EBITDA grew only 11% despite 17% revenue growth. Revenue growth was driven partly by the Clever Fit consolidation impact. Organic EBITDA leverage was weaker than the model implied. If 2026 EBITDA growth of 20% (guidance midpoint requires this relative to FY25) does not materialise, the P&L disappointment could be severe.

Second, the France 24/7 €10m saving is a known catalyst that the market has likely already partly priced. The incremental positive surprise from France would require faster-than-planned rollout or regulatory extension beyond the initial 200 clubs.

Third, the leverage trajectory depends on FCF. BFIT's FY25 FCF was just €26m. For leverage to decline from 2.7x to "just over 2.0x" by end-FY26 — a reduction of approximately €350-400m of net debt — the company must generate significantly higher FCF in 2026. This requires both EBITDA growth and capex reduction (hence the decision to limit new clubs to 50). If either element disappoints, leverage stays elevated.

4. ACCOUNTING AND GOVERNANCE RED FLAGS

Non-GAAP adjustments. BFIT's "underlying net result" of €54.3m in FY25 strips out: IFRS 16 impact (capitalised lease charges replaced by depreciation and interest); purchase price allocation amortisation from acquisitions (including Clever Fit goodwill amortisation going forward); interest rate swap fair value changes; convertible bond non-cash interest charges; and exceptional items. The difference between GAAP and adjusted net profit is material. GAAP profit before tax is a fraction of adjusted profit. Bears argue that "exceptional items" have become a recurring feature, that PPA amortisation is a real economic cost, and that IFRS 16 corrections overstate underlying profitability by removing the full economic cost of leases.

The counter-argument (which is correct) is that EBITDA less rent is the most economically informative metric for a lease-heavy gym operator. The pre-IFRS 16 EBITDA properly restates rent as an operating cost and removes the accounting distortion. This is the industry standard metric. However, non-specialist investors or quantitative screens that use GAAP net income generate misleading signals.

IFRS 16 lease liabilities. Total net debt including lease liabilities was €2.77bn at end-2024. Subsequent to Clever Fit acquisition (Q4 2025), this will have increased. Against FY25 adjusted EBITDA of €348m, the inclusion of lease liabilities implies total leverage of approximately 7-8x. This is the "true" leverage in the sense that lease obligations are contractually binding and cannot be cancelled without penalty. A prolonged revenue decline (recession, major pandemic) would expose this leverage. In 2020 (COVID), BFIT drew on credit facilities and agreed lease deferrals with landlords. The experience demonstrated that lease obligations are renegotiable in extremis, but the theoretical maximum downside leverage is very high.

Convertible bond put option (June 2026). The €303.7m senior unsecured convertible bond issued in June 2021 has a bondholder put option in June 2026 (two years before maturity in June 2028). Bondholders can force redemption at par. BFIT has secured €290m of bilateral banking facilities earmarked to cover this. The facilities mature in June 2028. The risk: if bondholders exercise the put in bulk (which is likely given the conversion price of €50.625 — far above current market price, making conversion uneconomic — and a coupon of only 1.50%), BFIT must deploy €290m of bilateral banking headroom simultaneously with its ongoing operating leverage reduction programme.

The bear case on the bond: the €290m facility was secured from ABN AMRO, ING, and Rabobank — the same banks supporting the Clever Fit acquisition financing. In a credit stress scenario, these banks could theoretically tighten terms or question covenants. However, BFIT is a well-established borrower with a strong operational track record, and Dutch banking relationships are durable. The probability of this becoming a genuine refinancing crisis is low but non-zero.

Related party / governance. BFIT founder René Moos remains CEO and holds a significant economic interest. This is a double-edged governance factor: aligned incentives and long-term orientation, but reduced board independence. The €4.2m Clever Fit acquisition scam (a reported fraud during the transaction process) raised questions about due diligence and acquisition oversight processes.

6. COUNTER-ARGUMENTS TO BULL CASE

Bull: FCF is turning positive and accelerating. Bear: FCF was €26m in FY25 — modest for a €1.42bn revenue business. FCF yield on market cap is very low. For leverage to fall meaningfully, FCF must step up dramatically. The 2026 guidance requires EBITDA to grow 20%+ while capex falls (50 new clubs vs prior pace). If EBITDA grows 15% but capex is only modestly lower, FCF improvement is insufficient to reduce leverage meaningfully. One bad quarter — a cold/wet spring reducing member inductions, an energy spike, a Q1 French regulatory delay — wipes the FCF target.

Bull: Clever Fit adds German scale at a low effective multiple. Bear: €175m for a franchise business with uncertain unit economics. Franchise businesses are operationally complex: 454 franchise partners each with their own P&L, operational decisions, and potential brand risk. The reported €4.2m scam during the transaction process suggests due diligence was imperfect. German gym market is competitive: Clever Fit has not been a fast-growth entity. Integration of a franchise model into a company with zero prior franchise experience carries substantial execution risk. The earn-out structure (€15m conditional) means the full price is contingent on Clever Fit performance — which creates divergent incentives with existing Clever Fit management.

Bull: France 24/7 saves €10m/year. Bear: The €10m is back-end loaded and conditional. Phase 1 is 50 clubs from May 2026; Phase 2 is approximately 150 additional by summer. Only ground-floor clubs are approved. Maximum 19 members at one time via reservation. These restrictions limit throughput at peak hours and may partially offset the staffing saving with revenue opportunity costs. Regulatory conditions could be tightened if safety incidents occur. The €10m figure is management's estimate and assumes full rollout without complications.

Bull: 2,000+ club target by 2028 is achievable. Bear: The organic owned-club opening rate has been deliberately reduced to 50/year. At 50/year, reaching 2,000+ by 2028 from 1,710 owned (FY25) requires either an acquisition or a sharp acceleration. Franchise clubs (Clever Fit) help the total count but are not BFIT's owned units. The bear argues the 2,000+ target is a marketing aspiration, not a credible near-term deliverable without another major acquisition.

7. HISTORICAL PATTERN

BFIT has not suffered a formal short-seller attack. The company has traded profitably through the COVID period (lease deferrals and credit facility drawdowns), demonstrated consistent membership growth, and rebuilt to operational leverage post-pandemic.

The most significant prior period of scepticism was FY22-23, when rising energy costs, post-pandemic revenue ramp slower than expected, and the convertible bond conversion price (€50.625, vs a lower market price) created concern about dilutive refinancing. The company navigated this without equity issuance.

The Clever Fit announcement in October 2025 was initially met with mixed reactions. Shares sold off on announcement before recovering on the Q4-25/FY25 results beat. The market has oscillated between enthusiasm for the strategic logic and concern about integration risk.

8. QUALITY OF BEAR EVIDENCE

The bear evidence on BFIT is best categorised as:

High-quality concern: Valuation (P/E ~130x) pricing in near-perfect execution is a legitimate and credible risk. Multiple compression in a disappointment scenario is the clearest downside pathway.

Medium-quality concern: Clever Fit integration risk. The concern is real (franchise model is new to BFIT), but management has a strong track record of club-level execution. German gym market is competitive but not collapsing.

Low-quality concern: Accounting fraud or deliberate manipulation of adjusted metrics. There is no evidence of this. BFIT's adjusted metric disclosure is transparent and consistent with industry practice.

Medium-quality concern: Convertible bond put option risk. The €290m backstop facility is specific and adequate (it exceeds the €303.7m principal by accounting for any partial exercise). The risk is not default — it is the cost of maintaining additional debt on the balance sheet and the reduced financial flexibility during 2026-28.

9. RISK ASSESSMENT

Downside scenario A: Convertible bond put option triggers mass exercise, €290m drawn in June 2026. Impact: leverage stays elevated at ~2.7x; no immediate solvency risk; additional interest cost on bilateral facility; market perceives persistent leverage as signal to reduce valuation multiple. Price impact: 10-20% de-rating from bond overhang removal disappointment (market had expected leverage to fall faster).

Downside scenario B: Clever Fit Germany integration disappoints in FY26. Franchise-level attrition (some Clever Fit franchisees defecting or underperforming). Revenue contribution from Clever Fit consolidation (39 owned clubs + franchise fees) below target. No German revenue acceleration materialises. Impact: FY26 revenue guidance top-end unreachable; EBITDA guidance lowered to the bottom of the range. Price impact: 20-30% de-rating given current "flawless execution" pricing.

Downside scenario C: FCF negative in FY26, leverage fails to decline. Requires: EBITDA grows only 10% (not 20%); capex is higher than guided (supply chain issues, site construction delays on 50 new clubs). FCF turns negative; leverage stays at 2.7x. Impact: complete de-rating of the leverage improvement story. This is BFIT's most valued narrative in 2026. A leverage reversal would be a severe sentiment shock. Price impact: 30-40% de-rating.

Bear case combined scenario: All three disappoint simultaneously. Valuation would likely revert from current levels toward a more reasonable 20-25x adjusted earnings multiple on downward-revised earnings. This would imply a 50-60% downside from current prices.

10. SCEPTICAL REBUTTAL

The strongest bull responses to the bear case:

Q1-26 data is already strong. Revenue of €396m (+19% YoY), memberships of 6.0m (+28% YoY, including Clever Fit), and raised EBITDA guidance undercut the bear narrative on operational momentum.

The €290m bilateral facility is specifically earmarked for the convertible bond put. This is not speculative — it is committed, documented financing from BFIT's core relationship banks. The probability of this facility being unavailable in June 2026 is negligible in a normal credit environment.

The franchise model transition is strategically correct. BFIT's owned-club model has served it well, but capital allocation at scale becomes increasingly constrained. Franchise economics (lower capex, higher ROIC) are the natural evolution. Clever Fit was the fastest route to German scale without building 454 clubs organically over a decade.

BFIT has consistently delivered on operational promises since IPO. The CEO (René Moos) founded the company and has managed it through COVID, high energy costs, and post-pandemic recovery without losing strategic direction.
