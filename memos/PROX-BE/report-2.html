Key Findings — Top 10 Bullets

  • JUDGEMENT: Proximus is mid-transformation, not early-stage — the fibre overbuild is the central value-creation mechanism, but the capex trough is still 2–3 years away; FCF recovery is the investment thesis, not current earnings.
  • Proximus Global (BICS + TeleSign + Route Mobile) is the most acute near-term negative change force — CPaaS SMS headwinds are structural not cyclical; Global EBITDA fell from €501M (2024) to €445M (2025) and guided €100–130M for 2026, a -70% collapse; this is the dominant risk.
  • Dividend cut of 50% (€0.60 → €0.30/share for 2026) triggered a 15–16% single-day share price crash in February 2026; the stock now trades on a materially lower yield anchor — this changes the investor register.
  • Digi Belgium's entry (December 2024) is the biggest competitive disruption in Belgian telecoms in a decade — €5 mobile / €10 fibre pricing forced incumbents into immediate tariff responses; long-run ARPU pressure is non-trivial.
  • Fibre take-up at ~68% migration rate in fibre-passed areas is a genuine positive — ~607,000 active fibre lines by mid-2025, broadband ARPU +5.2% YoY to €50.3 in Q1 2025; the flywheel is working on the domestic side.
  • CEO departure (Boutin → Vodafone, May 2025) and new CEO (Bijnens, September 2025) create genuine strategy execution risk — new 'Amplify/Elevate' plan is 6 months old; credibility unproven.
  • State ownership (53.5% via SFPIM) is structurally ambiguous — privatisation legislation is progressing but timing uncertain; state has historically extracted dividends, making the cut politically complex; capex discipline may improve post-cut.
  • Belgian regulatory environment is broadly neutral-to-constructive for Proximus — BIPT SMP designation enforces wholesale access obligations but does not prevent ARPU growth; Fiberklaar/Wyre Flanders collaboration under market test creates overbuild risk in medium-density areas.
  • FCF recovery trajectory is the 2028–2030 thesis: capex < €1B by 2030, organic FCF targeting ~€400M vs €130M organic FCF in 2025 — the math works if Global stabilises and domestic ARPU holds; both are contested assumptions.
  • ⚡ The Proximus Global implosion is an outlier negative within the cohort — Swisscom has no equivalent CPaaS exposure; Liberty Global's international complexity is different in kind; Proximus is uniquely exposed to CPaaS SMS commoditisation.

§1 — Proximus Overbuild (Fibre Network) — Central Change Force

The domestic fibre rollout is Proximus's defining multi-year capital allocation decision. The overbuild of Belgium's legacy copper DSL network with FTTH is the primary mechanism linking near-term capex pain to medium-term FCF recovery.

Scope and Timeline

  • Network coverage target: 95% of Belgian premises by fibre (FTTH/FTTB) by 2032; 100% gigabit coverage (including fixed-wireless) by 2032.
  • Progress to end-Q1 2025: 2.3 million premises passed (fibre at street level), covering ~43% of Belgian homes and businesses.
  • Proximus standalone rollout covers 2.2M premises in high-density zones; completion targeted by end-2026.
  • Joint ventures extend reach: Fiberklaar (Flanders, 1.5M homes by 2028) and Unifiber (Wallonia, 0.6M homes by 2028) handle medium-density zones with co-investment partners.
  • Annual build pace: ~400,000–500,000 homes passed per year at peak; gradually slowing post-2026 as dense-area build completes.

Strategic Rationale

  • Why overbuild rather than upgrade copper? Legacy VDSL2/copper infrastructure cannot deliver gigabit symmetrical speeds; Telenet's HFC cable network already offers 1Gbps downstream to ~55% of Belgium — copper ARPU is structurally disadvantaged.
  • Defensive logic: Proximus's DSL base was losing ARPU premium to cable; without FTTH it risks permanent structural disadvantage vs. Telenet's Liberty Global-owned HFC plant.
  • Competitive response asymmetry: Telenet faces its own DOCSIS 3.1 upgrade costs; fibre positions Proximus as the superior-technology incumbent in its footprint — this is the 'transformation-play' thesis.

Overbuild Progress and Take-Up

  • Active fibre lines: ~607,000 by Q1 2025 (up 43,000 in Q1 alone); growing sequentially every quarter.
  • Take-up rate: ~68% migration from copper to fibre in fibre-passed areas — above industry benchmarks for incumbent FTTH rollouts (European average closer to 40–55% at comparable rollout maturity).
  • ARPU uplift: Fibre customers generate approximately €5 more per month vs. copper customers; broadband ARPU +5.2% YoY to €50.3 in Q1 2025 — partly structural (fibre mix), partly price indexation.
  • Convergent customer base: 1.194 million convergent customers by Q2 2025, growing +4.2% YoY; ARPC €91.2 (+1.0% YoY).
  • JUDGEMENT: Take-up at 68% is genuinely strong, suggesting demand is not purely demand-pulse; however, the full-network completion is 2032 and payback at the network level is a decade-long story, not 18M.

Capex Peak and FCF Trough

  • Capex peak: 2022–2023; FY2025 CapEx €1,249M, down €107M YoY — gradual decline begun.
  • 2026 guidance: Accrued CapEx €1.2–1.25B — still elevated, no step-change yet.
  • FCF trough: Now; FY2025 organic FCF €130M vs. €57M in 2024 — improving but from very low base; reported FCF €480M includes asset sale proceeds.
  • FCF recovery path: CapEx expected < €1B by 2030 as rollout completes; organic FCF target ~€400M by 2030 (per Amplify strategy).
  • Payback inflection point: 2028–2029, when capex moderates below €1.1B and EBITDA benefits from higher fibre ARPU base compound.
  • ❌ Proximus vs. Swisscom: SCMN has no equivalent fibre overbuild capex drag — Swisscom operates in a market where Swissplus/regional operators handle rollout burden; Proximus bears full overbuild cost as incumbent.

EPS Transmission Mechanism

  • Fibre take-up → broadband ARPU uplift (€5/mo per migrated customer) → Domestic EBITDA expansion
  • Domestic EBITDA guided broadly stable 2026; growth of 1% CAGR through 2026–2028 — modest not explosive
  • FCF impact is larger than EBITDA impact because CapEx decline post-2026 drops directly to FCF
  • Rough arithmetic: 1M customers migrated to fibre × €5/mo uplift × 12 months = €60M incremental EBITDA run-rate; partially already in numbers
  • Depreciation drag: FTTH network depreciation rising as assets commissioned → EBITDA to net income conversion is diluted until D&A plateaus
  • SCEPTICISM: Management has guided 1% Domestic Services revenue CAGR for 2026–2028 — this is not transformative; the investment case depends on FCF conversion improvement, not EPS growth in the near term.

§2 — Proximus Global (TeleSign / BICS / Route Mobile) — The Acute Negative Change Force

What Proximus Global Is

  • In December 2024, Proximus merged BICS, TeleSign, and Route Mobile under a new holding company: Proximus Global.
  • The combined entity has ~2,500 employees across 100 countries; revenues of ~€1.9B in 2023; now the world's third-largest CPaaS provider by revenues.
  • Three legs: (1) BICS — international wholesale carrier / interconnect; (2) TeleSign — digital identity / communications APIs; (3) Route Mobile — customer engagement / messaging CPaaS.

TeleSign: Acquisition and History

  • BICS acquired TeleSign in 2017 for $230M; Proximus took 100% ownership in 2021.
  • TeleSign's core product: digital identity verification, phone number intelligence, two-factor authentication APIs — sold to large US technology companies (verification-as-a-service for onboarding).
  • Attempted SPAC listing in December 2021 at Enterprise Value $1.3B — deal did not close; market peak timing.
  • TeleSign revenue at point of attempted SPAC: not publicly disclosed precisely, but market valued it at ~5–6× group-level revenues; implied revenue ~$200–250M.
  • Strategic logic: CPaaS digital identity was a high-growth vertical in 2020–2022; Proximus sought to build a global digital communications platform beyond Belgian telecoms.

Route Mobile: Scale-Up Acquisition

  • Proximus acquired a majority stake in Route Mobile (Mumbai-listed CPaaS company) in 2023 for ~€643M.
  • Route Mobile specialises in enterprise messaging, omnichannel customer engagement, SMS A2P.
  • The acquisition took Proximus Global to ~€900M combined revenues, positioning it as a global CPaaS player.

Performance 2025 — The Collapse

  • FY2025 Global EBITDA: €445M — down 9.3% from €501M in FY2024; down from implied peak of ~€530M+ in 2022/2023.
  • Q3 2025 Global EBITDA: €38M — down 25.1% YoY; revenue -19.4% YoY to €377M.
  • Root cause: Accelerated commoditisation of CPaaS SMS market — specifically one-time-password (OTP) and international terminating A2P SMS traffic.
  • OTP SMS being displaced by app-based authentication (push notifications, authenticator apps) — secular, not cyclical.
  • International terminating SMS pricing under pressure from competing routes and grey-market carriers.
  • Operational integration headwinds from Route Mobile merger delay margin capture.
  • 2026 guidance: Global EBITDA €100–130M — this implies a -70% to -77% decline from the 2024 peak in just two years.
  • ⚡ OUTLIER: The scale of Proximus Global's EBITDA implosion is extraordinary within European telecoms — €500M→€115M EBITDA is a near-write-off of the CPaaS growth strategy in real economic terms.
  • ❌ Proximus Global vs. peers: Liberty Global has no equivalent CPaaS exposure; Swisscom divested non-core international units; Proximus carried the CPaaS bet the longest and is paying the full price.

Valuation and Strategic Direction

  • TeleSign: no current market value; attempted SPAC at $1.3B (2021) failed; goodwill impairment contributed to FY2025 net income decline of 11%.
  • Route Mobile: Proximus paid ~€643M; current implied value on declining EBITDA unclear but materially below acquisition price.
  • New 'Elevate' strategy for Global: Target return to EBITDA growth by 2027; "value crystallisation optionality" — code for potential partial disposal.
  • JUDGEMENT: 'Elevate' is management language for 'we are exploring our options for these assets.' The combination of goodwill impairment + EBITDA collapse + new CEO makes a disposal/JV of Proximus Global a live strategic scenario in 2026–2027. This is a binary change force — if executed at fair value, it would be significantly FCF-accretive; if retained, it remains an earnings drag.
  • Bear case: CPaaS SMS revenues continue to structurally decline as RCS (Rich Communication Services) and app-based authentication penetrate; Proximus Global reaches EBITDA break-even by 2027–2028 at best, with no recovery to 2023 levels. The entire Route Mobile acquisition (~€643M) proves to have been value-destructive.

§3 — BICS — International Wholesale Carrier

Scale and Market Position

  • BICS (formerly Belgacom International Carrier Services) is one of Europe's largest international wholesale voice, data, and roaming carriers.
  • Market position: top-tier IPX provider (interconnection for roaming, voice termination, signalling); serves mobile network operators globally.
  • Revenue scale: BICS contributed a substantial portion of the historical Proximus Global ~€1.9B revenues; exact split not disclosed separately post-merger into Proximus Global structure.
  • BICS designated preferred IPX provider in Europe for Starlink's direct-to-cell satellite connectivity (announced November 2025) — a new growth vector.
  • 5G roaming: BICS expanding standalone 5G roaming capability; 8 operators live and connected by Q1 2025, including Drei (Austria) and Elisa (Finland).

Strategic Direction: Growth, Sale, or Wind-Down?

  • BICS is now subsumed within Proximus Global — strategic decisions are made at that level, not for BICS independently.
  • The Proximus Global 'Elevate' strategy identifies BICS's connectivity infrastructure as the "global connectivity backbone" of the combined entity.
  • BICS itself (carrier services) faces the same secular pressures as all wholesale voice carriers: international voice volumes declining, SMS termination under pressure. Its growth comes from data/IPX/roaming and new services.
  • BICS is not being wound down; it is being positioned as the connectivity infrastructure layer beneath TeleSign's digital identity and Route Mobile's messaging — the theory is that owning the pipe creates margin retention vs. pure software CPaaS players who rent connectivity.
  • SCEPTICISM: The theory is structurally elegant; the 2025 results show the practice has not delivered the claimed margin advantage — Global EBITDA collapsed even with BICS providing the infrastructure layer. The carrier economics may be diluting, not insulating.

§4 — Belgian Market Competitive Dynamics — Change in Progress

Mobile Market Share

  • Belgian mobile market: three network operators — Proximus (~44–45% postpaid revenue share), Telenet/BASE (~30%), Orange Belgium (~25–26%).
  • Proximus gained market share in 2024 at the expense of Orange and Telenet per Telecompaper data.
  • 2025 trends: Proximus broadly stable; Digi Belgium emerged as 4th MNO in December 2024 but with only 91,000 customers by end-2025 — de minimis market share but large psychological and price signalling impact.
  • Digi's pricing: €5/month for 15GB unlimited calls/texts SIM-only; €10/month for 500Mbps fibre. This undercuts the market by 60–80%.
  • Incumbent response: Proximus increased mobile data allowances at no extra charge in March 2025 — indirect price concession. Avoids explicit tariff cuts but compresses margins at the margin.
  • JUDGEMENT: Digi's 91,000 customer base is not yet threatening, but its very existence anchors the market's price expectations lower. The key question is Digi's rollout pace — its own infrastructure build (4G/5G) target of 2M homes in 5 years is ambitious for a new entrant.

Fixed Market: Fibre vs. Cable

  • Telenet (owned by Liberty Global) operates Belgium's dominant HFC cable network covering ~55% of Belgian homes (primarily Flanders + Brussels).
  • Proximus fibre vs. Telenet HFC: technologically converging on gigabit speeds, but Proximus fibre is symmetric (upload = download) — meaningful advantage for cloud/WFH use cases.
  • ARPU differential: Proximus broadband ARPU €50.3 vs. Telenet's implied broadband ARPU slightly lower (Telenet has historically competed on price in fixed). The Belgian market has one of the highest broadband ARPUs in Europe (€16.6/GB for Proximus, above peer group).
  • Fiberklaar/Wyre collaboration (Flanders): Proximus proposed a co-build arrangement with Telenet in medium-density Flemish areas; Belgian Competition Authority market test launched October 2025. If approved, reduces overbuild duplication in lower-density zones — positive for Proximus FCF. If rejected, Wyre and Fiberklaar may compete head-to-head in medium-density Flanders.
  • Proximus/Orange Wallonia MOU (July 2025): Proximus and Orange Belgium signed an MOU to jointly expand fibre in rural Wallonia — reduces Proximus capex burden in low-density regions; Orange gains fibre access without full build cost.

Convergence Take-Up

  • Convergent customer base: 1.194M by Q2 2025 (+4.2% YoY).
  • Convergent ARPC: ~€91 — approximately 80% premium over single-service broadband ARPU.
  • Convergence penetration forecast to reach ~80% of Belgian households by 2027 — still a growth runway, but rate of penetration growth is moderating.
  • Belgian market is structurally receptive: small geography, high disposable income, high broadband penetration (~80%+), urban density.

Regulatory Change

  • BIPT market analysis: Proximus designated as SMP on copper and FTTH networks; mandatory wholesale access obligations apply. BIPT cost models for FTTH wholesale access published; margin squeeze prohibition in force.
  • Spectrum: Belgium 5G spectrum auctions completed 2022; no major spectrum event expected near-term. Digi Belgium is building on Proximus's 4G network under MVNO terms while rolling out own infrastructure.
  • EU Gigabit Society targets: Belgium was at ~43% FTTH coverage by mid-2025 but from a low base of <10% as recently as 2021. EU subsidy access is available for rural coverage; Proximus JVs structured to attract this.
  • ❌ Belgian regulatory overhang vs. Swisscom: SCMN operates in a market with less aggressive regulatory pressure and no Digi-equivalent low-cost entrant; Belgium is more exposed to disruptive pricing than Switzerland.

§5 — Management Change and Strategy Evolution

CEO Transition — A Material Governance Risk

  • Guillaume Boutin: CEO December 2019 – May 2025. Architect of bold2025 strategy, Proximus NXT (ICT/IT services), Proximus Global creation. Departed to Vodafone Group as CEO Investments & Strategy (February 2025 announcement; left mid-May 2025).
  • Interim period: Jan Van Acoleyen and co-interim CEOs from mid-May to September 2025 — 4-month leadership vacuum during which FY2025 strategy continued on autopilot.
  • Stijn Bijnens: CEO from September 1, 2025. Background: founded Ubizen (network security), led Cegeka (ICT company) 2019–2025. Technology-sector executive, not a career telco operator.
  • First CMD as Proximus CEO: February 2026 (alongside Q4 2025 results) — presented 'Amplify' (domestic) and 'Elevate' (global) strategies.
  • JUDGEMENT: Bijnens has no prior telco CEO track record. His ICT/technology background gives credibility on the B2B/digital transformation side (Proximus NXT) but raises questions about whether he will drive necessary cost restructuring in legacy operations. The dividend cut and Global impairment on his first CMD suggests he used the new-CEO 'kitchen sink' moment — which is strategically rational but operationally uncertain.

State Ownership: Constraint or Enabler?

  • Belgian state (via SFPIM) owns 53.51% — transferred from direct state ownership in May 2024.
  • Privatisation: New Belgian legislation is in progress to make privatisation legally possible; expected to yield €6B to Belgian state at current valuations — but market cap at ~€7/share implies enterprise value well below historical; state has little incentive to sell at current prices.
  • Dividend politics: The dividend cut from €0.60 to €0.30/share is a direct reduction in Belgian state income (~€120M gross reduction p.a. on state's 53.5% stake). This makes the cut politically significant — it implies the board prioritised capital allocation discipline over state income, a positive governance signal.
  • SCEPTICISM: State ownership historically biases management against aggressive restructuring (workforce reductions, asset disposals). The 'Elevate' strategy's "optionality for value crystallisation" of Proximus Global is constrained if the state views any disposal as politically sensitive.

Capital Allocation

  • Dividend reduction path: €0.60 (2025) → €0.30 (2026) → €0.40 (2027) → €0.50 (2028). This is a 50% cut in year one, with partial recovery. At €0.30/share, yield on ~€7/share = ~4.3% — no longer a high-yield income stock.
  • Capex commitment: Management guiding capex broadly flat at €1.2–1.25B through 2026–2028 before falling below €1B by 2030. No step-change acceleration or deceleration near-term.
  • Proximus Global disposal optionality: Management's "Elevate" language explicitly flags "future value crystallisation" — this is new language not present in Boutin-era communications. Could mean partial IPO, JV, or full disposal of Route Mobile and/or TeleSign. A full disposal of Proximus Global at even a 5× EBITDA multiple on €115M guided 2026 EBITDA = ~€575M enterprise value — modest relative to the ~€1B+ invested but would eliminate the drag.
  • Proximus NXT (ICT/IT services): Not a change force in itself but a stable domestic B2B revenue stream; growing as Belgian enterprises digitise. Not separately valued.

§6 — External Change Forces

EU Gigabit Society / Subsidy Tailwinds

  • EU Gigabit Society target: every European home passed by gigabit network by 2030; 100Mbps for all by 2025.
  • Belgium is behind on FTTH relative to EU peers (Netherlands: ~90% fibre coverage; Denmark: ~85%; Belgium: ~43% mid-2025). This creates both urgency and subsidy eligibility.
  • Belgian federal and regional governments are co-funding FTTH deployment in lower-density areas through structural funds; Unifiber (Wallonia JV) and Fiberklaar are structured to capture these subsidies.
  • JUDGEMENT: Subsidy tailwind is real but modest relative to the total capex envelope. The majority of Proximus's €1.2B+ annual capex is commercially funded. The subsidy benefit flows primarily to the JV level (reducing Proximus's proportionate share), not as a direct Proximus P&L benefit.

Belgian Regulatory Environment: BIPT

  • BIPT maintains Proximus as SMP on copper and FTTH networks — wholesale access mandatory.
  • Key risk: BIPT could set wholesale FTTH access pricing that compresses Proximus's retail margin, enabling MVNOs/resellers to free-ride on the infrastructure investment. This is the 'incumbent dilemma' tension.
  • Current BIPT stance: FTTH wholesale tariffs approved in March 2021 for GPON; price reflective of costs with reasonable margin. No indication of aggressive margin squeeze at regulatory level.
  • Fiberklaar/Wyre market test (October 2025): if co-build collaboration approved, reduces regulatory risk of being forced to provide access to a competing Wyre network in medium-density areas.
  • ❌ Proximus vs. Swisscom: SCMN operates in Switzerland (non-EU) with ComCom regulatory oversight that has been historically more benign; Proximus faces EU-level regulatory overlay in addition to BIPT.

Macro: Interest Rates and FX

  • Proximus net debt: ~€3.8–4.0B at FY2025 (approximate); net debt/EBITDA ~2.0–2.1× — moderate vs. LG which operates at 4–5× net debt/EBITDA.
  • Interest rate sensitivity: Proximus has a mix of fixed and floating debt; EUR-denominated. ECB rate normalisation post-2024 reduces refinancing risk vs. 2022–2023 peaks.
  • FX: Proximus is EUR-reported; Proximus Global revenues include USD/INR exposure (TeleSign USD-priced; Route Mobile INR-cost base). Route Mobile's INR cost base means EUR strengthening can compress margins on USD-revenue streams — a subtle headwind.
  • ⚡ OUTLIER relative to cohort: Swisscom (CHF-reported) is subject to CHF appreciation pressure on international revenues; Liberty Global (USD-listed, multi-currency) has more complex FX. Proximus is the most EUR-pure of the three — least FX distortion in domestic business.

AI/Cloud Demand for B2B Connectivity

  • Proximus NXT (B2B ICT unit) positions Proximus as an enterprise digital transformation partner — cloud, security, networking services.
  • AI demand is generating incremental bandwidth requirements in enterprise settings: data centre connectivity, private 5G for industrial applications, secure cloud-to-enterprise links.
  • Proximus has a Belgian market advantage as the incumbent with both the network and an ICT services overlay.
  • BICS (within Proximus Global) has a partnership with Starlink for direct-to-cell IPX routing — new connectivity category; revenue scale currently negligible but signals positioning in non-terrestrial networks.
  • SCEPTICISM: Belgian enterprise market is small (~11M population, high SME proportion). The AI/cloud demand uplift will be real but modest in absolute €M terms. This is not a major EPS driver within a 3-year horizon.

§7 — Change Forces Summary and EPS Transmission

Top 3 Change Forces Ranked by EPS Materiality

CF#1 — Proximus Global EBITDA Collapse (Negative, Most Material)

  • Magnitude: EBITDA falling from €501M (2024) to €100–130M (2026E) — a €370–400M EBITDA loss in 24 months.
  • EPS transmission: Direct → EBITDA decline flows to operating profit; goodwill impairments contributed to FY2025 net income -11%. At 40% EBITDA-to-EPS conversion (assuming ~€450M D&A and ~€200M financing costs and taxes), the €370M EBITDA decline translates to ~€150–180M net income destruction.
  • Bear case: CPaaS SMS OTP displacement by app-based authentication is structural and accelerating; Global EBITDA does not recover to €300M+ through the decade; cumulative impairment charges continue. Full impairment of Route Mobile (~€643M goodwill on books) would trigger further net income hit.
  • Bull case: Route Mobile stabilises at current margin levels; TeleSign finds new digital identity revenue streams beyond SMS (voice, app-based); Proximus Global disposes of assets at >5× EBITDA, recycling cash to domestic capex, and eliminating the EBITDA drag.

CF#2 — Domestic Fibre Build and ARPU Harvest (Positive, Medium-Term)

  • Magnitude: Every 100,000 customers migrated to fibre at €5 ARPU uplift = €6M incremental EBITDA p.a. Total addressable migration: ~4–5M Belgian households over a decade. Near-term active fibre lines: ~700,000E by end-2025; full payback at scale is 2029–2032.
  • EPS transmission: Domestic Services revenue CAGR 1% (management guidance 2026–2028); EBITDA broadly stable — this means fibre ARPU offsets legacy fixed voice decline and mobile ARPU pressure. Net EPS from domestic fibre harvest is +marginal over 2026–2028; the big payoff comes when capex falls post-2028.
  • FCF transmission (more powerful than EPS): CapEx €1.25B (2026) → <€1.0B (2030) = €250M+ capex release; plus rising EBITDA → organic FCF €400M by 2030 vs. €130M today. This is a 3× FCF per share expansion over 4 years — significant if delivered.
  • Bear case: Digi Belgium forces ARPU compression across Belgian market; convergence penetration plateaus at 60% (not 80% as forecast); fibre ARPU uplift is neutralised by price concessions to meet low-cost competition.

CF#3 — CEO Transition and Strategy Reset (Wildcard, Binary)

  • Magnitude: Hard to quantify; depends on execution. If Bijnens drives Proximus Global disposal (even at €500–600M), the capital recycled reduces net debt and restores dividend growth capacity. If he over-invests in domestic ICT (Proximus NXT) at the expense of network harvest, value creation delays by 2–3 years.
  • EPS transmission: Disposal of Proximus Global (hypothetical) → removes ~€150M EBITDA drag (post-2025 Global EBITDA net of corporate costs); proceeds reduce net debt ~€500M; financing cost saving ~€20–25M p.a.; net EPS accretion ~€0.05–0.10/share p.a. vs. current.
  • Bear case: New CEO pursues 'Elevate' recovery strategy, investing further in Proximus Global restructuring; 2027 "return to growth" target misses; another round of impairments and dividend reassessment in 2027–2028.

Counter-Hypothesis: Why Proximus Change Forces Fail

  • Demand pulse risk: Belgian broadband ARPU growth in 2025 (+5.2%) is partly inflation-indexed tariff adjustment (January 2025 indexation) — strip out the mechanical inflation pass-through and underlying volume/mix growth is more modest. If Belgian CPI normalises and Digi competes, ARPU growth reverts to 0–1%.
  • CPaaS secular, not cyclical: Management language around Proximus Global suggests they believe the SMS slowdown is a temporary headwind; the evidence (OTP displacement by push notifications, RCS migration) suggests it is a multi-year structural decline. A 2027 'return to growth' for Global may be aspirational.
  • Capex discipline erosion: Belgian state ownership historically resists workforce restructuring and asset disposals; if privatisation is delayed, the state's preference for stable employment and dividend income could bias the new CEO away from value-maximising capital allocation.
  • Fibre overbuild timing risk: Medium-density Flanders co-build with Wyre subject to competition authority approval. If rejected, Proximus must choose between overbuild duplication (higher capex) or ceding territory to Wyre; either outcome is worse than the approved co-build scenario.
  • Digi Belgium acceleration: Currently 91,000 customers (negligible). If Digi secures spectrum refarming or government support for a national buildout (as in Romania, its home market), it could reach 500,000–1M customers within 3 years and structurally compress Belgian ARPU. Romania precedent: Digi drove a 20–30% ARPU compression for incumbents over 5 years.

§8 — Cohort CQ Responses (Change Forces Lens)

CQ1: ARPU Trajectory (L3Y) — Structural or Demand-Pulse?

  • Proximus broadband ARPU: €50.3 in Q1 2025, +5.2% YoY. Convergent ARPC: ~€91, +1.0% YoY.
  • Structural drivers: fibre mix shift (€5 uplift per customer), convergence upsell, January annual CPI-linked tariff indexation.
  • Demand-pulse risk: the 5.2% broadband ARPU growth includes the January 2025 inflation indexation — this is a one-time mechanical uplift, not recurring at that rate.
  • JUDGEMENT (applying demand-pulse caution): Strip the inflation pass-through and underlying ARPU growth is likely 2–3% — respectable for an incumbent but not transformative. This is partially structural (fibre mix) and partially a demand pulse (indexation). Apply 50% structural / 50% demand-pulse weight pending further evidence.
  • Cohort comparison: Swisscom Switzerland ARPU trajectory is broadly similar — inflation-indexed tariffs in a premium market. Liberty Global's ARPU trajectory is more complex (multi-country, re-bundling post-cable disposal). Proximus domestic ARPU is the most directly comparable to SCMN on this metric.

CQ2: Alt-Net Fibre Overbuilder Competitive Intensity

  • Belgian alt-net risk is lower than markets like the UK or Netherlands — Belgium's urban density and small geography makes duplicate fibre economics difficult without scale.
  • Primary alt-net risk: Digi Belgium (mobile + fibre). €10/month fibre plans are loss-leading at current scale; Digi has committed to 2M home passings in 5 years, which would cover ~35% of Belgium.
  • Secondary risk: Wyre (Fluvius/Telenet) in medium-density Flanders — the competition authority market test is the swing variable. If Wyre builds independently (worst case for Proximus), it creates genuine overbuild pressure in 2.7M medium-density homes.
  • Regulatory buffer: BIPT wholesale access obligations mean even competitive infrastructure must be accessible to third-party operators — limits pure exclusivity plays.
  • JUDGEMENT: Alt-net competitive intensity is RISING but not yet critical. Digi's 91,000 customers in 12 months is a slow ramp given Belgian broadband market size (~5M fixed broadband subscriptions). The 2–3 year window before Digi becomes meaningful gives Proximus time to deepen convergence lock-in. ❌ vs. Liberty Global's markets (Netherlands, UK, Switzerland) where alt-net overbuild is already at 30–50% — Belgium is at early stage.

CQ3: Fixed+Mobile Bundle Economics — Flywheel or Defensive?

  • Proximus's convergent ARPC €91 vs. standalone broadband €50 = ~82% premium. Convergent customer churn is materially lower than single-service (telecoms industry norm: convergent churn 50–60% below standalone).
