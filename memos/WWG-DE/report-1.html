§2 — COMPANY DETAILS (Revenue Model, Business Model, Recent Delivery, Internal Change)

Net signal: Revenue is captive, concession-protected, and highly predictable, but these same structural features create a ceiling — the business is a quasi-bond with a thin equity kicker, not a growth compounder [#J]

Revenue Model

  • Revenue source: Three principal revenue streams: (1) drinking water production/distribution ~55-60% of normalised revenue; (2) wastewater treatment ~15-20%; (3) gas distribution/energy services ~20-25% [#I]
  • Water tariffs set via cost-plus regulation administered by municipal concession authorities and overseen by regional cartel offices (Landeskartellbehörden)
  • Gas revenue includes significant pass-through component (commodity cost); strip commodity, gas margin contribution is small
  • Geography: Almost entirely Germany, concentrated in NRW (Gelsenkirchen, Bottrop, Bochum, Recklinghausen); modest presence in Brandenburg and Saxony-Anhalt [#I]
  • NRW concentration is both protective (established franchise) and limiting (population declining ~0.3% pa in Ruhrgebiet, per German Federal Statistical Office)
  • Predictability: Revenue visibility is extremely high — water consumption is non-discretionary, tariffs are regulated, concessions are multi-decade; revenue volatility is among the lowest in the German mid-cap universe [#A]
  • Predictability score (subjectively): A-tier for downside protection; F-tier for upside optionality versus Richard's IRR requirement
  • Cyclicality: Effectively acyclical; Gelsenwasser's water revenues were flat to slightly positive through 2008-09 GFC and 2020 COVID — in line with Severn Trent and EVN pattern [#I]

Business Model

  • Competitive advantage: The core advantage is the geographic concession monopoly — no competitor can legally supply water in Gelsenkirchen without the concession [#A]
  • Concession renewal risk is low but not zero: German law (Gemeindeordnung NRW) technically allows municipalities to re-tender; in practice, incumbents win ~95%+ of renewals (sector base rate per BDEW industry data)
  • Customer behaviour: Residential and commercial water customers have zero switching ability (natural monopoly); no churn metric applicable; NPS not publicly disclosed but implicit satisfaction embedded in statutory service obligation [#I]
  • Lock-in: Physical infrastructure (pipes, treatment plants) creates near-permanent asset lock-in; re-entrants would face €bn capital requirements to build parallel networks — equivalent to UK water regulatory asset base moat [#A]
  • Operating leverage: Fixed-cost base is high (~60-65% of opex is fixed); incremental volume at the margin is high-contribution; however, volume growth near-zero means operating leverage is theoretically present but practically irrelevant [#A]

Structural / Secular Demand Trends

  • Secular trend: European water infrastructure investment cycle is accelerating — EU Water Framework Directive compliance, pipe replacement (Germany's water network average age ~40 years), and PFAS remediation create mandatory capex obligations [#I]
  • This drives asset base growth (positive for regulated returns) but also capital consumption (negative for FCF)
  • Volume secular: Per-capita water consumption declining ~0.5% pa in Germany (efficiency trends, demographic mix) — partially offsetting tariff increases on a total revenue basis [#A]
  • Thematic trend: Water scarcity / water quality (PFAS, nitrates) is rising up EU political agenda; regulatory tightening could require capex but also supports tariff increases to fund compliance [#I]

Business Model — Customer Side

  • Customer moat: Residential customers (c.60% of volume) have zero agency — monopoly service, no substitution; industrial customers (c.40%) theoretically have some self-supply optionality but practically negligible given infrastructure cost [#A]
  • Switching cost: Effectively infinite — no mechanism to switch water supplier in a concession area; structural switching cost moat, but it is a sector-wide characteristic, not a Gelsenwasser-specific advantage [#A]
  • Revenue per customer trends: Water revenue per household growing at roughly tariff rate (~2-3% pa) minus volume decline (~0.5% pa) = ~1.5-2.5% pa net; below inflation in most years [#A] ❌

Business Model — Economies of Scale

  • Scale economy: Density-based scale advantages exist in water distribution (fixed pipe network shared across more customers reduces unit cost); Gelsenwasser's NRW urban density is favourable relative to rural German water utilities [#A]
  • However, at ~3.5m people served, Gelsenwasser is not the scale leader in Germany (Berliner Wasserbetriebe, Rheinenergie, GELSENWASSER peers); scale advantages are localised, not systemic
  • Scale moat: Scale moat in existing geography is real; TAM for expansion is constrained by concession geography and political barriers to cross-municipal consolidation in Germany [#A]

Recent Delivery — Last Three Years

  • Recent delivery: Revenue grew from ~€1.0bn (2020) to ~€1.45bn (2023), driven almost entirely by energy price passthrough in gas segment during 2022 energy crisis — water volumes flat to slightly down [#A]
  • EBIT grew from ~€68m (2020) to ~€100m (2023) — nominally strong, but ~€15-20m of EBIT improvement attributable to gas margin windfall during energy crisis, likely non-recurring
  • Dividend increased from €2.80 (2020) to ~€3.40 (2023) — consistent with slow earnings growth; no special dividends or buybacks
  • Internal change: No major strategic pivots in L36M; management commentary (AS: annual report extracts 2022-2024) emphasises infrastructure investment, sustainability, and water security — no growth acceleration thesis [#I]
  • CEO: Dirk Waider (previously CEO since 2015-era; specific tenure confirmation required at ESA depth); no evidence of transformative leadership change
  • No activism, no M&A of scale, no partnerships that would change the earnings trajectory materially

Internal Change — L12M and N3Y

  • Leadership change: No CEO change flagged in AS data in L12M; Aufsichtsrat composition predominantly unchanged (municipal representatives dominate) [#I]
  • Strategic priorities (stated): Infrastructure renewal, digitisation of network operations, expansion of water treatment capacity for regulatory compliance, sustainability/ESG reporting — all incremental, no step-change [#I]
  • M&A: No significant acquisitions or disposals in L36M; no evidence of M&A pipeline signalling growth ambition [#I]

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on revenue model and execution is that the "strong" recent delivery (2022-2023 revenue/EBIT growth) is a gas-crisis artefact — normalise it and the underlying business shows <3% organic growth, essentially zero volume growth, and no management initiative that would change either dynamic; the moat is real but the moat has never and will likely never translate into shareholder value accretion beyond a slow dividend growth [#J]
