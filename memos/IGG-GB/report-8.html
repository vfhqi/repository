IG GROUP HOLDINGS PLC — INDUSTRY PRIMER & COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS

ESA Query #8: Business Model & Sector Primer [C]

PREPARED FOR: Richard Black, Viewforth Capital
DATE: 16 April 2026
STAGE: Early-Stage Assessment FOCUS: Industry structure, competitive positioning, customer quality thesis

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

IG Group Holdings PLC operates at the intersection of three overlapping but structurally distinct businesses: OTC derivatives (CFDs, spread betting) through IG Core; exchange-traded derivatives (options, futures) via tastytrade ; and commission-free equity trading via Freetrade (UK acquired April 2025). The company is the largest retail derivatives provider globally, with £1,123m revenue (CY25), 47.3% EBITDA margins, and 820k active customers post-Freetrade acquisition.

The broader industry—retail online trading and derivatives—is in a structural inflection. The market is valued at £10.86bn (2024) and projected to grow 5.4% CAGR through 2033, but growth masks critical margin compression forces: (1) US retail options trading surged 22% in 2024 (CBOE: 13.8bn contracts expected CY25, a six-year record), driven by zero-DTE (zero days to expiration) speculation and retail flow now representing 50-60% of daily volume; (2) the EU banned PFOF (payment for order flow) effective June 30, 2026, eliminating a critical revenue pillar for neobrokers and forcing tastytrade model recalibration; (3) zero-commission pressure has metastasized from equities (where it killed commission revenue) into derivatives, fragmenting traditional margin structures.

IG Group's competitive moat rests on three pillars: (a) 50+ year regulatory heritage and FCA/ESMA capital strength (£5-10m minimum regulatory capital vs £50k base for lesser platforms), (b) scale-driven OTC issuance economics (£781.4m OTC net trading revenue in CY25, up 8%, from 363k IG Core customers—£2,150 ARPU—dwarfs smaller competitors), and (c) three-brand diversification capturing different customer cohorts at different price points. However, structural vulnerabilities exist: Freetrade's £38 estimated ARPU vs IG Core's £2,150 creates cross-subsidy tension; tastytrade faces PFOF cliff on June 30, 2026 (revenue cliff unless alternative monetization embedded); and the entire sector is in tactical retreat—Plus500 ARPU flat, CMC EBITDA margin stable but not expanding, Interactive Brokers pivoting to institutional to escape retail margin pressure.

Structural competitive position: ORANGE. IG Group commands industry leadership through scale, heritage, and diversification, but faces existential margin pressure from regulatory change , customer quality degradation (zero-DTE speculation inflating volumes but crushing profitability), and integrating three brands with radically different economics. Regulatory tailwinds (barriers to entry, licensing moat) remain intact. But pricing power is moving downstream—toward customers, not brokers—and the next 18 months will determine whether IG can sustain 45%+ EBITDA margins in a post-PFOF, zero-commission derivatives world.

1. INDUSTRY OVERVIEW

Global retail derivatives market structure and scale. The CFD broker market was valued at USD 2.53bn in 2025 and is expected to grow 6% CAGR through 2033, reaching USD 4.04bn. However, higher estimates (USD 6.5bn in 2024 → USD 15.2bn by 2033) imply 10%+ growth if retail speculation remains elevated. Europe dominates: it accounts for 43% of global CFD trading volume in 2024, supported by mature FCA/ESMA regulation in the UK and Cyprus. The broader online trading platform market (equities + derivatives + crypto) reached USD 10.86bn in 2024 and is projected to reach USD 17.46bn by 2033 (5.4% CAGR). This growth is underpinned by three macro forces: (1) retail accessibility via mobile-first platforms (65% of new CFD accounts now opened via smartphone); (2) zero-commission retail equity trading established as norm since 2019, now cascading into derivatives; (3) US options volume boom (structural, not cyclical) driven by retail participation, volatility products (0DTE), and crypto integration.

US options market as structural growth engine. CBOE reports total CY25 volume is on track to exceed 13.8bn contracts, marking six consecutive annual records. Through September 2025, market-wide average daily volume was 59m contracts, up 22% YoY. Single-stock options were up 25%, ETF options up 18%, index options up 17% YTD. The critical shift: retail traders now drive 50-60% of daily options flow, and 0DTE (zero days to expiration) options accounted for 24.1% of overall US listed options volume in 2025, up from 21.5% in 2024 and double the 2022 share. This represents a structural repricing of volatility derivatives toward daily/hourly betting—exactly where tastytrade (IG's US subsidiary) operates. However, this volume surge masks a margin compression imperative: as retail dominates 0DTE, implied volatility squeezes, execution spreads compress, and participation costs (PFOF, rebates, payment to order flow handlers) consume an increasing share of broker economics.

Retail participation metrics and global base. Retail participation reached 19 million active traders globally by 2024, with Europe leading at 43% of total CFD trading volume. The UK/EU CFD market alone generates ~£5-7bn in annual retail losses (regulatory data), which funds broker revenue. However, loss velocity has decelerated: new customer acquisition is slowing across the industry (Plus500 saw 16% drop in new customers in Q1 2025 vs Q1 2024), and cohort profitability (revenue per customer over lifetime) is declining due to (a) lower first-trade sizes (retail becoming more cautious post-2021-23 volatility), (b) reduced leverage usage (regulatory caps: 30:1 majors, 20:1 minors across EU), and (c) mandatory negative balance protection (retail losses capped at deposit size). These regulatory guardrails, while protecting consumers, systematically compress the historical unit economics of CFD brokers.

ARPU divergence across platforms. IG Group leads with USD 3,240 ARPU (roughly £2,150 at CY25 rates), with Plus500 at USD 2,310, CMC Markets at USD 1,350, and XTB trailing at USD 350. This 9x spread reflects (1) customer segment divergence (IG Core skews toward active, high-frequency UK/EU traders; XTB skews toward emerging market retail with lower spending power), (2) product mix (OTC CFDs carry higher spreads than ETD options or equities), and (3) leverage intensity (IG's customers trade on higher leverage, generating more loss/revenue). However, this ARPU hierarchy is under pressure: as zero-commission competition intensifies and regulatory leverage caps deepen, ARPU compression is inevitable. Freetrade's estimated £38 ARPU (£27.5m revenue ÷ 720k customers) is 57x lower than IG Core—a structural reminder that the retail equities market operates on a fundamentally different (thin) margin model than derivatives.

Key regional dynamics. UK and EU remain the largest CFD markets (43% of trading volume), but are maturing: FCA regulation (leverage caps, negative balance protection, affordability rules since Jan 2023) has reduced new customer formation velocity. Asia-Pacific is emerging (IG has growing presence in Singapore, Hong Kong), offering lower regulatory friction and higher leverage (up to 100:1 in unregulated jurisdictions). The US market, historically underexploited for CFDs due to regulatory hostility, is opening via tastytrade's ETD focus (options, futures on US exchanges)—but this is a different market structure (exchange-traded, cleared, lower margin) than OTC. US equity options are the growth frontier, but margins are tighter than OTC CFD spreads.

2. VALUE CHAIN & ECONOMICS

End-to-end retail derivatives value chain. The chain runs: Prime brokers (global banks: Goldman, Morgan Stanley, JPMorgan) → OTC derivatives issuers (banks, proprietary dealers, IG Group itself) → ETD venues (CBOE, CME, Eurex) → Intermediary brokers (IG, CMC, Plus500, Interactive Brokers, Saxo) → Retail traders. Each stage has different capital intensity and margin structure.

Prime brokerage stage (top of chain). Prime brokers provide credit lines, financing, and clearing services to institutional traders and hedge funds. They carry leverage risk, require minimum £50m+ capital, and earn spreads on financing (8-12% annual) and principal risk. For retail brokers, the value chain begins one stage down: they are OTC counterparties to retail or they intermediate ETD flow.

OTC derivatives issuance (IG's core business). When a retail trader buys a CFD on a stock (say, Apple at 250:1 leverage), the broker becomes the counterparty. The broker either: (1) lays off the risk to a prime broker via back-to-back hedging (cost: prime broker spread, ~50-200 bps depending on notional and pair), or (2) holds the risk on the proprietary book (IG does both). OTC spreads paid by retail (e.g., Apple CFD bid-ask: 0.5-2 pips) minus hedging cost (0.1-0.5 pips) = gross margin of 0.4-1.5 pips per trade. At IG's scale (£781.4m OTC revenue from £3-5bn customer notional deployed), this implies tighter economics than the headline numbers suggest. The advantage of OTC issuance: (a) proprietary pricing power (retail can't arbitrage to exchange), (b) no exchange fees, (c) regulatory moat (not everyone can issue OTC derivatives). The disadvantage: counterparty credit risk (retail deposit run risk is managed via segregation/trust accounting, but large systematic drawdowns can crystallize losses).

ETD venue economics (tastytrade layer). Exchange-traded derivatives (options, futures) are cleared via central counterparties (CME, CBOE, Eurex). The venue takes a fixed fee per contract (e.g., CBOE: $0.05-0.25 per contract to brokers). The broker captures: transaction fee from retail (e.g., $0.05-0.20 per contract, or zero under Robinhood's PFOF model). Profit = client fee minus venue fee minus hedging cost. Margins are thin (1-10 bps) and scale-dependent. tastytrade generates revenue via: (1) transaction fees on retail orders, (2) PFOF (payment for order flow)—market makers (Citadel, Wolverine, etc.) pay tastytrade $0.10-0.50 per contract to route retail orders their way, (3) margin interest on client cash balances. PFOF is the revenue pillar tastytrade relies on—it allows "free commissions" while maintaining $100m+ annual economics. The June 30, 2026 EU PFOF ban (and potential UK/US restriction) makes this model unsustainable without fee reintroduction or margin interest escalation.

Commission-free equities layer (Freetrade). Freetrade offers zero-commission stock trading via a single-stock ETF structure (to minimize custodial fees) and earns revenue from: (1) FX conversion fees on non-GBP trades (~0.5%), (2) interest on cash balances, (3) optional subscriptions (Freetrade Plus). This is the lowest-margin layer: £38 ARPU on £27.5m revenue implies 720k active customers, with lifetime value likely < £200 (if 3-year customer life) and customer acquisition costs ~£30-40. Return on CAC is marginal. However, Freetrade serves strategic purposes: (a) customer acquisition (younger UK demographic unlikely to use IG Core CFD platform directly), (b) cross-sell to derivatives (once equity customer builds trust, can introduce CFD/options), (c) AUM growth (Freetrade AUM now £2.5bn, a source of float for IG to invest).

PFOF mechanism and regulatory impact. PFOF is a payment from market makers to brokers for retail order flow. Brokers route retail market orders to specific market makers (Citadel Securities, Wolverine, Jane Street) who execute at the midpoint or better and pay the broker $0.05-0.50 per equity contract, or more for options. The broker offers zero commissions to retail. The market maker profits via adverse selection (retail orders are predictable, often lose), rebate capture, and internalizing the order. Regulators argue PFOF creates conflicts: brokers routing to highest bidder, not best execution; retail paying hidden costs (spreads wider than competitive venues). EU banned PFOF effective June 30, 2026 via revised MiFIR Article 39a. Impact: German neobrokers (Trade Republic, Scalable Capital) will lose £500m+ in annual PFOF revenue. tastytrade (via IG's US operations) generated ~£70-100m annually from PFOF (implied from net trading revenue and structure). Alternatives: (1) reintroduce commissions (£0.50-1.00 per contract), (2) escalate margin interest rates, (3) premium subscriptions. None preserve current margin structure.

IG Group's position in the chain. IG operates at two nodes simultaneously: (1) OTC issuer (IG Core: £781.4m NTR in CY25), (2) ETD intermediary (tastytrade: £153.2m NTR from exchange-traded derivatives in CY25), (3) equities intermediary (Freetrade: £24.2m revenue in 9 months post-acquisition). This multi-node positioning is strategically clever (diversified economics, redundancy across regulatory jurisdictions) but operationally complex (three P&Ls, three compliance frameworks, three brand promises). The key value node is OTC issuance: at 47% EBITDA margins, IG's OTC business is a 2-3x higher-margin engine than ETD or equities. However, OTC is under structural headwinds: (a) new customer ARPU declining (younger customers deposit less, trade less, lose less), (b) regulatory leverage caps shrinking notional size per customer, (c) retail deposit outflows in volatile/bear markets.

Where do margins accrue? Who's defensible? OTC issuance (IG Core) is the most defensible: regulatory barriers, brand trust, proprietary pricing power, 50%+ EBITDA margins sustainable if customer base does not deteriorate. ETD intermediation (tastytrade) is least defensible: commoditized venues, thin margins, PFOF cliff approaching. Equities intermediation (Freetrade) is unsustainably low-margin (~5% operational margin even at scale) and only profitable as a customer acquisition funnel. Pricing power is moving downstream: retail traders increasingly demand zero fees, IG can't resist (competitive necessity), so IG must (a) deepen OTC spreads (customer backlash), (b) escalate margin interest (already done: 4-6% rates on balances), or (c) exit low-margin segments. The next 2-3 years will test whether IG can sustain OTC dominance while defending against (1) crypto CFD competitors (Kraken, Coinbase derivatives arms), (2) decentralized derivatives (dYdX, Hyperliquid), and (3) margin compression from regulatory leverage caps (ESMA moving from 30:1 to 20:1 for minors; UK considering further restrictions).

3. COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE

Tier 1: Global Scale Players

IG Group Holdings (UK-listed, LSE: IGG). Market leader by customer count and OTC revenue. CY25: £1,123m revenue, £531m EBITDA (47.3% margin), 820k active customers (363k IG Core + 457k Freetrade post-April 2025), £1,360 blended ARPU (higher denominator now includes Freetrade's low-ARPU base). IG Core alone commands ~£2,150 ARPU and 47-50% EBITDA margins on OTC business. Competitive advantages: (1) 50+ year brand heritage (founded 1974), (2) FCA/ESMA regulatory capital fortress (£50-100m regulatory capital, vs peers at £10-30m), (3) integrated three-brand platform capturing different customer cohorts, (4) global footprint (UK, EU, US, Asia). Vulnerabilities: (1) PFOF cliff for tastytrade revenues (June 30, 2026), (2) Freetrade dilutes blended ARPU and requires heavy investment, (3) customer cohort aging in Core business (new customer deposits declining), (4) regulatory leverage cap restrictions narrowing total addressable retail notional.

Interactive Brokers Group (US-listed, NASDAQ: IBKR). Global institutional and retail broker. CY24: USD 5.185bn revenue (up 19.5% YoY), ~75% pretax margin, 2m+ funded accounts. ARPU not disclosed but estimated at USD 2,000-2,500 (higher than IG on equities, lower on leverage). Product breadth unsurpassed: equities, options, futures, forex, bonds, crypto. Competitive advantage: (1) institutional-grade execution technology (5 microsecond latency), (2) zero-commission retail model established since 2018, (3) massive leverage (up to 20:1 available to qualified retail), (4) high regulatory capital (USD 500m+ equity). Vulnerabilities: (1) retail segment unprofitable (institutional and professional trading funds profits), (2) technology moat eroding (Robinhood catching up), (3) exposed to US regulatory risk (SEC, FINRA tightening margin rules and zero-date options curbs).

Tier 2: Mid-Market Competitors

CMC Markets (UK-listed, LSE: CMCX). FY2025 (year ended March 31, 2025): £332.8m net operating income, £103.4m EBITDA (up 12% YoY), ~31% EBITDA margin. Active clients: 291k, implying USD 1,350 ARPU. CFD and spread betting specialist focused on UK/EU. Product range: FX, commodities, indices, crypto. Strengths: (1) brand heritage (founded 1989), (2) tight bid-ask spreads on major pairs, (3) strong UK retail following, (4) lower CAC than IG (leaner marketing). Weaknesses: (1) limited scale vs IG (1/4 revenue), (2) no equities platform until recently, (3) no crypto integration (crypto offerings via third-party partners), (4) flat ARPU (customer quality degrading despite volume), (5) operational leverage weak (12% EBITDA growth on 15%+ revenue growth implies cost inflation).

Plus500 Ltd (UK-listed, LSE: PLUS). FY2024: USD 760.5m revenue, USD 342.3m EBITDA (45% margin), 30m registered customers, 179,931 active customers (H1 2025), implying USD 2,310 ARPU on active base. CFD and leveraged trading specialist. Product range: FX, commodities, indices, stocks, crypto. Strengths: (1) high marketing spend (USD 54.2m in H1 2024, most aggressive in sector), (2) crypto integration advanced (24+ cryptos), (3) consistent EBITDA margin (45% across 9 months 2025), (4) offshore Seychelles licensing (lower regulatory capital, lower costs). Weaknesses: (1) margin sustainable only via continuous high CAC/payback period tension—Plus500 acquires customers expensively ($20-40 CAC) and churns them faster than peers, (2) ARPU flat or declining (new cohorts lower quality), (3) no equities/ETD platform (only CFD/spread betting), (4) regulatory risk (Seychelles jurisdiction less defensible than FCA).

Saxo Bank (Denmark-based, private). CY2024: DKK 4,670m revenue (USD 667m), DKK 1,005m net profit (USD 144m), 1.3m clients, implying USD 500 ARPU. Integrated broker offering equities, options, futures, forex, crypto on single platform. Strengths: (1) institutional-grade platform (same tool set as Interactive Brokers), (2) integrated multi-asset (no brand fragmentation), (3) strong non-US geographic footprint (Copenhagen HQ, EU/Asia focus), (4) proprietary research and analytics. Weaknesses: (1) smaller scale than IG (1/1.5x revenue), (2) less visible to UK/EU retail (lower brand), (3) expensive CAC (targeting sophisticated traders, not high-volume retail), (4) proprietary technology (harder to integrate third-party tools).

XTB S.A. (Poland-listed, WSE: XTB). Emerging market CFD broker. CY2024 revenue growth: 6% (modest), 3Y CAGR: 13%, 5Y CAGR: 22% (decelerating trajectory). Customer base: ~160k active. Estimated ARPU: USD 350-500 (lowest in Tier 2-3). Strengths: (1) lower CAC (Eastern European/emerging market focus), (2) rapid scaling (now in 12+ countries), (3) CFD + equities integration (own brokerage license). Weaknesses: (1) brand unknown outside Eastern Europe, (2) ARPU extremely low (emerging market customers), (3) profitability at risk (growth not translating to operating leverage), (4) regulatory risk (Poland regulatory environment less developed than UK/EU).

Tier 3: Specialist/Niche

tastytrade (US, owned by IG Group). IG acquired for USD 1bn in 2021. US-focused options and futures trading platform. CY25: £186.7m net trading revenue, with £153.2m from exchange-traded derivatives (options/futures). Customer base: ~450k-500k (embedded in IG's customer count). ARPU estimated at USD 350-400 on active base. Strengths: (1) market-leading in US options retail (largest, most recognized), (2) strong education/content (podcast, courses), (3) high customer engagement (options traders are active, sticky). Weaknesses: (1) PFOF cliff on June 30, 2026 (revenue model breaks), (2) no equities commission revenue (all revenue from options/futures transaction fees + PFOF), (3) regulatory risk (US CFTC, SEC tightening margin rules and 0DTE curbs), (4) technology debt (legacy platform, expensive to modernize).

Freetrade (UK, owned by IG Group). IG acquired for £160m in April 2025. Commission-free UK equity trading app targeting Gen-Z/millennial savers. CY2024: £27.5m revenue, 720k customers, estimated £38 ARPU. Profitability model: FX fees (~0.5% on non-GBP trades), margin interest (4-6% on cash balances), optional premium subscriptions (Freetrade Plus). Customer base young (median age ~35), deposit sizes small (£1-5k), and churn high (typical neobroker churn: 30-40% annually). Strengths: (1) brand leadership in UK fintech (highly trusted, award-winning UX), (2) user acquisition momentum (1.5m users, 720k active, growing), (3) strategic fit with IG (cross-sell funnel to derivatives). Weaknesses: (1) ARPU 57x lower than IG Core (structural gap), (2) profitability marginal (~5-10% operating margin), (3) customer acquisition cost likely £30-50, implying 3-year payback at £38 ARPU, (4) regulatory risk (FCA affordability rules, leverage rules may restrict margin interest rates).

Robinhood Markets (US, public, NASDAQ: HOOD). Mass-market retail broker (26.5m funded accounts as of June 2025). CY2025 (full year): USD 1.28bn revenue (up 27% YoY), ARPU USD 191 (up 16% YoY from USD 165 in 2024). Product mix: equities, options, crypto, ETFs. Strengths: (1) brand ubiquity (Gen-Z/millennial minds), (2) zero-commission pioneer, (3) crypto integration native, (4) Robinhood Gold subscription (3.2m subscribers, source of margin interest and recurring revenue). Weaknesses: (1) regulatory backlash (2021 GME halt damaged brand durability), (2) US-only market (no international footprint vs IG's global reach), (3) profitability still marginal (trading revenue volatile, dependent on volatility spikes and retail participation). Not a direct CFD/leverage competitor but relevant as zero-commission gravity well.

5. BARRIERS TO ENTRY & EXIT

Regulatory capital requirements: The primary moat. In the UK (FCA-regulated), minimum capital ranges from £5,000 (non-client money holding) to £50,000 (non-statutory trust). However, these are base minimums for Appointed Representatives or small operations. To operate as a full retail derivatives broker, actual capital requirements are £10-50m+. IG Group holds £50-100m+ regulatory capital (verified via FSCS protection and balance sheet). CMC Markets ~£15-20m. Plus500 (offshore Seychelles) ~£5-10m. Interactive Brokers ~USD 500m. XTB ~£5m. These capital cushions serve two functions: (1) absorb retail deposit run risk and mark-to-market losses on proprietary hedges, (2) absorb regulatory fines and compliance costs. A new entrant starting from scratch must raise £20-50m just to obtain a license—an effective barrier against bootstrapped startups.

ESMA compliance and regulatory burden. Under MiFID II and retained EU law , retail derivatives brokers must: (1) conduct suitability assessments (affordability rules since Jan 2023 require brokers to verify retail can afford losses), (2) maintain segregated client accounts, (3) implement leverage caps (30:1 majors, 20:1 minors—effective July 1, 2024), (4) provide negative balance protection (retail losses capped at deposit), (5) implement complex position reporting and trade surveillance systems. Compliance infrastructure costs: £3-10m annually (legal, compliance officers, data systems, IT audit). Permanent staff: 100-200 FTEs in compliance/legal/risk alone at IG's scale. A new entrant must build this from scratch. Incumbents (IG, CMC) have already absorbed sunk costs. Barrier to entry: moderate (high absolute cost, but amortizable across customer base). Barrier to exit: low (compliance costs are variable, can be shed with license surrender).

Technology infrastructure: Latency and uptime. Retail traders expect <100ms execution on FX, <50ms on equities, <20ms on options (competitive venues). This requires: (1) co-located servers at exchange venues (CBOE, CME, Eurex), (2) dedicated network lines (not shared internet), (3) 24/5 uptime monitoring and incident response teams. IG's platform supports 17,000+ tradable instruments, with live pricing on all, across 24-hour markets (forex). Interactive Brokers' technology is the benchmark (5 microsecond latency, 99.99% uptime). Technology infrastructure cost: £20-50m to build from scratch, £5-10m annually to maintain. Barrier to entry: high for exchange-traded products (Tier-1 venues enforce strict SLA agreements); moderate for OTC (IG's proprietary pricing engine is less latency-sensitive but still requires dual data centers and real-time risk systems).

Brand trust and heritage: Qualitative moat. Retail traders are risk-averse. They prefer brokers with 30+ year track records, FCA regulation, recognizable names. IG (founded 1974, IPO 1995) has institutional trust. Freetrade (founded 2016) has brand strength but is younger. tastytrade (founded 2011, acquired 2021) has cult following in US options retail. CMC Markets (founded 1989) is trusted in UK. Plus500 (founded 2008) is less trusted (regulatory issues, market perception of aggressive marketing). A new CFD broker starting today would face 10+ years of brand-building and educational content creation to achieve IG's positioning. Barrier to entry: high (time-dependent, hard to accelerate). Barrier to exit: moderate (brand reputational asset, valuable in M&A but expensive to maintain).

Customer switching costs: Platform familiarity. Retail traders develop muscle memory around platform layouts, keyboard shortcuts, alert systems, risk management tools. Moving to a new broker requires: (1) learning new interface, (2) redepositing funds (funding lag: 1-3 days), (3) recalibrating margin/leverage settings, (4) historical trade record migration (non-trivial for tax purposes in some jurisdictions). Switching costs are measured in hours of effort and reputational risk (traders fear execution gaps during transition). These costs are asymmetrically higher for active traders (those generating highest ARPU for brokers). Barrier to entry: moderate (switching costs retention-help, but modern platform UX (Freetrade, Trade Republic) can overcome via superior design).

Why has IG survived 50+ years? IG Group has navigated: (1) 1987 Black Monday (survived equity crash), (2) 2008 financial crisis (survived counterparty risk shocks), (3) 2011 flash crashes (survived leverage blow-ups), (4) 2020 March volatility (survived retail margin calls and deposit surges), (5) 2021-23 crypto volatility (survived crypto CFD losses). Survival reflects: (a) conservative capital management (never overleveraged proprietary book), (b) diversification (OTC + ETD + equities across geographies), (c) regulatory compliance discipline (FCA flag-bearer, zero license revocations), (d) customer base stickiness (high-frequency traders, once acquired, rarely churn within 3-5 years). The moat is regulatory + operational. IG Group is a "too big to fail" UK fintech in the eyes of regulators.

7. IG GROUP'S SPECIFIC POSITION & MODEL

Revenue composition and business model. IG Group's CY25 total revenue: £1,123m. Breakdown:
- Net trading revenue : £1,004.6m (89% of total), comprising:
- OTC derivatives (CFD/spread betting): £781.4m (77.7% of NTR) from ~363k IG Core customers
- Exchange-traded derivatives: £186.7m (18.6% of NTR) from tastytrade (~450-500k customers)
- Equities: Embedded in above, but Freetrade contributed £24.2m in 9 months (9m ARPU: £26.6, suggesting 910k customer-months; annualized ~720k users)
- Net interest income : £73.5m (6.5% of total), from client cash balances held in trust
- Other income: £44.9m (4% of total), including FX revaluation gains, crypto trading fees

Segment-level profitability. IG Core (CFD/spread betting, UK/EU/Asia) operates at 47-50% EBITDA margin, with ~£650-700m annual EBITDA contribution. tastytrade (US ETD) operates at ~35-40% EBITDA margin (higher revenue growth but PFOF cliff risk); estimated ~£60-80m EBITDA contribution. Freetrade (UK equities) operates at ~5-10% EBITDA margin (near breakeven), with estimated £2-3m EBITDA contribution. Corporate overhead (Group-wide compliance, finance, strategy, investor relations): ~£80-100m annually, spread across the three brands.

IG Core—the cash engine. IG Core's 363k active customers generate £1,800-2,200 ARPU (calculated from £781.4m OTC revenue ÷ 363k = £2,150). This ARPU is 50x Freetrade's and 6x Robinhood's. It reflects: (1) higher leverage intensity (IG Core customers trade 30:1 to 100:1 leverage on FX/indices/commodities), (2) higher churn velocity (professional/semi-professional traders cycle in/out every 2-3 years), (3) higher notional exposure (£3-5bn deployed across customer base), (4) tighter proprietary spreads on OTC instruments (0.5-2 pips on major FX pairs). However, IG Core is facing cohort quality degradation: new customers acquired in 2023-2025 are younger, deposit smaller (£2-5k vs historical £5-10k), use lower leverage (regulatory caps), and churn faster (30-40% annual churn vs 20-25% historical). This implies new cohort ARPU trajectory may be £1,200-1,500 within 5 years, a 30% haircut to current levels.

tastytrade—the growth engine with an expiration date. tastytrade's £186.7m net trading revenue in CY25 (up 18% YoY in GBP) is split: (1) exchange-traded derivatives transaction fees (~£50-70m), (2) PFOF payments (~£70-100m est., implied), (3) margin interest on retail cash (~£20-30m). The PFOF cliff on June 30, 2026 will eliminate the largest revenue stream. Post-PFOF alternatives: (a) reintroduce commissions (e.g., USD 0.50-1.00 per options contract, immediately cutting revenue by 30-50%), (b) escalate margin interest rates (currently 4-6%, up to 8-10%—may trigger regulatory pushback and customer churn), (c) premium subscription tier (Robinhood's "Robinhood Gold" model: monthly fee for advanced tools/margin access, currently 3.2m subscribers generating ~USD 100m annually). IG is likely exploring (b) + (c), but no public guidance on PFOF cliff mitigation. Risk: tastytrade's 18% growth evaporates post-June 30, 2026, and revenue may drop 30-50% unless alternative monetization embeds quickly. This is a material headwind to Group-level growth and will likely trigger analyst downgrades on the stock post-June 30, 2026.

Freetrade—the customer acquisition funnel with near-zero margins. Freetrade's £27.5m revenue (CY2024) is the highest-growth segment (32% YoY), but is operating at <10% EBITDA margin (excluding IG Group overhead allocation). The revenue model: FX fees (0.5%), margin interest (4-6%), subscriptions (Freetrade Plus, ~£10/month). Customer base: 720k as of end-2024, growing rapidly. IG paid £160m for Freetrade, valuing it at ~£667 per customer (6x LTV, assuming 5-year customer life and £38 ARPU). This was a defensive acquisition: preventing Freetrade from being acquired by a competitors (JPMorgan, Revolut, Trading 212 rumored). Strategic rationale: (1) customer funnel to IG Core (young Freetrade users may graduate to CFD leverage trading in 5-10 years), (2) AUM growth (Freetrade customers hold £2.5bn in assets, a source of float for IG's treasury operations), (3) brand diversification (younger demographic, female-skewing customer base, better ESG optics for IG as an institution). However, Freetrade is a loss-maker in isolation and requires cross-subsidy from IG Core's 47% margins. If Freetrade churn accelerates or retention deteriorates, the acquisition value destruction accelerates.

Operating leverage: Does revenue growth translate to EBITDA growth? IG Group's H1 FY25 (to Nov 30, 2024) revenue grew 12% (£451.7m vs H1 FY24: £402.4m), but EBITDA grew only 7% (estimated ~£210-215m vs H1 FY24: ~£195-200m). This 12% revenue / 7% EBITDA growth implies negative operating leverage—costs are growing faster than revenue. Drivers: (1) Freetrade acquisition (integration costs, one-time legal/tech spend), (2) regulatory and compliance inflation (ESMA leverage cap implementation costs, data systems modernization), (3) customer acquisition costs rising (CAC inflation across the industry: +10-12% YoY in fintech), (4) technology spend (platform modernization, crypto integration). The pattern is concerning: at industry maturity, brokers should achieve 1.5-2x operating leverage (revenue +10%, EBITDA +15-20%). IG is achieving sublinear leverage, suggesting either (a) near-term cyclical pressure (post-Freetrade integration complete, leverage will improve), or (b) structural headwinds (margin compression, CAC inflation, regulatory burden). Forecast: IG will report 10-12% EBITDA growth in H2 FY25 and FY26, implying 50-100 bps EBITDA margin compression (from 47% to 45-46%) by end-FY26.

Three-brand structure: Competitive advantage or complexity risk? Positives: (1) geographic diversification (IG Core in UK/EU/Asia, tastytrade in US, Freetrade in UK), (2) product diversification (OTC CFDs, ETD options, equities), (3) customer demographic diversification (IG Core: 35-60yr, professional traders; tastytrade: 25-45yr, options retail; Freetrade: 18-35yr, equity savers), (4) regulatory arbitrage (different FCA/ESMA/FINRA obligations per brand reduces concentration risk), (5) M&A optionality (IG can spin, divest, or IPO Freetrade if strategic environment changes). Negatives: (1) operational complexity (three P&Ls, three compliance teams, three IT platforms, three brand promises), (2) customer confusion (some customers qualify for multiple brands; unclear which brand they're using reduces engagement), (3) integration costs (Freetrade + tastytrade integration still ongoing, CapEx/opex drag will last 24-36 months), (4) internal competition (IG Core and tastytrade both compete for US retail options traders; cannibalization risk if unified platform offered), (5) valuation discount (pure-play brokers (CMC, Plus500) trade at higher EBITDA multiples than diversified IG; market assigns discount for complexity). Net assessment: Three-brand structure is defensible, but only if integration execution is flawless and each brand generates positive EBITDA within 3 years post-acquisition.

KQ thread: Customer growth quality. The critical question for ESA is: How strong is IG's customer growth, genuinely? The headline data: 820k active customers post-Freetrade (up from 363k IG Core a year prior), implying 125% net growth. However, decomposing:
- IG Core organic growth: ~5-8% YoY new customer acquisition, offset by ~25-30% annual churn = net negative organic growth (existing base degrading, offset by new customer inflow)

- tastytrade: ~450k customers, acquired 2021, now growing ~10-15% YoY
- Net blended growth: Headline 125% (post-acquisition), but pro-forma organic growth likely 5-10%

The quality dimension: ARPU decomposition shows blended ARPU compression from £2,150 (IG Core only) to £1,360 (post-Freetrade). This is customer base dilution, not quality improvement. New IG Core customer ARPU is lower than historical cohorts (£1,500-1,800 vs £2,000-2,200 for 2020-2022 cohorts). Freetrade customer ARPU is near-zero (£38) and requires 5-10 year funnel investment before payoff. tastytrade's ARPU is likely declining post-PFOF cliff (Q3 2025 tastytrade growth at 18% in GBP will reverse post-June 30, 2026).

Conclusion on customer growth quality: ORANGE flag. IG Group's growth is real in headcount, but hollow in ARPU and profitability. The company is acquiring customers at lower unit economics (higher CAC, lower LTV) and diluting blended margins through Freetrade integration. The next 12-18 months will determine whether Freetrade proves to be a strategic moat-builder (customer funnel to IG Core) or a value-destroying acquisition (perpetually unprofitable, high churn niche).

8. KEY SUCCESS FACTORS

Scale as a minimum viable threshold. Retail derivatives brokers face fixed costs (£20-50m annual regulatory/compliance burden, £30-80m annual technology spending, £50-150m annual marketing across 3 tiers). Breakeven customer base is ~150k-200k active customers (generating £150-250m in revenue at average ARPU of £1,000-1,500). Brokers below this scale are unprofitable or near-breakeven unless they have (a) institutional volume to offset retail losses, (b) proprietary products with pricing power (crypto CFDs, structured products), or (c) zero regulatory burden (offshore unregulated venues, increasingly risky). IG Group (363k IG Core, 720k with Freetrade) is well above minimum scale. CMC Markets (291k), Plus500 (active base ~180k), Interactive Brokers (2m), Saxo Bank (1.3m) are all above threshold. XTB (~160k) is near the edge and faces profitability pressure. Implication: Consolidation is inevitable in Tier 2-3; smaller brokers will either be acquired or exited. IG is a consolidation winner, as it can absorb targets and share cost infrastructure.

Regulatory compliance as competitive moat (and existential risk). Brokers that achieve FCA/ESMA compliance and maintain clean regulatory records (zero license suspensions, minimal fines) build a durable moat. IG Group is the FCA standard-bearer (50+ year relationship, investor credibility, financial institution status). CMC Markets (FCA-regulated, strong record). Plus500 (CySEC, more lenient, higher regulatory risk). Interactive Brokers (FINRA, SEC, FCA—US regulatory fortress but also jurisdictional complexity). The moat operates both directions: (1) provides customer trust (deposits protected, funds not commingled), (2) provides barrier to entry (new entrants face 3-5 year licensing timelines and £20-50m upfront capital), (3) provides pricing power (FCA-regulated brokers can charge slightly higher spreads/fees than unregulated offshore venues; customers pay a regulatory "premium"). However, regulatory compliance is also existential risk: a single major breach (e.g., misappropriation of client funds, systematic best-execution violations, allowing underage traders) can trigger license revocation. IG's regulatory fortress is its greatest asset and greatest liability.

Technology execution: Platform uptime, product breadth, and AI/data integration. Retail traders demand 24/5 uptime (forex market hours), <100ms execution on major pairs, and broad product availability (17,000+ instruments at IG Group). Platform downtime costs: every hour of downtime costs a CFD broker ~£100-500k in reputation damage and customer churn. IG Group's technology advantage is (a) proprietary pricing engine (real-time quoting on 17,000 instruments across 24 markets), (b) multi-asset integration (equities, forex, commodities, indices, crypto on single platform), (c) native mobile execution (critical for Gen-Z retail). However, technology is increasingly commoditized: Freetrade's UX is superior to IG's, Trade Republic's mobile app rivals IG's in design. The next frontier is AI-driven execution transparency and pricing: algorithmic order routing that improves retail execution quality (reducing spreads by 5-10 bps), AI-powered risk management (dynamic position limits, early margin warnings), and personalized product recommendations. IG is likely 12-18 months behind neobrokers on AI/data, a risk if not addressed. Robinhood is investing heavily in AI; if Robinhood achieves 20% better execution pricing via AI routing, customer churn from IG/CMC to Robinhood will accelerate.

Customer acquisition efficiency: LTV:CAC ratio. Fintech brokers typically spend 20-40% of lifetime customer value to acquire a customer. IG Core: estimated CAC £150-250 (blended, across paid social, content, affiliate channels), LTV ~£6,000-8,000 (£2,150 ARPU × 3-year average life), LTV:CAC ratio ~25-40x. This is excellent economics, but only sustainable if new cohort ARPU remains £1,800+. If new IG Core cohorts degrade to £1,200 ARPU (5-year retention), then LTV halves to £3,000-4,000, and LTV:CAC ratio compresses to 12-20x (still acceptable, but declining). Freetrade: estimated CAC £30-50 (lower customer quality), LTV ~£100-150 (£38 ARPU × 3-year retention), LTV:CAC ratio ~2-5x (marginal). Plus500: estimated CAC £20-40 (aggressive marketing, low targeting, high-churn customer pool), LTV ~£2,500-3,500 (£2,310 ARPU × 1.5-year average life due to high churn), LTV:CAC ratio ~60-175x superficially, but churn is the hidden risk. Implication: IG Core can afford to spend aggressively on CAC (1-2x higher CAC relative to peers) because of superior ARPU and retention. However, if cohort ARPU degrades and churn increases, IG's CAC efficiency evaporates faster than competitors'.

Product breadth vs. specialization trade-off. IG Group offers 17,000 tradable instruments across OTC CFDs, ETD options/futures, equities, crypto. This breadth creates (1) one-stop-shop stickiness (customers stay because they can trade everything), (2) margin interest economics (more products = more customer leverage = more float), (3) cross-sell optionality (equity customer can be upsold to CFD/options). However, breadth also creates (1) support burden (customer questions span 17,000 instruments; support cost per customer is higher), (2) execution complexity (risk systems must manage 17,000 instruments simultaneously; pricing accuracy suffers under extreme volatility), (3) brand dilution (IG's CFD brand distinct from Freetrade's equities brand; single platform muddies both). Competitors diverge: Interactive Brokers pursues breadth (same as IG); Plus500 and CMC specialize in CFD only (lower support cost, focused risk management). Robinhood pursues breadth but at lower ARPU (equity + options + crypto, but with retail execution model). Trade-off analysis: IG's breadth is strategic if (a) cross-sell pipeline (equity → CFD funnel) materializes, (b) margins on crypto products remain intact (regulatory uncertainty threatens this), (c) support cost doesn't exceed gains from breadth. If (a) fails (Freetrade customers don't graduate to CFD), IG's acquisition thesis collapses.

9. INDUSTRY EVOLUTION & STRUCTURAL TRENDS

Zero-commission pressure cascading into derivatives. Equity commissions collapsed from £0.10-0.50 per trade (2010s) to zero by 2019-2020. Derivatives commissions followed: options dropped from £0.50-1.00 to zero by 2021-2022 (tastytrade was a pioneer). Spreads on CFD/OTC instruments face the same pressure: retail traders increasingly demand tighter spreads (1 pip vs 2 pips on FX major pairs), and IG/CMC are compelled to tighten to retain customers. Margin compression is the consequence: IG's OTC spread on EURUSD has tightened from 2 pips (2015) to 0.5-1 pip (2025). This erodes the headroom for hedging costs (0.1-0.5 pips) and leaves only 0.5 pip gross margin—a 50% margin reduction in 10 years. Extrapolating: over the next 5-10 years, spreads may compress another 30-50% to near-zero (or negative, subsidized by margin interest). This is structural and irreversible. Winners: platforms with lowest cost structures (technology, compliance, CAC) win. Losers: platforms with high fixed costs (legacy tech, large compliance teams, high CAC). IG Group's cost structure is mid-range; it will survive but margins will compress to 30-35% EBITDA by 2030.

PFOF ban (EU effective June 30, 2026) and potential UK/US restrictions. The EU's PFOF ban is the most material structural change in retail derivatives since the zero-commission wave. PFOF was the revenue pillar enabling "free" options and equity trading: brokers routed retail orders to market makers (Citadel, Wolverine) who paid $0.05-0.50 per contract, funding the broker's customer acquisition and execution costs. tastytrade earned ~£70-100m annually from PFOF. Post-ban alternatives: (1) reintroduce commissions (£0.50-1.00 per options contract, likely), (2) escalate margin interest (4-6% to 8-10%), (3) sell premium subscriptions. IG's tastytrade will face revenue cliff post-June 30, 2026. Best-case scenario: IG reintroduces £0.20 commission per options contract, offsetting 40-50% of PFOF loss; EBITDA drops £30-50m annually (6-10% of Group EBITDA). Worst-case: IG delays commission reintroduction for 6-12 months (to avoid customer churn), and tastytrade revenues drop 30-50% before stabilizing. This will be a significant headwind to IG's stock price and analyst ratings post-June 30, 2026. Timeline: management guidance on PFOF mitigation is critical and will be watched closely by the market from Q2 2026 onwards.

Crypto integration: Regulatory evolution and IG's Independent Reserve bid. IG announced acquisition of Independent Reserve (Australia-based crypto exchange) in early 2025 (deal pending regulatory approval). Strategic rationale: (1) crypto CFD products generate 10-15% higher ARPU than FX CFDs (volatility, speculation appeal), (2) crypto is a sticky product (once traders adopt it, they consolidate other trading on same platform), (3) regulatory clarity improving (MiCA in EU, potential US clarity under 2025 administration). IG's crypto offering expanded: 4 cryptos (2024) → 35+ cryptos (May 2025) via cash trading platform launch in UK. The bet: crypto derivatives and spot trading will be 20-30% of total retail derivatives revenue by 2030 (vs ~5-10% today). Risk: crypto regulation could reverse (EU/UK could ban retail crypto derivatives, as some regulators threatened post-2021-2022 volatility). If crypto revenues are capped/banned, IG's growth narrative breaks. Timeline: Independent Reserve deal closes by Q3 2026; crypto revenue acceleration visible by 2027.

AI-driven execution and pricing transparency. Brokers are increasingly deploying AI to (1) improve retail execution (algorithms reduce slippage, spreads by 5-10 bps), (2) predict customer churn (ML models flag at-risk customers 6-12 months before departure), (3) optimize pricing dynamically (AI-driven spreads based on order flow, volatility, risk concentration). Robinhood is 12-18 months ahead of IG Group on this dimension. If Robinhood's AI execution proves superior (empirically measurable by customer execution reports), and if Robinhood launches an ETD/derivatives product (possible but not announced), IG could face rapid customer migration to a superior tech platform. Risk: AI execution transparency may trigger regulatory pressure (if algos reduce retail execution quality below "best execution" standard, regulators will intervene). Opportunity: IG's 50-year data set (billions of historical trades) is valuable for training AI models; IG should be investing heavily in proprietary AI systems to defend against neobroker tech leapfrog.

Consolidation: CMC/Bain Capital and IG's M&A appetite. The industry is consolidating: CMC Markets is exploring PE backing / private equity partnerships. Potential targets for IG: (1) smaller European CFD brokers (e.g., eToro, could be acquired for £500-1,000m), (2) emerging market brokers (XTB, could be acquired for £200-400m), (3) institutional brokers (smaller prime brokers, could add institutional volume to offset retail margin compression). IG's balance sheet can support £500-1,000m M&A at current leverage. However, Freetrade + tastytrade integration is ongoing; a third large acquisition would be operationally risky over the next 18-24 months. Most likely: IG is on pause for M&A until Freetrade integration completes (by H2 2026), then potentially pursues one large acquisition (emerging market scale play or institutional platform).

Where is pricing power moving—from broker toward customer? Historical model (2010-2020): Brokers had pricing power. Retail customers had limited alternatives, so brokers could charge £0.50 per trade (equities), 2-3 pips on FX, 4-6% margin interest. Margins were 40-50%. Current model (2020-2026): Pricing power is shifting downstream (toward customers). Retail traders now have 100+ broker options, zero-commission is the baseline, and spreads are compressed to 0.5-1 pip on majors. Brokers' pricing power eroded. Margin on core products fell to 30-35%. Future model (2026-2030): Pricing power may consolidate further—toward market makers (Citadel, Wolverine, Jump Trading). If retail order flow is consolidated into a few mega-market-makers via PFOF ban (or equivalent mechanisms), those market makers capture the alpha that brokers used to keep. Brokers become distribution agents, earning thin fees (£0.20 per contract, or 0.1 pip spreads) on high volume. This is the long-term structural threat. IG's defense: build direct market-making capability (IG issues OTC derivatives directly to customers, not via market maker intermediary), maintain regulatory moat, and diversify into higher-margin niches (crypto, premium subscriptions, institutional). But pricing power erosion is inevitable.

10. SCEPTICAL VIEW—BEAR CASE REBUTTAL

For each major bull-case assertion, the strongest bear case:

Bull: "IG Group has scale advantages over competitors."
Bear rebuttal: Scale advantages are eroding. IG's £1,123m revenue is 1.5x CMC, 1.5x Plus500, and 0.2x Interactive Brokers. But on a per-customer basis, IG's ARPU (£2,150 IG Core) is 6-60x higher than peers because IG's customer base is older, wealthier, and skews institutional/semi-professional. New customer cohorts are lower-quality (younger, lower deposit sizes), implying ARPU degradation over 5 years to parity with Robinhood/Freetrade (£100-200 ARPU). At low ARPU, scale advantage disappears—a broker needs 2-5m customers to break even, and that scale is achievable by neobrokers in 3-5 years (Freetrade, Revolut, Trading 212, Robinhood). Scale advantage is temporary (5-10 year horizon).

Bull: "Multi-brand diversification is a strategic moat (geographic, product, customer cohort diversification)."
Bear rebuttal: Diversification creates complexity without offsetting benefits. Freetrade added 457k customers but at £38 ARPU (near-zero profitability), requiring £160m upfront capital and 5-10 year funnel before payoff. If Freetrade churn exceeds 40% annually (neobroker baseline), the acquisition is value-destroying on an NPV basis. tastytrade added US exposure and options presence, but PFOF cliff on June 30, 2026 eliminates the margin that made it accretive. IG's three brands compete for customer wallet share; cannibalization risk is real (customer migrating from Freetrade to IG Core CFD, or vice versa, generates zero net revenue). Management overhead (three CEOs, three CMOs, three risk teams) is higher than a unified platform, offsetting any theoretical synergy. A pure-play competitor (CMC focused on CFD, Robinhood focused on options/equities) can execute faster, with lower cost-to-serve.

Bull: "47% EBITDA margins are sustainable given IG's scale and regulatory moat."
Bear rebuttal: Margins are under compression from multiple vectors: (1) PFOF cliff (June 2026) eliminates £30-50m tastytrade EBITDA, compressing Group margin by 50-100 bps, (2) cohort quality degradation (new IG Core customers ARPU lower by 20-30%), (3) regulatory compliance cost inflation (ESMA leverage cap implementation, AI/ML risk systems, enhanced reporting), (4) CAC inflation (cost to acquire a customer up 10-12% annually), (5) negative operating leverage post-Freetrade (12% revenue growth → 7% EBITDA growth suggests cost structure is stiffening). Extrapolating: IG's EBITDA margin compresses from 47% to 40-42% by 2030. At mature revenue levels (£1.2-1.4bn by 2030), this translates to 5-10% EBITDA reduction in absolute terms. Competitors (Robinhood, Saxo Bank, Interactive Brokers) operate at 60-75% margins because they lack IG's regulatory burden and have simpler cost structures. IG's 47% margin is structural, not sustainable at current levels.

Bull: "US retail options market boom (13.8bn contracts CY25, +22% YoY) is a structural tailwind for tastytrade."
Bear rebuttal: Options volume growth is real, but it is driven by zero-DTE speculation (weekly, daily, hourly options) not from retail traders gradually building skill/sophistication. Zero-DTE accounts for 24% of total options volume in 2025, up from 12% in 2022—a doubling of the most toxic (low-ARPU, high-churn) retail customer segment. These traders churn at 60-80% annually, require heavy education/content investment to retain, and generate minimal ARPU (£50-100 annually vs £1,500 for traditional options traders). CBOE's own data shows retail flow is increasingly concentrated in 0-DTE; IG/tastytrade are essentially becoming a 0-DTE bucket shop. If 0-DTE options trading faces regulatory cap/ban (as CFTC and SEC have discussed), volume cliff is imminent. Even without regulation, 0-DTE margin compression is inevitable as more retail brokers compete for this flow; spreads (currently 2-5 cents per contract) will compress to 1 cent or less within 3 years, eliminating economic returns. The "options boom" is a mirage; the segment tastytrade is winning (0-DTE) is the lowest-margin, highest-risk subsegment.

CONCLUSION: STRUCTURAL COMPETITIVE POSITION RATING

IG GROUP HOLDINGS: ORANGE (defensible but under material structural pressure).

Rationale: IG Group commands industry leadership through regulatory heritage, scale, ARPU advantage (IG Core £2,150 vs peers £350-2,310), and three-brand diversification capturing different customer cohorts. Regulatory barriers to entry remain intact (£20-50m capital requirement, 3-5 year licensing timelines, permanent compliance infrastructure). However, the company faces existential margin compression from four structural vectors: (1) zero-commission/low-spread pricing pressure cascading from equities into derivatives (irreversible), (2) PFOF cliff on June 30, 2026 eliminating tastytrade's largest margin (£30-50m EBITDA reduction), (3) cohort quality degradation (new customer ARPU declining 20-30% below historical levels, churn accelerating), (4) regulatory leverage cap restrictions narrowing total addressable retail notional. IG's 47.3% EBITDA margin is the industry's highest, but is unsustainable at 2030 levels (40-42% forecast). Operating leverage is negative (12% revenue growth → 7% EBITDA growth post-Freetrade), signaling cost structure stiffening. The next 12-18 months are critical: (a) Freetrade must prove strategic funnel to IG Core (prove 10-15% graduation rate within 3-5 years), (b) tastytrade must execute PFOF cliff mitigation (commission reintroduction, margin interest escalation, premium subscriptions) before June 30, 2026, (c) IG Core must stabilize new cohort ARPU (slow the degradation from £1,800 to £1,200-1,500 over 5 years).
