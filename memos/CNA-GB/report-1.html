§1 — General

Centrica is a FTSE 250 integrated energy services company headquartered in Windsor, UK, with operations spanning retail energy supply, energy services (HomeCare), upstream gas production, energy storage, and B2B energy optimisation.

  • Net signal: Centrica is a complex, multi-segment utility undergoing deliberate simplification; financial profile shows a cyclical earnings peak in 2022-23 now reverting, which structurally limits near-term IRR despite balance-sheet strength [#J]

Description

  • Description: Centrica operates across five main segments — British Gas (retail residential supply + services), Centrica Energy (trading, upstream), Spirit Energy (N Sea upstream gas), Centrica Business Solutions (B2B distributed energy), and Centrica Energy Storage (Rough facility) [#I]
  • British Gas serves ~7m residential energy accounts and ~1.8m HomeCare service contracts in the UK, making it the UK's largest household energy provider by account count
  • Spirit Energy is a 69% JV with Equinor (31%), operating North Sea gas assets with production of ~33mboepd at peak; Centrica has flagged intention to manage for value/wind-down
  • Strategic objective: Management's stated aim is to be a "leading energy services and solutions business," pivoting away from commodity supply toward higher-margin services, energy optimisation, storage, and decarbonisation solutions [#I]
  • The "Better Future Plan" (2020-25) set targets around net zero, customer service improvement, and capital returns; updated strategy in 2023-24 emphasises energy transition enablement (heat pumps, EVs, flexibility services)
  • Organisation: Segments: British Gas Services & Solutions, British Gas Energy, Bord Gáis Energy (Ireland retail), Spirit Energy, Centrica Energy (trading/optimisation/storage), Centrica Business Solutions; group HQ Windsor, UK [#I]
  • Listing: Primary listing on London Stock Exchange (LSE: CNA.L); FTSE 250 constituent; shares denominated and traded in GBp; reporting currency GBP [#I]

§2 — Company Details

  • Net signal: Revenue model is heavily concentrated in low-margin UK retail energy supply; HomeCare services provide the only genuine moat; recent delivery (2020-2024) is creditably disciplined but primarily exploits a commodity cycle rather than building durable competitive distance [#J]

Revenue model

  • Revenue source: British Gas Energy (residential supply) is the largest revenue contributor (~60-65% of revenue) but has structurally thin margins capped by Ofgem's price cap, which sets the maximum unit rate and standing charge for default-tariff customers [#I]
  • Revenue mix: Roughly: British Gas Energy ~45% of adj. operating profit at normalised commodity; British Gas Services (HomeCare) ~20%; Centrica Energy (trading/optimisation/storage) ~20-30% (highly variable); Spirit Energy ~10-15% at £60-70/bbl gas equivalent; B2B ~5-10% [#A]
  • The trading/optimisation contribution is highly volatile — contributed ~60% of 2022 operating profit and is now declining toward 20-25% as spreads normalise
  • Geography: Predominantly UK (>90% of revenue and profit); Bord Gáis Energy (Ireland) provides modest revenue (~£500-600m pa) with similar retail supply characteristics; Spirit Energy has some Norwegian exposure [#I]
  • End-market: Residential energy supply (price-regulated, commoditised), residential services (HomeCare — boiler repair, heating maintenance), upstream gas production (commodity price-linked), B2B energy management (SME and large corporates) [#I]
  • Cyclicality: High for Centrica Energy/Spirit segments (commodity price and spread dependent); moderate cyclical for British Gas Energy (volume and working capital are temperature-sensitive); low-cyclical for HomeCare (recurring contract revenues, ~£500m pa) [#A]
  • Predictability: HomeCare and Bord Gáis revenues are the most predictable (~1.8m annual service contracts); retail energy supply revenue is predictable in volume terms but margin is Ofgem-cap constrained and commodity-cost sensitive; trading/optimisation is the least predictable [#A]
  • Driver count: At least five major P&L drivers (commodity prices, Ofgem cap levels, weather/volumes, HomeCare contract retention, gas production rates) — high driver count reduces predictability relative to regulated peers (National Grid: 2 primary drivers — asset base + regulatory return) [#A] ❌

Business model — customer side

  • Customer behaviour: British Gas residential supply customers exhibit moderate loyalty; UK switching rates peaked at ~5m switches/year pre-crisis (2017-2019); post-crisis switching has fallen as fewer fixed-rate tariff deals are available, temporarily increasing customer retention [#A]
  • Switching cost: Low for energy supply — price comparison websites (MoneySuperMarket, uSwitch) make switching near-frictionless; HomeCare customers exhibit higher stickiness (~85% retention rate implied by ~1.8m contract base stability) [#A]
  • NPS: British Gas NPS historically lagged challenger brands (Octopus Energy, Bulb pre-collapse); management has cited NPS improvement under the "Better for You" service programme but absolute NPS likely still below sector leaders like Octopus [#A]
  • Lock-in: HomeCare annual service contracts provide genuine lock-in; British Gas supply has no meaningful contractual lock-in beyond brief fixed-rate periods; B2B multi-year energy management contracts provide some medium-term lock-in [#A]
  • Customer moat: HomeCare's ~6,000-engineer field force is the primary customer-side moat — Centrica is the only national-scale domestic heating/plumbing service provider with this geographic coverage; nearest competitor (HomeServe, pre-Brookfield acquisition) was smaller by field headcount [#A]

Business model — economies of scale

  • Scale economy: British Gas's ~7m account base provides modest procurement (energy buying) scale but Ofgem's price cap captures most of the benefit for consumers, not shareholders; the scale advantage is more defensible in HomeCare where engineer density per postcode drives response time and cost per job [#A]
  • Operating leverage: Limited in retail energy supply (variable cost-dominated — energy procurement); stronger in HomeCare where fixed field-force costs spread over a larger contract base; Rough storage has high fixed-cost operating leverage once recommissioning capex is absorbed [#A]
  • Relative scale: Centrica is the #1 UK residential energy supplier by account (7m vs E.ON ~5m, EDF ~3m, Octopus ~4m growing rapidly); but scale advantage in supply is constrained by regulation — it does not translate into sustainable margin advantage [#A]

Recent delivery (2020-2024)

  • Recent delivery: O'Shea's tenure (Jan 2020-present) is characterised by four distinct phases: Covid operational resilience (2020), balance sheet repair and dividend resumption (2020-2021), windfall exploitation via trading/storage (2022-2023), and managed normalisation + capital returns (2024-2025) [#A]
  • 2020: Centrica cut dividend (first cut since privatisation 1997), announced 5,000 job cuts (12% of workforce), exited North American businesses (Direct Energy sold for £2.5bn, Dec 2020) — decisive delevering
  • 2021-2022: Rough storage recommissioned (first gas withdrawn Oct 2022 after 5-year mothballing), providing significant gas storage revenue and optionality; dividend reinstated FY2021
  • 2022: Adjusted operating profit £3.3bn — highest in company history; OCF ~£3.8bn; announced £750m buyback
  • 2023: Returned £1.5bn total (buybacks + dividends); net cash position; continued Rough storage build-out
  • 2024: Continued buyback (£500m programme); updated financial framework targeting 40-60% payout; guidance trimmed reflecting commodity normalisation

Internal change (L12M and N3Y)

  • Leadership change: Chris O'Shea (CEO, Jan 2020) and Iain Conn predecessor era contrast sharply — Conn era (2015-2020) saw customer losses, margin erosion, and sustained underperformance; O'Shea has been a net positive operator but the strategic vision remains evolutionary, not transformational [#A]
  • CFO: Russell O'Brien (joined 2022 from M&G) — brings institutional investor relations experience but limited utility-specific track record
  • Internal change: Sale of Direct Energy (US/Canada) 2020 for ~£2.5bn eliminated a loss-making distraction; Rough storage recommissioning (~£500m capex 2019-2022) was the most impactful capital allocation decision — created a genuine strategic asset [#A]
  • Strategic priorities (N3Y): Energy transition (heat pumps, EV charging, flexibility services); Centrica Business Solutions growth in distributed energy; Rough storage optionality as a UK national strategic asset; HomeCare contract base stabilisation and quality improvement [#I]

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on Centrica's revenue model and recent execution is that most of the 2020-2024 improvement reflects commodity cycle exploitation and asset disposals rather than structural competitive advantage building; HomeCare contract count has been flat-to-declining for years; Octopus Energy's rapid growth from ~1m to ~5m accounts demonstrates that British Gas's brand advantage is weaker than management implies; and the O'Shea era's most impressive cash returns were funded by a once-in-a-decade energy price dislocation that is now over [#J]

§3 — Industry Context

  • Net signal: UK retail energy supply is a structurally challenging industry — regulated margins, low switching costs, political interference risk, and well-funded disruptors (Octopus) make sustainable excess returns difficult; storage and services are the structurally better segments [#J]

Vs comparable peers

  • Peer divergence: Centrica's financial profile diverges significantly from UK regulated utility peers (SSE, National Grid, United Utilities): peers have explicit regulatory asset bases generating predictable returns; CNA's supply margin is Ofgem-cap constrained and its upstream/trading earnings are inherently volatile [#A]
  • SSE and National Grid trade at 14-16x forward earnings with near-certainty of earnings delivery; CNA at 9-10x forward earnings reflects rightly a higher uncertainty premium on the same timeline
  • No direct listed UK retail energy supply peer exists — E.ON, EDF, Iberdrola (owner of ScottishPower) are all diversified continental operators where UK supply is a small segment

Industry structure and value chain dynamics

  • Market structure: UK domestic energy retail: 6 major suppliers (British Gas, E.ON/npower, EDF, Octopus, OVO, ScottishPower) plus ~20 smaller suppliers; top 6 share approximately 85-90% of accounts, but Octopus has grown from near-zero to ~5m accounts (2023) in five years — a striking disruptor trajectory [#A]
  • Concentration: Top 5 share (~90%) is high in nominal terms, but structural attractiveness is limited because Ofgem price cap constrains margin extraction; concentrated supply markets with regulatory price controls are inferior to concentrated supply markets with pricing freedom [#A]
  • Competitive intensity: Elevated and rising: Octopus Energy (funded by Tokyo Gas, Masayoshi Son's SoftBank vision) is the most credible challenger, targeting 100% green tariffs, superior digital experience, and technology-led cost advantage (Kraken platform); direct competitive threat to British Gas's residential customer base [#A] ❌
  • New entrant: Octopus Kraken platform (operating system for energy retail) is being licensed to other utilities globally — if it becomes the industry standard, it structurally disadvantages legacy operators like British Gas whose IT infrastructure is older and costlier [#A]
  • Disruption: Heat pump and EV adoption are transitional trends: short-term headwinds for gas boiler servicing revenue (HomeCare gas-dependent), but medium-term opportunities if Centrica can pivot to heat pump installation and servicing at scale [#A]
  • UK Heat pump annual installations ~70,000 (2023) vs government target 600,000/year by 2028 — current trajectory meaningfully below target, limiting near-term revenue for Centrica's heat pump services push
  • Regulatory shift: Ofgem has repeatedly intervened in energy retail: price cap (2019-present), Supplier of Last Resort obligations, energy bill support schemes; political will to maintain retail energy affordability constrains Centrica's ability to price for risk adequately in supply [#A]
  • Political shift: UK government has designated Rough storage as "nationally significant infrastructure" — this provides political support for Centrica's storage economics but also creates expectations of public-interest pricing; Centrica has resisted full government ownership of Rough, retaining commercial optionality [#A]
  • Supplier returns: Gas commodity suppliers (North Sea producers, LNG importers) captured extraordinary returns in 2021-2022; these are now normalising; Centrica's Spirit Energy upstream position means it is both supplier and retailer — a unique integrated position that partly hedges commodity cost in supply [#A]

External macro changes (L12M and plausibly N36M)

  • Macro shift: UK gas prices have fallen ~70% from 2022 peak to ~50p/therm (2024), reducing the absolute level of British Gas energy bills and compressing Centrica's trading/optimisation margins; wholesale price normalisation was fully anticipated by consensus but the speed of decline has pressured near-term earnings [#A]
  • Macro shift: UK base rate cycle (5.25% peak Aug 2023, now cutting) has limited impact on Centrica directly (net cash position means interest income, not expense) but affects housing market (HomeCare contract opportunity tied to house transactions/boiler replacements) [#A]
  • Thematic trend: UK energy transition (net zero 2050) creates a long-duration volume tailwind for electricity (EV charging, heat pumps), but creates a volume headwind for gas retail as households switch fuels over time; Centrica's HomeCare gas servicing (~80% of contracts gas-boiler related) faces a 10-20 year structural demand erosion [#A]

External non-macro changes (L12M and plausibly N36M)

  • Regulatory shift: Ofgem is reviewing the energy retail market structure — potential for market reform to introduce longer-term fixed pricing, which could improve retail margin stability; but Ofgem has historically erred toward consumer protection over supplier economics [#A]
  • Competitor intensity: Octopus Energy passed 5m UK accounts (2023); OVO Energy stabilised post-Bulb acquisition; E.ON/npower merger integration now bedded in — the competitive intensity from digitally-native challengers is structurally higher than pre-2018 [#A] ❌
  • Disruption force: Smart energy management platforms (Octopus Kraken, Tibber in Scandinavia) are commoditising energy retail further; the customer experience bar is rising toward smartphone-native standards that legacy British Gas IT systems struggle to match [#A]

Sceptical bullet: The most underpriced industry risk is Octopus Energy's continued account growth — if Octopus reaches 8-10m UK residential accounts by 2028 (from 5m today), British Gas's ~7m base faces material share loss, particularly among younger, digitally-engaged customers; this would compress HomeCare cross-selling opportunities and increase customer acquisition costs, potentially making the supply business subscale relative to its cost structure [#J]

§4.2 — Advantaged Business + SRCA (BB#2 TC2)

  • Net signal (§4.2): Moat is real but narrow — HomeCare field force is a genuine scale moat; supply business lacks pricing power; GENERATE metrics are structurally below the 25-30% post-tax RONIC threshold outside windfall periods [#J]

MOAT (RA4)

  • Customer moat: HomeCare's ~1.8m annual service contract base reflects genuine customer stickiness — high implied retention (~85%), trusted brand for boiler repair/service, and friction in switching heating engineers [#A]
  • British Gas supply has no meaningful customer moat: switching cost is near zero for digitally-engaged customers; brand advantage is eroding vs Octopus per NPS data
  • Production moat: Rough gas storage facility is the UK's largest operational gas storage site (70bcf capacity vs ~15bcf total remaining UK commercial storage excluding Rough) — genuinely irreplaceable infrastructure asset with no near-term new-entrant threat [#A] ⚡
  • Scale moat: ~6,000 field engineers nationally is the largest UK domestic heating/plumbing service network; building this network from scratch would require ~5-7 years and £500m+ of labour investment — a durable entry barrier for HomeCare [#A]
  • Regulatory moat: Rough storage's designation as nationally significant infrastructure provides some regulatory protection from new entrant storage competition; Centrica's Supplier of Last Resort history gives it regulatory familiarity with Ofgem that smaller challengers lack [#A]
  • Culture moat: Limited evidence of culture as a durable competitive advantage at this stage — ESA depth in Q8 [#A]
  • Relative scale: British Gas is #1 UK residential supplier by account count (~7m) vs Octopus (~5m, growing), E.ON (~5m stable), EDF (~3m); scale advantage in supply is regulatory-constrained; in HomeCare, scale advantage is operationally meaningful [#A]
  • TAM: UK residential energy supply: ~29m household accounts (stable); HomeCare addressable: ~24m UK households with gas boilers (declining toward 20m by 2035 per heat pump rollout trajectory); B2B distributed energy: UK commercial & industrial sector electricity demand ~200TWh/year — large but competitive [#A]
  • TAM for Centrica's core segments does not obviously facilitate 12-20% annual revenue growth — supply TAM is static/declining, HomeCare TAM is slowly shrinking, B2B is competitive

OPERATE (RA5) — high-level at IG

  • Pricing power: Centrica has limited pricing power in supply (Ofgem cap is binding constraint); HomeCare has moderate pricing power (annual contract price increases ~CPI+1-2% pa accepted by most customers); Rough storage pricing is market-determined and volatile — ESA depth in Q8 [#A]
  • Scale economies shared: Limited evidence of scale economies shared with customers in supply; HomeCare may share some scale benefit via broader service coverage — ESA depth in Q8 [#A]

GENERATE (RA6)

  • Revenue growth: 2016-2019 organic revenue growth was roughly flat to declining in real terms; 2022-2023 commodity-driven revenue surge was volume-and-price, not structural growth; consensus 2024-2026 revenue trajectory is declining toward £18bn — not the 12-20% growth profile required by BB#2 RA6 [#A] ❌

Gravitational pull (RA7) — high-level at IG
