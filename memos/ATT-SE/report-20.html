3. §1 — EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (TOP-3 MOST-DIAGNOSTIC QUESTIONS)

  • Verdict: Three questions whose answers discriminate hypothesis profiles most sharply — net reimbursement spread, occupancy breakeven, and the single feared metric — identified
  • Q-D1 (most diagnostic): "What is the net spread between your average reimbursement rate increase and your average wage-cost increase in each geography, last 12 months?" — discriminates between genuine margin recovery (spread positive and widening) vs false-friend (spread flat or negative, margin recovery is mix/volume not pricing power)
  • Q-D2: "What occupancy rate in each geography is your EBIT breakeven, and where are you today vs that threshold?" — discriminates bull (above breakeven with headroom) vs bear (tight to breakeven, vulnerable to any volume softness)
  • Q-D3: "If you had to name one metric that, if it moved 200bps against you, would most concern you, what is it?" — management's answer to this reveals genuine risk-weighting vs PR-managed risk framing; a fluent, specific answer is positive; a pivot to macro is negative
  • Bull discriminator: Bull case requires (a) positive reimbursement-wage spread, (b) occupancy above breakeven with momentum, (c) staff turnover declining, and (d) management able to cite unit-level IRR on recent openings — all four should be probed
  • False-friend discriminator: False-friend profile = top-line growth via new-unit additions masking flat/declining same-unit economics; the diagnostic questions in §2 and §3 are specifically designed to surface this

Sceptical bullet: The analyst may not want to surface the false-friend hypothesis explicitly with management because it implies distrust; per Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 1, the discomfort is the signal — ask it directly via the unit-economics decomposition question

4. §2 — THEME 1: REVENUE DRIVERS + VISIBILITY

  • Verdict: Revenue visibility is structurally high (government framework contracts) but net revenue per bed is more uncertain than the contract structure suggests — rate-setting lag and political cycle are the live risks

Q1 — Reimbursement rate pipeline

  • Question: "Can you walk us through the rate-setting process in Sweden and Finland — who sets the rate, when, and with what lag to CPI?"
  • Expected answer: Rates set annually by municipal/county bodies, 6-12 month lag to inflation, some formula-linking to public-sector wage indices
  • Good answer: Specific formula, named municipalities, quantified lag, evidence of proactive renegotiation capability
  • Bad answer: "Rates have generally kept pace" — vague, avoids lag quantification, suggests management doesn't track this as tightly as required
  • Follow-up probe: "In FY24, what percentage of your beds were on contracts where the rate increase lagged actual wage inflation?"
  • Diagnostic note: If rate lag is >9 months AND wage inflation is >4%, the margin recovery thesis is structurally impaired — this is the bear-case confirming signal

Q2 — Private-pay vs public-pay mix

  • Question: "What share of revenue today is private-pay vs public-pay, and how has that mix shifted over three years?"
  • Expected answer: Predominantly public-pay (>80%), with private-pay a small but higher-margin segment growing modestly
  • Good answer: Deliberate private-pay growth strategy with named geographies, quantified margin differential per payer type
  • Bad answer: "We don't really break that out" — opacity on highest-margin revenue segment is a red flag
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the EBITDA margin differential between a private-pay bed and a public-pay bed, all else equal?"
  • Diagnostic note: Mix-shift toward private pay is the bull-case margin driver; stagnation here weakens the margin recovery thesis (Q19 ACH item)

Q3 — Occupancy trajectory and exit rate

  • Question: "What is current occupancy rate by geography, and what was the Q1 2025 exit rate vs Q1 2024 exit rate?"
  • Expected answer: Occupancy broadly recovering post-COVID toward 90%+ in Sweden, somewhat lower in Finland, improving trend
  • Good answer: Specific exit rates, visible momentum, commentary on pipeline of new referrals from municipal partners
  • Bad answer: Reporting only annual averages, reluctance to give current quarter data, attribution of flatness to "system-level capacity constraints"
  • Follow-up probe: "At what occupancy rate does an individual unit cross into positive EBIT territory?"
  • Diagnostic note: Occupancy below breakeven in a meaningful share of the estate is the false-friend trigger — growth via new units that drag aggregate occupancy is dilutive, not accretive

Q4 — Customer concentration and contract duration

  • Customer concentration: "What share of beds are contracted to your top-5 municipal purchasers, and when do those contracts next come up for renewal?"
  • Expected answer: Moderate concentration (top-5 = 25-40%), rolling 2-3 year contracts, low churn historically
  • Good answer: Long-duration contracts, diversified across municipalities, evidence of renewal track record
  • Bad answer: Concentration >50% in top-3, near-term renewals in 12 months, no stated retention rate
  • Follow-up probe: "Have you ever lost a contract renewal, and what happened operationally?"
  • Diagnostic note: Contract churn is the tail risk the market prices as near-zero — evidence it is nonzero is materially negative

Sceptical bullet: A fluent answer on revenue visibility may signal rehearsed investor-day framing rather than genuine insight — the diagnostic is whether management can give a specific breakeven occupancy number, not a confident narrative; press for the number

5. §3 — THEME 2: MARGIN TRAJECTORY + LEVERS

  • Verdict: Margin recovery is the bull-case load-bearing claim; the critical question is whether the lever is structural (new staffing model) or exhausted cyclical (post-COVID normalisation already in the run-rate)

Q5 — Staffing model and wage cost per bed

  • GM driver: "Can you walk us through cost-per-bed-day in 2022 vs 2024 — what drove the change, and what is the 2025 trajectory?"
  • Expected answer: Wage cost per bed elevated 2022-23 due to agency staff, now declining as agency share falls; direction positive
  • Good answer: Specific agency-to-permanent staff ratio, quantified cost differential, named target for agency share in 2025
  • Bad answer: "Labour costs are improving" without quantifying agency share or cost-per-bed trajectory — implies management is not tracking the right operational KPI
  • Follow-up probe: "What is your agency staff as a percentage of total care hours today vs 12 months ago, and what is the cost differential per hour?"
  • Diagnostic note: Agency-to-permanent shift is the primary near-term EBIT margin lever; if agency share is already low, the easy gains are behind

Q6 — Op leverage and fixed cost structure

  • Op leverage: "What percentage of your cost base is fixed vs variable, and at what revenue growth rate do you expect to see meaningful operating leverage flow-through?"
  • Expected answer: ~60-70% of costs are semi-fixed (staff, property); op leverage materialises at occupancy >88-90%
  • Good answer: Clear fixed/variable split, named occupancy threshold for leverage, historical evidence of leverage at prior cycle peaks
  • Bad answer: "We have a largely variable cost structure" — care homes have high fixed-cost structures; claiming otherwise is either confused or misleading
  • Follow-up probe: "In a unit at 95% occupancy vs 85%, what is the EBIT margin differential?"
  • Diagnostic note: Management that cannot answer the margin-at-occupancy question either doesn't measure it or is obscuring poor unit economics — both are negative signals

Q7 — Reimbursement-wage spread (most-diagnostic)

  • Pricing-cost spread: "In each geography, what was the net spread between average reimbursement rate increase and average wage increase in 2024 — positive, flat, or negative?"
  • Expected answer: Sweden approximately flat to slightly positive; Finland more variable
  • Good answer: Positive spread, management has a mechanism to track and manage the lag, names specific contracts where renegotiation improved the spread
  • Bad answer: "Broadly keeping pace" — vague; implies management does not track the spread as an operating KPI, which is a major operational discipline failure for a labour-intensive business
  • Follow-up probe: "What is your assumption for the 2025 spread in your internal budget?"
  • Diagnostic note: This is the single most financially consequential variable in the model; a negative spread trajectory invalidates the margin recovery bull thesis (maps directly to Q19 ACH matrix bear-case)

Q8 — Capex pacing and maintenance vs growth split

  • Capex pacing: "Of your 2024 capex envelope, what share was maintenance vs growth, and how does that split evolve over 2025-26?"
  • Expected answer: Growth-heavy capex cycle currently, maintenance capex low relative to asset age
  • Good answer: Specific split, maintenance capex benchmarked to asset-replacement lifecycle, growth capex tied to named projects with IRR hurdles
  • Bad answer: Reports only total capex figure without split — inability to decompose maintenance vs growth capex is a red flag in an asset-intensive care-home operator
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the average age of your owned estate, and what is the annual refurbishment budget per bed?"
  • Diagnostic note: Under-stated maintenance capex inflates short-term FCF; this is the false-friend capex-deferral pattern

6. §4 — THEME 3: CAPITAL ALLOCATION + BALANCE SHEET

  • Verdict: Capital allocation discipline is the differentiating variable between a compounder and a serial diluter — the unit-level IRR question is the diagnostic crystalliser

Q9 — Unit-level IRR and RONIC discipline

  • RONIC discipline: "What is your stated minimum IRR hurdle for a new unit opening, and what have recent openings actually delivered vs that hurdle?"
  • Expected answer: Stated hurdle 10-15% unlevered; recent openings modestly below hurdle due to ramp period
  • Good answer: Named hurdle, track record of hurdle achievement, willing to cite 2-3 specific units and their ramp trajectories
  • Bad answer: "We focus on strategic fit, IRR is one input" — management that cannot or will not give a unit-level IRR number is either not measuring it or knows the answer is unflattering
  • Follow-up probe: "What percentage of units opened in the last 3 years are currently generating EBIT above your stated hurdle?"
  • Diagnostic note: If majority of recently-opened units are below hurdle, the growth-via-openings thesis is capital-dilutive — this is the false-friend capital deployment pattern

Q10 — M&A philosophy and pipeline

  • M&A philosophy: "What is your acquisition criteria — do you prefer bolt-on care homes, platform acquisitions, or geographic adjacency, and what multiple are you willing to pay?"
  • Expected answer: Preference for bolt-on, EV/EBITDA discipline (<8x), focus on geographies with existing operational footprint
  • Good answer: Specific multiple ceiling, named rationale for geographic preference, evidence of walk-away discipline on overpriced assets
  • Bad answer: "We look at each deal on its merits" — absence of stated criteria is the hallmark of empire-building rather than RONIC-disciplined capital allocation
  • Follow-up probe: "What was the last acquisition you walked away from, and why?"
  • Diagnostic note: The walk-away question is the most diagnostic M&A discipline probe — inability to name one is a red flag (positive signal requires a specific deal and a credible reason)

Q11 — Leverage target and covenant headroom

  • Leverage target: "What is your stated net debt/EBITDA target, current position, and covenant headroom?"
  • Expected answer: Target 2-3x, currently within or approaching target, covenant headroom comfortable
  • Good answer: Specific ratio, named covenants, clear deleveraging pathway if above target
  • Bad answer: Vague answer, pivot to "comfortable leverage" without specific covenant detail — in a sector with high fixed costs and municipal-revenue cyclicality, leverage headroom is safety-critical
  • Follow-up probe: "Under your bear-case internal scenario (occupancy -300bps, wages +5%), what is your projected leverage in 12 months?"
  • Diagnostic note: If management has not stress-tested leverage under a plausible bear scenario, operational risk management is inadequate

7. §5 — THEME 4: STRATEGY + COMPETITIVE POSITION

  • Verdict: Strategic clarity on whether Attendo is a Nordic scale-operator or a diversifying pan-European platform is the key ambiguity — the answer has large capital-allocation implications

Q12 — Geographic concentration vs diversification

  • Strategic horizon: "Is the long-term strategic vision to deepen Nordic dominance or expand geographically, and what is the decision framework for entering a new market?"
  • Expected answer: Nordic-first for 2-3 years, then selective adjacency (Germany, Baltics possible); entry criteria include payer-system familiarity and wage-structure alignment
  • Good answer: Explicit capital allocation priority between deepening vs diversifying, named criteria for new market entry, evidence of prior market-entry discipline
  • Bad answer: "We see opportunities across Europe" — undisciplined geographic ambition is a capital-dilution risk for a business with high fixed-cost operations
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the minimum revenue scale in a geography at which you can operate at unit economics equivalent to Sweden?"
  • Diagnostic note: Geographic expansion that dilutes RONIC before reaching scale is the primary strategic value-destruction risk

Q13 — Competitive moat and switching costs

  • Moat trajectory: "What prevents a municipality from re-tendering your largest contract to a competitor, and how has your win rate on re-tenders changed over the last 5 years?"
  • Expected answer: Quality ratings, regulatory compliance track record, integration with municipal care systems create meaningful switching costs; win rate >85% on re-tenders
  • Good answer: Specific win rate data, named quality differentiators (CQC-equivalent scores), evidence of relationship depth with municipal procurement
  • Bad answer: "Municipalities prefer continuity" — passive moat framing; switching inertia without active relationship management is fragile
  • Follow-up probe: "Have you ever lost a re-tender, and what was the winning competitor's differentiated proposition?"
  • Diagnostic note: Re-tender loss data is the best objective moat-erosion signal available — if management cannot cite the data, they are not tracking moat attrition

Q14 — Disruption and new-care-model risk

  • Disruption risk: "Home-care and tech-enabled remote monitoring are growing — at what point does this structurally compress demand for residential beds, and what is your positioning?"
  • Expected answer: Near-term (5 years), high-dependency elderly care is not substitutable by home-care; Attendo has a home-care segment as a hedge
  • Good answer: Quantified share of demand that is home-care-substitutable vs not, explicit home-care growth strategy, evidence of cross-sell between home-care and residential referral pipeline
  • Bad answer: "Residential care demand is structural given demographics" — demographic demand is real, but demand composition may shift to higher-acuity/higher-cost cases that compress margins even as volume holds
  • Diagnostic note: The disruption risk is not volume replacement but acuity mix-shift (higher-acuity, lower-margin cases as lower-acuity cases shift to home-care) — probe this specifically

8. §6 — THEME 5: MANAGEMENT QUALITY + OPERATOR TRACK RECORD

  • Verdict: Management transparency on unit economics and sandbagging discipline are the primary quality signals — the incentive structure probe is load-bearing

Q15 — Compensation structure and LTI design

  • Power of incentives: "Can you walk us through how the LTI is structured — what metrics, what weighting, and what are the threshold and maximum performance hurdles?"
  • Expected answer: LTI weighted to TSR and/or EPS, 3-year vesting, threshold = median peer TSR or CPI+3% EPS growth
  • Good answer: Specific metrics with weightings, hurdle levels disclosed, alignment between LTI metrics and the business's actual value drivers (e.g. RONIC, occupancy, margin)
  • Bad answer: "We have a comprehensive incentive structure aligned to shareholder value" — non-answer; inability or unwillingness to specify metrics and hurdles suggests either investor-unfriendly design or embarrassment about the design
  • Follow-up probe (incentive probe): "Is there any metric in the LTI that management could maximise while impairing long-term RONIC — for example, a volume/revenue metric without an attached returns hurdle?"
  • Diagnostic note: LTI metrics without a returns hurdle create incentive to grow volume at dilutive RONIC — this is the single most predictive structural agency problem in care-home operators

Q16 — Guidance track record and sandbagging discipline

  • Sandbagging discipline: "Over the last four reporting years, how often has Attendo delivered above, in-line, or below guidance on EBIT?"
  • Expected answer: Mix — perhaps 2 beats, 1 in-line, 1 miss; management may note COVID distortion
  • Good answer: Consistent delivery or modest beats; management can explain the miss with a specific, non-recurring factor
  • Bad answer: Repeated below-guidance delivery with explanations that are systemic rather than one-off (e.g. repeated "wage headwinds" — if it recurs every year, it is not a one-off)
  • Follow-up probe: "What is your internal guidance philosophy — do you build conservatism into public guidance or aim to guide to your central case?"
  • Diagnostic note: Management that guides to central case without conservatism in a high-fixed-cost, labour-intensive sector is over-optimistic — sandbagging is a positive signal in this sector

Q17 — Operator track record on prior openings

  • Operator track record: "Of the units opened in 2021 and 2022, what percentage are now at or above your target occupancy and margin, and what was the average ramp time?"
  • Expected answer: 70-80% of cohort at target within 24-30 months of opening
  • Good answer: Specific cohort data, named tracking mechanism, evidence of early-warning system for underperforming units
  • Bad answer: "Most of our openings have been successful" — absence of cohort data means management either doesn't track this systematically or is obscuring underperformance
  • Follow-up probe: "What do you do with a persistently underperforming unit — is closure or disposal an option, and has it been exercised?"
  • Diagnostic note: Willingness to close/dispose of a persistently underperforming unit is strong evidence of RONIC discipline over vanity-revenue management

9. §7 — THEME 6: RISK + DOWNSIDE (PRE-MORTEM CROSS-REF)

  • Verdict: Three live risk vectors — wage inflation/rate-lag, political payer risk, and occupancy fragility — each warrant a direct bear-case probe; management dodging any of these is a red flag

Q18 — Wage inflation and collective bargaining exposure

  • Bear-case probe: "What percentage of your workforce is covered by collective bargaining agreements, when do the current agreements expire, and what is your assumption for the next negotiated increase?"
  • Expected answer: >80% covered (Scandinavian norm), agreements expire in cycles, 2025 negotiated increase embedded in 2025 budget at X%
  • Good answer: Specific expiry dates, explicit budget assumption, evidence of ability to renegotiate reimbursement rates to offset wage settlement
  • Bad answer: "We work constructively with unions" — no specifics on expiry timing or budget assumption; collective bargaining is the primary cost-side risk and vagueness here is a financial modelling failure
  • Follow-up probe: "In the event of a collective agreement that exceeds your budget assumption by 200bps, what is the direct EBIT impact and what mitigants are available within a 12-month window?"
  • Diagnostic note: The quantified sensitivity question is the diagnostic; management that cannot give an order-of-magnitude answer to this question is not managing the most material cost risk in the business

Q19 — Political and payer-model risk

  • Regulatory exposure: "What is the political risk to your reimbursement model — specifically, is there any active legislative process in Sweden or Finland that could change the structure of public funding for private elderly care?"
  • Expected answer: Sweden had political debate post-2023 election; current government favourable to private provision; Finland broadly stable
  • Good answer: Specific tracking of legislative pipeline, named legislation under review, evidence that Attendo engages proactively with the political process
  • Bad answer: "The regulatory environment has been stable" — stability today is not stability tomorrow; management that is not actively monitoring the legislative pipeline is naive to the sector's primary existential risk
  • Follow-up probe: "What would a shift to a fully municipal-only model mean for your Swedish operations, and what is your rough probability-weighted assessment of that scenario in the next five years?"
  • Diagnostic note: This is the tail risk that ends the business — management's willingness to think through the scenario openly is itself a quality signal; deflection is a red flag

Q20 — False-friend probe — growth masking unit deterioration

  • False-friend probe: "If you strip out the contribution from units opened in the last 24 months, what is the like-for-like EBIT growth rate of your mature estate?"
  • Expected answer: Management may not have this cut immediately available — that itself is diagnostic
  • Good answer: Can provide LFL data or commits to providing it; LFL mature-estate EBIT growing modestly
  • Bad answer: Cannot or will not provide LFL cut; insists on consolidated growth metrics — this is the false-friend suppression signal
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the 3-year CAGR of EBITDA per occupied bed in your Swedish estate?"
  • Diagnostic note: If management cannot give LFL mature-estate data, the growth story may be masking erosion in the core — this maps directly to the false-friend hypothesis in the Q19 ACH matrix

Sceptical bullet: Q19 ACH items NOT covered by any question in this memo: (a) potential Attendo-specific balance sheet risk from IFRS 16 lease liabilities if occupancy deteriorates sharply — the leverage/covenant question (Q11) partially covers this but does not probe lease covenant triggers specifically; this gap should be added if a CFO-only session is available

10. §8 — CROSS-CUTTING META + NON-OBVIOUS QUESTIONS

  • Verdict: Questions designed to surface what management would NOT volunteer centre on unit-level economics, LFL mature-estate performance, and the incentive structure — management dodging these is itself diagnostic signal

Non-obvious angle 1 (⚡):
- Question: "What is the most surprising piece of feedback you have received from a frontline care worker in the last six months, and what did you do with it?"
- Why non-obvious: The consensus interview asks about staff retention metrics; this question asks management to demonstrate they have a live information channel to the service-delivery layer — the answer reveals whether management is operationally connected or insulated
- Good answer: Specific piece of feedback, named operational change made in response — evidence of a learning organisation
- Bad answer: Pivot to "we have engagement surveys" — system-level deflection away from a personal, specific answer signals top-management insularity from operations

Non-obvious angle 2 (⚡):
- Question: "If you had to cut 20% of your growth capex tomorrow, which projects would you keep and which would you cut, and why?"
- Why non-obvious: Forces prioritisation ranking that management rarely articulates publicly — the answer reveals where genuine conviction sits in the project pipeline vs optionality-maintenance
- Good answer: Immediate, specific answer naming projects by geography and rationale — reveals a clear capital allocation hierarchy
- Bad answer: "We wouldn't want to be in that position" — hypothetical avoidance; management that cannot answer this question has not stress-tested their own capex programme

Multiple-truths probe (per Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 3):
- Question: "We can see the bull case — occupancy recovery, reimbursement rate normalisation, agency staff reduction. Can you articulate for us the scenario in which you are right on the occupancy and staffing trends but still miss your 2026 EBIT target, and how likely is that scenario in your internal view?"
- Why designed this way: Forces management to acknowledge that multiple vectors must converge for the bull case to materialise — the "and yet" structure prevents management from deflecting into either-or framing (either it all works, or there's a macro shock)
- Diagnostic crystalliser: If management cannot construct a coherent scenario where the operational trends are positive but the financial outcome still disappoints, they are not thinking rigorously about the execution-vs-financial-outcome gap

Incentive probe (Concept A):
- Power of incentives: "If you personally had to choose between a year where Attendo grows revenue 10% by opening new units vs a year where revenue is flat but RONIC on the existing estate improves 200bps — which do you prefer, and does your compensation structure reward the same preference?"
- Diagnostic note: The alignment or misalignment between the CEO's stated preference and the LTI design is the single most predictive governance signal; a CEO who prefers RONIC improvement but is compensated on revenue/EBITDA growth is an agency problem embedded in the structure

Edge-detection probe:
- Question: "Which competitor do you respect most and fear most — and are they the same one?"
- Why included: Management that names a competitor they both respect AND fear is demonstrating genuine competitive awareness; management that claims to have no feared competitor is either overconfident or deliberately managing investor perception
- Good answer: Names a specific competitor, articulates their differentiated approach, is candid about where Attendo is genuinely inferior
- Bad answer: "We don't really worry about competitors in a demand-driven sector" — sector-level demand protection does not preclude contract-level competitive pressure; this answer suggests competitive complacency

Stewing trigger — the uncomfortable question the analyst may not have included:
- Question: "Is there any financial metric in your public reporting that you believe investors systematically misread or over-weight, and what would you want them to focus on instead?"
- Why it may have been avoided: This question invites management to redirect analytical attention — it could be perceived as deferential; per Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 1, asking it gives management the opportunity to surface a genuine information asymmetry or to reveal their preferred narrative management metric (which is itself diagnostic)
- Diagnostic note: If management directs attention to a metric that conveniently obscures a weak area, the question has surfaced the framing risk

Sceptical bullet: Questions NOT included because they would feel uncomfortable — "Have any senior care staff raised formal concerns about care quality in the last 12 months, and how were they resolved?" — this is the most direct quality-of-care probe and the most likely to produce a defensive or legalistic response; per Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 1, the discomfort is proportional to the diagnostic value; if a care-quality incident is a live or latent risk, only a direct question surfaces it

11. CROSS-CUTTING META — MOST DIAGNOSTIC / RED-FLAG TRIGGERS

  • Most diagnostic 3: Q7 (reimbursement-wage spread), Q13 (re-tender win rate), Q20 (LFL mature-estate EBIT) — together these three questions test every major hypothesis in the bull/bear/false-friend framework
  • Least-likely candid answers: Management volunteering a specific re-tender loss with named competitor; management citing a specific LTI metric that could misalign incentives; management quantifying the political tail risk scenario
  • Red-flag triggers (any one of these shifts the default to sceptical):
  • Cannot give occupancy breakeven threshold by geography
  • Cannot give agency-to-permanent staff ratio and cost differential
  • Cannot give LFL mature-estate EBIT growth excluding new units
  • LTI structure contains a revenue or volume metric without an attached returns hurdle
  • Collective bargaining expiry dates not known to management

12. AGGREGATE WEAK SIGNALS / DOWNSIDE ❌

  • ❌ Structural: Wage-cost rigidity in Scandinavian collective bargaining structures is a secular headwind — if rate-setting lag persists AND collective agreements reset at elevated levels in 2025-26, the margin recovery thesis stalls before it completes
  • ❌ Execution: New-unit ramp periods create a recurring near-term drag on aggregate margins — if ramp times have extended (more competition for staff, higher opening costs), the growth-via-openings strategy is less attractive than historical unit economics imply
  • ❌ Political: The Swedish private-welfare debate is a live, multi-year political risk; a government shift could affect reimbursement structure on a 3-5 year horizon — management that dismisses this risk is not modelling it
  • ❌ False-friend: If consolidated EBIT growth is driven by new-unit additions while mature-unit per-bed economics are flat or declining, the reported growth is not investable at the current multiple
  • ❌ Governance: LTI metric design (if volume/revenue-weighted without returns hurdle) creates incentive to grow dilutively — this is a systemic agency risk not a one-period issue

13. AGGREGATE OUTLIERS ⚡

  • ⚡ Frontline feedback question (§8, Non-obvious angle 1): No earnings-call or standard investor-day Q&A asks management to cite specific frontline worker feedback — this question surfaces operational connectivity in a way that financial metrics cannot; it is categorically non-consensus
  • ⚡ 20% capex cut prioritisation question (§8, Non-obvious angle 2): Forces a real-time capital allocation prioritisation that management will not have pre-answered; the answer (or the evasion) directly reveals conviction depth in the project pipeline
  • ⚡ "Which competitor do you fear most" probe (§8, Edge-detection): Standard competitive-analysis questions ask about differentiation; asking for the feared competitor surfaces genuine competitive anxiety that management has no incentive to volunteer in a standard investor relations context
