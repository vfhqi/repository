Section 2 – The 80/20 (bottom line up front) 7 findings

Section summary
  1. Heijmans represents a highly profitable and predictable Dutch construction champion, supported by structural tailwinds and strict project selection that are accelerating earnings and margin growth.
1.
Comprehensive pure-play model: Heijmans makes money through property development, residential building, non-residential utility projects, technical services, and major road and water infrastructure works across the Netherlands, securing its profits through a mix of transactional residential sales and multi-year service contracts
2.
Critical housing volume: The single most important swing factor for forward earnings is the pace of Dutch residential permit issuances and volume-driven housing recovery, which remains constrained by domestic planning red tape
3.
Strong backlog momentum: The company's high-quality order book jumped 37% year-over-year to €3.70bn at the end of 2025, compared to €2.60bn at the end of 2024, providing visible revenue coverage for the next 18-to-24 months
4.
Regulated nitrogen ceilings: The most important operational risk that could break the investment case is the persistent Dutch nitrogen emission restriction, which threatens to delay project starts and building permit approvals
5.
Robust solvency cushion: Financial stability remains a core differentiator for the company, which boasts a solvent structure of 32.90% and has successfully expanded its net cash reserves to €57m to fund value-creative consolidation
6.
Clear warrant for study: A closer look is highly warranted because the market prices Heijmans as a low-margin cyclical builder, failing to capture its transition to a high-return predictable builder of energy infrastructure and modular homes with 20%+ return on equity

Section 3 – What the company does 9 findings

Section summary
  1. The group's segment model is highly robust, combining cash-generative residential development with long-term infrastructure and utility services that provide predictable, high-margin earnings.
1.
Diversified divisional structure: The company operates through three core divisions comprising Living, which focuses on property development and residential building; Working, which delivers non-residential construction and utility services; and Connecting, which handles civil road and water infrastructure
2.
Favourable upstream integration: Heijmans has positioned itself in a profit-favoured part of the value chain by integrating upstream property development and modular timber-frame prefabrication factories, allowing it to capture higher margins than pure-play subcontractors
3.
Growing contractual visibility: Recurring business from long-term maintenance contracts, asset management, and renovation partnerships accounts for 35% of total revenue, establishing a highly predictable and low-risk financial foundation
4.
High technical barriers: A durable competitive advantage stems from deep technical expertise in complex electrical grid engineering and high-voltage substations, which creates significant switching costs for critical state clients like TenneT
5.
Modular factory scaling: Heijmans leverages its proprietary prefabrication plant in Ede for high-voltage stations and Heerenveen for timber-frame houses, reducing labor dependencies and accelerating building speed relative to traditional competitors
6.
Fragmented public clients: The customer base consists of diversified public entities, municipal authorities, national transport agencies, and private homebuyers, presenting very low customer concentration and protecting the company from single-client failure
7.
Concentrated industry dynamics: The Dutch heavy construction and infrastructure sector is moderately concentrated, with Heijmans and Royal BAM Group dominating large-scale domestic bids after other major European contractors withdrew due to historical project risk losses
8.
Other side of the story: The high-margin property development segment remains highly exposed to cyclical interest rate volatility, which can severely compress homebuyer affordability and rapidly halt private-market apartment pre-sales

Section 5 – The possible investment case 11 findings

Section summary
  1. The investment case strengthens significantly due to a massive, high-quality order backlog, accelerating grid expansion partnerships, and high-margin modular house manufacturing that drives predictable mid-term EPS compounding.
1.
High driver plausibility: The plausibility of this grid driver is exceptionally high, because the Dutch high-voltage grid operates at its absolute capacity limits and TenneT must expand infrastructure to accommodate rising sustainable electricity demand
2.
Contract delay risks: The primary mechanism by which this grid driver would fail is unexpected engineering bottlenecks, supply delays in critical GIS installations, or labor disputes that postpone project delivery and penalize margins
3.
Visible defense growth: A secondary earnings driver lies in Rijkswaterstaat and Ministry of Defence infrastructure upgrades, including major contract wins like the €60m Eindhoven Air Base runway renovation and the Technology Centre Land in Leusden, which expand the Working division's high-margin order book
4.
Stable asset management: Growing commercial asset management and strategic building maintenance, which now represent a major part of the Working segment's record €1,509m order book, provide a highly predictable, recurring earnings stream
5.
Medium driver plausibility: The plausibility of this defense and services driver is medium to high, as the Ministry of Defence and grid operators are entering non-discretionary multi-year investment cycles
6.
Modular housing margin: A third key driver is the scale-up of modular timber-frame house manufacturing at its factory in Heerenveen, which lifts Living segment margins by reducing expensive site-built labor and speeding up residential area developments
7.
Low driver plausibility: The plausibility of this modular housing volume driver is currently medium, as it is heavily contingent on political decisiveness to resolve national nitrogen restrictions
8.
Red-tape volume barriers: The timber-frame housing driver would fail to hit its volume potential if municipal governments fail to accelerate residential permit issuance and fail to resolve local planning backlogs
9.
Independent growth factors: To achieve the target bull case, the drivers do not need to fire simultaneously as they are structurally independent, meaning that strong infrastructure grid spending can easily offset temporary delays in housing volume recovery
10.
Other side of the story: If central bank monetary policy maintains elevated interest rates to combat inflation, mortgage costs will stay high, depressing the private homebuyer demand needed to support Heijmans' land bank monetization

Section 6 – Valuation 5 findings

Section summary
  1. Valuation multiples look highly attractive as the company trades at a discount to peers despite displaying superior profitability, return on equity, and cash generation.
1.
Attractive forward multiples: Based on June 2026 broker forecasts, Heijmans' ordinary shares trade at a normalized price-to-earnings multiple of 17.60x for 2026F, which contracts to a highly compelling 14.80x for 2027F and 12.70x for 2028F
2.
Compelling valuation discount: Although Heijmans displays superior margins, its 2026F P/E multiple of 17.60x trades at a substantial discount compared to other high-return Benelux peers like Fagron at 16.90x and Lotus Bakeries at 49.10x
3.
Declining enterprise valuation: The group's forward EV/EBITDA multiple is highly supportive, trading at 9.80x for 2026F and falling to 8.20x for 2027F and 7.00x for 2028F, compared to its historical end-2024 level of 14.50x
4.
Priced-in cyclical discount: The current market valuation appears to be pricing in a standard cyclical construction downturn, totally failing to capture Heijmans' structural transformation into a high-margin utility and prefab housing operator

Section 7 – Key risks and the other side 9 findings

Section summary
  1. The risk profile is manageable, but investors must monitor Dutch nitrogen regulations, labor availability, and contract pricing execution on large-scale infrastructure.
1.
Severe nitrogen constraints: The primary regulatory threat to earnings is the strict Dutch nitrogen emission limit, which has led the Council of State to block or delay major construction projects, creating a risk that revenue recognition in the Living segment could stall
2.
Permit backlog bottlenecks: Delays in municipal planning approvals and environmental impact assessments serve as a direct transmission mechanism, preventing the conversion of Heijmans' land bank into active, revenue-generating construction sites
3.
Inflationary contract overruns: The single failure mode that would most damage the investment case is cost overruns on fixed-price infrastructure projects in the Connecting division, where unexpected raw material spikes can wipe out thin engineering margins
4.
Minimal short interest: There is currently no published short-seller report or unusually elevated short interest targeting Heijmans, reflecting the market's high confidence in the group's robust operational execution and cash generation
5.
False order backlog: A key false friend signal is rapid top-line growth in the order book, which can mask a dangerous deterioration in project quality if management begins chasing revenue volume over margin discipline
6.
Key early-warning metric: The single early-warning indicator to monitor closely is the trend in voluntary employee turnover and vacancy fill rates, as labor shortages could limit the Connecting division's capacity to execute its record backlogs
7.
Supply chain localized mitigation: Since Heijmans confines its supply chain to Europe, with more than 95% local sourcing, it is highly insulated from global shipping blockages and geopolitical tariffs
8.
Other side of the story: The bear argument regarding project margin risk is significantly overstated, because Heijmans has aggressively transitioned to a 'margin over volume' bidding strategy and uses indexation agreements to pass unexpected material price increases directly back to clients

Section 8 – Management: what is changing 6 findings

Section summary
  1. Executive leadership is exceptionally stable, with a highly experienced team focused on executing the 'Together towards 2030' strategy and maintaining strict risk selection.
1.
Stable senior leadership: The Executive Board of Heijmans consists of two long-serving members, with Ton Hillen acting as Chief Executive Officer since December 2016 and Gavin van Boekel serving as Chief Financial Officer since September 2021
2.
Deep operational experience: CEO Ton Hillen has been with Heijmans since 1992 and was reappointed in April 2024 for another four-year term, ensuring uninterrupted strategic continuity for the 'Together towards 2030' plan
3.
High insider ownership: Executive directors maintain significant personal skin in the game, with the CEO holding shares valued at 150% of his annual salary and the CFO holding 100%, aligning their personal wealth directly with shareholder returns
4.
Stable corporate governance: Corporate governance remains highly stable, with the Supervisory Board maintaining its focus on enforcing Heijmans' strict tender selection policies rather than pursuing high-risk, unpriced top-line growth
5.
Clear incentive alignment: Executive board variable compensation targets are directly tied to earnings per share and total shareholder return, ensuring that management is incentivized to prioritize high-margin execution

Section 9 – Net read and what to check next 6 findings

Section summary
  1. Heijmans warrants immediate, deep-dive due diligence as it represents a rare, high-predictability earnings compounding story with significant valuation re-rating potential.
1.
Compelling bull thesis: The strongest argument for investment is Heijmans' highly predictable, utility-like infrastructure order book combined with high-margin prefabrication, which will drive normalized EPS from €4.74 in 2025 to €8.20 by 2028F
2.
Other side of the story: The strongest argument against investment is the risk of a prolonged Dutch housing freeze, where political gridlock keeps residential permit issuances depressed and leaves Heijmans' expensive land bank under-utilized
3.
Actionable due diligence: The first next step is to verify the contract indexation clauses in the Connecting division to confirm that Heijmans can continue passing inflation costs directly to public utility clients
4.
Crucial backlog check: The second next step is to monitor the quarterly development of the modular prefabrication plant in Ede, confirming that modular assembly can successfully offset persistent labor shortages
5.
Peer comparison validation: Investors should closely cross-check Heijmans' prefabrication progress against Royal BAM Group's modular initiatives to assess if Heijmans maintains its lead in sustainable margin expansion
