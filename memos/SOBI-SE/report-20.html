3. THEME §1 — Revenue Drivers + Visibility

J-front verdict: Revenue visibility is PARTIALLY strong (haemophilia franchise long-cycle, contract-based) but Alhemo launch trajectory and Kineret normalisation create ±15-20% revenue uncertainty over 2025–2027 — questions should probe each independently

Q1 — Haemophilia franchise: volume vs price split in Elocta/Alprolix

Question: "Of Elocta/Alprolix revenue growth in FY2024, how much was volume, how much was price, and how much was mix — and is that split expected to hold in 2025?"

Expected answer: Management will likely describe modest volume growth in EU/MENA markets, limited pricing uplift due to tender structures, and note any mix shift toward longer-acting agents as competitive pressure

Good answer signature: Specific percentage split offered; acknowledgment of tender-cycle pressure; clear visibility on 2025 contracted volume

Bad answer signature: Vague "broadly stable" framing; deflection to total franchise; no differentiation between Elocta and Alprolix trajectories

Follow-up probe: "Which geography shows the highest pricing pressure today — and has any tender been lost to a competitor in the last 18 months?"

Diagnostic note: Bull case requires Elocta/Alprolix to hold ground against BIVV001 (fitusiran competitive pressure from subcutaneous agents); a volume-erosion signal here confirms the bear case thesis that haemophilia A/B revenue is in secular structural decline within SOBI's hands; false-friend is stable revenue hiding mix-degradation (lower-margin channels growing)

Q2 — Alhemo/fitusiran ramp: real-world uptake vs launch model

Question: "Where is Alhemo patient share tracking versus your internal launch model at this stage — and what's the bottleneck: physician awareness, reimbursement, or patient switching reluctance?"

Expected answer: Reimbursement access in key EU5 markets is the primary gating factor; physician awareness is ahead of reimbursement unlocks; patient switching is slower than modelled due to efficacy anchoring on current prophylaxis

Good answer signature: Identifies specific country-level reimbursement milestones with dates; acknowledges patient inertia quantitatively; has a clear re-forecast mechanism

Bad answer signature: "Launch is tracking to plan" without granularity; no acknowledgment of switching barriers; no country-level differentiation

Follow-up probe: "What's the average time from CHMP approval to national reimbursement in your largest EU markets — and how does that compare to your launch planning assumption?"

Diagnostic note: This is the single highest-variance revenue item in the 2025–2027 model; bull case requires Alhemo to reach 10–15% patient share in haemophilia A inhibitors within 36 months; bear case is prolonged reimbursement delays compressing the revenue ramp into a smaller window; false-friend risk is a fast ramp that exhausts the eligible pool quicker than modelled

Q3 — Kineret: post-COVID normalisation floor

Question: "Kineret revenue peaked with COVID/MAS demand — what's your internal base assumption for the sustainable annualised run-rate, and which indications are carrying the floor?"

Expected answer: SOBI will guide to a lower-but-stable Kineret revenue base anchored on Still's disease/NOMID/MAS core indications; COVID upside has largely normalised; new indications (e.g., CAPS, recurrent pericarditis) provide modest incremental growth

Good answer signature: Explicit floor revenue figure or range cited; specific indication-level breakdown offered; no double-counting of COVID tail as recurring

Bad answer signature: "Kineret remains an important part of the portfolio" without a revenue floor; conflation of one-time COVID demand with organic indication growth

Follow-up probe: "What percentage of 2024 Kineret revenue came from indications that were COVID-driven versus structural rare-disease demand — and how has that mix shifted quarter-on-quarter?"

Diagnostic note: Bull case needs Kineret floor to be ≥SEK 1.5–2bn pa sustainable; if management cannot articulate a floor, it signals internal uncertainty about demand durability; false-friend risk is Kineret masking haemophilia weakness in blended revenue growth

Q4 — Royalty economics: Elocta/Alprolix profit-sharing with Sanofi post-2026

Question: "The Elocta/Alprolix collaboration agreement has a step-down structure — can you walk through the royalty and profit-sharing rate changes expected post-2026 and the net P&L impact?"

Expected answer: Management will likely describe a tiered royalty structure that steps down at revenue thresholds; may be reluctant to give precise economics but should acknowledge directional P&L impact

Good answer signature: Explicit acknowledgment of step-down mechanics; willingness to quantify the margin impact in absolute or relative terms; clear timeline

Bad answer signature: "Contractually confidential" deflection; no directional guidance on P&L sensitivity; conflation of gross revenue with net economics to SOBI

Follow-up probe: "Is the step-down triggered by calendar date, cumulative revenue thresholds, or both — and what's your current estimate of when you hit the relevant threshold?"

Diagnostic note: This is the most under-discussed P&L lever in public sell-side coverage; a meaningful step-down in royalty rates is a bear-case accelerant; management fluency here discriminates between a CFO who owns the P&L vs one who defers to legal; cross-ref Q19 ACH matrix failure modes

SCEPTICAL BULLET: Fluent, rehearsed answers on haemophilia franchise KPIs should prompt suspicion — SOBI's investor relations is sophisticated; a smooth answer on Alhemo ramp may reflect a well-practised narrative rather than genuine confidence; push for the specific data point that's NOT in the IR deck

4. THEME §2 — Margin Trajectory + Levers

J-front verdict: Margin trajectory is the MOST UNCERTAIN P&L dimension — Kineret normalisation, Alhemo launch costs, and R&D investment cadence create ±300-500bp EBIT margin uncertainty in 2025-2027; questions should separate structural from transient

Q5 — Gross margin: product mix evolution 2025–2027

Question: "As Alhemo scales and Kineret normalises, how does gross margin evolve — which products are above/below the blended 70%+ gross margin, and does the mix shift help or hurt?"

Expected answer: Alhemo launch at scale should be margin-accretive long-term; Kineret normalisation removes a high-margin revenue line; net effect is likely a 1-3 year gross margin headwind before Alhemo scale offsets

Good answer signature: Product-level gross margin ranges offered; explicit acknowledgment of Kineret mix-out headwind; timeline for Alhemo gross margin maturation

Bad answer signature: "Blended gross margin remains in the high 60s/70s" without mix decomposition; no acknowledgment that Kineret was above-average margin

Follow-up probe: "At what Alhemo revenue level does the product reach your long-run target gross margin — is there a manufacturing scale inflection point?"

Diagnostic note: GM% erosion is the false-friend risk — revenue growth masking margin compression; bull case requires Alhemo to reach ≥75% gross margin at scale; bear case is persistent launch-phase COGS drag

Q6 — Operating leverage: SG&A discipline during Alhemo launch

Question: "Alhemo launch requires significant SG&A investment — what's the peak SG&A/revenue ratio you're willing to sustain, and at what revenue level does Alhemo SG&A leverage inflect?"

Expected answer: SOBI will likely guide to elevated SG&A in 2024–2026 during peak launch investment, with leverage expected post-2026 as Alhemo reaches critical mass; management will cite specific markets driving the SG&A step-up

Good answer signature: Explicit SG&A/revenue ratio targets stated; country-level launch investment phasing described; clear leverage inflection mechanism

Bad answer signature: "We are investing appropriately in the launch" without quantification; no inflection timeline

Follow-up probe: "Which markets represent the largest SG&A commitment for Alhemo, and is the investment fully reflected in current consensus guidance?"

Diagnostic note: SG&A overrun during launch is the most common rare-disease P&L miss; if management is vague here, operating leverage is more uncertain than modelled; cross-ref sell-side EBIT consensus for 2025–2026

Q7 — R&D adequacy: pipeline spend vs discovery-stage adequacy

Question: "R&D as a percentage of revenue has been 10–15% in recent years — is that the right level to sustain a pipeline that reduces royalty dependency on the haemophilia franchise post-2030?"

Expected answer: Management will likely acknowledge tension between near-term profit delivery and long-run pipeline adequacy; may cite specific R&D programmes as demonstrating capital efficiency

Good answer signature: Explicit R&D/revenue target range with strategic rationale; acknowledgment of the royalty cliff risk and how pipeline addresses it; willingness to increase R&D if warranted

Bad answer signature: "R&D is well-funded and we are comfortable with the pipeline" without reference to post-2030 royalty renewal risk; no quantification of pipeline NPV or time-to-revenue for key assets

Follow-up probe: "If Elocta and Alprolix face biosimilar or next-generation competition in 2029–2032, what revenue replacement is in the pipeline today to fill that gap?"

Diagnostic note: This is a long-cycle risk question that most sell-side analysts underweight; if management cannot articulate a specific revenue replacement pathway, the post-2028 bear case gains credibility; cross-ref Q19 ACH long-run competitive erosion scenario

Q8 — FX pass-through and tender exposure

Question: "With ~40% of revenues in SEK-soft currencies (EUR, USD, GBP) — how much FX is naturally hedged through cost base, and what's the net P&L sensitivity per 10% SEK appreciation?"

Expected answer: SOBI will cite a natural hedge through EUR-denominated COGS from manufacturing partners; net FX sensitivity is likely SEK 200–400m EBIT per 10% SEK move; some financial hedging in place

Good answer signature: Explicit natural hedge percentage quantified; residual financial hedge programme described; sensitivity range anchored to a specific FX assumption

Bad answer signature: "FX is broadly managed and is not a major concern" without quantification; no sensitivity disclosure

Follow-up probe: "Does the Sanofi collaboration royalty income create a structural USD long position — and how is that managed?"

Diagnostic note: FX is an often-dismissed but material earnings volatility source for SOBI; a CFO who cannot quantify FX sensitivity is either hedged by structure (good) or unaware (bad)

5. THEME §3 — Capital Allocation + Balance Sheet

J-front verdict: Capital allocation is the PIVOTAL question for the current investment case — SOBI carries a healthy balance sheet, generates strong FCF, and the use of that cash (M&A vs buyback vs dividend vs organic R&D) will define the IRR trajectory

Q9 — M&A philosophy: size, therapy area, and returns hurdle

Question: "What's the ideal bolt-on acquisition for SOBI in the next 24 months — therapy area, stage of asset, size range, and the minimum ROIC hurdle you'd apply?"

Expected answer: Management will likely describe a preference for rare-disease assets in the haematology/immunology adjacency; Stage IIb/III or commercial-stage preferred; size likely SEK 5–20bn range; ROIC hurdle probably 12–15% on internal models

Good answer signature: Explicit therapy area and stage criteria; quantified ROIC hurdle; acknowledgment of what they've passed on and why

Bad answer signature: "We look at everything in our therapeutic areas" without criteria; no acknowledgment of prior M&A misses (e.g., the Synagis assets); no ROIC threshold

Follow-up probe: "What's the highest-multiple asset you've seriously evaluated and walked away from in the last 18 months — and what made you say no?"

Diagnostic note: Willingness to name a deal they passed on is the strongest signal of capital discipline; refusal to engage is a yellow flag; cross-ref Q19 ACH empire-builder failure mode

Q10 — Buyback and dividend: returns framework under FCF abundance

Question: "At current FCF generation and leverage, SOBI has significant excess capital — what's the explicit framework for choosing between buybacks, special dividends, and M&A at any given point in time?"

Expected answer: Management will describe a capital hierarchy: M&A first if value-accretive, then buyback if trading below intrinsic value estimate, then progressive dividend; will likely reference a net debt/EBITDA comfort range

Good answer signature: Explicit capital hierarchy stated; internal intrinsic value framework acknowledged; net debt/EBITDA target range given

Bad answer signature: "We are committed to creating shareholder value" without explicit hierarchy; no leverage target; no framework for deciding when buyback beats M&A

Follow-up probe: "If no M&A is completed in the next 18 months, what's the default capital return mechanism — and at what share price does buyback become the dominant use of FCF?"

Diagnostic note: Absence of an explicit capital hierarchy in a cash-generative company is itself a signal of governance immaturity; bull case requires disciplined capital allocation with explicit RONIC discipline

Q11 — Working capital management: inventory risk in haemophilia products

Question: "Haemophilia biologics require significant safety stock — what's the inventory-to-revenue ratio for Elocta/Alprolix, and has there been any write-down risk as competitive dynamics shift?"

Expected answer: CFO will describe a managed inventory level tied to contract supply obligations; likely 60-90 day safety stock; will likely acknowledge that volume declines could create inventory mismatch risk

Good answer signature: Explicit days-inventory-outstanding figure; acknowledgment of inventory risk as Alhemo cannibalises Elocta/Alprolix in inhibitor patients; write-down policy described

Bad answer signature: "Inventory is managed per industry norms" without quantification; no acknowledgment of cannibalization inventory risk

Follow-up probe: "If Alhemo uptake causes Elocta volume to decline 10% faster than expected — what's the inventory write-down exposure and how quickly can you reduce manufacturing commitments?"

Diagnostic note: This is a CFO-specific probe that most buy-side investors skip; inventory write-down risk in a product transition is a classic false-friend earnings hit; cross-ref Q19 ACH near-term EPS miss scenarios

6. THEME §4 — Strategy + Competitive Position

Q12 — Competitive response to next-gen haemophilia agents

Question: "Roche's emicizumab and emerging gene therapy assets could structurally compress the prophylaxis-infusion market — what's SOBI's explicit 5-year response strategy if subcutaneous agents reach 30%+ share?"

Good answer signature: Explicit modelling of a 30% share loss scenario and its revenue/EBIT impact; clear articulation of which patient segments are defensible and why; acknowledgment that gene therapy timeline is uncertain but not zero

Bad answer signature: "Gene therapy faces significant durability challenges" without engagement with the 3-5 year commercial risk; deflection to inhibitor patients as protected; no revenue sensitivity analysis offered

Follow-up probe: "Which of SOBI's current revenue streams is most exposed if gene therapy achieves 20% patient penetration in haemophilia A by 2030 — and what's the revenue impact in your internal modelling?"

Diagnostic note: Management unwillingness to engage with the gene therapy scenario is the clearest bearish signal in a meeting; bull case requires SOBI to have a credible answer for haemophilia-post-gene-therapy; false-friend is management correctly identifying near-term safety as a barrier while ignoring the medium-term penetration risk

Q13 — Regulatory positioning: rare disease designations + HTA leverage

Question: "How is SOBI using orphan drug designations and HTA engagement to protect pricing power across Alhemo and the immunology portfolio — and where is that strategy most at risk?"

Expected answer: SOBI will describe active orphan designation maintenance; will cite specific HTA early dialogue processes in EU5; pricing protection is strongest in indications with ≤5,000 patient populations; HTA risk is highest in broader-use Kineret indications

Good answer signature: Specific example of HTA early engagement translating to pricing uplift; acknowledgment of HTA pressure in Sweden/Germany where AMNOG/TLV create pricing headwinds; explicit risk-rating of which products face most HTA pressure

Bad answer signature: Generic rare-disease pricing narrative without country-level specificity; no acknowledgment of TLV (Swedish reimbursement authority) creating near-home-market pricing pressure

Follow-up probe: "Has any SOBI product faced a significant TLV or AMNOG pricing re-evaluation in the last 24 months — and what was the outcome?"

Diagnostic note: As a Swedish company, SOBI's home-market regulatory relationship is ironically a risk factor (TLV is aggressive on rare disease pricing); a management team that can't articulate its HTA strategy in Sweden specifically is under-prepared

Q16 — Crisis playbook: how management handled Kineret COVID demand collapse

8. THEME §6 — Risk + Downside (Pre-mortem Cross-ref)

J-front verdict: Three failure modes dominate — haemophilia platform erosion faster than Alhemo can offset, Kineret floor lower than modelled, and M&A capital misallocation; questions here test each against management's own stress tests

Q17 — Bear-case probe: what keeps the CEO awake at night?

Question: "If you had to nominate the single development that would most disappoint you over the next 18 months — operationally, not macro — what would it be?"

Expected answer: Management will typically cite Alhemo reimbursement delays or a clinical setback in the pipeline; the honest answer is haemophilia volume erosion from subcutaneous competition moving faster than modelled

Good answer signature: Specific, falsifiable answer tied to a named product, geography, or competitive event; not a generic "macro uncertainty" deflection; willingness to say what the internal monitoring trigger is

Bad answer signature: "Our portfolio is well-diversified and we monitor risks closely" non-answer; any answer that describes macro/FX/political risk rather than operational/competitive risk

Follow-up probe: "What's the specific metric or data point you would monitor that would signal that downside is becoming real — and what's its current trend?"

Diagnostic note: CEO who gives a specific, internal-facing answer is demonstrating operational fluency; CEO who deflects to macro is either not operationally engaged or is deliberately avoiding the real bear case; this question is a management-quality discriminator independent of the factual answer

Q18 — Regulatory exposure: EMA lifecycle risk on key assets

Question: "Are there any EMA post-marketing commitments or label restrictions pending for Alhemo or Kineret that could affect commercial positioning or market access in 2025–2026?"

Expected answer: Management will likely describe standard post-marketing surveillance obligations; may acknowledge a specific PASS (Post-Authorisation Safety Study) for Alhemo given its novel MOA; Kineret label is well-established

Good answer signature: Proactive disclosure of any specific EMA commitments; timeline for PASS completion; willingness to describe what happens commercially if a PASS surfaces unexpected safety signals

Bad answer signature: "We are in full compliance with EMA requirements" without specifics; no acknowledgment of the conditional approval risk embedded in novel mechanism agents like fitusiran

Follow-up probe: "Fitusiran/Alhemo has a black-box thrombosis warning — what's the real-world monitoring burden this places on prescribers, and is that creating switching friction?"

Diagnostic note: The thrombosis warning on fitusiran is the most under-discussed commercial risk in SOBI's Alhemo launch; if prescribers face onerous monitoring protocols, adoption will be structurally slower than modelled; this is a false-friend risk — the label approval masks commercial friction

Q19 — Pipeline failure mode: what happens if the immunology pipeline disappoints?

9. CROSS-CUTTING META + NON-OBVIOUS QUESTIONS

J-front verdict: Non-obvious questions designed to surface what SOBI management would NOT volunteer are concentrated around three vectors — the internal failure narrative, the Sanofi relationship power dynamic, and the talent/culture depth below C-suite

Non-obvious angle 1 — The Sanofi relationship power dynamic

Question: "In the Elocta/Alprolix collaboration, who has commercial decision-making authority — and in the last 12 months, has there been any disagreement between SOBI and Sanofi on commercial strategy?"

  • Why non-obvious: Every investor asks about the collaboration economics; almost none ask about the power dynamic within the JV-like structure; the answer reveals whether SOBI is a genuine co-equal or a junior licensee
  • Expected answer: Management will describe a collaborative structure with shared governance; will be unlikely to acknowledge explicit disagreements
  • Good answer: Candid acknowledgment of a specific area of tension (e.g., pricing strategy in a specific market) and how it was resolved
  • Bad answer: "The relationship with Sanofi is excellent and well-structured" with no texture; this is the most-rehearsed non-answer in SOBI IR meetings
  • Diagnostic note: If SOBI lacks real commercial authority over Elocta/Alprolix, the revenue durability of that franchise is more exposed to Sanofi's strategic priorities than SOBI's own execution; this is a structural risk the market doesn't price

Non-obvious angle 2 — The talent depth probe (WH Smith CEO archetype)

Question: "If you had to identify the one person below the C-suite whose departure would most worry you — what function are they in, and what retention structure do you have in place?"

  • Why non-obvious: C-suite succession is standard governance; the below-C-suite talent dependency is the real operational fragility point in a specialty pharma company where one key medical affairs leader or HTA negotiator can be irreplaceable
  • Expected answer: CEO will name a function (likely regulatory/medical affairs or market access) rather than a name; may deflect on specifics for competitive reasons
  • Good answer: Specific function named with genuine reflection; acknowledgment that retention is an active management priority in that area; specific retention mechanism described
  • Bad answer: "We have a deep bench across all functions" generic response; or deflection to C-suite succession planning only
  • Diagnostic note: Medical affairs and HTA/market access talent is the rate-limiting factor in rare disease launch success; if SOBI has a single-point-of-failure in either function, Alhemo launch risk is higher than modelled

⚡ Non-obvious angle 3 — The internal accounting of strategic mistakes

Question: "What is the most expensive strategic mistake SOBI made in the 2016–2022 period, and what did it teach you about capital allocation?"

  • Why outlier-flagged ⚡: No standard sell-side analyst asks a sitting management team to explicitly characterise prior-regime mistakes; and yet this is the highest-information question in any management quality assessment — the Concept B "multiple truths coexist" probe — because the answer both reveals intellectual honesty AND tests whether the current team has genuinely learnt the right lessons from the prior regime
  • Expected answer: Management will likely reference the complexity of the legacy portfolio or the COVID demand forecasting challenge; unlikely to name a specific acquisition miss without prompting
  • Good answer: Named strategic error (e.g., over-paying for a specific asset, under-investing in pipeline at a specific stage, or misjudging a market entry) with explicit lesson and structural change in process as a result
  • Bad answer: "Each decision was made with the best information available at the time" revisionist framing with no accountability; or a very senior management team that reflexively attributes all errors to prior leadership only
  • Diagnostic note: A management team that cannot name its most expensive mistake is either (a) not intellectually honest, (b) genuinely new enough that they legitimately weren't present, or (c) coached into non-disclosure; all three are useful signal

⚡ Non-obvious angle 4 — The single-metric probe (cross-roads question)

Question: "What is the single internal metric — not in your investor presentations — that you look at first thing on Monday morning to know whether the business is on track?"

  • Why outlier-flagged ⚡: This is the asymmetric-information question — the metric management tracks privately but does not disclose publicly is the highest-signal data point available in any investor meeting; the answer reveals the true operational nerve centre vs the reported KPI architecture
  • Expected answer: CEO/CFO will likely name a customer or patient-flow metric (new patient starts for Alhemo, refill rates for Kineret, gross-to-net for Elocta) rather than a financial metric
  • Good answer: Specific named metric with a current directional trend and a threshold that would trigger concern; willingness to share the actual current reading
  • Bad answer: "We track a comprehensive dashboard" without naming one metric; or naming a metric already in investor presentations
  • Diagnostic note: Highest-return question in the meeting if answered candidly; if dodged, the non-answer itself is signal that management over-manages the investor narrative

Multiple-truths probe (Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 3)

Question: "I can build a credible 3-year model where Alhemo exceeds consensus by 30% AND a credible model where it disappoints by 30% — what is the single factor that determines which scenario plays out, and what's your current assessment of which way that factor is trending?"

  • Why load-bearing: This question explicitly acknowledges that both bull and bear cases are internally coherent and invites management to engage with the ambiguity rather than defaulting to the bull case
  • Expected answer: Management will likely name reimbursement timing in Germany/France as the single factor; or patient switching willingness as the key variable
  • Good answer: Names a specific factor, provides a current directional read, and is willing to say which scenario their internal data is currently pointing toward
  • Bad answer: "We are confident in the launch trajectory" without engaging with the bear-case scenario; refusal to name the single determining factor
  • Diagnostic note: Executives who refuse to engage with the bear case in a structured "I acknowledge it's plausible" framing are more likely to be managing the narrative than managing the business; this is the Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 3 test in its purest form
