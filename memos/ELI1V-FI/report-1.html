1. BLUF

Elisa Oyj is Finland's dominant telecommunications operator: the #1 mobile and integrated communications provider in a structurally rational three-player oligopoly, with a parallel and growing international SaaS and industrial AI export platform that is materially differentiated from any other European telco at comparable scale. The primary investment case in one sentence: a top-quartile GARP compounder — predictable, high-dividend-yield Finnish core with embedded software optionality that could, if confirmed, drive a SOTP re-rating over a 3-5 year horizon. This is an IG-stage orienting memo; the appropriate next step is Triaging. 2. Corporate Overview

Full name: Elisa Oyj
Ticker: ELI1V-FI (Nasdaq Helsinki)
Market capitalisation: approximately EUR 4.5-5.0bn (May 2026)
Headquarters: Helsinki, Finland
Sector: Integrated telecommunications (mobile, fixed, B2B managed services, SaaS/software)

Elisa is one of Finland's oldest and most institutionally embedded companies, tracing its origins to the Helsinki Telephone Association founded in 1882. It listed on the Helsinki Stock Exchange (subsequently Nasdaq Helsinki) and has been a continuously operating public company throughout. Over its modern history it has transitioned from a legacy wireline operator to an integrated mobile-first business, and in more recent years has added an international software export layer that is materially unusual for a European operator of its scale.

Shareholder structure: No controlling shareholder. The company is effectively 100% free-float in practice. The largest identifiable shareholder bloc is Finnish institutional capital — the major Finnish pension funds (Varma, Ilmarinen, Elo) typically hold 5-10% combined, supplemented by Finnish asset managers, domestic retail investors, and international institutional holders. There are no family, state, or strategic anchor shareholders that would distort governance or capital allocation incentives. This is a clean, minority-shareholder-friendly ownership structure — a positive differentiator relative to European telcos with state shareholder interference (Orange/French state, Telefonica/SEPI, OTE/Deutsche Telekom) or family controlling stakes.

Group structure: The business operates through four identifiable platforms:
- Elisa (Finland): Core operating entity covering Finnish consumer mobile, fixed broadband, pay-TV, and B2B enterprise services. This is the dominant segment by revenue (approximately 80-85% of group).
- Elisa Estonia: Mobile and fixed operator in Estonia. The #2 operator in the Estonian market (small market, approximately EUR 100-130m revenue, managed for profitable stability).
- Elisa Polystar: International SaaS subsidiary providing network analytics, operations intelligence, and network management software to telecommunications operators globally. Clients are primarily established mobile and fixed operators across APAC, Europe, and North America.
- Elisa IndustrIQ: Industrial AI and IoT subsidiary applying Elisa's data and connectivity capabilities to manufacturing, energy, and process industry clients. Principally Nordic-based clients but expanding internationally.

The combined SaaS/software cluster (Polystar + IndustrIQ) is estimated to represent approximately EUR 100-150m of group revenue at FY2024, growing at 20%+ annually.

4. Market Position

Finnish Mobile

Elisa is the unambiguous #1 Finnish mobile operator by revenue share, with approximately 40-45% of the Finnish mobile revenue market. The Finnish mobile market is a textbook three-player rational oligopoly:

  • Elisa: ~40-45% revenue share; market share leader; positioned as the premium quality operator
  • Telia Finland: ~30-35% revenue share; Swedish-owned; managed for profitability in the Telia Group Nordic portfolio
  • DNA / Telenor: ~25-30% revenue share; Norwegian-owned since Telenor acquired DNA in 2019; Telenor's established playbook in Nordic markets is to manage for margin, not share capture

The Finnish mobile market has approximately 1.9m consumer mobile subscribers for Elisa. Total SIM penetration in Finland is approximately 130% — one of the highest in Europe — meaning subscriber growth is structurally unavailable. The competitive game is therefore ARPU management, B2B mix, and retention — all of which Elisa leads on.

There is no credible fourth-entrant threat in Finnish mobile. New spectrum would be required, and the economics of building a fourth network in a small-geography market already served by three high-quality networks are not viable under current European operator economics. Regulatory appetite in Finland for forcing a fourth entrant is not evident. This structural stability is a permanent feature of the Finnish market, not a cyclical condition — it is one of the most important positive structural facts about the investment case.

🚩 RARE: Finnish mobile market concentration and competitive stability place it in the top decile of European mobile markets for rational pricing behaviour and oligopolistic value preservation. Most comparable European markets (UK, France, Germany, Italy, Spain) have experienced at least one episode of disruptive competitive repricing in the past decade. Finland has not.

Finnish Fixed Broadband and TV

Elisa operates in both fibre and cable fixed broadband in Finland, with a strong position in the major population centres (Helsinki, Tampere, Turku, Espoo). Finland has one of the highest fibre-to-the-home penetration rates in Europe, with approximately 60-70% of fixed connections either fibre or high-speed capable. Elisa has invested in urban fibre infrastructure and is the leading fibre-to-the-home operator in the key population centres.

The Finnish fixed broadband market does not face the FWA (Fixed Wireless Access) disruption threat that is relevant in Germany or Italy, where incumbent fibre deployment lagged and created a window for 5G-FWA substitution. Finland's existing network depth makes FWA economically unattractive as a disruptive entry strategy against Elisa's fixed portfolio.

Pay-TV is a complementary product to fixed broadband, with Elisa offering bundled TV and broadband packages. This is a competitive but not structurally challenged market.

Finnish B2B / Enterprise

Elisa has a well-developed enterprise and SME franchise covering managed connectivity, private networks, cloud and security services, and government accounts. Enterprise contract tenures are typically multi-year and exhibit lower price sensitivity than consumer segments. The B2B segment is the most strategically interesting near-term growth driver within the Finnish core, as IT-OT convergence increases the scope of services Elisa can deliver to enterprise clients beyond simple connectivity (managed security, IoT connectivity management, private 5G for industrial facilities).

Government and public sector accounts are a meaningful component of the Finnish B2B franchise — a stable, low-churn revenue base.

Estonia

Elisa is the #2 mobile and fixed operator in Estonia. Estonia is a small market (approximately 1.3m population) but digitally advanced — one of the most connected and digital-government-driven societies in Europe. The Estonian market is a two-to-three-player oligopoly (Telia Estonia is #1; Elisa #2; Tele2 Estonia is the third player). Estonia is managed as a stable, margin-accretive, FCF-generative operation — not a growth engine. Revenue contribution is estimated at EUR 100-130m, a modest but not insignificant 5-7% of group.

Estonia carries a latent geopolitical tail risk (proximity to Russia; NATO eastern flank considerations) that has not materialised into operational disruption over the past decade but should be logged as a monitoring item, particularly given the current risk-off market environment (Iran conflict, general geopolitical uncertainty as of March-April 2026).

International SaaS: Elisa Polystar

Elisa Polystar is the most strategically distinctive element of the group. Founded from Elisa's own internal network operations and analytics capabilities — tools built to manage Elisa's own network — Polystar was productised and commercialised externally as a standalone SaaS offering. The product suite covers: real-time network analytics, quality of experience monitoring, network operations intelligence, and AI-assisted network management for telecom operators.

Polystar's clients are primarily established mobile and fixed operators in APAC, Europe, and North America. The product is positioned as an independent, operator-agnostic alternative to vendor-tied OSS/BSS analytics from Nokia, Ericsson, Amdocs, or NetCracker. This independence is a genuine selling point: Elisa is itself an operator, which gives Polystar credibility and operational authenticity that pure software vendors lack.

Revenue is estimated at approximately EUR 80-100m (part of the combined software platform EUR 100-150m estimate), growing at 20%+ annually. Client base is estimated at 50-80 global telco operators, though precise figures are not publicly disclosed. Customer concentration is a risk: global telco clients are large individual accounts; a single non-renewal is a material revenue event.

International Industrial AI: Elisa IndustrIQ

Elisa IndustrIQ applies IoT connectivity management, industrial AI, and data analytics to manufacturing, energy, and process industry clients. The proposition: Elisa has built and operated large-scale, reliability-critical network infrastructure for decades; IndustrIQ packages that operational expertise as a service for industrial clients who need reliable connectivity and data intelligence in operational (OT) environments.

IndustrIQ is earlier-stage than Polystar and is currently more Nordic-centric in its client base. Its expansion into broader European and international industrial markets is a medium-term strategic ambition. Revenue is estimated at EUR 30-60m and growing rapidly, though the competitive landscape is more fragmented and the product differentiation relative to larger industrial AI platforms (Siemens, ABB, AWS IoT Greengrass, Microsoft Azure IoT) is less clearly established than Polystar's in its niche.

Customer Satisfaction

🚩 RARE: Elisa consistently ranks #1 or #2 in Finnish consumer satisfaction surveys — measured by NPS, EPSI (European Performance Satisfaction Index), and Finnish operator-specific benchmarks. Annual mobile churn is estimated at approximately 10-14%, versus a European peer average of 18-24%. This is a genuinely rare characteristic for a European incumbent mobile operator. The practical value of the satisfaction advantage is: lower acquisition spend per subscriber maintained, higher pricing confidence, and a defensible ARPU premium against competitor discounting. Low churn is a compounding advantage — it improves EBITDA margin directly and indirectly through reduced marketing intensity.

5. Business Model

Consumer mobile (Finland): Subscription-based mobile services — voice, data, and content. Revenue is primarily recurring monthly subscription, with handset subsidies managed as operating cost rather than capitalised. ARPU is positive in real terms. The consumer mobile business is essentially annuity-quality: high-penetration market, stable subscriber base, managed for ARPU optimisation and cost efficiency rather than subscriber growth. Low churn amplifies the annuity quality.

Consumer fixed broadband and TV (Finland): Fibre and cable broadband subscriptions plus pay-TV packages. Revenue is recurring monthly. Bundle economics are favourable — broadband + TV bundles exhibit lower churn than standalone broadband. Fixed broadband ARPU has benefited from ongoing migration from slower legacy copper connections to fibre, which commands a modest price premium.

B2B / enterprise (Finland): Multi-year managed service contracts for enterprise connectivity, security, cloud, private networks, and IoT. Revenue is recurring and long-dated. Contract terms are typically 2-5 years with renewal cycles. The B2B model has a broader service scope than consumer — Elisa is not simply providing connectivity but managing the wider enterprise communications and operational IT environment. This broadens the revenue relationship per client, raises switching costs, and improves margin on the managed services revenue relative to raw connectivity.

Estonia: Effectively a mirror of the Finnish consumer + B2B model in a smaller market. Revenue is recurring mobile and fixed subscription. Estonia is not an innovation market for Elisa — it is managed for stable returns.

Elisa Polystar (SaaS): Annual recurring revenue (ARR) model. Telco clients pay for access to Polystar's network analytics platform on a subscription basis, scaled to network size and volume of data processed. This is a classic SaaS model: high gross margins on incremental revenue (70-80%+ at the product level), low marginal cost per additional user, and customer stickiness driven by data integration depth and workflow embedding. The longer a telco client uses Polystar, the more embedded the product becomes in their operational workflow — a genuine switching cost accumulation mechanism.

Elisa IndustrIQ (SaaS/platform): Similar ARR model, with some elements of project delivery and professional services at contract initiation. Industrial IoT contracts involve a setup phase (connectivity hardware, data integration, platform configuration) followed by an ongoing software subscription. This hybrid model has lower gross margins than pure-SaaS Polystar but builds operational stickiness through hardware-software integration.

Combined software economics: At an estimated group-level software revenue of EUR 100-150m growing 20%+, the platform is generating meaningful EBITDA contribution. If Polystar and IndustrIQ earn 25-35% EBIT margins (consistent with mature SaaS products in comparable niches), the software cluster contributes approximately EUR 25-45m EBIT on EUR 100-150m revenue — a RONIC of approximately 25-45% on the estimated capital base. This compares favourably to the core Finnish telco RONIC of approximately 12-18% on regulated/competitive infrastructure.

7. Strategic Priorities

Software platform international scaling: The defining strategic ambition for Elisa as a business over the next 3-5 years is growing Polystar and IndustrIQ into material businesses that attract their own valuation recognition. The strategic logic is coherent: Elisa built operational capabilities to manage its own network that are world-class by operator standards; those capabilities have been productised and are being sold to operators and industrials who need them but could not build them as efficiently internally. The question is whether the commercial execution can match the product logic. Management's track record on this specific objective — incremental growth rather than transformational M&A — is consistent with their wider capital allocation style: patient, organic, disciplined.

Finnish 5G monetisation: The 5G network rollout in Finland is well advanced. The consumer 5G revenue uplift is modest (faster data, incremental ARPU), but the B2B 5G revenue opportunity is more interesting: private 5G networks for industrial facilities, enterprise IoT, and network slicing for B2B clients. Elisa is positioned well for this through its existing B2B enterprise franchise and IndustrIQ's industrial connectivity capabilities. The timing and scale of B2B 5G monetisation is industry-wide uncertain — but Elisa's operational credibility with enterprise clients is a genuine competitive advantage over pure-software competitors trying to sell industrial IoT without an operator's track record.

B2B mix shift: Elisa has been deliberately growing B2B managed services as a share of group revenue. This is a rational and sustained strategic priority that improves revenue quality (multi-year contracts, higher switching costs, better ARPU) without requiring capital-intensive infrastructure investment. The B2B mix shift is the most predictable and near-term value-creating strategic lever within the Finnish core.

Capital discipline: Elisa has committed to maintaining its dividend policy and avoiding large M&A. This is a consistent constraint on strategy — management will not sacrifice the dividend or the balance sheet to accelerate software growth. The implication is that software platform growth is paced by internally generated operating cash flows, which may limit the growth velocity relative to well-funded software-native competitors but also eliminates dilution risk or leverage risk for existing shareholders.

Estonia optimisation: Estonia is managed for stable profitability, not growth. Strategic priority is maintaining #2 market position and extracting FCF — not accelerating share gain or expanding into adjacent Baltic markets.

#A — Key Analytical Observations vs European Telco Cohort

Four observations stand out when Elisa is benchmarked against the broader European telco universe:

First, the Finnish market structure is genuinely unusual. A stable three-player oligopoly with no fourth-entrant threat and no period of sustained disruptive pricing in the past decade is an outlier characteristic in European telecoms. Most markets have at least one structural competitive disruption factor: the UK has Sky/Virgin Media competition in fixed; France has Iliad's chronic ARPU pressure on mobile; Germany has 1&1's fourth-entrant build; Italy has Iliad Italy and the long-running pricing war. Finland has none of these. This structural advantage is permanent and priced imperfectly by investors who apply a sector discount to all European telcos without distinguishing between market structures.

Second, the customer satisfaction and churn profile is a compound moat rather than a single-period metric. Elisa's estimated 10-14% annual mobile churn (vs 18-24% European peer average) means the business retains approximately 5-10 percentage points more of its subscriber base per year than the peer average. Over a 5-year period this compounds into a material structural advantage in operating cost (lower CAC and retention spend) and pricing confidence. This moat is durable — it reflects a decade of operational culture and investment in service quality that competitors cannot replicate in 2-3 years even with significant commercial investment.

Third, the SaaS export platform is genuinely differentiated. No other European telco at comparable scale (EUR 4.5-5.0bn market cap) has successfully productised its internal operational capabilities and built a global B2B SaaS business on top of the core. DKSH-style platform thinking (monetising operational capabilities beyond the core business) applied to a telco is structurally interesting — and the RONIC differential between the software platform and core telco infrastructure makes the incremental capital allocation logic compelling. The question is execution pace, not strategic logic.

Fourth, the capital allocation discipline is objectively superior to Sub-A peers on a comparable-period basis. Vodafone, BT, Telefonica, and Orange have all executed at least one major acquisition or leverage event in the past decade that has constrained subsequent capital returns or required strategic restructuring. Elisa has not. The track record of maintaining a high dividend, growing it, avoiding large M&A, and building software organically is coherent and shareholder-value-preserving in a way that most European telco track records are not.
