Driver 1: HVDC Supply Bottleneck -- Structural Pricing Power

The global HVDC submarine cable market is in a structural supply deficit that underpins the entire bull case for the cable triopoly. This is the tide that lifts all boats -- but it lifts Nexans more because its margin is furthest below the achievable level.

  • Judgement: This is the single most important structural driver -- 80% probability the bottleneck sustains through 2028. Demand is legislated (EU Grids Package EUR1.2 trillion 2024-2040), supply cannot respond quickly (3-5 year factory build times, EUR500m+ per facility), and the Big Three control ~75% of the market [C+AS] #J
  • Judgement: When capacity utilisation runs at 90%, cable manufacturers can be selective on projects and capture above-normal margins -- this is the primary margin lever and explains why Prysmian hit 20.9% Transmission margin in Q4 2025 [C] #J
  • Analysis: Morgan Stanley forecasts a EUR3bn (15%) structural supply deficit by 2028 against EUR14bn market demand -- this protects incumbent pricing power even if individual projects slip [C+AS] #A
  • Analysis: New entrants are distant: LS Cable broke ground on a South Korean submarine cable factory in Oct 2025, but European qualification takes years; NKT's Cologne expansion adds incremental capacity from 2027 (~EUR100m investment); no new Western HVDC factory is operational before 2028 [C+AS] #A
  • Analysis: Factory lead times exceed 800 days; waiting lists extend 5+ years; HV pricing has effectively doubled on new orders per industry expert calls [C+AS] #A
  • EU investment backing is substantial: EIB committed record EUR11bn to energy grids in 2025 (3x 2023); ENTSO-E estimates EUR3.4bn/year in interconnector investment; France's RTE plans to double interconnection to 18GW by 2030 [C] #I
  • Analysis: The bear scenario is demand disappointing (offshore wind permitting delays, fiscal austerity) while supply expands (NKT Cologne, Prysmian expansion, Asian entry) -- even in this scenario, the bottleneck likely persists through 2029-2030 given the scale of EU commitment [C] #A
  • Analysis: Sensitivity: if utilisation stays >85% through 2028, EBITDA trajectory to EUR1,150m is credible; if utilisation drops to 75%, pricing pressure emerges and EBITDA settles at EUR900-950m; below 70%, Nexans is most exposed as smallest scale player [C] #A

Driver 2: GSI Resolution -- Binary Catalyst

The Great Sea Interconnector is EUR1.43bn of backlog (~18%) suspended due to Greek-Turkish geopolitical tensions. Resolution = backlog confirmation + sentiment boost. Cancellation = material hit + negative signalling.

  • Judgement: GSI is a genuine binary catalyst with favourable asymmetry -- cancellation is partly priced in (stock at 52-week lows, GSI excluded from guidance, Jefferies cut Transmission multiple from 10x to 8x), while resumption would surprise positively [C+AS] #J
  • Judgement: Probability assessment: resumption with revised timeline 35%, indefinite delay (limbo 2+ years) 40%, permanent cancellation 25% -- institutional momentum (EIB, EU CEF EUR657m) favours eventual completion but Turkey's opposition is a hard constraint [C] #J
  • Analysis: Latest development (Apr 2026): Greece and Cyprus jointly wrote to EIB requesting a new due diligence study -- this is re-engagement, not abandonment; a European Parliament question was raised on GSI and EU energy security [C] #A
  • Analysis: Management explicitly excluded GSI from 2026 guidance and is seeking short-term IMR contracts to optimise MI cable line utilisation -- but Jefferies is highly sceptical these lower-visibility repair projects can substitute for a EUR1.2bn subsea contract [C+AS] #A
  • MI is a mature, diminishing technology being rapidly displaced by XLPE in modern subsea awards, making replacement orders on MI lines exceptionally difficult [AS]
  • Jefferies slashed 2026-2027 Transmission EBITDA estimates by 14% and 19% respectively [AS]
  • Analysis: Financial impact: if resumed, no direct 2026 EBITDA impact but backlog confirmation supports 2027-28 trajectory; if cancelled, EUR1.43bn backlog write-down but Nexans likely redeploys vessel time given supply-constrained market; net EBITDA impact -EUR50-100m over 2027-2030 [C] #A
  • Turkey's opposition (warships blocking surveys, July 2024) reflects deep Eastern Mediterranean tensions, not a temporary blockage; Cyprus's finance minister questions economic viability [C] #I
  • Open Question: Is the EIB due diligence request a genuine precursor to resumption or a face-saving exercise? If GSI dies, does it set a precedent that discourages other large interconnectors in geopolitically sensitive areas? #OQ

Driver 3: Nexans Electra -- Capacity Step-Change

Nexans' third cable-laying vessel, Electra, roughly doubles installation throughput. Vessel availability is the binding constraint on converting EUR8bn backlog into revenue.

  • Judgement: HIGH conviction driver -- Electra delivery (Q2 2026) is mechanical, not speculative; hull launched Nov 2025 on schedule, sea trials spring 2026. This directly addresses the bottleneck limiting Transmission revenue conversion from backlog [C+AS] #J
  • Analysis: 13,500-tonne capacity, four-cable simultaneous lay, advanced hybrid power system; investment EUR200m+. Global cable-laying vessel capacity is severely constrained (~10-15 capable vessels worldwide for HV submarine installation) [C] #A
  • Analysis: Vessel ownership is a competitive moat: owning three vessels gives Nexans scheduling flexibility and eliminates charter market dependency (day rates rising sharply); Prysmian has Leonardo da Vinci (operational), NKT relies more on chartered vessels [C] #A
  • Analysis: Financial impact: Electra enables 2-3 additional large submarine cable projects per year; at average EUR200-500m project value and 12-15% Transmission margins, each additional project adds EUR25-75m EBITDA; full impact flows through 2027-2028 [C] #A
  • The impending delivery marks the culmination of a massive multi-year strategic CAPEX cycle; management anticipates post-2026 structural reduction in Transmission-related capex [AS] #I
  • Risks: commissioning delays (weather, technical issues), first-project learning curve, fleet scheduling integration -- manageable risks but could delay revenue uplift by 1-2 quarters [C] #I

Driver 6: Data Centre Electrification -- Underpriced Demand Tailwind

Data centres need cables across all three Nexans segments. The demand is certain; Nexans' capture rate is the uncertainty.

  • Judgement: This is a 3-5 year driver, not a 12-month catalyst -- European data centre power demand growing ~15% annually, with 150 GW of new demand expected by 2030; data centres projected to double share of global electricity consumption to 3% by 2030 [C+AS] #J
  • Analysis: Every hyperscale facility needs: HV grid connection (Transmission), MV distribution (Grid), LV internal cabling (Connect) -- Nexans plays across all three; Dublin and Amsterdam pausing new data centre approvals due to grid constraints signals the infrastructure bottleneck [C+AS] #A
  • Analysis: Nexans' superconducting cable R&D (Stella Nova, Hanover) targets data centre applications -- if commercially viable, this is a first-mover technology advantage in ultra-high-density power distribution [C] #A
  • Analysis: Quantification is difficult: data centre cable demand is embedded across all segments and not separately reported; consensus models likely underestimate incremental contribution because it is diffused [C] #A
  • Spain and Italy are Europe's strongest data centre growth zones -- Nexans has manufacturing presence in both [C] #I
  • Nexans' Q4 2025 Connect segment saw 10.9% organic growth driven specifically by data centre infrastructure deliveries [AS] #I
  • Management is heavily targeting commercial infrastructure, gigafactories, and data centres to offset residential softness [AS] #I
