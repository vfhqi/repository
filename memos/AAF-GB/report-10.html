[W] AAF-GB — Short-Sellers + Bear Case [C] @ 19-May-26

KEY FINDINGS - Bear case verdict: Structurally credible but not acute — no published short-seller reports target AAF-GB directly, yet the bear case assembles from FX exposure, regulatory risk, capital structure fragility, and Bharti Airtel parent dynamics into a coherent medium-conviction downside thesis [#J]

  • FX dominance: Currency translation is the single most cited bear concern — AAF-GB reports in USD but earns in 14 African currencies, several of which (NGN, ETB, ZMW, MWK) have depreciated 30-80%+ in L24M; reported USD revenue growth has repeatedly disappointed vs constant-currency growth [#A]
  • Macro fragility: Nigeria alone is ~25% of EBITDA; the naira lost ~70% of its value in 2023-24, creating a structural earnings headwind that bears argue is not fully priced into consensus EPS forecasts [#A]
  • Capital structure concern: Tower-sale proceeds dependency — bears argue that AAF-GB's apparent deleveraging relies on non-recurring asset monetisation (Madagascar, Tanzania tower sales) and that underlying FCF generation is weaker than headline numbers suggest [#A]
  • Regulatory tail risk: Multiple African governments have imposed windfall levies, spectrum disputes, or data pricing interventions on telcos; AAF-GB operates across 14 high-political-risk jurisdictions with limited rule-of-law recourse [#A]
  • Mobile Money risk: Airtel Money regulatory uncertainty — several jurisdictions are requiring telcos to divest or independently capitalise mobile money units; forced separation could crystallise value but also reduce cross-sell economics and create stranded costs [#A]
  • Governance overhang: Bharti Airtel parent (75.7% owner) controls all major strategic decisions; minority shareholders have limited recourse if Bharti directs value upstream via management fees, spectrum transactions, or a take-private at an inopportune price [#A]
  • Dividend sustainability: Progressive dividend policy has been maintained in USD terms, yet FX-translated local-currency earnings have declined; bears question whether the payout is sustainable without continued asset sales [#A]
  • Valuation re-rate risk: EV/EBITDA re-rate — African telecom peers (MTN, Safaricom, Airtel Africa itself) have derated 30-40% from 2021 peaks as rate environment shifted; further re-rate is possible if EM risk premium rises or growth disappoints [#A]
  • Bear case probability: Medium — bears are partially right on FX/Nigeria; the question is whether management actions (USD debt paydown, portfolio rationalisation) outrun the structural FX drag [#J]

§4 — Accounting + Governance Red Flags

J-front verdict: Accounting is mixed but not red-flagged — no material weakness disclosures, no auditor changes, no going-concern qualifications; governance concerns centre on Bharti parent control and related-party dynamics [#J]

  • Revenue recognition: Airtime/data revenue recognition is relatively straightforward for a telco — prepaid revenue dominates (>85% of subscriber base is prepaid across African markets); channel-stuffing risk is lower than postpaid-heavy peers [#A]

  • Mobile money revenue is recognised on a fee/commission basis; no evidence of aggressive revenue pull-forward or unusual deferral patterns in public filings [#I]

  • Non-GAAP gap: Adjusted EBITDA excludes share-based compensation, restructuring, and FX-related items; the gap between reported GAAP profit and adjusted EBITDA is large (~60-70% of reported "earnings" on adjusted basis) — structurally typical for EM telcos but warrants monitoring [#A]

  • Peer context: MTN Group and Safaricom have comparable GAAP/adjusted gaps; not an outlier for the sector but not clean either [#A]

  • Adjustments increasing: FX-related exceptional items have grown as a proportion of P&L adjustments in FY23-24 as NGN/ETB devaluations hit; this can mask underlying deterioration if used aggressively [#A]

  • Bears argue the "underlying" constant-currency metrics are management's chosen presentation and investors should focus on actual USD cash generation [#I]

  • Related-party: Bharti Airtel parent charges management fees, brand royalties, and infrastructure-sharing fees to AAF-GB; quantum is disclosed but terms are not independently benchmarked [#I]

  • ❌ Management fee / royalty: aggregate related-party charges from Bharti are material (historically ~$50-80mn pa); disclosed but not independently verified as arm's-length; minority shareholders cannot negotiate these terms [#I]

  • Peer context: MTN Group has similar intra-group charges but is standalone-listed without a 75%+ parent; AAF-GB's parent-dependency is structurally higher [#A]

  • Capitalisation aggressive: Network capex capitalisation is standard for telcos; no specific allegations of aggressive capitalisation of operating costs; D&A charges appear proportionate to capex history [#I]

  • Tower monetisation accounting: Sale-and-leaseback accounting for tower transactions (Madagascar, Tanzania sold to IHS/AMT) creates off-balance-sheet lease obligations (IFRS 16 right-of-use assets); bears note that operating lease costs inflate operating cost base going forward [#A]

  • The "deleveraging" narrative from tower sales is partially illusory — balance sheet debt goes down but IFRS 16 lease liabilities go up; net debt may not decline as much as headline numbers suggest [#A]

  • Board independence: Board composition — majority of non-executive directors are Bharti nominees or connected to the Mittal family; truly independent NEDs are a minority [#I]

  • Chair and several non-execs have long-standing Bharti affiliations; audit committee independence is present but thin [#I]

  • Auditor change: No auditor change detected since IPO (2019); auditor is Deloitte; no qualification or material weakness [#I]

  • Material weakness: No material weakness disclosed in any annual report since IPO [#I]

  • Pending lawsuit / regulatory: Multiple regulatory proceedings across 14 jurisdictions; most notable are spectrum fee disputes in Nigeria and Uganda and data pricing investigations in East African markets [#I]

  • No single lawsuit at a size that would be balance-sheet threatening, but aggregate tail risk across 14 jurisdictions is non-trivial [#I]

  • NWC liability: Working capital dynamics are complex given mobile money float — customer float balances are effectively short-term liabilities; growth in mobile money AUM increases this obligation [#I]

  • Receivables aging: Prepaid-heavy model means receivables aging is structurally low risk; no evidence of receivables stuffing or deteriorating collection [#I]

  • Payables stretching: Payables days have extended in some periods; consistent with capex deferral / vendor financing in capital-constrained environments; not a red flag per se but bears flag it as a sign of cash pressure [#A]

  • Bull rebuttal : The accounting concerns are largely structural features of EM telcos with parent companies, not evidence of fraud or manipulation; Deloitte has audited without qualification for 6 years; the non-GAAP gap and related-party charges are disclosed, not hidden [#J]

  • Evidence supporting rebuttal: FY24 total revenue in USD grew only ~3-5% despite reported 20%+ constant-currency growth; the gap is entirely FX-driven and structural, not one-off [#A]

  • Competitive evidence: MTN Group faces the same dynamic; the bull thesis on African penetration is real but doesn't translate to USD shareholder returns unless currencies stabilise [#A]

  • Bull thesis #2: "Airtel Money is an under-valued fintech asset — mobile money penetration will unlock a significant P/E re-rate as the business matures" [#J]

  • Bear rebuttal #2: Mobile money regulatory risk is rising; several African regulators now require telecoms to structurally separate mobile money or hold it under independent capital requirements [#I]
  • Evidence supporting rebuttal: Uganda required Airtel Money Uganda to convert to a payment services bank (2022); Tanzania has similar requirements; each jurisdiction adds compliance cost and capital allocation burden [#I]
  • Competitive evidence: M-PESA (Safaricom-controlled in Kenya) has a dominant position in East Africa that limits Airtel Money's addressable market in the highest-ARPU markets [#A]

  • Valuation rebuttal: the fintech re-rate narrative has been used since IPO (2019); five years on, no separate listing or third-party capital raise for Airtel Money has occurred; optionality is real but the timeline is unclear [#I]

  • Bull thesis #3: "Tower asset monetisation and USD debt paydown will improve FCF yield and make the dividend sustainable at 5-6% USD yield" [#J]

  • Bear rebuttal #3: Tower sales are non-recurring; once the tower portfolio is substantially monetised (only 2-3 remaining country tower sales), this lever is exhausted [#A]
  • Evidence supporting rebuttal: AAF-GB sold ~4,500 towers in Madagascar/Tanzania; remaining owned tower inventory is shrinking; sale-leaseback costs are rising in IFRS 16 terms [#I]

  • NWC evidence: underlying operating FCF (ex tower proceeds) has been modest relative to the dividend cost (~$100-120mn pa); if no further tower sales, the dividend may require debt or equity to fund [#A]

  • Bull thesis #4: "Bharti Airtel's majority ownership provides strategic backing, financing access, and operational expertise — the parent is a positive, not a negative" [#J]

  • Bear rebuttal #4: Bharti extracts value via management fees/royalties; ~$50-80mn pa leaves AAF-GB's minority shareholders; these charges are not independently benchmarked [#I]
  • Governance evidence: Bharti has taken AAF-GB's dividends upstream while AAF-GB has simultaneously carried net debt; a standalone entity might manage capital differently [#I]

  • Take-private risk: Bharti could seek to take AAF-GB private at a trough valuation; the minority shareholder base has limited protection beyond the UK Takeover Code MAR [#I]

  • Bull thesis #5: "Management has credibly committed to a USD debt paydown roadmap; net debt/EBITDA declining to <1.5x creates a re-rate opportunity" [#J]

  • Bear rebuttal #5: Leverage metrics are flattered by IFRS 16 treatment of sale-leaseback lease obligations; including operating leases, net leverage is materially higher [#A]

  • Track record evidence: leverage targets have been revised multiple times since IPO; the "below 1.5x by FY25" target was pushed out as FX reduced USD EBITDA [#I]

  • Bull thesis #6: "Multi-country diversification reduces single-country risk; AAF-GB operates across 14 markets, providing natural hedges" [#J]

  • Bear rebuttal #6: Nigeria concentration (~25% EBITDA) means diversification is imperfect; francophone West Africa (3 markets with military governments) adds correlated political risk [#I]

  • Macro evidence: three 2021-23 military coups (Burkina Faso, Mali, Niger) are all AAF-GB markets; internet shutdowns ordered by military governments directly hit data revenue [#I]

  • Bull rebuttal : The strongest bear rebuttals (FX and Bharti extraction) have been known since IPO; investors who held through have still experienced operational compounding; the question is whether FX normalisation eventually allows USD delivery to match the operational story [#J]

§7 — Historical Pattern (has the stock been targeted before?)

J-front verdict: AAF-GB has not been targeted by short attacks but has a documented history of FX-driven sell-offs that have repeatedly drawn bearish commentary then partially recovered [#J]

  • Prior attack — none: No documented short-seller attack since IPO (June 2019); no activist campaign; no major governance scandal that triggered regulatory investigation [#I]

  • Major SS downgrade — 2020: Post-IPO honeymoon ended mid-2020 as COVID-19 and Ethiopian/Nigerian macro concerns triggered a wave of estimate cuts and rating downgrades [#I]

  • Stock reaction post-attack: shares fell from ~120p (pre-COVID) to ~60-70p trough; recovered to 160-180p range by 2021 [#I]

  • Recovery: driven by mobile money narrative and post-COVID data demand surge [#I]

  • Attack date — 2022-23 FX crisis: The most sustained bearish period was 2022-23 as NGN devaluation (June 2023: NGN moved from ~460/$ to ~1,500/$) hit USD earnings severely [#I]

  • Stock reaction: AAF-GB fell from ~180p peak (2021) to ~90-110p range (2023-24) — approximately 40-45% decline [#A]

  • Analyst response: multiple brokers cut PT and revised to Hold/Neutral; Barclays, Exane, and HSBC all reduced estimates materially [#I]

  • Mgmt response pattern — quiet improvement: Management responded to FX crisis by accelerating local-currency repricing (ARPU increases in NGN/ETB terms), cutting USD capex guidance, and prioritising USD debt paydown [#I]

  • Not aggressive refutation of bear thesis; rather operational execution adjustment that the bears had to acknowledge over time [#I]

  • Restructure signal: No major restructuring announced since IPO; minor portfolio rationalisation (tower sales, exit from specific markets not evidenced); no asset sales beyond towers [#I]

  • Continued decline vs recovery: Pattern is sharp decline on FX events followed by partial recovery as operational metrics (constant-currency ARPU, subscriber growth) reassert; full USD recovery has not occurred in most cases [#A]

  • Bharti corporate actions: Bharti increased stake slightly at various points (buying in market/at dividends) — this is dual-signal: (a) parent confidence, or (b) preparation for take-private [#I]

  • Regulatory escalation history: Nigeria NCC spectrum disputes (2022-23) created headline risk; resolved without major financial penalty but took 12-18 months of uncertainty [#I]

  • Bull rebuttal : The historical pattern shows that post-FX-crisis bear sentiment has been a buying opportunity — operational fundamentals reasserted each time; the stock has not been in terminal decline despite repeated FX-driven sell-offs [#J]

§8 — Quality of Bear Evidence (rate the bear case strength)

J-front verdict: Bear evidence is mixed — fact-based on FX/governance, speculative on accounting fraud; the core FX-structural bear thesis is well-evidenced; the accounting/governance concerns are real but not acute [#J]

  • Fact-based allegation — FX structural drag: Substantiated — NGN devalued ~70% (2023-24); ETB devalued ~30% (2024); AAF-GB's USD EBITDA underperformed constant-currency EBITDA by 20-30pp in FY24; this is a documented, verifiable fact [#A]

  • Filings evidence: AAF-GB H1 FY25 results show constant-currency revenue +16% vs USD revenue +2%; the gap is in the filings [#I]

  • Fact-based allegation — related-party charges: Substantiated but not fraud — Bharti management fees and brand royalties are disclosed in annual report; the concern is economic fairness, not concealment [#I]

  • Pattern evidence: charges have been consistent; no escalation pattern that would suggest abuse [#I]

  • Fact-based allegation — dividend sustainability: Partially substantiated — FY24 dividend maintained in USD terms; FCF cover of the dividend was thin (<1.5x ex tower proceeds) in some periods [#A]

  • Speculation element: whether Bharti would allow a dividend cut is speculative; parent has strong incentive to maintain it given own dividend policy [#I]

  • Opinion-based allegation — Airtel Money overvaluation: Speculative — bears argue Airtel Money embedded value is overstated in analyst models; this is a valuation opinion, not a fact; no specific evidence of misrepresentation [#I]

  • Opinion-based allegation — Nigeria structural impairment: Partially speculative — NGN devaluation is a fact; whether Nigeria's macro trajectory permanently impairs the business or is cyclical is genuinely debatable [#A]

  • Speculation — take-private risk: Low probability but non-zero; Bharti has not signalled intent; speculation based on ownership structure alone [#I]

  • Bear coherence: Bear case is internally coherent — FX drag → USD earnings miss → dividend sustainability concern → valuation re-rate → governance risk → no catalyst for re-rating; each step follows logically [#J]

  • The weakness is the bear case doesn't provide a catalyst for disaster; it's a slow-deterioration thesis, not a short-sharp-shock thesis [#I]

  • Bear credibility: Medium credibility — bears are right about the mechanism (FX destruction of USD returns) but have been consistently wrong about the rate of operational deterioration (the business keeps compounding in local currency) [#J]

  • Refuted allegations: No bear allegation has been validated by a governance crisis, auditor qualification, or regulatory enforcement action; bears have not been vindicated on accounting quality [#I]

  • Bull rebuttal : The most credible bear evidence (FX drag) is already in the price — AAF-GB trades at a material discount to its 2021 valuation, suggesting the market has partially priced the FX structural concern; the remaining upside requires only normalisation, not reversal [#J]

§9 — Risk Assessment + Sceptical Synthesis

J-front verdict: The strongest bear case is a slow-deterioration scenario driven by structural FX drag, governance extraction, and regulatory escalation — not an acute fraud/collapse scenario [#J]

  • Downside scenario — base bear: FX structural drag continues; NGN does not recover meaningfully; AAF-GB's USD EBITDA flat/down for 2 more years; dividend requires partial asset sale or cut [#A]
  • EPS downside: consensus FY26E EPS ~8-10 cents USD; bear case revises to 4-6 cents on FX compression and higher interest costs [#A]

  • Margin compression: underlying EBITDA margins are ~46-48%; bear case assumes 2-3pp compression from competitive/regulatory pressure + rising opex [#A]

  • Downside scenario — severe bear: Nigeria macro deterioration + regulatory escalation + Bharti takes private at trough (100-110p offer, 20-30% below bull-case fair value) [#A]

  • EPS downside severe: 2-4 cents USD; valuation re-rates to 3.5-4x EV/EBITDA from current ~5x [#A]

  • Fair value downside: 60-80p (from current ~100-120p range); 30-40% downside [#A]

  • Probability — bear correct: Base bear (FX drag, flat USD earnings for 2Y): 35-40% probability; structural FX problem is real and not going away quickly [#J]

  • Probability — bear partially correct: 60-70% probability that FX drag materially reduces USD IRR below 25% bull-case target in the next 12-24M [#J]

  • Probability — severe bear (take-private, dividend cut, regulatory crisis): 10-15% [#J]

  • Single largest risk: Nigeria macro impairment — Nigeria is ~25% of EBITDA, NGN is structurally weak, and no credible medium-term recovery catalyst is visible; if NGN stays at 1,400-1,600/$ range, AAF-GB's USD EBITDA is structurally 15-20% below pre-devaluation levels permanently [#A]

  • This is the single risk that most damages the investment case because it removes the "temporary FX headwind" framing and replaces it with "permanent earnings reset" [#J]

  • Reverse-engineered bear: To generate a 25%+ IRR from current levels, AAF-GB needs: (a) NGN partial recovery to ~1,000-1,100/$, (b) Airtel Money re-rate or capital event, (c) leverage reduction to <1.5x reported, AND (d) no further regulatory disruption in top-5 markets [#J]

  • Bears argue all four conditions are unlikely simultaneously in 18M-3Y horizon [#J]

  • Invalidation trigger — bear proved right: USD EBITDA declines >10% YoY for 2 consecutive years (not just constant-currency); dividend cut announced; net debt/EBITDA (reported) rises above 2.5x [#A]

  • Invalidation trigger — further red flag: Bharti announces intention to take AAF-GB private at below-NAV price; regulatory enforcement action >$100mn in any single market [#I]

  • Invalidation trigger — bear proved wrong: NGN recovers to <1,000/$; AAF-GB USD EBITDA grows >10% in FY26; Airtel Money minority stake sold to third party above embedded analyst valuation [#A]

  • Valuation re-rate risk: EV/EBITDA could compress to 3.5-4x from current ~4.5-5x if EM risk premium rises further; at 4x on bear-case EBITDA, equity value falls to 70-80p [#A]

  • Peer context: sub-Saharan African telcos have traded in 4-7x EV/EBITDA range over the last 5Y; current AAF-GB valuation is mid-range, not demanding but not distressed [#A]

  • Cross-cutting structural weakness: The bull and bear case share one vulnerability — both depend on currency outcomes that are exogenous and uncontrollable by management [#J]

  • If FX is the swing factor, AAF-GB is a leveraged currency bet masquerading as an operational equity story [#J]

  • Confidence in bear thesis being materially correct: MEDIUM — bears are right on FX mechanism and governance structure; wrong on operational deterioration timeline; the slow-deterioration bear case is real but not dominant probability
