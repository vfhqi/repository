§1 — Management Commentary Analysis (L18M earnings calls)

Tone Shifts, Hesitations, and Qualifying Language

A sequential analysis of Elekta's earnings calls over the last 18 months reveals a stark tonal evolution, moving from cautious optimism through frank admission of failure toward a decisive operational reset.

During the Q3 2025 call in February 2025, management's tone was noticeably cautious, utilizing qualifying language around execution timelines. The narrative leaned heavily on external factors — US installation delays, China market headwinds — rather than internal accountability. There was limited acknowledgement of structural competitive weakness.

By the Q1 2026 call in August 2025, management remained hesitant regarding the timeline for the Elekta Reset programme and progress toward margin recovery. The narrative continued to attribute underperformance to external market conditions and timing of installations, with no material acknowledgement of software competitive disadvantage.

The tone shifted dramatically during the Q2 2026 call in November 2025, which marked Just-Bomholt's first full earnings call as CEO. Management adopted a highly critical, diagnostic posture, bluntly stating that Elekta "is not performing at full potential." The language shifted from citing external market factors to acknowledging internal execution failures. Just-Bomholt set a new cultural tone of radical transparency that had been absent under the prior administration.

By the Q3 2026 call in March 2026, the tone transitioned again from reflective to highly assertive. Management spoke decisively about the rapid execution of their new operating model, highlighting tangible restructuring milestones and early signals of commercial stabilization.

Walk-Backs, Guidance Revisions, and Revised Assumptions

The most significant walk-back over the last 18 months occurred during the Q3 2025 earnings call, when Elekta delivered a significant profit warning. Originally projecting mid-single-digit sales growth and an improved EBIT margin, the company revised these targets sharply downward. This revision was heavily attributed to misjudged installation timelines in the United States and a weaker-than-expected contribution from the China market.

A second major revision occurred regarding the company's commercial backlog. In Q2 2026, the new management team announced a massive cancellation of SEK 2.2 billion in historical orders. Management utilized careful qualifying language here, framing this not as lost business but as the application of a "firmer interpretation of order criteria." While executives insisted this purge had no impact on near-term cash flow or revenue, it represented a material acknowledgement that prior order recognition had been optimistic and inconsistent.

Furthermore, management has revised its assumptions on R&D asset capitalization, absorbing a SEK 1.0 billion non-cash impairment charge to write off underperforming development assets. This accounting adjustment signals a significant reset of internal R&D portfolio quality standards.

The Unity Trajectory: Clinical Success vs. Commercial Reality

Elekta's narrative regarding its flagship MR-Linac platform, Unity, showcases a distinct divergence between clinical achievement and commercial delivery. Across multiple calls, management heavily promoted Unity's clinical milestones, frequently citing the milestone of 200 installed systems globally and highlighting peer-reviewed publications demonstrating the platform's clinical superiority in adaptive radiation therapy.

Executives repeatedly framed Unity as a cornerstone product that enables ultra-high hypofractionation — a treatment modality they positioned as a structural growth driver for radiation oncology globally.

However, during the Q2 2026 call, the new CEO provided a rare, non-consensus admission regarding the platform's commercial performance. Just-Bomholt explicitly stated that while Unity is a "wonderful product" making a clinical impact, commercial adoption has "not achieved what we hoped for." This represents a significant pivot from prior quarters where weak Unity sales were often masked by positive clinical milestones and revenue from upgrades of the installed base.

Moving forward, management stated they will apply these hard lessons to future R&D, shifting from massive monolithic platform bets toward modular, commercially validated product development cycles. Shorter development sprints and faster go-to-market feedback loops were highlighted as the new standard.

Margin Recovery and the Structural Cost-Out Programme

Management's commentary heavily prioritizes the recovery of profitability, continually anchoring investor messaging on the return to an EBIT margin above 14 percent. They also frequently reiterate their ambition to return gross margins to pre-pandemic levels of over 40 percent.

A key proof point was delivered in Q4 2025, when the company achieved an adjusted gross margin of 40.8 percent, briefly validating the management narrative. However, subsequent quarters faced renewed pressure, with the Q3 2026 gross margin settling at 38.3 percent. Management noted this was heavily suppressed by a 100-basis-point impact from US tariffs and a 130-basis-point negative impact from an unfavourable revenue mix, largely driven by a higher-than-expected proportion of low-margin Unity installations.

To structurally address profitability, management introduced a sweeping cost-reduction initiative labelled the "Elekta Reset." The primary operational focus is this plan, designed to simplify the organization by cutting approximately 450 positions, representing roughly 10 percent of the global workforce.

Executives project this will yield annualized run-rate savings of more than SEK 500 million, split between cost of goods sold (approximately 60 percent) and operating expenses (approximately 40 percent). Management confirmed in Q3 2026 that a SEK 417 million restructuring charge had been recognized, with actions expected to be substantially complete by the end of fiscal year 2026.

Competitive Positioning

Elekta consistently describes itself as the clear "number two" player in the global radiotherapy market, acknowledging Varian/Siemens Healthineers as the dominant leader. Rather than minimizing this lag, management attempts to frame it as an advantage, emphasizing that Elekta's smaller scale enables greater agility and a more customer-centric approach.

In China, management asserts they hold a market-leading position with a share in the high-30s. Despite intense pressure from local competitors like United Imaging, Elekta claims to be defending its position through deep localization strategies and a comprehensive service network.

Conversely, management openly acknowledges severe underperformance in the United States. They describe the US as the region where Elekta holds its lowest relative market share, explicitly noting the competitive entrenchment of Varian in US academic medical centres and large health systems. To reverse this, the narrative relies heavily on the newly FDA-cleared Elekta Evo system and the vendor-neutral Elekta ONE software platform as the primary competitive inflection catalysts.

Management Transition: Salford's Departure and Just-Bomholt's Arrival

The departure of former CEO Gustaf Salford and the incoming appointment of Jakob Just-Bomholt is viewed by experts as a generational shift in leadership philosophy. Salford is described by former Elekta executives as a consensus-oriented leader who was reluctant to make bold structural moves, prioritizing internal stakeholder harmony over commercial decisiveness.

In stark contrast, Just-Bomholt is described by former colleagues as a highly decisive, action-oriented executive with a strong commercial instinct. Experts who worked closely with Just-Bomholt at 3Shape emphasize his analytical rigour and his ability to rapidly assess an organisation's structural weaknesses and execute remediation plans with minimal delay.

Just-Bomholt's aggressive approach to change was immediately apparent, as he reportedly fired Elekta's entire top management team within weeks of taking the role. Internally, experts note that Elekta's engineering and product management teams had become disjointed, operating as separate business unit silos with limited coordination. Just-Bomholt is actively restructuring these engineering teams to diminish the power of isolated business unit fiefdoms and create greater organisational coherence. While he lacks a background in radiation oncology, his reputation as an ambitious but grounded commercial operator is viewed positively by former colleagues.

Unity MR-Linac: Clinical Promise vs. Commercial Reality

Expert commentary reveals a massive divergence between Elekta management's enthusiastic narrative regarding Unity and the platform's structural commercial limitations. The core issue is financial justification, as the Unity system is priced near $10 million, effectively double the cost of a premium conventional linear accelerator. Despite this massive upfront capital requirement, the system offers no preferential reimbursement rates in the United States or most other markets. Hospitals are reimbursed at the same rate for an MR-guided fraction as for a standard IMRT fraction, fundamentally undermining the investment case for most community health providers.

Furthermore, the operational throughput of the Unity system is significantly slower than standard linear accelerators. While a premium Varian or Elekta conventional linac can treat 25 to 35 patients per day, the Unity system is practically limited to 8 to 15 patients per day due to longer treatment fractions and mandatory pre-treatment MR imaging protocols.

Consequently, experts note that Unity adoption is almost entirely restricted to high-volume, elite academic medical centres with dedicated research budgets. Nationwide in the United States, MR-guided therapy is utilised in fewer than 5 percent of all oncology treatments, confirming the platform's niche status. Acknowledging these severe market limitations, former executives report that Elekta has recently scaled back its internal Unity production capacity and headcount, reallocating resources toward the development of the conventional Evo platform and the software-led Elekta ONE ecosystem.

The Software Ecosystem: MOSAIQ vs. ARIA and Epic

Perhaps the most damaging critique from the expert network centres on Elekta's software ecosystem, specifically the MOSAIQ oncology information system. Practicing oncologists report that the software lacks modern text editing capabilities, breaks down frequently during clinical workflows, and operates with an interface described as "clunky" and "reminiscent of Windows 1995."

While Elekta historically held a distinct software advantage after acquiring IMPAC in the 2000s, experts confirm that this advantage has been entirely eroded. MOSAIQ has received only incremental updates and lacks the modern workflow integration that hospital IT departments increasingly demand. The software lags significantly behind where it should be on a modern product development roadmap.

In direct contrast, Varian's ARIA platform is universally praised by clinical experts as the industry standard. Elekta's competitive position is further harmed by its restrictive, closed software policies. Former executives confirm that Elekta actively resists integration with third-party platforms, blocking academic research centres from installing specialist research modules and limiting the ability of hospital IT departments to customise or extend the platform.

While broader hospital EMR systems like Epic and Cerner are increasingly handling general clinical data management, expert commentary confirms that the oncology-specific workflow layer — treatment planning, dosimetry management, beam scheduling — still demands specialised platforms where Elekta is losing ground rapidly.

China Market Dynamics and Emerging Competitors

Management frequently highlights China as a stronghold where Elekta commands a leading market share, repeatedly citing the high-30s percentage figure. However, expert commentary reveals a significantly more threatening competitive landscape than management acknowledges publicly.

Competitors such as United Imaging and Shinva have transitioned from merely copying Western technology to genuinely developing competitive, full-featured oncology suites. Experts confirm these platforms now match Elekta's hardware specification at meaningfully lower price points, and increasingly come bundled with locally developed oncology software solutions.

Experts project that over the next five years, Chinese hospitals will increasingly abandon Western premium platforms unless there is a compelling clinical or software differentiator that cannot be matched domestically. Furthermore, these domestic players have successfully built comprehensive, end-to-end oncology suites that integrate treatment planning, OIS, and hardware delivery in a single vendor solution — a capability Elekta currently lacks with its fragmented MOSAIQ/linac ecosystem.

Armed with superior pricing structures and rapidly improving technology, these Chinese manufacturers are actively expanding beyond their home market into Southeast Asia and the Middle East, directly threatening Elekta's emerging market growth narrative.

§6 — Competitor Commentary

Market Dynamics and the Siemens Healthineers (Varian) Perspective

Siemens Healthineers characterises the global radiation oncology market as a resilient, long-term growth market, driven by increasing cancer incidence, expanding emerging market access to sophisticated treatments, and a clinical trend toward hypofractionation protocols that increase per-patient treatment revenue.

Within this segment, the company identifies a heightened demand for integrated, multi-modal precision oncology platforms that combine diagnostic imaging, treatment planning software, and delivery hardware in a single seamlessly integrated ecosystem. Siemens Healthineers positions its Varian segment as the industry leader, explicitly claiming a majority share of the global linac market by installed base. Management notes that in the five years following the combination, Varian has expanded its market share further while maintaining premium pricing discipline.

The company's focus remains on leveraging this integrated platform to drive "synergetic core" growth by cross-selling Siemens diagnostic imaging assets — CT scanners, MRI systems, PET/CT platforms — into Varian's installed base of oncology centres, creating switching costs and deepening customer relationships that Elekta cannot replicate as a standalone radiation therapy specialist.

Competitive Positioning and Market Sentiment

Competitors frequently acknowledge Varian's dominance as a primary hurdle in the global radiotherapy market. Accuray explicitly notes that new competitors, including those from China, are expected to enter the global linac market, directly increasing competitive pressure on all Western incumbents. Brokers observe that Siemens Healthineers' acquisition of Varian significantly heightened the competitive landscape for Elekta, as the combination created an integrated ecosystem that delivers compelling cross-selling advantages. This advantage is bolstered by Siemens' ability to bundle end-to-end oncology solutions, often making single-vendor Siemens/Varian proposals significantly more attractive to hospital CFOs managing capital allocation decisions across multiple clinical departments.

IBA's Market Position and Industry Views

IBA, a specialist in proton therapy and dosimetry, maintains a distinct position in the competitive landscape, operating primarily in the premium proton therapy segment rather than as a direct competitor in the conventional linac market. In its reporting, IBA highlights its role as a technological leader in proton therapy, emphasising the clinical benefits of proton delivery for paediatric and CNS tumours where minimising secondary radiation dose is critical.

IBA also notes its significant role in the quality assurance market through its dosimetry division, which supplies QA systems to virtually all linac manufacturers and operators, including Elekta. While not a direct mass-market competitor to the C-arm linac businesses of Elekta or Varian, IBA's commentary on market dynamics reflects the broader industry shift toward higher-precision, image-guided adaptive therapies.

Comparative Dynamics in Oncology Software

The competitive commentary also extends into the oncology software market, where software platforms are becoming an increasingly decisive battleground for equipment market share. RaySearch highlights that its market share expansion is partially dependent on the ability of hospitals to operate its RayStation planning system with hardware from multiple vendors, positioning the company as a vendor-neutral alternative that reduces customer lock-in.

Conversely, some market participants observe that the software ecosystem is becoming a primary battleground, with Varian's ARIA platform creating an integrated lock-in mechanism that is difficult for competitors to displace. This operational integration remains a critical factor for hospitals when choosing between Varian and Elekta platforms, with ARIA's workflow superiority frequently cited as a decisive purchasing factor in US academic medical centres.

Regarding the broader market for oncology software, broker research suggests that the industry is shifting toward cloud-based, interoperable oncology platforms that can integrate seamlessly with hospital EMR systems. This shift is expected to favour companies that can demonstrate better integration with existing hospital infrastructure — a competency where Varian currently holds a material advantage over Elekta.

§7 — Narrative vs Reality Check

Synthesis of Divergences: Management vs. Experts

A critical analysis of Elekta's stated corporate strategy compared against the unfiltered realities described by expert network participants reveals both areas of genuine credibility and areas of potentially dangerous narrative-reality divergence.

The most credible element of management's narrative is the operational reset. The "Elekta Reset" restructuring programme is well advanced, with tangible cost savings crystallising in the company's quarterly financials. However, expert network participants caution that this pivot may be too late to reverse deeply entrenched software ecosystem disadvantages.

The most glaring divergence exists within the software ecosystem. Elekta's management frequently touts the Elekta ONE platform as a transformational upgrade that will reposition the company competitively in the software market. In stark contrast, clinical experts explicitly identify Elekta's closed software architecture as a structural competitive liability. Former executives report that Elekta actively blocks academic research centres from installing specialist research modules, and limits the ability of hospital IT departments to customise or extend the platform. This restrictive strategy frustrates physicians and frequently drives purchasing decisions toward Varian at the hardware replacement cycle.

Practicing radiation oncologists echo this sentiment, stating that Elekta has failed to distinguish itself in the software layer and remains significantly behind the industry benchmark set by Varian's ARIA platform. While the newly launched Elekta ONE ecosystem is viewed as a technical improvement, clinical end-users express scepticism that it will be sufficient to displace Varian's deeply entrenched workflow ecosystem in high-value US academic medical centres.

Consequently, experts project that Elekta's overall market share in the United States will continue to erode incrementally, with gains from Evo hardware placements partially offset by continued MOSAIQ-driven churn at the software renewal cycle.

False-Friend Patterns in the Commercial Narrative

Several "false-friend" patterns — where positive superficial metrics obscure underlying structural weaknesses — are identifiable in Elekta's commercial narrative.

The first major false-friend pattern relates to the Unity MR-Linac's impact on historical market share metrics. Management has historically cited Elekta's market share by revenue as evidence of competitive health. However, industry experts reveal that this revenue parity was an illusion created by Unity's massive price premium rather than genuine volume competitiveness. Because a single Unity machine costs double or triple the price of a standard linear accelerator, Elekta's revenue share has been artificially inflated relative to its true unit volume share. As Unity adoption stalls and the premium pricing contribution diminishes, the underlying unit market share erosion will become more visible in the revenue figures.

The second false-friend pattern involves the recovery of the Chinese radiation oncology market. Elekta's management has repeatedly highlighted a post-COVID recovery in Chinese hospital procurement as a significant near-term revenue catalyst. Management asserts they are successfully defending their market-leading position, claiming a share in the high-30s. While experts agree that near-term hospital demand is uncorking, they warn that the long-term reality of the Chinese market is structurally deteriorating for Western manufacturers.

Competitors such as United Imaging have rapidly evolved from producing lower-tier alternatives to offering genuinely competitive, full-featured oncology suites at materially lower price points. Former industry executives note that Elekta's primary advantage in developing markets historically rested on service network superiority and brand credibility — both of which are being rapidly eroded by Chinese manufacturers building out national service infrastructure. As Chinese domestic companies apply insurmountable pricing pressure on Western hardware, Elekta's long-term China revenue trajectory faces structural decline regardless of near-term procurement cycles.

The Unity MR-Linac Disconnect

The narrative surrounding the Unity MR-Linac represents the most profound disconnect between Elekta's management narrative and market reality. Management highlights 200-plus global installations, clinical publication velocity, and adaptive therapy protocol adoption as evidence of platform success. However, the clinical brilliance of the machine is entirely overshadowed by a broken economic model.

Because the system treats fewer patients per day than a standard linear accelerator, standard community hospital economics do not support the investment case. As a result, experts estimate that MR-guided therapy is utilised in fewer than 5 percent of cases nationally in the United States. The failure of ViewRay, Elekta's primary competitor in the MR-Linac space, serves as a stark reality check — if the MR-Linac business model worked commercially, ViewRay would not have failed to scale it.

Acknowledging this severe limitation, new leadership has initiated a strategic pivot, reallocating resources toward conventional linac upgrades and the software-led Elekta ONE platform, effectively conceding that Unity will remain a niche academic platform rather than a mass-market growth driver.

Alignment in the Operational Reset

While the commercial and product narratives face severe external scepticism, there is a strong alignment between management's stated operational priorities and the expert assessment of what Elekta needs to do to stabilise financially. The rapid execution of this plan — with over 80 percent of headcount reductions completed by Q3 2026 — validates management's execution credibility on the cost side.

Furthermore, the CEO's massive open-market stock purchase immediately following his appointment provides a credible alignment signal that the expert network views as meaningfully positive. Experts describe Just-Bomholt as someone who "only does things he believes in," and the scale of the personal financial commitment — SEK 48 million in open-market purchases — is viewed as a genuine conviction signal rather than a performative one.
