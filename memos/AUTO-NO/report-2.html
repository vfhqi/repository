Market & Industry Dynamics
Macroeconomic Pressures and Geopolitical Uncertainty

The warehouse automation sector is currently navigating a complex macroeconomic environment characterized by supply chain volatility and shifting trade policies. Frequent trade policy shifts and rising tariff barriers have introduced heightened uncertainty into global economic growth. In response to these external shocks, organizations are reassessing traditional supply chain models built purely on cost efficiency. Companies are increasingly adopting "just-in-case" inventory strategies to build flexibility through diversified sourcing and regionalized supply networks.

Morgan Stanley notes that global supply chains are actively reconfiguring to mitigate tariff impacts, particularly as US trade policies motivate companies to shift production domestically.

Jefferies highlights that this tariff uncertainty has directly impacted capital expenditure decisions, prompting some customers to delay near-term investments in warehouse automation.

Morgan Stanley further observes that raw material volatility, such as persistently high aluminum prices, continues to exert pressure on gross margins for cubic storage providers like Store.

Labor Dynamics and the mation Imperative

A primary catalyst for the adoption of automated storage and retrieval systems (AS/RS) is the worsening global labor shortage. Traditional warehouse operations remain highly dependent on manual labor, which typically accounts for over 50% of a facility's operational budget.

Morgan Stanley highlights that 75% of warehouse operators in developed economies struggle to attract workers, with Europe alone facing a deficit of over one million logistics employees.

Jefferies adds that the combination of scarce skilled labor and rising wage costs has refocused corporate capital expenditure toward automation as a fundamental business continuity necessity.

GXO Logistics emphasizes that autonomous robots and goods-to-person systems are particularly valuable in mitigating operational risks in markets where wage inflation erodes customer margins. By replacing manual processes with scalable robotics, operators can simultaneously boost throughput and enhance overall inventory management.

E-Commerce Growth and the Rise of Micro-Fulfillment Centers

The structural expansion of e-commerce continues to reshape logistics requirements, moving the industry's focus heavily toward last-mile delivery optimization. Global online sales are expected to reach approximately $7.4 trillion by 2025, forcing retailers to process higher frequency picks with smaller basket sizes. To meet demands for same-day and next-day delivery, businesses are rapidly pivoting toward micro-fulfillment centers (MFCs) located in dense urban areas. The micro-fulfillment market in regions like India is projected to grow at a compound annual growth rate of 39.2% through 2030.

Jefferies points out that micro-fulfillment offers a highly economical answer to online delivery by eliminating over 75% of manual picking costs and expediting the time required to serve customers.

Store explains that MFCs combine the customer proximity of in-store picking with the operational efficiency of large automated facilities. These compact, highly automated hubs allow retailers to bypass major highway bottlenecks and fulfill orders at a fraction of the cost of centralized distribution.

B2B Distribution Demands and Warehouse Network Consolidation

Beyond consumer retail, B2B distribution models are undergoing significant transformations. B2B operators are increasingly moving toward centralized stock management while simultaneously requiring the flexibility to execute frequent, smaller deliveries. Mirroring the consumer market, there is a growing expectation for next-day delivery in B2B transactions, necessitating distribution centers to adapt for greater operational agility.

To combat rising real estate costs and improve service levels, major distributors are actively consolidating their warehouse networks. Bunzl PLC, for example, executed 36 group-wide warehouse consolidations and relocations in 2025 to drive operational efficiency.

Morgan Stanley notes that warehouses built in the past decade are almost three times the size of those built prior to 2000, underscoring the industry's drive to consolidate operations into massive, highly efficient hubs.

By deploying high-density automated solutions like Store within these consolidated facilities, operators can maximize cubic space utilization without the need for costly physical expansions.

Strategic Initiatives & Partnership Evolution
Evolving Go-To-Market and the Partner Ecosystem

Store relies on a global network of 23 distribution partners to design, install, and service its automated storage and retrieval systems. Historically, the company operated through a purely partner-led model to efficiently scale its operations and manage capital expenditures. Recently, Store has evolved this go-to-market strategy into an integrated approach where the company engages jointly with partners from early solution discussions through to ongoing operations. This shift is supported by an expanded commercial team, including the addition of over 50 regional business development managers and a dedicated global account management team. The integrated model aims to strengthen system design, improve execution, and provide continuous health checks throughout the lifecycle of the customer's system.

Existing customers accounted for approximately 60% of total revenues in 2025. For example, European electronics retailer Alza recently expanded its initial high-throughput site and is preparing to install a second complete facility next year. Shifting Commercial Models: Store-as-a-Service (ASaaS)

To accommodate customers facing high interest rates and capital expenditure constraints, the company launched Store-as-a-Service (ASaaS) in 2025. This subscription-based model removes the barrier of large upfront investments, providing clients with predictable operational expenses and faster time-to-value. By the end of 2025, Store had shipped ASaaS contracts representing $34.4 million in total contract value.

Jefferies highlights that the opex model is gaining significant traction in the pipeline and serves to improve the overall competitiveness of Store's offering against alternative automation technologies.

Morgan Stanley views this strategic pivot toward recurring revenue as a necessary step for Store to improve its through-the-cycle defensibility and reduce exposure to quarterly fluctuations in hardware orders.

Product Innovation & Technological Edge
The Technology Roadmap and CubeVerse Platform

Store has aggressively accelerated its product roadmap to support a strategic shift from traditional asset-centric automation toward "Intelligent Fulfillment." The company's three-year product strategy focuses on optimizing the cube, expanding core capabilities to encompass new applications, and leveraging a unified software platform to enable AI-driven optimizations.

A cornerstone of this transition is the Spring 2026 launch of the CubeVerse platform, a unified cloud and data environment that connects Store applications, partner integrations, and AI modules into a single ecosystem. By working alongside existing warehouse management systems rather than replacing them, CubeVerse unlocks hidden capacity in existing grids and simplifies the path to fully autonomous fulfillment.

Expanding Core Hardware: Case and FlexBins

To address the industry's demand for versatile end-to-end workflows, Store's Fall 2025 product portfolio introduced significant hardware advancements. A primary breakthrough is Case, a module that automates the movement of full cases directly into, through, and out of the storage grid. In traditional operations, incoming cartons must be manually opened and transferred into storage bins before entering automated picking workflows. Case eliminates this manual step by unifying case handling and piece-picking within a single automated flow. This innovation allows operators to seamlessly manage inbound storage, order consolidation, and outbound buffering.

AI Integrations and WMS Capabilities

Store is increasingly relying on software intelligence to differentiate its offering and drive operational efficiency. Embedded across the new platform is Store Intelligence, an AI layer utilizing over 20 proprietary models to predict issues and orchestrate fulfillment in real time. This intelligence powers CubeControl, which personalizes routing parameters to create optimized "robot highways" in dense grids, reducing congestion and boosting throughput without the need for additional hardware.

The company also introduced CubeAnalytics, an AI-driven predictive diagnostics application that turns raw system data into actionable insights. This software automatically highlights performance patterns and incident reports, saving operations teams more than two hours of manual analysis time per instance.

To automate labor-intensive order preparation, Store has rolled out AI-powered piece-picking technologies. VersaAI delivers robotic piece picking powered by computer vision, enabling autonomous order consolidation and continuous 24/7 operations. Similarly, CarouselAI, developed in collaboration with Berkshire Grey, automates piece-picking processes while ensuring best-in-class SKU handling.

These AI advancements build upon the foundational software architecture Store secured through its acquisition of Locai Solutions.

Locai's microservice architecture provides sophisticated WMS capabilities tailored to complex workflows like eGrocery, offering intelligent batching algorithms that minimize pick path lengths and reduce the total number of bin presentations required.

Defending the Competitive Moat

The combination of high-density cubic storage, modular hardware, and advanced AI creates a formidable defensive moat against alternative automation technologies.

Jefferies notes that while shuttle-based systems are mature technologies capable of addressing high-throughput demands, they require considerable vertical space, involve high capital expenditure, and suffer from expensive maintenance due to their complex moving parts. Furthermore, Jefferies highlights that autonomous mobile robots (AMRs) offer flexible and low-cost deployments but lack extreme space efficiency, as they require significant empty floor space to navigate and scale poorly in large warehouse environments.

Morgan Stanley points out that Store's proprietary Planner and Router software logic—refined over two decades—acts as a massive barrier to entry that prevents new hardware entrants from easily replicating the system's efficiency.

Ultimately, Store's standardized, modular architecture allows it to deliver a superior return on investment, with a typical customer payback period of just one to three years. The system's unparalleled reliability is evidenced by an average mechanical uptime of 99.7%. To secure this technological edge long-term, Store actively protects its innovations; the company expanded its intellectual property portfolio by over 200 patent families between 2023 and 2025, earning recognition as a Top 100 Global Innovator.

Operating model and execution discipline

Store’s manufacturing strategy outsources non-core production to skilled third-party manufacturers across Europe and Asia, allowing the company to concentrate engineering resources on robot design while leveraging partners for installation and service at scale. Upstream, the bill-of-materials supply chain spans aluminum for grids and plastics for bins alongside electronics and transport, with the company working closely with multiple trusted suppliers to ensure quality and to reduce single-source concentration risk. Morgan Stanley characterizes this as an “asset-light” partner-led model with historically exceptional financial characteristics—gross margins near ~70% and adjusted EBITDA margins near ~50%—driven by a standardized product sold on a price-per-module basis through a global integrator network.

Jefferies likewise highlights the outsourced, scalable architecture and partner ecosystem as key to sustaining structurally high margins versus peers while minimizing fixed-cost intensity through the cycle.

Supply chain resiliency and raw material risk management

Management reports the operational playbook has been deliberately evolved since the pandemic, pairing a highly standardized product set with a diversified supplier base and dual assembly plants to better withstand short-term input cost volatility and logistics disruptions. In 2025, the company added more bin and aluminum suppliers and brought its Thailand factory to readiness for all modules, deepening redundancy and regional optionality in procurement and assembly. Morgan Stanley notes Store has expanded to 15 aluminum suppliers and doubled tote suppliers, which has helped mitigate geopolitical risk and shortened production lead times, supporting margin stability even amid commodity swings.

Jefferies estimates aluminum represents roughly 29–30% of procurement and that a 10% aluminum price rise equates to an estimated ~50 bps headwind to gross and EBITDA margins absent pricing actions, underscoring the importance of multi-sourcing and product standardization.
