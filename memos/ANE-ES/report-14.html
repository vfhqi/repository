§2 — Key Driver: EBITDA Margin Recovery

J-front verdict: Partial recovery underway but headwinds from power-price mean reversion and cost inflation limit upside; medium plausibility for 18-24M improvement to management target band [#J]

Driver definition:
- Driver definition: EBITDA margin recovery from ~33-35% (FY24 reported range) toward management's implied ~38-40% target over 18-24 months [#A]
- Why material: A 300-500bps EBITDA margin expansion on ANE-ES's ~€4.3bn revenue base adds approximately €130-215m EBITDA, directly accelerating deleveraging and enabling capacity investment without equity dilution [#A]
- Baseline: FY24 EBITDA of approximately €1.44bn on revenues of approximately €4.3bn implies an EBITDA margin of ~33.5%; management guided €1.5-1.6bn EBITDA for FY25 [#A]
- Target state: Approximately €1.6bn EBITDA by end-2026 would represent ~37% margin at flat revenues, or ~35% at 5% revenue growth [#A]
- Required trajectory: Approximately €75-80m per half-year EBITDA improvement; implies roughly 150-200bps annual margin expansion [#A]

Bull case:
- Bull — historical: ANE-ES achieved EBITDA margins above 40% in 2021-2022 during elevated Spanish power prices; structural capability to reach those levels exists [#A]
- Bull — recent: Q1 2025 operating reports suggest Spain wind load factors improved year-on-year; hydrology recovery in Iberia is a near-term tailwind [#A]
- Bull — mgmt: Management's FY25 EBITDA guidance of €1.5-1.6bn implies H2-weighted recovery; management cited operational leverage from new capacity additions [#A]
- Bull — initiatives: Cost efficiency programme targeting ~€50m opex reduction over 2024-2026; early signs of procurement savings on O&M contracts [#A]
- Bull — industry: European renewable sector EBITDA margins for comparable offshore/onshore mixed portfolios typically range 38-45%; ANE-ES below sector median creates mean-reversion potential [#A]

Bear case:
- ❌ Bear — recent deterioration: FY24 EBITDA of €1.44bn was below FY23 levels on a reported basis, suggesting the recovery narrative is not yet evidenced in the trailing data [#A]
- Bear — power price: Iberdrola's 2025 guidance flagged Iberian power price normalisation below €60/MWh average; ANE-ES's merchant book amplifies this drag vs peers with higher PPA coverage [#A]
- Bear — cost inflation: Turbine O&M costs have risen ~15-20% since 2021 across European wind operators per public Vestas and Siemens Gamesa disclosures; offsetting cost savings are partial at best [#A]
- Bear — structural — depreciation drag: Rapid capacity additions (new GW coming online) accelerate the D&A line, which at high capex multiples mechanically compresses reported EBITDA margins even on strong revenue performance [#A]
- Bear — SS skeptic: No AS access; public broker commentary available via press reporting suggests consensus EBITDA for FY25 at approximately €1.48-1.52bn — below management's midpoint of the guidance range [#A]

Forward trajectory:
- Quarterly trajectory: Management's implied H2 2025 weighting means Q3 + Q4 2025 must each deliver ~€380-400m EBITDA vs H1 run rate; seasonality partially supports this but reliance on back-half delivery is a recurring execution risk [#A]
- Acceleration needed: From FY24's ~€1.44bn to a €1.6bn target requires ~11% growth; consensus expects ~5-6%, suggesting management guidance embeds a ~5% upside assumption not in consensus [#A]
- Probability — verbal: Medium plausibility — achieving the low end of management guidance (€1.5bn) is credible; achieving the midpoint (€1.55bn) or high end (€1.6bn) requires simultaneous improvement in power prices, load factors, and cost execution [#J]
- Key dependency: Iberian power price stabilisation at or above €55-60/MWh average through 2025-2026 [#A]
- Downside scenario: If Iberian power prices average €45-50/MWh in H2 2025 (below current forward curve), EBITDA could miss the low end of guidance by €80-120m, pushing net debt/EBITDA above 6.0× [#A]

Sell-side and management expectations:
- Consensus alignment: Public sources suggest consensus EBITDA estimates for FY25 at approximately €1.48-1.52bn; management guide of €1.5-1.6bn implies consensus is at the low end of the range [#A]
- Estimate revision: L12M revisions appear to have been slightly downward based on FY24 reported miss relative to original guidance; no AS access to confirm direction of L6M revisions [#A]
- Consensus dispersion: Cannot formally quantify without AS; anecdotally, sell-side views range from cautious (power price headwinds) to constructive (load factor recovery) [#A]

Evidence gaps:
- Evidence gap — PPA coverage: Precise percentage of 2025-2026 revenues contracted via fixed PPA vs merchant is not publicly disclosed at sufficient granularity [#A]
- Evidence gap — cost programme: Timeline and evidenced savings from the €50m opex programme are not verified in public filings reviewed [#A]
- Evidence gap — quarterly cadence: Q1 2025 EBITDA not yet available at time of this memo; Q1 print would sharply inform H1 trajectory confidence [#A]

Sceptical bullet: Is the margin recovery narrative simply a replay of 2022-2023 where management guided to recovery and delivered partial improvement before power price headwinds re-emerged? Track record of guidance accuracy over the last four reporting years warrants explicit APM scrutiny [#J]

§3 — Key Driver: Balance-Sheet Deleveraging

J-front verdict: Most consequential driver and most at risk; net debt/EBITDA at ~5.7× is above the sector comfort threshold and self-help levers are insufficient without asset recycling or parent support [#J]

Driver definition:
- Driver definition: Net debt/EBITDA reduction from ~5.7× (FY24) toward a ~4.5× level over 18-24 months [#A]
- Why material: Elevated leverage limits dividend capacity, increases refinancing risk in a still-elevated interest rate environment, and constrains new project financing; equity re-rating requires credible leverage trajectory [#A]
- Baseline: ANE-ES reported net debt of approximately €8.2bn at FY24 vs EBITDA of €1.44bn, implying ~5.7× net debt/EBITDA; investment-grade renewable developer target range is typically 3.5-4.5× [#A]
- Target state: Approximately 4.5-5.0× net debt/EBITDA by end-2026; requires either EBITDA growth to ~€1.6-1.7bn or net debt reduction of ~€0.5-1.0bn [#A]
- Required trajectory: Approximately €200-250m annual net debt reduction through FCF after capex and dividends, or equivalent via asset sales [#A]

Bull case:
- Bull — EBITDA growth: If EBITDA recovers to €1.6bn by end-2026 with net debt flat, leverage falls to ~5.1×; at €1.7bn EBITDA (upside case), leverage falls to ~4.8× [#A]
- Bull — asset recycling: ANE-ES has stated intent to monetise minority stakes in operational assets; sector precedent (Iberdrola's infrastructure fund sales, EDP Renovaveis's Voltalia partnership) suggests 6-8× EV/EBITDA transaction values are achievable on operational renewables [#A]
- Bull — capex moderation: Management's 2024 investor day signalled a shift toward more selective project selection; if annual capex moderates from ~€2.5bn to ~€2.0bn, FCF conversion improves by ~€500m per annum [#A]
- Bull — refinancing: ANE-ES successfully refinanced a €800m bond in early 2025 at manageable spreads, indicating market access remains intact [#A]

Bear case:
- ❌ Bear — structural — leverage floor: Project-finance structure means a significant portion of gross debt is locked at the asset level; consolidated net debt is structurally sticky and does not amortise at the pace visible in corporate-level FCF [#A]
- ❌ Bear — capex demand: 5 GW per annum installation target requires substantial ongoing capital commitment; announced pipeline of ~14 GW under development implies capex will remain elevated through 2027 at minimum [#A]
- Bear — interest burden: Average cost of debt has risen from ~2.5% (2021 vintage) toward ~3.5-4.0% on new tranches; annual interest cost of approximately €280-320m is a meaningful FCF drain [#A]
- Bear — parent dependency: If Acciona S.A. requires upstreamed dividends from ANE-ES to service its own debt, deleveraging at ANE-ES level is directly impaired [#A]
- Bear — asset recycling uncertainty: No contracted asset-sale proceeds are confirmed in public filings; timeline and value of potential disposals are speculative at ESA stage [#A]
- Bear — macro: Rising long-term rates in Europe compress the value of operational renewable assets (DCF-sensitive); this reduces the attractiveness and speed of asset-recycling transactions [#A]

Forward trajectory:
- Quarterly trajectory: FCF generation after maintenance capex and before growth capex is positive but consumed by the growth programme; net debt reduction is entirely contingent on asset sales or EBITDA step-change [#A]
- Acceleration needed: Achieving 4.5× by end-2026 requires roughly €1.5bn of EBITDA improvement and/or net debt reduction from current path; approximately €500-700m must come from sources other than organic EBITDA growth [#A]
- Probability — verbal: Low-medium plausibility for achieving 4.5× by end-2026; more plausible outcome is 5.0-5.3× absent confirmed asset recycling transactions [#J]
- Key dependency: At least one material (€300m+) asset-recycling transaction in 2025 or H1 2026 [#A]
- Downside scenario: If asset sales are delayed and power prices disappoint, net debt/EBITDA could rise above 6.0× by end-2025, triggering credit-rating pressure and potentially forcing an equity raise [#A]

Sell-side and management expectations:
- Consensus alignment: Management FY25 net debt guidance is not precisely quantified in public sources; sell-side base cases appear to assume leverage remains above 5× through 2025 [#A]
- Estimate revision: No formal revision data available without AS; leverage trajectory has been a recurring concern in analyst question-and-answer sessions per available earnings-call transcripts [#A]
- Mgmt guidance alignment: Management has not provided formal leverage targets at investor days reviewed; absence of explicit leverage guidance is itself an evidence gap [#A]

Evidence gaps:
- Evidence gap — asset recycling: No contracted proceeds, no named counterparties, no confirmed transaction timelines — the asset-recycling component of the bull case is entirely speculative [#A]
- Evidence gap — project-finance amortisation: Precise annual project-finance debt amortisation schedule not publicly available; limits bottom-up FCF build reliability [#A]
- Evidence gap — Acciona S.A. dividends: Upstream dividend requirement from ANE-ES to parent not disclosed explicitly; must be inferred from Acciona S.A. consolidated financials [#A]

Sceptical bullet: Is the deleveraging driver actually a driver at all, or is it the OUTPUT of the EBITDA recovery driver? If so, the case has only one truly independent fulcrum, not two — concentrating case risk even further [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Low-medium plausibility for the magnitude of deleveraging required over 18-24 months; directional improvement is credible but not the quantum. Fulcrum-level vs APM Pass 1: VALIDATES primary fulcrum status with increased concern [#J]

§4 — Key Driver: Capacity-Growth Execution

J-front verdict: Gross installed-capacity additions are broadly on track but ROIC on new additions is deteriorating, limiting the EPS contribution from growth capex [#J]

Driver definition:
- Driver definition: Successful commissioning of approximately 2-3 GW net new capacity per annum through 2025-2026, contributing incremental EBITDA of ~€150-200m per GW at normalised load factors [#A]
- Why material: New capacity additions are management's stated primary revenue growth engine; without delivery, the EBITDA recovery narrative has no organic growth leg [#A]
- Baseline: ANE-ES operated approximately 13.3 GW at end-FY24 across wind, solar, and other renewables globally [#A]
- Target state: Approximately 16-17 GW by end-2026, implying ~2.5-3.5 GW net additions over 24 months [#A]
- Required trajectory: Approximately 1.2-1.7 GW per half-year commissioning; management has historically delivered ~2 GW per annum additions in 2022-2024 [#A]

Bull case:
- Bull — historical: ANE-ES has demonstrated consistent 1.5-2.5 GW per annum addition capability over 2020-2024; track record of execution on the commissioning pipeline is relatively strong [#A]
- Bull — pipeline: 14 GW development pipeline provides optionality; 4-5 GW in construction or late-stage development as of FY24 investor presentation [#A]
- Bull — geographic diversification: ANE-ES operates in 40+ countries; delays in one geography (e.g. Spain grid connection queue) can be partially offset by faster-moving markets (Australia, Chile, Mexico) [#A]
- Bull — industry tailwinds: EU renewable build targets (REPowerEU — 45% renewable share by 2030) create regulatory urgency to accelerate permitting and grid connections [#A]
- Bull — technology mix: Increasing solar and battery storage additions (lower capital intensity than offshore wind) improve capital efficiency at the margin [#A]

Bear case:
- ❌ Bear — ROIC compression: European onshore wind IRRs have compressed from ~8-10% (2019-2021 vintage) to ~5-7% (2024-2025 vintage) on a levered basis, per public Siemens Energy and Vestas commentary; new capacity adds EBITDA but at sub-historic ROIC [#A]
- Bear — grid connection bottlenecks: Spain's REE (Red Electrica) grid queue is reportedly 2-3 years oversubscribed; Australian grid access disputes have affected ANE-ES projects in Queensland [#A]
- Bear — equipment cost normalisation: Turbine prices remain ~30-40% above 2020 lows despite modest deflation in 2024; new project IRRs remain under pressure [#A]
- Bear — execution risk — international: Multi-geography execution creates coordination complexity; FY23-FY24 saw write-downs and delays on Australian and US projects per press reporting [#A]
- Bear — structural — working capital: Large development pipeline requires substantial upfront capital commitment; working capital cycle for a 5 GW active pipeline is a meaningful FCF drain [#A]

Forward trajectory:
- Quarterly trajectory: Commissioning activity tends to be H2-weighted in European renewables; Q3-Q4 2025 should be the clearest signal for annual addition rate [#A]
- Acceleration needed: Current trajectory of ~2 GW per annum is already at the high end of ANE-ES's historical range; 2.5-3.0 GW per annum target requires operational capability stretch [#A]
- Probability — verbal: Medium plausibility for volume execution; the ROIC quality caveat means even successful capacity additions may not deliver the EPS uplift the growth narrative implies [#J]
- Key dependency: Grid connection and permitting milestones across Spain, Australia, and Chile in H2 2025 [#A]
- Downside scenario: If grid bottlenecks delay ~1 GW of 2025 additions into 2026, EBITDA shortfall of approximately €60-80m vs guidance [#A]

Sell-side and management expectations:
- Consensus alignment: Available press-sourced sell-side commentary broadly accepts the 2 GW per annum addition rate as achievable; ROIC quality of additions receives less analytical attention [#A]
- Mgmt guidance alignment: Management maintained 2025-2026 capacity guidance at the FY24 earnings call; no formal downward revision to installation targets [#A]

Evidence gaps:
- Evidence gap — ROIC by vintage: No public disclosure of project-level IRRs for 2023-2025 vintage additions vs management's cost of capital; APM cannot verify whether new capacity adds ROIC-positive value [#A]
- Evidence gap — Australia / US project status: Status of delayed projects not fully resolved in public filings; potential further write-downs [#A]

Sceptical bullet: If capacity additions are on track but sub-ROIC, the growth driver is a value-destruction engine wearing a volume-delivery costume; APM should explicitly model ROIC on new builds before treating capacity growth as a positive driver [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility for volume delivery; low plausibility for value-accretive delivery on current ROIC evidence. Fulcrum-level vs APM Pass 1: REVISES — supporting driver, not fulcrum; ROIC quality caveat substantially changes its contribution to the case [#J]

§5 — Key Driver: Power-Price Realisation

J-front verdict: This driver is the clearest near-term headwind; Iberian and European power markets are normalising post-2022 crisis and ANE-ES's merchant exposure makes it one of the most price-sensitive operators in the European renewable peer set [#J]

Driver definition:
- Driver definition: Average achieved power price realisation of ≥€60/MWh across ANE-ES's generation portfolio sustaining EBITDA at or above management guidance levels [#A]
- Why material: Power price is the single largest revenue driver for a utility-scale renewable operator with significant merchant exposure; a €5/MWh move on ANE-ES's generation base (~35-40 TWh per annum) implies approximately €175-200m EBITDA impact [#A]
- Baseline: ANE-ES's achieved average power price in FY24 was approximately €58-65/MWh (range reported across Spain, Australia, international operations) [#A]
- Target state: Sustained ≥€60/MWh average realisation through 2025-2026 to underpin management EBITDA guidance [#A]
- Required trajectory: No improvement needed from baseline; the driver is essentially "hold current levels" — but the bear case is further normalisation toward €45-55/MWh [#A]

Bull case:
- Bull — recent: Iberian forward power prices for 2025-2026 are in the €55-75/MWh range as of Q1 2025 data; the market is not pricing a catastrophic collapse [#A]
- Bull — PPA book: Portion of ANE-ES's revenues that are PPA-contracted provides a floor; exact coverage ratio not public but management has stated progressive PPA expansion strategy [#A]
- Bull — gas price support: If European gas prices remain above €25/MWh , marginal-cost power pricing provides a floor for renewable capture prices [#A]
- Bull — carbon pricing: EU ETS carbon prices sustaining above €60/tonne support the merit-order position of gas-backed marginal supply [#A]

Bear case:
- ❌ Bear — structural — renewable cannibalisation: As more renewables enter the grid, average capture prices for renewables fall relative to the spot price (the "cannibalisation" effect); Iberia, with its high renewable penetration, is the most advanced example of this effect in Europe [#A]
- ❌ Bear — Spain regulatory: Iberian power prices experienced negative pricing in multiple periods in 2023-2024 due to high renewable output; as more solar enters the grid in 2025-2026, capture price discounts will likely widen [#A]
- Bear — gas price decline: If European gas demand falls structurally (LNG oversupply, demand destruction), marginal-cost pricing will decline, pulling down ANE-ES's merchant book revenues [#A]
- Bear — merchant concentration: ANE-ES's merchant exposure is structurally higher than Iberdrola (which has approximately 50-60% of generation under long-term contracts) or EDP Renovaveis; no public data confirms ANE-ES's exact PPA coverage ratio [#A]
- Bear — international variability: Australia and Latin America power markets are structurally more volatile than European regulated markets; geographic diversification adds revenue risk, not just opportunity [#A]

Forward trajectory:
- Quarterly trajectory: Spanish power prices in Q1 2025 were reportedly below €50/MWh on average, driven by hydroelectric surplus and high solar output; this is a near-term headwind to H1 2025 EBITDA realisation [#A]
- Probability — verbal: Low plausibility for sustained ≥€60/MWh average realisation through 2025-2026 given Q1 2025 data points and structural renewable cannibalisation trends [#J]
- Key dependency: Gas price stability and absence of abnormal hydroelectric/solar conditions in Iberia [#A]
- Downside scenario: If average realisation falls to €50/MWh, EBITDA would be approximately €350-400m below management guidance; leverage would rise above 6× and the investment case would break [#A]

Evidence gaps:
- Evidence gap — PPA coverage ratio: Exact percentage of generation under fixed PPA by geography is not publicly disclosed with sufficient granularity to model price risk properly [#A]
- Evidence gap — Q1 2025 actuals: Q1 2025 results not yet available; this is the most time-sensitive evidence gap for APM Pass 3 [#A]

Sceptical bullet: Is power-price risk already in the price, or is the sell-side base case still embedding mid-cycle normalised prices rather than the structurally lower capture prices implied by renewable cannibalisation? If the latter, the EBITDA consensus may be 10-15% too high [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Low plausibility for the power-price driver contributing positively to the case over 18-24 months; the driver is a headwind, not a tailwind, in the current Iberian market structure. Fulcrum-level vs APM Pass 1: CHALLENGES — this driver is a case risk, not a case driver [#J]

§6 — Key Driver: Capital Allocation and Parent Governance

J-front verdict: The most underanalysed and highest-structural-risk driver; Acciona S.A.'s ~79% stake creates a governance structure where minority shareholders have limited recourse against capital-allocation decisions that serve parent interests [#J]

Driver definition:
- Driver definition: Acciona S.A. allowing ANE-ES to retain sufficient FCF to fund capex, reduce leverage, and pay meaningful minority dividends — without extracting value through related-party transactions or requiring upstream dividends that impair deleveraging [#A]
- Why material: Parent-controlled capital allocation is the structural constraint on all other drivers; EBITDA recovery and deleveraging both become moot if parent extracts value [#A]
- Baseline: Acciona S.A. holds approximately 79% of ANE-ES; related-party service agreements (engineering, project management) with Acciona S.A. subsidiaries represent a recurring cost line at ANE-ES level [#A]
- Target state: Parent behaviour aligned with minority interests — related-party costs at market rates, upstream dividend policy not exceeding FCF after maintenance capex and debt service, and no equity dilutive capital raises [#A]
- Required trajectory: Continued refinement of governance framework; any formal commitment to arm's-length pricing on related-party services would be positive [#A]

Bull case:
- Bull — structural alignment: Acciona S.A.'s own balance sheet benefits from ANE-ES's equity market capitalisation; destroying minority value destroys Acciona S.A.'s own net asset value [#A]
- Bull — recent — dividend policy: ANE-ES has maintained a dividend payout in line with stated policy; no emergency dividend cut or special upstream transfer evident in FY24 [#A]
- Bull — mgmt messaging: ANE-ES management has publicly stated commitment to independent governance practices; audit committee includes independent directors [#A]
- Bull — debt market discipline: ANE-ES's external project-finance and corporate bond market presence provides an implicit governance discipline — lenders require arms-length covenant compliance [#A]

Bear case:
- ❌ Bear — structural — parent-level stress: Acciona S.A. has its own leverage concerns; if Acciona S.A.'s own debt metrics deteriorate, the incentive to extract value from ANE-ES via related-party transactions or special dividends increases [#A]
- ❌ Bear — related-party opacity: The precise cost and margin of Acciona group-to-ANE-ES related-party service agreements is not fully disclosed; if group services are priced above market, ANE-ES EBITDA is systematically understated [#A]
- Bear — governance precedent: The IPO structure retained supermajority control; Acciona S.A. has demonstrated willingness to use its dominant position to set dividend policy over minority objections historically [#A]
- Bear — strategic misalignment: Acciona S.A. may have different risk/return preferences (e.g. higher growth capex even at sub-ROIC) than minority shareholders whose case depends on deleveraging and free-cash-flow generation [#A]
- Bear — ESG pressure: Acciona S.A.'s own sustainability commitments may require ANE-ES to fund unprofitable green hydrogen or social infrastructure projects that benefit the parent's ESG positioning but impair ANE-ES minority value [#A]

Forward trajectory:
- M/M/O — means: Acciona S.A. has the formal legal means to direct capital allocation via its board majority and shareholder agreements [#A]
- M/M/O — motive: Acciona S.A.'s own leverage and strategic agenda create a plausible (if uncertain) motive to prioritise group-level goals over ANE-ES minority value [#A]
- M/M/O — opportunity: Any related-party contract renewal or extraordinary transaction provides the mechanism for value extraction [#A]
- Probability — verbal: Medium plausibility that governance remains benign for minorities; the absence of a recent negative event is not the same as structural protection [#J]
- Key dependency: Acciona S.A.'s own balance-sheet health and refinancing requirements in 2025-2026 [#A]
- Downside scenario: If Acciona S.A. requires a special upstream dividend of €300-500m from ANE-ES, net debt at ANE-ES rises commensurately and the deleveraging case collapses [#A]

Evidence gaps:
- Evidence gap — Acciona S.A. leverage: Acciona S.A.'s own net debt and refinancing schedule is not integrated into the ANE-ES investment case in most public research; APM should review Acciona S.A.'s standalone debt profile [#A]
- Evidence gap — related-party pricing: No arm's-length benchmarking of related-party service charges available in public ANE-ES filings [#A]
