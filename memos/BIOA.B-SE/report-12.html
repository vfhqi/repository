§1 — Customer Analysis (concentration + relationships)

BioArctic operates in an unusual value-chain configuration: it is not a conventional product company with a customer base, but a royalty-stage biotech whose "customer" is its single commercial partner, Eisai. Understanding the customer layer therefore requires analysing Eisai as both customer (buyer of BioArctic's IP/co-development output) and agent (the entity executing commercial strategy on BioArctic's behalf).

  • Verdict: Extreme top-1 concentration — Eisai represents effectively 100% of BioArctic's commercial revenue exposure; no other customer exists; this is a single-node dependency structurally unlike any conventional pharma peer [#J]

  • Top-1 concentration: Eisai = ~100% of near-term revenue — all Leqembi royalties, all commercial milestones, and all co-development cost-sharing flows through a single counterparty; peer comparison: typical mid-cap European pharma royalty companies (e.g., IP-heavy specialty pharma) carry top-1 concentration of 35-55%; BioArctic is outlier territory [#A]

  • Sub: royalty rate on Leqembi net sales undisclosed publicly but estimated by sell-side (Berenberg, Carnegie) in the mid-single-digit to low-double-digit % range on Eisai's net sales share

  • Sub: commercial milestone payments contingent on cumulative sales thresholds add lumpiness to the revenue profile [#A]

  • Major customer: Eisai Co. Ltd (Tokyo-listed, ~¥1.0tn revenue) — large, financially stable counterparty with significant Alzheimer's franchise commitment; Leqembi is strategically central to Eisai's pipeline value and investor narrative, creating alignment of interest [#A]

  • Sub: Eisai's Alzheimer's business unit is under intense scrutiny from its own investors — Leqembi must succeed commercially for Eisai's strategic credibility, creating implicit pull-through alignment with BioArctic [#J]

  • Sub: Biogen is Eisai's US co-promotion partner; Biogen's recent strategic pivot away from Alzheimer's (following earlier Aduhelm debacle) creates a secondary risk — if Biogen reduces co-promotion commitment, Eisai/BioArctic royalty trajectory is impaired [#A]

  • Customer profile: Established large-cap pharma — Eisai is not a growth-stage buyer; it has deep balance sheet, existing commercial infrastructure, and institutional knowledge of the Alzheimer's market; relationship with BioArctic pre-dates commercialisation by over a decade [#A]

  • Contract length: Multi-decade partnership with no break clause apparent — the collaboration agreement (signed 2005, extended and expanded) is long-dated and tied to patent life of lecanemab; no evidence of annual or spot structure; switching is contractually and practically infeasible [#J]

  • Sub: patent expiry for lecanemab likely in the mid-2030s to 2040s depending on supplementary protection certificates — long runway for royalty generation if sales ramp [#A]

  • Exclusivity: De facto exclusive — BioArctic's lecanemab IP is exclusively licensed to Eisai for development and commercialisation; BioArctic retains co-promotion rights in certain Nordic markets but these are commercially negligible [#A]

  • Customer churn: Zero structural churn risk — there is no mechanism by which Eisai can "churn" away from this partnership; shared IP ownership, co-development obligations, and regulatory filings are jointly held; separation would require a renegotiation of extraordinary complexity [#J]

  • Sub: the correct framing of churn risk is not customer loss but commercial underperformance — Eisai persisting as partner but generating lower-than-expected royalty-bearing sales is the functional equivalent of partial churn for BioArctic economics [#A]

  • Satisfaction signal: Guarded positive — Eisai's public commentary (earnings calls Q2-Q4 2024, Q1 2025) describes the Leqembi partnership positively and reaffirms commitment to global rollout; no evidence of dissatisfaction with BioArctic's scientific contribution; friction exists at the commercial execution layer rather than the scientific/IP layer [#A]

  • Sub: Eisai has flagged ARIA monitoring infrastructure as the primary bottleneck to US penetration — this is a healthcare system constraint, not a BioArctic product quality issue [#J]

  • Customer complaint: Indirect friction visible — Eisai's investor communications reference reimbursement coverage gaps (CMS coverage with evidence development for traditional Medicare), ARIA monitoring centre capacity constraints, and neurologist prescribing hesitancy as active headwinds; these are demand-side frictions that suppress royalty-bearing sales [#A]

  • Concentration trend: Worsening absent pipeline diversification — BAN2401 (lecanemab) is the sole commercial-stage asset; pipeline (BAN2401 subcutaneous, BAN0805, AL002 collaboration with AbbVie/Neurimmune) is pre-revenue; top-1 concentration remains near-100% through at least 2027 in base case [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bear read on customer relationships is that apparent retention stability is structural lock-in masquerading as relationship health — Eisai cannot leave but it can under-resource the commercial effort, slow label expansion applications, deprioritise reimbursement advocacy, and let donanemab take share without BioArctic having any contractual recourse; the lock-in is asymmetric — BioArctic is far more locked in than Eisai [#J]

§2 — Switching Costs + Pricing Leverage

  • Verdict: Switching costs are infinite from Eisai's perspective on lecanemab specifically, but BioArctic's pricing leverage over Eisai is near-zero — the asymmetry is the core tension [#J]

  • Switching cost — product: Irreplaceable at the molecular level — lecanemab is BioArctic's own antibody, discovered in-house; Eisai cannot source a functionally equivalent molecule elsewhere; no competitor has lecanemab; switching cost is effectively infinite [#J]

  • Sub: this creates a misleading sense of BioArctic's power — infinite switching cost on the IP asset coexists with near-zero leverage over commercial execution, pricing to payers, or resource allocation [#A]

  • Switching cost — integration: Deep bilateral integration — joint development committee, shared regulatory filings (BLA in US, MAA in EU), co-ownership of clinical data packages; unwinding would require regulatory agency notifications, data-sharing agreement renegotiation, and likely litigation [#J]

  • Switching cost — qualification: Decade-plus development history is non-replicable — the CLARITY AD Phase 3 trial represents >SEK 10bn of cumulative investment (largely Eisai-funded); no competitor could replicate BioArctic's lecanemab clinical package in under 8-10 years [#A]

  • Pricing pass-through: BioArctic has no pricing agency — Leqembi's list price ($26,500/year in the US as of 2024) is set by Eisai unilaterally; BioArctic receives a royalty on net sales but cannot influence the list price, discount strategies, or payer rebate structures [#A]

  • Sub: Eisai reduced Leqembi's US price from the initial $26,500 launch level modestly in response to CMS/payer pressure — BioArctic had no input into this decision; peer context: branded Alzheimer's drug pricing is under sustained political and payer pressure in US 2024-2026 [#A]

  • Sub: ex-US pricing (Japan approved at ¥298,179/infusion cycle equivalent, European approval pending) is similarly Eisai-determined [#J]

  • Customer pushback: Payer pushback is severe and structurally embedded — CMS's coverage-with-evidence-development decision for Leqembi under traditional Medicare effectively gated the largest US payer population behind a restrictive access pathway; commercial payer uptake is faster but a minority of the target population [#A]

  • Sub: CMS proposed revised NCD in 2024 to broaden coverage — this is a positive signal but has not yet translated into volume acceleration [#J]

  • Renewal pricing: Royalty structure is fixed at contract inception — the royalty rate was negotiated pre-commercialisation; BioArctic does not renegotiate at "renewal" in the conventional sense; rate is fixed for patent life, creating long-term predictability but no upside leverage if Leqembi becomes a blockbuster [#A]

  • Competitive bidding: Donanemab creates indirect competitive pressure on Eisai's pricing discipline — if donanemab prices below or comparable to Leqembi and achieves superior convenience (four-dose regimen vs bi-weekly infusion), Eisai faces pressure to discount or offer rebates; this compresses net sales and therefore BioArctic's royalty base [#J]

  • Sub: Lilly's list price for donanemab (Kisunla) set at $32,000/year in the US — modestly above Leqembi; pricing war has not materialised as of Q1 2025, but is a forward risk [#A]

  • Concession signal: No direct concession evidence to BioArctic — because BioArctic's royalty is contractually fixed, the "concession" mechanism is indirect: Eisai conceding price to payers reduces net sales → reduces BioArctic royalty base; not a renegotiation risk but an erosion risk [#A]

  • RFP pressure: Not applicable in conventional sense — BioArctic does not participate in competitive RFP processes; however, the formulary inclusion / prior authorisation process at US health systems is functionally analogous; Eisai competes for formulary position against donanemab at health system level [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bear read is that infinite switching costs create complacency — BioArctic management cannot credibly threaten to walk away, cannot demand better royalty rates, cannot redirect the commercial strategy, and cannot accelerate payer access; the "irreplaceable IP" moat is real but provides no negotiating leverage whatsoever against its only commercial counterparty [#J]

§3 — Supplier Analysis (concentration + input costs)

  • Verdict: Supplier concentration is moderate with specific single-source risks in biologics manufacturing; direct margin impact is limited at current pipeline stage but will intensify as subcutaneous formulation and later-stage assets advance [#J]

  • Critical input: Biologics CMO/CDMOs for antibody manufacturing — lecanemab is a monoclonal antibody requiring large-scale CHO cell culture manufacturing; BioArctic itself does not manufacture; Eisai has primary CMO relationships for commercial supply [#A]

  • Sub: for pre-clinical and early clinical pipeline (BAN0805, AL002), BioArctic engages CROs and specialist biologics CDMOs; these are standard market participants with moderate concentration [#J]

  • Sub: supply for Leqembi commercial manufacture is managed by Eisai, not BioArctic — BioArctic's direct supplier exposure is primarily for its own R&D pipeline [#A]

  • Supplier concentration: R&D pipeline suppliers: moderate concentration — CRO market (IQVIA, Covance/Labcorp, PPD/Thermo Fisher, Syneos) is oligopolistic; 4-5 global firms control the majority of Phase 1-2 capacity relevant to BioArctic's pipeline; single-source risk for any specific trial is real [#A]

  • Sub: peer comparison — small European biotech peers (pre-commercial) typically use 2-4 CROs across a 5-asset pipeline; BioArctic's pipeline depth is modest, suggesting CRO breadth is similarly limited [#A]

  • Single-source: Specific CNS-specialised CROs are limited — Alzheimer's / CNS trials require specialised imaging (amyloid PET), specialised biomarker labs (CSF analysis), and experienced clinical sites; the universe of qualified CNS CROs is narrower than general pharma; this creates soft single-source dependencies [#J]

  • Sub: ARIA monitoring protocols require specific MRI imaging infrastructure — constraining both clinical trial and commercial ramp; BioArctic has no leverage over MRI centre supply [#A]

  • Long-term partnership: No publicly disclosed long-term CRO partnerships — BioArctic's scale is insufficient to secure preferred master service agreement terms typical of large pharma (AstraZeneca, Novartis negotiate multi-year, volume-committed CRO deals); BioArctic likely transacts on a trial-by-trial basis [#J]

  • Input cost trend: CRO/CDMO pricing inflation: 8-15% L2Y in CNS-specialised trials — industry-wide CRO pricing has increased materially post-COVID due to site capacity constraints, specialised CNS site scarcity, and inflationary labour costs; BioArctic faces higher per-patient costs without scale offsets; peer base rate: mid-cap European CNS biotechs have reported CRO cost increases of 10-20% over 2022-2024 [#A]

  • Sub: this has a direct effect on R&D burn rate (BioArctic's primary cost line) rather than a gross margin effect in the conventional sense [#J]

  • Inflation pressure: R&D cost inflation compressing runway — higher CRO costs extend the cash burn per trial, reducing the number of trials executable within a given cash envelope; given BioArctic's ~SEK 1-1.5bn cash position (approximate), R&D cost inflation is a real runway compression risk [#A]

  • Supplier bargaining power: CROs hold moderate bargaining power over BioArctic — BioArctic cannot threaten volume withdrawal; it is too small to be a preferred customer; CNS specialist CROs have multiple large pharma clients and can prioritise accordingly [#J]

  • Sub: in periods of CRO capacity tightness (2021-2023), small biotechs reported being deprioritised by CROs in favour of large pharma; capacity has loosened modestly in 2024-2025 but CNS remains tight [#A]

  • Geopolitical risk: Low for Swedish/European operations — BioArctic's R&D is Stockholm-based; no material China/Taiwan supply chain exposure; primary geopolitical exposure is USD/SEK FX on royalties (which is a financial rather than supply risk) [#J]

  • Supply chain risk: ARIA monitoring MRI infrastructure is a systemic bottleneck — not a supplier risk in the conventional sense but a critical input constraint on the commercial ramp of lecanemab; insufficient MRI capacity at US infusion centres has been cited repeatedly by Eisai as a commercial bottleneck [#A]

  • Sub: this is partially structural (fixed asset base of MRI machines) and partially addressable via point-of-care ARIA monitoring protocol approvals; resolution timeline uncertain [#J]

  • Multi-source: BioArctic's AbbVie/Neurimmune (AL002) collaboration provides one alternative development partner — diversifying slightly the supplier-of-capital-and-development-services dynamic; however AL002 is pre-Phase 3 and commercially negligible in the 18M-3Y horizon [#A]

  • Deflation tailwind: CRO market capacity loosening in 2024-2025 — post-biotech-funding-crunch, CRO demand from small biotechs has fallen; large pharma demand remains strong but CNS CRO capacity is modestly less constrained; may provide BioArctic some pricing relief at the margin [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bear read on supplier dynamics is hidden in the Eisai layer — Leqembi's commercial supply chain is entirely Eisai-managed and BioArctic has no visibility into CMO capacity, batch failure rates, supply chain disruption planning, or manufacturing cost trends; if Eisai faces a manufacturing disruption, BioArctic has no contingency and no information advantage [#A]

§4 — Competitor Analysis (market share + responses)

  • Verdict: Donanemab (Eli Lilly's Kisunla) is a credible, already-approved competitor with a differentiated dosing profile; competitive intensity in the amyloid-targeting Alzheimer's space is rising and threatens Leqembi's share trajectory [#J]

  • L3Y share trend: Leqembi is market-creating, not market-taking — the anti-amyloid Alzheimer's market did not exist commercially until Leqembi's accelerated approval (Jan 2023) and traditional approval (Jul 2023); market share metrics against historical incumbents are not meaningful; the contest is vs donanemab for a nascent market [#J]

  • Sub: as of Q4 2024, Leqembi global net sales approximately $100-140m annualised (BioArctic/Eisai disclosures); donanemab approved July 2024 and ramping from Q3 2024; early prescription data (IQVIA/Symphony) suggests approximately equal or Leqembi-leading share in US neurologist prescribing as of Q1 2025, but donanemab gaining [#A]

  • Geographic share: Leqembi leads in Japan (approved Sep 2023, home market advantage for Eisai) and early European access programmes; US market is the pivotal battleground; EU full approval pending as of early 2025 [#J]

  • Sub: Japan is strategically important — Eisai has strong domestic commercial infrastructure; Japan early mover advantage may prove durable if reimbursement is favourable [#A]

  • Sub: EU approval timeline (EMA CHMP opinion expected H2 2025) represents a significant revenue-unlocking event; donanemab EU filing is lagging Leqembi by approximately 12 months [#J]

  • Competitive response — product: Donanemab's fixed-duration dosing (treatment until amyloid clearance, then stop) is a genuine differentiator — Leqembi requires indefinite bi-weekly infusion; donanemab's average 5-7 dose course is materially more convenient; this is a patient-facing preference advantage not easily countered without a subcutaneous formulation [#A]

  • Sub: Leqembi subcutaneous formulation is in Phase 3 (AHEAD 3-45 and MIRAGE trials underway); SC formulation would partially close the convenience gap but data readout is 2026-2027 [#J]

  • Sub: donanemab's single-dose monthly IV vs Leqembi bi-weekly IV is also a convenience advantage even in the infusion period [#A]

  • Competitive response — price: Donanemab's $32,000/year US list price is modestly above Leqembi's ~$26,500 — Lilly chose a premium positioning; neither company has engaged in overt price competition as of Q1 2025; pricing war risk remains latent [#J]

  • Sub: per-course total cost of donanemab (fixed duration) may be lower than indefinite Leqembi — important payer framing that favours donanemab in formulary negotiations [#A]

  • Competitive response — M&A: No M&A responses from direct competitors directly affecting BioArctic — the anti-amyloid market is a duopoly; no acquisition of a third competitive programme has occurred in this mechanism class; broader Alzheimer's M&A (tau, synaptic, neuroinflammation) is not directly competitive [#J]

  • Innovation rate: Next-generation competitive threats: subcutaneous donanemab (Lilly developing), oral BACE inhibitors (failed historically), anti-tau programmes — none represent a near-term (<3Y) competitive threat to lecanemab's market position; the anti-amyloid mechanism is validated and both Leqembi and Kisunla are entrenched [#A]

  • Sub: BioArctic's own next-generation programme (BAN2401 SC formulation, next-gen antibody candidates) represents a pipeline optionality on maintaining relevance in the competitive cycle [#J]

  • Pricing discipline: Current pricing is rational — both Eisai and Lilly are maintaining list prices without obvious discounting as of Q1 2025; rebate structures to payers are not disclosed; pricing discipline may deteriorate if market penetration disappoints for both [#A]

  • New entrant threat: Remote in the near term — a third anti-amyloid antibody with Phase 3 data would require a minimum of 3-4 years to reach approval; the AHEAD consortium / prevention trial space (Roche's gantenerumab failed; aducanumab (Biogen) pulled) suggests the anti-amyloid field is de-risked around two validated antibodies [#J]

  • Sub: Roche's remternetug (anti-amyloid) in Phase 3 is a medium-term threat but data not expected before 2027-2028 [#A]

  • Disruption signal: Oral small molecule competitors (Novo Nordisk's semaglutide Alzheimer's trial; GLP-1 class adjunct therapy) — highly speculative in terms of mechanism but if GLP-1 class drugs show meaningful Alzheimer's disease modification, the market framing shifts materially away from infusion biologics; this is a 5Y+ risk not a 3Y risk [#J]

  • Segment share: Early-stage / mild cognitive impairment segment — Leqembi's Phase 3 data is strongest in early-stage patients; donanemab's TRAILBLAZER-ALZ 2 data showed stronger effects in low/medium tau patients; head-to-head data do not exist; market segmentation is emerging and unclear [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bear read on competitive dynamics is that donanemab's convenience profile (fixed duration + monthly dosing) represents a durable structural advantage over bi-weekly indefinite infusion that BioArctic/Eisai cannot fully close even with subcutaneous Leqembi; in a market where neurologist and patient time constraints are binding, convenience may matter more than any marginal clinical difference; sell-side models may systematically underweight this preference effect [#J]

§5 — Distributor + Channel Analysis

  • Verdict: BioArctic has zero direct channel involvement — all distribution, specialty pharmacy, infusion centre logistics, and reimbursement navigation are Eisai-controlled; this is an unusual "arms-length royalty owner" channel position [#J]

  • Direct-to-customer: BioArctic is entirely indirect — does not sell to any end-customer directly; no commercial infrastructure, no sales force, no medical affairs team of commercial scale; the entire route-to-market is Eisai's responsibility [#A]

  • Sub: BioArctic has a nominal co-promotion right in Nordic markets but has not exercised this meaningfully; no BioArctic sales representatives detailing neurologists [#J]

  • Indirect: Eisai + Biogen constitute the entire commercial channel — Leqembi in the US is co-promoted by Eisai and Biogen under their existing commercialisation agreement; Biogen's commitment has moderated post-Aduhelm; Eisai is the de facto lead commercial entity globally [#A]

  • Sub: Leqembi is dispensed through specialised infusion centres (hospital neurology departments, private infusion suites), not conventional retail pharmacy; this is a channel complexity that limits scale-up speed [#J]

  • Channel concentration: Top-1 channel = 100% (Eisai) — from BioArctic's perspective, there is one channel; no multi-channel optionality; channel concentration is absolute [#A]

  • Distributor leverage: Eisai holds all commercial leverage over BioArctic — Eisai determines detailing frequency, market access strategy, pricing to payers, support programmes, and reimbursement advocacy; BioArctic participates in joint governance committees but cannot override Eisai's commercial decisions [#J]

  • Pricing pressure — channel: Infusion centre economics create a channel-level margin pressure on Eisai — infusion centres charge administration fees; patient support programmes (Eisai's ACT AD programme) absorb some costs; hub-and-spoke infusion network economics are not disclosed but are a real cost layer between Eisai's net price and prescribing physician convenience [#A]

  • Channel evolution: Subcutaneous formulation would transform the channel — SC Leqembi, if approved, shifts from infusion-centre-dependent delivery to self-injection or home-care; this massively expands the addressable prescriber universe and reduces channel friction; timeline 2026-2028 [#J]

  • Sub: this is the single most important channel evolution event in BioArctic's 3-5Y horizon; SC approval would partially address the convenience disadvantage vs donanemab [#A]

  • Disintermediation: Not applicable — BioArctic is already maximally disintermediated; there is no further channel to bypass [#J]

  • Service level pressure: ARIA monitoring burden is a service-level constraint on neurologist adoption — the requirement for serial MRI monitoring (at initiation, 6 weeks, 12 weeks, and periodically thereafter) imposes a significant administrative burden on prescribing neurologists; this suppresses channel throughput [#A]

  • Sub: estimated MRI requirement adds approximately $3,000-5,000 per patient per year in associated monitoring costs — a channel-level friction not captured in drug price alone [#J]

  • Channel margin: Infusion centre margin on biologic drug administration is typically thin — hospital infusion suites are under reimbursement pressure; J-code reimbursement adequacy for Leqembi administration is an active market access issue; inadequate J-code coverage suppresses prescriber willingness [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bear read on channel dynamics is that BioArctic is entirely dependent on Eisai's commercial capability and commitment level — if Eisai's management changes Alzheimer's investment prioritisation (e.g., in response to donanemab share loss), BioArctic has no channel of its own to fall back on; the "asset-light" model is also a "commercially helpless" model [#J]
