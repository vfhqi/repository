EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (BLUF)

Stadler Rail operates as a highly integrated rolling stock manufacturer providing end-to-end lifecycle solutions across three reporting segments: vehicle manufacturing (approximately 80% of revenue), service and components (17%), and signalling (3%) .

The company generates revenue primarily through the design, manufacture, and delivery of passenger trains spanning high-speed intercity to urban metros, complemented by multi-decade maintenance contracts and proprietary automated train control systems. The business employs a highly conservative "units-of-delivery" accounting method that delays revenue recognition until vehicles are completely finished and accepted by customers , creating significant lag between operational output and reported

financials. This conservative approach meant production output exceeded reported revenue by over CHF 1 billion in 2025 , masking underlying operational momentum during heavy production ramp-ups.

The company maintains exceptionally long-term earnings visibility with an order backlog exceeding CHF 32 billion , providing multiple years of secured manufacturing pipeline and insulating the business from short-term macroeconomic volatility

. 1 3 5 2 4 6 profile and peer positioning Stadler achieved 13% revenue growth to CHF 3.68 billion in 2025, with operating margins improving to 4.4% from 3.1% in the prior year , though this performance remains well below management's 7-8% medium-term margin

target and structurally trails best-in-class peers. Alstom delivered a 6.1% adjusted EBIT margin on €19.2 billion revenue, while Siemens Mobility consistently achieves industry-leading profit margins near 8.5% to 10% ,

reflecting their substantially higher mix of software-rich signalling and service revenue. Stadler's 2025 free cash flow plunged to CHF -588.4 million, driven by massive working capital requirements as previous customer advance payments were exhausted , exposing the capital-intensive nature

of the rolling stock assembly model. Peer comparison reveals stark divergence in capital efficiency: Siemens Mobility delivered an exceptional 0.93 cash conversion rate generating over €1 billion in free cash flow through its asset-light model , while Stadler's structural inventory build-up during concurrent project execution permanently drags capital velocity.

The company maintains a shareholder-friendly dividend policy distributing approximately 60% of net income , though it entirely avoids buybacks in favour of aggressive capacity expansion investments totalling approximately CHF 250 million planned for 2026.

9 11 13 15 10 12 14 16 Industry structure and value chain economics The global rail equipment industry exhibits exceptionally high consolidation with the top four European players controlling over 90% of most rolling stock categories, though component suppliers historically capture the profit pool by consistently generating mid to high teens operating margins while rail OEMs struggle with mid to high single-digit operating margins .

Stadler operates primarily in the margin-constrained OEM assembly stage characterised by high capital intensity and 5-8% typical EBIT margins, while selectively participating in higher- margin subsystem components for bogies and converters . Downstream aftermarket services deliver substantially superior economics with 20-30% estimated gross margins and 10-15% EBIT margins, representing the most profitable stage as operators structurally outsource critical maintenance directly to equipment manufacturers .

The industry benefits from robust structural tailwinds including massive government infrastructure funding: Germany established a €500 billion Special Fund for Infrastructure with major rail allocations, while the European Recovery and Resilience Facility allocated up to €58 billion specifically for rail investments. However, tier-1 suppliers like Knorr-Bremse dominate critical subsystem niches through extreme homologation barriers, extracting premium margins and vastly outperforming downstream assemblers , prompting aggressive vertical integration efforts across major OEMs.

19 concentration and retention dynamics Stadler serves approximately 270 customers globally across 47 countries, with the DACH region accounting for 49% of net revenues , creating significant exposure to German-speaking public transit budgets and European fiscal policy.

The company aggressively secures 10-to-37 year full-service contracts alongside new rolling stock orders, with the Cologne CITYLINK agreement including a 37-year spare parts and maintenance contract ensuring multi-generational revenue , demonstrating exceptional customer lock-in .

Public transit operators rarely switch OEMs mid-lifecycle due to the extreme cost of fleet interoperability requirements , while deeply embedded proprietary digital signalling systems create massive technical and operational lock-in for municipal transit operators .

Approximately 80% of major long-term rolling stock contracts include vital price escalation clauses , providing structural inflation protection, though dominant publicly-owned customers like SNCF and Deutsche Bahn are highly cost-conscious and politically constrained, limiting OEM pricing power .

Major contract wins spanning ÖBB's 186 double-decker framework, KVB's 700 million euro tram order, and Saudi Arabia's 600 million CHF desert-adapted intercity trains validate Stadler's unique tailor-made customisation capability for extreme operating environments.

Supplier vulnerability and crisis adaptation The industry suffers from extreme supplier concentration with critical subsystems exclusively manufactured by select tier-1 suppliers, and approximately 90% of total procurement volume processed with contractual partners located within European countries , amplifying regional climate event exposure.

Natural disasters in 2024 devastated around 40 strategic suppliers, with Valencia storms completely destroying several factories and wiping out diesel engine inventories, postponing 200,000 working hours and shifting CHF 350 million in revenue to future years , exposing the profound fragility of Stadler's deeply interlinked European supplier network.

Management responded by overriding its traditionally decentralised procurement model to prioritise limited aluminium centrally and launching an in-house aluminium welding shop in Salt Lake City to bypass external European supply constraints , aggressively insourcing critical manufacturing steps to secure US Buy America compliance and dodge punitive 39% import tariffs.

Working capital dynamics expose exceptionally weak supplier payment leverage with massive CHF 853 million inventory versus just CHF 340 million in trade payables, while enormous customer advance payments drive a CHF 1.1 billion negative WIP balance proving customers entirely fund Stadler's protracted production cycles . positioning and differentiation Stadler ranks as the strong number two in European passenger trains with 18% market share, trailing Alstom's dominant 40% share in key segments and competing against Siemens Mobility's 12% rolling stock share , though Siemens aggressively dominates higher-margin signalling markets.

The company exploits a structural advantage through tailor-made manufacturing agility and dominates the green transition with a 50% European market share in battery and hydrogen-powered rail vehicles, selling over 280 alternative drive units , representing genuine technological leadership in decarbonisation.

Siemens and Alstom rely heavily on rigid platforms and struggle to modify them to niche operator needs, specifically lacking agility for unique gauge requirements , while Stadler remains the undisputed global monopolist in highly specialised rack-and-pinion and extreme-climate railway vehicles .

However, Alstom's acquisition of Bombardier Transportation created a dominant global player with unparalleled scale across all major geographies , and the top four European players now control over 90% of the market, structurally supporting long-term consolidated pricing power.

Alstom aggressively integrates upstream by acquiring tier-1 suppliers to control critical hydrogen propulsion technology internally , threatening to erode Stadler's component-level differentiation advantages.

Cross-value-chain signals confirm tier-1 suppliers like Knorr-Bremse expect to maintain best-in-class 16% to 19% EBIT margins through 2030, demonstrating the structural inability of rail OEMs to force equitable profit-sharing , while Siemens' explicit retreat from urban rail removes a formidable pricing competitor for Stadler's core tailor-made municipal segments.
