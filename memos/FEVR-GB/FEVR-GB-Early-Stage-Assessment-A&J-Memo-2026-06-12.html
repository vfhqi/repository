RA1 Change Forces (ESA Deepened) 11 findings

External change forces:

1.
The Molson Coors network expansion from 150,000 to 500,000 US accounts is now live and evidenced. FY25 US revenue grew 6% cc to £131.9m in the first year of the JV (transition year with significant structural disruption); the June 2026 AGM confirms US is "performing ahead of expectations" at a point when the distribution infrastructure is fully embedded and the marketing campaign has launched. The specific number of accounts reached, distributor coverage quality, and velocity-per-account metrics are the ESA-stage questions
2.
The first national US marketing campaign (launched April 2026) is a step-change in brand-building infrastructure. Prior to 2026, Fevertree's US marketing was predominantly local and on-trade focused. The doubling of US marketing spend and launch of the first-ever national campaign transforms Fevertree from a word-of-mouth premium brand into an actively advertised mass-premium brand. Comparable situations (e.g., Fever-Tree's UK trajectory 2012–2018 when it invested heavily in on-trade advocacy) suggest that marketing investment is a multiplier on distribution quality; it converts shelf presence into consumer pull-through. This is the mechanism that discriminates genuine depletion velocity from pipeline fill
3.
US premium spirits market context: mixed on volume but Fevertree is a premium mixer, not a spirit. US premium spirits volumes have declined for three consecutive years (2022–2024), which bear analysts cite as a structural headwind for Fevertree's US mixer TAM. However, the causal link from spirits volume decline to mixer volume decline is indirect: premium mixers benefit from the moderation trend (more consumers choosing one quality serve over multiple mediocre ones), the at-home premiumisation trend (consumers recreating cocktail bar experiences at home), and the expansion of non-alcoholic occasions (Fevertree's tonic and ginger beer are consumed without spirits). The external change force from spirits market normalisation is less severe than the bear case implies
4.
ROW acceleration (+22% cc FY25) confirms the globalisation thesis. Australia commenced local production in 2025, directly demonstrating the onshoring model that Fevertree intends to replicate in the US by 2027. France and Benelux delivery improvements are genuine. These ROW data points provide an external validation of management's claim that the Molson Coors distribution model is replicable; the smaller-scale but self-executed ROW expansion is delivering strong growth, supporting the hypothesis that the US JV's infrastructure investment will yield similar or better returns
5.
Glass cost headwinds are fully hedged for 2026 and 66% hedged for 2027. The external inflationary pressures that drove the 2021–2023 margin collapse are structurally contained for the near-to-medium term. Glass is the single largest input cost; energy (glass furnace fuel) is the primary glass cost driver. Full 2026 coverage and material 2027 coverage removes a key external headwind from the near-term P&L and reduces forecast uncertainty; a prerequisite for the earnings upgrade cycle that drives the TSR case
6.
Category structure change: Q Mixers has shrunk to one-fifth of Fevertree's size. Q Mixers was Fevertree's primary premium US competitor with genuine brand equity and distribution depth. Following its acquisition by Eurazeo (private equity), Q suffered inventory shortages, experienced experienced sales representative turnover, and adopted a retail discounting strategy that damaged its on-trade premium positioning. The competitive gap between Fevertree and the #2 US premium mixer has widened dramatically during exactly the period when Fevertree is investing most aggressively in US market-building. Structural competitive advantage is expanding, not contracting
7.
No/low alcohol sector growing at 7% CAGR underpins the secular demand basis. Premium mixers serve both the spirit-and-mixer occasion and the standalone premium soft drink occasion. The structural growth of the no/low alcohol sector directly expands Fevertree's addressable market beyond the traditional spirits customer base; a material positive external change force that was not operating in 2016–2020 when Fevertree's US strategy was primarily gin-and-tonic focused
8.
The Molson Coors relationship is institutional, not personal. Unlike prior US distribution relationships (where Fevertree's premium pricing power depended on the specific enthusiasm of individual distributor account managers), the Molson Coors partnership includes contractual minimum profit guarantees (2026–2030), Molson Coors' equity stake (initial strategic acquisition: 8.5% for £71m per CF primary source; current effective stake ~9.4% following Fevertree's £100m 2025 buyback reducing total float; both figures appear in the source reports at different dates), and an institutional incentive alignment that makes Fevertree's success directly linked to Molson Coors' portfolio returns. This is a qualitatively different level of distribution commitment than a standard third-party distributor agreement
