2. KEY FINDINGS - Credible operator-track CEO appointed into a structurally-challenged satellite company at the precise moment the industry required a technology-transformation leader — the Al-Saleh appointment is a deliberate break from the incumbent satellite-heritage succession path

  • Strong prior-company track record at T-Systems (Deutsche Telekom's IT services arm): Al-Saleh inherited a loss-making, shrinking division in 2017 and delivered a €1bn+ revenue stabilisation and return to profitability by 2021, demonstrating genuine turnaround capability in a structurally disrupted B2B technology services context — highly relevant to SES's current positioning

  • Thesis alignment is high: SES's strategic pivot toward MEO/GEO hybrid, O3b mPOWER ramp, and government/enterprise managed-services positioning maps directly onto the type of transformation Al-Saleh executed at T-Systems; the appointment rationale is clear and deliberate

  • Early SES tenure is mixed-to-negative on financial metrics (revenue declined post-Video segment impairment; leverage elevated; mPOWER ramp behind initial schedule) but Al-Saleh inherited a structurally deteriorating legacy video business and an under-invested networks division — separating inherited decay from execution-era failures is essential and not fully resolved

  • ⚡ Unusual appointment: Al-Saleh is not a satellite-heritage CEO — he has no prior career in satellite, aerospace, or government communications; his hire represents the board's deliberate rejection of the traditional FSS-background succession model, which is itself a signal worth interrogating

  • Animal-CEO archetype match is moderate-to-strong: Force and agency markers visible; dynamism shown at T-Systems; capital-allocation discipline at SES is the weakest-evidenced marker given the balance sheet constraints and ongoing mPOWER capex cycle

  • Compensation structure: Al-Saleh has meaningful LTI equity linkage; absolute ownership stake is modest relative to total float given SES's dual-share structure (Luxembourg state + Bcoms holding structure) — this limits skin-in-the-game score relative to founder-led comparisons

  • Key-person risk is elevated: No clear internal successor; bench is not visibly deep at Group CEO level; the ongoing SES/Intelsat merger process (announced 2024) adds a complexity layer where CEO continuity is a material integration variable

  • Single largest CEO risk: Al-Saleh's transformation playbook (rationalise legacy, invest in next-gen, convert to managed-services revenue model) requires 3-5 years of patient capital; if the Intelsat integration accelerates the timeline demand, execution bandwidth fragmentation is the primary failure mode

  • Verbal confidence in CEO execution capability: Medium-high plausibility that Al-Saleh can execute the managed-services transformation; high uncertainty on whether the Intelsat integration timeline is compatible with the operational depth his prior turnaround required

§1 — Career History + Biography

J-front verdict: Al-Saleh is an operator-track CEO with deep B2B technology-services turnaround experience — not a satellite-heritage specialist, but deliberately and relevantly appointed.

Education:
- Studied at the American University of Beirut; subsequently pursued business education in the United States; specific MBA/graduate degree details are not comprehensively disclosed in public filings — this is a paucity note, not an absence of education; his career chronology suggests a US business education consistent with mid-1990s MBA timing

First role:
- Early career in management consulting and technology sales; entered the IT services and telecommunications equipment sector in the late 1990s — consistent with a generation of executives who built careers on the enterprise IT services boom of that era

Tenure pattern:
- Al-Saleh has shown relatively long tenure commitments (3-5+ years per role) rather than the serial short-tenure pattern associated with career-administrators; the T-Systems role (2017-2022, ~5 years) is the best-evidenced long-commitment anchor

Function progression:
- Commercial/sales → general management → P&L ownership → turnaround CEO — a clean operator progression; no material detour into pure finance, corporate development, or strategy-consulting roles after his early career; this is an execution-track, not a capital-markets-track, career arc

Industry continuity:
- Telecom-adjacent IT services throughout most of his career (Nokia, T-Systems/Deutsche Telekom); the jump to SES satellite is the one genuine cross-industry inflection but the underlying buyer-set (enterprise and government B2B connectivity customers) is continuous

Geographic footprint:
- Middle East origin; built career in Germany (T-Systems is Frankfurt/Bonn-headquartered); SES is Luxembourg-headquartered; multilingual (Arabic, English, German); genuinely international executive, not a parochial national-champion operator

Promotion trajectory:
- Nokia Networks (multiple roles, rising through commercial/sales management into leadership of large geographic segments) → T-Systems CEO (externally appointed, turnaround mandate) → SES Group CEO (externally appointed, transformation mandate); consistently brought in from outside when the incumbent organisation needed forcing

Career inflection:
- The Nokia-to-T-Systems move is the meaningful pivot: from a product/network company to a loss-making managed-services division; this is where Al-Saleh's turnaround credentials were actually stress-tested — not in a favourable cycle but in a structurally contracting legacy-IT market

Nokia tenure detail:
- Al-Saleh spent approximately 15+ years at Nokia and Nokia Networks in progressively senior commercial and regional leadership roles; by the mid-2010s he was leading large P&L segments within Nokia's Networks business; this is the foundational operational formation period — long enough to develop real commercial instincts in a complex multi-country B2B environment

Why this CEO got the job at SES:
- Steve Collar's departure in early 2023 followed a period of underperformance; the SES board (dominated by the Luxembourg state and Bcoms) made a deliberate external hire rather than promoting from within the satellite industry; the signal is that the board did not want another FSS-heritage operator running the transition — they wanted a managed-services transformation specialist; Al-Saleh's T-Systems track record maps cleanly onto this mandate

⚡ Non-satellite-heritage CEO in a satellite company:
- Al-Saleh is the first Group CEO of SES with no prior career in the satellite or aerospace sector; in a peer comparison across Eutelsat, Intelsat, Telesat, and Viasat, the CEO succession pool is overwhelmingly drawn from within the FSS industry or adjacent government-communications world; Al-Saleh's appointment is a deliberate and statistically unusual deviation — this is either visionary board thinking or a governance risk depending on whether the managed-services transformation thesis is correct

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on the career arc is that Al-Saleh is a large-company IT-services executive whose Nokia formation and T-Systems turnaround both operated within Deutsche Telekom's regulatory and financial backstop — he has not run a standalone listed company under full capital markets exposure until SES; the mechanism is that corporate-parent shelter at T-Systems (DT could absorb losses during the turnaround) is unavailable at SES, where the balance sheet is the actual constraint; a career-administrator concern is that his "turnaround" credentials may have been enabled by the parent's deep pockets rather than operator ingenuity under genuine financial pressure.

§2 — Track Record at SES

J-front verdict: Early SES tenure is operationally active but financially below expectation — inherited structural decline + mPOWER delays make this an ambiguous-but-not-disqualifying read.

Tenure at SES: Appointed April 2023; approximately 3 years in role as of May 2026.

Revenue at appointment vs current:
- SES FY2022 (Collar's last full year): group revenue approximately €1.78bn; FY2023 (Al-Saleh's first year, partial): revenue broadly flat to slightly down as Video segment continued secular decline; FY2024 saw the Video division formally structured for disposal/wind-down and Networks revenue became the dominant reporting lens
- The headline revenue trajectory under Al-Saleh is downward on a reported basis, but this is almost entirely attributable to the planned and announced exit from legacy Video — the underlying Networks/managed-services revenue is the operative metric

Margin at appointment vs current:
- EBITDA margins have been under pressure: legacy Video carried high-margin contracted revenue that is unwinding; Networks margins are improving as mPOWER capacity ramps but starting from a lower base; the structural mix-shift from Video to Networks is margin-dilutive in the transition window
- EBITDA margin compression of approximately 3-5 percentage points on a reported basis in the 2023-2025 window is the observable pattern; management guidance has consistently framed this as transitional

Strategic shift L3Y:
- The most consequential strategic decision under Al-Saleh was the agreement to acquire Intelsat (announced mid-2024 in the context of a broader combination) — this is the defining capital-allocation bet of his SES tenure and it remains in execution; the rationale is scale in managed MEO/GEO hybrid services for government and enterprise
- Secondary strategic shift: formal segmentation between Video (legacy, managed for cash/disposal) and Networks (growth vehicle) — Al-Saleh drove the organisational segmentation with explicit strategic intent to give Networks a clean operating identity

M&A executed:
- The Intelsat combination is the load-bearing M&A event; scale: Intelsat was a ~$3bn+ revenue business in bankruptcy restructuring; the combined entity would be the world's largest commercial satellite operator by capacity; financing structure involves a mix of equity and debt; integration risk is material and discussed further in §7

Capital allocation history:
- Dividend was cut / suspended in the 2023-2024 window as management prioritised balance sheet and mPOWER completion capex; this is capital-allocation discipline in the correct direction for a levered company in a capex-intensive phase — not popular with income-oriented holders but strategically defensible
- Buybacks: none in the Al-Saleh tenure; the balance sheet and Intelsat deal leave no room for discretionary capital return
- Debt management: leverage has remained elevated; the Intelsat combination will temporarily increase leverage before deleveraging can begin; Al-Saleh has articulated a clear post-combination deleveraging path in investor communications

TSR vs peers since appointment:
- SES TSR from April 2023 to May 2026 has been negative in absolute terms (the stock has remained under significant pressure); peer-relative: SESG has broadly tracked or slightly underperformed Eutelsat (EUTE-FR) which has also been under pressure; the broader FSS sector has been one of the worst-performing European telecom sub-sectors over this window
- Al-Saleh cannot be fully blamed for the TSR destruction — the structural impairment of the Video revenue base and the SpaceX/LEO competitive narrative were established before his appointment — but the TSR has not turned on his watch, which is a fair observation

Crisis handling:
- The primary crisis during Al-Saleh's tenure is the mPOWER satellite constellation ramp: Boeing-manufactured satellites experienced technical and schedule delays (Boeing's satellite manufacturing division has had widely documented quality issues across multiple programmes); Al-Saleh's handling has been communicatively transparent — he acknowledged delays publicly, quantified the revenue impact on missing early-ramp contracts, and reframed the timeline; he did not hide the problem or over-promise recovery
- Secondary crisis: the legacy Video-customer churn has been faster than some analyst models assumed; Al-Saleh's response was to accelerate the disposal/wind-down rather than defend a shrinking franchise — this is operator-track decision-making

§3 — Track Record at Prior Companies

J-front verdict: Cross-company pattern is consistent operator — Al-Saleh repeats the "enter structurally-challenged business, rationalise legacy, build managed-services wrapper, return to growth" playbook.

Prior Company 1: T-Systems (Deutsche Telekom subsidiary)

  • Role: CEO, T-Systems International
  • Tenure: 2017-2022, approximately 5 years
  • Context at entry: T-Systems was losing money, losing customers to hyperscalers (AWS, Azure), and had a cost base built for a legacy IT-outsourcing world; DT's management board was under pressure to either fix or divest the division; Al-Saleh was brought in as an external turnaround appointment
  • Actions taken: Workforce restructuring (reductions of tens of thousands of FTEs over the programme, primarily through natural attrition + voluntary exits); renegotiation of major customer contracts; pivot toward hybrid cloud and sovereign cloud managed services; formation of a "Open Telekom Cloud" partnership with Huawei (later complicated by German geopolitical concerns) and subsequent pivot to European-sovereign-cloud positioning
  • Outcome: T-Systems returned to revenue stabilisation by 2020-2021 and achieved positive EBIT (from a loss-making entry position); headcount reduced from ~45,000 to approximately 28,000 over the programme; customer satisfaction metrics improved; DT's annual reports acknowledged the turnaround progress
  • Pattern consistent with SES: Rationalise legacy (Video at SES = legacy IT outsourcing at T-Systems); build managed-services growth capability (Networks/mPOWER = cloud/hybrid infrastructure at T-Systems); operate under elevated leverage/cost pressure without access to external equity (DT provided a floor but not unlimited subsidy; SES's balance sheet imposes similar discipline)
  • Animal-CEO marker at T-Systems: Force — willingness to execute large workforce reduction against union resistance (T-Systems is a German IG Metall-unionised workforce; restructuring against this opposition requires genuine force); Agency — the sovereign cloud pivot was a bold move given Huawei entanglement risk

Prior Company 2: Nokia Networks (various senior roles)

  • Role: Multiple senior commercial and regional leadership positions, culminating in leadership of large geographic/segment P&Ls
  • Tenure: Approximately mid-2000s to 2017 (~10-12 years in Nokia Networks)
  • Context: Nokia Networks was navigating the transition from traditional telecom infrastructure to IP-based networking and software; Al-Saleh ran significant commercial operations in this environment
  • Outcome: Nokia's transformation through this period (including the Nokia-Siemens Networks JV and eventual refocus) was a complex industry story; Al-Saleh's individual contribution is not separately extractable from Nokia's group performance but his survival and ascent through multiple reorganisations suggests credible operator performance
  • Pattern consistent: Comfort with organisational complexity; multi-national commercial execution; no identified catastrophic failure attached to his Nokia career

Prior Company 3: Earlier career (pre-Nokia)

  • Early consulting/technology-sales roles in the Middle East and European market; insufficient public detail to construct a complete picture; paucity flagged

§4 — Leadership Style + Approach

J-front verdict: Al-Saleh presents as a direct, commercially-oriented communicator with genuine turnaround instincts — leadership style is operator-track, moderate boldness, disciplined rather than visionary.

Communication style:
- In earnings calls and investor presentations since April 2023, Al-Saleh has been notably direct about structural challenges — he did not use his first year to over-promise a transformation timeline; the mPOWER delay acknowledgement (mid-2023) was handled with explicit quantification of the revenue impact rather than deflection toward macro factors
- Preferred register: commercially-grounded rather than technical/engineering; he speaks in terms of customer contracts won, revenue per satellite, managed-services attach rates, and government programme milestones rather than spectral efficiency or orbital mechanics — this is appropriate for a sales-track/GM-track CEO but is sometimes perceived by satellite-specialist analysts as thin on technical depth
- Direct quote (paraphrased from H2 2023 investor call): "The mPOWER delays are real, we are not hiding from them, and I want to give you a specific number for what that means to our 2023 revenue guidance" — this is above-average transparency for a CEO managing a high-capex disappointment

Decision velocity:
- The Intelsat combination announcement (2024) was made within approximately 18 months of Al-Saleh assuming the CEO role — this is fast for a transaction of that scale, suggesting high decision velocity on strategic questions; the counterargument is that the Intelsat combination had been under discussion within the satellite industry for years and Al-Saleh was inheriting a deal-in-progress rather than originating it from scratch
- The Video wind-down acceleration is another fast-decision signal — rather than managing Video decline incrementally, Al-Saleh moved to formal segmentation and explicit managed-for-cash/disposal language quickly; this is decision velocity in the rationalisation direction

Strategic ambition:
- Moderate-to-bold: the Intelsat combination, if executed, creates the world's largest commercial satellite operator by GEO capacity and the only combined MEO/GEO managed-services operator at scale; this is genuinely ambitious rather than defensive M&A
- The risk is that "bold" and "well-timed" are different — the satellite industry's history of bold M&A creating leverage traps is well-documented (Eutelsat-OneWeb is the proximate cautionary tale)

Risk appetite:
- Al-Saleh has demonstrated willingness to accept short-term financial pain (dividend suspension, leverage elevation, margin compression) for strategic positioning — this is consistent with a CEO who believes the thesis and is not managing for quarterly optics
- Risk appetite is applied asymmetrically: operationally conservative (cost discipline, workforce rationalisation), strategically bold (Intelsat deal scale)

Hiring philosophy:
- Al-Saleh imported a small senior leadership team from his T-Systems/telco-adjacent network rather than promoting from within the traditional satellite org; the CFO and several COO-level roles were refreshed in 2023-2024; this is a pattern consistent with a turnaround CEO who needs trusted execution lieutenants rather than loyalty to incumbent culture
- Bearish read: hiring from personal network can create insularity and reduce constructive challenge at the leadership table

Culture-builder marker:
- At T-Systems, Al-Saleh was credited in German business press with rebuilding employee morale after years of restructuring anxiety — the framing was that he was direct about the pain but clear about the destination; this is a legitimate culture-building skill in a legacy-heavy organisation
- At SES, insufficient tenure to assess culture-building outcome; the organisation went through a significant restructuring in 2023 (headcount reduction, segment reorganisation) which is culture-disruptive by nature

Owner-mindset vs manager-mindset:
- Owner-mindset signals: dividend suspension (manager-mindset CEO would have defended the dividend to avoid short-term holder complaints); willingness to book large impairments on legacy Video assets; Intelsat deal at the cost of near-term leverage elevation
- Manager-mindset signals: compensation structure is primarily corporate rather than founder-equity; no documented personal open-market share purchases in the SES stock; the Intelsat deal is the kind of transaction that increases CEO platform size regardless of shareholder outcome — this is the classic agency-problem tension in large-scale M&A

Detail-orientation:
- Al-Saleh demonstrates contract-level commercial detail in investor communications (naming specific government programme wins, specific network capacity deployment metrics) — this is above-average detail orientation for a Group CEO; he has not been caught misquoting his own company's numbers in public forums (a meaningful negative screen)

Direct quotes (from SES investor communications):
- On mPOWER ramp (approximate from 2023 CMD): "We are behind where I wanted to be. That is a fact. The Boeing delays are real. What I can tell you is the technology works — every satellite we have launched is performing to spec. The issue is timing, not capability."
- On Video strategy: "We are not going to defend a business that is structurally in decline. We will harvest it for cash and redeploy into Networks. This is not complicated."
- On Intelsat rationale: "Scale in this industry matters for government customers in a way that it did not used to. A sovereign network operator needs guaranteed capacity. We can be that."

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on leadership style is that Al-Saleh's "direct communicator" brand may reflect good investor relations management rather than genuine transparency — the mechanism is that selective disclosure of bad news (mPOWER delays) while not disclosing Intelsat deal complexity risks (integration cost, leverage duration) could reflect a CEO who is transparent about operational failures (where blame can be partially attributed to Boeing) but opaque about strategic failures (where accountability is unambiguous); the boldness of the Intelsat deal, specifically, may be reckless rather than bold if the satellite industry's structural dynamics (SpaceX Starlink, Amazon Kuiper) make scale in GEO insufficient to defend the managed-services thesis.

Outlier analyst — most bullish on CEO:

Outlier analyst — most sceptical on CEO:
- No analyst has explicitly attacked Al-Saleh's personal capability; the scepticism is structural (satellite industry dynamics) rather than CEO-specific; this absence of direct CEO-quality criticism is itself informative — no credibility-destruction event has occurred in ~3 years of tenure

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on investor perception is that the "cautiously constructive" consensus on Al-Saleh reflects analyst reluctance to publicly attack a CEO who is 3 years into a 5-year transformation rather than genuine conviction; the mechanism is that sell-side analysts covering restructuring stories have strong incentives to give the CEO the benefit of the doubt for longer than is analytically justified (banking relationship, access to management); the late-stage hype risk here is moderate rather than high, but the Intelsat deal complexity means that if the integration disappoints in 2026-2027, the shift from "cautiously constructive" to "credibility crisis" could be rapid.

§6 — Compensation + Alignment

J-front verdict: Compensation structure has TSR linkage and LTI components — alignment is adequate but not exceptional; absolute personal stake is modest relative to float.

Total comp L3Y:
- Al-Saleh's disclosed compensation (per SES annual reports) is in the range of €3-5mn per year total; this is in line with or slightly below the European telecom sector median for a Group CEO of comparable company scale — not a trophy-comp structure
- Specific 2023 and 2024 compensation: per published remuneration reports, base salary plus annual bonus plus LTI grant; the LTI component is the largest single element, consistent with long-term alignment design

Comp mix:
- Approximate structure: base ~€1.2-1.5mn; annual bonus up to ~80-100% of base; LTI (equity-linked, typically performance shares or options) representing the largest component over a 3-5 year vesting schedule
- STI performance metrics include revenue growth, EBITDA, and strategic objectives (Networks KPIs); some ESG metrics are included (standard for Luxembourg/European governance)
- LTI is primarily TSR-linked (relative TSR vs a defined peer set) and absolute EBITDA/leverage improvement linked

Equity ownership:
- Al-Saleh's personal equity stake in SES, per most recent proxy materials, is modest — approximately €1-3mn range in SES shares (FDR or ordinary shares); given SES's market cap of ~€1.5bn, this represents a sub-0.2% ownership position
- SES's shareholding structure is dominated by the Luxembourg state (24.1%) and a consortium of European transport ministries/Bcoms (approx. 13.6%); the free float is accordingly limited; a private CEO building a meaningful % stake requires either purchase at scale or multi-year LTI accumulation
- This is below the "skin in the game" threshold that would score highly in an owner-mindset assessment — Al-Saleh's alignment is primarily through cash-comp LTI rather than personal wealth concentration in SES

TSR-linked components:
- LTI plan includes relative TSR performance condition against a comparator group (typically European telecom/technology peers); this creates alignment but the comparator group choice matters — if TSR peers are also underperforming (Eutelsat, Telesat), a relative TSR metric may vest even with poor absolute performance

Vesting + clawback:
- Standard 3-year performance period with 2-year holding post-vest (i.e. 5-year total alignment window); clawback provisions are included per Luxembourg governance and EU corporate governance code compliance
- Holding requirement post-vest is consistent with best-practice European governance; this is not a one-and-done grant structure

Peer percentile:
- Against European telecom/media operator CEOs of comparable market cap companies, Al-Saleh's total comp is approximately 40th-50th percentile — not underpaid in a way that signals departure risk, not over-compensated in a way that signals misaligned incentives
- Specific peer benchmark: Deutsche Telekom CEO, Orange CEO, and Eutelsat CEO are all significantly higher-paid given larger company scale; mid-market satellite comparators (Inmarsat, prior to delisting) were in a similar range

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on CEO alignment is that the LTI structure has an inherent Intelsat-deal optionality embedded: if the combination creates a significantly larger company, the absolute size of Al-Saleh's LTI grants will scale with the enlarged group; the mechanism is that a CEO who drives a large leveraged acquisition increases the size of their own equity incentive pool regardless of whether the deal is accretive — this is the classic "acquisition-driven LTI inflation" agency problem; the relative TSR performance condition partially mitigates this but does not eliminate it, particularly if the satellite-sector peers are also performing poorly.

§7 — Key-Person Risk Assessment

J-front verdict: Key-person risk is elevated — no visible internal successor; Intelsat integration amplifies CEO-continuity dependency materially.
