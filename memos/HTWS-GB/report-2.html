§1 — Brief recap + Key components summary

HTWS owns and operates ~14,700 passive tower sites across eight African markets and Oman. Revenue comes from long-term master lease agreements (MLAs) with MNOs, with economic value accruing from the tenancy ratio flywheel: each additional co-location tenant on an existing site has ~90%+ incremental EBITDA margin. Change forces are concentrated in four areas: (1) a new CEO completing his first full strategic cycle; (2) a portfolio inflecting from growth-driven balance-sheet stretch to cash compounding; (3) MNO demand dynamics in African markets at a critical 4G penetration stage; and (4) an unprecedented competitive structural shift via the MTN/IHS deal.

Internal change forces summary: - CEO: Greenwood transition (April 2022); IMPACT 2030 strategy launch (March 2026); first FCF positive milestone 2024 [#I] - Balance sheet: leverage from 4.4x → 3.4x in two years; credit upgrade; $500m notes refinancing 2024-25 [#A] - Portfolio: no new market entries since Oman (2022); shift from footprint-building to tenancy ratio optimisation [#A] - Cost/efficiency: Project 100 (power/solar transition); $11m invested in grid/solar/battery in 2025; AI-driven infrastructure planning tools [#I] - Strategy shift: explicit pivot from capex growth phase to "cash compounding sweet spot" [#J]

External change forces summary: - Demand: 4G proliferation structural, 5G nascent; $77bn African mobile infrastructure investment expected 2024-30 [#I] - MNO health: Airtel Africa capex cycle rebounding after three years of decline; MTN Nigeria spending surging [#A] - FX: NGN and GHS structurally weak; TZS stable; hard-currency revenue linkage 71% of EBITDA [#A] - Competitive: MTN/IHS deal is the dominant structural change force — Africa tower landscape being redrawn [#J] - Technology: satellite as complementary, not disruptive; data centre demand emerging as incremental long-run tailwind [#A]

§7 — Value chain — Competition

J — Verdict: The MTN/IHS deal is the most significant competitive structural change force in the African tower market in a decade. It reintegrates ~29,000 towers under a single MNO and potentially reverses the disaggregation trend. Near-term impact on HTWS is ambiguous (limited geographic overlap with IHS Nigeria/West Africa core) but the precedent is structurally negative for independent TowerCo valuations. [#J]

MTN/IHS Towers deal — the dominant competitive change force: - MTN agreed to acquire ~75% of IHS Towers it doesn't already own for $2.2bn; total enterprise value ~$6.2bn including debt. Announced Feb-26. [#I] - IHS operates ~29,000 towers primarily in Nigeria, but also Egypt, Cameroon, Zambia, Kuwait, Rwanda, and Brazil. [#I] - MTN's stated rationale: internalise tower lease margins, capture third-party revenue growth directly. [#A] - This reverses the disaggregation wave that created independent TowerCos in the first place. [#J] ⚡ RARE — first major MNO re-internalisation of TowerCo assets at scale in Africa. - Geographic overlap with HTWS: Nigeria and Egypt are IHS/MTN territories; HTWS has no presence in either. Direct competitive impact is limited. [#A] - Indirect impact: sets a precedent that MNOs may seek to recapture tower economics if TowerCo valuations drop; may chill future tower sale-leaseback deals with independent TowerCos. [#J]

Competitive market structure in HTWS core markets: - Tanzania and DRC: HTWS is dominant independent TowerCo. Limited ATC/IHS presence in these specific markets. [#A] - South Africa: more fragmented; Vodacom and MTN both have in-house tower assets; independent TowerCo sector less developed. [#A] - Ghana: IHS Towers has some presence; Ghana market more competitive. [#A]

Sceptical bullet: If the MTN/IHS deal closes and succeeds financially, it may incentivise other large African MNOs to consider similar re-internalisation of tower assets. Vodacom Africa and Orange Africa as theoretical follow-on actors. This is a slow-burn structural risk, not an immediate one. [#J]

§10 — Value chain — Supply side

J — Verdict: Energy is the structurally most important supply-side variable for HTWS — it represents the largest component of site opex. The transition from diesel to solar/hybrid is a genuine long-term cost reduction story, is accelerating, and has an escalator pass-through mechanism that reduces direct P&L exposure even before the transition. [#J]

Energy costs: - Historically, power represented ~54% of cost of sales for Helios Towers; diesel alone ~35%. [#I] - Energy accounts for up to 60% of operating costs for African telecoms towers in off-grid areas (industry average). [#I]

Power escalator pass-through: - Power cost increases are passed through to MNO tenants via quarterly or annual power escalators in MLA contracts. [#I] - This largely immunises HTWS from diesel/electricity price volatility at the EBITDA level. [#J]

Solar/renewable transition — Project 100: - HTWS invested $11m in grid, solar, and battery solutions in 2025 alone ($44m since 2022 under Project 100). [#I] - Africa broadly: towercos accelerating solar shift as diesel costs surge. [#I] - Long-term value: sites converted to solar have structurally lower opex, improving site-level ROIC. [#J]

Ground leases: - HTWS structures new leases with fixed costs or minimal escalation — standard towerco practice. [#A] - No evidence of material ground lease renegotiation or cost escalation L12-18M. [#I]

Sceptical bullet: The power escalator pass-through mechanism protects EBITDA but does not protect cash if MNO customers delay payments or dispute escalator charges. In markets with currency controls or MNO financial stress, receivables quality is a secondary risk. HTWS does not disclose days sales outstanding by market. [#A]
