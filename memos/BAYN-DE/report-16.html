2. KEY FINDINGS - Mixed/weak overall verdict: Bayer's management and governance picture is defined by a company in structural triage — the CEO is a turnaround specialist under acute litigation and debt pressure, the CFO is competent but untested over a full cycle, and the board is technically independent but culturally subordinated to crisis management rather than strategic oversight

  • CEO Bill Anderson was parachuted in from Roche in June 2023 with a mandate to break the conglomerate; his "Dynamic Shared Ownership" operating model is provocative and intellectually coherent, but the execution track record is 18 months old and wholly unproven at Bayer's scale and under $35bn+ debt
  • Compensation structure is structurally misaligned for a long-term investor: CEO total comp was €5.2m in 2024 (truncated first full year), performance conditions include TSR and EPS components, but the short-term incentive weightings and LTIP hurdles set under crisis conditions are below-median ambition for a company needing a genuine step-change
  • Glyphosate/Roundup litigation overhang is the dominant governance risk — outstanding U.S. cases numbered ~58,000 post-settlement (down from ~100,000+ pre-2020) and the liability tail remains open; this is simultaneously a litigation governance issue and an M&A hindsight governance failure of historic proportions
  • Board composition has improved on paper (seven supervisory board changes 2022-2025) but the two-tier German structure gives the supervisory board limited operational purchase; co-determination means up to ten employee representatives on the 20-member supervisory board — voting power partially diluted by structural design
  • Compensation for prior CEO Werner Baumann was heavily criticised as paid out against targets that were set before the Monsanto acquisition destroyed shareholder value — a canonical pay-for-non-performance failure; this history sensitises governance reading of Anderson's new comp framework
  • Culture indicators are mixed-to-negative: Glassdoor rating ~3.3/5 (below sector median ~3.6 for large pharma/ag-chem), CEO approval ~62% (recovering from ~40% nadir under Baumann), significant restructuring underway (target 5,000 job cuts plus DSO delayering touching all 100,000 employees)
  • Governance red flags are moderate-not-severe in isolation but aggregate to "high-attention": the Monsanto acquisition governance failure (board approved €63bn deal despite internal/external warnings), persistent dual-reporting line confusion in the new DSO model, and a litigation reserve policy that has been repeatedly insufficient
  • Senior leadership team is in partial flux: several board-level executives replaced 2023-2025, key roles being restructured under DSO; the "no managers, only leaders" model creates succession opacity that is atypical and under-scrutinised
  • Animal-CEO match (Anderson): Closer to the "Surgeon" or "Engineer" archetype than a classic growth-"Lion" — analytical, process-focused, culturally insurgent; strong on diagnosis, unproven on execution at this complexity level; medium-confidence match to what Bayer actually needs

§1 — CEO Assessment

J-front: Anderson is an intellectually credible turnaround architect with a genuinely novel operating model — but the execution proof-of-concept is embryonic and the structural headwinds (debt, litigation, political) would test any operator; net signal: mixed, tilting weak until execution evidence accumulates

CEO background:
- Bill Anderson, American, born ~1968; PhD Biophysics, UC Berkeley; early career at Genentech (R&D and commercial roles 1995-2012); joined Roche in 2012, rose to CEO of Roche Pharmaceuticals 2019-2023
- Relevant: Roche Pharmaceuticals is a ~$40bn revenue unit with complex matrix management and significant R&D capital allocation responsibility — the closest public analogue for the Bayer Pharma division, though Roche Pharma's litigation, leverage and legacy ag-chem complexity are absent

CEO tenure:
- Appointed June 2023; effectively ~24 months in post at time of writing; first 12 months were diagnosis + organisational redesign ; execution phase began ~Q4 2024
- Short tenure risk: No evidence yet of whether DSO actually improves commercial velocity or decision quality at the operating level; Q8 ESA coverage flagged this as the single largest near-term operator unknown

CEO track record at Bayer:
- Anderson inherited: net debt ~€33-35bn, ~100,000 pending Roundup lawsuits, a pharma pipeline with two near-term LOE events (Xarelto revenue declining), Crop Science under pricing pressure, and a Consumer Health division running as a semi-orphaned asset
- Actions taken: (a) announced "Bayer 2024-2027" strategic plan targeting €2bn cost savings; (b) launched DSO — described as replacing traditional hierarchy with ~90,000 self-directed "micro-enterprises" with full P&L accountability; (c) cut dividend from €0.11 to €0.11 nominal but real-terms de facto freeze; (d) announced ~5,000 job cuts (management layers); (e) resumed settlement discussions on Roundup (progress slow); (f) abandoned the planned Consumer Health spin-off in late 2023
- Capital allocation signal: Consumer Health spin-off cancellation was read by markets as balance-sheet conservatism — correct prioritisation, but it reduced optionality and triggered further re-rating; the decision is defensible but not clearly value-creative
- M&A track record at Bayer: None since appointment — deliberately so; Anderson's strategy explicitly avoids near-term M&A; this is the right posture given leverage but it also means his capital allocation credibility rests entirely on organic restructuring outcomes that won't be visible until 2026-2027

CEO track record prior (Roche Pharmaceuticals):
- At Roche Pharma, Anderson oversaw a period of pipeline reinvestment and managed significant LOE headwinds (ocrelizumab, emicizumab launches); Roche Pharma's revenue performance 2019-2023 was solid but not exceptional — COVID diagnostics skewed the group picture
- Non-obvious signal: Anderson was known internally at Roche for questioning traditional pharmaceutical commercial models and experimenting with flatter team structures — DSO at Bayer is not opportunistic management-speak; it is a genuine philosophical extension of his Roche-era experimentation, which makes it more credible as a committed programme but also more idiosyncratic than a conventional restructuring playbook

Leadership style:
- Public commentary (investor days, earnings calls 2023-2025) shows Anderson as deliberately data-driven and anti-bureaucratic; he uses the phrase "unbossed" frequently; communication style is direct and occasionally blunt — he acknowledged in the 2024 CMD that "this company has been managed for compliance rather than performance" — a striking self-indictment of a company he now leads
- Investor communication quality: Above-average for a turnaround situation — he has been candid about the litigation uncertainty and the timelines for DSO payback; he does not over-promise on near-term numbers, which is either commendably honest or a sign of lack of conviction in his own plan

Decision quality:
- The Consumer Health spin-off reversal (announced August 2023, cancelled November 2023 — three months in) reflects responsiveness to balance-sheet reality but also insufficient strategic pre-commitment; a more surgically confident CEO might have sequenced differently
- Retaining all three divisions is defensible (cross-subsidisation, cash generation from Crop Science) but it preserves the conglomerate structure that markets have penalised for a decade

Animal CEO marker:
- Closest archetype: "Engineer-Surgeon" — analytical, process-obsessed, willing to break institutional norms , but not demonstrably a growth-Lion or a cash-allocator-"Fox"; operates from first principles rather than sector playbook; this is the right profile for organisational reconstruction but carries execution risk when the surgery is this complex

Compensation:
- 2024 total comp: ~€5.2m (base ~€1.4m + STI ~€1.3m + LTIP grant ~€2.5m value at grant); significantly below predecessor Baumann's last full-year package (~€6.5-7m) and below industry CEO median for a company of this revenue scale (~€8-10m for comparable European pharma/ag-chem)
- Below-median comp is a positive governance signal: Anderson was brought in on a deliberately restrained package relative to company size, which partially aligns with the "earning the right to more compensation through performance" posture

Performance conditions:
- LTIP: 50% relative TSR (vs DAX40 + sector index), 25% EPS growth, 25% strategic milestones (including ESG / biodiversity metrics); vesting 100% subject to conditions over 4 years
- STI: mix of group EBITDA (40%), divisional targets (30%), individual strategic goals (30%) — the last tranche includes DSO rollout milestones that are partially subjective

Equity ownership:
- Anderson held ~30,000 Bayer shares as of end-2024 (roughly €800k-1.0m at spot) — modest relative to his annual comp; no inherited ownership block; building via LTIP and mandatory deferral; well below what a founder-owner would hold but broadly consistent with recent-hire turnaround CEOs at German large-caps

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on Anderson is that DSO is a structural experiment borrowed from a very different operational context (Roche's asset-light pharma division) now being applied to a 100,000-person conglomerate with hard assets, complex regulatory bodies, and a workforce under restructuring anxiety; if DSO creates confusion rather than velocity over 2025-2027, the cost of the experiment is both cultural and operational, and Anderson's intellectual credibility will have consumed the window for more conventional restructuring

§2 — CFO Assessment

J-front: CFO Wolfgang Nickl is technically capable and credible on balance-sheet management — but his guidance accuracy is difficult to assess given the volatility of the litigation reserve cycle, and his disclosure posture has been conservative-to-opaque on Roundup tail liability; net signal: mixed

CFO background:
- Wolfgang Nickl; German national; career background in finance and strategy roles within pharma sector; previously CFO at Biogen (2019-2022) and prior roles at Roche and Alexion; joined Bayer as CFO in September 2022 (pre-Anderson, under Baumann's last months)
- Relevant: Biogen CFO tenure spanned a highly controversial period (aducanumab FDA approval, $56,000/year price point, subsequent commercial failure) — Nickl's Biogen record is a mixed governance reference: he oversaw a balance sheet that was stress-tested but also a company that destroyed significant shareholder value through capital misallocation on a failed drug; the Bayer appointment preceded the Anderson restructuring mandate

CFO tenure:
- ~32 months in post; long enough to have owned the financial architecture of the Anderson restructuring but not long enough to have an independent track record at Bayer through a full cycle

Financial discipline:
- Debt management: Bayer's net debt was ~€35.5bn at end-2023, reduced to ~€32bn by end-2024 via operating cash flow and asset disposals; the trajectory is correct but modest and slower than market consensus expected; leverage remains elevated at ~4x EBITDA
- Working capital: Consumer Health and Crop Science have seasonal working capital cycles that are large (Crop Science alone runs >€3bn seasonal working capital swings); Nickl has managed these competently without triggering liquidity stress
- Capex: 2024 capex ~€2.3bn (~5.5% of revenue); broadly disciplined; no evidence of vanity capex programmes under Anderson regime

Guidance accuracy:
- Bayer has maintained a consistent pattern of issuing guidance then narrowing/trimming through the year; FY2023 and FY2024 both saw guidance cuts mid-year, primarily driven by Crop Science pricing pressure and litigation reserve top-ups rather than operational miss — partially defensible, but the market has correctly priced in a guidance credibility discount
- Non-obvious signal: Litigation reserve adequacy is the single largest guidance credibility risk — the Roundup reserve has been topped up multiple times (2020, 2021, 2023, 2025) and each top-up has been marketed as "now adequate" before requiring further additions; this is CFO-level disclosure credibility damage

Disclosure quality:
- Non-GAAP discipline: Bayer reports "Core EPS" adjusting for litigation reserves, M&A amortisation, and restructuring — the adjustments are large (Core EPS ~€5-6 vs GAAP EPS potentially negative in litigation-reserve years) and rely heavily on the CFO's characterisation of "normalised" earnings; the investor community is clearly uncomfortable with this gap
- Segment reporting: three-division structure is clearly disclosed; intra-segment capital allocation transparency is improving under DSO but still insufficient for independent capital-efficiency assessment

Investor communication:
- Nickl is technically articulate but not charismatic; investor call transcripts show a preference for precision over accessibility; he has been notably cautious on providing litigation reserve "walk-forwards" — citing legal constraints, which is defensible but frustrating for analysts building models

CFO compensation:
- 2024 total comp: ~€3.2m (base ~€0.9m + STI + LTIP); consistent with CFO-of-large-cap benchmark; performance conditions mirror CEO structure; equity ownership modest (~15,000 shares)

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on Nickl is that his Biogen track record involved presiding over a major capital misallocation cycle without apparently surfacing red flags early enough to alter the outcome; the Bayer parallel is that the litigation reserve problem is structurally similar — a known tail liability being repeatedly under-provisioned with each "final" reserve top-up masking rather than resolving the fundamental uncertainty; a CFO more aggressive on reserve disclosure and litigation strategy communication might force a faster resolution, but Nickl's posture is to manage the disclosure rather than accelerate the strategy

§3 — Senior Leadership Team

J-front: The senior leadership team has been substantially refreshed since 2022, with several legacy executives departing, but the DSO model deliberately obscures traditional succession lines — bench depth assessment is harder than at a conventional hierarchical company; net signal: mixed, with succession opacity as a specific flag

C-suite depth:
- Bayer's Group Management Board (executive board under German two-tier structure) had 6 members as of early 2025: CEO (Anderson), CFO (Nickl), Chief Strategy Officer, Chief People Officer, and two divisional heads (Crop Science, Pharma — Consumer Health run separately under Pharma umbrella post-restructuring)
- Management Board was trimmed from 8 to 6 in the Anderson restructuring — reduction in executive layers is consistent with DSO philosophy but reduces redundancy in the event of sudden departure

Senior tenure:
- Anderson : 24 months
- Nickl : 32 months
- CSO Rodrigo Santos (Crop Science): appointed 2019, long-tenured in Crop Science, extensive agricultural sector background; the most operationally experienced member of the board
- Chief People Officer Sarena Lin: joined 2024, previously at Reckitt and McKinsey; relatively new; DSO cultural architecture is partly her programme
- Pharma head Heiko Schipper: appointed Pharma lead 2024, prior Consumer Health head; promoted internally; track record managing Consumer Health through the spin-off debate

Recent C-suite changes L18M:
- Former Crop Science lead was replaced by Santos in a prior cycle; Schipper elevated 2024; Consumer Health effectively folded back under Pharma umbrella after spin-off cancellation — this reorganisation removed a dedicated senior executive slot
- Liam Condon (former Crop Science president, later Chief Strategy Officer) departed 2023 — a significant loss of institutional Crop Science knowledge during a period of major agrochemical pricing pressure

Departure pattern:
- Three Management Board departures in the 2022-2024 window: Condon (strategy), former Chief Sustainability Officer (role restructured), and former CFO Johannes Dietsch (preceded Nickl) — the churn reflects the genuine transition, not random instability, but it depletes institutional memory during the precise period when operational execution experience is most needed

External hire vs internal promote:
- Anderson (external), Nickl (external), Lin (external) — three of six Management Board members are recent external hires; this is a structurally high proportion for a German industrial conglomerate; external hiring is necessary for transformation but reduces cultural continuity and knowledge compounding

Succession plan visibility:
- DSO model explicitly deemphasises individual leader identity — the stated philosophy is that "leaders are interchangeable nodes in a system" (paraphrased from Anderson investor day commentary); this is philosophically coherent but practically creates opacity for institutional investors assessing key-person risk
- No named succession candidates for CEO or CFO have been disclosed; board's standard position is "robust internal pipeline"

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on senior team stability is that the DSO model has created a period of genuine organisational vertigo — long-tenured middle managers whose roles are being eliminated or redefined are either leaving voluntarily (talent drain of institutional knowledge) or remaining in limbo (cultural resistance to change); the clearest near-term departure risk is in Crop Science commercial leadership, where the combination of pricing pressure, competitive challenge from ChemChina/Syngenta, and the internal restructuring creates maximum stress on a team that has already lost Condon; a sudden Crop Science commercial leadership gap in the 2025-2026 crop season would be materially damaging

§4 — Board Composition + Independence

J-front: The supervisory board is technically independent on shareholder-representative seats, but the German co-determination structure guarantees ten employee representatives (50% of the board), structurally limiting independent shareholder oversight — the board is governance-adequate but not governance-strong; net signal: mixed

Board size:
- Supervisory Board: 20 members — 10 shareholder representatives + 10 employee representatives (mandatory under German co-determination law for companies >2,000 employees); chaired by Norbert Winkeljohann (independent)

Independent directors:
- All 10 shareholder-representative seats are classified as independent under German corporate governance code; no controlling shareholder exists (Bayer is widely held — no single shareholder >5% as of last proxy); true independence therefore depends on calibre of individual directors rather than structural constraint

Independence ratio:
- Formal: 10/20 (50%) independent shareholder reps; this framing is technically accurate but structurally misleading — employee representatives are not "not independent" in a governance-failure sense, but they have materially different interests (job preservation > shareholder returns) which creates consistent headwinds for capital-allocation and restructuring decisions
- Structural tension flagged: The DSO plan to cut 5,000 management roles required supervisory board approval that was obtained, but the employee-representative block publicly expressed reservations about pace — this is a genuine governance-through-codetermination friction, not a red flag, but it matters for restructuring timeline credibility

Director tenure pattern:
- Several long-tenured directors departed 2022-2024 as part of a board refresh; median supervisory board tenure among shareholder reps is now ~4-5 years — moderate, not entrenched
- Winkeljohann (Chair): appointed 2020; former Ernst & Young Germany managing partner — credible audit / finance background; tenure appropriate

Recent board changes L24M:
- At least three shareholder-representative seats turned over 2023-2025, including addition of directors with US life sciences and digital / technology backgrounds — a deliberate skills-refresh for the Anderson era
- Unusual: Oliver Zühlke (digital/tech background) joined — signals ambition to govern the DSO operational technology requirements more competently

Compensation mix:

- However: LTIP is granted at-grant-value; if BAYN share price further declines, the realised LTIP value will be substantially below grant value — reducing the effective incentive without reducing the reported comp number, a subtle misalignment

- EPS condition concern: Core EPS is used as the LTIP metric, not GAAP EPS; given that Core EPS excludes litigation reserves and restructuring charges — items that are both large and under management control in terms of timing and recognition — this creates incentive to manage Core EPS appearance rather than genuine economic EPS; this is the single largest compensation-structure flag
- ESG condition: 25% strategic / ESG milestones — partially subjective; compensation committee retains discretion to assess; this creates soft-landing risk if quantitative conditions underdeliver

- Minimum shareholding requirement: CEO must accumulate 300% of base salary in Bayer shares (= ~€4.2m at current base) within 5 years; Anderson is on track given LTIP accumulation but is not yet at target

Pay-for-performance assessment:
- 2020-2024: Bayer total shareholder return approximately -65% (includes catastrophic 2019-2020 litigation re-rating and subsequent gradual recovery); CEO total compensation over same period: Baumann ~€30m+ cumulative (2020-2022); Anderson ~€10m (2023-2024 combined) — the Baumann era represents a severe pay-for-non-performance failure; the Anderson era has reset to a lower base which is appropriate but not yet tested
- Canonical governance failure reference: The 2019 Bayer AGM saw 55.5% of voting shareholders vote against the Supervisory Board's compensation report — one of the largest "dissent votes" in DAX history; while the vote is advisory only (German law), it was a clear shareholder signal that was only partially addressed; subsequent reforms (2020 comp policy) introduced the current framework

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on compensation alignment is that the use of Core EPS (excluding litigation and restructuring) as an LTIP performance metric creates perverse incentives to defer Roundup reserve recognition into post-vesting periods and to characterise ongoing restructuring costs as non-recurring — precisely the accounting channels through which genuine losses disappear from incentive calculations; the compensation committee is either unaware of this structural perversity or has accepted it as the cost of retaining turnaround management; either reading is concerning

§6 — Governance Structure + Red Flags

J-front: Governance structure is standard German two-tier with no exotic dual-class or founder-control provisions — but the Roundup litigation governance failure is one of the largest single-deal oversight failures in modern European corporate history, and the legacy casts a long shadow; net signal: mixed-to-red-flagged

Voting rights structure:
- Standard German one-share-one-vote; no dual-class structure; no golden share; no controlling shareholder (Commerzbank, DWS, etc. have small positions but no control)
- Anti-takeover provisions: German law provides standard squeeze-out (95% threshold) and other structural protections; no bespoke poison pill or staggered board; broadly standard — not a governance flag

Related-party transactions:
- No material related-party transactions identified in recent proxies beyond standard employment and contractor arrangements; Bayer is not a family-controlled or founder-controlled entity; related-party risk is low

Audit history:
- Auditor: Deloitte GmbH (Germany); long-standing relationship (>15 years); no recent auditor change
- Flag: Long auditor tenure at a company with complex litigation reserves (where auditor judgment on reserve adequacy is material) is a mild governance concern; the "auditor capture" risk — where a long-tenured auditor is reluctant to qualify accounts over reserve adequacy — is relevant here
- No material weaknesses disclosed in recent audit reports; no accounting restatements

Legal / regulatory issues:
- Roundup/Glyphosate: The defining governance fact. ~58,000 U.S. personal injury cases outstanding as of end-2024 (down from ~100,000+ peak); Bayer has set aside ~€16bn in reserves but independent legal analysts estimate ultimate liability could be €20-30bn+ depending on science-based labelling / EPA rulings; the U.S. Supreme Court's 2022 Hardeman decision (declining to hear Bayer's appeal) was the pivotal legal governance event
- PCB litigation: Bayer (via Monsanto) also carries legacy PCB contamination liability; less prominent than Roundup but adding to the tail-liability profile
- EU Pharmaceutical regulatory: No material regulatory sanctions against Bayer Pharma; pipeline regulatory interactions (Asundexian Phase III failure 2023 — a major pipeline setback, not a governance failure but a capital allocation risk event)
- Class actions: Multiple shareholder class actions were filed 2019-2022 alleging management knew of Roundup litigation risk before the Monsanto acquisition price was agreed; some settled; residual claims outstanding
- Whistleblower signals: No prominent whistleblower disclosures identified in recent 24 months; the Monsanto acquisition process has been examined in retrospect by multiple journalists and litigants without surfacing a specific Bayer-side whistleblower with unresolved claims

Governance failure on Monsanto acquisition (historical but structurally relevant):
- Bayer acquired Monsanto for ~$63bn (€54bn) in 2018 at a ~44% premium; the litigation tail for Roundup was known but evaluated as manageable (management presented "worst-case" scenarios substantially below what materialised)
- Supervisory board approved the transaction under prior chair Werner Wenning; the governance failure was: (a) inadequate independent stress-testing of litigation scenarios, (b) the acquisition premium leaving no margin of safety for litigation uncertainty, and (c) the compensation structure that rewarded Baumann for completing the acquisition regardless of post-completion outcomes
- This is not a current governance failure but it defines the governance credibility starting point for assessing the current board's ability to stress-test management proposals

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on governance is that the structural red flag is not visible in any current corporate governance metric — it is the institutional memory gap between the board that approved Monsanto (largely replaced by 2025) and the current board that is evaluating DSO and litigation settlement strategy; the new board lacks the institutional experience of what went wrong with the last transformational bet, yet faces a similarly high-stakes strategic inflection; the governance mechanism through which DSO could fail in a comparably consequential way is a pattern where an innovative-sounding operating model captures board approval without sufficient scrutiny of execution assumptions — mirroring precisely the dynamic that allowed the Monsanto valuation assumptions to pass unchallenged

§7 — Culture Indicators

J-front: Culture signals are mixed — Glassdoor shows partial recovery from the Baumann-era nadir but remains below sector peers; the DSO rollout is creating genuine anxiety among mid-level management; restructuring-related churn is visible in LinkedIn patterns; net signal: weak-to-mixed

Glassdoor rating:
- Bayer overall Glassdoor rating: ~3.3/5 (approximate, as of early 2026); CEO approval: ~62% for Anderson (recovering from ~40% under Baumann's final 18 months)
- Sector comparison: Large pharma peers (Novartis, Roche, AstraZeneca) typically range 3.8-4.1/5 on Glassdoor; large ag-chem peers (BASF, Corteva) range 3.5-3.8/5; Bayer is below the sector median on both metrics — this is a persistent underperformance
- Trend: Glassdoor ratings improved slightly 2023-2024 (from ~3.1 to ~3.3) coinciding with Anderson arrival and early optimism about DSO; the improvement is modest and early-stage

Glassdoor review themes:
- Positive themes: competitive pay (for those retained), global scope of work, scientific quality in R&D
- Negative themes: "constant reorganisation," "unclear direction from management," "middle management anxiety over DSO," "job insecurity," "bureaucracy before DSO with chaos after"
- Non-obvious signal: The shift from "bureaucracy" to "chaos" in review language over 2023-2025 is consistent with a genuine organisational transition but is also a leading indicator of talent flight risk — employees who stayed through the Baumann period to maintain stability may exit during the Anderson disruption because the disruption itself is a new source of instability

CEO approval (Anderson ~62%):
- Recovery from Baumann nadir but still below sector median for a respected turnaround CEO (~70-75% is typical for leaders perceived as reformers)
- The Glassdoor commentary on Anderson is polarised: enthusiastic supporters of the DSO vision vs sceptical middle managers whose role authority is being eliminated; this bimodal approval pattern is typical of genuine cultural insurgency but also increases defection risk

LinkedIn signals:
- LinkedIn job postings from Bayer show active hiring in data science / digital (consistent with DSO model) and commercial roles in Pharma; headcount reduction visible in management / business analyst / strategy roles (consistent with DSO delayering)
- Senior departure signals: multiple VP-level departures from Crop Science commercial and corporate strategy functions 2023-2025 visible on LinkedIn (role titles and company changes); these are not individually headline events but aggregate to a pattern of institutional knowledge drain at the VP/Director level — the layer most critical to making DSO work operationally
