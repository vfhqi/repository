§2 — Quality of Earnings

Quality verdict: Earnings quality is structurally clean for a concession infrastructure business — high cash conversion, limited working capital complexity, and no evidence of recurring items dressed as one-offs; the primary complexity is concession amortisation accounting and interest cost trajectory [#J]

Cash conversion: OCF/EBITDA has been consistently >80% across L3Y — estimated at approximately 82-87% range based on AS-sourced cash flow data; this is in line with European infrastructure concession peers (base rate ~80-90% OCF/EBITDA) [#A]

  • OCF vs NI: Operating cash flow materially exceeds net income in every period due to substantial D&A add-back on the concession asset; this is expected and structurally normal for a long-duration tunnel concession [#A]
  • NWC complexity: Near-zero working capital cycle — Getlink collects cash at point of service (Le Shuttle ticketing, Eurostar access fees, freight charges) with minimal receivables lag; B2B freight contracts have short payment terms [#A]

One-off impact: Getlink's reported earnings are notably clean of large recurring exceptional charges [#J]

  • Recurring vs one-off: No evidence of restructuring charges reappearing across multiple periods; the ElecLink construction phase involved some capex-related interest capitalisation but this is disclosed and finite [#A]
  • Adjustments trend: The company's "adjusted" EPS (excluding concession amortisation — a non-cash charge) is the primary non-GAAP measure; this is a legitimate and sector-standard adjustment for concession businesses (comparable to AFFO in REITs); the gap between reported and adjusted EPS is stable and well-disclosed [#A]

Non-GAAP gap: Adjusted EPS runs approximately 40-50% above reported EPS throughout L3Y (e.g. FY2024: reported ~EUR 0.60 vs adjusted ~EUR 0.90); this gap is explained almost entirely by concession amortisation and is not widening, suggesting no creep in adjustments scope [#A]

D&A trend: Depreciation and amortisation is the largest single reconciling item between EBITDA and EBIT; the D&A line has been stable-to-modestly-rising as the ElecLink asset enters the depreciation schedule and tunnel maintenance CapEx flows through [#A]

  • D&A trajectory: No evidence of D&A being compressed to flatter EBITDA margins — the effective depreciation rate on the fixed asset base appears stable; EBIT margin broadly tracks EBITDA margin movement [#A]

Receivables turn: Not a material concern for a largely cash-at-point-of-sale business; trade receivables are small relative to revenue and turn rapidly; no deterioration visible in L3Y balance sheet data [#A]

CapEx treatment: Maintenance CapEx (~EUR 80-100m p.a. estimated) and expansion CapEx (ElecLink completion, tunnel infrastructure) are clearly separated in company disclosures; no evidence of maintenance CapEx being reclassified as growth to inflate FCF optics [#A]

  • ❌ Concession amortisation scale: The non-cash concession amortisation charge (~EUR 250-280m p.a.) is large relative to reported net income — it creates a structurally depressed reported EPS that may confuse investors anchoring to reported rather than adjusted earnings [Cross-ref: §3, §8]

Interest cost trajectory: Net finance costs have been declining as gross debt amortises on schedule; this is the primary driver of EPS growth ahead of EBITDA growth — a structural tailwind but finite [#A]

  • Debt structure: Getlink's project finance debt (Channel Tunnel bonds/RCF) amortises on a fixed schedule; each year of amortisation reduces interest expense and boosts EPS; this tailwind is transparent and quantifiable [#A]

Sceptical bullet: The bearish quality read centres on the concession amortisation treatment — while sector-standard, the gap between adjusted and reported EPS (~40-50%) means investors relying on adjusted metrics must trust that the underlying concession asset is genuinely not impaired; any revision to tunnel asset life, required maintenance CapEx uplift, or regulatory change to concession terms would directly challenge the "amortisation is non-economic" narrative [#J]

§3 — Revenue vs Profit Growth (Margin Sustainability)

Margin verdict: Margins are structurally anchored by the fixed-cost concession model — incremental revenue at high contribution margin; expansion potential is real but limited to +100-200bps from current ~65% EBITDA margin base [#J]

Revenue CAGR: FY2022-FY2024 revenue CAGR ~6.3%; note that revenue growth is predominantly volume-driven (traffic units × tariff) with modest pricing power embedded in regulated and commercial tariff structures [#A]

Profit CAGR: FY2022-FY2024 EBITDA CAGR ~7.5% — approximately +120bps above revenue CAGR, confirming modest but real operating leverage [#A]

  • EPS CAGR vs EBITDA CAGR: EPS (adjusted) CAGR ~9.5% vs EBITDA CAGR ~7.5% — the ~200bps differential is driven by interest cost reduction as project debt amortises [#A]
  • Operating leverage mechanism: Getlink's cost base is predominantly fixed (staffing, maintenance, debt service); incremental revenue from additional traffic units or tariff increases flows through at contribution margins >90%; this is the structural reason profit growth exceeds revenue growth [#A]

Gross margin: Not separately disclosed in Getlink's concession cost structure; revenue flows to EBITDA without a traditional COGS line — the relevant top-line quality metric is EBITDA margin directly [#I]

EBITDA margin trend: Expanded from ~63.3% (FY2022) to ~64.8% (FY2024) — modest +150bps over 2 years; consistent with fixed-cost leverage on modest volume growth [#A]

  • H1 2024 signal: H1 2024 EBITDA margin of ~65.1% is the highest observed in the L3Y window — a positive directional signal for FY2025 margin trajectory [#A]
  • Margin ceiling consideration: At ~65% EBITDA margin, Getlink is already at the high end of European transport concession peers (typical range ~55-68%); incremental expansion above ~67% would require either meaningful traffic acceleration or a step-down in fixed cost base — neither likely in the near term [#A]

EBIT margin: Broadly stable at ~35-36% across FY2022-FY2024; EBIT margin has not expanded meaningfully because D&A is rising alongside the asset base; this is technically neutral quality (D&A rising is expected as ElecLink depreciates) [#A]

Cost cut driver: Not a cost-cut story — Getlink's margin expansion is volume/revenue-driven, not headcount or efficiency-driven; no evidence of restructuring-driven margin improvement in L3Y data [#J]

Mix shift driver: ElecLink adds a different revenue mix (electricity capacity fees, less correlated to passenger/freight volumes) — positive mix shift toward contracted/regulated revenue streams, modest in quantum (~5% of total revenue estimated) [#A]

Sustainability assessment: Margin expansion sustainability is medium-high — the fixed-cost structure means any volume growth above ~3% p.a. generates operating leverage; the risk is that volumes plateau or decline, in which case fixed costs become a drag rather than a lever [#J]

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on margin sustainability is that FY2022-FY2024 benefited from a particularly benign Le Shuttle Freight environment — post-Brexit trade normalisation drove Freight volumes to record levels in FY2022-FY2023; if EU-UK trade dynamics deteriorate (regulatory divergence, political disruption), Freight EBITDA contribution could reverse sharply and the fixed cost base would then compress margins [#J]

§4 — Seasonal Patterns

Seasonality verdict: Pronounced and well-established H2 seasonality — summer leisure travel drives H2 > H1 for passenger revenues; Le Shuttle Freight is more evenly split but modestly H2-weighted; seasonality has been stable across L3Y [#J]

Q-level data limitation: Getlink does not publish quarterly P&L; operational traffic data is released quarterly but full financial results are semi-annual; the seasonality analysis is therefore H1/H2 rather than Q1-Q4 [#I]

H1 pattern: H1 revenues consistently represent approximately 47-48% of full-year revenues across FY2022-FY2024; H1 EBITDA similarly ~47-49% of full year — indicating mild H2 skew [#A]

  • Cause — travel pattern: Le Shuttle Passenger and Eurostar volumes peak in July-August (summer holiday) and December (Christmas/New Year); freight is more evenly distributed [#A]
  • H2 revenue share: H2 typically ~52-53% of full-year revenue — not extreme seasonality but meaningful enough to make H1 stand-alone results read conservatively vs full-year run-rate [#A]

Seasonality direction: Stable and predictable across L3Y — the H1/H2 split has not shifted materially, suggesting no structural change in customer demand patterns [#A]

Strongest period: H2 (particularly July-August traffic peak); Getlink's H2 is reliably the stronger period for passenger-driven revenue [#J]

Weakest period: H1 (January-March are the weakest traffic months for leisure travel); freight is more resilient in H1 [#A]

Seasonality changing: No evidence of seasonality becoming more or less pronounced across L3Y; post-COVID demand normalisation did not alter the fundamental seasonal structure [#A]

Cause — Brexit impact: The 2021-2022 post-Brexit normalisation modestly altered the freight seasonal pattern (border documentation requirements creating lumpy loading patterns) but this has since stabilised [#A]

  • Investor implication: H1 results will always appear weak relative to consensus full-year forecasts — this is a structural feature, not a signal; analysts covering Getlink are well aware and H1 consensus is appropriately calibrated [#A]

Sceptical bullet: The bearish seasonal read is that the stable H2-skew pattern could break if UK/European leisure travel faces a structural headwind (fuel prices, cost-of-living squeeze, Eurostar reliability issues); the FY2022-FY2024 period did not stress-test a sustained summer travel downturn, meaning the seasonal pattern is reliable in benign conditions but untested in stress [#J]
