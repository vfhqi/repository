§2 — Quality of Earnings

  • Quality verdict: Mixed quality — OCF conversion is reasonable but the persistent and growing gap between adjusted and reported EPS from PPA amortisation is the single most material quality concern in this earnings history [#J]

  • Adjustments trend: Adjusted EPS consistently ~45-60% higher than reported EPS across L3Y; the gap has widened in absolute terms as acquisitions accumulate; this is a structurally inflated adjusted earnings presentation [#J]

  • PPA amortisation: ~£2.5m FY22, ~£3.5m FY23, ~£4.0m FY24 — a growing real cash-economic drag disguised as a non-cash add-back [#A]

  • Recurring vs one-off: Management consistently classifies PPA amortisation and acquisition costs as "one-off" or "non-underlying"; however, for a serial acquirer, these charges recur with near-certainty each cycle and should be considered semi-permanent [#J]

  • Acquisition costs (legal, advisory, earn-out) have appeared in adjustments every year of L3Y — approximately £0.5-1.5m per year [#A]

  • ❌ Non-GAAP gap: Adjusted EPS overstates earnings power by an estimated 45-55% vs reported EPS consistently across L3Y — above the 20-30% gap that is typical for UK small-cap industrial acquirers [#J]

  • Cash conversion: Operating cash flow has broadly tracked adjusted EBITDA at ~75-85% conversion across L3Y — acceptable but not exceptional; UK small-cap industrials median is approximately 80-90% [#A]

  • FY24 OCF conversion slightly below FY23 on higher working capital tied to order book growth; not alarming in isolation [#A]

  • NWC tailwind/headwind: Working capital built in FY23-FY24 as order book grew (inventory and receivables increased); H1 FY25 destocking should release NWC, providing a cash tailwind even as revenue falls [#J]

  • Receivables turn and payables turn broadly stable across L3Y — no aggressive extension of receivables or compression of payables flagged [#A]

  • D&A trend: D&A increasing each year in absolute terms (driven by PPA amortisation on acquisitions + capital investment in manufacturing capacity); D&A as % of revenue broadly stable at ~3-4% [#A]

  • ❌ Reported EPS reliability: Reported EPS is the more conservative and arguably more honest earnings metric; using adjusted EPS for valuation without haircut for recurring PPA amortisation overstates earnings power [#J]

  • OCF vs NI: OCF has generally exceeded reported net income across L3Y — a positive quality signal; working capital movements account for the variance [#A]

  • FY23 and FY24 both showed OCF > reported NI — consistent with genuine cash earnings conversion [#A]

  • Conservative accounting: Inventory is not aggressively valued; no evidence of revenue recognition manipulation; contract assets are minimal given the product-distribution-dominant model [#A]

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on earnings quality is not that Solid State is cooking the books but that the adjusted EPS figure used by management and sell-side as the primary valuation anchor substantially overstates sustainable earnings power; a fair-value investor must apply a PPA amortisation haircut (approximately £3-4m per annum, or ~30-35p per share) that reduces the adjusted multiple significantly; and the acquisition pipeline, if it continues, will extend this drag indefinitely — meaning "adjusted" EPS never actually arrives as cash-available-to-equity.

§3 — Revenue vs Profit Growth (Margin Sustainability)

  • Sustainability verdict: Margin expansion FY22-FY24 was partly structural (MTSL mix enrichment toward higher-margin manufacturing) and partly cyclical (volume leverage at peak demand); H1 FY25 compression suggests the cyclical component is real and is now reversing [#J]

  • Revenue CAGR: ~21% headline FY22-FY24 (acquisition-inflated); organic estimated ~8-12% CAGR [#A]

  • Profit CAGR: EBITDA CAGR ~33% FY22-FY24, materially above revenue CAGR — apparent operating leverage [#J]

  • However, EBITDA growth partially reflects MTSL manufacturing margin (higher than pure distribution) blending in — mix shift, not same-store margin improvement [#A]

  • Operating leverage: At the peak (FY24) EBITDA margin hit ~10.9% vs ~9.0% in FY22 — +190bps expansion; this is meaningful but not exceptional for a value-adding distributor/manufacturer [#A]

  • UK small-cap industrial distributor median EBITDA margin is approximately 7-9%; Solid State is at the upper end of that range at cycle peak [#A]

  • Gross margin: Gross margin broadly stable-to-slightly-improving: ~27% FY22, ~28% FY23, ~29% FY24, ~27% H1 FY25 [#A]

  • Gross margin compression in H1 FY25 is consistent with lower volumes and adverse mix (distribution revenue mix rose relative to higher-margin manufacturing during the downturn) [#A]

  • Mix shift driver: MTSL brought manufacturing capability into a previously distribution-dominated group; this is the primary structural margin driver, not pricing power or volume scale per se [#J]

  • Cost cut driver: No material cost restructuring programmes L3Y; overhead base has grown with revenue; H1 FY25 management commentary flagged "cost actions underway" suggesting overhead leverage is now negative [#J]

  • Sustainability: Gross margin structural floor is approximately 26-28% based on L3Y data; EBITDA margin structural range is ~8-11% depending on volume cycle; the peak (~10.9%) required favourable volume and mix simultaneously [#J]

  • Scale driver: Solid State lacks the scale to extract meaningful volume rebates from suppliers; margin sustainability relies on maintaining product mix (high-value components) rather than procurement leverage [#A]

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on margin sustainability is that the ~190bps EBITDA margin expansion FY22-FY24 was majority cyclical (volume leverage at distributor cycle peak + favourable defence programme timing) rather than structural; H1 FY25's rapid compression back to ~8.2% — approximately the FY22 baseline — suggests the structural margin floor is closer to 8-9% than the 10-11% cycle-peak figure, and any forward model requiring >10% EBITDA margin as a base case is front-running a margin level that proved fragile.

§4 — Seasonal Patterns

  • Seasonality verdict: H2 (October–March) is consistently and materially stronger than H1 (April–September) for both revenue and profit; this is structural and well-established across the L3Y window [#J]

  • H2 pattern: H2 revenue is typically 52-58% of full-year revenue across L3Y; H2 EBITDA is typically 55-60% of full-year EBITDA — heavier profit skew than revenue skew [#A]

  • H2 FY23: ~£85m revenue vs H1 FY23 ~£75m; H2 FY24: ~£94m revenue vs H1 FY24 ~£85m — consistent pattern [#A]

  • H1 pattern: H1 is structurally the weaker half; H1 FY25 revenue ~£79m vs FY24H1 ~£85m represents both seasonal position and cyclical weakness compounding [#A]

  • Strongest quarter: Within H2, the January–March quarter (Q4 UK fiscal) is anecdotally the strongest for defence deliveries and year-end budget spending by industrial customers [#J]

  • Cause — buying pattern: Defence customer delivery timing (milestones, acceptance testing) clusters in H2; industrial distribution customers front-load H2 with year-end budget expenditure [#J]

  • Cause — M&A timing: No evidence of M&A-driven seasonal distortion within L3Y; MTSL was consolidated from December 2021 and is fully in-base by FY22 [#A]

  • Seasonality changing: No material change in seasonal pattern across L3Y; the H2 skew may become more pronounced if defence programme timing delays shift deliveries further into H2 — as appeared to be happening in H1 FY25 [#J]

  • Investor implication: H1 results (November release) should always be read with the H2 recovery expectation in mind; a weak H1 is not automatically bearish if order book and management commentary support H2 delivery [#J]

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on seasonal patterns is that management and sell-side consistently rationalise a weak H1 with "H2 loading" language — which was valid FY22-FY24 but failed to prevent a full-year miss in FY25 if H2 FY25 does not recover; the H1 FY25 miss (-7% revenue vs consensus) may therefore be signal, not noise, that the seasonal recovery framework is being used to defer bad news rather than genuinely forecast H2 strength.

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on management credibility is that four consecutive modest beats through FY22-FY24 may have reflected a deliberately managed "beat cadence" facilitated by a rising demand cycle that masked the underlying fragility of guidance; in a downcycle, conservative guidance becomes harder to maintain without explicit quantitative targets, and the H1 FY25 miss suggests the qualitative guidance language ("confident," "on track") carried no real informational precision — management may not have visibility substantially superior to the brokers they were supposedly nudging lower.

§8 — Forward Plausibility

  • Forward plausibility verdict: L3Y track record is conditionally supportive of recovery to prior-peak earnings but does NOT support a straight-line forward extrapolation; a bull case requires a visible defence order release + distributor restock cycle, neither of which is confirmed in H1 FY25 data [#J]

  • Track-record support: FY22-FY24 demonstrated Solid State can operate at ~10-11% EBITDA margins and ~130p+ adjusted EPS; the capability exists; the question is timing of cycle recovery [#J]

  • Required CAGR: For a 25% IRR from the current (cycle-trough) entry point, the required EPS CAGR depends on the entry price; at ~500-600p share price (approximate cycle-trough level), returning to ~133p EPS by FY26 would require roughly +50-60% EPS growth from the depressed H1 FY25 run-rate — plausible but demanding [#A]

  • Achievable from L3Y: The FY24 peak of ~133p adjusted EPS is the near-term recovery target; getting back to FY24 levels requires defence order release + distributor restock + no further deterioration in gross margin [#J]

  • Acceleration required: Beyond recovery, a genuine growth thesis requires earnings above FY24 peak; this would require either further acquisitions (consistent with management strategy) or organic growth above the ~8-12% organic CAGR delivered pre-cycle [#J]

  • No evidence in L3Y data of an organic growth rate sustainably above 15% that would support acceleration to new highs without M&A [#A]

  • Margin runway: Meaningful EBITDA margin runway above 10.9% is uncertain; the structural MTSL manufacturing contribution has already been largely captured; further margin expansion would require either pricing power (not demonstrated) or scale from acquisition [#J]

  • Quality runway: Cash conversion has been acceptable at ~75-85% of adjusted EBITDA; no obvious deterioration; NWC release in FY25 downturn is a near-term cash tailwind [#J]
