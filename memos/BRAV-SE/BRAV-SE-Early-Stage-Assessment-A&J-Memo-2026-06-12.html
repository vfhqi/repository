B.1 Business Description — Numbered Findings
Business Description -- A plain-English description of what the company does, how it makes money, and its primary market.

1. Bravida Holding AB (BRAV-SE) is the leading Nordic technical installation and service group, providing electrical, HVAC (heating, ventilation, air-conditioning), and security disciplines across new builds, renovations, and ongoing building maintenance. FY2025 revenue was SEK 29.7bn with approximately 13,000 employees across 206 locations in Sweden (48% of revenue), Denmark (25%), Norway (19%), and Finland (8%).

2. Revenue is structurally bifurcated between installation (approximately 52% — project contracts for new builds and renovations) and service (approximately 47-49% — ongoing maintenance and technical management), with the service segment generating higher and more stable EBITA margins. Bravida has 84,000 clients with a 94% recurring customer retention rate, providing revenue visibility rare among Nordic construction-adjacent businesses.

3. The company operates a highly decentralised model: approximately 160 independently operating local entities run profit-and-loss autonomy at the regional level. Group management coordinates M&A, balance sheet, pricing benchmarks, and scheduling systems; but invoicing authority — the operationally and governance-sensitive function — remains at the local unit level. Expert insight obtained in the Leadership Foundations report confirms this structure has not changed despite the Jan 2026 Swedish consolidation.

4. Under CEO Mattias Johansson (17 years at Bravida, 100% industry focus), the company has completed approximately 160 acquisitions since 2014, growing from SEK 12bn revenue at IPO (2015) to SEK 29.6bn at year-end 2024. M&A cadence slowed in FY2025 (SEK 244m spend vs SEK 540m in 2024) as management prioritised organic improvement and restructuring over acquisition-led growth.

5. The FY2025 EBITA margin of 5.9% sits below the company's mid-term group target of >7%. The gap (approximately 110bps) is the primary financial case thesis: if Bravida reaches >7% from 5.9%, the EPS step-up is material (~25% on current base), and the multiple should re-rate from 14.7x toward sector-justified levels. The case is a margin recovery and cash conversion normalisation story, anchored by confirmed corporate restructuring delivery.

6. Near-term financial dynamics: Q1 2026 EBITA was SEK 325m (+6% YoY) at 4.6% margin; underlying margin improved +40bps ex the SEK 60m restructuring charge. Order intake was SEK 8.2bn (+5% YoY) and backlog SEK 16.7bn (+7% YoY), providing growing revenue visibility for H2 2026 and 2027. Cash conversion LTM Q1 2026 stands at 77%, still well below the 99%+ historical average — the deviation is unexplained in management communications and is a key monitoring variable.

7. The Norwegian receivables dispute is the primary binary near-term catalyst. Bravida has SEK 1.2bn in disputed receivables across Denmark and Norway. The Norwegian court ruling is expected in June 2026 — within days to weeks of this memo's completion. A positive ruling (Watson probability 25%) releases SEK 400-550m and drives +13-18% EPS and P/E re-rating toward 17-18x (implied price: SEK 130+). A full write-down (Watson probability 25%) destroys SEK 600-800m and cuts EPS by 33-40% (implied price: SEK 80-90). The base case (50%) is SEK 200-300m partial recovery — neutral to mildly negative on EPS, no re-rating. The asymmetry of magnitudes (bear > bull in EPS impact) has position sizing implications.

8. Governance damage is structural and unresolved. Region Skåne and Örnsköldsvik municipality have filed police reports against Bravida for alleged over-invoicing. The investigations are active as of June 2026. These reports reflect the structural risk in the decentralised model — local invoicing authority without corresponding audit controls. Management has not publicly addressed the structural corrective action, and the J&A expert confirms the model has not changed post-consolidation. This is an ongoing overhang that would, in isolation, reduce the core philosophy rating at Deep Dive.

1.
The Nordic data-centre investment cycle is the most powerful external tailwind: hyperscalers (Microsoft, Google, Meta, Amazon) have all announced material Scandinavian capacity expansions for 2025-2028, driven by cheap renewable electricity (Sweden hydroelectric, Norway offshore wind) and favourable land/cooling conditions. Bravida's electrical and cooling installation capability is directly required: data centres are among the most installation-intensive building types per square metre, and the scale of the planned capacity requires precisely the large-project electrical contracting in which Bravida specialises.
2.
Nordic energy efficiency legislation creates a multi-year retrofit demand cycle that is independent of the new-construction market. Sweden's transposition of the EU Energy Efficiency Directive (EED) mandates 20-25% reductions in commercial and public building energy consumption by 2030. This is a legally binding programme driving HVAC upgrades (heat pumps, intelligent ventilation), electrical optimisation (LED, smart metering), and renewable integration (rooftop solar): all within Bravida's licensed capability set.
3.
The EV charging infrastructure mandate is an underappreciated near-term demand driver. Sweden requires charging readiness in all new and renovated commercial buildings effective 2025; Norway has equivalent requirements from 2026. Bravida's electrical division captures the vast majority of this work: EV charging infrastructure is fundamentally a low-voltage electrical installation job, requiring the same technician skills and accreditations as existing Bravida work.
4.
Infrastructure Sweden (Infrastrukturpropositionen) commits SEK 900bn+ in public infrastructure investment through 2033 with a substantial buildings component (hospitals, schools, prisons, defence facilities). Technical installation services typically represent 15-25% of total building investment, implying a SEK 135-225bn addressable opportunity over the programme period. Bravida is the preferred supplier to several major Swedish government agencies and has reference contracts on the Karolinska hospital expansion.
5.
Nordic building stock is significantly pre-1980 vintage, requiring mandatory technical system upgrades due to updated electrical safety codes, fire safety regulations, and accessibility requirements. This is a permanent, regulation-driven demand component that is not correlated with construction cycles or GDP growth: it exists regardless of economic conditions and creates a revenue floor for Bravida's service and renovation operations.
6.
The post-COVID office renovation cycle in Stockholm, Oslo, and Copenhagen is a medium-term demand wave. Office tenants renegotiating leases post-COVID are requiring landlords to upgrade HVAC (indoor air quality, hybrid-work-compatible zoning), lighting (circadian rhythm, energy efficiency), security, and IT infrastructure. Bravida's multi-discipline capability (electrical + HVAC + security) makes it the preferred turnkey partner for landlord upgrade programmes.
7.
Nordic public hospital investment is an underappreciated long-cycle tailwind: Sweden has the OUS Aker hospital project and the Karolinska Phase 2; Norway has multiple regional hospital expansions; Finland has the Tampere hospital project. Hospital construction is among the most technically complex and margin-generative installation environments: specialist mechanical, electrical, and medical gas systems command significant pricing premiums. Bravida has growing reference credentials in this segment.
8.
Nordic residential heat-pump mandate is a partial tailwind: more relevant to smaller HVAC specialists than Bravida's scale, but the company's 206-location footprint means it participates in the residential service and maintenance market. The mandate-driven conversion of oil and district heating to heat pumps (Norway targeting 90% of residential heat from low-carbon sources by 2035) creates recurring service demand for installation, commissioning, and annual maintenance contracts.

E1 RA2 CQ1 — Absence of External Headwinds? C 8 findings
Required Input Change Forces -- We are looking for credible external tailwinds (regulatory, structural, or cyclical) AND active internal change programmes (turnaround, restructuring, re-acceleration); both must be real and sufficiently strong. Avoiding cases where change is incremental or forced.
1.
Nordic residential construction is in a multi-year downturn that directly impacts Bravida's installation revenue. Sweden housing starts fell approximately 50% between 2022 and 2025 and remain at trough levels. Southern Sweden organic growth of −19% in FY2025 was the most acute expression of this headwind: this is not a temporary disruption but a multi-year structural reset driven by elevated financing costs and planning system delays. The headwind is beginning to moderate (interest rates declining, government support schemes announced) but is not resolved.
2.
FX translation risk is active and unhedged. Bravida reports in SEK but generates 19% of revenue in NOK and 25% in DKK. The company does not hedge translation exposure. The CF report confirms that a 10% SEK/NOK move generates SEK 176m equity impact: approximately 8% of the current EBITA base. The SEK has appreciated against NOK in H1 2025, creating a genuine headwind that does not reflect underlying operational performance. Translation FX is an external headwind that management cannot control.
3.
Finland remains in structural weakness. EBITA margin fell to 0.6% in Q3 2025: near breakeven: and has recovered only partially. Finland's construction market is the most depressed in the Nordic region (GDP growth below Scandinavia, construction permits at multi-decade lows). Bravida's Finnish operations are 8% of revenue but absorb disproportionate management attention and drag on group margin. No clear catalyst for rapid Finnish recovery is visible within the memo's horizon.
4.
Labour cost inflation: skilled electrical and HVAC technician wages have increased approximately 20-30% cumulatively since 2021 across Scandinavia, driven by tight labour markets and skilled-trade shortages. Fixed-price contracts signed before 2023 were materially loss-making on labour cost grounds: the primary driver of the Danish and Southern Swedish margin collapses. While the repricing programme is substantially complete, wage inflation continues above historical norms, creating ongoing cost pressure on new fixed-price bids.
5.
Competitive pricing pressure from procurement platforms: Nordic municipalities and large corporate clients are increasingly using digital procurement platforms (e-auctions, framework agreements with mandatory lowest-cost awards) that commoditise project bids and eliminate pricing power. The more volume Bravida does through these platforms, the more its project margins are capped at commodity levels. This is a secular headwind against the thesis that the company can sustainably improve project margins.
6.
Working capital pressure: cash conversion dropped from 105% (2024) to 79% (2025) to 77% LTM Q1 2026. The magnitude and duration of this deterioration is the most concerning unexplained financial metric in the case. Possible causes: receivables dispute accrual (revenue booked, cash uncollected), project working capital build in large data-centre contracts (materials pre-purchased ahead of installation), or structural deterioration in invoice collection practice. None of these has been explicitly acknowledged by management in public communications. If the deterioration is structural, it permanently impairs the FCF quality of the business.
7.
Interest rate exposure: 100% of Bravida's financial debt is variable rate. The company is not operationally interest-rate sensitive (short-duration installation contracts pass cost through), but the balance sheet interest cost tracks benchmark rates. With SEK ~4-5bn of debt (net debt/EBITDA 1.1x at ~SEK 2.6bn EBITDA), a 100bp increase in benchmark rates increases interest cost by approximately SEK 40-50m, or approximately 2-3% of EBITA. As benchmark rates have stabilised and begun declining, this headwind is becoming a modest tailwind.
8.
Norwegian receivables binary: the uncertainty surrounding SEK 1.2bn in disputed receivables (June 2026 ruling expected) is itself an ongoing headwind on management attention, working capital reporting, and investor confidence. Even if the base case (partial recovery) materialises, the distraction cost and the working capital noise in the reported financials is a real external headwind to operational focus and investor communication clarity.
