§2 — Key Driver #1: Channel Tunnel Volume Recovery (Eurostar + Freight)

J-front verdict: High-plausibility, high-impact driver — volumes are structurally recovering and the fixed-cost base means EBITDA leverage to incremental passengers and trucks is very high [#J]

Driver definition: Eurostar passenger services and Le Shuttle truck/freight volumes are the primary revenue streams through the Channel Tunnel concession; given ~80% fixed cost base, each marginal tonne or passenger falls through to EBITDA at very high conversion [#I]

Mechanism to EPS: Higher passenger and freight volumes → higher concession revenue → near-linear EBITDA uplift (given fixed cost leverage) → EPS accretion; volume is the dominant operating lever [#I]

Mechanism to SP: Volume recovery is directly visible in consensus EBITDA upgrades and provides the foundation for leverage reduction, re-rating, and dividend resumption — multiple price catalysts linked to one driver [#J]

Company guide: Management has consistently guided for volume recovery to, and then through, pre-COVID (2019) baseline; Eurostar is the primary swing factor given its near-full recovery dependency on leisure and business travel [#A]

Management commentary: Getlink management has highlighted sustained recovery in Le Shuttle and HGV (truck) volumes; Eurostar's financial recovery has been slower given its own balance sheet restructuring post-COVID [#I]

Track record: Le Shuttle and HGV volumes recovered faster than Eurostar; by 2023-24, freight volumes had largely normalised; passenger recovery lagged by 18-24 months given Eurostar's own operational restructuring [#I]

Conservatism vs optimism: Management guidance on volume recovery has been broadly conservative — actual delivery has met or modestly exceeded guidance in recent periods; no pattern of sandbagging but also no aggressive over-promising [#J]

SS consensus: Sell-side consensus embeds continued volume growth toward and past 2019 levels; most brokers model Eurostar passenger volumes reaching 90-95% of 2019 by 2025-26 and freight volumes already normalised [#I]

Consensus changes L12M: Broadly stable; no material upgrade or downgrade cycle on volumes specifically; consensus has been more volatile on NeuConnect than on the core tunnel business [#I]

Estimated EPS impact: A 10% upside vs consensus volume assumption likely translates to 8-12% EBITDA uplift given fixed-cost leverage — meaningful EPS accretion given current leverage [#I]

Recent delivery: H2 2024 and early 2025 reporting showed continued volume recovery; HGV and truck volumes tracking well; Eurostar passenger growth positive but capacity-constrained by rolling-stock availability [#J]

  • Eurostar's fleet limitations (delayed new train deliveries) are a capacity ceiling that partially suppresses the volume upside in the near term [#A]

Peer benchmark: No cohort peer data available (D-BATCH-25 gap); comparable infrastructure concessions (toll roads, airport operators) in Europe typically exhibit 70-80% operating leverage to volume — consistent with GET-FR's observed EBITDA sensitivity [#I]

L3Y baseline: Post-COVID volume recovery has been the defining narrative since 2022; the recovery trajectory has been sustained but uneven (freight faster than passengers) [#I]

Macro tailwind: UK-France leisure travel is structurally growing; short-break tourism and cross-Channel freight benefit from structural EU-UK trade normalisation; no signs of structural demand destruction [#J]

Macro headwind: UK consumer confidence and disposable income affect leisure travel demand; a UK recession scenario would suppress Eurostar volumes; HGV volumes sensitive to UK-EU trade volumes [#A]

Visibility: High visibility on freight volumes (contracted and structural); medium visibility on Eurostar passengers (discretionary leisure + business); capacity ceiling from rolling-stock availability partially caps upside [#I]

Competitive dynamics: No direct infrastructure substitute for cross-Channel passenger and freight — ferry operators and air carriers are the competitive set but the tunnel's speed/convenience advantage is durable; competitive intensity is not a near-term threat [#J]

Bear case: Eurostar rolling-stock delays or a UK demand recession could hold passenger volumes flat vs 2024 for 2-3 years, suppressing the volume growth that consensus has embedded; freight volumes could slow if UK-EU trade normalization stalls [#A]

Miss scenario: Eurostar passenger volumes flat at 90% of 2019 rather than recovering to 100%+ — would cause EBITDA to miss consensus by an estimated 5-8% and remove the primary upgrade catalyst for 12-18 months [#A]

Miss magnitude: A sustained 10% volume undershoot vs consensus likely causes 10-15% EPS downside and could trigger multiple compression on an infrastructure name that re-rates on earnings trajectory visibility [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bear case is not a collapse — it is a "slow recovery" scenario where the EBITDA trajectory flattens and the investment case becomes a bond-proxy rather than a growth compounder; the risk is opportunity cost, not impairment [#J]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — volume recovery is structurally underway, freight normalised, Eurostar recovering; the key assumption is that Eurostar rolling-stock capacity resolves by 2026 [#J]

§3 — Key Driver #2: NeuConnect Interconnector (Construction Progress, Financing, Optionality)

J-front verdict: The highest-impact but lowest-plausibility near-term driver — NeuConnect is the primary NAV upside lever but timeline risk is high and the capex/financing overhang is the single largest balance-sheet uncertainty [#J]

Driver definition: NeuConnect is a planned UK-Germany electricity interconnector (~700MW capacity) in which Getlink holds a significant stake; successful completion would add a material regulated/contracted revenue stream and expand GET-FR's infrastructure asset base [#I]

Mechanism to EPS: NeuConnect completion → contracted interconnector revenues → new EBITDA stream → EPS accretion; the project also diversifies revenue away from pure Channel Tunnel concession dependence [#I]

Mechanism to SP: NeuConnect represents the primary source of NAV premium above the existing concession value; positive construction milestones, financing confirmations, or regulatory approvals would be direct share price catalysts [#J]

  • NeuConnect completion would also re-rate GET-FR from "single-asset concession" to "multi-asset energy infrastructure" — a potentially significant multiple expansion event [#A]

Company guide: Getlink has been explicit that NeuConnect is a strategic priority; the company has communicated a long development timeline consistent with large energy infrastructure projects; no specific completion date widely confirmed in public filings reviewed [#I]

Management commentary: Management has presented NeuConnect as a value-creation vehicle and highlighted the UK-Germany power price differential as the commercial rationale; they have been less explicit on financing structure and capex quantum in public communications [#A]

  • Opacity on capex and financing terms is a yellow flag at Triaging stage — ESA must extract specific capex guidance and financing structure [#A]

Track record: ElecLink (UK-France interconnector), which Getlink completed and operationalised, provides a precedent for execution capability; ElecLink has been commercially operating and contributing revenues — a positive read-across for NeuConnect [#J]

  • ElecLink delivered and is generating revenues; this is the most important piece of positive evidence on NeuConnect execution credibility [#I]

Conservatism vs optimism: Management has been optimistic on NeuConnect timeline relative to the complexity of a cross-North-Sea cable project; large interconnector projects in Europe have historically run 20-40% over schedule [#A]

SS consensus: Sell-side is divided — bulls embed NeuConnect NAV in price targets; bears apply a haircut or exclude it entirely pending financing confirmation; the spread between bull and bear price targets is largely explained by NeuConnect NAV assumptions [#I]

Consensus changes L12M: No clear directional revision trend on NeuConnect specifically; uncertainty has persisted without resolution — extended uncertainty itself is a mild negative signal [#A]

Estimated NAV impact: NeuConnect, if successfully completed and financed, likely adds 15-25% to assessed NAV based on comparable interconnector valuations (ElecLink precedent, National Grid Viking interconnector comps); the range is wide given financing uncertainty [#I]

Estimated EPS impact: Operating contributions from NeuConnect likely 3-5 years out; near-term EPS impact is negative (capex drag, potential equity dilution or debt issuance) — NeuConnect is an NAV story, not a near-term EPS story [#A]

Recent delivery: No major NeuConnect milestone announced in the L6M public record reviewed; absence of newsflow is ambiguous — could reflect project proceeding on plan without headline events, or could reflect delays [#I]

L3Y baseline: NeuConnect has been in development for several years; the project has not yet broken ground on full construction as of available public data; regulatory approvals and financing are pre-conditions [#I]

Macro tailwind: UK-Germany power price differential remains significant; energy security post-Ukraine war has elevated political appetite for interconnector capacity; both UK and German governments have strategic interest in cross-North-Sea cables [#J]

Macro headwind: Rising interest rates have increased the cost of long-duration infrastructure financing; project IRRs are compressed vs the low-rate environment in which NeuConnect was originally underwritten [#A]

  • Higher financing costs may require renegotiation of equity returns or government subsidy — a meaningful execution risk [#A]

Visibility: Low near-term visibility — no confirmed financial close, no confirmed construction start date in public data; this is the single most opaque driver in the GET-FR investment case [#A]

Competitive dynamics: Multiple interconnector projects are competing for North Sea cable capacity and government support; NeuConnect is not the only project targeting UK-Germany connectivity [#I]

Bear case: NeuConnect is delayed by 3-5 years due to financing constraints or regulatory complexity; capex drag on the balance sheet persists without revenue offset; NAV premium unwinds and GET-FR re-rates to a pure concession valuation [#A]

  • In an extended delay scenario, NeuConnect transitions from an asset to a liability — capex committed but revenues deferred [#A]

Miss scenario: Financial close not achieved by end-2026 — would cause bull-case analysts to cut price targets by 10-20%, compress the NAV premium, and remove the primary re-rating catalyst [#A]

Miss magnitude: Failure or multi-year delay would likely cause 15-25% share price downside from bull-case estimates if NeuConnect NAV is currently embedded in consensus [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The ElecLink precedent is the strongest bull argument, but ElecLink was a much shorter cable (UK-France) with simpler regulatory jurisdiction vs the UK-Germany offshore project; the complexity scaling is non-linear [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Low plausibility for near-term (24M) milestone delivery — financial close and construction start within 24 months requires financing markets and regulatory approvals to align; medium plausibility for longer-term (3-5Y) delivery based on ElecLink precedent [#J]

§4 — Key Driver #3: Leverage Reduction and Balance Sheet Normalisation

J-front verdict: Medium-plausibility re-rating catalyst — leverage reduction is underway but pace is modest; de-leveraging below 4x net debt/EBITDA would unlock a material valuation re-rating for an infrastructure name [#J]

Driver definition: Getlink carries significant legacy project-finance debt from the Channel Tunnel construction; as EBITDA recovers and cash generation improves, net debt/EBITDA is declining; crossing key leverage thresholds unlocks dividend capacity and multiple re-rating [#I]

Mechanism to EPS: Lower gross debt → lower interest expense → higher EPS; leverage reduction also reduces refinancing risk premium embedded in valuation [#I]

Mechanism to SP: Infrastructure assets typically re-rate as leverage normalises toward sector peers (4-5x net debt/EBITDA); a move from ~6x toward 4-5x could drive 15-25% multiple expansion independent of EBITDA growth [#J]

Company guide: Management has consistently cited leverage reduction as a medium-term priority; they have highlighted the debt maturity profile and target leverage corridors; no specific year-end target widely confirmed in public materials reviewed [#I]

Management commentary: Management has communicated that free cash flow generation supports organic de-leveraging; they have signalled that dividend reinstatement or growth is contingent on leverage normalisation [#I]

Track record: Leverage has been declining as EBITDA recovered post-COVID; the trajectory is in the right direction but the pace has been gradual given the scale of legacy project-finance debt [#I]

Conservatism vs optimism: Management guidance on leverage trajectory has been broadly met; no instances of leverage guidance being missed materially in L3Y; EBITDA recovery has been the primary driver as expected [#J]

SS consensus: Sell-side broadly expects continued de-leveraging; consensus models net debt/EBITDA declining toward 4-5x by 2026-27; key sensitivity is EBITDA trajectory (Driver #1) and NeuConnect capex drag (Driver #2) [#I]

Consensus changes L12M: Stable to slightly positive — volume recovery has underpinned gradual EBITDA upgrades which flow through to improved leverage trajectory [#I]

Estimated EPS impact: Interest cost reduction from de-leveraging by 1x net debt/EBITDA likely translates to 5-10% EPS uplift depending on debt maturity and refinancing terms; multiple re-rating impact is larger [#I]

Peer benchmark: No cohort peer data (D-BATCH-25 gap); comparable European infrastructure concessions typically trade at 4-5x net debt/EBITDA at steady state; GET-FR's premium to that range reflects historical project-finance legacy [#I]

Recent delivery: Leverage has declined year-on-year in recent reporting periods; FCF generation positive and directed at debt reduction; no adverse refinancing events noted [#J]

Macro tailwind: Stable-to-declining European interest rates (ECB cutting cycle 2024-25) reduce refinancing risk and potentially lower future interest expense [#J]

Macro headwind: NeuConnect capex could pause or reverse de-leveraging if funded via balance sheet debt; this is the primary conflict between Driver #2 and Driver #3 [#A]

Visibility: Medium-high visibility — debt maturity schedule is known, EBITDA trajectory is visible; primary uncertainty is NeuConnect capex quantum and timing [#I]

Bear case: NeuConnect capex is debt-funded at the Getlink holdco level, pausing or reversing de-leveraging; combined with a flat volume environment, leverage stays above 5.5x for 3+ years and re-rating does not materialise [#A]

Miss scenario: NeuConnect capex announced in late 2026 at scale, financed via new holdco debt — leverage trajectory inverts and the multiple re-rating story is deferred 2-3 years [#A]

Miss magnitude: Leverage plateau or reversal likely causes 10-15% valuation de-rating vs current infrastructure peer multiples [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The leverage story is implicitly dependent on NeuConnect not consuming the FCF; the two biggest drivers are therefore correlated in an adverse way — de-leveraging and NeuConnect investment are in tension [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — trajectory is right but NeuConnect capex introduces a binary fork in the leverage path; ESA must model both scenarios explicitly [#J]

§5 — Key Driver #4: Regulated Pricing and Concession Economics

J-front verdict: High-plausibility, low-volatility structural floor — the concession framework provides pricing power with inflation linkage; this driver is largely in management's control and not dependent on external variables [#J]

Driver definition: The Channel Tunnel operates under a concession framework that includes regulated or quasi-regulated pricing mechanisms; Le Shuttle and HGV tariffs have inflation linkage embedded in contracts; this provides a revenue floor independent of volume variability [#I]

Mechanism to EPS: Inflation-linked tariff escalation → revenue per unit increases regardless of volume → EBITDA floor preserved; the mechanism is automatic and does not require management action [#I]

Mechanism to SP: Pricing certainty reduces downside EBITDA risk; infrastructure investors ascribe a premium to earnings visibility; the concession framework is the primary reason GET-FR is a defensible investment in a recession [#J]

Company guide: Management consistently highlights the inflation-linked revenue characteristics of the business; no specific tariff guidance issued publicly but the mechanism is structurally documented [#I]

Management commentary: Getlink management presents the concession economics as a key differentiator vs pure transport operators; they highlight that even in weak volume years, revenue per unit escalates [#I]

Track record: Tariff escalation has been consistent with inflation over L5Y; no instances of regulatory override or forced tariff reduction noted in public materials reviewed [#I]

Conservatism vs optimism: This driver is definitionally conservative — it is mechanistic, not aspirational; the risk is regulatory change, not management execution [#J]

SS consensus: Sell-side consensus treats concession pricing as a given; it forms the baseline revenue assumption in all models; no material divergence across brokers on this driver [#I]

Estimated revenue impact: Inflation-linked escalation on a ~€1.5bn revenue base at 3-4% CPI implies €45-60m annual revenue uplift per year from pricing alone; this is independent of volume growth [#I]

Recent delivery: Tariff escalation functioning as designed; no adverse regulatory developments noted; ElecLink revenues adding incrementally to the earnings base [#J]

Macro tailwind: Elevated European inflation (2022-2025 cycle) has been a near-term positive for inflation-linked concessions; disinflation from 2025 onward reduces the annual tailwind from this driver [#I]

Macro headwind: If European inflation drops below 2%, the tariff escalation benefit shrinks toward minimal; this is a moderating headwind but not a reversal [#A]

Visibility: Very high visibility — this is a contractual mechanism; the primary risk is regulatory regime change at the UK-France concession level, which is a low-probability, high-impact tail risk [#I]

Bear case: A fundamental renegotiation of the Channel Tunnel concession framework — triggered by UK-France political pressure or regulatory review — could strip the inflation-linkage mechanism; this is a low-probability but high-impact scenario [#A]

Miss scenario: Disinflation to sub-2% CPI reduces annual tariff uplift from ~4% to ~1.5%; not a miss per se, but a meaningful tailwind reduction vs recent years [#A]

Miss magnitude: Disinflation scenario reduces annual revenue uplift by ~€20-30m; not a material EPS driver in isolation but removes a tailwind that consensus has implicitly embedded [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The concession framework's stability is contingent on UK-France bilateral relations; post-Brexit friction has been a low-level but persistent source of regulatory uncertainty that ESA should probe [#J]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — the pricing mechanism is contractual and has functioned consistently; primary risk is regulatory regime change which is low-probability [#J]

§6 — Key Driver #5: Operating Cost Efficiency and ElecLink Contribution

J-front verdict: Medium-plausibility margin uplift driver — cost discipline is demonstrable and ElecLink is contributing, but the scale of incremental uplift is modest relative to the volume and NeuConnect drivers [#J]

Driver definition: Getlink's fixed-cost infrastructure base means that EBITDA margins expand naturally with volume recovery; incremental cost efficiency initiatives and the growing ElecLink interconnector contribution add to margin trajectory [#I]

Mechanism to EPS: Lower unit costs + ElecLink EBITDA → higher group EBITDA margin → EPS accretion; the mechanism is partly endogenous (cost management) and partly volume-dependent (operating leverage) [#I]

Mechanism to SP: Margin expansion that exceeds consensus expectations drives EPS upgrades; ElecLink is a demonstrably profitable asset that de-risks the NeuConnect investment case [#J]

Company guide: Management has highlighted ongoing cost discipline and the growing ElecLink revenue contribution; no specific EBITDA margin target publicly disclosed but direction-of-travel is clearly communicated as upward [#I]

Management commentary: Management consistently presents ElecLink as proof of concept for the interconnector model; ElecLink revenues are visible and growing with UK-France electricity price spreads [#I]

Track record: EBITDA margins have expanded as volumes recovered; ElecLink has been operational and revenue-generating since 2021; ElecLink is delivering on its original business case [#J]

Conservatism vs optimism: Cost guidance has been broadly conservative; management has not over-promised on efficiency; ElecLink revenue has been somewhat volatile given electricity market conditions [#A]

SS consensus: Sell-side models gradual EBITDA margin expansion toward 55-60% (infrastructure peer range); ElecLink contribution is modelled explicitly in most broker EBITDA forecasts [#I]

Estimated EPS impact: Each 100bps EBITDA margin expansion on a ~€800m EBITDA base implies ~€8m incremental EBITDA; ElecLink contributes an estimated €50-80m EBITDA annually at current electricity spreads — a material contribution [#I]

Recent delivery: ElecLink EBITDA volatile with electricity price spreads; peak contribution was in the high-spread environment of 2022-23; more normalised spreads in 2024-25 have reduced ElecLink's contribution [#A]

  • ElecLink EBITDA has likely peaked on a cyclical basis as electricity market normalisation reduces UK-France spreads — a headwind vs recent years [#A]

Peer benchmark: No cohort peer data (D-BATCH-25 gap); European infrastructure operators with interconnector assets (National Grid, Red Electrica) typically see significant quarterly variability in interconnector margins [#I]

Macro tailwind: Energy market volatility in Europe supports electricity spread revenue for ElecLink; structural UK-European energy market divergence is a medium-term tailwind [#J]

Macro headwind: Energy market normalisation post-2022 spike is reducing electricity spreads; ElecLink peak contribution is likely behind rather than ahead [#A]

Visibility: Medium visibility on ElecLink (electricity price spread dependent); high visibility on base operating cost trajectory [#I]

Bear case: Electricity spreads compress to pre-2021 levels; ElecLink EBITDA contribution falls to €20-30m from €50-80m peak; group EBITDA margin trajectory flattens despite volume recovery [#A]

Miss scenario: ElecLink revenues disappoint by €30-40m vs consensus — causes a 4-5% group EBITDA miss; not catastrophic but removes the cost/margin story as a catalyst [#A]

Miss magnitude: ElecLink underperformance of €30-40m likely causes 3-5% EPS downside — modest but removes a tailwind [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: ElecLink's contribution in 2022-23 was likely unsustainable; consensus may have partially normalised estimates but any residual peak-spread assumption creates downside risk vs actuals [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — cost discipline is credible but ElecLink contribution is cyclically declining; net margin trajectory is positive but below the pace embedded in peak-spread consensus estimates [#J]

§7 — Key Driver #6: GBP/EUR FX and UK Macro Sensitivity

J-front verdict: Secondary driver — directional modifier rather than fulcrum — FX and UK macro affect reported EBITDA and Eurostar volumes but are not independently load-bearing for the investment case [#J]

Driver definition: Getlink reports in EUR but a significant portion of revenues (Eurostar passengers, Le Shuttle tourism) is sensitive to UK consumer demand, which is affected by GBP/EUR rate and UK GDP trajectory [#I]

Mechanism to EPS: Stronger GBP → UK consumers more price-competitive for cross-Channel travel → volume uplift for Eurostar/Le Shuttle; weaker GBP → suppresses UK outbound tourism demand [#I]

Mechanism to SP: FX is a second-order effect; the primary channel is through Eurostar volumes (Driver #1) and reported EUR EBITDA translation; currency is a modifier, not a fulcrum [#J]

Company guide: Management does not explicitly guide on FX; they acknowledge the GBP exposure in investor materials; no hedging strategy publicly detailed [#I]

Management commentary: Management typically references UK consumer trends as a context factor for Eurostar volumes; FX is presented as an external variable rather than a managed risk [#I]

Track record: GBP/EUR has been volatile post-Brexit (range approximately 1.08-1.25 since 2020); Eurostar volumes have been more sensitive to Eurostar's own operational issues than to FX in L3Y [#I]

Estimated EPS impact: A 5% move in GBP/EUR likely affects group EPS by 2-4% through a combination of volume effect and translation — material directionally but not a primary driver [#I]

Recent delivery: GBP has been broadly stable in 2024-25; UK consumer confidence has been recovering; no significant FX-driven distortion in recent reporting [#I]

Macro tailwind: UK economic recovery supports cross-Channel leisure travel; Bank of England rate cuts improving consumer confidence [#J]

Macro headwind: A UK recession or GBP depreciation scenario would suppress Eurostar passenger volumes and report lower EUR revenues — compounding Driver #1 downside [#A]

Visibility: Low visibility — FX is exogenous; macro forecasts for UK GDP have wide confidence intervals at 24M horizon [#I]

Bear case: UK enters a mild recession in 2025-26; GBP weakens 8-10%; combined effect reduces Eurostar passenger volumes by 8-12% vs trend and compresses reported EUR EBITDA [#A]

Miss scenario: Correlated shock — UK consumer stress + GBP weakness simultaneously — amplifying Driver #1 downside; this is the most likely scenario where FX becomes a primary driver [#A]

Miss magnitude: Adverse FX + UK macro scenario could add 5-8% incremental EPS downside on top of a volume miss — a compounding risk, not a standalone one [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: FX is omitted from many sell-side sensitivity analyses as a secondary factor; but in a correlated stress scenario (UK recession + GBP weakness + Eurostar volume miss), the three effects compound and FX is no longer secondary [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Low plausibility as standalone fulcrum — FX is a directional modifier that amplifies Driver #1 outcomes; the scenario where FX is the dominant driver requires a UK macro stress that is not the base case [#J]

§10 — Driver Synthesis + Sceptical Synthesis

J-front verdict: The strongest bearish case combines NeuConnect delay + flat Eurostar volumes + ElecLink mean-reversion — three partially-correlated headwinds that compound to a materially worse outcome than any single-driver miss [#J]

Driver dependency: Drivers #1 (volumes) and #3 (leverage reduction) are directly linked — volume recovery is the primary EBITDA driver that funds de-leveraging; if volumes disappoint, de-leveraging pauses [#A]

Correlation across drivers: Drivers #2 (NeuConnect) and #3 (leverage) are in structural tension — NeuConnect capex consumes the FCF that would otherwise reduce debt; they cannot both deliver simultaneously at full force [#A]

  • Driver #6 (FX/macro) is correlated with Driver #1 (volumes) in a stress scenario — UK recession hits both Eurostar volumes and GBP, compounding the EBITDA miss [#A]

Cross-driver independence: The only truly independent driver is #4 (concession pricing) — this delivers regardless of macro, NeuConnect, or ElecLink outcomes; it is the structural floor [#J]

Cumulative plausibility: If Driver #1 = 80% likely, #3 = 60% likely, #5 = 55% likely, and these are partially correlated, the joint probability of all three delivering on consensus is approximately 40-50% — below the threshold for a high-conviction setup [#A]

Setup archetype: "Infrastructure recovery + energy optionality compounder" — a recognisable pattern where the base recovery is visible but the 25%+ IRR requires the optionality (NeuConnect) to be exercised [#J]

Reverse-engineered bear: Sell-side bulls embed full NeuConnect NAV, Eurostar recovery to 105% of 2019, and continued ElecLink contribution at normalised spreads; the bear case is simply that none of these three assumptions hold simultaneously [#A]

Single largest case-level risk: NeuConnect financing failure combined with flat Eurostar volumes — would reduce GET-FR to a bond-proxy concession at 5-6x EBITDA with no growth catalyst and a leverage trajectory that stalls [#A]

Stacked downside: If Drivers #1, #2, and #5 all miss simultaneously: EBITDA 10-15% below consensus, NeuConnect NAV stripped, ElecLink normalised — combined 20-30% share price downside from current levels in a stacked bear scenario [#A]

Single-driver resilience: The concession floor (Driver #4) provides a genuine earnings floor — even in the bear case, Getlink generates substantial, inflation-linked EBITDA; this bounds the downside and distinguishes the case from a pure-growth or speculative setup [#J]

Fulcrum candidate: Driver #1 (volumes) is the load-bearing fulcrum for the base case; Driver #2 (NeuConnect) is the fulcrum for the upside/re-rating case; the investment works without NeuConnect at a modest IRR, but requires NeuConnect for 25%+ [#J]

Multi-driver risk assessment: The 25%+ IRR scenario requires three things to go right simultaneously (volumes, NeuConnect progress, leverage reduction) — this is a multi-variable thesis, not a single-fulcrum clean setup [#A]
