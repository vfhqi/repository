§1 — Fastweb Italy — The Central Change Story

1.1 Acquisition structure and price

  • Swisscom acquired Vodafone Italia for EUR 8bn (enterprise value), closed 31 December 2024 — after receiving all regulatory approvals including AGCOM sign-off
  • Pre-existing ownership: Swisscom has owned Fastweb (Italian fixed broadband operator) since 2007, initially paying ~EUR 3.1bn; Fastweb contributed 10-15% of group revenue pre-deal
  • Combined entity: Fastweb+Vodafone is now Italy's #2 telco by revenue, #1 by some infrastructure metrics; ~20m mobile lines + 5.8m fixed lines at merger; branded as "Fastweb+Vodafone" for market-facing purposes
  • Transaction rationale stated by CEO Aeschlimann: convert Italy from a subscale fixed-only operator to a converged FMC challenger at scale; Fastweb had MVNO dependency on Wind Tre — acquiring Vodafone gave Swisscom full mobile network ownership in Italy

1.2 Fastweb market position in Italy (post-merger)

  • Fixed broadband: ~30% retail market share combined (roughly double the pre-deal share of ~13-14% for standalone Fastweb); still materially below TIM (~40%+ retail)
  • Mobile: ~20m mobile access lines combined — second-largest mobile operator in Italy after TIM, ahead of Wind Tre and Iliad
  • FTTH coverage: 56% of Italian households and businesses covered with optical fibre at end-2025 (+14pp YOY at mid-2025); target ~60%+ by end-2026
  • 5G mobile: 89% population coverage with 5G at end-2025; strong network quality position

1.3 Fixed-mobile convergence strategy in Italy

  • Pre-merger: Fastweb was fixed-only retail; relied on Wind Tre as MVNO host for mobile; structural disadvantage vs converged peers
  • Post-merger: Full FMC capability — own mobile spectrum + FTTH; convergence enables bundle pricing, churn reduction, and ARPU uplift
  • FMC flywheel thesis: Converged customers have lower churn (typically 30-50% lower in European markets) and higher ARPU (~EUR 5-10/month premium). With 30% fixed share, converting to FMC bundles could drive sustained ARPU uplift across ~5-6m households
  • ❌ Sceptic's challenge: Italian ARPU for mobile has been structurally depressed by Iliad (12.5m subs, 15% share by mid-2025). Bundle premium requires customer willingness to pay; Italian consumers are notoriously price-sensitive. FMC flywheel assumptions need stress-testing against Italian, not Swiss, consumer behaviour

1.4 Post-acquisition integration progress

  • 2025 integration milestones achieved:
  • New combined organisation structure established
  • Migration of Fastweb mobile SIMs to Vodafone Italia network "almost complete" by end-2025 (this is the largest operational complexity — physically migrating ~4m SIMs)
  • First joint Fastweb+Vodafone product portfolio launched for residential customers
  • Joint sales distribution: products and services of both brands available in all sales outlets
  • FastwebAI Suite launched for B2B customers
  • Synergies achieved: EUR 95m in FY2025 — below the proportional run-rate of EUR 600m/year final target but tracking early-stage integration curve
  • Integration capex: EUR ~700m total over first 3 years; capex intensity elevated in 2025-2027 before normalising; group capex guided CHF 3.0-3.1bn for 2026 vs CHF 3.1-3.2bn in 2025

1.5 Synergy timeline — the EPS transmission mechanism

  • EUR 600m annual run-rate synergies by ~2029 (5-year post-close, i.e. 2030 at latest; management language implies late 2028/early 2029)
  • Breakdown: EUR 240m direct cost savings (mainly mobile network migration eliminating Wind Tre MVNO payments); EUR 360m from sales/distribution/G&A rationalisation and network integration economies
  • 2026 milestone: EUR 300m target — EUR 77m achieved in Q1 2026 alone, tracking ahead
  • EPS transmission (rough order of magnitude):
  • EUR 300m synergy → at ~40% EBITDA margin pass-through, adds ~EUR 120m to EBITDA incrementally
  • After D&A, interest, tax (~28% Italian + Swiss blended), adds ~EUR 65-75m to net profit
  • Group net profit ~CHF 1.6-1.8bn range implies ~EUR 65-75m = ~4-5% EPS uplift from synergy step alone
  • Full synergy (EUR 600m vs EUR 95m baseline) implies 20-25% EPS accretion from this change force alone over 4 years — this is the primary bull case
  • ⚡ Key transmission risk: synergy delivery depends on successful network integration and absence of accelerating revenue erosion. If Italian revenue decline steepens (say -3% to -5%), synergy gains are partly offset by revenue mix deterioration

1.6 Bear case: Italy as a price-war market

  • Iliad disruption: Iliad entered Italy in 2018 and has never relented — 12.5m mobile subs (15% share) by mid-2025, gaining 287k subs in Q2 2025 alone, now entering fixed broadband aggressively
  • TIM margin evidence: TIM's adjusted EBITDA margin fell from ~40% to ~33% between 2018 and 2024, driven by Iliad-induced price competition
  • Fastweb+Vodafone residential revenue: -2.9% in 2025 — not yet stabilised
  • Open Fiber risk: State-backed Open Fiber buildout means Fastweb+Vodafone's FTTH is not the only wholesale option; TIM Wholesale and Open Fiber offer competing infrastructure, which commoditises the access layer
  • ❌ The real bear case for the transformation-play: cost synergies are binary (achievable or not), but revenue is exposed to structural Italian market dynamics that no single operator controls. If Italian broadband ARPU continues drifting -2% to -3%/year, EUR 600m of synergy still leaves flat EBITDA — not the step-change the market is pricing
  • Massive-corporate-change 4-point credibility test:
    1. Strategic logic clear? Yes — Fastweb was sub-scale in mobile; Vodafone Italia was losing mobile share; combined = #2 converged player. Logic is sound
    2. Price paid reasonable? EUR 8bn for ~EUR 1.8bn EBITDA (pro forma) = ~4.4x EV/EBITDA pre-synergies, ~2.9x fully synergised. Reasonable if synergies deliver
    3. Execution capability demonstrated? Early evidence positive — EUR 95m achieved in year 1, SIM migration near-complete, joint product launched on schedule. Credit given but full test is 2027-2028
    4. External environment supportive? Italy mobile competition (Iliad) and macro (Italy GDP growth ~0.7% in 2025) are headwinds. Italian regulatory (AGCOM, Open Fiber) adds uncertainty. External environment is the weakest credibility point

§2 — Swiss Core Market — Defensive vs Growth

2.1 Swiss broadband: ARPU trajectory and structural vs demand-pulse question

  • Market position: Swisscom holds ~47% broadband share (Q2 2025, -1.6pp YOY) and ~54% mobile share (-1.2pp YOY); declining slowly but still dominant
  • Mobile postpaid ARPU: CHF 48 in Q2 2025, up +CHF 1 YOY — slight growth but minimal. Swiss ARPU is structurally high vs EU peers (~2x Italian mobile ARPU) reflecting premium network quality and regulatory stability
  • Demand-pulse caution applied: Swiss broadband penetration is already >95% of households; the COVID-era demand surge (higher speeds, data usage) appears absorbed. ARPU growth of ~2% per year is structural (price tiering, speed upgrades) not demand-pulse reversal. Risk of reversion is low in Swiss context — structural, not cyclical
  • Fixed-line revenue: CHF 1.5-1.6bn range, stable-to-slightly declining as customers shift from traditional voice to data-only; managed decline, not cliff

2.2 Sunrise (Liberty Global subsidiary) competitive pressure

  • Sunrise position: ~30.5% broadband share, ~26.5% mobile share at end-2024; gaining slowly but not accelerating
  • Broadband gains: -1.6pp Swisscom share implies ~0.5-1pp gain for Sunrise and ~0.5pp for Salt
  • Sunrise 5G SA: Launched nationwide 5G Standalone in March 2025 — enables differentiated low-latency enterprise slices; strengthens B2B proposition vs Swisscom
  • Sunrise fibre: Sunrise relies primarily on Swiss Fibre Net (SFN — utility-owned open access FTTH) for fixed broadband delivery. This is DOCSIS-free; Sunrise is not building a competing cable network — it is an access buyer, not a network builder in Switzerland
  • Incumbent dilemma assessment: Swisscom is not complacent — CHF 1.7bn/year invested in fibre and 5G SA; seven consecutive years of independent network quality leadership. But Sunrise's SFN-access model removes network quality as the sole differentiator in areas where SFN covers. As SFN expands, Swisscom's structural advantage narrows in those geographies
  • ComCom regulation (Oct 2025): Regulator and Federal Administrative Court sided with Swisscom in a dispute regarding shared use of fibre infrastructure — favourable outcome preserving Swisscom's wholesale terms. Reduces regulatory risk of forced access at below-market rates

2.3 Salt as third force

  • Mobile share: ~18% (2024), growing — 2,120,000 customers, +142k postpaid in 2024; customer service improving (closed gap to #2 Sunrise in 2025 connect survey)
  • Broadband share: ~4% — limited fixed footprint; relies on Open Access collaboration; not yet a material broadband threat
  • Assessment: Salt is a price challenger in mobile but lacks the fixed infrastructure to mount a converged FMC challenge. Unlikely to displace Swisscom or Sunrise in the medium term

2.4 Swiss FTTH rollout vs Swisscom strategic positioning

  • FTTH progress: 56% household coverage end-2025; targeting 60% end-2026; 75-80% by 2030; ~90% by 2035
  • Strategic importance: Copper decommissioning underway since 2024; FTTH replaces copper, enabling: (a) elimination of copper maintenance capex (~CHF 200-300m/year), (b) capacity uplift enabling speed-tier revenue, (c) structural cost reduction post-migration
  • Regulatory outcome (WEKO/ComCom, December 2023 decision upheld 2025): Swisscom's network strategy cleared — it is not required to grant access to its FTTH infrastructure on the same terms as copper unbundling. This is significant: Swisscom can use FTTH as a competitive differentiator in multi-dwelling units where it is the sole owner
  • Digitec Galaxus disruption: March 2025 10 Gbps FTTH plan launched at CHF 39 — a structural price anchor disruption at the low end. Swisscom's premium positioning means this is partially mitigated, but it will pressure entry-level broadband ARPU over time

2.5 Swiss market verdict: mature cash cow with limited upside

  • Swiss core is a CHF ~4.0-4.2bn revenue, CHF ~2.1-2.2bn EBITDA business — stable, high-margin, slowly eroding on volume but defended by quality premium
  • ARPU trajectory: structural +1-2% on pricing/speed mix; structural -0.5-1% on volume loss to Sunrise → net flat to +1%. Not a growth driver for the investment case
  • EPS transmission from Swiss market: minimal incremental change; the marginal value driver is copper-to-fibre cost savings (2027+), not revenue growth

§3 — IT Services / Cloud — B2B Change

3.1 Scale and current financials

  • IT Services revenue: CHF 1,215m in 2025 (+2.0% YOY) — roughly 11% of group pro forma revenue
  • Context: Swisscom IT Services provides cloud, managed IT, cybersecurity, and outsourcing to Swiss enterprises and public sector. It is Switzerland's #1 cybersecurity provider (by revenue) and #1 private cloud provider for Swiss enterprises
  • 2025 headwind: Many B2B customers entered cost-saving mode or delayed IT investments — growth of 2% was below trend; management guided for improving trajectory as corporate spend normalises
  • Margin profile: IT services margins are lower than core telecom (~10-15% EBITDA margin vs telecom's 40%+) but growing. Cross-sell with telecoms infrastructure generates margin uplift at portfolio level

3.2 Sovereign cloud and AI platform — structural growth driver

  • Swiss AI Platform: Swisscom's sovereign AI infrastructure play for enterprise customers — positioned as trusted, Swiss-hosted, GDPR-plus compliant AI compute
  • Regulatory tailwind: Swiss data residency expectations are tightening (not yet legally mandated but strong cultural/regulatory preference). EU Digital Markets Act and Swiss political sensitivity to foreign cloud providers (US hyperscalers) create a genuine addressable market for sovereign cloud
  • FastwebAI Suite in Italy: Replication of AI platform strategy for Italian B2B and public sector — early-stage but positions Fastweb+Vodafone for Italian sovereign cloud opportunity
  • Government sector: Swiss federal and cantonal governments are Swisscom IT's largest anchor clients; long-term outsourcing contracts with renewal visibility 3-5 years forward
  • ❌ Bear case: AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud are investing heavily in Swiss data centres (AWS Zurich region open). Swisscom's sovereign advantage is real but eroding at the margins as hyperscalers gain Swiss regulatory acceptance. IT services growth may be structurally capped at 3-5% absent step-change in contract wins

3.3 EPS materiality of IT services

  • At CHF 1,215m revenue and ~12% EBITDA margin, IT services contributes ~CHF 145m EBITDA
  • After D&A and tax, net contribution ~CHF 85-95m — roughly 5-6% of group net profit
  • Growth of 5%/year adds ~CHF 5-7m to net profit annually — marginal at group level
  • IT services is strategically important for customer retention and B2B cross-sell but is not an EPS inflection driver in 2025-2027 timeframe

§4 — Capital Structure Change

4.1 CHF 26/share dividend — FCF cover and sustainability

  • Dividend trajectory: CHF 22/share held flat 2011→2024 (13 years) → CHF 26/share proposed for 2025 (AGM March 2026) → CHF 27/share planned for 2026 (if targets met)
  • Operating FCF: CHF 1,920m in 2025 (+9.6% YOY); guided CHF ~2.0bn in 2026
  • Dividend obligation: 51.8m shares × CHF 26 = CHF 1,347m. FCF cover = 1.43x — adequate, not generous
  • Confederation dividend dependency: The Swiss Confederation owns 51% of shares and receives ~CHF 688m/year at CHF 26/share. This is effectively a quasi-fiscal transfer. Any dividend cut would require political justification to the federal government — strong floor on dividend, but also creates pressure not to cut even under adversity
  • ❌ FCF stress test: If Italy integration costs overrun or Italy revenue declines steepen, FCF cover narrows. At CHF 1,700m FCF (stress case), CHF 26/share (CHF 1,347m obligation) is covered 1.26x — still positive but with limited buffer

4.2 Leverage trajectory

  • Net debt: CHF ~15.63bn end-2025; CHF 16.18bn at March 2026 (Q1 increase due to seasonal capex)
  • Net debt/EBITDA: ~2.4x end-2025; guided ~2.3x end-2026 — declining ~0.1x per year
  • Context: Leverage was ~1.7x pre-Vodafone Italia acquisition. The EUR 8bn deal has durably increased leverage. Path back to ~2.0x is 2028-2029 at current pace — i.e., deleveraging is a slow-burn story
  • Investment-grade credit: Maintained; BBB+ equivalent. Swiss Confederation backing effectively acts as implicit guarantor. No near-term refinancing risk

4.3 Buyback optionality

  • Assessment: None. Last buyback programme was 2006. Since then, 51.8m shares outstanding unchanged. State-ownership structure (Confederation must maintain 51% minimum) makes share buyback functionally impossible — any buyback would either: (a) require Confederation proportional participation (no precedent), or (b) reduce Confederation below 51% (illegal under Telecommunications Enterprise Act)
  • Conclusion: Swisscom is a dividend story, not a capital return flexibility story. The FCF-to-dividend transmission is the only return-of-capital mechanism. This limits the EPS-per-share growth relative to peers who can reduce share count

4.4 M&A constraint

  • Swiss Confederation majority ownership constrains further large-scale M&A — the Vodafone Italia deal stretched balance sheet to 2.4x; another EUR 5bn+ deal is not feasible before 2029
  • Bolt-on IT services acquisitions (CHF 200-500m range) are feasible and consistent with Swiss B2B strategy
  • International expansion beyond Italy is effectively closed unless leverage re-rates significantly — Switzerland/Italy duopoly of geography is durable constraint

§5 — Management & Strategy Change

5.1 CEO Christoph Aeschlimann — background and credibility

  • Appointment: CEO since June 2022 (previously CTO/CDO, with Swisscom since 2014)
  • Technical profile: Engineer background; oversaw Swisscom's 5G SA network build and fibre rollout strategy before becoming CEO. Not a traditional M&A dealmaker
  • Fastweb decision: Aeschlimann championed the Vodafone Italia acquisition as the centrepiece of his growth strategy. The EUR 8bn price and the strategic logic (converged scale in Italy) were his calls — he owns the thesis
  • Animal CEO test: Methodical, technically credible, cautious by Swiss corporate standards. Not an aggressive capital allocator; more in the mode of a disciplined operator. The Vodafone Italia deal was bold relative to his persona — positive signal that it was strategy-driven not ego-driven

5.2 Fastweb decision rationale — why Italy?

  • Strategic logic in three parts:
    1. Fastweb was subscale in Italy without mobile — EUR 600m/year EBITDA ceiling as fixed-only operator growing at 5-7%
    2. MVNO dependence on Wind Tre was structurally inferior — Swisscom paying wholesale rates to a competitor, with no network differentiation
    3. Vodafone Italia was available at a distressed multiple (4.4x EV/EBITDA pre-synergies) due to Vodafone Group's portfolio simplification and Italian market challenges
  • Capital allocation assessment: The deal is financially sound if synergies deliver. The risk was buying into a structurally challenged market at a price that required execution on EUR 600m of cost savings. Year 1 evidence (EUR 95m) is on track but the full test is 2027-2028 when the revenue stabilisation question resolves

5.3 Swiss market strategy — complacent or adapting?

  • Evidence of adaptation: CHF 1.7bn/year capex into FTTH and 5G SA; network quality leadership maintained; ComCom regulatory wins; copper decommissioning programme (cost reduction); AI/sovereign cloud investment
  • Evidence of residual complacency: Swisscom's pricing remains premium — the arrival of Digitec Galaxus (CHF 39/10Gbps FTTH) in March 2025 suggests management has not preemptively moved to defend the value segment. Salt is gaining mobile subs at the low end, and Swisscom's response has been network quality promotion rather than price competition — correct long-term but leaves value-segment flank open
  • Assessment: Management is adapting, not complacent. The premium strategy is appropriate for the Swiss market structure. The risk is a sudden acceleration of price competition from a Sunrise-owner Liberty Global looking to replicate its cable-to-converged transformation playbook (as seen in UK/Netherlands)

5.4 Liberty Global as shadow competitor

  • Sunrise is Liberty Global's Swiss asset. LG's strategy across Europe is DOCSIS 3.1/4.0 upgrade + FMC bundle convergence + operational discipline. In Switzerland, LG/Sunrise does not own a cable plant (Swiss cable is fragmented); Sunrise competes as an MNO + SFN FTTH access buyer
  • Risk: If LG decides to invest more aggressively in Sunrise — price discounting, enterprise 5G SA build-out — this could accelerate Swisscom's Swiss market share erosion. LG has the balance sheet and playbook experience. This is a low-probability, high-impact risk for the next 2-3 years

§6 — External Change Forces

6.1 Swiss regulatory environment

  • ComCom Oct 2025 decision: Sided with Swisscom on FTTH sharing dispute — positive, preserves network differentiation advantage in multi-dwelling unit coverage where Swisscom is sole builder
  • Wholesale obligations: Swisscom is obliged to provide wholesale broadband access on its copper network under regulated terms. As copper retires, the regulated access obligation migrates to FTTH — but on commercial rather than regulated terms where Swisscom owns the only FTTH infrastructure in that location. This is a regulatory moat as copper retires
  • 5G spectrum: Swisscom holds the strongest 5G SA spectrum position (700MHz + 3.5GHz + mmWave) following the 2019 auction. No near-term re-auction risk

6.2 Italian regulatory: AGCOM and Open Fiber

  • AGCOM: Italian regulator approved the Fastweb+Vodafone merger with remedies (network sharing obligations, wholesale access commitments). AGCOM will monitor Fastweb+Vodafone's wholesale terms ongoing — risk of asymmetric regulation favouring TIM/Open Fiber
  • Open Fiber: State-owned fibre operator building FTTH in underserved areas under the Piano Italia 1Giga (EU-funded). Open Fiber coverage will expand to ~7m premises, creating a third wholesale option alongside TIM Wholesale. This is a moderate competitive pressure on Fastweb+Vodafone's fixed wholesale revenue
  • RAN sharing with TIM (Jan 2026 preliminary agreement): Preliminary agreement to cooperate on Radio Access Network development in rural/less dense areas. Strategic: reduces rural 5G capex burden for both parties. Regulatory approval required; not yet binding. If closed, could save EUR 100-200m NPV over 5 years in network roll duplication costs
  • ❌ Italian regulatory risk: AGCOM has historically favoured infrastructure competition over incumbency protection. Any tightening of Fastweb+Vodafone's retail pricing dominance (post-merger, they are Italy's #2 — not the incumbent) could impose remedies on commercial terms. Watch the AGCOM market review scheduled for 2026-2027

6.3 EU Digital Decade and Swiss positioning

  • Switzerland is not an EU member but has bilateral agreements that align Swiss standards with EU digital policy. Swiss companies benefit from EU Digital Decade targets (1Gbps to all households by 2030) in terms of demand creation without facing full EU regulatory burden
  • Fastweb+Vodafone directly benefits from EU Digital Decade in Italy: Piano Italia 1Giga government subsidies for FTTH build partially offset Fastweb+Vodafone's capex in underserved areas
  • Swiss non-EU status: No exposure to EU roaming regulations, BEREC oversight, or European Electronic Communications Code in the same manner — regulatory risk for Swiss domestic operations is lower than for fully EU-domiciled peers (Proximus)

6.4 FX: CHF strength and EUR investor impact

  • CHF/EUR structural dynamic: CHF has consistently appreciated vs EUR over decades. For a EUR-denominated investor, SCMN-CH generates a meaningful proportion of earnings in CHF (Swiss operations = ~60% of EBITDA), which is structurally appreciating
  • Group reporting in CHF: Italy segment reported in EUR, translated to CHF. CHF appreciation vs EUR is a mild headwind to reported group numbers — every 1% EUR/CHF depreciation reduces Italy's CHF-translated EBITDA by ~EUR 20m translated value
  • EUR investor note: Swiss francs provide a natural hedge against EUR depreciation — positive for EUR-based investors who are structurally long-CHF via SCMN exposure
  • 2025 FX impact: EUR weakened ~2-3% vs CHF in 2025; immaterial at this magnitude but noteworthy for EUR investors

§7 — Change Forces Summary & EPS Transmission

7.1 Top 3 change forces by EPS materiality

CF#1 — Italy synergy ramp (Fastweb+Vodafone)
- Magnitude: EUR 95m achieved (2025) → EUR 300m target (2026) → EUR 600m run-rate (2029)
- EPS transmission: Each EUR 100m incremental synergy = ~CHF 0.40-0.50 EPS (after tax, D&A, minority). EUR 505m of remaining synergy upside = CHF 2.00-2.50 EPS upside by 2029 relative to 2025 baseline
- Probability: Moderate-high (early evidence tracking, EUR 77m in Q1 2026 alone). Downside risk: Italy revenue erosion offsets cost gains
- Bear case: Italy residential revenue declines at -3% vs -1% base; synergies deliver only 70% (EUR 420m not EUR 600m); net EPS impact ~CHF 1.00-1.50 vs CHF 2.00-2.50 bull

CF#2 — Swiss FCF generation and dividend growth
- Magnitude: Swiss operations generate ~CHF 2.2bn EBITDA, capex ~CHF 1.7bn → Swiss OpFCF ~CHF 900m-1.0bn. As FTTH replaces copper, maintenance capex declines 2027+; estimated CHF 200-300m capex savings by 2030
- EPS transmission: CHF 200m capex reduction = ~CHF 0.80-1.00 EPS improvement (low tax shield; near-direct pass-through to FCF and net profit). Timeline: 2027-2030. Not 2025-2026 story
- Bear case: FTTH take-up lower than expected; copper decommissioning delayed by ComCom challenges; capex saving of only CHF 100-150m by 2030

CF#3 — Italian revenue stabilisation (binary, medium probability)
- Magnitude: Italy revenue -1.1% in 2025 (adjusted); stabilising to flat/+1% from 2027 as FMC bundles gain traction = ~EUR 70-100m revenue delta at ~30% incremental margin = ~EUR 20-30m EBITDA and ~CHF 0.15-0.25 EPS
- Probability: Medium — depends on Iliad trajectory (still gaining subscribers in 2025), bundle adoption pace, and competitive intensity
- Bear case: Italy revenue continues -2% to -3%/year through 2028; EBITDA impact is ~EUR 300-450m below synergy-only case; effectively wipes out 50% of synergy value

7.2 Counter-hypothesis — why SCMN change forces fail

  • Italy execution fails: Integration costs overrun EUR 700m; SIM migration causes customer churn acceleration; EUR 600m synergy achieves only EUR 350m by 2029. EPS trajectory flat-to-negative in Italy
  • Iliad attacks fixed broadband in Italy with pricing aggression: Already happening per mid-2025 data. If Iliad and Wind Tre launch CHF 20/month fixed broadband offers, Fastweb+Vodafone residential ARPU collapses
  • Swiss market accelerates competitive intensity: LG/Sunrise invests aggressively in 5G SA B2B and targets Swisscom's enterprise accounts; Swiss broadband share loss accelerates from -1.6pp to -2.5pp/year
  • FCF disappoints: Group capex remains elevated (CHF 3.0-3.1bn) longer than expected; synergies don't offset higher D&A from Vodafone Italia acquisition; FCF cover of dividend narrows to <1.2x; dividend confidence challenged
  • CHF appreciation shock: EUR/CHF moves to 0.85-0.90 range (not implausible in risk-off environment); Italy EBITDA translated to CHF is worth 10-15% less
