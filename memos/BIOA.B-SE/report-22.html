Competing hypotheses (mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive, falsifiable):

  • Hypothesis A — Structural moat, durable pricing power: Stock X has built defensible competitive advantages (switching costs, network effects, scale economics, or IP) that allow sustained above-cost-of-capital returns; pricing power holds through cycle; EPS trajectory is predictable; 25%+ IRR achievable if valuation entry is reasonable

  • Falsifiable claim: If gross margins compress materially in a demand slowdown, or if a well-capitalised competitor wins share without pricing response from Stock X, Hypothesis A is falsified

  • Hypothesis B — Cyclical tailwind misread as structural moat: Stock X's current returns and margin profile are primarily driven by a benign macro/sector cycle (capacity tightness, demand surge, input cost deflation) rather than structural advantage; the moat narrative is a post-hoc rationalisation of cyclical luck; EPS trajectory is unreliable through the cycle

  • Falsifiable claim: If returns prove sticky through a demand normalisation event without margin compression, Hypothesis B is falsified

  • Hypothesis C — Management optionality story obscuring structural decline: Management is deploying a narrative of transformation, M&A, or platform expansion to distract from erosion in the core business; disclosed metrics (adjusted EBITDA, organic growth rates) are obscuring underlying deterioration; structural advantage is eroding, not compounding

  • Falsifiable claim: If organic core-business metrics (ex-acquisition, ex-currency) show stable or improving unit economics, Hypothesis C is falsified

  • Hypothesis D — Null / Neither (commodity-like business with no durable advantage): Stock X operates in a structurally competitive market with no durable moat; returns will mean-revert to cost of capital over the investment horizon; EPS is predictable only in a direction (downward); 25%+ IRR is not achievable from the competitive positioning leg

  • Falsifiable claim: If ROCE persistently exceeds WACC by >300bps over 3+ years without cyclical explanation, Hypothesis D is falsified

Runner-up hypothesis and why it isn't the winner:

  • Runner-up is Hypothesis B — it accumulates +2 more hard inconsistencies than Hypothesis A in the ACH matrix (see §3)
  • Key disconfirming evidence for B: If management has demonstrated pricing discipline in prior downturns and if customer retention data shows low churn even under cost pressure, Hypothesis B's causal mechanism (cycle-driven rather than structure-driven returns) is substantially weakened
  • Why B retains runner-up status: The mechanism is economically plausible (many businesses confuse cycle-peak returns with structural advantage), and without detailed cycle-normalised margin data, the analyst cannot fully disconfirm B — close-call status preserved

Sceptical bullet: Runner-up Hypothesis B is dangerously close — the +2 disconfirming-item margin is within the range where a single new diagnostic finding (cycle-trough gross margin data or competitor pricing aggression evidence) could flip the winner; the analyst should not treat A's lead as stable until that data is sourced
