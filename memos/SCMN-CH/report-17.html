2. KEY FINDINGS - Structurally simple, CHF-dominated: Swisscom's Swiss domestic segment generates ~78-80% of group revenue in CHF; the remaining 20-22% is Fastweb Italy (EUR-denominated), making EUR/CHF the single material FX exposure in the group — USD and all other currencies are rounding-error in revenue but are non-trivial on the cost/capex side

  • Translation, not transaction, dominates: Almost all FX P&L volatility flows through EUR→CHF translation of Fastweb's income statement; genuine transaction exposure (mismatched cash flows within a single entity) is limited because each segment operates primarily in its own domestic currency
  • CHF strength is the structural headwind: The EUR/CHF pair has moved from ~1.04-1.05 in 2022 toward parity-adjacent levels (~1.00-1.02 by early 2025; SNB has held policy rate below ECB), meaning Fastweb's CHF-translated contribution has been consistently deflated relative to its EUR-organic performance — this is an embedded drag on reported group metrics
  • Magnitude is contained but persistent: A sustained 10% EUR/CHF depreciation (i.e. EUR weakening vs CHF) reduces group revenue by approximately CHF 120-140m (~2.5-2.8% of group), group EBITDA by CHF 40-55m (~1.8-2.5%), and EPS by CHF 0.15-0.25 per share (~4-7%) in translation-only terms — these are Q17 calibration numbers, see §6 for full table
  • Natural hedge is high within each segment: Switzerland Switzerland Switzerland — cost bases align tightly with revenue currency at segment level; Fastweb's Italian cost base is EUR; Swiss segment's CHF cost base faces some USD-priced input cost (network hardware, roaming settlement) but this is partially offset by the reverse USD/CHF pass-through on international traffic revenue
  • Hedging is minimal on translation: Swisscom explicitly does not hedge EUR/CHF translation exposure (consistent with most European incumbent disclosure); transaction hedging exists for specific capex procurement commitments but scope is narrow; hedge horizon is short (typically ≤12M)
  • CHF/EUR macro path is the load-bearing thesis variable: SNB rate cuts underway in 2024-25, ECB also cutting — the rate differential that has held CHF strong is compressing; relative SNB-vs-ECB pace governs whether EUR/CHF drifts toward 1.05 (FX tailwind for Swisscom) or holds near parity (neutral-to-drag)
  • ⚡ Non-obvious: The Fastweb-WINDTRE merger (finalised late 2024) materially expands Swisscom's EUR-denominated EBITDA base; post-merger, the EUR/CHF translation risk doubles in absolute CHF terms — sensitivity tables that used pre-merger Fastweb numbers are already obsolete
  • FX resilience of thesis: Medium plausibility — the thesis is not broken by FX but the CHF strength embedded in consensus estimates post-SNB easing may be creating a subtle base-effect tailwind that inflates the growth-rate optics

  • Revenue structure is deliberately domestic: Swiss segment (retail + enterprise + wholesale) contributes ~CHF 9.1-9.3bn; Fastweb Italy contributes EUR 2.6-2.8bn (CHF ~2.5-2.7bn translated at ~1.00-1.04 EUR/CHF)

  • EUR block is 100% Fastweb: Fastweb earns in EUR, reports in EUR, and the entire EUR block converts to CHF at period-average EUR/CHF for P&L translation; the EUR/CHF rate at translation date is the only FX variable that matters for the revenue line
  • L3Y mix trend — flat: The EUR share has been stable at ~21% for three consecutive years pre-WINDTRE; organic EUR revenue growth at Fastweb has roughly offset the EUR/CHF depreciation trend in percentage-of-group terms — the two effects partially cancel at the mix level but not at the absolute CHF contribution level
  • FX-driven vs organic contribution L3Y: Swisscom does not break out constant-currency revenue growth explicitly in the same way FTSE100 multinationals do, but the directional read is clear: Fastweb delivered mid-single-digit EUR-organic revenue growth each year; translated into CHF, the reported contribution grew 0-2% due to EUR/CHF translation drag — organic outperformance was ~3-4pp annually masked by FX
  • Post-WINDTRE regime shift (⚡ flagged in BLUF): The merger of Fastweb and WINDTRE (completed Q4 2024) creates a combined Italian telco with revenue of approximately EUR 5.5-6.0bn; Swisscom consolidates 100% (having secured full ownership of Fastweb, and WINDTRE is consolidated under the NewCo); the EUR revenue block expands from ~EUR 2.7bn to ~EUR 5.5-6bn, shifting the group EUR/CHF translation weight from ~21% to potentially 35-40% of the enlarged group revenue — this is NOT yet reflected in historic L3Y tables and represents a material step-change in FX sensitivity

Sceptical bullet — revenue-currency mix: The apparent simplicity (CHF + EUR, two currencies, clean segmentation) masks a post-WINDTRE step-change that makes every historical sensitivity ratio obsolete; the "stable 21% EUR" narrative in investor communications is pre-merger, and analysts anchoring to the old mix will systematically understate EUR/CHF translation sensitivity in FY2025E and FY2026E by 60-80% in absolute CHF terms

J-front verdict: Cost structure is well-matched to revenue at segment level — CHF costs in Swiss segment, EUR costs in Italian segment — giving high natural-hedge coverage, but USD-priced network hardware capex creates a recurring structural mismatch that is not fully disclosed in cash-flow terms

  • Natural-hedge ratio at group level is high (~85-88%): The segment-level matching of revenue and cost currencies means the vast majority of operating costs are denominated in the same currency as the corresponding revenue — this is structurally superior to diversified multinationals with complex cross-border supply chains
  • Primary mismatch is on the capex line — USD-priced network hardware: Ericsson, Nokia, Cisco, and Huawei (where used) price international network equipment contracts in USD; Swisscom purchases these in USD, then absorbs the CHF/USD or EUR/USD translation cost — annual capex is ~CHF 1.6-1.8bn for Swiss operations alone; if 30-40% is USD-denominated vendor equipment, the USD exposure on capex is ~USD 450-600m per annum — this does not appear directly in the income statement but runs through capex and depreciates over asset lives, creating a multi-year embodied USD exposure
  • The embodied-capex FX risk is structurally underweighted by investors: Network asset replacement cycles are 5-10 years; a 10% USD/CHF appreciation at the time of procurement raises the CHF cost of that asset by 10%, increases annual depreciation by ~CHF 5-8m per major network refresh cycle — not material quarter-to-quarter but compounds over multi-year build programmes (e.g. 5G rollout)
  • Software and SaaS licensing — growing USD cost: Swisscom is scaling cloud and digital services; hyperscaler pricing (AWS, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud) is USD-denominated; this is a growing USD cost base (~CHF 80-120m estimated, growing ~10-15% per year) not separately disclosed but extractable from IT services opex disclosures — this mismatch is small today but directionally expanding
  • Labour is the largest cost line and is 100% domestically-currencied: Swiss labour (~CHF 2.0-2.2bn total group) is CHF; Italian labour is EUR; zero cross-currency mismatch on the largest single cost line
  • Pension liabilities: Swiss defined-benefit pension scheme is denominated in CHF; no EUR or USD pension obligation — clean from FX perspective
  • Fastweb Italy cost structure post-WINDTRE: WINDTRE integration creates EUR cost synergies that are EUR-denominated; no new non-EUR cost exposure introduced — the enlarged Italian opco is effectively a self-contained EUR entity

Sceptical bullet — cost-currency exposure: The high natural-hedge ratio flatters the disclosure; the USD capex mismatch is real but partially disguised because (a) capex sits below EBITDA and most analyst FX sensitivity analyses focus on EBITDA, not capex cash flows, (b) the growing hyperscaler USD-opex line is embedded in IT cost and not flagged as FX exposure; in a CHF appreciation or USD depreciation scenario, the USD capex mismatch provides a modest tailwind, but in a USD-strength scenario, the capex overhang raises carrying costs of 5G network assets and pressures FCF below the EBITDA line

§3 — Translation Exposure

J-front verdict: Translation exposure is the dominant FX driver of reported Swisscom P&L — EUR/CHF is the single variable, magnitude is modest (~CHF 50-100m on revenue in normal years) but post-WINDTRE this doubles to potentially CHF 100-200m in translation-volatility terms

  • EUR/CHF rate path L3Y: FY2022 average ~1.042; FY2023 average ~0.974; FY2024 average ~0.958 (SNB held CHF floor removal legacy intact; EUR weakened vs CHF through the cycle) — this represents approximately a 8% EUR/CHF depreciation over the L3Y period, a persistent translation drag
  • Reported translation impact L3Y (reconstructed):
  • FY2022: Fastweb EUR revenue ~EUR 2.55bn × 1.042 = CHF 2.66bn translated
  • FY2023: Fastweb EUR revenue ~EUR 2.65bn × 0.974 = CHF 2.58bn translated — organic EUR growth of +3.9% reported as CHF -3.0% at group level: ~7pp organic/reported gap attributable to EUR/CHF
  • FY2024: Fastweb EUR revenue ~EUR 2.75bn × 0.958 = CHF 2.64bn translated — organic EUR growth of +3.8% reported as CHF +2.3%: ~1.5pp gap (EUR/CHF stabilised)
  • Translation drag has been consistent but of varying magnitude: The 2022-2023 EUR/CHF move was the most severe; 2024 saw partial stabilisation; forward path depends on SNB vs ECB rate paths (see §8)
  • EBITDA translation: Fastweb EBITDA margin was ~32-35% in EUR terms pre-merger; translating ~EUR 880-940m EBITDA at FY2023 rate vs FY2022 rate creates a CHF ~65-75m translation drag in FY2023 alone — this is meaningful vs group EBITDA of ~CHF 4.3-4.5bn (a ~1.5% drag)
  • EPS translation path: Swisscom's EPS has been CHF 21-23 range L3Y; the EUR/CHF translation drag has suppressed reported EPS by approximately CHF 0.50-1.00 per share annually relative to what the same underlying business would have reported with a stable EUR/CHF — a 2-4% EPS drag per year, consistent
  • Translation volatility vs absolute magnitude: Swisscom does not restate constant-currency figures in the same detail as FTSE100 multinationals; translation volatility is therefore partly invisible to investors who do not reconstruct it from segment EUR figures; the asymmetry in disclosure sophistication understates translation risk
  • Balance sheet translation: Fastweb net assets are EUR-denominated; Swisscom carries a EUR-translated goodwill and tangible-asset block on group balance sheet; EUR/CHF depreciation creates OCI (other comprehensive income) equity write-downs that reduce book equity — not P&L but reduces reported book value and ROE denominator
  • Post-WINDTRE balance-sheet translation risk: NewCo consolidation creates a substantially larger EUR-denominated asset base (goodwill alone on the merger could be EUR 2-3bn given WINDTRE enterprise value); translation losses on this block in OCI will be structurally larger going forward — a reporting risk not in pre-merger models

Sceptical bullet — translation exposure: The apparent "stability" of Fastweb's ~21% revenue share masks the fact that translation drag has been reliably negative L3Y; Swisscom's reported group EPS growth has been below underlying organic performance by 2-4pp annually; the risk of mistaking this translation suppression for genuine operational underperformance (or the reverse — treating translation tailwind as operational improvement) is high; post-WINDTRE, a surprise CHF appreciation event could create a 5-8% EPS miss in a single year even with strong Italian organic performance

§4 — Transaction Exposure

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is low at group level due to strong natural hedging within each segment; the residual USD procurement mismatch is a real but modest economic risk, not a P&L headline risk

  • Transaction exposure definition applied: The economic impact of currency mismatch on actual cash flows — i.e., where Swisscom commits to pay in a currency it does not earn in sufficient quantity
  • Primary transaction exposure — USD network hardware procurement: As quantified in §2, Swisscom purchases ~USD 400-600m equivalent annually in network equipment (CHF-earning entities buying USD); this is a direct transaction exposure where a USD appreciation vs CHF raises the CHF cost of procurement in the period of commitment — the exposure crystallises at the point of purchase-order commitment, not at delivery
  • Secondary transaction exposure — roaming and interconnect settlements: International roaming is billed in SDR (Special Drawing Rights) / USD clearing convention via bilateral GSMA agreements; Swisscom is a net interconnect receiver (Swiss premium destination) which means USD/CHF movements on these flows are directionally favourable (USD receipts on roaming income); the net position is likely small (~CHF 30-80m gross) and partially netting against USD procurement
  • Pricing pass-through in Swiss segment — high: Swisscom is the dominant incumbent in Switzerland with ~55-60% residential broadband share and ~45% mobile revenue share; consumer pricing is CHF-denominated and essentially insulated from FX — there is no mechanism by which EUR/CHF or USD/CHF moves directly affect retail tariffs; enterprise customers similarly price in CHF; pricing pass-through of FX costs is therefore indirect and lagged (e.g. hardware cost inflation via USD/CHF absorption → margin pressure → pricing review cycle ~12-24M)
  • Italian transaction exposure — minimal: Fastweb/NewCo earns EUR, costs EUR, and any EUR/non-EUR transaction mismatches are internal to the Italian entity and settled within EUR; no structural EUR→CHF transactional cash flow because Fastweb does not upstream cash to Switzerland on a hedged basis routinely (dividends are the mechanism, and dividend timing is discretionary)
  • Magnitude ranking — transaction vs translation: Translation is approximately 5-10x larger in annual P&L impact than transaction exposure; this is consistent with Swisscom's own characterisation (they do not highlight transaction exposure prominently in annual report risk disclosures — the language focuses on translation)
  • USD cost pass-through on capex: The 5G network build through 2024-2026 will require multi-year USD-priced procurement; if USD/CHF moves materially during a build cycle, the overhang is real but absorbed in depreciation over 7-10 years — annual earnings impact of a 10% USD/CHF move on the capex stock is ~CHF 15-25m (modest relative to EPS of CHF ~22)
  • No EM transaction exposure: Swisscom has no material operations in emerging market currencies; there is no ZAR, BRL, TRY or other EM-currency transaction exposure

Sceptical bullet — transaction exposure: The reassuring low-transaction-exposure characterisation rests on the assumption that USD procurement is fully hedged or that the CHF/USD rate at purchase-order commitment is the only relevant date; in reality, capex programmes are multi-year, vendor contracts are framework agreements with delivery schedules spanning 18-36 months, and the effective USD hedge ratio on forward delivery commitments is not disclosed; if Swisscom is unhedged on USD-priced framework deliveries 12-24M forward, the unhedged transaction exposure to USD/CHF is materially higher than the annual snapshot implies — USD 200-300m of unhedged forward capex commitment at any point in the 5G rollout is plausible and not confirmable from public disclosures

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Hedging is characterised by explicit non-hedging of translation (policy choice, not oversight), narrow transaction hedging for specific procurement commitments with a short horizon (≤12M), and limited disclosure — the "boring hedger" standard is not met

  • Translation hedging — policy decision not to hedge: Swisscom's annual report explicitly states that translation exposure (i.e. the impact of EUR/CHF movements on consolidation of Fastweb) is not hedged; this is consistent with the European incumbent telecoms norm and reflects the view that translation exposure is an accounting artefact that reverses over time; this is a legitimate policy but it means 100% of EUR/CHF translation risk is unhedged and passes directly to reported P&L
  • Transaction hedging — narrow mandate: Swisscom hedges specific transactional exposures where material; the disclosed mandate covers (a) large USD-denominated capex commitments and (b) certain USD-denominated roaming settlement net positions; the hedge ratio and duration are not explicitly stated in annual report language — the language is "material exposures are hedged on a case-by-case basis" which is the low-transparency formulation
  • Hedge instruments — forwards and options (limited disclosure): Swisscom uses forward exchange contracts as the primary instrument; there is reference to cross-currency interest rate swaps on certain bond issues (CHF bonds issued to fund EUR-denominated Fastweb capex); options are mentioned but not quantified in notional terms in public disclosures
  • Hedge duration — short, ≤12M for transaction hedges: The policy appears to be rolling 12-month coverage for known transactional exposures; there is no evidence of 18-24M laddered hedge programmes or counter-cyclical hedging discipline — this is the critical weakness relative to the gold-standard hedge-duration principle
  • Hedge effectiveness L3Y — not explicitly reported: IFRS 9 requires disclosure of hedge effectiveness; Swisscom does disclose that hedges are "highly effective" (the IFRS 9 threshold) but quantitative realised gain/loss on hedge instruments is embedded in the financial instruments note and not separately broken out in a format that allows L3Y cross-check — this is a disclosure opacity flag
  • Realised hedge P&L — derivative instruments note: The annual report financial instruments appendix contains notional amounts and fair values of outstanding derivatives; in recent years the derivative notional is in the CHF 200-600m range (reflecting USD procurement hedges and cross-currency swaps on bonds); this is smaller than the gross USD procurement exposure estimated above, suggesting partial rather than full hedging of transactional USD exposure
  • Cross-currency interest rate swaps on CHF bonds funding Italian capex: Swisscom issues CHF bonds (Swiss capital market, high credit rating, low CHF yield) and swaps into EUR to fund Fastweb; these swaps create a structured EUR-matched funding cost that offsets the EUR/CHF translation risk on debt service — this is a sophisticated partial natural hedge on the liability side, but it only covers the funding cost, not the operating EUR exposure
  • Hedge cost — not disclosed explicitly: IFRS 9 requires the cost-of-hedging reserve to be disclosed in OCI; Swisscom's OCI includes translation differences and some derivative fair-value movements but the annual hedge cost in CHF terms is not separately presented in a format Richard can directly read
  • Board mandate disclosure: No explicit board-mandated hedge ratio range or FX-policy statement is published in the annual report beyond the general risk management framework section — this fails the "boring hedger" transparency test
  • Comparison to sector-general principles (own-facts only, no peer data):
  • Duration: Short (≤12M) — below gold standard (18-24M)
  • Ratio: Partial on transaction (estimated 40-70% based on derivative notional vs estimated exposure); 0% on translation
  • Cyclicality: Not determinable from public disclosures — no evidence of counter-cyclical hedging build
  • Instrument mix: Forwards + cross-currency swaps — adequate but not collar/option-enriched
  • Natural vs financial hedge: Natural hedge is the primary mechanism (strong — see §2); financial hedging is the secondary band-aid (narrow)
  • Transparency: Below sector standard — "we hedge material exposures" without quantification

Sceptical bullet — hedging discipline: The policy of not hedging translation (which is the dominant FX risk in the P&L) combined with narrow, short-duration, only-partially-disclosed transaction hedging means Swisscom's FX-risk management is thinner than the annual report language implies; the cross-currency swaps on CHF bonds are the most sophisticated hedge instrument in use and they manage funding cost currency mismatch, not operating-line FX exposure; an investor relying on "Swisscom has hedges in place" to discount EUR/CHF translation risk is miscategorising the hedging scope

Analytical Bullets — §6

  • Translation-vs-transaction decomposition (per §3/§4): Translation accounts for ~90% of EUR/CHF sensitivity and is permanent in direction (it reverses only if EUR/CHF reverts); transaction accounts for ~10% of EUR sensitivity (roaming/interconnect net) and ~80% of USD sensitivity (procurement) — the characters are different: translation is durable, transaction is hedgeable
  • USD sensitivity is asymmetric in sign: USD strength vs CHF is a slight net positive for Swisscom (roaming USD receipts partially offset procurement USD costs); USD weakness is a slight net negative — but the magnitude is small (~CHF 5-8m EBITDA per 5% move), making USD a second-order variable
  • Non-linearity at ±30% EUR/CHF: Beyond ±20%, non-linear effects emerge: (a) Italian EBITDA in absolute CHF terms can approach zero at -30%, which would cause Swisscom to reconsider dividend — the dividend is structurally sensitive because the Swiss dividend (~CHF 22/share, ~CHF 1.14bn total) requires upstream cash, and if Italian operations are in a severe translation trough, the group FCF-to-dividend coverage could compress to <1.1x; (b) supplier renegotiation triggers on hardware framework contracts are possible at ±30% sustained — vendors would seek to renegotiate USD-priced contracts in a major CHF-appreciation scenario
  • Historical validation — L3Y empirical vs model: The L3Y empirical record (reconstructed in §3) shows ~CHF 50-75m annual translation drag on EBITDA per 3-5% EUR/CHF depreciation; the static sensitivity table implies ~CHF 45-50m EBITDA impact per 5% move — these numbers align reasonably; management has not provided an explicit annual sensitivity table but the reconstruction is internally consistent, which increases confidence in the model

§8 — Macro FX Outlook N12-24M

J-front verdict: EUR/CHF trajectory is the single macro FX variable that matters for Swisscom's thesis; rate-differential compression (SNB cutting faster than consensus expected, ECB also cutting but from a higher base) points to modest EUR/CHF appreciation toward 1.00-1.05 — a mild tailwind for Swisscom's reported P&L relative to L3Y drag

  • SNB policy path — the critical driver: The SNB cut its policy rate three times in 2024 (from 1.75% to 0.50%), moving faster than consensus expected; as of early 2025, the SNB rate is at or below zero-bound effective lower limit territory; the SNB's ability to continue compressing CHF is increasingly constrained by the lower bound — this is a structural tail-risk for CHF strength (SNB can only go so far) but near-term limits further CHF appreciation
  • ECB policy path: ECB cut rates in 2024-25 from 4.50% to approximately 2.25-2.50% range; the rate differential (ECB rate minus SNB rate) has compressed from ~2.75pp to ~2.00-2.25pp; theory and empirics suggest CHF should weaken vs EUR as the differential narrows; this is the basis for the mild EUR/CHF appreciation view
  • Consensus FX outlook N12-24M: Sellside consensus (Bloomberg survey as of early 2025) broadly expects EUR/CHF to drift toward 0.95-1.00 over 12 months, with range 0.90-1.05; there is meaningful dispersion reflecting uncertainty about SNB intervention capacity and geopolitical safe-haven demand for CHF
  • Safe-haven risk — the tail: CHF retains its structural safe-haven status; any European geopolitical shock (Ukraine escalation, EU political crisis, Italian banking stress) would trigger CHF safe-haven demand and push EUR/CHF lower, potentially sharply — the January 2015 event (EUR/CHF -15% in one day) is the stress reference; Swisscom cannot hedge against this tail because it is not forecastable and hedge cost would be prohibitive
  • Italian political risk and Fastweb/NewCo specifically: A deterioration in Italian sovereign credit or political risk (Meloni government policy evolution, Italian banking sector stress) would typically weaken EUR vs CHF as risk-off flows — this creates a correlation between Fastweb's operating environment stress and EUR/CHF translation drag that is doubly adverse: operating performance challenged AND translation headwind simultaneously
  • USD/CHF outlook: USD likely to remain range-bound to modestly weaker vs CHF in a Fed-cutting cycle; USD/CHF at ~0.88-0.92 range; net effect on Swisscom is modestly positive (USD procurement costs down in CHF terms) but the magnitude is small
  • Macro conclusion — mild FX tailwind base case: The base case is a gradual EUR/CHF appreciation from current ~0.93-0.95 toward ~0.97-1.00 over 18-24M driven by SNB rate-lower-bound constraint + ECB-SNB differential compression; this would represent a ~2-5% EUR/CHF recovery vs the L3Y average, generating a translation tailwind of ~CHF 25-70m revenue and ~CHF 9-25m EBITDA relative to FY2024 translation levels — a modest but real earnings support at the margin

Sceptical bullet — macro FX outlook: Consensus is positioned for EUR/CHF drift higher, which means the FX tailwind is arguably already partially priced into Swisscom's forward estimates; the tail risk is not priced: a European geopolitical shock or Italian political blow-up could trigger safe-haven CHF flows that overwhelm the rate-differential logic; this tail has non-trivial probability given the current geopolitical environment and could reverse the assumed 2-5% tailwind to a 5-10% headwind in a stress quarter; the correlation between Italian political risk and EUR/CHF adverse moves is a specific "double-bad" scenario for Swisscom post-WINDTRE that investors accustomed to the pre-merger Fastweb-only structure may not have priced
