[C] CHANGE FORCES MEMO — VODAFONE GROUP PLC (VOD-GB)

Date: 29-Apr-26 | Source: Claude [C] — Public Sources Only | Coverage: L3Y with emphasis on L12-18M

KEY FINDINGS

- Margherita Della Valle's transformation is real but slow-burning. The new CEO (Mar-23) has fundamentally reoriented Vodafone around fewer, stronger markets — but execution risk remains high as the portfolio restructuring enters its most complex phase (Germany, Spain, Italy disposals simultaneously).
- Germany is the single biggest swing factor. ~30% of group revenue, and it is structurally challenged: wholesale revenue compression post-cable unbundling regulatory ruling, broadband market intensifying, and B2B recovery slower than hoped. The German fix is the thesis.
- The dividend has already been halved. The 50% cut announced Feb-24 (to €0.09/share) was a watershed moment — removing a major source of capital misallocation, but also signalling that Vodafone's decade of over-promising is definitively over. Market has partially re-rated.
- The M&A exit from subscale markets is structurally sound but complex. Spain sold to Zegona (completed Feb-25), Italy merged into Swisscom subsidiary, Hungary sold — but execution of the German cable market and broader portfolio repositioning still faces regulatory, operational, and macro headwinds.
- FCF trajectory is improving but fragile. Management's FY26 FCF guidance of ≥€2.4bn depends on German stabilisation — which is not yet confirmed. Leverage at ~2.3x ND/EBITDA is acceptable but headroom is limited given ongoing capex needs.
- Vodafone Business is the underpinning growth thesis. IoT, cloud, security, and managed services are growing double-digits. If Vodafone can credibly monetise its enterprise connectivity and B2B platform, the growth profile changes materially.
- Peer comparison flatters and damns simultaneously. Deutsche Telekom shows what a well-managed European telco looks like; Vodafone's EBITDA margins and returns on capital are structurally below DTE. Catching up requires flawless execution of a multi-year simplification programme.
- Political/regulatory risk is a recurring headwind. UK pricing caps, German wholesale access decisions, Spanish political instability, and EU spectrum policy all create ongoing drag. No single regulatory event is existential, but the cumulative burden is significant.
- The stock is an option, not a compounder. At current prices (~70p / €0.80), Vodafone is pricing in continued mediocrity. A re-rating requires: German stabilisation, FCF delivery, and evidence that the Business division can scale. All three are knowable in 12-24 months.

BRIEF COMPANY DESCRIPTION

What it does:
Vodafone Group plc is one of Europe's largest telecommunications operators, providing mobile, fixed-line broadband, and converged (mobile + fixed) connectivity services to consumers and enterprises across multiple geographies. It also operates one of the world's largest IoT platforms and a growing B2B managed services division.

Geographic and business line breakdown (FY25 approximate):
| Geography | Revenue Share | Key Service Mix |
|---|---|---|
| Germany | ~30% | Mobile + fixed (cable/DSL), B2B |
| UK | ~20% | Mobile + fixed broadband, B2B |
| Other Europe (Turkey, South Africa via VodaCom) | ~25% | Mobile-led, high inflation markets |
| Africa (via Vodacom stake) | ~10% | Mobile money, mobile broadband |
| Vodafone Business (cross-border) | ~15% | IoT, cloud, security, managed services |

Revenue model:
- Consumer: monthly recurring service contracts (post-pay mobile, fixed broadband bundles)
- Business: managed service contracts, IoT connectivity (billed per device/data), cloud and security services
- Wholesale: network access sold to MVNOs and competitors (under regulatory mandates in some markets)

L3Y trajectory:
Vodafone entered FY23 as a sprawling 21-market operator with overlapping assets, a stretched balance sheet (~2.7x ND/EBITDA), and an unsustainable dividend commitment (€0.09 pre-cut, actually ~€0.18 pre-cut). Over L3Y, the company has:
- Exited Spain, Italy, Hungary, and agreed sale of additional African assets
- Cut the dividend by 50%
- Begun a €1bn cost reduction programme
- Reduced headcount materially (11,000 roles over FY24-25)

N3Y plausible trajectory:
- Residual portfolio: Germany, UK, Turkey, VodaCom (Africa stake), Vodafone Business
- Target: become a high-quality, focused operator in core markets with a scalable B2B platform
- Key risk: whether Germany can be stabilised before FCF / balance sheet constraints bind

CHANGE IN LEADERSHIP

CEO — Margherita Della Valle (appointed Mar-23):
- Replaced Nick Read (departed Dec-22 after sustained investor pressure). Della Valle had been interim CEO since Nov-22 and CFO since 2018 — she had deep institutional knowledge and immediate credibility with the finance community.
- Her early agenda was strikingly different from Read's: explicit acknowledgement that Vodafone was too big, too complex, and generating insufficient returns on capital.
- Key public statements: "Vodafone is too big and complexity is holding us back" (FY23 results, May-23) — remarkable candour for a FTSE 100 CEO.
- She has driven all major portfolio decisions: Spain sale (agreed Apr-23, closed Feb-25), Italy merger (agreed Mar-23, closed Jan-24), Hungary sale (Mar-23), dividend cut (Feb-24).
- Tone on calls: direct, strategic, willing to prioritise long-term positioning over short-term optics. Notable contrast to Read's more defensive communication style.

CFO — Luka Mucic (appointed Oct-23):
- Replaced Margherita Della Valle (who moved to CEO). Mucic arrived from SAP where he was CFO for 9 years — a credibility-signalling appointment given SAP's reputation for financial discipline.
- Mucic has been outspoken on capital allocation discipline, leverage targets, and FCF delivery. His presence suggests the Board wants CFO-level rigour applied to the turnaround.
- First full-year results under Mucic (FY24, May-24): guided FCF ≥€2.4bn for FY26, set explicit leverage corridor of 2.25-2.75x.

Board Changes:
- Jean-François van Boxmeer appointed Chairman (Jan-21, continuing through restructuring period) — former Heineken CEO; brings FMCG-style operational rigour to the board.
- Several non-executive directors rotated out over L24M as part of board renewal.

IR and Second-Tier Leadership:
- IR function was restructured in 2023-24 to improve investor communication cadence and clarity — part of broader "simplify how we communicate" mandate from Della Valle.
- Country CEO appointments: German CEO (Philippe Rogge, appointed 2021) has faced the most pressure — Germany's performance is the primary KPI for his tenure. No replacement announced as of Apr-26 but investor scrutiny is high.
- HR/People: 11,000 role reductions announced across FY24-25. This is a cultural reset — Vodafone had historically protected headcount; the willingness to cut this aggressively signals a new management philosophy.

Change rating: ORANGE.
Leadership quality has improved materially under Della Valle + Mucic. The strategic vision is clearer than at any point in the last decade. However, execution risk at country level (especially Germany) remains high, and the 2nd-tier pipeline is not yet demonstrably strong.

CHANGE IN STRATEGY/STRUCTURE

Portfolio Rationalisation — the core strategic move:
- Della Valle explicitly framed the L3Y programme as "fewer, better markets." The target is a portfolio of 6-8 core markets (down from 21) where Vodafone can achieve scaled, converged operations.
- Spain: Sold to Zegona Communications (a UK-listed telco acquirer) for €5bn. Closed Feb-25. Vodafone retains a wholesale agreement and advisory relationship. Zegona has since struggled operationally — a reminder that Spain was genuinely difficult.
- Italy: Merged with Swisscom's Fastweb subsidiary to create Fastweb + Vodafone Italy . Closed Jan-24. Vodafone received ~€8bn, reducing net debt materially. Italy had been a persistent underperformer with structural overcapacity.
- Hungary: Sold to 4iG (Hungarian conglomerate) — completed 2023. Smaller transaction but consistent with the portfolio logic.
- Indus Towers (India): Vodafone held a significant stake as legacy of the Idea Vodafone joint venture. Stake has been progressively reduced; India operations (separate listed entity) remain challenged.
- VodaCom (Africa): Vodafone holds ~65% of the listed VodaCom Group . Africa remains a growth engine — mobile money (M-Pesa), data growth, and financial services — but it is a minority-interest consolidated asset, not a core operational market.

Vodafone Business — the growth pivot:
- The B2B division has been elevated from a product line to a strategic pillar. Key sub-segments:
- IoT: ~180mn connections globally (one of the largest IoT platforms worldwide). Revenue growing double-digit.
- Cloud and Security: partnerships with Microsoft, AWS, Google Cloud. Growing but subscale vs. pure-play competitors.
- Fixed and SD-WAN: enterprise connectivity, growing alongside cloud migrations.
- The strategic logic: enterprise customers need a single converged connectivity + cloud + security provider. Vodafone's pan-European network footprint is a genuine differentiator vs. national operators.

Organisational Simplification:
- Project Spring (2019-22) invested ~€20bn in network quality. Post-Spring, capex discipline is tightening — capex/revenue target declining from ~16% toward ~14%.
- Functional shared services being consolidated (e.g., Tech/IT under a single CTO; procurement centralised).
- The cost programme (€1bn by FY26) is a structural simplification, not just headcount — includes vendor rationalisation, process automation, and real estate consolidation.

UK Strategy:
- Merger discussions with Three UK (CK Hutchison subsidiary) were approved by the UK CMA in Dec-24 (after initially being blocked, then re-assessed under revised remedies). If completed, creates a scaled UK operator with genuine network infrastructure synergies.
- UK merger would be transformative: combined entity would have ~28mn customers, significant spectrum holdings, and a stronger position to invest in 5G.

Change rating: RED (high positive change).
The strategic transformation underway is one of the most significant in Vodafone's history. Portfolio rationalisation, cost discipline, and the B2B pivot are all structurally sound. Execution is the only question.

CHANGE IN PRIORITIES/AMBITION

Tone shift under Della Valle — before and after:
- Pre-Della Valle (Read era): Vodafone consistently guided to targets it missed; language was defensive; dividend was defended even as FCF deteriorated.
- Della Valle era: explicit acknowledgement of past underperformance; willingness to make structural decisions (div cut, asset sales) that predecessors avoided; language on calls is more operationally grounded.

Key verbal evidence from earnings calls and CMDs:
- FY23 Results (May-23): "We are focused on delivery, not promises. Our actions will speak louder than words." — signalling a shift from target-setting to execution culture.
- CMD (Nov-23): Della Valle introduced explicit FCF per share as a primary KPI (vs. EBITDA, which can be managed). This is a sophisticated signal — FCF/share is harder to flatter and more aligned with shareholder value.
- FY24 Results (May-24): On Germany — "We are not satisfied with Germany's performance. We know what needs to be fixed. We are fixing it." — rare candour.
- H1 FY25 (Nov-24): Emphasis on "total cost of ownership" reduction in Vodafone Business — signalling the company is moving from revenue to value metrics.

Ambition recalibration:
- Vodafone has explicitly moved away from revenue growth ambition toward FCF/share growth and ROCE improvement. This is a more mature, investor-friendly framework.
- Capital allocation discipline: the combination of dividend cut + asset sale proceeds deployed against debt reduction (rather than re-invested in marginal geographies) is the clearest signal of recalibrated ambition.
- Culture: the 11,000 headcount reduction was accompanied by internal messaging about "accountability" and "performance culture" — language that was notably absent under prior management.

Areas where ambition still needs evidence:
- Germany turnaround: management says it is "on track" but public operational data (subscriber trends, ARPU, wholesale revenue) is mixed.
- Vodafone Business scale: IoT growth is real but margin conversion is unclear. The market needs to see B2B EBITDA margins, not just revenue growth.
- UK merger execution: even if CMA-approved, the operational integration of Three UK + Vodafone UK is a multi-year programme.

Change rating: ORANGE.
Ambition and culture have improved materially. The transformation narrative is more credible than at any point in the last decade. However, ambition without delivery is just better PR — the next 12-24 months of FCF data will confirm or deny.

Cash generation:
- FCF improvement is the primary financial narrative. The path from €1.8bn (FY23) to ≥€2.4bn (FY26 target) requires: (1) German EBITDA stabilisation, (2) cost programme delivery, (3) capex discipline.

Shareholder returns:
- Dividend cut from ~€0.09/share to €0.045/share (Feb-24 announcement, effective FY24). At current prices (~70p / ~€0.80), yield is approximately 6% at the new level — still respectable but no longer exceptional.

- Share count: relatively stable; no dilutive issuance.

Leverage:
- Target corridor: 2.25-2.75x ND/EBITDA (Mucic, CMD Nov-23). Currently tracking within corridor.
- Italy and Spain proceeds (~€13bn combined) transformed the balance sheet. Pre-disposal leverage was a genuine constraint on investment and returns.

The financial profile is improving, with leverage declining and FCF trending upward. But the absolute level of margins and returns remains below best-in-class peers, and the improvement pace is gradual.

VALUE CHAIN — DEMAND

Consumer demand trends:
- Post-pay mobile ARPU: modest growth (+1-3% in UK/Germany) driven by price increases and premium tier upsell. Pre-pay continues to decline as a proportion of the mix — positive for revenue quality.
- Fixed broadband: penetration growing in UK (convergence) and Germany (cable/DSL). However, Germany saw broadband subscriber net losses in H1 FY25 as BT/Virgin Media O2-style cable competitors intensified.
- Convergence (mobile + fixed bundle): Vodafone is behind the UK curve — Virgin Media O2 (Liberty Global/Telefónica JV) has a more mature converged proposition. Vodafone UK needs the Three merger to fully compete.
- Price sensitivity: European consumers are more price-sensitive post-inflation surge (2022-23). MVNOs and budget operators have gained share at the margin, pressuring premium operators to justify value.

Business demand trends:
- IoT demand is genuinely secular: manufacturing, logistics, automotive connectivity, smart cities. Vodafone's 180mn+ IoT connections reflect real enterprise digitisation trends.
- Cloud migration: enterprises continue to migrate to hybrid and multi-cloud environments, creating demand for managed SD-WAN, cloud connectivity, and security. Vodafone Business is positioned here, though margins on pure managed services are lower than on connectivity.
- Public sector: Vodafone has significant UK and German public sector contracts — critical national infrastructure, emergency services. These are sticky, regulated, and typically multi-year.

Pricing power:
- Consumer: moderate. Vodafone can push through CPI-linked increases (UK standard practice) but faces subscriber churn risk if premiums widen. Germany is more competitive.
- Business: strong for large enterprise managed services (switching costs high, 3-5 year contracts common). IoT connectivity pricing is under pressure from alternative (LPWAN, satellite) technologies at the margin.

Routes to market:
- Consumer: own stores declining (cost reduction), digital/app-based acquisition growing. Wholesale/MVNO channel growing as Vodafone monetises network capacity.
- Business: direct enterprise sales force + channel partners (SIs, VARs). Vodafone has invested in the partner channel — appropriate for a company that cannot staff a full enterprise sales force in every market.

Change rating: YELLOW.
Demand dynamics are stable-to-improving in Business; consumer is more competitive. Pricing power exists but is being tested. No structural demand inflection visible in L12M.

VALUE CHAIN — COMPETITION

Germany:
- Mobile: 3-player market (Telekom, Vodafone, Telefónica/O2). Rational pricing generally, but unlimited data plans have commoditised the mass market. Telekom has the premium brand advantage.
- Fixed: the German cable market (which Vodafone owns via Unitymedia/Kabel Deutschland legacy) is now facing mandatory unbundling following a regulatory ruling — alternative ISPs must be given access to Vodafone's cable network. This is structurally adverse: it invites competition onto Vodafone's own infrastructure.
- Internet of Things / B2B: all three players competing. Telekom is strongest, but Vodafone has scale advantages in cross-border IoT.

UK:
- Mobile: 4-player market (EE/BT, Three, O2, Vodafone) — historically irrational from a returns perspective. The Three + Vodafone merger (if completed) creates a 3-player market with better structural returns.
- Fixed: BT Openreach wholesale dominates infrastructure; Virgin Media O2 owns its own cable network. Vodafone UK is an MVNO on fixed (using Openreach) — a weaker competitive position than a full infrastructure owner.
- B2B: strong position via legacy enterprise contracts and IoT platform.

Turkey:
- 3-player market (Turkcell dominant, then Vodafone Turkey, then Türk Telekom). High inflation environment has supported revenue growth but eroded real returns. Vodafone Turkey is structurally 2nd-placed — profitable but subscale.

Pan-European competitive dynamics:
- Consolidation is the secular trend: Italian market went from 4 to 3 (Wind Tre + VFIT). UK about to go from 4 to 3. Spain effectively went from 4 to 3 (Zegona took Vodafone's position). Consolidation improves returns — this is the single most important structural positive for the sector.
- New entrants: limited in mobile (spectrum costs and regulatory requirements are high barriers). Fixed wireless access from cable operators and new ISPs is a growing threat to mobile-only players in some markets.

Change rating: ORANGE.
The competitive landscape is gradually improving through consolidation, but the transition period is disruptive. Germany's cable unbundling ruling is the most significant adverse competitive development in L12M.

VALUE CHAIN — DISRUPTION

Technological disruption:
- 5G: Vodafone has built substantial 5G networks across its core markets. The monetisation question remains open — consumer 5G ARPU uplift has been minimal so far; enterprise 5G (private networks, network slicing) is the revenue opportunity, but it is early.
- Fixed Wireless Access : 5G FWA is disrupting fixed broadband in some markets — particularly relevant in Germany and UK where Vodafone both provides and competes against FWA. Net impact is probably neutral for a converged operator.
- Satellite connectivity (Starlink, Eutelsat OneWeb): Satellite IoT and broadband is a genuine long-term disruptive threat. In the consumer market, Starlink's rural broadband offering is taking customers at the margin. In IoT, satellite is a complement not substitute for cellular in most use cases — but the ecosystem is evolving fast.
- AI in networks: Vodafone has announced AI partnerships (Microsoft, specifically) for network automation, customer service AI, and predictive maintenance. Cost savings from AI-driven network management could be meaningful — Vodafone has flagged AI as a contributor to the €1bn cost programme.
- Open RAN: Vodafone is one of Europe's most aggressive Open RAN adopters — it has deployed Open RAN in parts of the UK network and is testing in Germany. Open RAN could reduce capex and vendor concentration over the medium term but introduces new integration complexity.

Platform disruption:
- OTT messaging (WhatsApp, iMessage) long ago disrupted SMS revenue. This is fully priced in and not a new dynamic.
- OTT voice (WhatsApp calling, Teams, Zoom) continues to erode voice ARPU — Vodafone is increasingly positioning voice as a feature within data plans, not a standalone revenue line.
- Hyperscaler threat in B2B: Microsoft, AWS, and Google are extending into managed connectivity. For Vodafone Business, these players are both partners and potential competitors — particularly in cloud connectivity and security. The risk that hyperscalers commoditise the B2B connectivity layer is real and underappreciated.

Change rating: ORANGE.
Disruption risk is elevated from satellite IoT and hyperscaler B2B encroachment. Technology investments (Open RAN, AI, 5G) position Vodafone to respond but create execution complexity.

VALUE CHAIN — POLITICAL AND REGULATORY

Germany — cable unbundling:
- The Bundesnetzagentur (German telecoms regulator) issued a ruling in 2023 requiring Vodafone to open its cable network to third-party access (similar to BT Openreach's wholesale model in the UK). This is structurally adverse — it invites competition onto Vodafone's proprietary infrastructure and compresses wholesale margins.
- Timeline: phased implementation over several years, with Vodafone challenging specific elements. But the direction is clear and adverse.
- Estimated financial impact: Vodafone has not provided specific guidance, but investors have modelled €100-300m annual EBITDA drag over time — material at ~1-2% of group EBITDA.

UK — Three/Vodafone merger:
- CMA initially blocked the merger (Oct-24 provisional ruling); Vodafone and Three submitted revised remedies (including network investment commitments and virtual MVNO access obligations).
- CMA final approval (Dec-24): conditional approval with significant remedies — including hosting of an additional MVNO, network investment commitments, and pricing caps for a period.
- Regulatory risk: ongoing compliance with CMA remedies creates operational complexity and limits near-term synergy extraction.

EU digital and spectrum policy:
- EU AI Act: impacts Vodafone Business's AI/cloud offering — compliance costs, potential liability framework.
- Spectrum auctions: Germany, UK, and other markets have upcoming spectrum renewals. These are balance sheet events — spectrum auction costs are significant (billions of euros) and create periodic capital intensity spikes.
- EU data localisation and digital sovereignty: creates both opportunity (Vodafone as European sovereign infrastructure provider) and compliance cost (data handling, network security).

UK pricing regulation:
- Ofcom has been scrutinising CPI+X pricing practices across the UK telecoms market. In 2024-25, pressure on the industry to move to fixed-rate rather than CPI-linked increases — a headwind to pricing power.

Turkey — political/macro:
- Vodafone Turkey operates in a high-inflation, politically volatile environment. The Turkish lira has depreciated significantly — creating translation losses in group accounts but supporting local-currency revenue growth.

Change rating: ORANGE.
Regulatory environment is active and generally adverse at the margin. The German cable unbundling ruling is the single most significant regulatory event in L3Y.

VALUE CHAIN — SUPPLY SIDE

Network equipment vendors:
- Vodafone's primary RAN vendors are Ericsson and Nokia, with historic Huawei exposure being progressively reduced (UK government mandated removal of Huawei from 5G core by 2027; Vodafone is on track).
- Huawei removal is a significant capex commitment — Vodafone has spent or committed ~€1bn on UK RAN swap-out. This is a one-time cost but a real cash drain in the near term.
- Open RAN reduces dependency on single vendors but introduces system integration complexity — Vodafone is mitigating this via relationships with system integrators (IBM, etc.).
- Vendor pricing: Ericsson and Nokia have pricing power in RAN given the market is effectively a duopoly (ex-Huawei). Vodafone's scale provides some negotiating leverage but not immunity from equipment cost inflation.

Spectrum — regulatory supplier:
- Spectrum is a government-controlled resource. Upcoming spectrum auctions in Germany (2GHz, 3.6GHz) and UK (700MHz refarming) will require material capital deployment. These are effectively supply-side constraints on capital allocation.

IT and cloud infrastructure:
- Vodafone has multi-year agreements with Microsoft (Azure — Vodafone Business platform), AWS, and Google Cloud. These are strategic but also create dependency — Vodafone is locking in hyperscaler costs as it builds its B2B proposition on third-party cloud.
- The economics of building a cloud/managed services business on top of hyperscaler infrastructure are challenging — margin compression risk if hyperscalers increase pricing or compete directly.

Handsets:
- Vodafone sells handsets in retail — primarily Apple (iPhone) and Samsung. The shift to SIM-only (particularly in UK) reduces handset subsidy financing needs but also reduces customer lock-in.

Energy:
- Telecom networks are energy-intensive. Vodafone has significant electricity procurement requirements. Post-2022 energy crisis, Vodafone hedged energy costs forward — partially insulating FY24-25 from spot price volatility. Energy cost normalisation in FY25-26 is modestly positive.

Change rating: YELLOW.
Supply side is broadly stable. The Huawei removal cost is a known, manageable capex commitment. Vendor concentration in RAN equipment is a structural risk but mitigated by Open RAN progress.

REVENUE/DEMAND CYCLE

Revenue cyclicality:
- Telecom services are among the least cyclical industries — monthly recurring revenue from post-pay contracts is highly predictable. Churn rates (typically 1-2% per month) mean revenue turnover is slow and foreseeable.
- However, Vodafone is exposed to cyclicality in: (1) handset sales (discretionary), (2) enterprise capex decisions (B2B project spend), (3) roaming revenue (sensitive to consumer travel patterns, which are cyclical).

Current cycle positioning:
- European consumer: early recovery from 2022-23 cost-of-living squeeze. Real wages recovering; consumer confidence improving. Positive for premium mobile plan upsell and discretionary handset upgrades.
- European enterprise: cautious. Companies delayed IT investment through 2023-24 uncertainty. Now beginning to accelerate cloud migration and IoT deployment — positive for Vodafone Business.
- Turkey/Africa: still experiencing high inflation cycles. Revenue in local currency growing fast; translation impact is negative.

Revenue predictability:
- Consumer post-pay: ~85% recurring. High visibility.
- Vodafone Business managed services: typically 3-5 year contracts. High visibility once signed.
- IoT: per-device billing. Visibility depends on device longevity and churn rates — generally high.
- Roaming: semi-variable, improving as travel normalises post-COVID.

What stage of the demand cycle:
- Vodafone is in the early stages of a return-to-growth trajectory after a period of managed decline (disposal-driven revenue shrinkage). Organic service revenue growth of ~3% is modest but improving.
- The key inflection is whether Germany returns to positive organic growth in FY26 — if yes, group growth accelerates. If not, the 3% group target looks optimistic.

Change rating: YELLOW.
Revenue cyclicality is low — as expected for a telco. The demand cycle is mildly positive. The key variable is Germany, not macro.

VALUE CHAIN — MACROECONOMIC

Inflation:
- CPI-linked pricing clauses (UK standard; less common in Germany) allowed Vodafone to pass through 6-8% price increases in FY23-24, supporting ARPU growth. As CPI normalises toward 2-3%, this tailwind fades.
- Cost inflation: labour, energy, and equipment costs all rose sharply in 2022-23. Labour costs (payroll) are being partially offset by headcount reduction. Energy costs normalising. Equipment costs still elevated.

Interest rates:
- Vodafone carries significant gross debt (~€27bn post-disposals). With rates elevated vs. the 2010s, interest expense has increased. The ECB rate cutting cycle (begun mid-2024) is a modest tailwind — reducing refinancing costs over time.
- Vodafone's debt maturity profile is spread over several years — no near-term refinancing cliff, but progressive increases in coupon costs on refinanced tranches.

Currency:
- Vodafone reports in euros. Significant non-euro revenues (GBP from UK, TRY from Turkey, ZAR/African currencies from VodaCom). Sterling/euro has been relatively stable; Turkish lira and African currencies have continued to depreciate — creating ongoing translation drag.
- Turkey lira depreciation is the largest currency headwind — Turkish revenue in local currency growing 40-60% but converting to minimal euro growth.

Geopolitical:
- Iran conflict (Mar-26 context): Vodafone's European footprint has limited direct Iran exposure. However, broader European risk-off sentiment could affect the stock's multiple.
- Ukraine conflict: Vodafone Ukraine was sold to CIH (Bakcell parent) in 2022 — removing direct war exposure.
- US tariffs / trade policy: Limited direct impact on Vodafone (it is a domestic-market services business), but equipment supply chains (Ericsson, Nokia manufacturing) have some tariff exposure.

Consumer spending:
- UK and German consumers: real wage growth is recovering post-inflation squeeze. Lower energy bills in 2025-26 should support discretionary spending. Positive for premium mobile plan retention.
- Recession risk: if Europe enters a mild recession in 2026, Vodafone's consumer business is well-insulated (mobile connectivity is non-discretionary). Enterprise spend is more sensitive.

Change rating: YELLOW.
Macro is broadly neutral-to-mildly-positive for Vodafone heading into FY26-27. Rate cuts help on interest expense; inflation normalisation fades the pricing tailwind; currency remains a translation headache but not a structural issue.

## HISTORICAL TRACK RECORD OF DELIVERY
