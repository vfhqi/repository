§5 — Revenue + Growth Forecasts

  • J-front verdict: Revenue growth assumptions are moderate and mostly credible on a volume basis, but are critically dependent on Iberian and international power prices not deteriorating further from current depressed levels [#J]

  • Consensus revenue — FY25E: ~€1.88bn — a ~3% decline vs FY24A (~€1.94bn); driven by lower realised power prices despite modest volume growth [#A]

  • Consensus revenue — FY26E: ~€2.05bn — ~9% recovery; assumes power price normalisation and ~600–800MW of additional capacity entering operation [#A]

  • Consensus revenue — FY27E: ~€2.28bn — ~11% growth; assumes continued MW rollout and a return to mid-cycle power prices [#A]

  • Organic vs inorganic: All consensus revenue forecasts are organic — no M&A assumed; one broker (Berenberg) applies a small M&A optionality premium in their DCF (5–7% NPV uplift) but it does not flow into their revenue line [#J]

  • Segment forecast: Limited public segment disclosure constrains analysts; most model by geography/technology:

  • Spain onshore wind: ~35–40% of revenue, flat to modestly declining as merchant prices weigh
  • Spain solar PV: ~25–30% of revenue, growing with new additions
  • International (US, Australia, Chile, Mexico): ~25–30% of revenue, growing fastest but also highest execution risk

  • Offshore (pipeline, not yet material): negligible near-term contribution but cited by 7 of 12 analysts as a medium-term re-rating catalyst [#A]

  • Geographic forecast: International growth is the consensus growth engine — US PTC-supported projects and Chilean/Australian pipeline are the primary volume additions driving FY26–27 estimates above FY25 levels [#J]

  • Revision L1M: No revenue revisions in L1M [#A]

  • Acceleration assumed: FY26–27 acceleration is assumed by the full consensus — but acceleration is contingent on three simultaneous conditions: MW commissioning on schedule, power price recovery, and no additional regulatory headwinds in Spain [#J]

  • Market growth assumed: European renewable capacity market growing ~15–20% annually (IEA estimates); ANE-ES assumed to grow broadly in line, not outpace [#A]

  • Share gain assumed: None explicitly — analysts do not model share gains; revenue growth is volume × price, with volume driven by pipeline execution [#J]

  • Pricing assumed: Power price normalisation (Spain) to ~€70–80/MWh by FY26E vs ~€55–65/MWh in FY25E — a ~15–20% price assumption; this is the single largest swing variable and has the least empirical support [#A]

  • End-market outlook: Analyst commentary is broadly constructive on European renewable demand fundamentals but increasingly cautious on near-term Iberian merchant power prices; Spain's power price is the dominant variable and it has surprised to the downside in three consecutive quarters [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: The revenue recovery thesis for FY26–27 rests heavily on power price assumptions (~€70–80/MWh) that are above the FY25 run-rate (~€55–65/MWh); if power prices stay depressed — as Spain's grid hydro surplus and low carbon costs could cause — the FY26 revenue step-up does not materialise and the entire earnings recovery narrative collapses [#J]

§6 — Margin + Profitability Outlook

  • J-front verdict: Margin assumptions are broadly credible on EBITDA but optimistic on net margin, where interest costs and D&A are rising faster than analysts' base cases assumed 12 months ago [#J]

  • Consensus EBITDA margin — FY25E: ~77–79% (EBITDA/revenue) — consistent with the asset-heavy renewable utility model; broadly stable vs FY24A [#A]

  • Consensus EBIT margin — FY25E: ~35–38% (EBIT/revenue) — meaningfully below EBITDA margin given high D&A load; D&A has risen as a % of revenue vs FY23 levels due to accelerated capex and some asset impairments [#A]

  • Consensus net margin — FY25E: ~8–12% (wide range reflecting EPS dispersion); the net margin spread reflects different interest cost assumptions more than EBITDA differences [#A]

  • Margin trend assumed: EBITDA margin broadly flat FY25→FY27 (~77–80%); net margin expanding from ~8–12% (FY25E) to ~15–20% (FY27E) as assumed debt refinancing and volume scaling reduce per-unit interest cost [#J]

  • Productivity assumption: Operating costs per MWh assumed to decline modestly (~2–3% per year) as scale benefits kick in; this assumption is modestly aggressive — actual O&M cost per MWh has been rising, not declining, in ANE-ES's recent reporting [#A]

  • Pricing assumption: As noted in §5 — power price recovery is the primary margin driver, not cost reduction [#J]

  • Mix assumption: Improving geographic/technology mix (more contracted international, less merchant Spain) assumed to reduce revenue volatility; this is credible if execution follows pipeline [#J]

  • Operating leverage: Limited operating leverage on EBITDA (already ~78% margin); leverage is primarily financial (interest costs) rather than operational; the operating leverage story is more a financial deleveraging story — EBITDA/interest coverage expanding as EBITDA grows and debt is refinanced [#J]

  • Fixed cost: D&A and interest are both fixed-cost dominant — D&A ~€300–350m pa (FY25E); interest expense ~€350–400m pa (FY25E); together these consume ~€650–750m of ~€1.45bn EBITDA, leaving ~€700–800m for PBT before tax [#A]

  • One-off vs structural: FY24 included ~€50–80m of one-off impairments (US project delays, Australian wind farm commissioning slippage); consensus models these as non-recurring in FY25 — if execution issues persist, the one-off characterisation may be incorrect [#J]

  • Margin upgrade/downgrade: EBIT margin has been revised down ~300–400bp on FY25E over the L12M cycle; EBITDA margin has been more resilient (cut ~50–100bp only) — divergence driven by D&A creep [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The net margin expansion story (FY25→FY27) is entirely dependent on simultaneous achievement of: (a) power price recovery, (b) no further D&A step-ups, and (c) debt refinancing at lower spreads; the probability of all three materialising on schedule is lower than consensus implies, and a miss on any one of them materially dents the FY27 EPS target [#J]

§7 — Narrative + Market Story

  • J-front verdict: Consensus narrative is a coherent but increasingly tested "trough-to-recovery" story; the bear narrative has gained empirical traction over L12M and is no longer purely hypothetical [#J]

  • Bull case: The dominant bull narrative across 8 Buy-rated brokers runs as follows: ANE-ES is a high-quality renewable generator temporarily depressed by a combination of trough Iberian power prices, peak capex cycle, and post-IPO overhang from parent Acciona's balance sheet; as 2.5–3GW of pipeline comes online in FY25–27, EBITDA grows through the leverage peak and free cash flow inflects positively; re-rating to 12–14× EV/EBITDA (currently ~10× on depressed EBITDA) produces 40–60% total return [#J]

  • The bull case further cites the parent Acciona's strategic commitment to ANE-ES as a flagship renewable vehicle and the company's track record of permitting and construction execution (pre-2024)

  • Bear case: The Hold/Underweight bloc (Goldman, Deutsche Bank, UBS, SocGen, Barclays) articulates: ANE-ES is structurally over-leveraged for a company with 30–35% merchant power price exposure; Iberian power prices are structurally depressed (hydro surplus + low carbon), not cyclically so; execution slippage in FY24–25 reveals project management risks that prior consensus underweighted; parent Acciona's own balance sheet stress creates dividend and governance risks [#J]

  • Barclays specifically argues the stock is mispriced as a utility (defensive cash flows) when it should be priced as a leveraged merchant energy company

  • Bullish theme — contracted capacity: Seven of eight Buy brokers lead with contracted/PPA-backed capacity as the primary defensive anchor — ~65–70% of generation is contracted for the near years [#J]

  • Bullish theme — energy transition pipeline: European offshore and international pipeline cited by all 12 active brokers as the medium-term growth engine; Spain, Poland, Italy, and US are the most-cited markets [#J]

  • Bullish theme — parent support: Acciona parent's €100–200m equity commitment optionality and strategic alignment are cited by Citi, BofA, and Santander as backstops against downside scenarios [#J]

  • Sceptical theme — leverage overhang: Net debt/EBITDA above 4.0× is the #1 concern across the sceptical bloc; Deutsche Bank and Goldman cite this as the primary reason for their Hold [#A]

  • Sceptical theme — Iberian power price: SocGen and Barclays argue Spain power prices are structurally depressed by hydro and nuclear economics, not a transitory cycle [#J]

  • Sceptical theme — execution risk: Goldman and Deutsche Bank cite the FY24 project slippage in Australia and the US as evidence of a capability overhang [#J]

  • Narrative shift L12M: The tone has shifted from "buying the dip" (H1-25, most brokers) to "waiting for catalysts to confirm recovery" (H2-25/early-26); a subtle but meaningful shift in conviction language across even the Buy bloc [#J]

  • Near-term vs medium-term focus: Buy brokers are increasingly medium-term focused (FY26–27 re-rating thesis); sceptical brokers are increasingly near-term focused (FY25 earnings risk, leverage covenant, Q1-26 test) [#J]

  • Original insight: Limited original insight observed — most broker notes recycle the Acciona group's CMD framing; Barclays' initiation (Dec-25) and Goldman's Aug-25 downgrade note are the two pieces of research showing genuine independent analysis [#J]

  • Management echo: Citi, BofA, Santander, and BBVA notes show high management-echo characteristics — language tracks CMD presentations closely with limited independent stress-testing of assumptions [#J]

  • Narrative credibility: The bull narrative is internally consistent but requires multiple simultaneous positive outcomes; the bear narrative requires only one or two negatives to materialise [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bull narrative's key vulnerability is its implicit assumption that management's FY26–27 MW commissioning schedule will be achieved despite FY24's slippage; if FY25 shows another year of delayed commissioning, the "peak capex cycle will pass" thesis loses its anchoring timeline and the re-rating trigger recedes indefinitely [#J]
