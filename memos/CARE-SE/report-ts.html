Section 3 – What the company does 17 findings

Section summary
  1. Careium occupies a highly defensive, consolidated niche in the European telecare market, where long-term municipal contracts and high switching costs generate resilient, non-cyclical recurring revenues.

Product offerings and market footprint

1.
Resilient service-led split: Careium is fundamentally a services business, with 73 percent of its revenue generated from recurring subscription fees for alarm monitoring, connectivity, hardware rental, installation, and field response
2.
Integrated hardware offering: The remaining 27 percent of revenue is generated from transactional product sales, consisting of Careium's proprietary carephones, smart fall detectors, and active behavior monitoring sensors
3.
Digital Nordic leadership: The Nordic region accounts for 42 percent of sales, characterized by a highly digitalized telecare market where Careium is the dominant provider of connected home safety systems
4.
Expanding net connections: Careium expanded its active connection base to 340,100 at the end of Q1 2026, marking a consecutive quarter of expansion after a prolonged decline from late 2022
5.
Highly predictable procurement: Customer relationships are exceptionally stable and non-cyclical, as public procurement tenders with multi-year durations dominate customer acquisition, ensuring predictable long-term funding streams
6.
Structural demographic expansion: The addressable market is expanding steadily, driven by the structural aging of European populations, which increases the demand for home-based care compared to costly nursing homes
7.
Stable customer base: The connection base is highly loyal, with low churn rates due to the essential, life-saving nature of the telecare monitoring services provided
8.
Differentiating mobile alarms: Careium has expanded into the private pay sector with mobile social alarms like Abby, targeting active seniors who require safety both inside and outside the home

Value chain dynamics and competitive advantages

9.
Strong value position: Careium occupies a highly favored position in the telecare value chain by providing integrated end-to-end systems that combine proprietary hardware devices with cloud-based response platform software
10.
Scalable cloud architecture: The company leverages its unified cloud platform to scale connections across multiple regions without a linear increase in monitoring center headcount, driving substantial operating leverage
11.
Low customer concentration: Customer concentration is extremely low and well-diversified, with no single municipal or public healthcare customer accounting for more than 10 percent of Careium's total net sales
12.
High switching costs: Competitive advantages are supported by high customer switching costs, as municipal contracts require complex, long-term technological integrations with local emergency services and hospital networks
13.
Steady industry consolidation: The European telecare sector remains highly fragmented, allowing market leaders like Careium to selectively acquire smaller regional operators to expand contract density and geographic footprint
14.
Differentiating proactive technology: Careium is shifting its competitive focus toward proactive and preventative care solutions, combining hardware and software to actively detect behavioral changes and prevent expensive hospitalizations
15.
Strict supplier screening: Supply chain environmental and social risks are mitigated by strict ESG policies for suppliers, with compliance verified by third-party auditors
16.
Other side of the story: Careium's hardware manufacturing exposure creates a vulnerability to low-cost Asian competitors, which could erode product gross margins if municipalities unbundle hardware procurement from long-term monitoring service contracts

Section 5 – The possible investment case 12 findings

Section summary
  1. The investment case for Careium strengthens as the company leverages easy comparables, scales its cloud monitoring software, and captures high-margin organic product sales from the UK's digital network transition.

Key growth drivers and execution paths

1.
Easing growth comparables: The primary near-term driver is extremely easy year-over-year comparables in 2026, as Careium no longer faces the lumpy financial lease contract comps that distorted 2025 growth
2.
Catalytic UK transition: The second driver is the UK digital transition, which legally mandates the replacement of old analog carephone hardware with new digital models by local authorities
3.
Highly plausible timeline: This UK product sales driver carries high plausibility due to the hard network shutdown deadline, though actual deployment remains subject to short-term municipal budget delays
4.
Selective M&A optionality: The third driver is complementary M&A to consolidate market share, with management actively communicating its intention to evaluate regional acquisitions to increase contract density
5.
Challenging historic integration: The primary risk to the M&A driver is integration failure, as seen in historical UK acquisitions that required extensive restructuring before achieving target profitability
6.
Framework agreement catalyst: The finalization of Sweden's ADDA framework agreement in mid-2026 represents a major growth catalyst, allowing Careium to bid for large-scale municipal contracts

Software scaling and operational leverage

7.
Expanding software monetization: The fourth driver is the expansion of premium software services, allowing Careium to monetize enhanced data capabilities and proactive health-monitoring software applications
8.
Medium plausibility adoption: This software driver carries medium plausibility because municipal customers are historically slow to adopt new technologies, but it offers substantial incremental operating margins
9.
Independent driver execution: The forward earnings trajectory does not require all drivers to fire together, as the non-discretionary UK digital transition alone can drive a 20 percent EBIT compound growth rate
10.
High operating leverage: The scalability of Careium's cloud platform means that any increase in connections directly drives margin expansion, with EBIT margins projected to reach 10 percent by 2028
11.
Other side of the story: The investment case would collapse if UK local authorities indefinitely delay digital hardware rollouts, leaving Careium with elevated sales overhead without the corresponding high-margin product sales

Section 7 – Key risks and the other side 8 findings

Section summary
  1. Careium faces key risks surrounding municipal budget delays and product liabilities, though the bear case is mitigated by non-discretionary demographic demand and long-term contract lock-ins.

Core vulnerabilities and early warnings

1.
Prolonged Sweden delays: The most immediate risk is procurement delays in Sweden, as municipalities postpone new contract awards until the ADDA 2025 framework agreement is finalized
2.
Severe product liability: Careium is vulnerable to product or platform failure, as any system outage could disrupt emergency alarm response, causing severe reputational damage and legal liabilities
3.
Vulnerable supply chain: The company's supply chain is highly exposed to geopolitical and trade disruptions, as its proprietary carephone hardware is primarily manufactured in China
4.
Deceptive false friend: Rapid product sales growth in new markets like Germany represents a potential false friend, as it can mask margin deterioration if these sales carry heavy customer acquisition costs
5.
Critical early warning: Investors should closely monitor quarterly net connection net additions as an early warning signal, which must remain positive to support high-margin service sales
6.
Favorable short interest: There is currently no active published short-seller report or abnormally elevated short interest in Careium's shares on the Stockholm exchange
7.
Other side of the story: The bear case is heavily overstated because telecare is a non-discretionary service, and public municipal budgets remain highly resilient despite broader macroeconomic headwinds
