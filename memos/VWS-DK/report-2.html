  • Sceptical bullet: The benign competitive environment is ENTIRELY contingent on Siemens Gamesa remaining distressed and Chinese OEMs remaining politically excluded from Western markets; either assumption is a single-point-of-failure; Siemens Gamesa has EUR 4bn+ of backstop capital and every incentive to recover; Chinese OEMs are entering tender processes in Southern Europe (Portugal, Greece) where political barriers are lower; the window of rational pricing may be narrower than consensus assumes [#J]

§8 — Value Chain: Disruption

J-front verdict: Near-term disruption risk is low; medium-term risk from Chinese technology convergence and alternative storage/generation substitution is real but not priced or near-term relevant [#J]

  • Technology disruption: No disruptive wind turbine technology in commercial development; incremental innovation (larger rotors, taller towers, smarter SCADA systems) favours incumbents with manufacturing scale [#A]

  • Disruption pace: Wind turbine technology advancement is evolutionary, not revolutionary; platform cycles run 5–7 years; Vestas V172 class is competitive on LCOE with any current Western alternative

  • Adoption curve: Blade length scaling (rotor diameter inflation) is the primary technological trend; Vestas V172 at 7MW class is current market leader; GE Vernova's Cypress platform in similar class [#A]

  • New entrant — innovative: No credible innovative new entrant identified in the onshore wind OEM space; capital requirements and certification timelines are prohibitive BtE for new entrants [#I]

  • New entrant — traditional: Chinese OEMs (Goldwind 6.x MW platform, Envision EN-193/6.x) are converging on Western turbine specifications; if Western market access widens, technology gap narrows [#A]

  • Substitute — solar + storage: Utility-scale solar LCOE has fallen below onshore wind LCOE in sun-belt regions; this is a genuine substitution threat in Southern Europe, MENA, India; Vestas has no solar exposure [#J]

  • Transmission mechanism: If solar LCOE continues to fall, developers in sun-belt markets will switch; Vestas's addressable market in these geographies could structurally shrink

  • Incumbent response: Vestas has NOT diversified into solar or storage — strategy is pure-play wind; this is coherent for now but a structural concentration risk if wind's share of new build capacity declines vs solar [#J]

  • Disruption cost: Digitisation of turbine performance (predictive maintenance, remote monitoring) is an area where Vestas has invested; digital service capability creates a modest disruption-from-below moat [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: Vestas's pure-play wind positioning is currently fashionable given wind's policy tailwind; the same pure-play positioning was a liability in 2020–22 when wind costs blew out while solar continued to fall; if solar cost curves continue to outpace wind, Vestas has no product adjacency to pivot into — the disruption cost is permanent market share loss to a substitute, not a new entrant [#J]

§9 — Value Chain: Political and Regulatory

J-front verdict: The regulatory environment is the most unambiguous structural tailwind in Vestas's change-force matrix; EU and US policy commitments are materially unprecedented in scale [#J]

  • Regulatory shift — EU: EU Green Deal + REPowerEU mandate 42.5% renewable share by 2030; EU Wind Power Package (2023) specifically addressed permitting acceleration as the primary bottleneck [#A]

  • Magnitude: EU Wind Power Package commits member states to streamlined permit timelines; maximum 2-year permitting target for repowering; 3.5 years for new sites; not yet fully implemented but directionally clear

  • Regulatory shift — US: IRA Production Tax Credit (USD 26/MWh for wind) and Investment Tax Credit create a ~50% improvement in wind project economics vs pre-IRA baseline [#A]

  • Political actor: Trump administration has expressed hostility to wind energy; executive orders in 2025 paused offshore leasing and attempted IRA rollback; IRA rollback faces significant Congressional opposition given manufacturing jobs in Republican districts

  • Geography — Germany: Vestas's largest single market; German Onshore Wind Expansion Act (2023) tripled annual installation targets; permitting simplification in practice is lagging legislation [#A]

  • Tariff: No direct tariffs on wind turbines in EU or US currently; potential tariff on Chinese components is a risk (EU anti-subsidy investigation into Chinese wind equipment ongoing as of 2024–25) [#I]

  • Subsidy: Green hydrogen policy creates indirect demand for additional wind capacity for electrolysis; not yet material to near-term orders [#I]

  • Trade policy: US "Buy American" provisions in IRA favour Vestas's Colorado manufacturing vs Chinese OEMs; this is a structural political moat in the US market [#J]

  • Compliance cost: EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive adds reporting burden; immaterial to EBIT [#I]

  • Environmental regulation: Biodiversity protection rules in some EU jurisdictions (notably Germany's bat/bird habitat protections) are the primary source of permit delays; structural, not cyclical [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The EU regulatory acceleration narrative has been a recurring theme since 2019 and permitting timelines have improved only modestly; structural opposition from local communities (NIMBYism) and biodiversity rules cannot be legislated away; US IRA political risk is non-trivial with an openly hostile executive branch; the regulatory tailwind is real but the transmission from policy target to installed MW is consistently slower than policy implies [#J]

§10 — Value Chain: Supply Side

J-front verdict: Supply chain normalisation is a genuine earnings tailwind for 2024–26 after the 2021–23 cost blowout; concentration risks remain in speciality materials [#J]

  • Supplier economics: Steel, fibreglass, epoxy, rare earth magnets are the primary cost inputs; all experienced exceptional cost inflation in 2021–22; as of 2024, steel and fibreglass costs have broadly normalised [#A]

  • Peer anchor: GE Vernova guided to >200bps gross margin improvement in 2024 attributable to supply chain normalisation — consistent with Vestas's margin recovery narrative

  • Concentration: Rare earth supply (neodymium for permanent magnets in direct-drive variants) concentrated in China (~90% of global supply); Vestas's geared turbines use less rare earth than competitors' direct-drive designs — a structural supply-risk advantage [#J]

  • Relationship: Vestas has historically maintained preferred supplier relationships and multi-year supply agreements; during 2021–22, these were insufficient to prevent spot-price cost blowouts on short-cycle components [#A]

  • Supply risk: Blade manufacturing — Vestas insources blade production at own factories; insourcing is a BtE but also concentrates operational risk; the Isle of Wight closure (2022) was a supply-side rationalisation [#I]

  • Vertical integration: Higher than peers for blades; outsourced for nacelle electronics, bearings, gearboxes; selective vertical integration in highest-cost, highest-quality-risk components [#A]

  • Backward integration: No material backward integration into materials; exposed to commodity price cycles; steel represents ~20–25% of turbine material cost [#I]

  • Supplier health: Major bearing suppliers (SKF, Timken) and gearbox suppliers (ZF, Moventas) are financially healthy; no near-term supply chain vulnerability identified [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet: Supply chain normalisation is a one-time margin tailwind, not a structural improvement; once normalisation is fully priced into the P&L (~2025–26), the next margin improvement lever must come from pricing or mix, both of which are harder to sustain; if commodity prices re-inflate (energy transition itself is materially commodity-intensive), Vestas's fixed-price contract exposure re-creates the same loss dynamic as 2021–22 [#J]

  • Cycle stage: Current assessment is early-to-mid upswing; FIDs recovering from 2023 trough; order intake leading indicators (record 2024 intake) confirm 2025–26 revenue visibility [#J]

  • Peer anchor: GE Vernova and Nordex both reported improving order intake in 2024, consistent with sector-wide cycle upturn

  • Predictability: Service segment revenue (~35% of total) is highly predictable (10–20 year O&M contracts); turbine segment revenue is moderately predictable (12–24 month backlog); group revenue predictability improving vs 2019–21 cycle [#A]

  • Cycle length: Global wind installation cycles run approximately 4–7 years peak-to-trough; current upswing from 2023 trough is plausibly 2024–2029 given policy commitments [#A]

  • Trough behaviour: 2022–23 trough was unusually severe due to simultaneous cost inflation + rate rise + supply chain dislocation; this confluence is unlikely to repeat identically but individual components can recur [#J]

  • Demand pulse: Policy-driven demand has compressed the amplitude of cyclical troughs compared to pre-2015 cycles; but it has NOT eliminated cyclicality — it has shifted the driver from pure power price cycles to policy + power price + rate cycles [#A]

  • Inventory cycle: Vestas does not hold significant turbine inventory (assembled-to-order model); working capital cycle is driven by construction milestones rather than inventory build [#I]

  • Capex cycle: The current capex/investment cycle for major turbine platforms (V172 etc.) is largely complete; entering a harvest phase that typically supports FCF generation [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: "Early-to-mid upswing" is the most optimistic cycle-stage characterisation for a company whose order intake just hit records; record order intake has historically marked mid-to-late cycle more often than early cycle; the 2024 order intake records should trigger demand-pulse caution per right-to-left thinking — what will actually install in 2026–27 from this backlog, at what margins, and what does that backlog reveal about 2028+ demand once the current policy impulse normalises?
