§2 — D1 Deep Dive: North America Capex Normalisation

Make-or-break status: North America contributes approximately 41% of Ericsson group revenue and is the single largest swing variable in the earnings model; no other driver comes close in impact magnitude over a 24-month horizon. #J

Current state: Q1 2026 NA revenue declined mid-single-digit YoY against a particularly strong Q1 2025 comparative. Management explicitly guided that "Q1 trajectory is probably similar to what should be expected for the year" — this is a de facto profit warning on NA for FY2026. #I

Verizon: the hardest data point: Verizon guided FY2026 capex of $16.0–16.5bn, representing a ~$4bn YoY reduction. The C-band 5G coverage rollout (300 million POPs) is essentially complete, removing the dominant spend impulse of the 2021–2023 cycle. This is not a soft signal — it is public guidance from the largest US carrier. #I

AT&T floor mechanism: The $14bn five-year O-RAN contract awarded to Ericsson by AT&T provides a hard revenue floor not fully captured in simple run-rate models. Annualised, this is approximately SEK 28–30bn of NA revenue with multi-year contractual backing. The floor character of this contract de-risks the extreme NA collapse scenario. #A

T-Mobile dynamics: T-Mobile has been the most aggressive share-gainer in US mobile, which creates sustained competitive pressure on AT&T and Verizon to invest in network quality differentiation. This dynamic is an underappreciated floor on total NA RAN capex — operators cannot afford persistent network quality gaps vs T-Mobile in a contract renewal market. #A

5G Advanced refresh timing: Release 18/19 features (5G Advanced) enable AI-native RAN capabilities including beam management AI, energy optimisation, and new spectrum use cases. Operator procurement for 5G Advanced equipment is expected to begin 2027–2028. This refresh does not save 2026 but it defines the next capex impulse and provides the basis for modelling NA recovery. #A

Historical cycle context: The 2014–2016 US RAN downcycle following LTE densification ran approximately 2.5 years before 4G small-cell densification restarted spending. The current 5G primary build began normalising from 2022–2023 — meaning the downcycle is already 2+ years old. Base effect alone supports H2 2026 relative improvement. #A

Revenue base effect: Ericsson's NA revenue peaked at approximately SEK 95–105bn equivalent in 2022 at the C-band deployment peak. Current run-rate implies a meaningful step-down. A flat-to-slightly-declining 2026 creates a substantially easier 2027 comparison, meaning even modest spending recovery would produce double-digit YoY reported NA growth in 2027. #A

Market share dynamics: Ericsson holds approximately 40–45% of the non-Huawei, non-ZTE NA RAN market. Nokia is the primary domestic competitor; Samsung is a smaller but growing challenger. Share is broadly stable, meaning revenue trajectory is a function of market volume, not competitive erosion. #A

⚡ RARE: Deutsche Bank quantifies Ericsson's NA share post-AT&T contract at >60% of the addressable market — if accurate, this concentration creates a "floor plus upside" rather than "only way is down" dynamic, since at-scale share is already locked into long-term contracts that continue to execute regardless of new build activity. #J

FWA as partial offset: Fixed Wireless Access infrastructure spending by US carriers is a growing sub-category. Jio's success as the world's largest FWA provider with 9.5 million connections (Q3 2025) is driving US operator interest; Verizon and T-Mobile are both investing in FWA capacity additions that partially offset traditional macro-RAN softness. #A

Scenario framing:
- Bear case: NA capex remains depressed through 2027 (operators use 5G Advanced as software-only upgrade); NA revenue declines further 5–8% in 2027 on top of 2026 decline; EBITA margin stays 12–14%.
- Base case: NA finds floor in H2 2026; mid-single-digit organic recovery in 2027 driven by base effect + FWA + early 5G Advanced; EBITA margin 15–16%.
- Bull case: AT&T accelerates Open RAN deployment + T-Mobile triggers competitive capex response from Verizon; NA grows 5–10% in 2027; EBITA margin 17–18%; SEK also weakens to amplify reported numbers.

❌ Duration risk: Management's comfort with "Q1 trajectory repeating through the year" pre-guides a full-year NA decline; investors modelling even flat NA for H2 2026 are likely too optimistic. The consensus treatment of 2026 as a trough year may be premature by 6–12 months. #J

Single most important NA tracker: Verizon's Q2 and Q3 2026 capex guidance updates. Any upward revision vs current $16.0–16.5bn guidance would be the single highest-signal data point for NA recovery confirmation. #J

§3 — D2 Deep Dive: Networks Gross Margin

Quality signal verdict: Networks gross margin above 49% sustained through a meaningful hardware downcycle is the most important evidence of structural — not cyclical — quality improvement. Peer Nokia runs 38–42% group gross margin. The gap has widened during the downcycle, not narrowed. #J

Current state: Networks segment gross margin was 50.4% in Q1 2026. Management guided Q2 2026 Networks adjusted gross margin of 49–51%, confirming the corridor is intact. The rolling 4-quarter adjusted gross margin for Networks reached 49.9% at end-FY2025. #I

Four drivers of margin improvement: (1) Product mix shift — hardware declining as % of revenue, software and managed services growing; (2) Contract discipline — exit from sub-scale geographies and unprofitable turnkey contracts post-2022; (3) Manufacturing rationalisation — global footprint reduction and supply chain simplification; (4) Headcount — ~11,000 roles cut in 2023 with recurring cost savings flowing through P&L. #A

Semiconductor resilience: DNB Carnegie quantified that even a tripling of memory chip costs would create only a -75bps Networks gross margin impact. This extreme stress scenario being bounded is exceptional resilience for a hardware-adjacent vendor and provides high confidence in the 49–51% corridor being structurally durable. #A

Restructuring payback timeline: FY2026 restructuring charges are front-loaded ("a fairly large part" in Q1 per CFO Sandström). Operating expenses ex-restructuring dropped SEK 2bn YoY in Q1 2026 (partly FX, partly genuine savings). If 2026 charges deliver visible run-rate savings from FY2027, the H2 2026 earnings progression sets up well for a 2027 re-rating thesis. #A

Group EBITA target dependency: The 15–18% adjusted EBITA margin for FY2026 requires meaningful H2 step-up from 11.3% in Q1. Networks segment EBITA margin (rolling 4Q) was 20.3% at end-FY2025 — already at target. The drag is coming from CSS and Enterprise, not Networks. This matters because it means Networks margin is not the variable that closes the gap to target — CSS delivery is. #A

Deleverage risk from NA volume: The primary threat to the 49–51% corridor is revenue volume deleverage in Networks: software licensing has high incremental margins but the fixed cost base of the RAN R&D organisation and manufacturing network creates operating leverage that cuts both ways. A sustained NA revenue decline of >10% would test the corridor's lower bound. #A

Peer comparison: Nokia's Networks gross margin is structurally lower (~38–42%) partly due to different product mix (Nokia's Networks segment includes fixed access equipment which carries lower margins) and partly due to less aggressive contract discipline historically. Ericsson's demonstrated ability to hold 50%+ Networks gross margin is a genuine peer-relative advantage. #A

❌ H2 concentration risk: Historical revenue seasonality is Q4-heavy — Q4 is typically 25–30% of full-year group revenue vs Q1 at ~20%. The 15–18% group EBITA target requires this seasonality to materialise as expected; if Q4 2026 disappoints on revenue timing (delayed deliveries, project slippage), the full-year target misses and consensus resets. #J

Conviction level on D2: High. The Networks gross margin story has the most data support, the least execution risk, and the clearest peer comparison. It is the anchor of the investment case — without it, the quality-cyclical framing collapses. #J

Product leadership underpinning: Ericsson has ranked #1 in ABI Research RAN Automation Platform competitive assessment for six consecutive years; Frost & Sullivan 5G Infrastructure recognition for six consecutive years. This sustained leadership provides pricing power that underpins margin sustainability beyond the current cycle. #I

§4 — D3 Deep Dive: Cloud Software & Services

CSS inflection verdict: CSS EBIT margin swung from -0.3% (FY2023) to 9.6% (FY2025) — this is the most significant P&L transformation in the Ericsson story and justifies the quality-cyclical upgrade in archetype framing vs a pure hardware vendor. #J

Q4 2025 organic growth: CSS achieved 12% organic revenue growth in Q4 2025, contributing to nine consecutive quarters of YoY adjusted EBITA margin expansion at group level. The trajectory is real and data-supported. #I

CSS revenue composition: The segment encompasses 5G Core, OSS/BSS, managed services, and cloud-native network functions — all of which benefit from the operator transition to Standalone 5G architectures. The architecture shift is structural, not discretionary. #I

5G SA transition mechanism: The move from Non-Standalone to Standalone 5G is a software-rich network upgrade. It requires 5G Core replacement, network slicing tooling, and new OSS/BSS capability — all flowing through CSS rather than Networks hardware. As of Q3 2025, 72 5G SA core networks were deployed globally (Dell'Oro data) in a market growing +14% YoY. #A

Anchor contract evidence: Airtel's multi-billion dollar 5G Core partnership with Ericsson (February 2025) for SA evolution is representative of the contract quality shift — multi-year duration, software-heavy structure, operator-driven demand rather than vendor push. #I

Managed services recurring dynamic: Operators rationalising engineering headcount are increasingly outsourcing network management to vendors. Ericsson's managed services contracts are multi-year with switching costs embedded in institutional knowledge transfer. This creates revenue durability different in character from hardware project cycles. #A

CSS margin ceiling — DNB Carnegie: DNB projects CSS adjusted EBITA margins to stabilise at 5–10% through 2028, which is below Networks margins and below the group average target. The implication: CSS helps close the group EBITA gap but does not become the dominant margin engine within the investment horizon. This is a more conservative ceiling than [C]'s "group-average returns by 2027–2028" framing. #A

❌ Multi-axis execution requirement: CSS margins reaching 9.6% required simultaneous improvement across 5G Core growth, managed services efficiency, and OSS/BSS contract wins. Sustaining double-digit organic growth requires all three to continue — any sub-segment stalling (e.g., managed services pricing pressure from cloud-native competitors) would decelerate the CSS trajectory. #J

Competitive threats: Cloud-native network vendors (Mavenir, Rakuten Symphony), hyperscalers (AWS Wavelength, Microsoft Azure for Operators), and Nokia compete for overlapping CSS opportunity. Unlike RAN hardware where Ericsson/Nokia/Huawei dominate at 96%+ share, CSS competitive dynamics are more fluid with lower switching barriers in software than in hardware. #A

Network API upside (non-base-case): GSMA Open Gateway contracts expose carrier network capabilities (location, QoS, edge compute) to enterprise developers. Early contracts are small but the addressable market is large. This is embedded optionality within CSS — not in base case numbers but not zero probability either. #A

CSS verdict for ESA stage: CSS improvement is real and the mechanism is well-understood. The caution is that the 12% Q4 2025 organic growth represents early-stage inflection, not steady-state cruising speed; sustaining it through 2026–2027 requires continued 5G SA contract wins in a market that is still early in the SA transition globally. #J

§5 — D5 Deep Dive: IPR Licensing

Floor mechanism: IPR licensing at SEK 14.5bn FY2025 represents approximately 7–8% of group revenue but carries near-100% EBITA margin flow-through, making it disproportionately important to group profitability. Loss of this floor would require ~3–4 percentage points of additional operational EBITA margin to compensate — equivalent to removing the entire CSS margin contribution. #J

IPR growth track record: Revenue has grown from approximately SEK 10bn (2020) to SEK 14.5bn (2025) at approximately 7–8% CAGR while equipment revenues declined. This decoupling — licensing compounding while hardware softens — demonstrates genuine portfolio resilience and creates a natural hedge against the hardware cycle. #I

⚡ RARE: Ericsson's IPR revenue has grown at 7–8% CAGR over 2020–2025 while the company simultaneously lost network equipment revenue — this degree of decoupling between patent licensing and hardware cycles is genuinely rare among capital equipment vendors and represents a compounding earnings floor not typically seen in this sector. #J

Quarterly lumpiness: IPR revenues are lumpy due to retroactive settlements and new agreements. Q2 2025 was elevated at SEK 4.9bn (settlement-driven) while Q3–Q4 2025 averaged SEK 3.2bn. Q1 2026 run-rate annualises to approximately SEK 13bn (DNB Carnegie), below the FY2025 total of SEK 14.5bn — the difference reflects the settlement lumpiness rather than structural deterioration. #I

Chinese OEM contract expiry — live binary: A contract with a Chinese smartphone vendor expired at end-2025. DNB Carnegie elevates IPR to its #3 ranked driver specifically because of this near-term renegotiation binary. The outcome will likely be resolved in 2026 but has not been disclosed. Upside: renewal at higher rates reflecting expanded 5G device volumes. Downside: protracted dispute, arbitration, or below-rate settlement that reduces FY2026 IPR revenue vs the SEK 14.5bn FY2025 base. #J

5G SEP portfolio strength: LexisNexis (January 2026) ranks Ericsson among the top holders of 5G standard-essential patents globally. The $15bn annual 5G licensing market estimate positions Ericsson's SEK 14.5bn as approximately 20–22% market share — consistent with its declared SEP portfolio position and a reasonable floor assumption for renewal negotiations. #A

Automotive/IoT expansion via Avanci: The Avanci platform's expansion into 5G automotive licensing offers a pathway to grow IPR revenue toward the upper end of the SEK 14–15bn range through new device categories. Automotive 5G penetration is in early innings — this is incremental upside not in the base case but with increasing plausibility as OEMs adopt 5G connectivity. #A

Huawei cross-licensing interaction: Huawei is both a competitor in RAN and a significant cross-licensee in the patent domain. Any geopolitical resolution affecting Huawei's market access could alter cross-licensing economics. In exclusion scenarios, Ericsson's net position is likely positive — Huawei loses some leverage in negotiations while remaining obligated to pay for SEPs used in its global device shipments. #A

IPR verdict for ESA stage: IPR is the most reliable earnings component in the portfolio — contractually backed, structurally growing, and decoupled from hardware cycles. The Chinese OEM contract expiry is the one near-term risk that needs monitoring. Resolution by mid-2026 would be a positive catalyst; protracted dispute would be a modest earnings headwind, not a thesis-breaker. #J

§6 — D4 + D6 + D7 + D8: Supporting and Optionality Drivers

D4 — FX Translation (SEK vs USD/EUR)

FX masking verdict: Q1 2026 reported revenue -10% YoY vs +6% organic growth — the SEK 7.8bn headwind is the dominant explanation for the miss vs analyst consensus of SEK 51bn+. Reported numbers are systematically misleading investors on business trajectory. #J

Structural exposure: Ericsson bills approximately 70–75% of revenues in non-SEK currencies (primarily USD, EUR) while the cost base is more SEK-anchored. A stronger SEK is structurally margin-negative in reported terms. The Riksbank's rate path and US Fed divergence are the primary macro drivers of this exposure. #I

Asymmetric upside framing: FX stabilisation — not further SEK depreciation, merely stabilisation — would immediately improve reported metrics without any underlying business change. This creates an asymmetric setup where SEK weakening is a free earnings upgrade while SEK strengthening from here has diminishing incremental impact (already at multi-year highs). #J

Investor communication challenge: Ericsson provides guidance in SEK terms; the gap between organic guidance and reported outcome is substantial. US-listed investors (NASDAQ ADR) face materially different reported trajectory from Stockholm-listed investors for the same underlying business — suppressing cross-border buying interest in the current FX environment. #A

❌ FX is not directionally controllable: Hedging covers near-term exposures only; structural translation effects are unhedged. If the SEK strengthens further due to Riksbank policy divergence or risk-off flows, even strong organic delivery will continue to appear weak in reported P&L. #J

D4 tracker: EUR/SEK and USD/SEK rates. Current levels embed a substantial organic-to-reported gap; any move toward USD/SEK 11+ would be a reported-number tailwind for an unchanged organic business. #I

D6 — India 5G Phase 2 + FWA

India verdict: India is a positive partial offset to NA weakness but not a step-change driver — the initial pan-India coverage rollout is substantially complete and the post-rollout normalisation is an active headwind before the densification/SA upgrade cycle restarts. #J

Contract floor: Ericsson holds multi-billion dollar multi-year contracts with both Jio and Airtel. Airtel's December 2024 renewal ($1.2bn for 4G/5G equipment alongside Nokia and Samsung) and the February 2025 5G Core partnership provide revenue floor for 2025–2027. #I

FWA structural growth: Jio reached 9.5 million FWA connections (world's largest FWA provider by Q3 2025); combined Jio + Airtel FWA at 12 million by September 2025. Continued FWA growth in India requires RAN capacity additions — this is a recurring demand driver independent of new 5G SA build. #A

Deutsche Bank caution: Deutsche Bank projects moderate India capex decline in 2026 after the 2023 5G peak — slightly more cautious than [C]'s "steady-state positive" view. The [AS] data point that India's share of Ericsson sales increased from 5% to 8% sequentially in Q1 2026 suggests renewed activity, which partially contradicts the cautious DB view. The truth is likely somewhere between the two: renewed but volatile post-rollout spending. #A

❌ Capex digestion risk: Indian operators Jio and Airtel are both post-peak in primary 5G coverage investment. A 12–18 month digestion period before the next spending impulse (SA upgrade, densification, new spectrum) is the base-case risk. Not a thesis-breaker but a timing hedge on India contribution. #J

D7 — AI-Native RAN Leadership + Defence/Mission-Critical

Optionality verdict: AI-native networks and defence are genuine long-term growth vectors with two distinct mechanisms to share price: (1) near-term premium pricing defence vs Nokia/Samsung in RAN tenders; (2) longer-dated new TAM expansion in network software and government-grade communications. Neither is a 24-month earnings driver. #J

AI-RAN competitive fork: Nokia has partnered with Nvidia for AI-RAN development; Ericsson is sticking to custom silicon and internal AI development. This strategic fork creates a near-term narrative disadvantage for Ericsson in investor conversations about AI exposure — regardless of which approach is technically superior, Nokia captures AI headline risk-on flows more directly. #A

Defence structural tailwind: European NATO members are committing to higher defence budgets post-Ukraine and post-US posture reassessment. Government-grade communications infrastructure (secure, resilient, 3GPP-based MCPTT/MCPTT platforms) is a procurement priority. Ericsson CEO explicitly targeted defence R&D investment increase in 2026. #A

Defence TAM: The mission-critical communications market is estimated at $34bn TAM by 2030, growing at ~10.8% CAGR. Ericsson competes with legacy TETRA providers and Motorola Solutions. Revenue contribution is 2–3 years from meaningful scale. #I

❌ Vonage precedent overhang: The $6.2bn Vonage acquisition was substantially written down. Any signals of large acquisitive moves into adjacent markets (AI software, defence) will face heightened investor scepticism on capital allocation discipline — constraining strategic optionality precisely when adjacent markets require investment. #J

D8 — Huawei Geopolitical Displacement

Asymmetry characterisation: Huawei displacement is low probability in any given 24-month window for new country-level bans but, when displacement events occur, they create step-change revenue opportunities unmodelled in consensus. The relevant question is not "will displacement continue" (it will, gradually) but "is a step-change event imminent" (probably not). #J

Germany quantification: Deutsche Bank cites the July 2024 German BMI ruling (core removal by 2026, antennas by 2029) as creating approximately $1bn annual opportunity for Ericsson/Nokia combined. This is concrete and partially captured in current European revenue run-rate. #I

Market share data: Dell'Oro Q3 2025 shows top-5 vendors at 96% of global RAN revenue — Open RAN challengers have not displaced incumbents despite years of operator experimentation. Huawei's restricted-market share loss flows to Ericsson/Nokia, not to new entrants. #A

SE Asia watch: Thailand, Malaysia, and Indonesia continue mixed-vendor strategies; any government-level Huawei restriction in a large SE Asian market would be an unmodelled upside binary. Vietnam and India have de facto reduced Huawei reliance. #I

❌ Rehabilitation risk: A trade deal or geopolitical reset that partially rehabilitates Huawei in Western markets would remove displacement tailwinds and reintroduce competitive pricing pressure. Not the base case, but a genuine tail risk that is not reflected in the optionality framing most sell-side analysts apply. #J

§7 — Interaction Effects and Scenario Matrix

Driver correlation architecture: The eight drivers are not independent — they interact in ways that amplify both upside and downside. The most important correlation structure is: #A

  • D1 (NA capex) and D2 (Networks margin) are negatively correlated in the downside tail: sustained NA decline creates operating deleverage that will eventually breach the 49–51% Networks gross margin corridor, likely around the 10–12% sustained revenue decline mark.
  • D2 (Networks margin) and D3 (CSS growth) are independently driven but both required for the group EBITA target — they do not substitute for each other.
  • D4 amplifies or masks all other drivers without changing underlying economics; it is a presentation layer, not a fundamental driver, but it matters enormously to investor sentiment and re-rating timing.
  • D5 provides earnings floor independent of D1–D4 correlation — this is its structural value in the portfolio.
  • D7 (AI-RAN) and D8 (Huawei displacement) are low-correlation with near-term drivers and function as portfolio tail optionality.

It is the margin insurance policy in the base and bear cases. #A

H2 2026 as the critical reporting window: Q3 2026 results (October 2026) and Q4 2026 results (January 2027) are the two pivotal data points where the base vs bear scenario distinction becomes observable. An investor entering Ericsson today is buying approximately 6 months of uncertainty before the first clear read on whether H2 delivery is on track. #J

❌ Simultaneous execution dependency: The bull case requires NA floor (D1) + Networks margin hold (D2) + CSS double-digit organic (D3) + IPR renewal (D5) + restructuring delivery — five concurrent conditions, each plausible independently, but the joint probability of all five resolving positively within the same 24-month window is materially lower than any single driver's plausibility score. #J

§8 — ESA Verdict on Driver Quality

Holistic driver quality judgement: The Ericsson driver architecture has three distinct quality tiers: #J

  • Tier 1 (High Quality, High Confidence): D2 (Networks gross margin) and D5 (IPR floor) — both are data-evidenced, structurally grounded, and provide earnings floor regardless of cycle dynamics. These are the quality anchors.
  • Tier 2 (Plausible, Execution-Dependent): D1 (NA capex normalisation) and D3 (CSS organic growth) — both are directionally sound but require 18–24 months of evidence accumulation before confirmation. These are the value realisation drivers.
  • Tier 3 (Optionality, Not Base Case): D4 , D6 (India), D7 (AI/defence), D8 (Huawei) — real but not load-bearing in the 24-month investment thesis.

Single most important tracker: Verizon's capex guidance trajectory. Any upward revision to the current $16.0–16.5bn FY2026 capex guide would be the highest-conviction signal that NA is troughing. Conversely, any further cut would reset the base case to bear. No other data point carries equivalent signal power. #J

Changes vs Triaging assessment:
1. IPR (D5) elevated from #5 to co-equal with D3 in near-term attention priority, driven by the Chinese OEM contract expiry binary that Triaging understated.
2. CSS (D3) execution requirement clarified — the DNB Carnegie ceiling of 5–10% long-term EBITA margin (vs [C]'s more optimistic group-average framing) introduces more realistic margin expectation for the segment.
3. Nokia strategic divergence elevated from a background observation to a valuation ceiling risk — Deutsche Bank's 50% EV/EBITDA premium for Nokia crystallises the re-rating constraint, not just the narrative one.
4. The H2 2026 execution dependency is more acute than Triaging framed — the front-loaded restructuring charges in Q1 create a more pronounced H2 requirement than initial analysis suggested.

Wisdom Library lens — flywheel vs treadmill: Ericsson's networks business has elements of both.
