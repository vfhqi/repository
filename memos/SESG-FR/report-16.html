KEY FINDINGS - Mixed-to-weak operator signal overall: CEO Adel Al-Saleh is a credible turnaround-era hire with genuine telco-restructuring pedigree, but his tenure record at SES is one of strategic proliferation (O3b mPOWER overruns, SES-17 write-down, C-band windfall allocation dispute) more than disciplined compounding

  • CFO is a relative governance bright spot: Sandeep Jalan is financially literate, disclosure-improving, and has tightened non-GAAP presentation since 2022 — but guidance accuracy through the MEO satellite programme remains spotty
  • Senior team depth is adequate but thinned: multiple C-suite departures L36M, notably COO and Chief Strategy Officer, leave SES more CEO-dependent than is comfortable for a business executing a multi-year network modernisation
  • Board composition is formally solid — majority independent, experienced telecoms/infrastructure directors — but director equity stakes are largely symbolic, and the Luxembourg governance regime offers fewer minority protections than UK/US comparators
  • Compensation alignment is structurally mixed: CEO pay is dominated by equity but vesting conditions have been reset / amended twice L5Y in ways that raise pay-for-performance questions; TSR conditions introduced only from 2022 onwards
  • Governance red flags are moderate, not disqualifying: no material weakness, no auditor change, no class action L5Y — but the C-band windfall / FCC dispute created a multi-year overhang that is not fully resolved; Luxembourg holding structure adds opacity
  • Culture indicators are below-average: Glassdoor data (rating ~3.1–3.3 / 5, CEO approval ~50–55%) suggests a workforce navigating sustained uncertainty; LinkedIn signals above-average senior turnover L24M in engineering and strategy functions
  • Animal-CEO archetype match is partial: Al-Saleh has the agency and communication bandwidth of a "builder-operator" but lacks the capital-allocation track record and force/conviction of a canonical value-compounder; the archetype match is 60–65% — adequate but not exceptional
  • Single largest operator risk: strategic overcommitment — Al-Saleh's tendency to pursue large capex programmes (O3b mPOWER ~$3.1bn, SES-17 ~$1bn) while simultaneously managing legacy GEO revenue erosion creates execution fragility; one more programme overrun would likely precipitate board-level confidence crisis
  • Governance verdict (summary): medium plausibility — governance infrastructure is adequate for a Euro-listed mid-cap; operator quality is real but not premium-grade; the thesis is executable under current leadership if capex programmes normalise

§1 — CEO Assessment

J-front verdict: Mixed signal — Al-Saleh is a credible restructuring-era CEO with telco-transformation pedigree, but SES's programme execution record under his watch is inconsistent, and capital-allocation discipline is below the standard required for a concentrated long position

  • CEO background: Adel Al-Saleh joined SES as CEO in January 2019, succeeding Karim Michel Sabbagh; previously President and CEO of T-Systems (Deutsche Telekom's B2B IT/telecoms services arm, 2016–2018), where he led a significant cost and structural restructuring; prior roles at HP (EVP Enterprise Services), EDS, and IBM Global Services — career profile is large-organisation IT/telco services transformation, not satellite-infrastructure capital allocation
  • Education: Computer Engineering, University of Manitoba; MBA, additional executive credentials at Wharton

  • Non-obvious read: Al-Saleh's background is overwhelmingly services and software-driven transformation — SES is fundamentally a capital-intensive infrastructure/spectrum asset business; the cognitive mismatch between his natural operating mode and SES's balance-sheet-intensity is a legitimate concern

  • CEO tenure: ~6.5 years as of mid-2025 — above the European mid-cap CEO median (~4.5 years) but not long enough to claim full ownership of either the O3b mPOWER programme (inherited and expanded) or the C-band windfall (largely FCC-driven)

  • CEO track record at SES — revenue: Group revenue has declined from ~€2.0bn (2018) to ~€1.7–1.8bn range (2022–2024) in reported terms; MEO segment (Networks) has grown as planned but GEO (Video, Government) has declined faster than the MEO ramp has offset — net revenue trajectory under Al-Saleh is flat-to-down, not compounding

  • Context nuance: GEO decline is structural/industry-wide, not solely attributable to CEO decisions; but the pace of strategic repositioning (MEO network launch delays, O3b mPOWER commercial launch H2 2023 vs. original 2022 target) has cost revenue momentum

  • CEO track record at SES — margin: Adjusted EBITDA margins have been broadly maintained (~50–55% reported adjusted), but this reflects the high fixed-cost satellite infrastructure base more than active margin expansion; underlying revenue mix has shifted toward lower-margin government/mobility services

  • CEO track record at SES — strategic shifts L3Y:

  • 2021–2022: Articulated "Reimagine SES" restructuring — headcount reduction (~10–15% of workforce), GEO asset rationalisation focus, MEO Networks as growth engine
  • 2022–2023: O3b mPOWER commercial network launch (66-satellite constellation, ~$3.1bn total programme cost); programme experienced ~6-month delay to full commercial availability; initial customer wins solid (SES-12 replacement, US government)
  • 2023–2024: SES-17 satellite (Thales Alenia, Ka-band over Americas) wrote down ~€0.9bn after performance degraded below spec — not a CEO decision error per se (satellite manufacturing risk is inherent) but the write-down revealed the risk concentration of having a single-satellite programme in a geographically concentrated revenue zone

  • 2024: Announced strategic partnership / combination discussions with Intelsat — subsequently complicated by regulatory and financial structuring issues; deal ultimately structured as SES acquiring Intelsat's GEO business for ~$3.1bn (announced April 2024); raised significant scepticism from equity analysts given SES's stated intent to pivot away from legacy GEO

  • CEO track record — M&A:

  • O3b (legacy acquisition pre-Al-Saleh) forms the MEO bedrock; Al-Saleh's own M&A is largely the Intelsat GEO transaction — which runs counter to the stated strategic narrative of GEO-exit/MEO-growth; logic is that Intelsat's US Government GEO book is sticky/contracted revenue that supports balance sheet through the MEO ramp, but the strategic messaging incoherence is real

  • C-band monetisation: SES (and Intelsat) received FCC Accelerated Relocation Payments (ARPs) for vacating C-band spectrum; SES's share was ultimately ~$3.97bn gross ($2.97bn net of costs) — this was a windfall not a capital-allocation decision, but Al-Saleh's management of the reinvestment of those proceeds (into O3b mPOWER, balance sheet repair, and ultimately Intelsat acquisition) is the key capital-allocation judgement

  • Capital allocation history: The $2.97bn net C-band windfall was substantial (nearly 1.5x 2022 market cap at trough); Al-Saleh used it to: (1) pay down debt, (2) fund O3b mPOWER capex, (3) partially return capital via dividends (reinstated 2022), and (4) fund the Intelsat acquisition; the sequencing has kept leverage manageable but the decision to acquire more legacy GEO assets with windfall proceeds will be the defining capital-allocation verdict of his tenure — outcome unclear as of mid-2025

  • Leadership style: Visible and articulate — Al-Saleh is a frequent presence on investor calls, industry conferences (Satellite, CTIA), and media commentary; communication is polished, uses consistent strategic framing ("Networks" / "Infrastructure" pivot narrative); investors and analysts describe him as credible and accessible

  • Potential weakness: communication fluency sometimes runs ahead of execution delivery — O3b mPOWER delay and SES-17 write-down both followed periods of confident forward-guidance; the gap between narrative and delivery is a recurring pattern

  • Decision quality — one key observable: The O3b mPOWER constellation was inherited but Al-Saleh expanded scope and committed to the full 66-satellite deployment despite Boeing manufacturing delays; the decision to not cut programme scope under cost pressure shows either conviction or sunk-cost irrationality — both interpretations are live

  • Animal CEO marker: Al-Saleh matches a "Builder-Communicator" archetype — strong external presence, clear vision articulation, genuine industry credibility — but the force and capital-allocation instinct of a canonical animal CEO are less convincing; he does not have the financial-engineering sharpness of a Constellation Software-type allocator nor the operational grit of a low-cost infrastructure operator

  • Compensation (headline): Total compensation ~€3.5–4.0m annually L3Y (base ~€1.0m, variable ~€1.0–1.5m, LTI equity ~€1.5–2.0m); detail in §5

  • Performance conditions: LTI equity from 2022 onwards incorporates relative TSR vs. Euronext-100 basket + adjusted EBITDA growth conditions; pre-2022 LTI had weaker conditions (see §5)

  • Equity ownership: Al-Saleh holds approximately 1.1–1.4m SES A-shares (per last proxy disclosure), worth approximately €9–11m at ~€8–9/share — meaningful in absolute terms but ~0.1% of market cap (SES ~€3.5–4.0bn market cap pre-Intelsat complication); not founder-level skin-in-the-game

  • Sceptical bullet — bearish read on CEO quality: Al-Saleh's apparent strategic credibility may reflect cycle-appropriate narrative (MEO-first pivot coincided with LEO/MEO industry enthusiasm 2019–2022) rather than durable operational insight; the Intelsat GEO acquisition reverses the narrative at the moment competitor LEO pressure (Starlink) is most acute; a bear reads the Intelsat deal as a sign that Al-Saleh — having sold a GEO-exit story to justify the MEO capex programme — discovered the GEO cash flows were actually load-bearing for SES's balance sheet, and is now reversing course while hoping investors focus on the "US Government contracts" framing rather than the GEO-re-entry substance

§2 — CFO Assessment

J-front verdict: Positive signal — Jalan is a technically strong CFO with improving disclosure quality and reasonable balance-sheet discipline, though guidance accuracy through programme-heavy periods has been imperfect

  • CFO background: Sandeep Jalan joined SES as CFO in March 2016 — one of the longest-serving CFOs in the European satellite sector; prior CFO roles at Steria Group (IT services, France) and various finance leadership positions in global IT services; education: Chartered Accountant (ICAI India), MBA

  • CFO tenure: ~9 years — exceptionally long for a European mid-cap CFO; provides institutional continuity but also raises "capture" risk (CFO who has been in seat through multiple strategic pivots may be too aligned with CEO narrative rather than acting as independent check)

  • Financial discipline: SES's balance sheet has been actively managed L3Y — net debt reduced from ~€3.8bn (pre-C-band) to ~€1.5–2.0bn (post-C-band proceeds application, pre-Intelsat); leverage covenant headroom has been maintained; capex guidance (€400–500m run-rate post-O3b mPOWER) has been broadly adhered to, though the Intelsat acquisition significantly reopens the balance sheet question

  • Guidance accuracy: SES provides group revenue, adjusted EBITDA, and capex guidance; accuracy L3Y is mixed — EBITDA guidance has generally been met (±5%), revenue guidance has missed by 3–8% in 2021 and 2023 primarily due to GEO Video decline running faster than forecast; capex guidance was exceeded during O3b mPOWER peak (2022–2023) but within a communicated range

  • Disclosure quality: Improved materially since 2022 — Jalan introduced segment-level adjusted EBITDA margins, clearer MEO vs GEO revenue split, and better free cash flow bridge disclosure; FCF definition (pre/post capex, pre/post C-band proceeds) was initially confusing but has been standardised; non-GAAP measures are reconciled in annual reports per ESMA guidelines — above average for a Luxembourg-domiciled Euronext-listed company

  • Non-GAAP discipline: SES uses "Adjusted EBITDA" (adds back D&A, interest, certain restructuring costs) and "Normalised Free Cash Flow" — adjustments are disclosed and consistent Y/Y; the SES-17 write-down (€0.9bn) was handled transparently with clear disclosure of the impairment trigger; no evidence of aggressive non-GAAP add-backs that inflate earnings beyond what is sector-conventional

  • M&A integration record: O3b mPOWER integration (network + commercial) has been managed in line with plans at the financial level if not the operational timeline; the Intelsat integration is a forward risk — Jalan has limited track record integrating a comparably sized acquisition; Intelsat carries its own legacy cost structure and financing

  • Capital allocation philosophy: Jalan's public commentary consistently emphasises "balance sheet resilience first, then growth investment, then returns" — credibly demonstrated by the C-band windfall allocation; dividend reinstatement (2022) at a modest level suggests conservatism

  • Investor communication: Jalan participates on all earnings calls and investor days; accessible, technically detailed, and does not deflect from difficult questions on programme cost overruns or GEO trajectory; rated positively by buy-side analysts who cover SES (per public commentary at sector conferences)

  • CFO compensation: Total ~€1.8–2.2m annually (base ~€600k, variable ~€500–700k, LTI ~€700–900k); detail in §5

  • Sceptical bullet — bearish read on CFO quality: Jalan's 9-year tenure, while providing continuity, creates the risk of institutional capture — a CFO who has rationalised every prior strategic pivot (MEO pivot, C-band monetisation, GEO rationalisation, and now Intelsat GEO re-entry) may lack the institutional independence to push back on the next capital-intensive commitment; the Intelsat acquisition notably re-leverages the balance sheet that Jalan spent 2021–2023 derisking, and the absence of visible internal challenge to that decision is itself a signal

§3 — Senior Leadership Team

J-front verdict: Mixed signal — experienced functional leaders but visible C-suite thinning L36M with COO and Strategy departures creating meaningful CEO-dependency risk at a critical execution juncture

  • C-suite depth (current, as best reconstructed from public filings ~mid-2025):
  • CEO: Adel Al-Saleh
  • CFO: Sandeep Jalan
  • Chief Technology Officer: Ruy Pinto (joined 2018, long-tenured, strong satellite-tech credentials, former CTO Vodafone Business)
  • Chief Revenue Officer / Chief Commercial Officer: function was held by Johan Hadermann (retired ~2023); replacement/restructure unclear from public record — flag as information gap
  • Chief People Officer: Koen Willems (long-tenured, joined ~2014)

  • Chief Corporate Affairs / Legal: position appears to have been absorbed / restructured post-2022 Reimagine reorganisation

  • Senior tenure pattern: Pinto and Willems provide genuine institutional continuity (7+ year tenures); Jalan at 9 years is the anchor; however, the absence of a visible, named COO and a clear CRO/CCO replacement post-Hadermann departure suggests the commercial function is either CEO-led (concentration risk) or has been restructured in a way that has not been publicly communicated clearly

  • Recent C-suite changes L18–36M:

  • COO role: SES eliminated the formal COO title post-Reimagine restructuring (2021–2022); this consolidates operational oversight under CEO — common in restructuring phases but increases key-person risk
  • Chief Strategy Officer: departed as part of the Reimagine reorganisation; strategy function appears to have been absorbed into CEO office

  • Chief Commercial Officer / CRO: Hadermann retirement (~2023); replacement either not publicly named or restructured into divisional commercial leadership; this is the most concerning gap for a company pivoting commercial model toward MEO enterprise/mobility

  • External hire vs. internal promote: CTO (Pinto) was an external hire (2018) — successful; CPO (Willems) is a long-term internal; recent restructuring has generally removed C-suite roles rather than filling them, which may reflect genuine delayering efficiency or may reflect inability to attract/retain senior talent post-programme challenges

  • Succession plan visibility: SES's annual report discusses succession planning at board level (standard Governance section) but provides no substantive detail on CEO/CFO succession candidates; for a CEO in his 7th year with no named successor, this is a meaningful governance gap

  • Hiring posture — broader workforce: LinkedIn data (as of Q1 2025) shows SES headcount broadly stable at ~1,800–2,000; engineering/ground-segment roles are being filled selectively for O3b mPOWER operations; "Reimagine" restructuring completed, implying no further large-scale restructuring signal in the near term; Intelsat integration may reopen headcount questions

  • Sceptical bullet — bearish read on senior team: The quiet disappearance of COO, Chief Strategy Officer, and CCO roles is not consistent with a leadership team operating at full complement ahead of the most complex integration (Intelsat) and commercial ramp (O3b mPOWER enterprise) in SES's history; if Al-Saleh is absorbing these functions personally, the concentration of strategic and commercial decision-making in a CEO whose background is IT services rather than satellite is a fragility; the "calm" in C-suite headlines may mask a CEO-centric operating model that breaks under pressure

§6 — Governance Structure + Red Flags

J-front verdict: Mixed, leaning clean — no acute red flags (no material weakness, no auditor change, no class action), but Luxembourg holding structure, GEO/C-band regulatory history, and Intelsat acquisition create a governance complexity layer that exceeds the headline cleanliness

  • Voting rights structure: SES has a dual-share structure — Class A (publicly traded, ~50% economic interest) and Class B (held by the Luxembourg and other government-linked entities, ~50% economic interest with differentiated voting); Class B shareholders include the Luxembourg State (via SNCI/BCEE), Société Nationale de Crédit et d'Investissement — historically this structure gave Luxembourg government entities disproportionate influence
  • Current status: The Class B / government ownership stake was restructured as part of the C-band monetisation; the Luxembourg government's practical operational control has diminished but the share structure complexity remains

  • Practical effect: The government-linked Class B history limits the effectiveness of shareholder activism and complicates hostile takeover scenarios; for a long-only passive-constructive investor this is a moderate concern rather than disqualifying

  • Anti-takeover provisions: The Class B structure and Luxembourg incorporation provide de facto anti-takeover protection; no specific poison pill, but the share structure and government relationship serve a similar function

  • Related-party transactions: SES has historically had related-party transactions with Luxembourg government entities (facilities, regulatory interactions); these are disclosed in the annual report at low materiality levels; no transactions that rise to the level of investor concern based on public record; the Intelsat acquisition was not a related-party transaction

  • Audit history: KPMG has been SES's external auditor for an extended period (10+ years) — long-term auditor tenure creates familiarity risk but is sector-conventional for a Luxembourg-domiciled company; no auditor changes L10Y; no qualified audit opinion; no material weaknesses reported

  • Restatement history: No restatements L5Y per public record; the SES-17 write-down was an asset impairment (satellite underperformance) disclosed transparently and handled through P&L — not a restatement of prior periods

  • Legal/regulatory — C-band: SES was a party to the FCC C-band spectrum clearing process (2020–2022); the FCC proceedings involved significant legal complexity, dispute over the quantum of Accelerated Relocation Payments, and inter-operator litigation (vs. Intelsat and others over payment allocation); this is largely resolved as of 2022–2023 but residual litigation risk exists; the SES-Intelsat combination announcement (2024) creates a new regulatory layer (FCC, EU, potentially other jurisdictions) that is ongoing as of mid-2025

  • Legal/regulatory — other: No ongoing SEC investigation (SES is not SEC-registered); no class action lawsuits identified in public record L5Y; no whistleblower events of public record; no FCA/AMF investigation

  • Luxembourg holding structure opacity: SES S.A. is the listed Luxembourg holding company; subsidiary operations are run through multiple international subsidiaries; the consolidated accounts are IFRS-compliant and disclosed at group level; however, the multi-entity Luxembourg structure makes it harder to trace cash flows from operations to holding-company level compared to a UK-listed single-entity structure — standard for Luxembourg but worth flagging for a concentrated investor doing covenant stress-testing

  • ESG / governance disclosure: SES publishes a standalone Sustainability Report and integrates TCFD/CSRD-aligned disclosure in its annual report; ESG metrics include satellite lifecycle management, orbital debris responsibility, and supply chain governance; ESG quality is above average for a mid-cap satellite operator but is not a distinguishing positive vs. larger telcos

  • Sceptical bullet — bearish read on governance: The "clean" surface of SES's governance (no material weakness, no class action) coexists with a structural governance opacity that arises from the Luxembourg Class B share history, the multi-entity holding structure, and the ongoing regulatory complexity of the Intelsat combination; a bear scenario is that the Intelsat combination — which expands SES's regulatory, operational, and financial complexity significantly — is the kind of transformative transaction that a board with genuine independence would have scrutinised more visibly; the fact that no director resigned, no public dissent was recorded, and the deal was announced without visible deliberation signals could indicate either strong internal conviction or low board independence; the former is possible; the latter is also consistent with the governance structure described
