3. THEME §1 — Revenue Drivers + Visibility

J-front verdict: Revenue visibility is the single highest-uncertainty pillar; LOE exposure on Cosentyx (biosimilar entries ongoing) and Entresto patent cliff timing creates a ~$8bn revenue gap that the pipeline must bridge — diagnostic questions should force a year-by-year bridge, not a 2027-endpoint answer

Q1-A — LOE Revenue Bridge

Question: "Can you walk us, year by year 2025-2028, through net revenue impact of Cosentyx biosimilar erosion — what erosion curve are you assuming, and what replaces each dollar?"

Expected answer: Management will cite Kesimpta, Leqvio, and Kisqali as offsetting vectors; likely to give aggregate guidance rather than year-by-year granularity

Good answer signature: Specific erosion-rate assumptions by geography (EU faster, US slower); named offsetting product with market-share trajectory cited

Bad answer signature: Aggregate 2027 endpoint given without intermediate year bridge; "portfolio resilience" language without product-level decomposition

Follow-up probe: "In 2026 specifically — what is the net revenue delta from LOE minus new-launch contribution, on your base case?"

Diagnostic note: Bull case requires Cosentyx erosion being offset before 2027; bear case requires a 2025-2026 trough that depresses reported EPS and triggers multiple compression; false-friend case is management showing a smooth bridge that relies on aggressive Leqvio market penetration assumptions that haven't materialised yet — cross-ref Q19 ACH matrix on Leqvio ramp

Q1-B — Leqvio (Inclisiran) Penetration Realism

Question: "Leqvio peak-sales assumptions have been revised down from initial consensus. What is your internal peak-year forecast and what is the physician-adoption bottleneck?"

Expected answer: Management likely to cite reimbursement and care-pathway integration as primary bottlenecks; peak-sales language likely to remain vague

Good answer signature: Specificity on formulary coverage %, time-to-treat (days from prescription to first dose), and reimbursement trajectory by major market

Bad answer signature: "Significant opportunity" framing; reliance on Alnylam partnership narrative without adoption data

Follow-up probe: "What is your current physician-prescribed patient count, and what's the 12-month compound growth rate?"

Diagnostic note: Leqvio is a critical revenue gap-filler; if adoption is structurally slower than modelled, the LOE bridge fails — this is the single most important false-friend test in the revenue section; cross-ref Q16/Q17 findings on cardiovascular market access

Q1-C — Kisqali Share vs CDK4/6 Competitive Set

Question: "Kisqali global market-share trend vs Ibrance and Verzenio — where are you in the curve, and what is your share-gain assumption in the US specifically?"

Expected answer: Management will highlight overall CDK4/6 market growth; less likely to give explicit share-gain assumptions vs Pfizer Ibrance

Good answer signature: Share data by line of therapy (1L vs 2L), geography, and OS data leverage cited; specific US payer-coverage improvement cited

Bad answer signature: Category-level framing only; avoidance of head-to-head data in 2L; failure to acknowledge Eli Lilly Verzenio share gains

Follow-up probe: "Is Kisqali's growth in 2025 driven more by market expansion or share gain from Ibrance?"

Diagnostic note: Bull case needs Kisqali as a durable growth engine through 2028; if growth is market-expansion-dependent rather than share-gain-dependent, the thesis becomes hostage to class-level growth continuation — cross-ref Q15 competitive moat work

Q1-D — Geographic Mix + Emerging Market Exposure

Question: "What share of 2025-2027 revenue growth is EM-sourced, and what is the currency-hedging policy on EM revenue?"

Expected answer: EM contribution acknowledged as growing; hedging policy likely partially disclosed; management may redirect to innovative medicine division's developed-market emphasis

Good answer signature: EM revenue as % of total + growth rate, with explicit hedging overlay and local-currency margin comment

Bad answer signature: Consolidation of "international" without EM breakout; hedging described as "standard policy" without specifics

Follow-up probe: "In which EM geographies are you seeing pricing pressure, and are tender volumes in Turkey/Brazil in-line with plan?"

Diagnostic note: EM currency drag is a recurring earnings headwind for NOVN; if management can't articulate the hedge book with precision the CFO should own this answer cold — lack of specificity here is a management-quality signal, not just an IR gap

Sceptical bullet: Fluent answers on revenue drivers should increase — not reduce — scepticism if management recites the Leqvio/Kisqali/Entresto trifecta without acknowledging the 2025-2026 gap year; rehearsed answers on LOE replacement are a hallmark of large-cap pharma IR, not evidence of bull case integrity

4. THEME §2 — Margin Trajectory + Levers

J-front verdict: The ~40% core operating margin 2027 target is credible on paper but rests on revenue growth absorbing the cost base — the CFO must be pressed on which lever (pricing, mix, cost-out) is doing the work, and in which year**

Q2-A — Operating Leverage Decomposition

Question: "Of the ~350-400bps of core operating margin improvement guided to 2027, how much is volume leverage vs mix shift vs cost-out vs pricing — and which is most uncertain?"

Expected answer: CFO likely to frame it as "combination of factors"; some disclosure of cost-transformation program (announced ~$1bn efficiency program) but unlikely to give precise decomposition

Good answer signature: Explicit percentage attribution (e.g., "50% cost-out, 30% operating leverage, 20% mix") with uncertainty ranking; acknowledges which lever requires revenue growth to materialise

Bad answer signature: "Balanced contribution from multiple sources"; failure to rank uncertainty across levers; citing efficiency program without connecting it to the P&L line

Follow-up probe: "If revenue growth in 2026 is 200bps below plan, what is the margin outcome — do you have a cost-lever buffer?"

Diagnostic note: If the margin target is primarily operating-leverage-dependent, revenue miss = margin miss = double compression; if it is primarily cost-out-dependent, downside protection is better — this is a bull/bear discriminator

Q2-B — R&D Spend Adequacy + Prioritisation

Question: "R&D as % of sales is running ~20%. Is that the right level post-Sandoz, and are there pipeline programmes currently under-resourced?"

Expected answer: Management likely to assert R&D intensity is appropriate; may cite capital-light partnership structures (e.g., Alnylam, Chinook) as supplementary

Good answer signature: Named programmes with resource-allocation rationale; acknowledgement of trade-offs between early vs late-stage spend; ROI thinking on discovery vs licensing

Bad answer signature: "We invest where science leads us" — no prioritisation logic; avoidance of question on under-resourced programmes

Follow-up probe: "If you received an unexpected $2bn windfall, would you put it into R&D, BD, or buyback — and why?"

Diagnostic note: R&D adequacy is a false-friend test: a company cutting R&D to hit margin targets in 2027 destroys 2030+ EPS — cross-ref Q18 findings on pipeline depth vs disclosed spend

Q2-C — COGS and Manufacturing Efficiency

Question: "What is the trajectory of cost-of-goods-sold as % of sales 2025-2027, and where are the manufacturing network optimisation savings expected to land?"

Expected answer: COGS improvement cited as part of efficiency program; site rationalisation (post-Sandoz) expected to be a tailwind

Good answer signature: Site count reduction timeline; specific $ savings from manufacturing network; COGS/sales % trajectory by year

Bad answer signature: Aggregate efficiency program framing without COGS-specific line; manufacturing savings "embedded in guidance" without granularity

Follow-up probe: "Are there any legacy manufacturing sites that remain on the Innovative Medicines network that are dilutive to COGS — and what's the timeline to resolution?"

Diagnostic note: Post-Sandoz separation, Novartis Innovative Medicines should have a leaner manufacturing footprint — if COGS/sales isn't improving, the demerger thesis on margin re-rating is weaker

Q2-D — FX Pass-Through + Real Margin

Question: "Core operating margin is reported in constant currency. What is the reported (FX-inclusive) margin expectation for 2026-2027, and what is the hedge book duration?"

Expected answer: CFO typically guides to constant-currency; FX hedging details partially disclosed in annual report; reported margin discussion often deflected

Good answer signature: Specific hedge coverage ratio and duration; FX sensitivity (e.g., "1% CHF/USD move = Xbps margin impact"); reported vs CC bridge explicitly quantified

Bad answer signature: "We guide on constant currency" without reported-margin expectation; hedge book described as "standard" without specifics

Follow-up probe: "At current spot rates, what is the FX headwind or tailwind to reported margins in 2026?"

Diagnostic note: Most published models use CC guidance and apply own FX assumptions — if management won't give a reported-margin anchor, the uncertainty range on reported EPS widens materially; cross-ref Q16 on FX risk modelling

Sceptical bullet: Margin expansion narratives at large-cap pharma often prove durable in years 1-2 and then plateau as commercial spend for next-wave launches consumes the cost savings; probing the SG&A trajectory on Leqvio and Kisqali launch spending is a second-order question the template should capture in follow-up

5. THEME §3 — Capital Allocation + Balance Sheet

J-front verdict: Post-Sandoz demerger, Novartis is generating ~$12-14bn annual FCF with a relatively light balance sheet — the capital allocation question is whether management will deploy BD/M&A at scale or return to shareholders, and the incentive structure predicts behaviour more than stated policy**

Q3-A — M&A Philosophy + Size Appetite

Question: "Post-Sandoz, you have significant balance-sheet capacity. What is the ceiling on deal size you'd be comfortable with — and what does a target have to look like?"

Expected answer: Management will likely cite tuck-ins + bolt-ons + potentially a mid-size deal; unlikely to specify deal-size ceiling publicly

Good answer signature: Explicit IRR hurdle (e.g., "double-digit ROIC by year 5"); named therapeutic area focus; explicit statement on what they will NOT do (e.g., "no diversified conglomerate deals")

Bad answer signature: "We look at everything that fits our strategy"; valuation-agnostic framing; failure to articulate what they'd turn down

Follow-up probe: "Have you walked away from a deal in the last 18 months where you were outbid, and what was the price discipline that caused you to walk?"

Diagnostic note: The bear case for NOVN includes a large, expensive M&A deal that depletes the balance sheet and destroys ROE — a CFO who can't articulate a walk-away price creates tail risk; cross-ref Q14 on capital deployment risk

Q3-B — Buyback Cadence + Conviction Signal

Question: "The current buyback program runs to 2025. What is the board's conviction on continuing buybacks vs deploying capital in BD — and at what valuation multiple would you pause buybacks?"

Expected answer: Management will signal continued buyback discipline; unlikely to give explicit pause-trigger multiple

Good answer signature: Explicit NAV-based or P/E-based buyback framework; acknowledgement that BD pipeline determines buyback pace; CFO states the hurdle rate comparison clearly

Bad answer signature: "We remain committed to returning capital to shareholders" — boilerplate; no hurdle rate disclosed; framing buybacks as unconditional

Follow-up probe: "If you had a $5bn deal in advanced diligence today, would the buyback be suspended — and for how long?"

Diagnostic note: RONIC discipline test — if management can't articulate the buyback vs M&A trade-off with specificity, the capital allocation process may be ad-hoc; cross-ref Q17 on FCF conversion quality

Q3-C — Dividend Sustainability + Payout Policy

Question: "The progressive dividend policy implies ~4% annual growth. What FCF coverage ratio is the board targeting at the minimum, and is there a scenario where the growth trajectory is paused?"

Expected answer: Management will assert strong FCF coverage and progressive policy; unlikely to discuss pause scenarios

Good answer signature: Explicit FCF payout ratio target (e.g., "we target 40-50% of FCF"); acknowledgement of LOE impact on FCF in 2025-2026; downside scenario acknowledged

Bad answer signature: "We have a strong track record of dividend growth" without coverage ratio; dismissal of downside scenario entirely

Follow-up probe: "In a bear-case revenue scenario (e.g., Leqvio ramp significantly below plan), what is the prioritisation — dividend, buyback, or BD investment?"

Diagnostic note: Dividend sustainability is a false-friend signal for long-only investors; if progressive policy is maintained at the expense of R&D or BD when LOE gap hits, it is value-destroying discipline masquerading as shareholder friendliness

Q3-D — Working Capital + FCF Conversion Quality

Question: "Core net income to reported FCF conversion has been <90% in recent years. What are the primary working capital drag items, and what's the path to >90% conversion?"

Expected answer: CFO will cite milestones/receivables; post-Sandoz working capital clean-up expected; likely to frame conversion as improving

Good answer signature: Specific receivables days / payables days targets; milestone payment timing explained; explicit conversion ratio target

Bad answer signature: Deflection to "operational complexity"; no conversion ratio target stated; working capital drag attributed to "investment phase"

Follow-up probe: "Is any portion of the receivables book in markets with elevated credit risk, and what is your bad-debt provision rate?"

Diagnostic note: FCF conversion quality is a consistency signal — companies with persistently sub-90% conversion tend to have structural working capital issues or aggressive revenue recognition; cross-ref Q17 FCF model

Sceptical bullet: RONIC discipline questions at large-cap pharma meetings are frequently answered with "disciplined capital allocation" boilerplate; the real test is whether management can name a deal they turned down and why — the presence of a real example is signal, the absence is not necessarily a red flag but demands follow-up

6. THEME §4 — Strategy + Competitive Position

J-front verdict: Novartis's "focused innovative medicines" strategy post-Sandoz demerger is coherent in structure but depends on five therapeutic area bets (cardiovascular, immunology, oncology, neuroscience, radioligand therapy) firing simultaneously — the strategic question is whether the portfolio is genuinely focused or merely renamed**

Q4-A — Radioligand Therapy Platform — Win Condition

Question: "RLT is your most capital-intensive bet. What is the specific win condition by 2027 — what does success look like in revenue, indication count, and manufacturing capacity?"

Expected answer: Management will cite Lutathera + Pluvicto as current anchors; pipeline expansion into prostate and other solid tumours; manufacturing investment at Millburn and Indianapolis

Good answer signature: Revenue trajectory for Pluvicto by 2027 with indication-expansion milestones; manufacturing throughput (doses/year) targets; explicit acknowledgement of production constraint and timeline to resolution

Bad answer signature: "Exciting platform with significant long-term potential" — no specific 2027 revenue or capacity anchor; competition from Eli Lilly (RLT acquisitions) not acknowledged

Follow-up probe: "What is your current manufacturing capacity for Pluvicto in doses/week, and when does the capacity bottleneck resolve?"

Diagnostic note: RLT manufacturing constraints have been the primary near-term Pluvicto revenue limiter — bull case requires constraint resolution by 2025H2; if management is vague on capacity timeline the near-term upside is uncertain; cross-ref Q15 on competitive moat in RLT vs Lilly

Q4-B — Neuroscience — Portfolio Coherence vs Optionality Spread

Question: "The neuroscience portfolio spans multiple mechanisms and indications. Is the strategy deep focus in one disease or optionality spread — and which would you cut if forced to choose two programmes?"

Expected answer: Management will defend breadth of neuroscience portfolio; unlikely to volunteer cuts

Good answer signature: Explicit prioritisation rationale (e.g., mechanism-based platform vs indication-by-indication); acknowledgement that some programmes are options, not core bets; named programme they would cut last

Bad answer signature: "We see multiple opportunities across neuroscience" — no prioritisation logic; failure to acknowledge resource constraint

Follow-up probe: "If Kesimpta growth plateaus in 2026, does the neuroscience investment thesis change?"

Diagnostic note: Strategic spread in neuroscience with limited near-term catalysts could signal a capital allocation problem disguised as strategic optionality; cross-ref Q18 pipeline risk

Q4-C — Competitive Response to Biosimilar Entrants

Question: "Cosentyx biosimilar entrants are now in market in Europe. What is your retention strategy — price, service, or switching friction — and what is the managed-erosion assumption?"

Expected answer: Management will cite patient retention programmes, biosimilar co-exist strategies, and value-based contracts; erosion rate likely partially disclosed

Good answer signature: Specific retention rate by geography; price-erosion assumption by year; NBT (next biologic therapy) pathway leveraged for at-risk patients

Bad answer signature: "Biosimilars are a normal part of the product lifecycle" — no specific retention rate; failure to distinguish EU vs US erosion timeline

Follow-up probe: "In Germany and France specifically, what is your Cosentyx volume retention rate since first biosimilar entry?"

Diagnostic note: The rate of Cosentyx volume erosion in early biosimilar markets is the leading indicator for the US erosion curve — precise EU data is the most important single data point in the meeting for LOE modelling accuracy

Sceptical bullet: Strategic coherence narratives post-Sandoz demerger are structurally motivated by the need to justify the split premium; the question the analyst should probe but rarely does is "what is Novartis's theory of why it wins in cardiology AND immunology AND oncology AND neuroscience simultaneously when most focused peers choose two?"

7. THEME §5 — Management Quality + Operator Track Record

J-front verdict: Vas Narasimhan has held the CEO role since 2018; the Sandoz demerger is his signature strategic act; management quality questions should probe whether the organisation has been rebuilt for the focused model, and whether LTIP metrics drive the right behaviours**

Q5-A — Power of Incentives — LTIP Metric Weighting

[Q20-MMO gate: power-of-incentives probe in §6]

Question: "Your LTIP is publicly disclosed. What is the internal emphasis between net sales growth, core operating income, and relative TSR — and is there a metric that gets informal management attention beyond the formal weighting?"

Expected answer: Management will refer to public disclosure; unlikely to acknowledge informal emphasis vs formal weighting

Good answer signature: Candid acknowledgement of which metric drives most internal management time (e.g., "core OI is the daily operating metric — TSR is the scorecard"); honest discussion of whether short-term compensation is aligned with 2030 pipeline value

Bad answer signature: Pure reference to annual report disclosure; refusal to distinguish formal vs informal emphasis; no acknowledgement of potential misalignment

Follow-up probe: "If you were redesigning the LTIP today with a 5-year hold, what would you change?"

Diagnostic note: Power of incentives — the metric that receives informal management emphasis predicts behaviour more than stated strategy; a CEO who acknowledges the tension between short-term core OI and long-term R&D investment is exhibiting management quality; one who deflects is not

Q5-B — Sandbagging Discipline + Guidance Cadence

Question: "Looking at Novartis's guidance history 2019-2024, you beat consensus more often than not. Is that structural conservatism or coincidence — how do you calibrate the guidance range?"

Expected answer: Management will acknowledge conservative guidance culture but attribute to "uncertainty in pharma"; unlikely to explicitly claim sandbagging

Good answer signature: Explicit discussion of the internal confidence interval vs the published range; acknowledgement of which business unit tends to beat most consistently

Bad answer signature: "We guide to what we can see" — no discussion of calibration methodology; deflection to industry uncertainty as explanation for consistent beats

Follow-up probe: "In which division or product is guidance most frequently set conservatively, and why?"

Diagnostic note: Consistent guidance beats at large-cap pharma are often structural (conservative sandbagging is a feature of IR discipline) — this is a quality signal, not a red flag; the question is whether management will own it explicitly, which tells you about communication clarity

Q5-C — Crisis Playbook — COVID/Supply Chain Learning

Question: "During COVID and the subsequent supply-chain disruptions, what was the single most important operational lesson, and has it changed your supply-chain architecture permanently?"

Expected answer: Management will cite supply-chain resilience investments; dual-sourcing; regional manufacturing

Good answer signature: Named structural change (e.g., specific API dual-sourcing programme, regional inventory buffer policy); candid acknowledgement of where they were caught short

Bad answer signature: Generic "resilience" framing; no named structural change; crisis framed as "we managed well" without self-critique

Follow-up probe: "Is there a current single-source API risk that you haven't yet resolved — and what's the mitigation plan?"

Diagnostic note: Candour on historical operational failures is the most reliable proxy for management honesty in real-time; a CEO who can name a specific failure and what changed is exhibiting the communication clarity that predicts trustworthy guidance

Q5-D — Organisation Design Post-Sandoz

Question: "Post-Sandoz demerger, how many net headcount have you removed from the Novartis corporate centre, and what is the stranded-cost timeline to zero?"

Expected answer: Management will cite the cost efficiency program; headcount reduction partially disclosed; stranded-cost elimination timeline likely 2025-2026

Good answer signature: Specific headcount number removed; stranded cost quantum in CHF millions and elimination timeline; named function that was simplified (e.g., GBS shared services rationalisation)

Bad answer signature: "We're making good progress on stranded costs" — no quantum or timeline; headcount reduction described in percentage without base

Follow-up probe: "What is the remaining stranded-cost exposure as of Q1 2025, and in which P&L line does it sit?"

Diagnostic note: Stranded cost elimination is a near-term margin lever that is binary — either executed on schedule or it slips; CFO precision here predicts 2025-2026 margin beat/miss more than any other near-term cost variable

Sceptical bullet: Culture and communication clarity are almost always described positively by management in investor meetings; the actual test is whether management will describe a decision they got wrong in the last 12 months — if no example surfaces, communication clarity should be downgraded regardless of fluency

8. THEME §6 — Risk + Downside (Pre-Mortem Cross-Ref)

J-front verdict: Three failure modes dominate the Q19 pre-mortem matrix — LOE gap-year EPS trough deeper than modelled, RLT competitive disruption from Lilly/AstraZeneca, and a large dilutive M&A transaction; questions below are designed to discriminate between these scenarios**

Q6-A — Bear-Case Probe — LOE Trough Depth

Question: "What is your internal downside case for core EPS in 2026 — not guidance, but the scenario the management team stress-tests for board purposes?"

Expected answer: Management will not disclose internal downside case; likely to redirect to published guidance range

Good answer signature: Any engagement with the question beyond redirection — e.g., "we stress-test a scenario where Leqvio penetration is 50% of base, and the EPS impact is X" — even partial engagement is signal

Bad answer signature: Pure redirect to guidance range; no acknowledgement that a downside scenario exists; formulaic "we don't guide to scenarios" response

Follow-up probe: "What is the single variable in your 2026 model that has the widest confidence interval?"

Diagnostic note: Management willingness to engage with downside scenarios is a management-quality signal; refusal is not necessarily a red flag but is consistent with the false-friend profile — a company that only presents the central/upside case at DD stage is managing perception, not information

Q6-B — RLT Competitive Disruption Risk

Question: "Eli Lilly and AstraZeneca are both investing heavily in RLT. In 3 years, is Novartis still the leading RLT platform, or is that lead at risk?"

Expected answer: Management will cite manufacturing scale, clinical data depth, and PSMA expertise as durable advantages

Good answer signature: Specific acknowledgement of where the lead is and where it could be attacked; named mechanism by which Lilly or AZ could close the manufacturing gap; honest probability weighting on competitive outcome

Bad answer signature: "We have a significant head start in RLT" — no acknowledgement of competitive timeline; failure to engage with Lilly's manufacturing investment

Follow-up probe: "If a competitor achieves equivalent PSMA RLT manufacturing capacity by 2027, what is the Pluvicto market-share scenario — and how does that affect the RLT platform ROI?"

Diagnostic note: RLT platform dominance is a central bull-case assumption — if management can't articulate why the lead is durable at a mechanism level (not just scale), the bull case contains a fragile assumption; cross-ref Q15 moat durability work

Q6-C — Regulatory Exposure — FDA/EMA Pipeline Risk

Question: "Which late-stage pipeline asset, if it received a complete response letter or negative opinion in 2025-2026, would most materially impact the revenue bridge — and what is your assessment of that risk?"

Expected answer: Management will likely decline to rank CRL risk by asset; general reference to "strong regulatory track record"

Good answer signature: Any specific engagement with regulatory risk on a named asset — even "we're most watchful on [X] because of the endpoints" is diagnostic

Bad answer signature: Generic "we have experienced regulatory teams and strong data packages" — no specific asset named; no risk acknowledgement

Follow-up probe: "For iptacopan specifically — given the ATLAS data, what is the regulatory risk in the US approval, and when do you expect the FDA decision?"

Diagnostic note: Regulatory risk is the most binary near-term catalyst at large-cap pharma; a management team that can't rank its own CRL risk by asset is either genuinely uncertain (neutral signal) or managing perception (negative signal) — the Q&A exchange itself discriminates between the two

Q6-D — False-Friend Probe — Cost Discipline vs Investment Adequacy

Question: "Is there a risk that the margin expansion story is being achieved by under-investing in early-stage R&D — what does your discovery pipeline look like in 2028-2032 vintage?"

Expected answer: Management will assert R&D investment adequacy; reference to gene therapy, cell therapy, and radioligand platforms as early-stage builders

Good answer signature: Specific disclosure of Phase I/II portfolio count by year; comparison to 2018-2020 discovery pipeline vintage as a benchmark; honest acknowledgement of any gap year in early-stage

Bad answer signature: Late-stage pipeline cited as evidence of R&D health (circular — late-stage was early-stage 5-7 years ago); no engagement with 2028-2032 horizon

Follow-up probe: "How many IND-stage assets does Novartis have today vs 5 years ago — and how does the mechanism diversity compare?"

Diagnostic note: The false-friend profile at large-cap pharma is a company showing strong 2025-2027 EPS driven by cost discipline and LOE management while hollowing out the 2030+ pipeline — this question is the primary false-friend discriminator in the memo

Sceptical bullet — Q19 ACH matrix gaps: The following Q19 pre-mortem items are NOT explicitly covered by questions in this memo: (1) Chinese market volume risk and pricing reform impact on NOVN's China revenue, (2) biosimilar entrant to Entresto in the post-2027 period, (3) political/legislative drug pricing reform in the US (IRA Inflation Reduction Act IRA implications for Novartis's Medicare Part D exposure) — these should be added as follow-up questions in a second-pass meeting if time allows

9. CROSS-CUTTING META + NON-OBVIOUS QUESTIONS

J-front verdict: The most diagnostic non-obvious angles are (a) the IRA Medicare negotiation exposure question management hasn't been asked precisely, (b) the internal culture of dissent question, and (c) the "what would cause you to leave" question as an animal-CEO probe**

§8-A — Non-Obvious Angle: IRA Medicare Negotiation Exposure

Non-obvious angle: "Entresto is now subject to Medicare price negotiation under the IRA. What is the net revenue impact of the negotiated price vs the current net price after rebates — is the headline cut misleading vs the real economics?"

Expected answer: Management will cite IRA negotiated price; less likely to quantify net price vs existing rebate-adjusted price

Good answer signature: Explicit comparison of statutory negotiated price vs pre-IRA net price after Part D redesign; acknowledgement of volume tailwind from co-pay cap

Bad answer signature: IRA cited as pure headwind without net-price analysis; no engagement with the Medicare Part D redesign volume offset

Follow-up probe: "On a per-unit basis, is Entresto's economics under IRA better or worse than pre-IRA net realisation in 2023?"

Diagnostic note: Most consensus commentary presents IRA as a revenue headwind without modelling the net-price vs gross-price offset; a management team that can articulate the net economics precisely is exhibiting financial sophistication that predicts forecast accuracy

§8-B — WH Smith Probe: The Unconventional Management Integrity Test ⚡

Cross-roads question: "What is the most important thing about Novartis that is true but that you've never said publicly in an investor meeting?"

[⚡ outlier — this question has extremely low base-rate usage in large-cap pharma investor meetings; the answer, or the quality of the deflection, is more diagnostic than any product-specific question]

Expected answer: Deflection with high probability; some CEOs engage with genuine candour; a small number give a substantive answer that surfaces material non-public belief

Good answer signature: Any genuine, specific answer that reveals an assumption or belief not in public communications — even directional signal ("we're more worried about X than we let on") is a top-quality answer

Bad answer signature: "I think Novartis's culture is something that doesn't come through fully in filings" — generic culture answer; or formal deflection to "I can't say anything that isn't public"

Follow-up probe (if engaged): "And what's the implication of that for how you'd advise us to model 2027?"

Diagnostic note: This question is designed to surface the CEO's private risk hierarchy; the quality of engagement with the question (not just the content) is itself a management-quality score — a CEO who engages thoughtfully with the "what's true but unsaid" question has higher communication-clarity credentials than one who cannot

§8-C — Incentive Probe: What Would Cause You to Leave

Animal-CEO probe: "Under what conditions would you consider leaving the CEO role before the end of your current term — and what would that signal about Novartis?"

Expected answer: Deflection or "I'm committed to delivering the strategy" — most CEOs decline to engage; a small number give a substantive answer about their personal ambition vs company stage fit

Good answer signature: Candid acknowledgement that CEO-company stage fit matters; reference to a specific milestone after which a transition could be appropriate; demonstrates self-awareness about leadership tenure dynamics
