[W] FEVR-GB — Business Description Memo (IG Stage)

Source: [C] Claude | Date: 2026-05-11 | Stage: IG | Role: RESEARCHER

KEY FINDINGS - Fulcrum verdict: Fevertree is a structurally sound premium brand in a growing category, but the investment case hinges entirely on US margin delivery — the Molson Coors partnership is the single variable that makes or breaks the 2026-2028 EPS story. [#J]

  • US transition risk crystallised: FY2025 adjusted EBITDA fell 16% YoY to £42.4m (margin 11.3%, -240bps) as the Molson Coors transition consumed incremental cost; the structure includes guaranteed profits 2026-2030, partially de-risking the P&L. [#A]
  • Brand position is genuine: Fevertree holds ~40% value share of the premium UK mixer market and #1 position in US tonic and ginger beer (27% and 32% share respectively) — displacement of Schweppes in its core category is a landmark competitive achievement. [#A]
  • Revenue model is asset-light but margin-constrained: The contract-manufacturing model (>90% outsourced) limits gross margin upside and creates input-cost pass-through exposure; glass (80% of packaging) and freight costs drove a peak-to-trough gross margin swing of ~600bps 2021-2023. [#A]
  • UK is structurally challenged: UK revenue fell 2% in FY2025 to £108.4m; on-trade -9%; gin category decline is a structural headwind. UK is no longer the growth engine — it is a cash-generative but slowly eroding base. [#J]
  • Non-tonic diversification is real: Non-tonic products (ginger beer, lemonade, soda water, cocktail mixers) now represent ~45% of group revenue, growing at a 21% CAGR over five years — this is a genuine category extension, not brand dilution. [#A]
  • Valuation remains a significant barrier: FEVR trades at ~18-20x EV/EBITDA vs Diageo at ~10x and Remy Cointreau at ~11x; the premium is justified only if US double-digit EBITDA growth materialises as guided from 2026. [#J]
  • Management quality is high but founder-led transition risk exists: Tim Warrillow (CEO since 2014, 4.85% owner) is a strong brand builder; the exit of co-founder Charles Rolls in 2018 and reliance on one operational leader is a concentration risk. [#A]
  • Destocking is largely resolved: UK channel inventory normalised through H2 2024-H1 2025; the on-trade contraction appears structural (gin decline + consumer spend) rather than cyclical overstocking. [#J]
  • The flywheel is under stress: Premium mixer category growth is slowing in mature markets; marketing spend is rising (Molson Coors investing significantly in US); the brand must invest to hold share, suggesting treadmill dynamics in UK/Europe even as the flywheel remains credible in the US. [#J]

§1 BUSINESS OVERVIEW & REVENUE MODEL

Fevertree Drinks plc (AIM: FEVR) is the world's leading premium mixer brand, founded in 2004 by Charles Rolls and Tim Warrillow, floated on AIM in 2014. The business is built on a simple thesis: premium spirits deserve premium mixers. It operates an asset-light model — brand ownership, ingredient sourcing, and marketing — with manufacturing outsourced to contract partners.

  • Model summary: Fevertree owns the brand, sources proprietary botanical ingredients globally, and outsources >90% of production to contract manufacturers; capital intensity is structurally low. [#I]
  • Revenue is generated by selling branded bottles to distributors, retailers, and on-trade accounts across 75+ countries.

  • There is no owned manufacturing or distribution infrastructure outside the US (pre-Molson Coors) — differentiated from most FMCG peers.

  • Revenue base: FY2025 total revenue £375.3m (+3% reported; +4% constant currency); Fever-Tree brand revenue £372.7m. [#I]

  • FY2024 total revenue £368.5m (+1.1% on FY2023 £364.4m) — illustrating a period of muted top-line growth.

  • Non-tonic products ~45% of group revenue (FY2025), up from ~30% five years ago; 21% CAGR over five years. [#A]

  • Geographic mix (FY2025 brand revenue): [#I]

  • UK: £108.4m (-2% YoY); c.29% of total.
  • US: adjusted revenue £131.9m (+6% constant currency); c.35% of total — note: statutory US revenue £81.6m, with £50.3m added back under new Molson Coors reporting convention.
  • Europe: £94.7m (c.25% of total); broadly flat YoY.

  • Rest of World: £37.7m (+17% YoY); c.10% of total — fastest-growing region from a low base.

  • Channel mix: Split broadly between on-trade (bars, restaurants, hotels) and off-trade (retail, grocery). [#I]

  • UK on-trade c.35-40% of UK revenue historically; contracted sharply (-9% FY2025) reflecting gin category decline and consumer spending pressure.

  • US channel is predominantly off-trade (grocery/liquor retail) with growing on-trade ambitions via Molson Coors hospitality network.

  • Product portfolio: 15+ SKUs across tonics, ginger beer, ginger ale, lemonade, soda water, cocktail mixers. [#I]

  • Core: Indian Tonic Water (flagship), Mediterranean Tonic Water, Elderflower Tonic Water.
  • Growth: Ginger Beer (double-digit volume growth FY2025), Madagascan Cola, Sicilian Lemonade, Cocktail Mixers.

  • Tonic's share of revenue declining from c.70% (2018) to ~55% (FY2025) — deliberate diversification.

  • Sceptical lens §1: The asset-light model is elegant in theory but creates structural dependency on third-party manufacturers and distributors; the Molson Coors deal effectively re-creates an in-house US operation via a contractual proxy — the economics and accountability are less transparent than direct ownership. [#J]

§2 FINANCIAL PROFILE

  • Revenue trend verdict: Revenue growth has compressed from peak 20%+ CAGR (2014-2019) to low single digits (2022-2025) — the base effect of scale is biting; recovery to double digits requires US to fully execute. [#J]

  • Revenue (£m, reported): FY2021: £311.1m; FY2022: £363.1m; FY2023: £364.4m; FY2024: £368.5m; FY2025: £375.3m. [#I]

  • Effective three-year revenue growth rate FY2022-FY2025: c.3.4% CAGR — materially below the 7-10% category growth rate. ❌

  • EBITDA margin trajectory: [#I]

  • FY2019: ~32% adjusted EBITDA margin (peak era).
  • FY2022: Margins collapsed to ~11% as glass/freight/energy costs spiked post-pandemic.
  • FY2024: £50.5m adjusted EBITDA (margin ~13.7%).
  • FY2025: £42.4m adjusted EBITDA (margin ~11.3%) — -240bps YoY driven by US transition costs and EPR levy provision. ❌

  • Vs Diageo EBITDA margin ~28-30%; Campari ~25%; Remy Cointreau ~20%: Fevertree trails all large-cap spirits peers on margin. ❌

  • Gross margin: Recovering from 2022-2023 trough driven by glass, freight, and energy cost normalisation. [#A]

  • Peak gross margin c.58-60% (pre-2021); compressed to c.52% trough; recovering toward 54-56% range by FY2025.
  • Glass packaging represents ~80% of packaging cost base — high vulnerability to energy price spikes affecting glass manufacturing. [#I]

  • Vs Diageo gross margin ~60%; Remy Cointreau ~68%: structurally lower as Fevertree owns no premium spirits inventory/ageing advantage. ❌

  • EPS trajectory: [#I]

  • FY2023: ~16.5p adj. EPS; FY2024: ~21.6p adj. EPS (+31%); FY2025: ~21.6p (broadly flat).
  • Consensus FY2026 EPS: ~26-28p (+21-28% YoY) — predicated on US EBITDA recovery.

  • EPS growth 20%+ pa forecast FY2026-2028; this is entirely dependent on Molson Coors US execution. [#A]

  • Capex intensity: Low by design — asset-light model means capex/revenue typically <2%. [#I]

  • No owned manufacturing = no heavy capex cycle; capital returned to shareholders rather than reinvested in plant.

  • Vs Diageo capex/revenue ~4-5%; Campari ~5-6%: Fevertree structurally more capital-efficient. ⚡

  • Cash generation and returns: Strong FCF conversion; >£120m returned to shareholders via dividends and buybacks over recent years. [#A]

  • £100m buyback completed FY2025; additional £30m buyback announced; Molson Coors paid £71m for 8.5% stake (Jan 2025).

  • Net cash position (no meaningful debt) — balance sheet strength is a genuine differentiator vs spirits peers carrying acquisition-related leverage.

  • Sceptical lens §2: The declared EBITDA margin improvement story for 2026 depends on (a) normalised US transition costs, (b) no further input cost inflation, and (c) US volume growth meeting plan — all three assumptions must hold simultaneously. Historical margin volatility (600bps peak-to-trough) suggests the range is wide. [#J]

§3 MARKET STRUCTURE & COMPETITIVE POSITION

  • Category verdict: Premium mixers are a structurally growing sub-category within non-alcoholic beverages, but Fevertree is now the category incumbent — growth rates available to an incumbent are materially lower than those available to a challenger. [#J]

  • Market size: Global premium mixers market ~$2.5bn (2024); CAGR estimated 7.2% to 2033 (~$4.7bn). Broader cocktail mixers market $11.9bn (2024) at 6.8% CAGR. UK market ~£600-650m (mixers total). [#I]

  • Fevertree market share positions: [#A]

  • UK: ~40% value share of premium mixer segment; #1 in tonic; market leadership vs Schweppes (~26% share).
  • US: #1 in tonic water (27% share); #1 in ginger beer (32% share) — overtook Schweppes as top-selling tonic in US in 2022. ⚡
  • Europe: Category leadership in key markets (Germany, Spain, Italy); specific share data not publicly disclosed.

  • ROW: Early-stage presence; 17% revenue growth FY2025 from low base.

  • Competitive dynamics: [#A]

  • Schweppes (Coca-Cola/local franchisees): Mass-market incumbent; premium positioning is harder for Schweppes given brand heritage and price point; has launched premium sub-ranges with limited impact.
  • London Essence Company: Premium tier competitor in UK; distributed via Coca-Cola; gaining on-trade presence.
  • Fentimans: Artisanal UK competitor; premium but subscale and niche.
  • Q Mixers : Credible premium US competitor; craft positioning; national retail presence. Distribution scale remains below Fevertree.

  • Private label: Growing in off-trade as retailers trade premiumise own-label; margin threat rather than volume threat.

  • Barriers to entry (BtE): [#A]

  • Hard BtE: Brand equity built over 20 years; proprietary ingredient sourcing (natural botanicals globally — quinine from Rwanda, ginger from Ivory Coast); scale in on-trade listing relationships.
  • Soft BtE: Distribution relationships (on-trade listings hard to displace once established); retailer shelf positioning and category management agreements.

  • BtE are real but not insurmountable — London Essence has established credible UK on-trade presence with Coca-Cola backing.

  • Category headwinds: [#A]

  • UK gin category in structural decline (gin was Fevertree's key spirit pairing for UK on-trade); rum and tequila mixers partially offsetting.
  • No-low alcohol trend creates opportunity (premium soft drink mixers for mocktails) but also risk (if spirits consumption declines overall).

  • Consumer spending pressure in UK on-trade channels (documented -9% FY2025 on-trade revenue).

  • Sceptical lens §3: ❌ Fevertree's share gain story is largely complete in the UK — it has won the category. Future growth must come from (a) category expansion (gin recovery or new spirits pairings), (b) non-tonic products, or (c) US/international. This limits the UK re-rating story absent a category catalyst. [#J]

§4 BRAND & PRICING POWER

  • Pricing power verdict: Fevertree has demonstrated tangible pricing power in the premium tier — it has taken price through inflationary cycles — but volume elasticity in the UK on-trade channel is observable and concerning. Pricing power is real but not unlimited. [#J]

  • Brand portfolio depth: [#I]

  • 40+ tonic variants globally; product range built around premium ingredient sourcing as the core differentiating narrative.
  • Brand associations: crafted, natural, premium, mixer for premium spirits — consistent for 20 years.

  • No advertising spend on traditional media historically; brand built through on-trade endorsement, bartender advocacy, spirits brand partnerships. [#A]

  • Pricing history: [#A]

  • Mid-single to high-single digit price/mix increases taken through 2022-2023 inflationary cycle.
  • Revenue per case has consistently risen over time — price/mix positive across most geographies.

  • UK off-trade price increases implemented without significant volume loss; on-trade saw volume pressure from overall category softness (distinguishing volume elasticity from price elasticity is difficult here). [#A]

  • Volume response to pricing: [#A]

  • FY2024 UK: volume flat to marginally negative; revenue decline driven by on-trade channel mix vs price resistance.
  • The volume pressure in UK appears to be gin category decline rather than Fevertree brand resistance to price.

  • Globally: non-tonic volumes growing double digit — pricing and volume growth coexisting outside tonic/UK.

  • Raw material cost sensitivity: [#A]

  • Glass packaging (~80% of packaging cost): highly sensitive to energy prices; 2022-2023 glass cost inflation "material" per management, causing EBITDA margin to collapse from ~32% to ~11%.
  • Aluminium LME exposure: secondary packaging (cans in US); LME +12% YoY to Q4 2025.
  • Botanicals (natural quinine, ginger, elderflower): sourced globally; geopolitically sensitive; proprietary sourcing relationships are competitive moat but also single-source risk.

  • Freight: significant input post-pandemic; UK-manufactured product shipped to US pre-Molson Coors deal was structurally expensive.

  • Wisdom Library — Pricing Power (Gold) assessment: [#J]

  • Fevertree is cited explicitly as a Gold-tier pricing power example. The evidence: consistent revenue per case growth, premium price maintained vs mass market (2-4x price premium to Schweppes), on-trade listing at premium price points in high-end venues.
  • However, the margin evidence weakens the pricing power narrative — if pricing power was complete, input cost inflation should be passable without margin compression. The reality: margins halved 2019-2022 despite price increases. Pricing power is real but incomplete. [#A]

  • Verdict: Fevertree has pricing power in the brand/channel sense (consumers will pay for premium); it does not have full pricing power in the economic sense (it cannot fully offset input cost inflation).

  • Sceptical lens §4: The "premium mixer" category is not a luxury good — the price differential to mainstream (£3-4 for 4-pack vs £1-1.50) is meaningful to price-sensitive consumers in a cost-of-living squeeze. The UK on-trade's -9% FY2025 is partly a macro story, but sustained pressure could reshape consumer behaviour. [#J]

§5 DISTRIBUTION & ROUTE TO MARKET

  • Distribution verdict: Fevertree's route to market strength in the UK and Europe is a genuine competitive moat; the US route to market was its structural weakness and has been fixed — in theory — by the Molson Coors deal. The execution of that fix is the £200m question. [#J]

  • UK on-trade distribution: [#A]

  • Listed in substantially all major UK pub groups, hotel chains, and premium restaurant groups.
  • On-trade relationship is mutually reinforcing: premium bars stock Fevertree because customers expect it; consumers expect it because premium bars stock it.

  • Managed by direct salesforce in UK; historically relationship-intensive.

  • UK off-trade: [#I]

  • 1 value share across all major UK grocery retailers (Tesco, Sainsbury's, Waitrose, M&S, Asda, Lidl).

  • Off-trade revenue +5% FY2025, demonstrating resilience as on-trade declined.

  • Consumer shift from on-trade to off-trade (home cocktails/entertaining) is partially offsetting on-trade softness.

  • Europe route to market: [#A]

  • Distribution primarily via local third-party distribution partners by country.
  • Strong positions in Germany, Spain, Belgium, Netherlands, Scandinavia.

  • European revenue broadly flat FY2025 (£94.7m vs £92.7m FY2024) — category growth partially offset by distributors managing inventory.

  • US route to market (pre and post-Molson Coors): [#A]

  • Pre-Jan 2025: Self-distributed/third-party distributors with fragmented coverage; imported product from UK manufacturing = structural cost disadvantage.
  • Jan 2025: Molson Coors takes exclusive US commercialisation rights — access to 400 regional distributors serving ~500,000 accounts nationally.
  • US local manufacturing via Molson Coors' existing network — eliminates transatlantic freight cost and improves on-shelf availability.

  • Molson Coors owns 8.5% of Fevertree (paid £71m) — alignment of incentives is structural.

  • US channel composition: [#A]

  • Predominantly off-trade (large format grocery/liquor retail — Walmart, Kroger, Total Wine); growing on-trade via Molson Coors hospitality relationships.
  • Ginger beer strong in US (32% share); tonic water #1 with 27% share — category leadership already established pre-Molson Coors.

  • US revenue adjusted £131.9m FY2025 (+6% constant currency) — growing faster than UK but from a restructured reporting basis.

  • ROW distribution: [#I]

  • Australia, Canada, South Africa, Middle East — early-stage premium on-trade footholds.
  • ROW revenue £37.7m (+17% FY2025) — smallest segment but fastest growth.

  • Distribution model: local premium spirits distributors; capital-light approach.

  • Sceptical lens §5: ❌ The Molson Coors deal creates a critical dependency — Fevertree has effectively outsourced its most important market's commercialisation. If Molson Coors' attention or priorities shift (the company is primarily a beer business managing a significant secular decline in beer volumes), Fevertree's US ambitions could stall regardless of brand demand. [#J]

§6 US MARKET — FULCRUM DRIVER ASSESSMENT

  • Fulcrum verdict: The US is unambiguously the fulcrum driver for Fevertree's re-rating. The Molson Coors partnership has structurally de-risked the revenue floor (guaranteed profit structure 2026-2030); the upside case requires Molson Coors to invest behind the brand and execute distribution scale, not just maintain it. [#J]

  • Scale and trajectory: [#A]

  • US represents ~35% of group revenue on adjusted basis (£131.9m FY2025); largest single geographic segment.
  • US revenue has grown from ~£50m (FY2019) to £132m adjusted (FY2025) — 6-year CAGR of c.18%.

  • FY2025 growth was 6% constant currency — below the double-digit aspiration but ahead of guidance revisions.

  • US profitability — the challenge: [#A]

  • US adjusted EBITDA margin fell 820bps to 6.3% in FY2025 — the transition year absorbed significant costs.
  • US EBITDA approximately halved in absolute terms in FY2025 — the short-term cost of structural improvement. ❌

  • From 2026, Molson Coors' US EBITDA contribution is guaranteed at a minimum floor per the agreed business plan — financial visibility materially improved.

  • What the Molson Coors deal delivers: [#A]

  • Distribution: from fragmented coverage to 400 distributors, ~500,000 accounts — a step change in channel reach.
  • Manufacturing: local US production eliminates transatlantic freight and reduces COGS materially.
  • Marketing: Molson Coors committing to "significant step-up" in US marketing investment from 2026 — doubling of brand investment vs pre-deal levels.

  • Equity alignment: 8.5% shareholding aligns Molson Coors with Fevertree long-term value.

  • Right-to-Left Thinking (Gold) — what has to be true for 2026-2028 EPS: [#J]

  • Working from target EPS of ~34-40p by FY2028 (analyst consensus), the transmission mechanism requires:
    1. US revenue growth of 10-15% pa (driven by Molson Coors distribution ramp + increased marketing).
    2. US EBITDA margin recovery to 15-18% (from 6.3% FY2025) — via local manufacturing cost savings + marketing leverage.
    3. UK stabilisation (flat to marginally positive revenue) with margin recovery as input costs normalise.
    4. Europe continuing at low single digit growth — largely "do no harm" assumption.

  • The probability of all four holding simultaneously is the core investment judgement.

  • US bear case: [#A]

  • Molson Coors underinvests in marketing (internal priority conflicts with core beer business in structural decline).
  • US premium mixer category growth slows from 6-8% to 3-4% (premiumisation fatigue).
  • Local manufacturing encounters quality/consistency issues (brand equity risk).

  • Guaranteed profit floor gets tested in Year 1 (2026) rather than being exceeded.

  • Sceptical lens §6: ❌ The Molson Coors partnership is a rational solution to a structural problem, but Fevertree investors now own a royalty-like income stream from a US operation they do not control, run by a partner whose core business (beer) is in secular decline and whose management attention and incentive structure may not align perfectly with premium mixer growth. [#J]

§7 MANAGEMENT & OPERATOR QUALITY

  • Management verdict: Tim Warrillow is a high-quality brand builder with genuine long-term owner mentality; the Molson Coors deal represents a bold and arguably necessary structural decision. Capital allocation has been shareholder-friendly. Succession risk is the most underappreciated risk. [#J]

  • CEO Tim Warrillow: [#I]

  • Co-founder (2004), CEO since 2014 (replacing Charles Rolls who retired).
  • Advertising/marketing background — brought brand discipline from day one.
  • Owns 4.85% of company (~£63m at current prices) — genuinely aligned with shareholder value.
  • Tenure: 12+ years as CEO; long institutional knowledge.

  • Key strategic decisions: IPO (2014), US expansion, Molson Coors partnership (2025) — all directionally correct in strategic intent even if execution has been challenging.

  • CFO Andy Branchflower: [#I]

  • Standard corporate finance background; LTIP options over 380,286 shares vesting 2028 (performance 2025-2027).
  • Incentive structure aligned with medium-term delivery — good.

  • Less publicly visible than CEO; little independent track record to assess.

  • Charles Rolls (co-founder, exited 2018): [#A]

  • Gin industry veteran (ex-MD Plymouth Gin); the "route to market" half of the founding partnership.
  • Departed in 2018; residual impact on culture and strategy is fading.

  • Loss of commercial instinct he brought is partially a risk factor.

  • Capital allocation track record: [#A]

  • No major acquisitions — pure organic brand builder; discipline is admirable.
  • £100m buyback FY2025; £30m further buyback announced; >£120m total returned over recent years.
  • £71m received from Molson Coors for 8.5% stake — effectively partially funded the buyback from US partnership proceeds.

  • No debt — balance sheet conservatism is appropriate for a brand-heavy, margin-volatile business.

  • Incentive structure: [#A]

  • LTIP performance conditions 2025-2027 — aligned with medium-term EBITDA recovery.

  • CEO share ownership (4.85%) means wealth is correlated directly with long-term stock performance.

  • Sceptical lens §7: The founding team has been responsible for building one of the best premium beverage brands in Europe — but the Molson Coors deal is the first time Fevertree has outsourced strategic control of its most important market. The quality of execution now depends as much on Molson Coors management as on Fevertree management. [#J]

§8 KEY RISKS

  • Risk summary verdict: The risk profile is concentrated in three areas: US execution dependency (exogenous), UK structural decline (slow-moving but real), and input cost volatility (cyclical but episodic). Valuation multiples leave limited margin of safety if any of these materialise. [#J]

  • US execution risk : [#A]

  • Molson Coors fails to invest at promised marketing levels or diverts resource to core beer business.
  • Distribution build slower than anticipated — 500,000 accounts requires multi-year rollout.

  • Guaranteed profit floor provides a floor, not a ceiling — upside is contingent on execution.

  • UK structural decline (MEDIUM-HIGH): [#A]

  • Gin in structural decline (-5 to -10% category volume trend) — Fevertree's #1 spirit pairing.
  • Consumer spending pressure on on-trade unlikely to reverse quickly post-cost-of-living crisis.

  • UK revenue £108.4m FY2025 (-2%); on-trade -9% — the drag is significant at 29% of group revenue. ❌

  • Input cost volatility (MEDIUM): [#A]

  • Glass: energy-price-driven; any recurrence of 2021-2022 energy spike could compress margins sharply.
  • Aluminium: LME exposure for US canned products; Trump-era tariff risk on aluminium imports (partially mitigated by Molson Coors local manufacturing).

  • Freight: global shipping disruption risk (Red Sea, Panama Canal); Fevertree's distributed manufacturing model means continued shipping exposure.

  • Competition / market share loss (MEDIUM): [#A]

  • London Essence (Coca-Cola-backed) growing in UK on-trade; well-funded and credible.
  • Q Mixers in US; Fever-Tree's US on-trade listings at risk if Q Mixers leverages local relationships.

  • Private label premiumisation in off-trade (Waitrose, M&S own-label premium mixers).

  • Concentration risk (MEDIUM): [#I]

  • Single brand (Fever-Tree) drives >95% of revenue — no diversification across brands.
  • Single CEO (Warrillow) drives strategic decisions — key man risk.

  • Molson Coors is now a single counterparty for the US — commercial concentration risk.

  • Regulatory / health (LOW-MEDIUM): [#A]

  • UK EPR (Extended Producer Responsibility) levy: £2.8m provision taken in FY2025 — ongoing compliance cost.
  • No-low alcohol trend: could reduce overall spirits consumption but also creates non-alcoholic mixer opportunity.

  • Sugar taxation: soft drinks levy does not currently apply to premium mixers at Fevertree's formulation levels.

  • Sceptical lens §8: ❌ Fevertree's risk profile is asymmetric: the downside scenarios (UK structural decline + US execution miss) are individually manageable but in combination would challenge both the revenue trajectory and the valuation multiple simultaneously. At 18-20x EV/EBITDA, there is very limited multiple compression tolerance. [#J]

If US execution disappoints in Year 1 (2026), the multiple and earnings will compress together — a dangerous combination. [#J]

  • Current Fevertree metrics (approx. May 2026): [#I]

  • Market cap: ~£1.1bn (c.£9.20 per share).
  • EV: ~£1.0-1.1bn (net cash).
  • EV/EBITDA (trailing, FY2025): ~24-26x (£42.4m EBITDA).
  • EV/EBITDA (forward, FY2026E consensus): ~18-20x (£52-55m consensus EBITDA).

  • P/E (trailing): ~40-47x; P/E (FY2026E): ~33-35x.

The same multiple applied to a company earning 11-12% EBITDA margins (vs peers at 25-30%) implies significant faith in the growth trajectory. If 2026 US EBITDA comes in at the guaranteed floor rather than the base case, the multiple will re-rate toward the peer average — implying 30-40% downside on a combined earnings + multiple compression scenario. §10 BEAR CASE & SCEPTICAL LENS

  • Bear case verdict: The value trap scenario for Fevertree is entirely plausible — this is not a remote tail risk. It requires no catastrophic event: simply that US growth disappoints consensus expectations in 2026-2027 while UK continues to decline. The stock has already de-rated from 4,000p (2018 peak) to ~920p — the question is whether the current price already reflects sufficient bad news. [#J]

  • Value trap conditions — what must be true for FEVR to be a value trap: [#A]

  • Condition 1: Molson Coors delivers the guaranteed minimum but no meaningful upside growth in US — US revenue grows at 5-8% rather than 12-15%, reflecting beer-company mindset applied to premium mixer brand.
  • Condition 2: UK on-trade does not recover — gin category decline is structural, consumer spending on premium on-trade normalises at a lower level permanently.
  • Condition 3: Gross margins fail to recover to 55%+ as glass cost inflation re-emerges or US local manufacturing has teething cost inefficiencies.

  • Condition 4: Non-tonic diversification (ginger beer, lemonade) approaches category maturity faster than expected in core markets.

  • Bear case EPS scenario: [#A]

  • If US EBITDA margin recovers to only 10% (not 15-18%) and UK is flat: group EBITDA c.£52-55m (vs bull case £70-80m by FY2028).
