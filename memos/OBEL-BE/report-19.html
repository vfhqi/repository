3. PART A — FAILURE NARRATIVES

§2 — Failure Narrative 1: Revenue-Quality Erosion Under Volume Growth

Failure narrative: Revenue grows in headline as the company wins new contracts, but per-unit revenue realisation falls because new contracts are priced below legacy book rates to win competitive tenders; the EPS transmission mechanism is: headline revenue growth → analyst confidence → valuation re-rate → then reported margin compression two quarters later as the dilutive contract cohort reaches full run-rate, triggering a sharp de-rate.

Early warning signals:
- Revenue-per-customer-account trending down while total revenue trends up
- Contract win announcements accompanied by silence on contract value-per-unit vs prior book
- Sales cycle length extending (sign of harder price negotiations)
- Backlog revenue divided by backlog unit count compressing
- Management commentary shifts from "value-add wins" to "volume wins"

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — this pattern is well-precedented in European B2B services; the typical sector base rate for volume-driven revenue growth masking per-unit deterioration is observed in roughly one-in-three mid-cap growth narratives where the company is under competitive pressure to grow share

Means / Motive / Opportunity test:
- Means: pricing lever is available — management can choose to price below book rate
- Motive: management incentives are volume/revenue-weighted rather than margin-weighted (confirmed in prior memos)
- Opportunity: new contract cycles are opaque to outside investors; ✅ all three legs aligned — full activation risk

Disconfirming evidence: If average revenue per account is flat-to-rising and new contract margins are disclosed as at-or-above book-rate, this failure hypothesis is invalidated; a disclosed "pricing discipline" metric or auditor commentary on contract accounting would also disconfirm

Monitoring checklist:
- Average revenue per account (ARR/count if SaaS-adjacent; revenue/client otherwise)
- Gross margin per contract cohort vintage (disclosed or estimable)
- Days Sales Outstanding trend (rising DSO often co-travels with price-concession structures)
- Disclosed contract value at announcement vs. implicit book rates
- Management guidance language on "mix" vs "price" drivers of growth

Interaction with other failure modes: Directly correlated with Failure Narrative 2 (margin compression); feeds Failure Narrative 8 (FALSE FRIEND — earnings beat from unsustainable mix); null interaction with Failure Narratives 5-6 (execution/management)

❌ Sceptical bullet: Disconfirming evidence would be explicit per-unit revenue disclosure trending upward — absent that, revenue-quality erosion cannot be ruled out from public data alone

§3 — Failure Narrative 2: Operating-Leverage Inversion from Cost-Base Stickiness

Failure narrative: The investment thesis models significant operating leverage as revenues scale, but Belgian labour law creates near-irreversible fixed-cost commitments; if revenue growth slows or plateaus, the cost base does not flex downward, inverting the operating leverage assumption and compressing EBIT margins toward or below the sector floor; transmission: revenue growth deceleration → fixed-cost absorption falls → EBIT margin contracts → EPS misses guidance → multiple de-rate.

Early warning signals:
- Headcount growth running ahead of revenue growth for more than two consecutive quarters
- Staff costs as a percentage of revenue rising (especially if total revenue is still growing)
- Belgian CPI / wage index running above assumed cost-escalation rates in management guidance
- Operating leverage metric (incremental EBIT / incremental revenue) trending below historical average
- Energy cost line in Belgium rising materially (relevant if operations are energy-intensive)

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — Belgian labour market rigidity is among the highest in the EU; the structural precedent for cost-base stickiness in Belgian mid-caps is very well established; any company with >40% of COGS or OPEX in Belgian domestic labour is exposed to this failure mode as a near-structural risk, not a tail risk

Means / Motive / Opportunity test:
- Means: the fixed-cost structure exists structurally; management cannot easily reduce it
- Motive: there is no motive for management to cause this (it is a structural trap, not a choice) — ⚠️ motive leg is absent as a managerial choice, but the failure does not require management motivation; it is environmental
- Opportunity: any demand plateau or revenue miss activates it; ✅ two-of-three legs (means + opportunity) — treat as "watch only" but elevated watch given Belgian labour law environment

Disconfirming evidence: If the company can demonstrate genuine variable-cost structure (contractors, project-based workforce, output-based pay) rather than headcount-heavy fixed structure, or if Belgian wage indexation is explicitly hedged in disclosed cost guidance, this hypothesis weakens materially

Monitoring checklist:
- Staff costs / revenue ratio (quarterly)
- FTE count vs. revenue growth rate (trailing 4-quarter basis)
- Belgian automatic wage indexation events (pivotal index threshold crossings)
- Gross margin stability under revenue growth scenarios
- Any disclosed workforce flexibility initiatives or contractor ratios

Interaction with other failure modes: Directly correlated with Failure Narrative 1 (pricing erosion amplifies the cost-absorption problem); correlated with Failure Narrative 6 (management capability ceiling — management may not recognise the trap until late); amplifies FALSE FRIEND Narrative 9

Sceptical bullet: The disconfirming evidence for this narrative requires structural workforce flexibility disclosure — without it, cost-base stickiness is a resident risk regardless of near-term margin trend

§4 — Failure Narrative 3: Customer Concentration Cliff

Failure narrative: If the revenue base is concentrated in a small number of large customers, the loss or renegotiation of one anchor relationship can trigger a step-change revenue miss with no short-term recovery path; transmission: anchor customer loss / contract repricing → revenue gap → operating deleverage (Narrative 2 activates simultaneously) → double-hit to EPS → thesis collapses on a single event

Early warning signals:
- Top-3 or top-5 customer revenue share remaining elevated (>30% in top 3)
- Any disclosed contract renewal approaching maturity in an 18-month window
- Anchor customer public commentary on cost discipline, insourcing, or vendor consolidation
- Unusual DSO spike in accounts where one large debtor drives the trend
- Management silence on customer concentration metrics when previously disclosed

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — customer concentration cliffs are well-precedented in European B2B services but require the conjunction of (a) high concentration AND (b) an active renewal risk; plausibility is elevated if prior memos flagged top-customer concentration above 25% of revenues

Means / Motive / Opportunity test:
- Means: the customer can walk (contract expiry is the structural means)
- Motive: customers always have motive to reduce cost or diversify suppliers
- Opportunity: upcoming contract renewals are the trigger; ✅ all three legs present — full activation risk if renewal calendar is near-term

Disconfirming evidence: Multi-year contracted revenue with explicit renewal terms, customer diversification trend (concentration falling over trailing 8 quarters), or disclosed long-term partnership agreements with termination costs would significantly disconfirm

Monitoring checklist:
- Customer concentration metrics (top 3 / top 5 as % of revenue)
- Contract maturity schedule (disclosed or inferrable)
- Customer churn / net revenue retention rate
- Anchor customer financial health (any stress signals in their own financials)
- Management commentary on contract tenure and renewal pipeline

Interaction with other failure modes: High correlation with Failure Narrative 2 (deleverage amplifier); can trigger Failure Narrative 7 (management reaction — acquisition to fill gap); interacts with FALSE FRIEND Narrative 10 (near-term beat from retained anchor before cliff)

Sceptical bullet: Disconfirming evidence requires explicit contract maturity and concentration data — without it, this is an unresolved risk regardless of the pipeline narrative

§5 — Failure Narrative 4: Growth Narrative Break at Consensus-Inflection Point

Early warning signals:
- Management guidance language shifts from "accelerating" to "sustainable" or "steady"
- Organic growth rate in most-recent quarter below prior four-quarter average
- Sell-side consensus revision turning negative for the first time after a long positive streak
- New customer acquisition rate slowing while churn rate is stable (pipeline depletion signal)
- Peer companies in the European B2B services sector reporting demand softness

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — growth-narrative breaks are extremely common in the 18-36 month post-discovery window for mid-cap growth names; the base rate for at least one consensus-revision event causing a >15% de-rate in the European mid-cap B2B space within any 18-month hold is materially above 50%

Means / Motive / Opportunity test:
- Means: organic growth deceleration is an inherent risk in any maturing growth story
- Motive: not a management choice (no motive leg); environmental
- Opportunity: any macro slowdown or sector saturation provides the trigger; ⚠️ two-of-three (means + opportunity) — structural watch

Disconfirming evidence: An addressable market that is structurally expanding (not share-of-wallet), with explicit new-vertical or new-geography optionality not yet priced into consensus, would disconfirm the growth-ceiling hypothesis

Monitoring checklist:

- New customer count / logo additions per quarter
- Pipeline conversion rate and average deal size trends

Interaction with other failure modes: Correlated with Failure Narrative 2 (deleverage) and Narrative 8 (FALSE FRIEND); triggers narrative 11 (M&A as compensatory action with integration risk); null interaction with cost narratives independently

Sceptical bullet: Disconfirming evidence requires addressable market data and pipeline conversion transparency — absent these, growth narrative sustainability is an open question

Failure narrative: A well-capitalised new entrant — European-scale or private-equity-backed — targets the company's core market with a lower-price offer, forcing price concessions on renewals and new wins; transmission: competitive pressure → pricing erosion → revenue per unit falls → gross margin contracts → EPS trajectory breaks below consensus → de-rate

Early warning signals:
- New entrant announcements in the company's core service category
- Tender win-rate declining (if disclosed or inferrable from pipeline vs win data)
- Customer reference checks (if available through expert network) indicating alternatives being evaluated
- Average contract duration shortening (customers hedging by signing shorter terms)
- Bid/ask spread on new contract pricing widening

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — the base rate for meaningful competitive entry in European B2B services mid-markets over any 24-month window is moderate; structural barriers are often lower than management implies; PE-backed competitor roll-up strategies are an active playbook in Belgium and the Netherlands

Means / Motive / Opportunity test:
- Means: capital markets provide the means for well-funded entrants
- Motive: an attractive-margin niche attracts competitors structurally
- Opportunity: a visible, well-covered growth story is a market signal to competitors; ✅ all three legs aligned — full activation risk

Disconfirming evidence: Demonstrated pricing-power retention over trailing 8 quarters with disclosed average selling price trends; evidence of high switching costs (data, integration, regulatory); certified barriers that are not merely incumbent scale

Monitoring checklist:
- Disclosed or inferrable average selling price per unit
- Tender win-rate trend (or proxy metrics)
- Competitor announcements in Belgian / European home market
- Customer switching cost proxies (integration depth, data lock-in, regulatory switching barriers)

Interaction with other failure modes: Correlated with Narratives 1 (revenue quality) and 2 (cost stickiness); amplifies compound failure risk significantly; interacts with Narrative 8 (FALSE FRIEND — short-term beat before competitive impact visible)

Sceptical bullet: Disconfirming evidence requires explicit pricing-trend data — management's qualitative "strong competitive position" commentary is NOT disconfirming evidence

§7 — Failure Narrative 6: Management Capability Ceiling

Failure narrative: The management team has performed well in a benign growth environment but lacks the depth and process to manage complexity at scale; as the company grows through an inflection point, execution slippage accumulates in multiple functions simultaneously — operations, finance, people — until it surfaces in a profit warning; transmission: execution complexity exceeds management bandwidth → multiple small misses accumulate → a single quarter's guidance miss triggers re-evaluation of the entire management credibility narrative → multiple and EPS estimates both reset downward simultaneously

Early warning signals:
- Senior management departures (CFO, COO, divisional MDs) within 6-month window
- Internal audit or control comments in the annual report flagging process weaknesses
- Working capital deterioration as a proxy for operational complexity (DSO, DPO trends)
- Customer complaint indicators or service quality deterioration (if measurable externally)
- Unusual volatility in quarterly results (sequential guidance accuracy declining)

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — management capability ceilings are a known failure pattern for founder-led or early-stage-PE-backed Belgian mid-caps transitioning to institutional-scale governance; the base rate for at least one management transition event in this company size range over a 24-month window is non-trivial; prior memos should be consulted for management depth assessment

Means / Motive / Opportunity test:
- Means: the capability gap (if it exists) is already present
- Motive: not a management motive (no intentional failure); environmental
- Opportunity: a period of rapid organic growth or integration of acquisitions activates the risk; ⚠️ two-of-three (means + opportunity) — elevated watch during growth phases

Disconfirming evidence: A documented second-tier management bench, explicit succession planning disclosure, demonstrated clean execution track record in multiple simultaneous strategic initiatives, and absence of working-capital deterioration would disconfirm

Monitoring checklist:
- C-suite and divisional management tenure and departure rate
- Working capital cycle stability (DSO, DIO, DPO quarterly trend)
- Guidance accuracy (actual vs. guided, trailing 6-8 quarters)
- Annual report control framework disclosures

Interaction with other failure modes: Correlated with Narrative 7 (M&A execution failure); feeds Narrative 4 (growth narrative break); partial interaction with FALSE FRIEND Narratives 8-10 (management may suppress warning signals)

§8 — Failure Narrative 7: M&A Integration Failure

Failure narrative: If the company pursues an acquisition to maintain growth narrative (a compensatory M&A response to organic deceleration), the integration absorbs disproportionate management bandwidth and generates cost overruns, goodwill impairment risk, or revenue dis-synergies; transmission: acquisition → integration costs exceed estimates → headline EPS diluted → goodwill impairment risk flagged → investors re-price for balance sheet risk → de-rate amplified by leverage if deal is debt-financed

Early warning signals:
- Acquisition announced with aggressive synergy assumptions and tight integration timeline
- Target company had prior profitability issues or customer concentration risk
- Disclosed integration costs rising quarter-on-quarter post-close
- Revenue of acquired entity declining post-integration (customer flight)
- Management time allocation visibly skewing toward integration at expense of organic business

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — M&A as a compensatory growth mechanism is a well-documented failure pathway in European mid-cap B2B; the base rate for at least one material integration stumble in deals where the acquirer is under organic growth pressure is elevated above the general M&A base rate

Means / Motive / Opportunity test:
- Means: access to debt markets + equity provides acquisition means
- Motive: management incentives and narrative maintenance create the motive to pursue compensatory M&A when organic slows
- Opportunity: acquisition targets exist in a fragmented European B2B market; ✅ all three legs aligned if organic growth decelerates

Disconfirming evidence: A stated and enforced capital allocation discipline (explicit M&A size ceiling, hurdle rate disclosure, integration-track-record evidence), or current balance sheet that structurally limits acquisition capacity, would disconfirm

Monitoring checklist:
- Management commentary on M&A pipeline and appetite
- Balance sheet leverage ratio trend
- Any announced or rumoured acquisition targets
- Post-acquisition revenue and margin performance of prior deals
- Sell-side reaction to any acquisition announcement (upgrade vs. concern split)

Interaction with other failure modes: Triggers and amplifies Narrative 6 (capability ceiling); correlated with Narrative 4 (growth narrative compensatory mechanism); potentially interacts with Narrative 3 (if acquisition targets a customer-concentration risk mitigation)

Sceptical bullet: Disconfirming evidence is a formal, disclosed capital allocation framework — management's verbal "discipline" statements are not disconfirming without structural constraints

§9 — Failure Narrative 8 (FALSE FRIEND): Earnings Beat from Unsustainable Mix

Failure narrative (FALSE FRIEND): The company reports an earnings beat in the next 1-2 quarters driven by a favourable revenue mix (higher-margin legacy contracts renewing at peak, one-time contract completions with high recognition, or deferred costs hitting the P&L late); investors interpret the beat as confirmation of the thesis; in reality, the mix benefit is non-recurring, and the underlying run-rate margin is materially below the implied guidance; transmission: reported beat → investor confidence → position size increases → then sustainable-margin reality emerges → double de-rate (EPS + multiple)

Early warning signals:
- Beat driven by "revenue mix" with management unable or unwilling to quantify the sustainable component
- Gross margin above the trend line by an unusual amount in the beat quarter
- One-time items or project completions cited as partial beat drivers
- Backlog margin (if disclosed) declining even as reported margin is expanding
- Customer concentration in high-margin accounts remaining elevated despite diversification narrative

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — unsustainable-mix beats are the single most common FALSE FRIEND pattern in European mid-cap B2B services; the base rate for at least one such event in a name with growth-narrative exposure is very high; this is the canonical "confirm-at-peak" risk

Means / Motive / Opportunity test:
- Means: accounting for revenue mix is complex and opaque; management has structural information advantage
- Motive: management incentives (bonus, RSUs, personal shareholding) create a motive to present beats positively
- Opportunity: every quarter provides the opportunity to present mix benefits as structural improvements; ✅ all three legs aligned — highest false-friend activation risk

Disconfirming evidence: Explicit disclosure of sustainable vs. one-time margin components, cohort-level margin disclosure showing consistency across vintages, and management guidance that does NOT raise the bar after a beat quarter (which would implicitly confirm sustainability)

Monitoring checklist:
- Beat attribution breakdown (price vs. volume vs. mix vs. one-time)
- Gross margin trend line (10-quarter smoothed vs. single-quarter actuals)
- Forward guidance raise vs. beat magnitude ratio (if guidance raise < beat, sustainability is questioned)
- Backlog composition and margin profile
- Management commentary tone: "normalised" vs. "structural" language

Interaction with other failure modes: This is the amplifier of Narratives 1, 2, and 5 — the FALSE FRIEND beat provides the window of confidence during which the underlying deterioration accelerates; also interacts with Narrative 10 (capex deferral FALSE FRIEND)

Sceptical bullet: The critical disconfirming data is a management guidance raise that exceeds the beat magnitude — absent that, every beat is suspect as a mix artefact until proven otherwise

§10 — Failure Narrative 9 (FALSE FRIEND): Margin Expansion from Capex Deferral / R&D Under-Investment

Failure narrative (FALSE FRIEND): Reported EBIT margin expands because the company has been deferring maintenance capex, technology investment, or R&D spend; investors read the margin expansion as operating leverage confirmation; in reality, the deferred investment creates a capability deficit that will require accelerated catch-up capex or will manifest as customer attrition from declining service quality; transmission: capex deferral → short-term margin expansion → thesis confirmation → then catch-up capex wave → FCF compression → EPS misses → de-rate

Early warning signals:
- Capex as a percentage of revenue falling below sector norms for 2+ consecutive years
- R&D spend (if applicable) declining in absolute terms or as percentage of revenue
- Technology refresh cycle commentary absent from management discussions
- Asset age indicators rising (disclosed in fixed-asset notes)
- Customer satisfaction scores (if externally available) or service quality metrics declining

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — capex deferral as a short-term margin inflation tool is well-documented in European mid-cap industrials and services; the base rate for this pattern specifically is lower than the mix-beat false friend but higher than the market prices; requires the company to have a significant ongoing capex requirement (conditional on business model)

Means / Motive / Opportunity test:
- Means: CFO has direct control over capex timing; deferral is mechanically straightforward
- Motive: management incentives linked to EBIT or EBIT margin create explicit motive for deferral
- Opportunity: short investment cycles or discretionary technology spend are the opportunity; ✅ all three legs aligned if business model has meaningful ongoing capex requirements

Disconfirming evidence: Consistent capex-to-revenue ratio at or above industry norms, explicit technology refresh disclosures in annual reports, absence of asset impairment flags in auditor commentary, and FCF conversion rate that is consistent with (not higher than) reported EBIT margins would disconfirm

Monitoring checklist:
- Capex / revenue ratio (quarterly, sector-benchmarked)
- D&A / capex ratio (rising D&A/capex implies aging asset base)
- FCF conversion vs. EBIT (unusually high FCF conversion can signal under-investment)
- Technology investment commentary in management discussions
- Any disclosed IT or infrastructure modernisation programmes

Interaction with other failure modes: Directly correlated with Narrative 6 (management capability ceiling); amplifies Narrative 8 (both false-friend modes can co-occur, creating a compounding confirmation trap); interacts with Narrative 2 (cost stickiness — deferred tech creates future cost to replace)

Sceptical bullet: Disconfirming evidence requires explicit capex ratio benchmarking — FCF that is materially higher than EBIT-minus-taxes should be a red flag, not a positive signal

§11 — Failure Narrative 10 (FALSE FRIEND): Balance-Sheet and Working-Capital Normalisation Concealment

Failure narrative (FALSE FRIEND): Reported cash generation appears strong, but is partly a function of working-capital timing (deferred payables, accelerated receivables collection, or customer prepayments that represent pull-forward demand); the market reads strong cash conversion as evidence of earnings quality; in reality, the working-capital benefit reverses in subsequent quarters and FCF normalises materially lower; transmission: elevated apparent cash conversion → thesis reinforced → then WC reversal → FCF misses → re-evaluation of earnings quality → de-rate

Early warning signals:
- DPO (days payable outstanding) rising quarter-on-quarter (stretched supplier payments)
- DSO declining unusually (accelerated collection that may not be repeatable)
- Deferred revenue or customer advance payments growing on the balance sheet
- FCF materially above net income for two or more consecutive quarters without capex deferral explanation
- Management emphasising "cash conversion" without granular WC bridge disclosure

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — working-capital timing as a false-friend signal is a classic forensic accounting pattern; base rate for at least one quarter of WC-inflated cash conversion in a growing mid-cap is moderate; the risk escalates if the company is growing revenue rapidly (new customer onboarding often generates advance payments that are non-recurring in nature)

Means / Motive / Opportunity test:
- Means: WC management is within CFO's control
- Motive: strong cash conversion supports thesis narrative and potentially covenants
- Opportunity: rapid growth provides natural WC timing opportunities; ✅ all three legs present

Disconfirming evidence: Explicit working-capital bridge disclosure (DSO / DIO / DPO separately), stable or declining DPO trend, FCF consistently aligned with net income minus capex without large WC movements, and third-party audit commentary on WC policies would disconfirm

Monitoring checklist:
- DSO, DIO, DPO quarterly trend (cash conversion cycle)
- Deferred revenue balance as % of revenues
- FCF / EBITDA conversion ratio vs. prior four quarters
- Payables creditor days vs. sector norms
- Any audit committee or auditor commentary on revenue recognition or working capital

Interaction with other failure modes: Correlated with Narrative 6 (management capability); feeds FALSE FRIEND density (this is the third false-friend mode, creating high false-friend density overall); directly interacts with Narrative 9 (both false-friend modes can operate simultaneously — capex deferral + WC timing = peak-false-friend quarter)

Sceptical bullet: The disconfirming evidence is a transparent, granular WC bridge — without it, cash conversion is an unauditable thesis-support claim

§12 — Failure Narrative 11: Regulatory / Political Risk in Belgian Domestic Market

Failure narrative: Belgian political fragmentation creates policy volatility — including potential sector-specific regulation, public procurement rule changes, or state-aid / subsidy withdrawal — that disrupts the company's operating environment or contract base; transmission: regulatory change → contract renegotiation or cancellation → revenue step-down → EPS miss → sector de-rate (Belgium-specific discount applied)

Early warning signals:
- Belgian federal/regional government formation negotiations producing adversarial policy signals for the sector
- Regulatory consultation documents in the company's sector category
- EU-level directives that conflict with existing Belgian operational practices
- Management commentary introducing regulatory risk language not previously present
- Peer companies in the Belgian domestic market flagging regulatory uncertainty

Plausibility verdict: Low plausibility for immediate activation (Belgium's regulatory environment is relatively stable for established B2B services); elevated to medium plausibility if the company has public-sector contract exposure or operates in a regulated subsector; Belgian political complexity creates a structural background risk that cannot be dismissed

Means / Motive / Opportunity test:
- Means: regulatory / legislative changes are within government's power
- Motive: policy motive depends on sector; if the company is in a politically sensitive area (healthcare, energy, infrastructure), motive is higher
- Opportunity: Belgian coalition government formation regularly creates policy uncertainty windows; ⚠️ two-of-three (means + opportunity) — treat as watch unless sector is politically sensitive

Disconfirming evidence: Revenue base that is predominantly private-sector with limited government/regulated exposure; explicit contractual protections against regulatory changes; diversified geographic exposure beyond Belgian domestic market would disconfirm

Monitoring checklist:
- Belgian federal policy announcements in the relevant sector
- Public-sector revenue concentration (% of total revenues)
- EU regulatory pipeline relevant to the company's activity
- Geographic revenue split (Belgian domestic vs. other EU)
- Any disclosed regulatory risk language in annual report

Interaction with other failure modes: Limited direct correlation; can interact with Narrative 3 (customer concentration if government is an anchor customer); provides a geographic-concentration compound risk alongside Narrative 4

Sceptical bullet: Disconfirming evidence is explicit geographic and customer-type diversification data — Belgian political risk is a resident background factor that cannot be qualitatively dismissed

4. PART B — META-ANALYSIS

J-front verdict: Two correlated failure clusters — revenue-quality/cost-stickiness and false-friend confirmation — dominate the scenario space; joint probability of zero failures in an 18-month hold is low

  • Scenario cluster A — Revenue deterioration: Narratives 1, 4, 5 cluster together; all three involve revenue quality or growth narrative; they can activate sequentially (pricing erosion leads to growth deceleration leads to narrative break) or simultaneously under competitive entry
  • Scenario cluster B — Cost and operating leverage: Narratives 2 and 6 are tightly correlated; Belgian labour rigidity amplifies the management-capability risk because the cost structure cannot be adjusted even if management recognises the problem
  • Scenario cluster C — FALSE FRIEND confirmation trap: Narratives 8, 9, 10 are all false-friend modes and they can co-activate in the same quarter; a single reporting period can feature a mix-driven beat (8) + capex-deferred margin expansion (9) + WC-inflated cash conversion (10) simultaneously, creating maximum confirmation bias at the moment of greatest underlying risk
  • Scenario cluster D — Strategic / governance: Narratives 6, 7, 11 are relatively independent of the core revenue/margin clusters; they represent background risks that can cascade if triggered but are not the primary failure driver absent a specific catalyst
  • Correlated failures — highest risk pair: Narratives 1 + 2 (revenue quality erosion + cost stickiness) — these two activate together without any external catalyst; a company that is losing per-unit pricing while its cost base is fixed will generate a compound EPS miss that is larger than either narrative alone
  • Correlated failures — highest surprise pair: Narratives 8 + 9 + 10 (three false-friend modes co-activating) — this trio creates a compound confirmation window that is the most dangerous scenario for a concentrated long investor because it produces maximum conviction at the point of maximum risk
  • Joint probability of zero failures: Low — given 11 distinct narratives, 5 of which have high-or-medium plausibility AND means/motive/opportunity fully aligned, the probability that NONE of these scenarios partially activates in an 18-24 month hold is low; this is NOT a bearish conclusion on its own (partial activations can be priced in), but it indicates the thesis is not stress-free
  • Joint probability of 2+ failures: Medium-to-elevated — the cluster structure means failures are correlated, not independent; if Cluster A activates, Cluster B likely follows; the compounding mechanism is the key risk

Compound monitorables — most-referenced across all narratives:
- Revenue-per-unit / average selling price trend (appears in Narratives 1, 4, 5)
- Staff costs / revenue ratio (appears in Narratives 2, 6, 7)
- Management guidance language shift (appears in Narratives 3, 4, 6, 8)
- Capex / revenue ratio (appears in Narratives 9, 10)
- Customer concentration metrics (appears in Narratives 1, 3, 8)
