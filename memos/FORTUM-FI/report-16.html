§1 — CEO Assessment

- Prior Fortum career: Executive Vice President, City Solutions and India (2018-2021); SVP Thermal Power and India before that — deep operational background in Fortum's core asset base
- Education: M.Sc. (Tech.) Helsinki University of Technology (now Aalto University); MBA Henley Business School — standard Nordic utility C-suite profile, no red flag
- Finnish national, career essentially built entirely within Fortum ecosystem — rare to have a CEO with this degree of internal continuity at a utility of this size; no prior external CEO tenure

CEO tenure at Fortum:
- Just over 2.5 years in the CEO seat as of May 2026 — tenure is short; the track record window is narrow
- Fortum's context at his appointment was exceptional: the company had just absorbed a ~€28-30bn write-off on Uniper, was navigating Uniper's separation from the group, and managing political/reputational fallout with the Finnish State — the transition was crisis management first, strategy second

- Capital allocation: FY2023 and FY2024 saw stepped-up shareholder returns (dividends); no significant buyback programme which is consistent with the Finnish State's preferred income-extraction route; capex directed at hydro maintenance and Baltic wind development — moderate ambition

CEO track record prior (pre-CEO roles at Fortum):

- India operations: Fortum has a small solar presence in India; managed during a growth phase for that market but the India segment remains subscale

- Education: not publicly prominent in Fortum filings; standard European utility CFO profile

§6 — Governance Structure + Red Flags

J-front verdict: Clean-to-mixed — no material governance red flags on the standard checklist; Finnish governance standards are procedurally strong, but State ownership creates structural governance constraints not captured in standard independence metrics

Voting rights structure:
- Standard one-share-one-vote under Finnish company law; no dual-class structure
- The Finnish State holds ~50.76% economic and voting interest (via combination of direct holding and Solidium holding) — this is not a dual-class structure technically, but it is a majority-control structure economically
- Solidium Oy is the Finnish State's investment company vehicle; it holds approximately 15-16% directly; the Finnish State also holds approximately 35-36% directly through government shareholding — combined ~51% effective voting control
- No golden share; no special veto rights beyond majority voting — however, majority ownership confers all necessary veto power without requiring special share classes
- Anti-takeover: Finnish law provides standard takeover bid rules; no specific Fortum poison pill identified; the State's majority stake is itself the ultimate anti-takeover defence

Related-party transactions:
- State as majority shareholder: Fortum's primary related-party exposure is to the Finnish State; this manifests through: (a) political influence on dividend policy — dividend maintained/prioritised partly as State income mechanism; (b) regulated electricity network tariffs in Finnish grid (Fingrid — Fortum sold its Fingrid stake historically, so limited current exposure); (c) potential future policy direction on nuclear or CHP assets
- No evidence of commercially concerning related-party transactions in the L3Y public record
- Uniper: the Uniper transaction (historical) represented a spectacular related-party-adjacent governance failure — not technically a related-party transaction, but a €28bn impairment driven by decisions made before Ollus/Günther's appointment; the new management team explicitly distances itself from the Uniper era

Audit history:
- Auditor: PricewaterhouseCoopers (PwC) — long-standing auditor for Fortum; no auditor change identified in L5Y
- No material weaknesses noted in L3Y audit reports
- No restatements identified
- Audit committee independence: confirmed by board composition (see §4)

Legal and regulatory issues:
- No SEC filings (Fortum is Finnish-listed only; no US ADR programme generating SEC disclosure obligations)
- Finnish FSA (Finanssivalvonta) supervision: standard utility regulatory oversight; no FSA enforcement action identified in public record L3Y
- Nordpool/electricity market regulatory compliance: standard compliance exposure; no material breach identified
- Uniper-related litigation: there are ongoing legacy matters related to Uniper separation but these are Uniper's (now wholly German-state-owned) issues, not Fortum's ongoing liabilities; Fortum executed a clean separation agreement — no material residual Uniper litigation risk on Fortum's balance sheet beyond what is disclosed and provisioned
- Environmental permits: Finnish nuclear regulator oversees Fortum's nuclear interests (TVO/OL3 participation); no identified permit breaches
- Russian asset: Fortum had significant Russian power generation assets (PAO Fortum subsidiary) that were effectively expropriated/blocked by Russia in 2022-2023 following Ukraine invasion sanctions; total Russian asset impairment was approximately €1.6-2.0bn; this was a recognised loss, not a governance failure per se, though it raises questions about how long management held onto Russian assets post-invasion (this was under Rauramo/predecessor management, not Ollus)
- No class action lawsuits or securities fraud suits identified in Finnish or European courts against current management

Whistleblower / governance complaints:
- No public whistleblower incidents identified in Fortum's L3Y record
- Fortum operates a standard ethics hotline (Fortum Speak Up channel) per Finnish CG Code requirement; no disclosure of material investigations arising from the channel in Annual Report L3Y

Anti-takeover provisions:
- Finnish State's majority ownership is the primary (and overwhelming) anti-takeover mechanism; any would-be acquirer would require Finnish government approval to acquire Fortum — effectively impossible for a foreign industrial buyer without political consent
- This protects Fortum's stability but also eliminates takeover premium optionality for minority shareholders

Bearish read on governance:
- The bear case: the single red flag that would materially invalidate the operator-quality thesis is not a technical governance failure (dual-class, fraud, restatement) but a structural one — the Finnish State's majority control means that capital allocation, dividend policy, strategic direction, and board composition are all ultimately subordinate to Finnish government policy priorities; if the Finnish government decides (a) to prioritise Fortum as a national energy security instrument over a profit-maximising vehicle, (b) to suppress dividends in favour of capex directed at politically motivated projects, or (c) to direct Fortum into another politically-driven M&A adventure analogous to the Uniper disaster — minority shareholders have limited recourse; there is no activist threat, no hostile takeover premium, and no board independence sufficient to resist a coordinated government shareholder push
