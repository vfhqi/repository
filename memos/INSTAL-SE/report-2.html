[C] INSTAL — Q2: Change Forces — IG Stage

Source: Claude [C] | Date: 2026-05-06 | Target: ~5,500w | Stage: IG

KEY FINDINGS (BLUF)

  • [#J] Verdict: Multiple overlapping change forces are active simultaneously — CEO return, restructuring, Germany entry, and cycle turn. The convergence is unusual; most turnaround situations have one primary catalyst. Here there are three: (1) founder CEO re-engagement with explicit margin mandate; (2) first international step via Fabri, funded pre-majority; (3) Nordic cycle inflecting upward after two-year contraction. Whether these reinforce or distract from each other is the central analytical question at IG stage.

  • [#J] Fulcrum Driver tentative verdict: PLAUSIBLE but NOT YET CONFIRMED. The CEO change + Instalco 2.0 restructuring is the strongest candidate FD. Q4 2025 (+39% EBITA YoY on flat revenue) and Q1 2026 (EBITA margin 5.8%, backlog +15%) provide early validation. But Q4 2025 was the first post-restructuring quarter; sample size of one is insufficient to confirm structural vs cyclical.

  • [#J] BRAV comparison: INSTAL has superior Q1 2026 EBITA margin (5.8%) vs BRAV (4.6%) — INSTAL now leads on margin despite being the smaller, lower-service-mix player. This is a genuine outlier relative to base rate expectations. BRAV guided SEK 50-60M further restructuring costs through 2026; INSTAL has no equivalent restructuring drag flagged.

  • [#J] Assemblin-Caverion (ASCA) at SEK ~43bn creates strategic urgency for INSTAL. At SEK ~13-14bn revenue, INSTAL is now roughly one-third the size of the new Nordic #1. The scale gap is not immediately threatening to INSTAL's local-density model, but it compresses the window to build a durable #2 position before ASCA uses its scale to price down on multi-site contracts.

  • [#J] Germany/Fabri: structurally interesting, near-term financially immaterial. Fabri at ~EUR 100-105M revenue (est. 20 companies by Q1 2026) is less than 8% of INSTAL group revenue. Majority consolidation expected H2 2026 at earliest. Not a near-term EPS driver; a 3-5 year optionality call.

  • [#J] Service mix at 37-38% remains a structural disadvantage vs peers. BRAV historically ~50% service; ASCA skews even higher post-Caverion. INSTAL's project-heavy mix amplifies cycle exposure, reduces earnings quality, and caps achievable steady-state EBITA margin. The gap to BRAV is ~10-12pp; closing this is a multi-year, not quarterly, task.

S1 — INTERNAL CHANGE FORCES (Leadership, Strategy, Capital Allocation)

[#J] CEO change: Per Sjostrand's return is the single most significant company-specific event since the IPO. The founder-CEO archetype (WL: animal-ceo) carries a clear strategic fingerprint — decentralised, acquisition-driven, margin-disciplined — but 4 years of absence from the operating seat creates genuine uncertainty about whether the original playbook still fits the evolved structure.

  • CEO change: Robin Boheman left 31 July 2025; Per Sjostrand (founder, former CEO 2014-2021) took over as interim CEO immediately, confirmed permanent in December 2025. Board member Johnny Alvarsson elevated to Chairman.
  • CEO change: Sjostrand simultaneously holds 6 external Chairman roles (Green Landscaping, Uniwater, Handverksgruppen, Eltel, ByggPartnerGruppen, Knowit) — governance yellow flag; no Swedish code breach but attention dilution risk is real.
  • CEO change: Boheman's tenure (2021-2025) coincided with margin compression, leverage expansion, and the 2022-2024 Nordic downturn. Whether compression was primarily macro-driven or management-driven is analytically important — Sjostrand's return implies the board judged at least partial management causation.
  • CEO change: Sjostrand's stated mandate is explicit: "ensure the Group's strategy Instalco 2.0 is put into practice, that profitability is strengthened, and that the entrepreneur-driven way of working continues to develop." Margin before volume is the declared priority.
  • CEO change: Applying WL animal-ceo lens — Sjostrand is a "builder-operator" archetype: founded at SEK 0, built to SEK 13.6bn, 100+ acquisitions, 6,200 employees. First return action: restructured the org (country-based, September 2025), not a new acquisition. Consistent with builder-operators who return to fix operational foundations before resuming capital deployment.
  • CEO change: [X] Sceptical frame — at age ~67, with 6 board seats, Sjostrand's operating bandwidth is finite. The original build (2014-2021) was done with full-time focus on a greenfield model. The current task (fixing a SEK 13bn complex, restructuring mid-cycle, integrating Germany) is arguably harder. Succession planning is not discussed publicly.

[#J] Instalco 2.0 restructuring: The new country-based organisation (Sweden / Norway / Finland + Tech/Consulting unit, effective Sep 2025) is the operational expression of the CEO mandate. Early financial results are directionally positive but two quarters is an insufficient sample for structural confirmation. [#A]

  • CEO change: Old structure had 13 business areas with limited cross-subsidiary transparency. New structure creates country P&Ls, COO accountability per geography, shared-services leverage, and clearer benchmarking across the portfolio.
  • CEO change: Q4 2025 outcomes attributable to Instalco 2.0: 8 underperforming subsidiary closures, ~110 layoffs, ~100 contractor terminations. Not large in absolute terms (vs ~6,200 employees) but symbolically significant as operational triage.
  • CEO change: EBITA Q4 2025 +39% YoY on revenue +4.4% — margin +190bp YoY to 7.2%. If structural (not seasonal), the run-rate EBITA margin is meaningfully above FY2025's 5.9%.
  • CEO change: Q1 2026 (first quarter under new country-segment reporting): EBITA SEK 201M, margin 5.8%, organic growth 4.9%, order backlog +15% to SEK 10.38bn. Revenue +4.4% to SEK 3,438M. Beats both revenue and EBIT estimates.
  • CEO change: [X] Sceptical frame — Instalco 2.0 is unproven beyond two quarters. The 13-business-area structure that it replaced was itself the product of years of acquisitions; unwinding it creates subsidiary CEO attrition risk if autonomy is perceived as reduced. No public KPI targets disclosed (overhead ratio, labour cost %, project GM thresholds).

[#J] Capital allocation: post-restructuring regime shift from volume to discipline. Net debt/EBITDA at 2.8x (Dec 2025), above 2.5x target. New SEK 3.4bn credit facility signed June 2025. M&A optionality is rebuilding but constrained until 2.5x target is achieved. [#A]

  • M&A engine: FY2025 Nordic acquisition count deliberately reduced vs the 2017-2021 pace (~15-20 deals/year). Only 2-3 deals confirmed in 2025: Alf Naslunds Eltjanst (March 2025, electrical); TSM Taksakerhetsmontorerna (April 2026, SEK 190M revenue, 60 employees, scaffolding/fall protection).
  • M&A engine: TSM acquisition signals platform-building within Sweden (joining Highcon and Enter Stallningar to create a nationwide scaffolding vertical) — willingness to build service verticals, not just add discipline capacity.
  • M&A engine: The Fabri step-2 majority acquisition (expected H2 2026, conditional on EBITDA thresholds) will require EUR 40-60M+ additional capital. This is the primary near-term capital allocation event and will consume most incremental M&A capacity in 2026. [X]
  • M&A engine: WL flywheel-vs-treadmill: INSTAL's original flywheel — acquire, integrate, replicate model, acquire again — slowed to treadmill pace under Boheman (margin compression + leverage overhang). Sjostrand's re-engagement is rebuilding the flywheel foundation; the full cycle is not yet re-engaged. Risk: if integration quality has declined over 100+ deals, the portfolio may require more remediation before the next flywheel turn.
  • M&A engine: [X] Sceptical frame — reduced acquisition cadence means reduced near-term inorganic revenue growth. The 2026 revenue consensus (~SEK 14bn, +3%) is almost entirely organic-growth-dependent. If organic disappoints, there is limited M&A pull-forward to offset.

S2 — DEMAND ENVIRONMENT AND CYCLE POSITION

[#J] Cycle position: INSTAL is at the early stages of a Nordic construction cycle recovery after a 2023-2025 contraction. The recovery is real but uneven — infrastructure and industrial lead, residential lags, commercial mixed. INSTAL's multi-technical model means it benefits broadly but unevenly across segments. [#A]

  • Cycle position: Nordic construction contracted ~4% in 2025 (Veidekke data). Recovery forecast from 2026: Sweden +10% (housing starts +21% around Stockholm/Gothenburg), Finland +2.6%, Norway +3.6% CAGR 2026-2029. Scandinavia market 4.14% CAGR 2026-2031 (Mordor Intelligence).
  • Cycle position: Sweden = ~76% of INSTAL revenue. Sweden recovery is infrastructure-led (SEK 564bn National Infrastructure Plan 2026-2037: SEK 354bn roads, SEK 210bn rail). INSTAL's electrical and mechanical installations are direct beneficiaries of infrastructure and rail projects.
  • Cycle position: Swedish residential still weak in 2025 (sector -2.6%), but mortgage rate cuts and housing shortage are building pent-up demand. [X] The 2026 +10% Sweden construction forecast implies residential recovery — which has disappointed in 2023 and 2024. Delivery risk remains high.
  • Cycle position: Q1 2026 order backlog +15% to SEK 10.38bn — the strongest early-cycle demand signal in INSTAL's recent history. Combined with 4.9% organic growth vs BRAV's 1%, demand is clearly accelerating ahead of the peer group.
  • Cycle position: INSTAL's portfolio: 80% recurring customers, 5,000+ active projects, top 10 projects each below SEK 50M. Deliberately diversified, small-project orientation provides cycle cushioning but also means INSTAL is more exposed to broad construction volumes than to any single large contract.
  • Cycle position: Norway: backlog +5.8% YoY at Q3 2025 to SEK 9,026M (Norway segment). Norway macroeconomic outlook: +3.6% CAGR construction 2026-2029 (GlobalData). Norway est. ~15% of INSTAL revenue.
  • Cycle position: Finland est. ~8-10% of INSTAL revenue. Finland +2.6% 2026 construction growth — the only Nordic market already positive in 2025.
  • Cycle position: [X] Sceptical frame — the +15% backlog growth includes equity-accounted Fabri contributions and possible normalisation of postponed work. True like-for-like organic backlog growth by geography is not disclosed.

[#J] Energy transition demand: a genuine structural tailwind, not a cyclical bounce. INSTAL's electrical capability (largest discipline by revenue) is directly positioned for the building electrification agenda across Sweden, Norway, and Finland. [#A]

  • Cycle position: EU Energy Performance of Buildings Directive (EPBD) recast requires all EU buildings to eliminate direct fossil fuel heating by 2040. Sweden/Norway are ahead of EU average in heat pump penetration, but the commercial real estate renovation wave is a structural order driver for the next decade.
  • Cycle position: Nordic data centre construction: USD 2.21bn (2024) to USD 7.83bn (2030) at 23.42% CAGR (Arizton). Nordics = 15% of European pipeline. Microsoft, Meta, Google active. INSTAL won Stockholm Exergi reference project (district heating + data centre cooling integration). Requires sophisticated HVAC, electrical distribution, UPS — core INSTAL disciplines.
  • Cycle position: Sweden's EV charging infrastructure: ~2.5M EVs targeted by 2030 vs ~750k in 2024. Commercial EV charging (logistics hubs, retail parks, housing associations) requires licensed electrical installers. INSTAL subsidiary Vallacom is a named specialist in EV charging and solar storage.
  • Cycle position: Sweden's National Infrastructure Plan 2026-2037 (SEK 564bn total): high-voltage electrical installation along rail corridors, signalling, depot electrical — each a potential revenue line for INSTAL electrical subsidiaries.
  • Cycle position: [X] Sceptical frame — energy transition work is competitive. Heat pump-only, solar-only, and EV-charging-only specialists aggressively price against multi-technical generalists in individual project categories. INSTAL's cross-sell advantage requires winning the initial project competitively, which is not guaranteed.

[#J] Labour market: a dual-edged force — scarcity creates pricing power for well-resourced players but wage inflation is a margin headwind for all. [#A]

  • Labour dynamic: Sweden, Norway, and Finland all have electrical and HVAC technicians on official shortage occupation lists (2025/2026). Norway explicitly lists construction technicians across engineering disciplines as critically short.
  • Labour dynamic: For INSTAL, technician scarcity is a barrier to entry (new entrants cannot hire at scale) and a pricing power support (customers willing to pay premium for reliable, licensed installer capacity). INSTAL at ~6,200 employees has an existing workforce that competitors cannot easily replicate.
  • Labour dynamic: [X] Counter: labour scarcity also means wage inflation. INSTAL's largest cost line is labour (~55-65% of installation revenue). Wage escalation above contract price escalation clauses creates margin compression — this was the 2022 problem. Mitigant: 80% recurring customers allows price re-setting at contract renewal, but the lag risk is permanent.
  • Labour dynamic: WL pricing-power lens: service contracts (38% of revenue) carry better pricing power than project work — negotiated annually, switching costs real. At 38% service, INSTAL has partial but not majority exposure to high-pricing-power work. Raising service to ~45% would materially improve the wage-price dynamic.
  • Labour dynamic: [X] Sceptical frame — INSTAL does not disclose labour cost per employee trends, limiting external assessment of the wage-margin equation. The Q4 2025 and Q1 2026 margin improvement may partly reflect reduced overtime costs as construction volumes fell in 2025, which would reverse in a recovery environment.

S3 — COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE CHANGES (incl. Assemblin-Caverion)

[#J] Assemblin-Caverion (ASCA) merger is the defining competitive event of the cycle. The creation of a SEK ~43bn Nordic #1 (vs INSTAL ~SEK 13-14bn) changes market dynamics across multiple dimensions. The threat to INSTAL's local-density model is less acute than the headline scale differential implies, but the strategic window for INSTAL to cement a durable #2 position is compressing. [#A]

  • Assemblin impact: ASCA formed April 2024 via Triton-backed combination of Assemblin and Caverion. Revenue ~EUR 3.8bn / SEK 43.5bn, ~21,900 employees, operations in 10 countries including Germany and Austria — immediately relevant given INSTAL's Fabri move into Germany.
  • Assemblin impact: ASCA's stated strategy: clear market-leading positions in all current geographies + geographical diversification + increased service revenue share. Pursuing the same mix shift as INSTAL, with far greater scale. Two players pursuing the same service improvement simultaneously may inflate competition for INSTAL's target contract segment.
  • Assemblin impact: ASCA holds est. ~14-16% of the Nordic installation market. Post-merger integration complete, its multi-site tendering capability — particularly for national accounts (large retail chains, property companies, public sector frameworks) — outstrips INSTAL's. INSTAL cannot credibly compete for a Castellum or Vasakronan national framework agreement; ASCA can.
  • Assemblin impact: INSTAL's competitive advantage vs ASCA is local density, deep subsidiary relationships, and speed on small-to-mid projects. ASCA's integration complexity creates a 12-24 month window where INSTAL can move faster on small project wins and local service contracts.
  • Assemblin impact: BRAV at ~SEK 28-29bn (Q1 2026 annualised) sits between INSTAL and ASCA. BRAV is INSTAL's most direct competitor for the mid-market SEK 5-50M project segment. BRAV's Q1 2026 EBITA margin (4.6%) is 120bp below INSTAL's (5.8%) — INSTAL can price to win margin-accretive work that BRAV cannot afford at current margin levels.
  • Assemblin impact: [X] Sceptical frame — the scale gap between INSTAL and ASCA will likely widen over 3-5 years as ASCA deploys M&A. INSTAL needs a structural positioning response (deepen service mix, build Germany platform, develop verticals like scaffolding) to avoid being squeezed between ASCA at the top and smaller local independents at the bottom.

[#J] BRAV as the direct peer benchmark: INSTAL currently outperforming on margin despite lower service mix — this is the outlier signal worth investigating further. [#A]

  • Assemblin impact: BRAV Q1 2026: EBITA margin 4.6% (SEK 325M EBITA on SEK 7,045M revenue), +1% organic growth (returning to positive after 8 consecutive quarters of organic decline). BRAV carrying SEK 20M restructuring charges in Q1 and guided SEK 50-60M restructuring through full-year 2026.
  • Assemblin impact: INSTAL Q1 2026: EBITA margin 5.8% (SEK 201M on SEK 3,438M), +4.9% organic growth, no restructuring charges flagged. INSTAL outperforming BRAV across both margin and organic growth simultaneously.
  • Assemblin impact: WL means-motive-opportunity lens on INSTAL margin outperformance: MEANS — Sjostrand cost discipline + subsidiary closure programme; MOTIVE — market knows INSTAL needs to prove 8% target; OPPORTUNITY — cycle recovery improving project selectivity. All three present, elevating signal credibility.
  • Assemblin impact: Historical base rate: BRAV has ~50% service (structurally higher quality mix). INSTAL at 38% service should structurally trail on margin. The reversal in Q1 2026 may be temporary (BRAV mid-restructuring) or may signal INSTAL's operating leverage on the cycle turn exceeds what the service-mix differential would predict.
  • Assemblin impact: BRAV adjusted underlying margin (~4.9% ex-restructuring) still 90bp below INSTAL's 5.8%. The gap is real and not fully explained by BRAV's restructuring costs alone.
  • Assemblin impact: [X] Sceptical frame — BRAV's lower Q1 2026 margin is partly restructuring drag (SEK 20M / SEK 7,045M revenue = 28bp). Adjusting: BRAV underlying ~4.9%. INSTAL gap narrows but remains material. Monitor whether INSTAL margin lead persists at Q2 2026 when BRAV's restructuring savings start flowing.

S4 — MIX SHIFT AND BUSINESS MODEL EVOLUTION

[#J] Service mix at 37-38% is INSTAL's clearest structural deficiency. The gap to BRAV (~50%) and ASCA (>50%) is not closing at pace. Without deliberate intervention — acquisitions of service-heavy businesses or organic service contract wins — the mix will drift slowly rather than shift structurally. [#A] [X]

  • Service mix shift: Q3 2025: service 37% of net sales, +2% YoY. Q4 2025: service 38%. Service growth is positive but not accelerating — not keeping pace with what would be needed to close the gap to BRAV within a 3-year horizon.
  • Service mix shift: Mechanism: service contracts arise from (a) maintenance agreements post-project completion — INSTAL's high project volumes provide a natural conversion funnel; (b) acquisitions of service-heavy businesses; (c) framework agreements with recurring property managers. INSTAL has not disclosed a dedicated service salesforce or service-specific KPIs.
  • Service mix shift: WL pricing-power lens: service work offers non-competitive pricing (customer locked in for maintenance cycle), annual re-pricing opportunity, and typically 5-8pp higher gross margin vs project work. At 38% service, ~62% of INSTAL's business is project-mode, exposed to price competition and execution risk.
  • Service mix shift: The 2022 margin collapse was concentrated in fixed-price project work where INSTAL absorbed cost inflation on locked-in prices. The service book (38%) was the margin anchor that prevented a worse outcome — the clearest empirical evidence for the value of mix improvement.
  • Service mix shift: Instalco 2.0 introduces no explicit service mix target in public communications. The 8% EBITA margin target is presented as achievable through project selectivity + cost discipline — management does not appear to treat service mix shift as the primary lever. This is a meaningful omission given BRAV's playbook.
  • Service mix shift: [X] Sceptical frame — raising service mix from 38% to 45% organically requires winning and retaining service contracts that ASCA and BRAV are also pursuing with greater scale. Given ASCA's national framework advantage, INSTAL may find the mid-market service segment increasingly contested.

[#J] Project selectivity is improving post-2022 — the risk book has been cleaned, but institutional discipline is not yet proven at scale across 100+ subsidiaries. [#A]

  • Margin path: INSTAL operates 5,000+ concurrent projects. Projects above SEK 50M require group-level approval (only 10 such projects outstanding at any time). Projects below SEK 20M approved by local subsidiary CEO. Decentralised approval is a feature (speed, local knowledge) but also a risk (inconsistent risk standards across 100+ subsidiaries).
  • Margin path: Post-2022, management stated "margin, margin, margin" — volume growth subordinated to margin. The 8 subsidiary closures and contractor terminations in 2025 demonstrate actual execution, not just rhetoric.
  • Margin path: Q4 2025 EBITA +39% on revenue +4.4% implies ~200bp of structural margin improvement in a single quarter — not explainable by volume alone. Mix improvement (exiting low-margin work) and execution quality improvement are the most plausible explanations.
  • Margin path: Q1 2026 margin 5.8% — seasonally Q1 is INSTAL's weakest quarter (lowest revenue, highest fixed cost drag). A 5.8% Q1 margin provides a clear path to 7.0-7.5% FY2026 and the stated 8% medium-term target, assuming seasonal patterns hold.
  • Margin path: BRAV Q1 2026 underlying ~4.9%. INSTAL 5.8% in its weakest quarter at lower service mix — if INSTAL reaches 7-7.5% in Q3-Q4 2026, vs a likely BRAV trajectory of 5-6% post-restructuring, INSTAL's margin lead will widen further.
  • Margin path: [X] Sceptical frame — order backlog growth (+15%) in a recovering cycle could bring forward new fixed-price project risk. The 2022 lesson is that high backlog growth preceded margin destruction. Selectivity discipline demonstrated in a downturn will be tested when the order book tightens and management faces capacity pressure.

The WL look-at-the-edges-deliberately-weird lens flags this as the most notable element: INSTAL simultaneously executing a domestic restructuring AND a first international platform investment. This is atypically ambitious for a mid-tier installer.

  • Germany platform: March 2025: INSTAL acquired 24% stake in Fabri AG for ~EUR 15M (EUR 13M new shares + EUR 2M cash). 4,647,727 INSTAL consideration shares issued. Fabri at acquisition: Nuremberg-based, 400 employees, 12 subsidiaries, 14+ locations, EUR 70M revenue.
  • Germany platform: Multi-step structure: Step 2 (majority, +27% to 51%) expected no earlier than Q1 2026 and no later than Q2 2027, conditional on agreed EBITDA thresholds. Current assessment: step 2 relevant in H2 2026. Step 3 (+17% to ~68%) follows step 2.
  • Germany platform: Fabri growth trajectory: EUR 70M at announcement (Nov 2024), 17 companies with +EUR 14M bolt-ons (Jul 2025), +EUR 18.7M and 3 more acquisitions (Q3 2025), 20 companies with +EUR 19M (Q1 2026). Estimated Fabri run-rate: EUR 100-105M annualised.
  • Germany platform: Germany installation market: ~EUR 150bn vs ~EUR 20bn total Nordics. Highly fragmented, same consolidation dynamics INSTAL exploited in Sweden 2014-2021. Germany = ~30% of European building installation market. A 1-2% German market share over 10 years would add EUR 1.5-3bn revenue — transformative at current INSTAL group scale.
  • Germany platform: WL look-at-the-edges-deliberately-weird assessment: simultaneous domestic restructuring + first international expansion is unusual. Could signal exceptional ambition or stretched management bandwidth. Mitigant: Fabri has its own management team; step 2 structure requires limited INSTAL operational input pre-majority; the Fabri opportunity was initiated under Boheman's tenure, not Sjostrand's.
  • Germany platform: WL means-motive-opportunity on Germany: MEANS — EUR 15M initial stake (small, digestible); MOTIVE — geographic diversification before Nordic consolidation saturates; OPPORTUNITY — Fabri management motivated to sell to a strategic partner rather than PE. All three present.
  • Germany platform: [X] Germany construction contracted 2-3% in 2025. German regulatory complexity (labour law, tax, building codes, certification) differs materially from Nordic. INSTAL's playbook (decentralised subsidiary model, local entrepreneur retention) is untested in the German legal/cultural environment. Fabri's EBITDA margin is not disclosed; consolidation could dilute group margins at step 2.
  • Germany platform: [X] Capital risk: step 2 majority acquisition estimated EUR 40-60M+ (based on EBITDA threshold structure). This capital competes directly with Nordic bolt-on M&A. With the flywheel in low gear, the Germany capital requirement is more constraining than it would have been at peak INSTAL FCF generation.

[#J] Nordic M&A flywheel: deliberately in low gear through 2025 but showing signs of re-engagement in 2026. [#A]

  • M&A engine: TSM Taksakerhetsmontorerna (April 2026, SEK 190M revenue, 60 employees) — scaffolding and fall protection. Combines with Highcon and Enter Stallningar into a nationwide Swedish scaffolding vertical. Signals INSTAL building services verticals alongside traditional discipline-based tuck-ins.

When leverage returns to 2.5x target (expected H2 2026/H1 2027), the flywheel can re-engage. But Nordic deal economics may have changed: ASCA and BRAV also compete for the same acquisition targets.
  • M&A engine: WL flywheel-vs-treadmill updated assessment: the flywheel mechanics (local entrepreneur motivation, integration model, cross-selling) remain intact structurally. The treadmill phase (2022-2025) was driven by macro + leverage, not an intrinsic flywheel breakdown. Sjostrand's return is the most credible catalyst to re-accelerate — his personal relationships with Nordic entrepreneur-owners are the primary sourcing advantage.
  • M&A engine: [X] Sceptical frame — historical INSTAL acquisition multiple: 5-8x EBITDA on small deals. The universe of SEK 50-200M revenue Nordic installers at that price continues to shrink as ASCA and BRAV also pursue the same targets. Competition for the best targets will increase deal multiples or force INSTAL toward lower-quality acquisitions to sustain cadence.

[#J] Pricing power trajectory: improving but unevenly distributed across work types. [#A]

  • Pricing power: Project work (62% of revenue): pricing power MEDIUM — competitive tendering, but INSTAL's local density and reputation reduce full commoditisation. Backlog +15% suggests customers are awarding contracts at terms acceptable to INSTAL, implying selectivity without volume sacrifice.
  • Pricing power: [X] Fixed-price risk: project work in a recovering market creates temptation to rebuild backlog at aggressive prices. The 2022 pattern was exactly this — high backlog, aggressive pricing, cost inflation hit. FULCRUM DRIVER ASSESSMENT

[#J] FD Verdict: PLAUSIBLE — CEO change + Instalco 2.0 restructuring is the primary candidate FD, with two quarters of early validation. [#A]

FD assessment framework: A genuine FD must be (a) company-specific (not sector beta), (b) durable (not one-quarter), and (c) financially material (>200bp margin or >15% earnings impact separable from cycle).

Candidate 1 — CEO Change + Instalco 2.0 (STRONGEST candidate):

  • Company-specific: Yes — BRAV and ASCA are not undergoing equivalent founder-return restructuring.
  • Durable signal: Q4 2025 EBITA +39% YoY; Q1 2026 EBITA margin 5.8% vs BRAV 4.6%. Two data points, below the 4-quarter threshold for structural confirmation.
