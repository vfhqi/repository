§2 — Key Driver: FX / Currency Translation

J-front verdict: FX is the gating driver — without partial NGN/KES/ZMW stabilisation, no other driver can deliver USD-denominated 25%+ IRR; medium plausibility of adequate abatement over N18-24M [#J]

Driver definition: USD-reported revenue, EBITDA, and EPS are systematically reduced by functional-currency devaluation across AAF's 14-country footprint; 17% of revenue is Nigeria , 20% Kenya , 10% Zambia [#A]

Magnitude required: Thesis requires FX translation drag to narrow from approximately -20 to -25% (FY24 reported vs CC) to -5 to -10% range over N24M to allow CC growth to flow through to USD EPS [#J]

Baseline: FY25 reported revenue approximately $1.96bn vs constant-currency growth of +12%; translation drag absorbed ~12-13 percentage points of growth [#A]
- Sub: Four consecutive half-year periods of reported USD revenue decline despite positive CC growth — this is the key bear signal [#A]

Target state: Stabilisation of NGN at ~1,600-1,700/USD, KES at ~130-135/USD, ZMW at current levels; management has explicitly stated they are not forecasting further devaluation in guidance [#A]

Required trajectory: FX drag must compress to ≤-8% by FY26 (half of recent run-rate); requires no further major devaluation of NGN specifically [#J]

Bull — historical: Post-devaluation currency stabilisation is the base pattern in sub-Saharan Africa; Nigeria CBN unified FX regime (implemented mid-2023) was the triggering event; NGN has broadly stabilised at ~1,600-1,700/USD since mid-2024 [#A]
- Sub: Kenya's FX volatility has also moderated in 2024-25; IMF programme support reduces tail-risk of disorderly adjustment [#A]

Bull — recent: H2 FY25 (Oct 2024-Mar 2025) showed narrowing of the CC-to-reported gap vs H1 FY25, consistent with base-effect improvement as the worst devaluations annualise out [#A]

Bull — mgmt: Management has guided that "the worst of the FX headwind is behind us" in FY25 results commentary; CFO commentary pointed to H1 FY26 as the first period where translation drag should be materially lower [#A]

Bull — macro: Nigeria's FX reserves have improved modestly under CBN reform; IMF Article IV for Nigeria (2024) projects NGN stability at current levels as base case [#A]

Bear — historical drag: ❌ Sub-Saharan Africa has experienced recurring devaluation cycles; NGN has depreciated in 7 of the last 10 calendar years; a single CBN policy reversal or oil-price shock can re-open the devaluation path [#A]

Bear — structural: ❌ Nigeria's fiscal deficit and dependence on oil revenue creates structural NGN vulnerability; any oil price move below $60/barrel historically creates NGN pressure [#A]

Bear — recent deterioration: KES weakened further in early 2025 on Kenya fiscal concerns; ZMW remains structurally weak linked to copper price; AAF's diversified exposure does not eliminate concentration — three currencies drive >45% of revenue [#A]

Bear — macro: ❌ USD strength cycle (Fed higher-for-longer) exerts persistent appreciation pressure on all EM currencies; DXY at 104-106 in 2025 is a persistent headwind [#A]

Quarterly trajectory: FX drag should mechanically narrow through FY26 as devaluation base effects roll off; the question is whether a NEW devaluation event (particularly Nigeria) re-starts the clock [#J]

Acceleration needed: No acceleration needed in the driver itself — stabilisation is the requirement; the bear case is a re-acceleration of devaluation, not failure to improve from here [#J]

Key dependency: Nigerian naira stability is the single most important variable; NGN represents the largest single country exposure and has historically been the largest contributor to translation drag [#J]

Downside scenario: If NGN re-devalues 30% (to ~2,200/USD), FY26 USD revenue could be flat-to-down even with 12-15% CC growth; EPS impact would be -20 to -25% vs current consensus [#J]
- Sub: This scenario is not base case but is within a 25-30% probability tail given Nigerian fiscal dynamics [#A]

Consensus alignment: Public broker commentary (Barclays, Berenberg, JP Morgan — where accessible via public channels) assumes NGN stability as the base case for FY26; estimates embed approximately -5 to -8% residual translation drag [#A]

Estimate revision: Post-FY25 results, several brokers revised FY26 USD revenue estimates modestly upward on the assumption that H1 FY26 benefits from base effects; consensus still embeds caution [#A]

Evidence gap: Watson cannot access real-time consensus FX assumptions embedded in model builds; APM should extract per-model FX assumptions from SS model notes in Pass 3 [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — the base case (stabilisation) is reasonably grounded, but a fat tail on NGN re-devaluation creates asymmetric downside that makes high plausibility inappropriate. Fulcrum-level vs APM Pass 1: VALIDATES — this is genuinely gating [#J]

Sceptical bullet: What if consensus FX assumptions are already over-optimistic? If the 14-broker SS pool has uniformly assumed NGN stability, consensus could be simultaneously optimistic on FX and the downside scenario is under-discounted in current consensus estimates [#J]

§3 — Key Driver: Mobile Money (MoMo) Revenue & Monetisation

J-front verdict: MoMo is the highest-conviction constant-currency growth driver; the underappreciated variable is monetisation (take-rate), not transaction volume — bull case rests on rate improvement, not just volume [#J]

Driver definition: Airtel Money (the MoMo segment) generates revenue through transaction fees, merchant commissions, international remittances, and financial-services products; revenue is a function of (transactions × average transaction value × take-rate) [#A]

Magnitude required: MoMo revenue must grow from ~$330-340m (FY25 reported, USD) to ~$450-500m within N24M; in CC terms this requires approximately 15-20% CAGR — achievable given recent trajectory [#J]

Baseline: MoMo revenue in FY25: ~$331m reported; ~$390m in constant currency; transaction value ~$112bn annualised; registered users ~39m; active 30-day users ~20-22m [#A]
- Sub: MoMo take-rate (revenue / transaction value) estimated at approximately 0.30-0.35% — below Kenya's M-Pesa (~0.50-0.60%) suggesting meaningful monetisation headroom [#A]

Target state: MoMo revenue ~$450-480m USD (FY27); requires either CC growth at 15-18% p.a. without FX headwind, or CC growth of 20-25% with partial FX drag of -5 to -8% [#J]

Required trajectory: Quarterly MoMo revenue run-rate needs to exit FY26 at ~$110-115m/quarter USD; FY25 Q4 run-rate was approximately $85-90m/quarter [#A]

Bull — historical: MoMo transaction value has grown at 25-30% CC CAGR over three years — one of the fastest-growing mobile money ecosystems in Africa; consistent outperformance of guidance [#A]

Bull — recent: FY25 full-year MoMo revenue +27% in CC; merchant payments (higher-margin revenue stream) grew faster than basic P2P at ~35% CC; remittances growing [#A]

Bull — mgmt: Management targets MoMo as the primary long-term value driver; CEO commentary emphasises financial services beyond basic payments (lending, savings, insurance); product roadmap expanding [#A]

Bull — competitive: AAF MoMo is the #1 or #2 mobile money player in 9 of its 14 markets; network effects in mobile money are strong once market position is established [#A]
- Sub: In Uganda and Tanzania, AAF MoMo competes directly with MTN MoMo at parity; differentiation is a live concern but not an existential threat given installed base [#A]

Bull — industry: Sub-Saharan Africa mobile money industry gross transaction value estimated at $900bn+ annually (GSMA 2024); AAF's $112bn represents ~12% share with significant headroom [#A]

Bull — initiatives: MoMo lending (Airtel Money loans) product rollout in Kenya, Tanzania, Uganda; merchant aggregation platform targeting SME merchants; international money transfer corridor expansion [#A]

Bear — historical drag: ❌ MoMo revenue has never delivered USD-reported growth above +5% in any full year due to FX translation — the constant-currency story has never converted to reported USD growth at sufficient magnitude [#A]

Bear — competitive pressure: ❌ M-Pesa (Safaricom / Vodacom) remains structurally dominant in Kenya (~84% market share in Kenya mobile money) — AAF cannot meaningfully challenge in its largest CC-revenue market [#A]
- Sub: ⚡ RARE: Watson flags this as a deliberately-weird signal — management rarely discusses M-Pesa competitive dynamics explicitly, yet Kenya is AAF's second-largest market; the competitive moat in Kenya mobile money is asymmetrically in Safaricom's favour [Cross-ref: §3]

Bear — regulatory: Kenya's Competition Authority and CBN in Nigeria have both shown appetite for regulating mobile money fees; any cap on transaction fees directly compresses take-rate [#A]

Bear — structural: MoMo monetisation (take-rate expansion) in mature markets competes against customer switching and informal alternatives; in Nigeria, bank transfer via NIBSS (effectively free via USSD) is a structural ceiling on AAF Money monetisation [#A]

Bear — execution: MoMo lending requires credit infrastructure (CRB integrations, KYC), which is being built; until infrastructure is scaled, higher-margin lending revenue remains a future-period story, not a N12M driver [#J]

Bear — SS skeptic: Some public commentary (Berenberg, accessible via public channels) notes that MoMo USD revenue has disappointed relative to CC momentum for three consecutive years; question is whether 2026 is finally the conversion year or whether it's the same story again [#A]

Quarterly trajectory: MoMo revenue trajectory needs to inflect in H1 FY26 (Apr-Sep 2025); if H1 FY26 MoMo USD revenue is not up YoY, the FX-abatement thesis is not working [#J]

Key dependency: (a) NGN/KES stability (FX — §2), (b) take-rate improvement or stability in face of regulatory pressure, (c) active-user base growth sustaining 10%+ YoY [#J]

Downside scenario: If take-rate is compressed by regulatory action AND FX re-devalues, MoMo USD revenue could be flat-to-down; this would remove the single strongest growth narrative and materially re-rate the stock downward [#J]

Evidence gap: Published take-rate by market (revenue / GMV per geography) is not disclosed; APM should attempt to extract implied take-rates from segment disclosures and management Q&A; this is the highest-value evidence gap in the driver set [#J]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility in constant-currency terms; medium plausibility in USD-reported terms — the engine is demonstrably working; the conversion challenge is FX. Fulcrum-level vs APM Pass 1: VALIDATES [#J]

Sceptical bullet: What if MoMo transaction volume growth is slowing as markets mature and the residual CC growth is entirely dependent on take-rate improvement that is structurally capped by competition and regulation? The volume-vs-rate decomposition is the most important analytical disaggregation and it is not clearly disclosed [#J]

§4 — Key Driver: Voice/Data ARPU Expansion

J-front verdict: ARPU expansion is real but pace-constrained; voice ARPU is structurally under pressure while data ARPU is growing from a low base — net ARPU trend is positive but moderate, not a primary IRR driver [#J]

Driver definition: Average Revenue Per User across voice + data services; blended ARPU improvement driven by data monetisation as 4G penetration increases and voice ARPU erosion decelerates [#A]

Magnitude required: Blended ARPU needs to grow from approximately $3.1/month (FY25 reported average across 14 markets) to $3.6-3.8/month by FY27 in CC terms; approximately 8-10% CC ARPU CAGR [#J]

Baseline: FY25 ARPU approximately $3.1/month blended (USD, across 14 markets); Nigeria ARPU notably depressed post-devaluation; East Africa ARPU is higher; data-only customers have ARPU 2-3× basic voice customers [#A]

Target state: Blended ARPU $3.5-3.8/month (CC terms) by FY27; data subscribers as % of total reaching 50%+ (from ~48% in FY25) [#J]

Bull — historical: In markets where 4G penetration crossed 40%, AAF has historically demonstrated 12-15% data ARPU uplift within 18 months; East Africa shows this pattern clearly [#A]

Bull — recent: Data ARPU grew +11% CC in FY25; total data traffic up ~40% YoY; data subscription penetration increasing across all 14 markets [#A]

Bull — mgmt: Management targets revenue per data user improvement through bundle pricing (combined voice+data+MoMo packages); tiered data pricing implementation underway in Nigeria and Tanzania [#A]

Bull — industry: Sub-Saharan Africa data ARPU remains 5-8× below comparable South/Southeast Asian markets — structural catch-up potential over a 5-10Y horizon; N24M upside from the sector mean reversion is meaningful but not dramatic [#A]

Bear — competitive pressure: ❌ Voice ARPU is in secular decline across all 14 markets as OTT substitution (WhatsApp, Facebook) displaces voice minutes; voice represented ~55% of mobile revenue in FY25 — this segment is a structural headwind [#A]

Bear — macro: ❌ Handset affordability constraints limit 4G upgrade velocity; average household income in AAF's core markets is $2-5/day — data bundle pricing is price-elastic at margins that matter for ARPU [#A]

Bear — structural: Smartphone penetration in francophone West Africa (a meaningful AAF market share) remains below 40%; 4G expansion is physically dependent on handset upgrade cycle that management cannot accelerate unilaterally [#A]

Bear — Nigeria regulatory: Nigerian Communications Commission has historically resisted tariff increases; AAF Nigeria sought a 100% tariff increase in 2024 and received limited approval — regulatory pricing ceiling constrains ARPU upside in the largest single market [#A]

Quarterly trajectory: Data ARPU likely grows +8-12% CC in FY26; voice ARPU likely declines -3 to -5% CC; net blended ARPU +4-7% CC; below the 8-10% target, suggesting modest underperformance vs required [#J]

Acceleration needed: Requires voice ARPU stabilisation (not currently visible) OR data ARPU growth accelerating above +15% CC — either requires regulatory relief or competitive normalisation [#J]

Key dependency: Nigeria tariff environment; smartphone handset penetration rate; MoMo bundling adoption (data+MoMo bundles improve blended ARPU mechanically) [#J]

Downside scenario: If voice ARPU declines -8% CC and data ARPU grows only +8% CC, blended ARPU is flat; combined with subscriber growth of +5%, service revenue growth is ~5% CC — well below thesis requirements [#J]

Evidence gap: Country-level ARPU breakdown and decomposition of voice vs data ARPU trends by market is partially disclosed; Nigeria-specific ARPU trajectory is the most important gap [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — data ARPU momentum is genuine but voice ARPU headwind and regulatory constraints on Nigeria pricing make the 8-10% CC ARPU CAGR optimistic; 5-7% CC ARPU growth is more probable. Fulcrum-level vs APM Pass 1: REVISES — not fulcrum, secondary enabling driver [#J]

Sceptical bullet: What if the Nigerian regulatory environment normalises tariff increases in 2025-26 (some indication post-NCC consultation)? This would be a positive surprise to the ARPU thesis not currently in consensus; APM should track NCC tariff rulings as a specific monitoring item [#J]

§5 — Key Driver: Cost Efficiency / EBITDA Margin Recovery

J-front verdict: Cost efficiency is a credible medium-term story but pace is constrained by energy-cost stickiness and tower lease renegotiation timelines; margin recovery is real but modest in N18M horizon [#J]

Driver definition: EBITDA margin expansion through opex reduction: energy cost (off-grid diesel to solar transition), tower lease renegotiations, and G&A efficiency; EBITDA margin targeted to recover toward 48-50% from ~46-47% in FY25 [#A]

Magnitude required: EBITDA margin improvement of 150-300bps over N24M; requires operating leverage on revenue growth plus specific cost initiatives delivering ahead of inflationary headwinds [#J]

Baseline: FY25 EBITDA margin approximately 46-47% (reported); CC EBITDA margin approximately 48-49% — FX impacts margins as USD-denominated lease and energy costs become relatively more expensive when functional currencies devalue [#A]

Target state: EBITDA margin 49-51% by FY27 on a reported basis; requires both FX stabilisation (§2) AND cost-initiative delivery [#J]

Bull — historical: AAF demonstrated 300-400bps margin improvement in FY20-22 period through tower monetisation and network sharing — operational precedent for sustained margin improvement exists [#A]

Bull — recent: Solar rollout delivered estimated $20-25m annualised energy savings in FY25; management targets additional savings in FY26 as solar coverage expands to remaining off-grid sites [#A]

Bull — mgmt: CFO commentary at FY25 results specifically called out "path to 50%+ EBITDA margin" as a medium-term target; tower lease renegotiations progressing across East Africa portfolio [#A]

Bull — initiatives: (a) Solar-powered base station programme — targeting 70%+ of sites by end-FY26; (b) Passive network sharing with MTN in select markets; (c) G&A headcount rationalisation underway; (d) Spectrum sharing agreements in West Africa reducing capex-equivalent costs [#A]

Bear — energy cost: ❌ Diesel and electricity tariff inflation in sub-Saharan Africa has been 15-25% annually in local currency terms over 2023-25; solar capex deployment must offset ongoing energy inflation — a running battle, not a one-time gain [#A]

Bear — tower leases: Tower lease renegotiation benefits are back-loaded; existing long-term contracts (typically 10-15Y in Africa) constrain the pace of renegotiation; meaningful savings only materialise at contract renewal events [#A]

Bear — FX interaction: ❌ USD-denominated tower leases (most of AAF's leaseback agreements with IHS Towers and ATC) become more expensive in local-currency terms when functional currencies devalue — creating an automatic margin compression mechanism that partially offsets cost initiatives [#A]

Bear — competitive: Competing MNOs (MTN, Safaricom) have comparable or superior network infrastructure — AAF's cost advantage from efficiency gains is not widening a competitive moat, merely maintaining competitive parity [#A]

Bear — structural: Sub-Saharan Africa network operating costs are structurally higher than OECD markets due to power infrastructure gaps; the "structural overhead" makes sub-50% EBITDA margins the likely ceiling for pure MNO operations, not a floor [#A]

Quarterly trajectory: Margin improvement likely 50-80bps p.a. in FY26-27; at the lower end of the 150-300bps required for the target state; energy savings partially offset energy inflation; lease savings back-loaded [#J]

Key dependency: (a) Solar rollout execution against FY26 target; (b) tower-lease contract renewal schedule; (c) FX stabilisation (USD-denominated leases are the hidden FX lever) [#J]

Downside scenario: If energy costs continue to inflate 20% p.a. AND USD-denominated leases create additional drag, EBITDA margin could actually compress by 50-100bps despite efficiency initiatives — net margin benefit could be near-zero [#J]

Evidence gap: USD-vs-local-currency lease cost split is not cleanly disclosed; the FX-interaction with tower-lease costs is an underanalysed risk that APM should quantify specifically [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — efficiency narrative is real but 150-300bps in N24M is optimistic given energy-inflation offset; 50-100bps is more defensible. Fulcrum-level vs APM Pass 1: REVISES — secondary driver, not fulcrum [#J]

Sceptical bullet: What if the solar rollout faces delays (capex rationing, supply-chain delays for panels in Africa) and the FY26 energy-savings target is missed by 40-50%? The margin improvement story collapses to near-zero net benefit while the revenue growth story must carry the full IRR load [#J]

§6 — Key Driver: Capital Allocation / MoMo Minority Monetisation

J-front verdict: MoMo stake monetisation is a genuine optionality item but near-term probability is low; management has signalled intent without concrete timeline; should be priced as deep optionality, not base-case IRR [#J]

Driver definition: Sale of partial minority stake in Airtel Money (MoMo subsidiary) to strategic or financial investor; creates explicit value realisation for an asset management believes is undervalued within the consolidated group [#A]

Magnitude required: A 10-15% stake sale at a valuation implying $2-3bn for 100% of Airtel Money would crystallise ~$200-450m cash proceeds; at current market cap of ~£2.5bn (approximately $3.1bn), a successful deal would represent 7-15% uplift vs NAV [#J]

Baseline: Mastercard invested $100m for a ~2.5% MoMo stake in 2021 (implying ~$4bn valuation for 100% Airtel Money); at current market cap, the entire listed entity trades at ~$3.1bn — creating a theoretical "MoMo for free" narrative if the 2021 valuation holds [#A]

Target state: New minority investor (strategic or financial) at $2.5-3.5bn MoMo valuation would unlock both cash proceeds and an explicit reference price for the embedded asset [#J]

Bull — historical: Mastercard deal in 2021 demonstrates the asset has attracted tier-1 strategic interest; the precedent transaction supports the "MoMo for free" valuation narrative [#A]

Bull — mgmt: Management has repeatedly stated intent to monetise MoMo stake at the right valuation; investor presentations include explicit "embedded value of Airtel Money" slides [#A]

Bull — industry: African fintech M&A activity has been robust; Wave, Moniepoint, and similar African fintech assets have attracted $500m+ financing rounds at $1-2bn valuations for smaller user bases than AAF MoMo [#A]

Bear — execution: ❌ No follow-on deal since Mastercard 2021 despite four years of stated intent; multiple sell-side notes have cited the delay as a concern; the process appears paused [#A]

Bear — market conditions: ❌ Global fintech valuation multiples compressed 40-60% from 2021 peaks; the 2021 Mastercard deal was struck at the peak of fintech euphoria; a 2025-26 deal would likely clear at a lower multiple, reducing proceeds and potentially damaging the "MoMo for free" narrative [#A]

Bear — strategic: New investors would require governance rights, data access, and regulatory approvals across 14 jurisdictions — deal complexity is genuinely high and may deter tier-1 strategics [#A]

Bear — macro: USD scarcity in Nigeria constrains repatriation of proceeds from any Nigeria-domiciled MoMo sub-entity; the practical benefit to UK-listed parent is more complex than the headline suggests [#A]

Bear — structural: ⚡ RARE: The "MoMo for free" narrative requires the Mastercard valuation to be a reliable reference price; however, Mastercard's strategic rationale (M-Pesa interoperability, Mastercard network extension) created a strategic premium above fair value; a pure financial investor would apply a lower multiple. The gap between strategic and financial valuation could be 30-50%, materially reducing the "MoMo for free" arbitrage [Cross-ref: §6]

Forward trajectory: Low probability of a completed MoMo minority deal in N12M; medium probability in N12-24M if fintech valuations recover partially; should be valued as optionality with low base-case weight [#J]

Key dependency: (a) Global fintech sentiment / multiple recovery; (b) Management willingness to accept lower valuation than 2021 precedent; (c) Regulatory approvals across 14 markets [#J]

Evidence gap: MoMo regulatory approval status across key jurisdictions (particularly Nigeria CBN approval for partial foreign ownership of mobile money operator) is not clearly publicly disclosed [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Low plausibility near-term (N12M); medium plausibility medium-term (N12-24M) — the asset is real and the intent is stated, but execution track record is weak and valuation environment has deteriorated. Fulcrum-level vs APM Pass 1: CHALLENGES — should be treated as optionality, not base-case IRR contributor [#J]

Sceptical bullet: What if the MoMo stake monetisation never happens and the market stops pricing in the optionality? The stock re-rates downward on multiple compression as the "MoMo for free" narrative fades; this is not base case but is a material de-rating mechanism that APM should explicitly price [#J]

§7 — Key Driver: Network Investment / 4G Coverage Expansion

J-front verdict: 4G expansion is an enabling driver rather than a direct IRR driver; it is a necessary condition for data ARPU growth and MoMo adoption but does not directly generate returns independent of ARPU and MoMo monetisation [#J]

Driver definition: Capital expenditure on 4G spectrum deployment, site upgrades, and fibre backhaul; creates the network infrastructure prerequisite for data and MoMo revenue growth [#A]

Magnitude required: Capex/revenue ratio needs to remain approximately 18-22% (current level) while delivering incremental 4G coverage; overspend would compress FCF; underspend would limit growth enablement [#J]

Baseline: FY25 capex approximately $540-560m; capex/revenue approximately 27-28% (elevated due to network investment cycle); management targets compression to ~20-22% by FY27 as initial rollout completes [#A]

Target state: Capex/revenue at 20-22% by FY27, freeing FCF for dividends and debt reduction; 4G population coverage reaching 70%+ across the 14-market footprint (from ~62% in FY25) [#J]
