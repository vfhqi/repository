[C] SATS AS — ESA — Value Chain Map @ 02-May-26

Executive Summary

SATS AS operates as a vertically integrated gym operator and group fitness content producer in the Nordic consumer health and fitness value chain. Unlike pure-play gym franchisors or asset-light fitness app businesses, SATS controls the full customer experience from membership acquisition through class delivery, and — critically — develops its proprietary group training content in-house rather than licensing formats from third parties such as Les Mills. This vertical integration in content is structurally significant: SATS's group training members exhibit 2x tenure versus solo members, generate 2x revenue density per square metre of floor space, and produce -2.2 percentage points lower monthly churn, making the group training programme the single most important retention mechanism in the business. SATS operates across Norway (its dominant home market), Sweden, Denmark, and Finland, with approximately 300+ clubs and a dual-brand strategy: the flagship SATS premium brand targets urban professionals willing to pay for high-specification facilities and premium group content; Fresh Fitness (40 clubs in Norway) targets budget-conscious members in a low-cost format designed to foreclose the low end of the market against Basic-Fit, McFit, and local budget entrants. The competitive moat rests on three pillars: proprietary content IP, network density in premium urban locations in capital cities and major Nordic cities, and a technology stack with 99.9% sensor and system uptime that supports digital access, capacity management, and member engagement.

The margin structure reflects the asset-intensive gym model: high fixed costs (predominantly CPI-linked leases totalling NOK 5,177m, approximately 90% CPI-adjusted), meaningful depreciation on fit-out, and a largely fixed labour base of instructors and front-of-house staff. Gross margins are serviceable (gym sector typically 40-55% at contribution level), but EBIT margins are modest and highly operationally leveraged — incremental members at high utilisation rates flow through powerfully to earnings. The key margin risk is the CPI-linkage of lease liabilities: in an elevated inflation environment, rent escalators compress margins unless membership pricing is raised commensurately. SATS has historically demonstrated ability to pass through price increases given premium positioning, but sustained real-term rent increases represent the most structural cost headwind in the model. Equipment sourcing from Chinese manufacturers introduces supply chain optionality risk (OQ — no confirmed COGS %) in a tariff-volatile environment, though this is a secondary concern versus leases. The competitive landscape features no truly pan-Nordic premium rival at SATS's scale: Actic, Nordic Wellness (Sweden-heavy), and a fragmented set of local operators are the main mid-market competitors, while Fresh Fitness faces Basic-Fit and McFit on the budget tier. SATS's scale, premium content, and brand in Norway and Sweden are defensible; the position in Denmark and Finland is less entrenched.

SATS's value chain position is reinforced by proprietary digital infrastructure and the proprietary class format library, both of which competitors cannot easily replicate on a short timeline. The group training moat is self-reinforcing: higher tenure members generate word-of-mouth, create full class rosters (a social proof mechanism for new members), and justify the capital investment in larger studio footprints. However, three structural vulnerabilities temper conviction. First, the high proportion of CPI-linked leases means SATS is partially a bet on real estate cost dynamics, not purely on gym operations. Second, the Fresh Fitness segment, while strategically rational, operates in a lower-margin format that competes on price with well-capitalised European budget chains which have far greater scale. Third, membership is a discretionary recurring spend — SATS's churn dynamics in a Nordic consumer downturn have not been stress-tested at scale. The GREEN-YELLOW rating reflects a well-run, competitively differentiated operator with a genuine content moat but meaningful lease cost risk and limited evidence of the model's resilience through a full demand cycle.

1. End-to-End Value Chain (12 stages)

The Nordic gym and fitness value chain runs from upstream equipment and content manufacturers through facility operators to the end member paying a monthly subscription. SATS participates at multiple stages and has selectively vertically integrated into content production — a structurally important decision that distinguishes it from operators that merely rent floor space and licence external class formats.

Stage 1: Fitness Equipment Manufacturing. Cardio machines (treadmills, bikes, rowers), free weights, resistance machines, and functional training rigs are manufactured predominantly in China and Taiwan, with branded suppliers including Technogym, Life Fitness, Precor (Amer Sports, now Chinese-owned), and others. Private label equipment for budget segments is sourced directly from Chinese OEMs. SATS sources equipment for both the premium SATS brand (likely from premium branded suppliers) and Fresh Fitness (likely with greater weight on cost-optimised Chinese OEM supply). #I OQ — precise supplier mix and Chinese OEM share of SATS's equipment COGS not publicly disclosed; tariff risk on Chinese-sourced equipment is live given 2025-26 US trade policy spillovers into European sourcing decisions. Equipment life cycles run 5-10 years; capex is ongoing. Margins at equipment manufacturer level are 30-50%, squeezed by volume purchasing from large gym chains. #A

Stage 2: Fitness Wearables and Member Tech. Apple Watch, Garmin, Polar, and Whoop device ecosystems generate fitness data that members increasingly expect to integrate with gym bookings and class tracking. This stage is upstream and not controlled by SATS, but it is relevant because SATS's proprietary tech stack (99.9% uptime) must interoperate with member devices to deliver a seamless digital experience. The wearables market is dominated by Apple and Garmin; SATS is a downstream integrator of data, not a hardware participant. #I

Stage 3: Group Fitness Content Creation. This is a critical and unusual stage — most gym operators licence content from third parties (notably Les Mills International, which franchises BodyPump, BodyCombat, RPM, and 20+ other formats globally to approximately 21,000 gym partners). SATS has made the strategic decision to develop its own proprietary group training formats in-house, functioning as a content studio as well as an operator. This means SATS bears the full cost of format development, choreography, instructor training, and content refresh (typically quarterly in Les Mills's model) but retains 100% of the IP and pays no royalty stream to a third party. The economics of in-house content are better at scale (no per-member licensing fee) and create switching costs: SATS instructors are trained specifically on SATS formats, and member attachment is to the SATS class experience, not to a portable Les Mills format that any competitor can also licence. #J This is the most structurally important step in the value chain for SATS's competitive positioning.

Stage 4: Gym Software and Management Platforms. Booking systems, capacity management tools, access control (sensor networks), CRM, and member engagement apps are either developed in-house or sourced from specialist vendors. SATS has invested in a proprietary technology stack with 99.9% sensor/system uptime — a notably high availability target that implies meaningful internal infrastructure investment and system reliability. #I The tech stack enables: real-time capacity management (members can see class availability and book from the app), digital access (no card friction), automated member communication, and data capture for retention analytics. Proprietary tech is a switching cost for members (muscle memory on the app) and for SATS (not dependent on third-party software vendors for core operations). #A OQ — degree to which SATS has operationalised this data asset for proactive churn management not confirmed at ESA stage.

Stage 5: Real Estate and Gym Leasing. Gym operators do not typically own the physical premises; they lease commercial space on long-term agreements. This is the largest fixed cost line in the gym value chain. SATS's total lease liabilities are NOK 5,177m, with approximately 90% CPI-adjusted. #I This means SATS's single largest cost base automatically escalates with Norwegian and Nordic inflation indices — a structural margin headwind in elevated-inflation environments that cannot be easily offset short-term. Landlords are typically institutional property owners (commercial REITs, pension funds, municipal property companies). Lease terms are commonly 10-15 years in the gym sector, creating long-duration fixed commitments. The CPI-link creates a direct transmission mechanism from macroeconomic inflation into SATS's cost base — this is the dominant structural risk in the model. #J

Stage 6: Gym Fit-Out and Construction. The physical transformation of commercial space into a gym (cardio zones, weights areas, group fitness studios, changing rooms, reception) involves significant capex per club. Premium gym fit-outs for a SATS-format club (with multiple studio spaces for group training, premium equipment, and high-specification changing facilities) typically cost NOK 15-30m per site depending on size. Contractors and fit-out specialists are the key counterparties. Fresh Fitness fit-out is lower-cost (budget format, fewer studios, simpler spec). This stage is a one-off capex event per club with ongoing refresh spend; premium fit-out quality supports the brand proposition and justifies premium pricing. #A

Stage 7: Instructor Training and Certification. Group fitness instructors must be trained in specific class formats. Because SATS uses proprietary formats, it runs its own instructor training academy — instructors learn SATS's internal choreography and delivery standards rather than attending Les Mills or other third-party certification programmes. This is a meaningful operational investment (training costs, instructor time, ongoing quarterly format refreshes), but it creates a talent moat: SATS-certified instructors are embedded in the SATS content ecosystem and are not directly portable to competitors using different formats. #A Instructor quality and consistency is the primary driver of class experience, which drives group training member retention. This is where the -2.2pp churn advantage is actually produced — in the room, by the instructor. #J

Stage 8: Marketing and Member Acquisition. Gym operators invest in performance marketing (social media, paid search), physical marketing (high-street presence, corporate partnerships), referral schemes, and trial membership offers to acquire new members. SATS's dual-brand strategy means it operates two distinct acquisition funnels: premium SATS (targeting urban professionals, higher CAC but also higher LTV given lower churn and higher ARPM) and Fresh Fitness (broader budget appeal, higher volume acquisition at lower ARPM). #A Corporate B2B penetration — where SATS targets employer wellness programmes and negotiates bulk membership deals with corporate HR departments — is a meaningful segment in Norway (OQ — exact % of membership base not confirmed), providing more stable, less churn-prone membership cohorts tied to employer relationships.

Stage 9: Club Operations. Day-to-day management of approximately 300+ clubs across five countries — staffing (front-of-house, cleaning, maintenance, instructors), energy (a growing cost given European energy price volatility post-2022), insurance, and local marketing. SATS operates clubs directly (not a franchise model at the premium brand level); Fresh Fitness operates similarly. Direct operation means SATS captures the full operating margin of each club but also bears all operational risk. High-utilisation mature clubs deliver strong contribution (revenue per sqm is maximised by the 2x density effect of group training rooms over cardio floors), while new clubs in ramp-up are dilutive until membership builds. #A

Stage 10: Group Training Class Delivery. The live delivery of group fitness classes — HIIT, cycle, yoga, strength, dance, and other format types — by SATS-certified instructors in purpose-designed studios. This is where the group training competitive moat is actually realised. The 2x revenue density per sqm versus solo cardio floor reflects that a 40-person group fitness studio generates 40 simultaneous member sessions per hour-long class versus a cardio floor where average utilisation per machine is much lower. The 2x tenure advantage reflects that members with social commitment to class schedules and instructor relationships are substantially harder to cancel (friction and social cost of leaving is higher). #J This stage is operationally intensive but is the highest-value stage in SATS's internal value chain.

Stage 11: Digital and On-Demand Fitness. A portion of SATS's content is deliverable digitally (live-streamed or on-demand class recordings), addressing the post-COVID member segment that wants hybrid gym/home access. SATS competes here with digital-only fitness platforms (Peloton, Apple Fitness+, Alo Moves) that have large content libraries. SATS's physical presence and social group class experience are the primary differentiation from digital substitutes — the community and in-person experience are not replicable by Peloton. #J

Stage 12: Member Retention and Upsell. The final and most economically important stage — retaining existing members month after month. SATS's churn economics are driven heavily by the group training cohort (-2.2pp lower monthly churn), which means the composition of the membership base towards group training participants is a key lever in LTV maximisation. Upsell mechanisms include: personal training sessions (high-margin add-on), premium locker rental, nutrition services, and tier upgrades. Retention analytics (using the proprietary tech stack data) allow SATS to identify at-risk members and intervene proactively. #A

2. SATS AS's Position in the Value Chain (12 dimensions)

SATS occupies a vertically integrated operator position spanning content creation, technology infrastructure, direct club operations, and member retention. It does not participate in equipment manufacturing or real estate ownership. Its competitive distinctiveness resides in the conjunction of proprietary content IP and premium brand positioning in a geographically concentrated market.

Dimension 1: Proprietary Content IP — the Core Moat. SATS's decision to develop group training formats in-house rather than licence from Les Mills is the single most consequential strategic choice in its value chain position. #J Competitors using Les Mills formats pay per-member royalties and provide a portable, non-exclusive experience — any other gym can offer identical content. SATS's formats are proprietary and non-replicable without significant time investment (developing new formats, training instructors, building member familiarity takes 2-4 years minimum). #A The financial evidence — 2x tenure, 2x revenue density per sqm, -2.2pp churn — is the quantified output of this moat. #I At ESA stage these metrics are compelling prima facie; KQ is whether these metrics are stable across markets (Norway vs Sweden vs Denmark vs Finland) and hold through economic cycles. With Fresh Fitness, SATS can compete on price in the budget segment without cannibalising SATS premium brand economics. 3. Key Risks & Opportunities (10 bullets)

SATS's value chain position is well-constructed but faces a set of identifiable structural risks alongside genuine optionality from underappreciated aspects of the group training moat and corporate segment.

Risk 1: CPI-Linked Lease Escalation Outpacing Pricing Power. #J With NOK 5,177m in lease liabilities and ~90% CPI-adjusted, SATS operates one of the more inflation-exposed cost structures in the Nordic consumer space. In a scenario where Norwegian CPI runs persistently above 3% (as it did in 2022-24), annual rent escalation compounds rapidly against a revenue base that can only be raised by membership price increases or net member adds. Premium positioning gives SATS some pricing power headroom, but there is a consumer sensitivity ceiling — at some membership price point, premium gym value proposition erodes vs. digital fitness substitutes or budget gyms. This risk is structural and permanent, not cyclical; it is baked into the lease contract terms. #A

Risk 2: Fresh Fitness Unable to Defend Against European Budget Chain Scale. #J Basic-Fit has 1,300+ clubs and deep experience in ultra-low-cost gym operations; SATS's Fresh Fitness has 40 clubs confined to Norway. If Basic-Fit accelerates Nordic expansion (or McFit follows), SATS faces a budget competitor with structural cost advantages (procurement scale, operational experience, lower debt cost of capital from public listing). Fresh Fitness's defensive value is contingent on Basic-Fit not committing serious capital to Norway — a strategic assumption, not a guarantee. #A OQ — Basic-Fit's stated Nordic strategy and any evidence of site acquisition in Norway/Sweden is a live intelligence gap.

Risk 3: Consumer Discretionary Downturn Driving Membership Churn. #J Gym memberships are contractually monthly but behaviourally discretionary — in a sharp consumer downturn, gym cancellations typically spike. SATS's group training moat reduces but does not eliminate this risk: even committed group fitness members cancel when financial pressure is acute. The relevant stress test is 2022-23 when Nordic real incomes fell sharply due to energy prices and inflation. OQ — SATS's net membership growth and churn trends during this period are a key empirical test that DD P1 should interrogate. The corporate segment provides a partial buffer (corporate contracts are institutional, not individually cancellable).

Risk 4: Digital Fitness Substitution at the Margin. #A Apple Fitness+, YouTube fitness content, and specialised digital platforms (Alo Moves, Centr) continue to improve quality and breadth. For solo cardio members, the value proposition of a gym membership vs. a £10/month digital subscription is increasingly challenged. SATS's group training moat is robust against digital substitution (live social classes are not replicable by a screen); the solo cardio segment is more exposed. This risk is real but manageable given SATS's active push toward group training as the primary membership value driver. #J

Risk 5: Chinese Equipment Supply Chain Disruption. #A If tariff escalation or logistics disruption materially increases the cost of Chinese-sourced fitness equipment (particularly for Fresh Fitness refurbishment cycles), SATS faces a capex cost headwind. The magnitude is uncertain (OQ — Chinese OEM share of equipment COGS not disclosed) but the risk is non-zero and live in the current trade environment. Mitigation: premium SATS clubs likely use more established branded suppliers (Technogym, Life Fitness) that have European manufacturing or inventory; Fresh Fitness is the higher-exposure segment.

Risk 6: Instructor Recruitment and Retention Quality Risk. #A SATS's group training moat depends on instructors delivering consistently excellent classes across 300+ clubs. In a tight Nordic labour market, high-quality fitness instructors are in demand (boutique studios, competitor gyms, independent PT). If SATS instructor quality or consistency deteriorates — whether through wage pressure, high turnover, or insufficient training investment — the group training churn advantage will erode. This is an operational execution risk that is not visible from outside the business and requires management conversation to assess.

Opportunity 1: Group Training Cohort Expansion — Increasing Mix Improves LTV. #J SATS's most value-creating lever is increasing the proportion of its membership base that participates in group training classes. Every percentage point of mix shift from solo to group training improves blended LTV (via -2.2pp churn, 2x tenure). This is a compounding revenue quality improvement that does not require new clubs — it is an operational and programming lever within existing infrastructure. Management's ability and willingness to actively drive this mix shift is a key APM judgement question. #A

Opportunity 2: Corporate B2B Deepening in High-Penetration Norway. #J Norway's corporate wellness culture creates an unusual demand pool for employer-subsidised gym access. SATS's existing corporate penetration (OQ — % unconfirmed) positions it to expand B2B relationships — multi-year wellness contracts with large Norwegian employers (oil & gas, finance, public sector, technology) provide structurally stable recurring revenue that is less sensitive to consumer sentiment. Opportunity scale is meaningful if corporate penetration is currently 20-30% and can grow to 35-45% — these cohorts would anchor the revenue base against consumer downturns. #A

Opportunity 3: Proprietary Tech Stack as Retention and Data Asset. #J SATS's 99.9% uptime technology infrastructure captures granular member behaviour data — class attendance frequency, booking patterns, cancellation-risk signals. If SATS has operationalised this data asset into a proactive retention management system (identifying at-risk members before they cancel and intervening with targeted offers), this is a structural churn reduction tool that competitors without proprietary tech cannot replicate. #A The value of this capability is highest for the group training cohort (where behaviour patterns are regular and predictable, making early warning signals more actionable). If SATS's format and content translates successfully into these markets (KQ — uncertain given different competitive dynamics vs Norway), incremental clubs in Copenhagen, Helsinki, and secondary cities could be opened against a largely fixed content and technology infrastructure cost base, improving group-level unit economics. #J OQ — current club-level EBITDA margins in Denmark and Finland vs Norway/Sweden cohorts are a key comparator.
