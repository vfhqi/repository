KEY FINDINGS - Structural verdict: Solaria occupies a structurally favoured position — the integrated IPP/generation stage in solar PV — which retains the highest EBIT margin in the renewable energy value chain, ahead of both manufacturing and grid services [#J]

  • Position concentration: Solaria is a pure-play single-stage specialist in utility-scale solar generation + long-term PPA contracting, with no meaningful upstream (manufacturing) or downstream (retail supply) presence — positional clarity is a feature, not a bug, at current scale [#J]

  • Margin pool: IPP/generation stage captures 30–55% EBIT margins where asset economics are locked via long-tenor PPAs or regulated tariffs; manufacturing and EPC stages operate at 5–15% EBIT — Solaria's stage is the value-chain's profit apex [#A]

  • Pricing power: PPA structuring gives Solaria substantial pricing-power capture at contract signature; once signed, revenues are fixed for 10–15 years with limited customer renegotiation leverage — but merchant exposure on uncontracted volumes creates asymmetric downside when wholesale prices fall [#J]

  • Supplier leverage: Solar module procurement is the single largest cost lever — Chinese manufacturers (LONGi, Jinko, Trina) dominate global module supply with ~80% market share, creating structurally low module prices but concentrated supplier dependency; BOS (balance-of-system) inputs remain fragmented [#A]

  • Competitive intensity: Solaria competes against well-capitalised European peers (Iberdrola, Enel Green Power, RWE Renewables, Ørsted) with materially larger balance sheets and greater geographic diversification; within the Iberian solar specialist sub-tier Solaria holds scale advantage, but the broader tier-one cohort outscales it significantly [#J]

  • Vertical integration threat: Downstream customer integration risk is real but slow-moving — large Spanish industrial offtakers are building direct PPA procurement capabilities, but Solaria's project development pipeline and land bank constitute a genuine barrier that slows this threat [#I]

  • Structural evolution: Consolidation at the IPP/generation stage is accelerating — M&A among pure-play solar IPPs is compressing the fragmented mid-tier; Solaria's Spanish land bank and grid connection queue positions are a scarce resource that larger acquirors value [#J]

  • Single largest positional risk: Electricity price trajectory in Iberia is the central variable — if Spanish wholesale power prices revert to 2019–20 lows (~€40–50/MWh) from recent elevated levels, merchant/uncontracted revenue collapses and PPA re-pricing at renewal destroys the margin stack [#J]

  • ⚡ RARE signal: Solaria's land-bank-and-grid-connection-queue asset constitutes a structural bottleneck asset with very low substitutability — grid connection rights in Spain are administratively rationed, making Solaria's queued capacity a non-replicable positional asset at the project-development stage [#J]

§1 — Value Chain Map + Stage Table

Solaria operates within the solar photovoltaic power generation value chain, which is broadly linear — from upstream silicon/wafer production through module manufacturing, system integration, generation/operation, and ultimately power delivery to end consumers. The chain has hub-like features at the project-development and grid-services nodes, but the core flow is directional.

  • Structural shape: The utility-scale solar value chain is predominantly linear from raw material to electrons, with a hub node at the IPP/developer stage where capital, land, permits, and offtake contracts converge [#J]

  • Linear chain: Raw polysilicon → wafers/cells → modules → BOS components → EPC construction → generation asset → grid → offtaker/retailer → end consumer; each stage is structurally separable [#A]

  • Hub node: The IPP/developer stage acts as a de facto hub — aggregating upstream equipment supply, land/permit assets, financing, and downstream offtake contracts; this aggregating function is where structural pricing power concentrates [#J]

  • Stage separation: Vertical integration across the full chain is rare for European players — most established IPPs source modules externally and sell power via PPAs or spot markets; full-chain integration (SunPower/First Solar model) is a US-centric pattern [#A]

  • Geographic layering: Upstream manufacturing (polysilicon, wafers, cells, modules) is overwhelmingly China-concentrated (~80% module production), while downstream generation and retail are geographically dispersed and nationally regulated [#A]

  • Regulatory inflection: The generation stage is uniquely regulated — grid connection rights, subsidy regimes (Spain's RECORE/RD900 framework historical; current merchant + PPA model), and capacity market rules create a state-enforced barrier to entry at the generation/IPP stage not present in manufacturing [#J]

  • Platform risk note: The chain does not exhibit platform economics (no network effects, no data flywheel) — it is a physical capital chain; attempts to re-label solar IPPs as "energy platforms" are structurally ungrounded [#J]

§2 — Solaria Energia y Medio Ambiente's Position Within the Chain

  • Positional verdict: Solaria is a two-stage specialist — integrated across project development (Stage 6) and IPP/generation (Stage 7) only; it is not a manufacturing company, not a grid operator, and not a retailer [#J]

  • Stage occupied: Solaria's revenue is generated almost entirely at Stage 7 (electricity generation + PPA delivery); Stage 6 value is partially monetised via asset-rotation (sell-down of development-stage assets to infrastructure funds) and partially retained as the seeding activity for Stage 7 growth [#A]

  • Single-stage: With respect to revenue and capital deployment, Solaria is a single-stage operator — its GW of installed capacity generates contracted and merchant power sales; the development pipeline is the asset-creation funnel feeding Stage 7 [#J]

  • Upstream integration: Solaria has zero upstream integration into module manufacturing or BOS components — it is entirely a buyer of commoditised equipment from third-party suppliers; this is consistent with European IPP peers (Grenergy, Opdenergy, ERG) [#A]

  • Downstream integration: Solaria has no retail supply arm — it does not sell power directly to residential or small commercial customers; its downstream reach terminates at the PPA counterparty (large industrial/commercial offtaker or utility) or at the spot market [#A]

  • Distribution ownership: Solaria does not own grid infrastructure — connection to the Red Eléctrica transmission network is a regulatory entitlement, not a proprietary asset; grid connection rights are however a scarce administrative asset that constitutes a form of quasi-vertical control over the interconnection stage [#J]

  • Peer integration vs: Iberdrola operates across Stages 6–9 (development, generation, grid, retail); Enel Green Power similarly; Solaria's pure-play Stage 6–7 focus makes it comparatively narrow but higher-margin per asset than the integrated utility model [#J]

  • Land bank as structural asset: Solaria's land acquisition and permitting pipeline constitutes a proprietary Stage 6 asset — its Spanish land bank underpins a GW-scale development queue that newer entrants cannot replicate quickly given Spain's administrative permitting timelines (3–6 years per project typically) [#J]

  • Asset-rotation optionality: Solaria has partial monetisation optionality at Stage 6 — selling development-stage assets (ready-to-build or construction-ready projects) to infrastructure capital at a development margin; this is a real strategic lever even if not currently the primary revenue model [#A]

  • Multi-stage: The Stage 6 → Stage 7 integration is genuinely value-additive — Solaria captures the development margin (land + permit option value) AND the long-run generation margin, rather than selling development upside to an acquiror [#J]

  • Peer integration vs (Grenergy): Grenergy (smaller Spanish peer) follows a similar Stage 6–7 integrated model with an explicit asset-rotation strategy; the key differentiator is Solaria's larger installed base and longer Spain-specific land bank [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on Solaria's positional choice — single-stage focus on generation leaves Solaria fully exposed to wholesale power price risk on uncontracted volumes; more-integrated peers (Iberdrola) can cross-subsidise via retail margin during wholesale price downturns; Solaria cannot; conversely, integration into retail would compress Solaria's EBIT margin profile toward the blended utility average of 15–25%, destroying the pure-play premium [#J]

§4 — Margin Distribution Across the Chain

  • Margin pool verdict: The margin pool in the solar PV value chain is heavily concentrated at Stages 6–7 (development and generation); manufacturing stages operate at cyclical, thin margins; grid/retail stages are regulated-return or structurally low [#J]

  • Most-profitable stage: Stage 7 (IPP/Generation) is the structural margin apex — zero fuel cost, high operating leverage, long-tenor contracted revenues create 30–55% EBIT margins; Solaria's 2022–23 EBIT margins (consensus ~50–55%) confirm placement at the high end [#A]

  • Margin driver — regulatory: Spain's historical regulated tariff regime (pre-2013 RDL 9/2013) and subsequent PPA market created a contractually locked revenue floor; even at merchant prices, Solaria's cost structure (OpEx ~€15–20/MWh estimated) leaves wide margin at €50–80/MWh wholesale [#A]

  • Margin driver — scale: Fixed-cost leverage is extreme at Stage 7 — once a solar farm is built, incremental revenue drops almost entirely to EBITDA; Solaria's EBITDA margins run 75–85% (consensus estimate range), consistent with sector structure [#A]

  • Margin-thin stage: Stage 3 (module assembly) is the most structurally thin stage — Chinese manufacturing overcapacity has compressed module gross margins to single digits in 2023–24; First Solar (US thin-film, non-China) is a partial exception at ~40% gross margin but sells at premium pricing [#A]

  • Margin driver — differentiation: Stage 4 (BOS — inverters, trackers) shows bifurcation — commodity tracker/inverter suppliers at 8–12% EBIT vs Nextracker (~20% EBIT, FY24) which commands premium via software-differentiated tracking intelligence; differentiation at this stage is real but narrow [#A]

  • Margin trend: Module manufacturing margins are contractually deteriorating — Chinese overcapacity drove module prices from ~$0.30/W in 2022 to ~$0.09–0.12/W in 2024 (BloombergNEF/Wood Mackenzie); this is structurally positive for Solaria as a module buyer [#A]

  • Share gain: Falling module costs expand Solaria's project IRRs — each $0.01/W fall in module price reduces total project CapEx by ~1–2% at utility scale; Solaria benefits from this secular trend without bearing the manufacturing-side margin destruction [#J]

  • Margin trend — generation: Spanish merchant power prices are volatile — 2022 average OMIE price ~€167/MWh; 2023 ~€87/MWh; H1 2024 ~€50–60/MWh — the cycle creates margin volatility at Stage 7 that is not structural but cyclical [#A]

§5 — Power Dynamics + Pricing Leverage

  • Power dynamics verdict: Solaria holds moderate-to-strong pricing power at PPA origination but faces structurally weak positions on both the merchant side (price-taker) and vs dominant module suppliers [#J]

  • Pricing power: At the point of PPA negotiation, Solaria benefits from scarcity of firm, long-term renewable supply — large Spanish industrial offtakers (IBEX energy-intensive corporates, data centre developers) face growing demand for clean power certificates and baseload supply, which gives Solaria negotiating leverage [#J]

  • Customer concentration: Solaria's PPA counterparties are typically large investment-grade industrial and commercial offtakers; while customer names are not fully public, the PPA market in Spain is concentrated in a handful of large corporates (Endesa, Iberdrola Supply, Amazon, Google, large industrials) — moderate concentration risk [#A]

  • Switching cost: PPA contracts with 10–15 year terms and penalty clauses for early termination create high switching costs for the offtaker once signed; Solaria effectively locks in pricing power for the contract duration [#J]

  • Differentiation: Solar power is partially commoditised at the energy level — a MWh from Solaria's plants is functionally identical to that from Grenergy or Iberdrola; differentiation occurs on credit quality, project certainty of delivery, PPA structuring flexibility, and GO (Guarantee of Origin) attributes [#A]

  • Regulatory barrier: Spain's grid connection queue is administratively controlled and non-purchasable — a new entrant cannot replicate Solaria's grid connection rights by simply writing a cheque; this creates a durable supply-side constraint that underpins Solaria's pricing in bilateral PPA negotiations [#J]

  • Scale economy: Solaria's multi-GW installed base creates moderate procurement scale economies in module purchasing and O&M contracting — sufficient to negotiate framework agreements — but not dominant at the level of Iberdrola (50+ GW renewable portfolio) [#A]

  • Supplier leverage: Module suppliers (LONGi, Jinko, Trina) hold concentrated market power in aggregate (top 5 Chinese manufacturers = ~70% of global module supply), but paradoxically this concentration has driven price competition among themselves — Solaria as buyer benefits from intra-supplier rivalry pushing module prices down [#J]

  • Supplier leverage — inverters/trackers: BOS supplier market is more fragmented — Solaria has adequate switching options across SMA, Sungrow, Huawei (inverters) and Nextracker, Array Technologies, PV Hardware (trackers); no single BOS supplier has oligopoly power over Solaria [#A]

  • Supplier leverage — EPC: Spanish EPC contractors (Acciona, Elecnor, Cobra/ACS, Solarpack) compete actively for large-scale solar contracts; Solaria has leverage as a large, repeat buyer in a competitive contractor market [#A]

  • Customer leverage — spot market: For uncontracted output (~20–30% of generation), Solaria is a pure price-taker on OMIE (Spanish day-ahead market) — zero pricing power; this is the primary pricing-power vulnerability [#J]

  • Segment-specific power: In the corporate PPA segment (where demand from tech hyperscalers and energy-intensive industry is growing), Solaria has meaningfully above-average leverage — additionality requirements and GO certificate demands give IPPs with new-build pipeline a structural bargaining advantage over retail utilities [#J]

  • Dictated terms — network: REE as the Spanish TSO dictates grid connection terms, timing, and curtailment decisions — Solaria has no pricing power at the transmission interface [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on power dynamics — corporate PPA pricing has been falling alongside wholesale power prices (Spanish PPA benchmark indices declined ~30% in 2023); as more renewable capacity enters the Spanish market, the supply scarcity argument that underpins Solaria's PPA leverage weakens; by 2027–28, when Solaria's development pipeline is substantially built out, the market may be structurally long solar generation in Spain, reversing the current power dynamic [#J]

§6 — Competitive Map at Solaria's Stage

  • Competitive intensity verdict: The IPP/generation stage in European solar is consolidating but remains multi-tiered — Solaria is a credible mid-tier operator, dominant in the Spanish solar specialist sub-segment, but outscaled by integrated European utilities by an order of magnitude [#J]

  • Competitor — leader: Iberdrola (Spain) and Enel Green Power (Italy/global) are the undisputed scale leaders at Stage 7 in Europe — Iberdrola has ~45 GW renewables globally (c.20 GW solar), Enel Green Power ~60 GW; Solaria's ~1.5–2 GW installed is in a different weight class [#A]

  • Competitor — leader: RWE Renewables (Germany) and Ørsted (Denmark) represent the northern European scale tier — primarily wind-heavy but with growing solar portfolios; their balance sheet depth (~€40–60bn assets) dwarfs Solaria's (~€2–3bn) [#A]

  • Competitor — challenger (Spain-specific): Grenergy Renovables is the closest Spanish pure-play solar+storage comparator — similar Stage 6–7 model, smaller installed base (~0.5–1 GW operational), explicit asset-rotation strategy; Grenergy is the most direct competitive analogue at Solaria's strategic sub-segment [#A]

  • Competitor — challenger: Opdenergy (Spanish, now partially acquired by Galp) and Solarpack (acquired by Infrastructure Capital Partners) represent the consolidation-target tier — Spanish solar pure-plays at 200–500 MW installed that have been acquired by larger capital pools; this validates Solaria's strategic asset value to acquirors [#A]

  • Competitor — specialist: Neoen (France, acquired by AGL/BlackRock 2024) and BayWa r.e. represent the mid-scale European solar specialists — comparable positioned players in other European markets; Neoen's acquisition confirms infrastructure capital's appetite for pure-play IPP assets [#A]

  • Scale-cost dimension: Solaria sits in the €500m–€2bn market cap / 1–5 GW installed tier — above Spanish micro-players but well below European majors; this position is structurally vulnerable to either being acquired or being competed out of the best PPA opportunities by larger counterparties [#J]

  • Differentiation dimension: Solaria's Spain-specific land bank, permitting queue, and grid connection rights are genuine differentiation within the Iberian geography — these are not easily replicable by Iberdrola (who has its own queue) or by foreign entrants (who face Spanish administrative complexity) [#J]

  • Geographic dimension: Solaria is Spain-concentrated — this is both a competitive moat (deep local expertise, relationships, land positions) and a concentration risk (all material assets in one regulatory jurisdiction) [#A]

  • Stage concentration: The IPP/generation stage in European solar is moderately concentrated at the top (3–5 majors control majority of GW) but fragmented in the mid-tier (dozens of IPPs in the 0.5–5 GW range); the mid-tier is actively consolidating via M&A [#J]

  • Customer-segment dimension: Solaria's focus on utility-scale assets (>50 MW per project) is a competitive qualifier — it excludes Solaria from distributed/rooftop competition and focuses it on the industrial/corporate PPA market where margins are highest [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on the competitive map at Solaria's stage — Iberdrola and Enel Green Power can and do directly approach the same large Spanish corporate offtakers that Solaria targets; their superior credit ratings, brand recognition, and ability to bundle renewable + baseload + balancing services give them a structural PPA bidding advantage that Solaria cannot match; the scale gap is not a temporary condition — it widens as majors continue their multi-GW buildout programs [#J]

§7 — Competitive Maps at Adjacent Stages + Vertical Integration Threats

  • Adjacent-stage verdict: Adjacent stages are structurally hostile to vertical integration — the upstream module manufacturing stage is China-dominated and capital-destructive for Western entrants; the downstream retail stage requires a customer franchise Solaria does not possess; integration threats to Solaria's stage come most credibly from large utilities integrating development capabilities and from infrastructure funds internalising IPP functions [#J]

  • Supplier — leader (modules): LONGi Green Energy and Jinko Solar are the global module supply duopoly at scale — each producing 80–100 GW/yr equivalent; their cost positions (integrated wafer-to-module, Chinese manufacturing cost base) are structurally unreplicable by Western entrants [#A]

  • Supplier consolidation: The module manufacturing stage is paradoxically consolidating at the top while fragmenting in the mid-tier — Chinese tier-1 manufacturers are gaining share vs tier-2/3; this benefits Solaria via lower prices but increases dependence on a small number of Chinese suppliers [#A]

  • Supplier vertical integration: Chinese module manufacturers (LONGi, Jinko) have explicitly targeted downstream IPP activities in EMEA — LONGi has announced project development ambitions in Europe; however, regulatory and administrative barriers (land, permits, grid connections) have substantially impeded progress; this threat is real but currently constrained by Spain's permitting complexity [#J]

  • Supplier — inverters: Huawei and Sungrow (both Chinese) together hold ~55–60% of the global solar inverter market; Huawei's presence in Spanish grid infrastructure has faced regulatory scrutiny — this creates a partial procurement risk for Solaria relying on Chinese inverter technology in a security-sensitive grid context [#A]

  • Customer — leader: Endesa (Enel subsidiary) and Naturgy are the largest Spanish electricity retailers and also significant wholesale power buyers; as PPA counterparties they have considerable contract negotiation leverage given their balance sheet scale vs Solaria's [#A]

  • Customer consolidation: The large-industrial and hyperscaler PPA buyer segment is itself consolidating its procurement — Amazon, Google, Microsoft have dedicated energy procurement teams that negotiate directly and have multi-GW renewable procurement ambitions across Europe; this consolidation increases buyer leverage over time [#A]

  • Customer vertical integration: Major Spanish industrial groups (ArcelorMittal Iberia, Acerinox, cement producers) have initiated self-generation / direct PV development activities — bypassing IPPs via direct ownership of generation assets; this is a slow-moving but structurally credible disintermediation threat [#J]

  • Adjacent threat — infrastructure funds: Brookfield Renewable, Copenhagen Infrastructure Partners, Macquarie Green Investment Group, and BlackRock (via acquisitions like Neoen) are acquiring Stage 6–7 assets rather than building organic development pipelines — this represents validation of Solaria's asset value rather than pure competitive threat, but infrastructure funds with development capabilities (e.g., Brookfield) do compete for the same project pipeline [#J]

  • Customer vertical integration — hyperscalers: Google, Amazon and Microsoft have explored direct solar farm development in Iberia via PPAs + equity co-investment structures; if they move to self-develop, they become competitors at Stage 6; currently the administrative complexity deters this, but long-term the risk is non-trivial [#J]

  • Supplier — EPC: Spanish EPC contractors (Acciona, ACS/Cobra, Elecnor) are themselves Stage 6 participants — they develop, build, and sometimes retain solar assets; Acciona Energía in particular is a direct competitor at Stage 7 as well as an EPC supplier; this blurring of supplier/competitor roles is structurally uncomfortable for Solaria [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The most credible vertical integration vector against Solaria is upstream: a Chinese module manufacturer (LONGi most likely) or a Middle Eastern sovereign fund (ACWA Power, Masdar) acquiring a Spanish project development platform and using it to self-develop; ACWA Power's €10bn+ Spanish renewable pipeline ambitions (reported 2023) represent precisely this threat; Solaria's project development expertise would be replicated rather than need Solaria's specific assets [#J]

§8 — Structural Changes + Evolution

  • Structural evolution verdict: The solar PV value chain is undergoing mid-tier consolidation at Stage 7, accelerating Chinese dominance at Stages 1–4, and early-stage disintermediation pressures at Stage 6 from infrastructure capital and hyperscalers — net effect is a more polarised chain, advantaging well-capitalised IPPs at the expense of undercapitalised specialists [#J]

  • Stage consolidation: Mid-tier European solar IPPs (0.5–5 GW operational) are being rapidly acquired — Neoen, Opdenergy, Solarpack, Akuo Energy all have seen M&A activity 2022–24; this consolidation premium validates Solaria's asset values and creates an M&A floor under the share price [#J]

  • Stage fragmentation: Below the mid-tier, distributed/community solar developers are proliferating at Stage 6 but lack the capital and grid access for utility-scale projects; this fragmentation of smaller players does not directly threaten Solaria's utility-scale positioning [#A]

  • Vertical integration trend (upstream): Solaria and European peers are explicitly NOT integrating upstream into manufacturing — European solar manufacturing policy (Net Zero Industry Act targets) may incentivise some EPC contractors and utilities to take minority stakes in European module manufacturers; Solaria has no stated intention in this direction [#A]

  • Vertical integration trend (downstream): Some European IPPs are exploring supply/retail integration — Iberdrola's integrated model already does this; smaller IPPs like Solaria could theoretically develop an industrial customer supply platform but this would require a retail licence and significant customer acquisition investment [#J]

  • Disintermediation: The traditional intermediary role of utilities (buying wholesale, selling retail) is being partially disintermediated by corporate PPA structures where IPPs sell direct to industrial consumers; Solaria benefits from this trend (higher PPA prices than wholesale market-mediated prices) [#J]

  • DTC trend: Direct-to-corporate PPA (effectively DTC for energy) is structurally growing — Spain's CNMC regulatory framework supports direct bilateral PPAs; Solaria's PPA-first strategy positions it to benefit from continued corporate clean energy procurement growth [#J]

  • New entrant — sovereign funds: ACWA Power (Saudi), Masdar (Abu Dhabi), and GCC sovereign vehicles are entering European solar IPP markets with low cost-of-capital advantages — their project-level WACC may be 100–200bps lower than Solaria's, allowing them to bid on PPAs or land acquisitions at prices Solaria cannot match [#A]

  • Technology disruption — storage: Battery storage integration at solar farms (solar+storage) is becoming a competitive differentiator — projects with co-located BESS can shift generation to higher-price evening/morning periods, capturing price arbitrage; Solaria's current portfolio is primarily pure solar; failure to integrate storage could disadvantage it vs storage-integrated peers as solar cannibalises midday prices [#J]

  • Technology disruption — perovskite: Next-generation solar cell technology (perovskite, tandem cells) could render current silicon-based module economics less advantaged by 2030–35; however, this affects the manufacturing stage (not Solaria's) and represents module price deflation (Solaria as buyer benefits) [#A]

  • Pricing power shift — upstream: The module price collapse of 2023–24 has shifted pricing power entirely to buyers (IPPs including Solaria) — this is a structural shift driven by Chinese capacity overinvestment; it benefits Solaria's project economics but makes the module supply chain fragile to trade policy disruption [#J]

  • Pricing power shift — downstream: Spanish wholesale power prices have structurally softened as renewable penetration increases — solar cannibalisation effect is emerging (midday prices increasingly near zero during high-sun periods); this shifts pricing power away from Stage 7 generators toward flexible generation and storage assets over the 2025–2030 horizon [#J]

  • Business model innovation: The emergence of green hydrogen as a solar power offtake pathway (electrolysis projects co-located with or adjacent to solar farms) represents a structural new demand channel for Solaria-type generation assets; if hydrogen economics mature (current cost ~€3–5/kg in Spain vs green target <€2/kg), this creates a new Stage 7 revenue pathway independent of electricity markets [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on structural evolution — the accelerating M&A consolidation premium that currently validates Solaria's asset value could reverse if interest rates remain elevated longer than consensus expects; infrastructure fund return requirements are acutely interest-rate sensitive, and a prolonged high-rate environment could compress IPP acquisition multiples from current ~15–20x EBITDA toward 10–12x, dramatically reducing both the M&A floor and Solaria's equity multiple [#J]
