KEY FINDINGS — J→A→I Order

  • Recovery verdict: [#J] Campari is the strongest-positioned mid-cap spirits compounder in Europe right now — Aperol's structural underpenetration in the US, a credible deleverage story 12 months ahead of plan, and a new CEO with a specific US mandate give it a differentiated near-term catalyst set vs peers.
  • Destocking largely cleared but not done: [#J] US destocking was the dominant 2024-25 headwind; Q3 2025 and Q1 2026 data show sequential improvement, but Grand Marnier remains actively destocking and Wild Turkey is only barely back to growth — full cycle completion is probably late-2026.

  • Fulcrum driver for 18-36M EPS: [#J] Three interlocking levers — (1) interest cost reduction as deleverage continues, (2) Aperol US penetration lifting House of Aperitifs from 2% to mid-single-digit organic, (3) Wild Turkey/Espolòn normalisation in the US — any two of three landing is a meaningful EPS compounder.

§1 BUSINESS OVERVIEW & REVENUE MODEL

Section verdict: Campari is a focused premium spirits platform built on four brand "houses," with Aperol as the dominant earnings engine; the revenue model blends genuine organic brand compounding with serial M&A — the strategic shift to a "House of Brands" GBO structure in 2024 is a meaningful operational evolution.

  • Business description: Davide Campari-Milano NV (CPR-IT), listed Borsa Italiana, is the world's sixth-largest premium spirits company with net sales of €3,051m in FY2025. [#I]
  • Headquartered Milan; incorporated Netherlands; c.3,000 staff globally.

  • Majority-controlled by Lagfin SCA (Garavoglia family), limiting float and M&A vulnerability.

  • House of Brands operating model: [#I] In 2024, Campari reorganised around four "houses," each with end-to-end P&L responsibility and distinct category focus:

  • House of Aperitifs (45% of group sales, Q1-26): Aperol, Campari aperitif, Campari Soda, Aperol Spritz RTD.
  • House of Whiskey & Rum (~20%): Wild Turkey, Russell's Reserve, Appleton Estate, Jamaican rum.
  • House of Agave (~8%): Espolòn tequila; elevated to "global priority" brand status in 2024.
  • House of Cognac & Champagne (~9%): Courvoisier (acq. 2024), Grand Marnier, Bisquit, Lallier.

  • Local brands/others (~18%): SKYY vodka (being deprioritised), Forty Creek, Cinzano, regional brands.

  • Revenue model composition: [#A] Organic growth has historically been c.50% of long-run contribution, with M&A providing the remainder — this 50/50 split is now under revision as M&A paused.

  • FY2025 organic topline: +2.4% (vs +9.7% in FY2023, +2.4% in FY2024 — deceleration real).
  • Reported net sales €3,051m FY2025 vs €3,070m FY2024 (flat on reported; FX drag meaningful).

  • Q1 2026: +2.9% organic, MAT organic accelerating to +3.9%.

  • Channel mix: [#I] On-premise is the key channel for aperitif brands — Campari has consistently outperformed the on-premise sector, with +5% YTD on-premise growth in US through 9M-25 vs sector average (c.+1pp outperformance vs category).

  • Off-premise and e-commerce growing but remain secondary; travel retail double-digit growth in 2024.

  • Organic vs inorganic contribution: [#A] Post-Courvoisier, the inorganic contribution to 2025 revenue is included in base; going forward, Campari has explicitly paused M&A, meaning all growth must come organically until leverage drops further.

  • Sceptical lens: The House of Brands reorganisation was announced in Oct-24 — too early to judge whether giving each house "end-to-end P&L responsibility" improves marketing discipline vs prior centralised model, or creates coordination friction across a relatively small organisation.

§3 BRAND PORTFOLIO QUALITY

Section verdict: Aperol is a genuine ⚡ RARE compounder — arguably the best single brand in European spirits of the past 20 years; the rest of the portfolio is respectable but not exceptional, with Wild Turkey recovering and Espolòn still early-stage; Grand Marnier and Courvoisier are current underperformers.

  • Aperol — flagship: [#J] Aperol grew from €25m revenue at acquisition (2003) to >€700m today — c.28x growth in 22 years. This is a genuine category-creator, not a brand maintainer.
  • ⚡ RARE: Aperol is the only global spirits brand to have created and led its own occasion (Aperol Spritz aperitivo ritual) at scale across c.100 markets.
  • FY2025: House of Aperitifs +5% (Aperol specifically grew 1% overall; +15% in the US; double-digit in UK, Brazil).
  • Q1-2026: House of Aperitifs +2.1% organically; Aperol grew across 18 of 22 tracked markets.

  • US opportunity: CEO Hunt's own framing — "one in two US consumers have never heard of Aperol" — implies the US penetration curve is still early innings. US was only 7-8% of Aperol's global volume as of FY2024 (vs US being 28% of group sales).

  • Campari aperitif — the eponymous brand: [#A] Campari brand itself is smaller than Aperol but strategically important; Italian aperitivo classic with strong Italy/Europe presence, growing in US cocktail culture.

  • Campari aperitif grew in Q1-26 as part of aperitifs house; specific brand-level data not always disclosed separately.

  • Wild Turkey (bourbon/whiskey): [#A] Wild Turkey is the anchor of the House of Whiskey & Rum; suffered through 2024-25 bourbon industry destocking alongside category peers.

  • FY2024: Wild Turkey declined c.10% in Q1-24 due to US/Australia shipment pressure.
  • FY2025: Wild Turkey grew +14% in US in Q3-25; overall House of Whiskey & Rum +5% for year; Russell's Reserve (premium sub-brand) growing faster.
  • Q1-2026: House of Whiskey & Rum -5% — renewed pressure suggests bourbon cycle not fully resolved.

  • Capex commitment ($161m new distillery) signals long-run bullishness on bourbon demand.

  • Espolòn tequila: [#A] Espolòn is Campari's fastest-growing global priority brand; 1.67m cases in US in 2025 (+5.6% per Impact Databank); expanded by 600,000+ cases in US since 2021.

  • H1-24 growth +22.2% in core US market; growth rate moderating as base builds (3% organic in FY2025 group-wide).
  • Q1-26: House of Agave +4.9% — Espolòn Blanco leading growth; still gaining share in premium tequila.

  • ❌ Espolòn faces intensifying competition in premium tequila (Don Julio, Lunazul, Casamigos) — Campari's tequila is a challenger brand in a crowded field, not a category leader.

  • Grand Marnier: [#A] Grand Marnier (acquired 2016 for €684m) has been a problematic performer recently.

  • FY2024: +13.3% growth, riding a strong US showing.
  • 9M-2025: -14% organic; H1-25 alone -20% due to destocking, high comparison base, and pricing focus.
  • Structural concern: Grand Marnier's US-heavy, cognac-liqueur positioning makes it vulnerable to the same cognac cycle downturn hitting Courvoisier.

  • ❌ Grand Marnier at -14% in 9M-25 is a significant drag; the 2024 surge may have reflected temporary pantry loading rather than genuine demand recovery.

  • Courvoisier — newest acquisition: [#A] Acquired April 2024 from Beam Suntory for $1.17bn (Campari's largest ever deal); cognac's 3rd largest global brand by volume; derives 60% of sales from US.

  • Post-acquisition performance: cognac category globally under pressure; Courvoisier early performance described by management as "early re-orders" and "exciting plans" — no hard growth data yet disclosed.
  • Rationale: fourth leg of portfolio (cognac alongside aperitifs, bourbon, tequila); creates French brand cluster synergies with Grand Marnier and Lallier champagne.
  • Integration thesis: premiumise via SKU rationalisation and packaging upgrade (same playbook as Grand Marnier post-2016).

  • Risk: paid peak-cycle price for cognac into US consumer weakness; the $0.12bn earnout payable in 2029 based on 2028 sales targets adds contingent downside.

  • Other brands: [#I] SKYY Vodka being strategically deprioritised; Appleton Estate rum (Jamaica) performing well in on-premium segment; Forty Creek Canadian whisky niche; Cinzano vermouth stabilising.

  • Sceptical lens: The portfolio is top-heavy — Aperol alone may represent 20-25% of group sales and a disproportionate share of group profit. If the aperitivo occasion slows (weather, fashion, competitive aperitivo alternatives), there is no single brand large enough to compensate. Grand Marnier and Courvoisier together represent c.£2bn+ of acquisition cost performing poorly simultaneously.

§4 M&A STRATEGY & TRACK RECORD

Section verdict: Campari's serial acquirer model has been net value-creative over the BKC era (27 acquisitions), but the evidence for hard SRCA from M&A integration is mixed — Aperol was the transformative deal; Grand Marnier is inconclusive; Courvoisier is the major unresolved test; M&A is now explicitly paused.

  • Track record overview: [#I] Under Bob Kunze-Concewitz (CEO 2007-2024), Campari made c.27 acquisitions including Appleton Estate (2012), Bulldog Gin, Grand Marnier (2016, €684m), and most recently Courvoisier (2024, $1.17bn). This is a genuine M&A-as-growth-engine model.

  • Aperol — the transformative deal: [#J] Acquired from Barbero in 2003 for c.€60m. Now worth >€700m revenue annually. IRR on this single acquisition is almost incalculable — it is the defining justification for Campari's premium M&A valuation.

  • ⚡ RARE: Aperol is arguably the best single-brand acquisition in European spirits history by any measure of value creation.

  • Grand Marnier (2016, €684m) — inconclusive: [#A] Acquired for a substantial premium; undergone visual overhaul and SKU premiumisation since. Sales track record shows recent boom-bust — +20% in H1-24, then -20% in H1-25. It is too early to declare integration failure, but the pattern suggests the US channel is more volatile than the brand thesis implied.

  • Returns on Grand Marnier acquisition unclear; no disclosure of brand-level profitability vs acquisition price.

  • Courvoisier (2024, $1.17bn) — unproven: [#A] Largest deal in Campari history; paid at a significant EV multiple for a brand in a declining cognac cycle; closed April 2024.

  • Strategic rationale sound: creates French house cluster (Courvoisier + Grand Marnier + Lallier), US distribution leverage with 60% of Courvoisier US-weighted.
  • Integration playbook: SKU rationalisation + packaging upgrade + premiumise — identical to Grand Marnier approach.

  • ❌ Deal peak leverage (3.6x EBITDA) came directly from this acquisition; capital allocation timing was poor given category cycle.

  • M&A pause (2025 onwards): [#A] Simon Hunt explicitly confirmed M&A pause until leverage falls further: "the main reason for pausing M&A is the company's net debt." At 2.5x Dec-25, headroom returning.

  • This is a meaningful strategic pivot — 50% of growth historically from M&A is now 100% organic.

  • Risk: without M&A pipeline, medium-term growth depends entirely on organic brand performance and category recovery.

  • Flywheel vs treadmill assessment: [#J] The Aperol precedent is a flywheel — one transformational acquisition compounding for 20 years. But Grand Marnier and Courvoisier look more like treadmill assets requiring constant A&P spend to stabilise without generating self-reinforcing growth. The honest answer: partially flywheel (aperitifs), partially treadmill (cognac/whisky tail).

  • Sceptical lens: The M&A pause is presented as capital discipline, but it may also reflect that there are no obvious deals available at sensible prices given current sector valuations. If organic growth in 2026-27 disappoints, the pressure to acquire again — potentially at the wrong point of the cycle again — will return.

  • Americas (45% of sales, 2024-25): [#A] US is c.27-28% of total group sales — Campari's single largest market. US performance:
  • FY2024: US broadly flat (Espolòn +22% H1; Aperol +11%; SKYY/Wild Turkey drags offset).
  • 9M-2025: US -2% cumulative (destocking in Q1; sequential improvement Q2/Q3).
  • Q3-2025: US +1% organic — "tentative recovery"; on-premise +5% YTD (4pp ahead of sector).
  • Q1-2026: North America +2.8% — below 2024's +6.5%, dragging slightly on group.
  • Tariff risk: €30m full-year 2026 impact estimated (at current 15% EU tariff rate). A 200% tariff scenario would be existential but is not current policy.

  • Canada/LATAM: strong growth; Brazil and Argentina double-digit in Q1-26.

  • EMEA (48% of sales): [#A] Europe is the Aperol heartland and Campari's most stable revenue base.

  • Italy: meaningful Q1-2024 decline (poor weather, high comparison base); stabilised in FY2025.
  • Germany, UK: strong Aperol growth — UK particularly standout in FY2025.
  • Wider Europe: "24 countries delivered growth" per FY2025 commentary; geographic diversification within Europe broad.

  • Africa (developing markets): double-digit emerging growth.

  • APAC (c.7% of sales): [#A] Small but growing; direct markets include Australia, China, South Korea, India, Japan, New Zealand.

  • China: positive growth in FY2024-25 driven by Wild Turkey, Russell's Reserve and initial Courvoisier re-orders; not a major near-term contributor.
  • ❌ APAC as region declined 3% in FY2024; Q1-26 is the one region showing organic decline; cognac cycle weakness and Chinese consumer caution the headwind.
  • India: growing market for Wild Turkey and premium spirits; early stage.

  • Australia: Wild Turkey destocking a headwind in 2024-25 — significant market for the brand.

  • LATAM developing markets: [#I] Q1-2026 developing markets grew +12.7% (Brazil, Argentina leading). Small base but strong momentum; Aperol expanding in South American aperitivo culture.

  • Sceptical lens: EMEA/Europe is 48% of sales but Aperol-dependent; Europe's aperitivo occasion is not immune to fashion risk. Italy's 2024 decline shows even the heartland can disappoint. If Aperol US penetration stalls, the company has limited alternative geographic engines at scale.

§6 PRICING POWER & VOLUME DYNAMICS

Section verdict: Campari has demonstrated real but not absolute pricing power — three years of double-digit organic growth (2021-2023) included genuine price/mix lift; but the 2024 volume softness on weather/consumer confidence shows the brand portfolio cannot fully override macro headwinds; destocking now largely cleared at group level but individual brands (Grand Marnier, Wild Turkey) remain in-cycle.

  • CQ-C3 answer — pricing power reality: [#J] Price increases taken in 2021-2023 were absorbed at the group level without catastrophic volume loss — but this was a strong demand environment. The honest pricing power test was 2024, when volumes softened despite higher prices.
  • 2021-23: organic growth driven c.60% by volume, c.40% by price/mix (BKC era characterisation in investor presentations).
  • 2024: organic +2.4% with price/mix positive but volumes soft — Italy declined 5.2%; US flat; the residual growth came from geographic expansion, not per-unit demand resilience.

  • Pricing power verdict (Wisdom Library Gold model): Campari has demonstrable pricing power in aperitifs where brand equity is strong; in whisky and cognac, pricing discipline is more contested and destocking suggests channel elasticity is real.

  • Destocking status — CQ-C1: [#A] Group-level: largely complete. Management characterised FY2025 as post-destocking normalisation. Evidence:

  • US depletions (consumer takeaway) running ahead of shipments through 9M-25 — positive signal.
  • Q3-25 and Q1-26 sequential improvement in US organic growth.
  • Grand Marnier: still actively destocking as of 9M-25; H2-25 saw "stabilising performance" — incomplete.
  • Wild Turkey: Q1-26 back in negative territory (-5% for House of Whiskey & Rum) despite Q3-25 recovery — incomplete.

  • Overall verdict: destocking cycle approximately 70-80% complete at group level; two specific brands (Grand Marnier, Wild Turkey/bourbon) carrying remaining inventory overhang into 2026.

  • Volume vs price decomposition (most recent): [#A] FY2025 organic growth of +2.4% decomposed approximately:

  • Price/mix: +3-4% contribution (management commentary on gross margin accretion and pricing actions).
  • Volume: slightly negative (net volumes down given portfolio streamlining of secondary brands like SKYY).

  • This means organic "growth" in FY2025 was almost entirely price-led — volume growth is not yet confirmed.

  • Input cost environment: [#I] COGS tailwinds expected in 2026 (commodity deflation; agave prices normalising after tequila boom). Gross margin expected "moderate expansion" in 2026 despite tariff headwind of €30m.

  • Agave: a critical input for Espolòn; agave prices normalising post-2022 peak.

  • Glass, cardboard, logistics: deflating from 2022-23 peaks.

  • Sceptical lens: The pricing power narrative was built during an exceptional demand environment (post-pandemic spirits boom, 2021-23). Campari has not yet been tested through a genuine recessionary premium-spending contraction. Italy's 2024 performance — poor weather as explanation — may partly mask deeper penetration saturation risk for Aperol in its core market.

His William Grant stint ended in 2020 — he has not been in a large-cap spirits CEO seat for 5 years. The BKC-era track record is the basis for management premium in the valuation, but BKC is gone.

§8 KEY RISKS

Section verdict: Four risks dominate: (1) Aperol brand concentration is the existential risk; (2) Courvoisier execution in a difficult cognac cycle is the capital-allocation test; (3) US tariff permanence would structurally impair the earnings model; (4) leverage leaves limited flexibility if revenue misses.

  • Brand concentration — Aperol: [#J] House of Aperitifs now 45% of group sales and growing; Aperol specifically estimated c.20-25% of group sales. If Aperol decelerates sharply (category saturation, competitive aperitivo alternatives, fashion cycle), there is no brand of comparable profitability to compensate.
  • Historical precedent: Aperol has been compounding for 22 years without category reversal — but the cadence of growth has slowed (1% overall in FY2025 vs double-digits previously).

  • Weather sensitivity: two consecutive years (H1-24, H1-25) of weather-impacted European aperitif consumption is not definitively a trend but raises questions.

  • Courvoisier execution risk: [#A] ❌ Paid $1.17bn for a cognac brand at the top of the cognac cycle, into a category now structurally challenged in the US (consumer trading down, Chinese ultra-premium cognac demand collapsing with luxury contraction).

  • Grand Marnier -14% in 9M-25 is the canary — US cognac-liqueur weakness is real, not temporary.
  • Integration timeline: Grand Marnier took 6-8 years to see meaningful brand transformation; Courvoisier may require similar patience.

  • The $0.12bn earn-out payable 2029 based on 2028 sales targets adds asymmetric contingent downside.

  • US tariff permanence: [#A] Current 15% EU tariff on spirits imports; €30m estimated FY2026 group impact (vs €37m initially estimated). A return to 200% tariff scenario (threatened but not enacted) would be existential for ~€100m of European-produced spirits revenues.

  • Campari has explicitly not passed tariffs to US consumers as of Q1-26 — absorbing in margin.

  • Mitigation: local US production (Wild Turkey) partially insulates bourbon; Aperol (Italian-produced) has limited near-term production shift options.

  • Leverage: [#I] Net debt €1,958m, 2.5x EBITDA; comfortable vs covenant levels but leaves limited capacity for opportunistic M&A or balance sheet shock absorption. Interest cost at 4.4% consumes meaningful EBITDA.

  • Category risk — bourbon: [#A] US bourbon industry is in its first meaningful inventory correction in a decade; Wild Turkey is caught in this alongside Brown-Forman's Woodford Reserve and Beam's Maker's Mark. Q1-26 regression (-5% House of Whiskey & Rum) after Q3-25 recovery is concerning.

  • Premium spending cyclicality: [#I] In a European/UK recession scenario (Iran conflict, energy shock, consumer confidence erosion per Richard's current risk-off macro), aperitivo occasions may compress. On-premise traffic is already under pressure in multiple markets.

  • Sceptical lens: The combination of three simultaneous headwinds — Grand Marnier destocking, Wild Turkey bourbon cycle, US tariffs — all hitting in the same 12-month window while leverage remains elevated, means there is less room for a further demand disappointment than the 2.5x leverage ratio implies.
