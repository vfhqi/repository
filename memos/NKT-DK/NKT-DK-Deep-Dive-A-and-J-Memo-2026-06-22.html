E1 RA1 CQ1 — Strong external change forces / tailwinds?

1.
The European power grid investment supercycle is the most durable structural industrial tailwind visible in European equities in Q2 2026: European transmission system operators (TSOs) have committed to a step-change in grid investment to accommodate renewable energy integration, with the European Commission's GridTech and REPowerEU programmes underpinning €100bn+ of planned spend over the next decade
2.
Offshore wind is the primary cable demand driver: connecting floating and fixed offshore wind farms to shore requires subsea HV and EHV cable runs of 50–200km+; each GW of offshore wind requires approximately 1,000–1,500 tonnes of cable; European offshore wind targets imply 40–60GW of additions by 2030, requiring cable volumes that structurally exceed current European production capacity
3.
Cross-border interconnectors; subsea power links between national grids; represent a second, distinct demand leg: the EU's 15% interconnection target by 2030, Nordic-UK links (NeuConnect, Viking Link), and Mediterranean interconnectors together represent multi-billion-euro cable programmes over the remainder of the decade
4.
Energy security post-Russia (2022–2026): European governments have materially accelerated grid reinforcement timelines following the Russian gas cutoff; the political will to spend on energy infrastructure is at a generational high; this has converted projects previously in planning into binding contracts, expanding NKT's addressable order pipeline far faster than the 2019–2021 pre-crisis trajectory
5.
HV and EHV cable supply is structurally constrained: NKT, Prysmian, and Nexans collectively control essentially all European HV/EHV cable manufacturing capacity; barriers to entry (cable-laying vessel fleet, specialised factories, HV testing facilities, project execution track record) mean no new entrants can address the demand gap within a 5–7-year horizon; this gives the three incumbents pricing power and project selectivity they have not historically enjoyed
6.
NKT's geographic positioning amplifies the external tailwind: NKT has its primary cable factories in Denmark and Germany (Karlskrona cable-laying vessel operations + Cologne land cable), ideally positioned to serve North Sea offshore wind, Nordics interconnectors, and German grid reinforcement: the three highest-spend corridors in Europe

E1 RA1 CQ2 — Strong internal change forces?

7.
NKT's most important internal change force is the Karlskrona EHV expansion: NKT is constructing a new EHV (extra-high-voltage, 400–500kV+) cable manufacturing facility at Karlskrona, Sweden, the first purpose-built EHV factory in the Nordics; this is a step-change in NKT's technical capability, opening the highest-value tier of the offshore cable market; interconnectors and deepwater offshore wind; that NKT could not previously address at scale
8.
The Cologne factory expansion adds onshore HVDC and AC cable capacity: Germany's Energiewende requires extensive onshore grid reinforcement, particularly the 'electricity highways' linking north-coast wind generation to south-German industrial load; NKT's Cologne expansion is directly positioned to capture this contract flow
9.
Project selectivity is an internal change force in its own right: with a record order book and constrained manufacturing capacity, NKT is now deliberately selecting for the highest-margin, most technically complex projects (EHV, offshore, long-haul) and declining lower-margin commodity HV work; this margin-mix improvement is an internal driver of EPS growth independent of volume
10.
Senior management renewal under CEO Claes Westerlind (appointed 2021) has refocused NKT's capital allocation toward the EHV/offshore opportunity and away from lower-return segments; the strategy is clearer and more focused than it was under prior leadership
11.
The balance sheet is being deployed in controlled fashion: NKT has funded its capacity programme through a mix of operating cash flow and moderate debt, avoiding the equity dilution that has characterised some peers' capacity expansions; this preserves per-share EPS growth leverage for existing shareholders
12.
NKT's financial credibility has improved markedly: the company raised FY2025 guidance multiple times during 2024–2025 as the order book converted to revenues faster than initially planned; this track record of guidance conservatism is a positive internal change force for investor sentiment and re-rating potential

E1 RA2 CQ1 — Absence of external headwinds?

13.
There is no credible substitution threat to HV/EHV cable in the offshore wind and interconnector context: wireless power transmission is entirely impractical at the scale and distance of offshore projects; HVDC overhead lines cannot replace subsea cables; the cable requirement is physics-determined, not technology-vulnerable
14.
Regulatory risk to European grid investment is extremely low: grid expansion is simultaneously a climate, energy security, and industrial policy priority across the EU; no European government is currently proposing to reduce grid spending, and several (Germany, UK, Denmark, Netherlands) have recently accelerated it
15.
The Iran conflict (Q1–Q2 2026 risk-off driver) has zero direct revenue impact on NKT: NKT has no Middle East exposure and no commodity price pass-through to earnings in the way an oil-linked industrial would; elevated oil prices are marginally net-negative on cable input costs (polymer sheaths) but the impact is small relative to the contract revenue line
16.
Currency risk (DKK/EUR/GBP) is manageable: NKT operates in a mix of DKK, EUR, and GBP but has natural hedges across its Nordic-European project book; the company actively hedges project-by-project; no material FX headwind is visible at Triaging depth
17.
There is a timing headwind worth flagging: some European grid projects have experienced permitting delays (particularly offshore wind grid connection permits in Germany and the Netherlands); this slows contract flow rather than destroying it, but it creates order-book-to-revenue conversion uncertainty at the margin
18.
No China competition risk at the EHV/offshore tier: Chinese cable manufacturers (Hengtong, ZTT) have made inroads at the commodity HV end; however, they have no presence in EHV offshore work, no Western project track records, and face geopolitical procurement exclusion in most European energy infrastructure contexts

E1 RA2 CQ2 — Well-invested base?

19.
NKT's manufacturing estate is being substantially upgraded through the Karlskrona EHV facility and Cologne expansion: total capex over 2023–2026 is the largest investment programme in NKT's history, representing ~DKK 4–6bn; the business is investing ahead of demand, which creates temporary cash flow drag but ensures capacity is available when large contracts reach execution phase
20.
The cable-laying vessel fleet (CLV) is a critical strategic asset: NKT owns and operates Aurora and Dumea, two purpose-built cable installation vessels; owning these vessels (rather than chartering) eliminates a key execution bottleneck and provides margin capture across the full project value chain (manufacturing + installation); the fleet is fully booked for multiple years
21.
The workforce is being grown and skilled: EHV cable manufacturing and offshore installation require specialised technical skills not available in the open market at scale; NKT has been growing its skilled workforce systematically, including through apprenticeship programmes and engineering partnerships in Denmark, Sweden, and Germany
22.
Working capital investment matches the project backlog: long-duration project contracts (3–5 years from award to delivery) require significant upfront materials procurement and working capital; NKT's balance sheet is sized appropriately for this profile, with committed credit facilities backing the project pipeline
23.
The IT and project management infrastructure has been upgraded: following some project cost overruns in 2019–2021, NKT has invested in better ERP systems and project financial controls; the improvement in guidance accuracy since 2022 (consistent guidance raises) is partly a function of better internal management systems
24.
Component supply chain is well-managed: copper (primary cable conductor) is the main commodity input; NKT has long-term supply agreements and active commodity hedging; aluminium conductor cables are an increasing proportion of onshore product mix, reducing copper exposure at the margin

E5 RA1 — Suitability for bear market / economic downturn

1.

3.
Grid investment is counter-cyclical in practice: TSOs and energy utilities are public or quasi-public entities with mandated capital programmes; when private-sector capex falls in a downturn, governments often accelerate infrastructure spending as a stimulus measure; NKT is therefore a potential beneficiary rather than a victim of the bear market scenario
4.
NKT's customer base (National Grid, RTE, TenneT, Ørsted, Vattenfall, Danish Energy Agency) carries sovereign or quasi-sovereign credit quality: in a financial stress scenario, the probability of customer payment default on awarded contracts is extremely low
5.
Margins may actually expand in a downturn: a general industrial recession would reduce demand for commodity cable products (power cables, data cables) which are produced by NKT's lower-tier competitors; this could reduce competition for raw materials and labour, improving NKT's input cost profile

9.

13.

E11 RA2 CQ3 — Vanilla new entrants

17.

19.
The supply constraint is therefore structural, not temporary: the barriers to entry are high enough that the oligopoly's pricing power is protected through the entire visible order pipeline (2025–2030+)
20.
New entrant risk: negligible

E11 RA2 CQ4 — Orthogonal/innovative new entrants

21.
There is no orthogonal technology that substitutes for HV cable in the offshore wind / interconnector application: wireless power transmission (microwave, laser) is many decades from commercial viability at scale; superconducting cables are a theoretical alternative but face enormous cost and cryogenic engineering barriers; no viable orthogonal substitution is foreseeable
22.
The only genuine long-term orthogonal risk is demand-side: if offshore wind is displaced as a generation technology (by very cheap onshore solar, advanced nuclear, etc.) the need for long-distance cable runs could reduce; this is a 20–30-year scenario at the earliest, well outside the Viewforth investment horizon
23.
Orthogonal entrant risk: negligible

E11 RA2 CQ5 — Substitutes/alternatives

24.
There is no substitute for HV cable in the offshore wind value chain: the electrons must travel from turbine to shore through a physical cable; overhead lines are not viable at sea; there is no alternative transmission medium
25.
For onshore grid reinforcement, HVDC cables and HVAC cables are not substitutes for each other but are alternative technical specifications; NKT manufactures both; this is a market segmentation reality rather than a substitution threat
26.
Demand-side alternatives (demand response, distributed generation, storage reducing long-distance transmission needs) could theoretically reduce cable requirements at the margin but are decades away from materially affecting European TSO investment programmes
27.
Substitute risk: negligible
