§1.2 — Trajectory Analysis

  • L3Y revenue CAGR: ~14% (FY23 SEK 6,550m → FY25 SEK 8,650m), with YoY stepping: +18% (FY23), +19% (FY24), +11% (FY25) — clean acceleration then controlled deceleration [#J]

  • Organic vs inorganic split: approximately 6-8% organic, 6-10% inorganic per year; FY24 saw the highest inorganic contribution (~9%) as acquisition cadence peaked at 7 deals [#A]

  • FX contribution: minor; LAGR revenue is predominantly SEK/EUR — FX tailwind in FY23 (weak SEK) partially reversed in FY25 [#I]

  • L3Y EBIT CAGR: ~23% (SEK 895m → SEK 1,360m) — materially above revenue CAGR, confirming operating leverage delivery [#J]

  • Margin walk: EBIT% expanded from 13.7% (FY23) → 15.3% (FY24) → 15.7% (FY25); the 200bp FY23→FY24 expansion is the most material single-year move in the L3Y window [#A]

  • Operating leverage source: mix shift toward niche / proprietary product divisions (higher gross margin) plus scale benefits on fixed costs; not cost cutting [#A]

  • L3Y EPS CAGR (adjusted): ~22% (SEK 6.60 → SEK 10.05) — tracks EBIT CAGR closely, confirming limited financial engineering in EPS accretion [#J]

  • EPS accretion drivers: ~70% earnings growth, ~15% margin expansion, ~15% acquisition accretion; no material share buybacks or financial leverage changes [#A]

  • Inflection point — FY24: FY24 was the peak-quality acceleration year — organic growth ran double-digit, acquisition contribution was high, and margin expanded simultaneously; this combination is unlikely to recur at same magnitude [#A]

  • FY25 deceleration context: organic growth slowed to ~5% as end-market demand softened (industrial automation, electronics); acquisition contribution held but couldn't offset fully [#I]

  • Inflection point — FY25 H1 minor miss: FY25 H1 is the only semi-annual period in L3Y where consensus estimates were NOT beaten — revenue -2% and EPS -3% vs prior consensus; recovered in H2 [#A]

  • Sceptical read on trajectory: Through what mechanism might apparent growth be flattered? Acquisitions contribute ~40% of revenue CAGR — strip that out and the organic story is a 6-7% CAGR grower, not exceptional by Swedish industrial standards. PPA amortisation in adjusted EPS obscures true acquisition economics — if acquired businesses are earning lower returns than implied by add-back, reported trajectory flatters quality [#J]

§2 — Quality of Earnings

Quality verdict: Earnings quality is broadly clean with one structural flag: PPA amortisation add-backs are large, growing, and the underlying acquisition economics are not fully transparent from headline adjusted figures [#J]

  • Cash conversion: OCF/EBIT averaged ~88% across L3Y (FY23 ~85%, FY24 ~92%, FY25 ~87%) — above the ~80% base-rate for European serial acquirers; no deterioration trend [#A]

  • OCF vs NI detail: FY24 OCF ~SEK 1,050m vs NI ~SEK 780m (135% conversion on net income) — healthy; FY25 OCF ~SEK 1,180m vs NI ~SEK 860m (137%) — stable [#A]

  • PPA amortisation trend: PPA amortisation growing as M&A compounds — SEK 155m (FY23) → SEK 185m (FY24) → SEK 215m (FY25); each new acquisition adds ~8-12 years of amortisation drag [#A]

  • Non-GAAP gap expanding: adjusted EPS is ~7-8% above reported EPS and gap is widening; this is a structural feature of serial acquirers, not cosmetic, but buyer must underwrite the PPA charges as real economic cost if acquisition returns are adequate [#I]

  • Recurring vs one-off: Management consistently adjusts for PPA and M&A transaction costs; restructuring charges (~SEK 10-15m per period) are genuinely small and non-recurring — no pattern of items dressed as one-off but recurring [#J]

  • M&A costs trend: transaction costs in income statement running SEK 15-25m per year — consistent with active acquirer; not inflating adjusted earnings materially [#I]

  • Working capital trend: NWC/revenue broadly stable at ~14-16% across L3Y; no deteriorating receivables or inventory build suggesting demand pull-forward [#A]

  • Receivables turn: DSO approximately 55-60 days — stable across L3Y; no elongation that would signal collection stress [#I]

  • Inventory turn: stable at ~4-5x; slight inventory build in FY25 H1 (pre-announced by management) reversed cleanly in H2 [#A]

  • D&A trend: D&A (ex-PPA) growing broadly in line with revenue (~2.5-3.0% of revenue) — no evidence of D&A compression to flatter EBITDA; right-of-use asset D&A (IFRS 16) stable [#I]

  • Adjustments trend assessment: Adjusted EPS is higher than reported in every period (expected for acquirer), but the size of adjustment is growing predictably with acquisition cadence — not a red flag per se, but requires DD scrutiny on whether individual deal IRRs justify the PPA drag [#A]

  • ❌ Non-GAAP gap growing: PPA-adjusted EPS gap has widened from ~7% (FY23) to ~7.5% (FY25) — small but directionally negative; in a slowdown, PPA drag would persist while earnings compress [Cross-ref: §9]

  • Sceptical read on quality: Bearish mechanism: if acquired businesses earn lower returns than the PPA-adjusted earnings imply (i.e., the acquisitions are genuinely dilutive at full-cycle), then reported adjusted EPS chronically overstates economic reality. This is undetectable from L3Y income statement analysis alone — requires ROIC by vintage analysis (DD scope) [#J]

§3 — Revenue vs Profit Growth (Margin Sustainability)

Margin sustainability verdict: Margin expansion to date appears structural (mix shift toward niche/proprietary divisions), but the bulk of easy gains have been captured and incremental expansion requires continued high-quality M&A deployment [#J]

  • Revenue CAGR: ~14% L3Y (FY23-FY25); profit CAGR materially higher: EBIT ~23%, EPS (adj.) ~22% — confirms operating leverage delivery [#J]

  • Profit/revenue CAGR spread: ~900bp spread (EBIT vs revenue CAGR) is strong for an industrial serial acquirer; European industrial peer base-rate for this spread is ~200-400bp over a comparable L3Y window [#A]

  • Gross margin trend: Expanding from ~43.8% (FY23) → 45.8% (FY24) → 45.7% (FY25) — ~200bp lift driven by mix shift toward higher-margin niche product segments; FY25 stabilisation suggests mix-shift gains near full capture [#A]

  • Mix shift driver: acquisitions in H2 FY23 and FY24 skewed toward proprietary electronics/automation sub-segments (higher GM) vs earlier distribution-heavy portfolio; visible in per-division disclosures [#I]

  • EBITDA margin trend: 19.2% (FY23) → 19.7% (FY24) → 19.4% (FY25) — plateauing; EBITDA margin expansion less pronounced than EBIT margin expansion because D&A has grown in line [#J]

  • EBIT margin trend: 13.7% → 15.3% → 15.7% — 200bp cumulative expansion; the FY23→FY24 step-up (+160bp) is the most structural single-year improvement in L3Y; FY25 incremental gain (+40bp) reflects diminishing marginal returns [#A]

  • Operating leverage assessment: Scale leverage on SG&A is real but modest (SG&A/revenue ~27-28% stable); the dominant leverage driver is gross margin mix, not SG&A deleveraging — this means margin expansion is contingent on continuing to acquire higher-margin businesses [#A]

  • Cost-cut contribution: Minimal; Lagercrantz does not run cost-restructuring campaigns — margins expand through portfolio evolution, not efficiency drives; this is a structural positive (expansion is durable) but also a ceiling (no tactical lever available if organic softens) [#J]

  • Sustainability assessment: 15-16% EBIT margin appears achievable/sustainable at current portfolio composition; expansion toward 17%+ requires material shift in portfolio mix toward even-higher-margin niches — possible but not assured from FY25 trajectory [#A]

  • Peer context (no formal cohort): European serial acquirers with comparable decentralised models (Addtech, Indutrade reference class) typically operate 10-14% EBIT margins at comparable scale; Lagercrantz's 15-16% sits at the high end of this reference class [#A]

  • Sceptical read on margin sustainability: Bearish mechanism: current portfolio GM% of ~45% assumes recent acquisitions hold margin post-integration; if acquired businesses experience margin mean-reversion (new management, customer repricing, cost inflation), gross margin could compress 100-150bp without warning. The decentralised model makes early detection difficult [#J]

§4 — Seasonal Patterns

Seasonality verdict: Stable and well-understood H2-weighting (Oct–Mar stronger than Apr–Sep); pronounced across L3Y with no evidence of structural shift [#J]

  • H2 vs H1 split : FY23: H2 ~54% of full-year EBIT; FY24: H2 ~53%; FY25: H2 ~53% — remarkably consistent across three fiscal years; H2 carries ~200-300bp higher EBIT margin than H1 in each year [#A]

  • H2 dominance driver: industrial customer budget cycles (capital goods purchasing peaks Oct–Dec in Nordic/European markets); also construction-adjacent divisions more active Oct–Mar in warmer Southern European geographies [#I]

  • Q1 pattern (Apr–Jun, within H1): historically weakest sub-period — customer restocking post year-end lags; not separately disclosed but implied by H1 vs H2 split and management commentary [#I]

  • Strongest quarter: H2 (Oct–Mar) consistently strongest by both revenue and EBIT; no evidence of Q4 (Jan–Mar) acceleration above Oct–Dec within H2 from available data [#I]

  • Seasonality changing?: No material change across L3Y; the H2 weighting has been a feature of Lagercrantz for at least 5+ fiscal years per management commentary; acquisition cadence (which could shift mix) has not disrupted the pattern [#J]

  • Acquisition timing and seasonality: Acquisitions tend to close H1 (April–September) — management comments suggest deliberate timing to see a full H2 contribution in the same fiscal year; this slightly flatters H2 growth rates vs underlying organic seasonality [#A]

  • FY25 H1 miss context: The FY25 H1 slight revenue/EPS miss vs consensus is partially explained by the H1 weakness pattern being more pronounced in a macro-soft environment — not a structural break from seasonal norms [#I]

  • Sceptical read on seasonality: Bearish mechanism: if industrial end-markets structurally weaken in H2 (e.g., sustained capex freeze by Nordic industrial customers), the seasonal H2 recovery expected each year would disappoint — what looks like a timing pattern could mask a weakening underlying cycle [#J]

§5 — Beat/Miss Pattern + Management Credibility

Management credibility verdict: Consistent modest sandbagger — beats consensus on EBIT and EPS in 5 of 6 semi-annual periods in L3Y; single FY25 H1 mild miss is the only exception and was recovered [#J]

  • Beat/miss frequency: 5/6 semi-annual periods with EBIT beats (83%); 5/6 with EPS beats; the one miss (FY25 H1) was modest at -3% vs consensus EPS — no dramatic misses in L3Y [#A]

  • Full-year beats: All 3 full years (FY23, FY24, FY25) delivered above full-year consensus EBIT prior — 100% full-year beat record across L3Y [#J]

  • Beat magnitude: Average beat vs consensus (where available) approximately +3-5% on EBIT, +4-5% on EPS adjusted — consistent modest positive surprises rather than large sandbagging events [#A]

  • Beat distribution: beats are not concentrated in one metric — EBIT, revenue (less consistent), and EPS all show positive skew, suggesting operational outperformance rather than financial engineering [#A]

  • Sandbagging signal: Management guidance style is characteristically conservative — "cautious on macro," "uncertainty in order intake" language precedes periods that deliver above expectations; this pattern is consistent with deliberate sandbagging but is common to the Swedish industrial model (Addtech, Indutrade use identical language) [#A]

  • Guidance accuracy vs Swedish industrial base-rate: Swedish decentralised industrials typically run 60-75% beat frequency on EBIT consensus; LAGR's 83% semi-annual beat rate is above this reference range [#A]

  • FY25 H1 miss dissection: Revenue -2% vs consensus, EPS -3%; driven by organic softness in automation-exposed divisions (LAGR explicitly flagged slower order intake in Q2 of H1); management tone shifted to "cautious" — first visible guidance reset in L3Y [#J]

  • Recovery signal: FY25 H2 returned to beat pattern (+3% EPS vs consensus) — H1 miss appears demand-timing rather than structural [#I]

  • Sell-side revision pattern: Post-FY23 and FY24 earnings releases, sell-side upward revised forward estimates by 3-6% on EBIT; post-FY25 H1 mild miss, revisions were flat to slightly down (-1-2%); FY25 H2 recovery prompted modest upward revisions (+2%) [#A]

  • Direction of revisions over L3Y: Net upward across the period — consensus full-year estimates have been consistently revised higher after earnings releases; downward revisions have been limited and short-lived [#J]

  • Guidance tracking precision: Management does not provide formal point guidance (characteristic of Nordic decentralised model); guidance is qualitative ("continued profitable growth," "active M&A pipeline"); this makes formal guidance tracking impossible but also limits overpromise risk [#I]

  • Sceptical read on management credibility: Bearish mechanism: consistent beats may be running out of cushion — if macro headwinds sustain into FY26, the sandbagging buffer shrinks and beat frequency reverts to base-rate or below. The FY25 H1 miss is the first signal that the buffer is not unlimited. Additionally, without formal quantitative guidance, "management credibility" is being assessed against sell-side estimates that management may have been informally managing [#J]
