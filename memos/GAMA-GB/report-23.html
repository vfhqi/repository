1. DEFINITION

European Tariff and Trade Fragmentation is the ongoing restructuring of global goods and services flows driven by US unilateral tariffs (10–25%+ on EU-origin goods), EU retaliatory measures, and the broader shift toward friend-shoring and supply chain regionalisation. The primary transmission mechanism to European corporate earnings is twofold: (1) direct margin compression where European companies export to tariff-imposing markets without the ability to fully pass through cost increases to end customers, and (2) demand destruction in tariff-exposed sectors where volume declines compound the margin hit. Secondary transmission includes EUR appreciation against USD (earnings translation headwind) and supply chain cost inflation as companies accelerate near-shoring. Time horizon: 2–4 years; this is not a 12-month cyclical event. Confidence in persistence: High. Political economy on both sides of the Atlantic locks in elevated barriers even under normalisation scenarios.

2. BENEFICIARY SUMMARY

Companies with high domestic European revenue, pricing power over their local customer bases, and either (a) imported input cost tailwinds from tariff-exposed suppliers or (b) zero direct exposure to tariff-targeted export markets. The winners are not "safe" defensives — they are businesses that actively gain competitive advantage as export-oriented peers are structurally disadvantaged.

3. AT-RISK SUMMARY

European exporters with >20% revenue exposure to the US or other tariff-imposing markets, thin gross margins, long supply chains crossing tariff borders, and limited ability to reprice quickly. Also at risk: companies with USD-denominated cost bases facing EUR strength, and businesses whose customers are themselves exporters facing margin pressure.

5. BENEFICIARY PROBABLE SETUPS

Ideal entry profile. The cleanest beneficiary setup combines: (1) domestic revenue >65%, (2) pricing power confirmed in most recent earnings print (gross margin flat or expanding YoY), (3) net debt/EBITDA <1.5x, and (4) EPS consensus that has not yet been revised upward to reflect competitive tailwind from peer margin compression. The last point is critical — the alpha is in buying before the consensus recognises the competitive share gain, which typically lags 2–3 quarters after the initial tariff shock.

Valuation at entry. Do not require cheapness. The highest-quality domestic compounders (RELX, Halma, Diploma-type) trade at 20–30x forward earnings. The thesis is that this multiple is sticky or expanding, not compressing — because trade uncertainty drives a defensive premium toward predictable earners. The 2022 European energy crisis provides the parallel: RELX and Sage re-rated by 15–20% relative to industrials over 12 months as earnings predictability was repriced.

Catalysts that crystallise the thesis. (a) Earnings print showing gross margin expansion or flat while sector peers report compression — the divergence trade; (b) management guidance raise citing competitive pricing opportunity; (c) acquisition announced at compressed multiple in trade-disrupted subsector; (d) index reweighting toward defensives as macro deteriorates; (e) sector rotation out of industrials/exporters into domestics by quantitative funds.

Risks for beneficiaries. Three specific risks:
- Valuation stretch: If EUR/USD normalises toward 1.05 and trade deal progress accelerates, the defensive premium collapses faster than earnings recover — multiple contraction risk of 20–25% for highest-rated names
- Second-order demand hit: Even domestic companies face demand headwinds if European consumer confidence collapses on recession fears — the beneficiary thesis requires European GDP holding at +0.5% to +1.5%, not entering contraction
- Crowding: Quality/defensive positioning is already elevated in European portfolios per most fund positioning surveys; overcrowding means the marginal buyer has already acted, limiting upside asymmetry in the very short term (3–6M); 12–18M horizon required to capture full thesis

Standard setup mapping. This maps primarily to the defensive premium re-rating setup and secondarily to earnings divergence / relative EPS upgrade setup. Sizing should be largest where both apply simultaneously.

7. AT-RISK PROBABLE SETUPS

What the typical at-risk stock looks like. The highest-risk configuration is: (1) 20–35% US revenue exposure, (2) EPS consensus built on pre-tariff margin assumptions (gross margin in consensus is flat or +50bps when operational reality is -100 to -200bps), (3) stretched valuation relative to sector (trading at or above 5Y average P/E), and (4) no explicit management commentary on tariff mitigation in most recent earnings. Where all four are present, the at-risk thesis is structural, not temporary.

Triggers that crystallise the thesis. In order of likely impact:
1. Q2/Q3 2026 earnings miss with guidance cut — the single most powerful trigger; sell-side downgrades cascade within 48 hours; quantitative funds exit on earnings momentum flip
2. Management disclosure of supply chain restructuring costs — one-off charges signal that the tariff impact is being formally acknowledged as structural; re-rating follows
3. US volume data below plan for 2+ consecutive quarters — removes the "temporary dislocation" narrative; consensus EPS revision cycle begins
4. Acquisition by a competitor at a lower-than-expected multiple — signals market has repriced the sector's intrinsic value
5. EUR/USD moving decisively above 1.15 — compounds the operating headwind with translation headwind; model convergence accelerates sell-side EPS cuts

Separating temporary from structural at-risk. The key distinction is repricing ability and supply chain flexibility.
- Temporarily at-risk: Company has contractual pricing mechanisms that allow pass-through within 6–12 months, AND has domestic production capacity that can partially substitute for imported inputs, AND US revenue < 15% of group — the tariff shock is real but bounded. These names should be monitored but do not warrant immediate exit.
- Structurally at-risk: Company cannot reprice (competitive market, or multi-year fixed contracts), has no credible near-shoring path within 3 years, and has US/tariff-exposed revenue >25% with limited hedging. These names face permanent EPS step-down, not cyclical dip. Structural at-risk = E–F thematic grade; exit unless other factors dominate.

The false negative risk. Two categories look at-risk but may not be:
- European luxury exporters to the US (LVMH, Hermès, Richemont): brand pricing power is extraordinary; historical evidence from 2018–2019 shows <5% volume elasticity to price in ultra-luxury. However, the mid-tier luxury names (Burberry, Mulberry, Watches of Switzerland) lack this pricing insulation and are genuine E–F at-risk names.
- European defence exporters: appear US-exposed but their US revenues are primarily domestic US subsidiary revenues or NATO-aligned procurement, not subject to EU-US tariff regime in the standard sense.

Standard setup mapping. The classic at-risk setup maps to earnings deterioration (miss + cut cycle) followed by multiple compression (defensive premium erodes, re-rating to cycle trough multiples). In severe cases with balance sheet stress, the liquidity trap setup applies: operating cash flow compression tightens covenant headroom, triggering distressed asset sales at poor prices, further impairing EPS capacity. The 2008–2009 auto supplier crisis (see Delphi, Visteon European subsidiaries) and the 2015–2016 oil services compression (see Wood Group, Petrofac) are the closest historical analogues for the combined margin + multiple compression dynamic.

Final view. The asymmetry favours staying long domestic/services compounders and reducing or avoiding export-heavy industrials with US revenue >20% until one of two conditions is met: (a) explicit management confirmation of tariff pass-through in pricing, or (b) consensus EPS has already been cut to embed the full tariff impact (typically 2–3 quarters after the initial shock). Do not hold through the full cut cycle on the expectation that "it's already priced in" — historical parallels show the cut cycle consistently runs longer and deeper than the initial shock implies.
