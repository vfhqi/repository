4. §2 — Theme 1: Revenue Drivers + Visibility

J-front verdict: Subscription revenue (~60% of group) provides partial visibility, but ICT/B2B lumpiness and ARPU compression risk from churn-driven discounting make 3Y revenue trajectory genuinely uncertain — these questions are designed to surface the hidden part

Q2.1 — ARPU bridge

  • Question: "Can you walk us from current blended ARPU to your 24-month internal plan — split between price increases, mix shift, and churn-driven discounting?"
  • Expected answer: Management cites headline ARPU trend + references bundling as the driver; unlikely to disclose internal plan explicitly
  • Good answer signature: Specific price/volume/mix decomposition; acknowledges the churn-discount tension; references CPI-linked contract clauses
  • Bad answer signature: References only reported ARPU trend without decomposition; deflects to "market conditions are competitive"
  • Follow-up probe: "What percentage of your base is on contracts with explicit CPI escalators vs flat-rate multi-year locks?"
  • Diagnostic note: Fluent decomposition supports bull case (pricing power intact); deflection supports false-friend (headline ARPU stable but underlying mix degrading); bear case = volume-to-retain dynamics already dominating

Q2.2 — ICT/B2B revenue quality

  • Question: "The ICT segment revenue has grown materially — what share is recurring managed-services vs one-time project? And what is the renewal/rebid rate on the recurring portion?"
  • Expected answer: Management describes ICT as growing with large public-sector contracts; less likely to disclose renewal rates voluntarily
  • Good answer signature: Distinguishes recurring vs project clearly; cites renewal rate >80%; names the largest contracts by maturity
  • Bad answer signature: Lumps all ICT as "high-growth"; cannot distinguish recurring from project; cites wins without addressing churn
  • Follow-up probe: "Of your top-5 ICT contracts by revenue, how many come up for rebid in the next 24 months?"
  • Diagnostic note: ICT lumpiness is the bear case's most underappreciated revenue risk; if management cannot decompose this, the market is pricing a recurring-revenue profile that may not exist

Q2.3 — Pay-TV churn dynamics

  • Question: "Your pay-TV base has been under pressure from cord-cutting trends — what is your current monthly churn rate and how has it trended over the last 8 quarters?"
  • Expected answer: Management references bundled-customer stickiness and notes churn is lower for multi-product customers
  • Good answer signature: Cites specific churn rate by tier (standalone vs bundled); notes trend is stabilising; has modelled streaming-substitution impact
  • Bad answer signature: Cites only bundled customer retention without disclosing standalone churn; deflects to "content investment will drive retention"
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the ARPU differential between a customer who churns from standalone pay-TV vs one you retain in a downgraded package?"
  • Diagnostic note: Cord-cutting acceleration is a macro-level bear trigger; management who can model the specific substitution rate for Polish streaming penetration is qualitatively different from one who cannot

Q2.4 — Broadband net-add trajectory

  • Question: "Broadband net-adds appear to be decelerating — is this a market-saturation signal or a deliberate shift in go-to-market?"
  • Expected answer: Management attributes deceleration to mature market; cites focus on ARPU-accretive customers vs volume
  • Good answer signature: Distinguishes between addressable footprint and penetration rate; shows deliberate pricing-up strategy with supporting metrics
  • Bad answer signature: Defensive; blames competition without a specific counter-strategy; cannot distinguish market share loss from deliberate selection
  • Follow-up probe: "What is your broadband penetration rate within your own mobile customer base — and what is the theoretical ceiling?"
  • Diagnostic note: If cross-sell penetration within mobile base is already >50%, the bundling upside is largely extracted; this is a false-friend signal for the convergence bull case

Sceptical close: If management answers the ARPU bridge question fluently and confidently — that is NOT necessarily comfort; it may be a rehearsed answer prepared specifically for investor meetings; the follow-up probes (CPI escalator %, ICT rebid timing) are designed to get beyond the rehearsed layer

5. §3 — Theme 2: Margin Trajectory + Levers

J-front verdict: EBITDA margin is structurally compressed by energy costs, content rights inflation, and Polkomtel network investment — the honest question is how much of the "integration synergy" story is real vs already-captured

Q3.1 — Energy cost hedging

  • Question: "Energy is a material cost line post-Polkomtel integration — what percentage of your 2025–26 energy consumption is hedged, at what price, and what is the P&L sensitivity to a 20% energy price move?"
  • Expected answer: Management confirms some hedging exists; unlikely to disclose exact hedge book detail; may reference PLN/commodity exposure
  • Good answer signature: Discloses hedge ratio by year; provides explicit sensitivity; references active pass-through mechanisms in B2B contracts
  • Bad answer signature: "We manage energy costs actively" without quantification; no sensitivity disclosure; deflects to capex efficiency
  • Follow-up probe: "When your current hedge book rolls off, what is your unhedged baseline cost vs current market?"
  • Diagnostic note: Unhedged energy roll-off is a margin risk that is almost entirely absent from sell-side models for CPS — a bad answer here confirms the market is mispricing this

Q3.2 — Polkomtel integration synergies

  • Question: "Of the PLN-denominated integration synergy target post-Polkomtel, how much has been realised to date, and what is the remaining runway — broken out by cost line?"
  • Expected answer: Management claims synergies are on track; specific remaining quantum and timeline will be harder to extract
  • Good answer signature: Cites realised vs targeted synergies by category (network, headcount, procurement); acknowledges any slippage; gives timeline for remaining capture
  • Bad answer signature: "Integration is progressing well" without a specific synergy bridge; cannot distinguish one-time costs from structural saves
  • Follow-up probe: "What is your network-sharing arrangement with Play/Iliad on the legacy Polkomtel infrastructure — and are there any revenue-share clauses that cap your cost savings?"
  • Diagnostic note: If integration synergies are already fully captured, the forward margin improvement thesis depends entirely on revenue mix improvement — a much weaker foundation

Q3.3 — Content rights inflation

  • Question: "Polsat's linear TV and sports content rights are renewing in a period of rights inflation — what is your forward content cost commitment as a % of revenue for the next 3 years?"
  • Expected answer: Management cites content as a competitive differentiator; will resist disclosing exact forward commitments
  • Good answer signature: Provides directional content cost trajectory; distinguishes owned/produced content (lower marginal cost) from acquired rights; references Formula 1 / Polsat Sport rights renewal economics
  • Bad answer signature: "Content is our strength" without cost structure; cannot distinguish amortisation from cash commitment
  • Follow-up probe: "If you lost exclusive Formula 1 rights, what is your modelled impact on pay-TV subscriber retention?"
  • Diagnostic note: Sports rights are the most visible single-point content risk; Formula 1 exclusivity in Poland has been a retention anchor — losing it would accelerate cord-cutting, which is the highest-plausibility bear trigger in this theme

Q3.4 — Operating leverage

  • Question: "At what revenue growth rate does your fixed-cost base generate meaningful operating leverage — and what is the incremental EBITDA margin on the next PLN 500m of revenue?"
  • Expected answer: Management will claim operating leverage exists but may not be able to quantify the incremental margin
  • Good answer signature: Provides a clear incremental margin estimate; separates network (high fixed cost) from content (variable); references specific cost-efficiency programmes
  • Bad answer signature: Points to historical EBITDA margin as evidence of leverage without forward-looking decomposition
  • Follow-up probe: "Your staff costs have grown faster than revenue in recent years — what is the headcount plan for 2025–26 and what productivity metric do you manage to?"
  • Diagnostic note: If management cannot articulate the incremental margin, the operating leverage thesis is analyst-constructed, not management-guided — a standard false-friend pattern in telecom convergence plays

6. §4 — Theme 3: Capital Allocation + Balance Sheet

J-front verdict: This is the load-bearing uncertainty pillar — CPS has generated cash but leverage remains elevated and the holding-company structure creates opaque cash-extraction risk; these questions are designed to stress-test the deleveraging commitment

Q4.1 — Leverage target and path

  • Question: "Your net debt/EBITDA is approximately 4×. What is your explicit target, by when, and what is the quarterly FCF bridge that gets you there?"
  • Expected answer: Management cites a leverage target (likely 2.5–3.5× range); will be reluctant to give a specific date
  • Good answer signature: States a specific target AND a credible FCF bridge with explicit assumptions; acknowledges any covenant headroom constraint
  • Bad answer signature: "We are committed to deleveraging" without a specific target or timeline; cannot bridge FCF to debt reduction
  • Follow-up probe: "What are your nearest covenant thresholds and what is your current headroom on each?"
  • Diagnostic note: Covenant proximity is the single clearest financial stress signal; any answer that avoids quantifying headroom is a red flag

Q4.2 — Holding-company cash extraction

  • Question: "Polsat Cyfrowy is part of a broader Solorz holding structure — can you walk us through all cash flows out of the listed entity to the holding company over the last 3 years?"
  • Expected answer: Management will reference disclosed dividends and intercompany loan terms; likely to resist full transparency
  • Good answer signature: Proactively discloses all related-party transfers; explains the commercial rationale; references arm's-length review process
  • Bad answer signature: Cites "standard related-party disclosure in the annual report" and redirects; cannot quantify total holding-company cash extraction
  • Follow-up probe: "Are there any management fees, royalty payments, or intercompany service agreements that are not separately line-itemed in the consolidated accounts?"
  • Diagnostic note: ⚡ This is the single most-avoided question in CPS investor meetings based on public transcript review; the non-answer IS the answer — see §8

Q4.3 — M&A discipline

  • Question: "With leverage still elevated, how do you evaluate M&A opportunities — is there a hard leverage ceiling above which you will not transact?"
  • Expected answer: Management cites disciplined approach; will not commit to a hard ceiling
  • Good answer signature: States an explicit leverage ceiling for M&A; references a returns hurdle rate; names asset categories they will and will not consider
  • Bad answer signature: "We evaluate each opportunity on its merits" without a leverage constraint; references strategic fit as the primary screen
  • Follow-up probe: "Have you been approached about media consolidation targets in Poland or CEE in the last 12 months?"
  • Diagnostic note: M&A at elevated leverage is the fastest route to the bear case; a management team without a hard ceiling is a capital allocation risk

Q4.4 — Dividend policy credibility

  • Question: "Your dividend has been under pressure from leverage — what is the earnings coverage ratio at which you would reinstate or increase the dividend, and is that a board commitment or a management aspiration?"
  • Expected answer: Management references leverage as the gating item; will characterise dividend restoration as a future priority
  • Good answer signature: States a specific earnings coverage or leverage threshold for dividend reinstatement; references board-approved policy
  • Bad answer signature: "We aim to return value to shareholders when conditions allow" — vague, non-committal
  • Follow-up probe: "Has the board formally approved a dividend policy framework, or is this decided ad hoc each year?"
  • Diagnostic note: Absence of a formal policy creates uncertainty that disproportionately impacts the minority shareholder position — central to the "false friend" profile where the company is cash-generative but minority shareholders capture little of it

7. §5 — Theme 4: Strategy + Competitive Position

J-front verdict: The convergent bundle narrative is well-constructed but the key strategic uncertainty is whether CPS can sustain competitive position in mobile against P4/Play (Iliad-owned) without either material network capex escalation or spectrum disadvantage

Q5.1 — 5G spectrum and competitive positioning

  • Question: "Your 5G rollout timeline and spectrum position relative to Orange and T-Mobile Poland — where do you believe you'll be competitively disadvantaged in 5G coverage by end-2026?"
  • Expected answer: Management will assert competitive 5G roadmap; reluctant to name a specific disadvantage
  • Good answer signature: Honest about coverage gaps; quantifies the capex required to close them; discusses spectrum re-farming economics vs greenfield investment
  • Bad answer signature: "Our 5G rollout is on track" without coverage benchmarks vs peers; no discussion of spectrum position
  • Follow-up probe: "If the Polish regulator awards additional mid-band spectrum in 2025–26, what is your budget for participation and what is your modelled ARPU uplift from winning it?"
  • Diagnostic note: 5G coverage gaps are a medium-term churn vector for premium mobile customers; if management cannot quantify the gap, the market is likely mispricing this risk

Q5.2 — Streaming/OTT competitive response

  • Question: "Netflix and Disney+ penetration in Poland is accelerating — how specifically has this changed your pay-TV churn rate, and what is your strategic response over the next 24 months?"
  • Expected answer: Management cites bundling with CANAL+ or Polsat Box as the response; will characterise OTT as complementary not substitutional
  • Good answer signature: Provides churn-by-cohort data showing OTT impact; describes a specific content/bundle response with economics; acknowledges the structural threat honestly
  • Bad answer signature: "We are not in competition with Netflix" or "our content is differentiated" without data; no churn-by-cohort analysis
  • Follow-up probe: "What percentage of your current pay-TV base also subscribes to at least one SVOD service — and how does their churn rate compare to the non-SVOD base?"
  • Diagnostic note: Co-subscription rate is a leading indicator of substitution risk; high co-subscription rate with lower churn suggests complementarity; high co-subscription with rising churn = substitution accelerating

Q5.3 — Win condition in B2B/ICT

  • Question: "Your ICT segment is targeting enterprise and public sector — who are your top 3 competitors on each of the last 3 major contract wins, and what did you win on?"
  • Expected answer: Management names competitors (likely Comarch, Orange Business Services); will cite price, local knowledge, and integrated telecom offering as differentiators
  • Good answer signature: Specific competitor names; honest about whether wins were price-led or capability-led; notes any contracts where CPS was the incumbent vs challenger
  • Bad answer signature: "We won on the strength of our integrated offering" without naming competitors or win attributes
  • Follow-up probe: "What is your win rate on competitive rebids vs new-to-CPS contracts?"
  • Diagnostic note: If ICT wins are primarily price-led, the segment has low moat and the growth story is not durable — a false-friend risk within the most-hyped growth segment

8. §6 — Theme 5: Management Quality + Operator Track Record

J-front verdict: CEO Tobias Solorz (Zygmunt's son) has limited independent operating track record; CFO Ostap-Tomann is more credible on financial communication but public disclosures have been thin — these questions probe the operator beneath the presentation

Q6.1 — Sandbagging discipline

  • Question: "Looking at your last 4 annual guidance sets — in how many cases did you beat, meet, or miss your own EBITDA guidance, and what drove the misses?"
  • Expected answer: Management will point to beats; will attribute misses to exogenous factors (energy, FX, macro)
  • Good answer signature: Honest attribution of internal vs external drivers of misses; references a specific internal failure and what changed operationally
  • Bad answer signature: Cannot recall specific guidance misses; attributes all underperformance to external factors
  • Follow-up probe: "When you set guidance, what is your internal confidence interval — and do you present a conservative or central-case number publicly?"
  • Diagnostic note: A management team that cannot recall its own guidance track record has a credibility problem; one that honestly discusses internal failures has qualitatively higher trust calibration

Q6.2 — Incentive structure (Concept A probe)

  • Question: "What are the specific KPIs that drive CEO and CFO short-term and long-term compensation — and how has the weighting between revenue, EBITDA, and leverage changed in the last 3 years?"
  • Expected answer: Management will reference the remuneration report; will be reluctant to go beyond disclosed metrics
  • Good answer signature: Explains the weighting logic; notes whether leverage reduction is explicitly incentivised; confirms the LTI vesting cliff aligns with minority-shareholder interests
  • Bad answer signature: "Please refer to our remuneration report" without elaboration; cannot explain the logic of the weighting
  • Follow-up probe: "Is there any component of your LTI that is tied to total shareholder return rather than absolute earnings metrics?"
  • Diagnostic note (Concept A): If compensation is weighted to EBITDA without a leverage component, management is incentivised to grow EBITDA even at the cost of balance sheet health — directly adverse to the minority deleveraging thesis; if TSR is absent from LTI, management is not aligned with share price

Q6.3 — Crisis playbook

  • Question: "If your largest pay-TV content partner (e.g. Formula 1 rights holder, Canal+ parent) threatened to exit the partnership, what is your Plan B — have you modelled it?"
  • Expected answer: Management will characterise content partnerships as stable and strategic; will not have a detailed Plan B disclosed publicly
  • Good answer signature: Describes a specific contingency plan with a quantified subscriber impact range; references negotiating leverage they hold
  • Bad answer signature: "We have strong relationships with all content partners" — non-answer; no contingency modelling
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the contractual notice period on your largest content agreement?"
  • Diagnostic note: Absence of a rehearsed Plan B on the single-biggest content dependency is an operational risk flag; management quality is partly measured by contingency preparedness

Q6.4 — Hiring posture and talent quality

  • Question: "The ICT and digital segments require different talent than traditional broadcast/telecom — how have you restructured hiring and compensation to compete with tech firms for this talent?"
  • Expected answer: Management references training and career development; will not disclose competitive salary benchmarks
  • Good answer signature: Specific talent initiatives; acknowledges the compensation gap vs tech; describes retention metrics for digital talent
  • Bad answer signature: "We offer an attractive employer brand" — generic; no data on attrition in digital/ICT functions
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the voluntary attrition rate in your ICT division vs your legacy broadcast/telecom divisions?"
  • Diagnostic note: ICT segment growth is human-capital-dependent; high attrition in the growth segment is a leading indicator of project execution risk

9. §7 — Theme 6: Risk + Downside (Pre-Mortem Cross-Reference)

J-front verdict: The three highest-plausibility failure modes are (1) regulatory intervention on content/spectrum, (2) leverage covenant breach in a revenue slowdown, and (3) holding-company cash extraction accelerating — these questions stress-test each

Q7.1 — Regulatory exposure

  • Question: "What is your current regulatory risk register — specifically, which open regulatory proceedings could most materially affect your revenue or cost structure in the next 24 months?"
  • Expected answer: Management will name known proceedings; will characterise regulatory relationships as constructive
  • Good answer signature: Names specific proceedings with quantified P&L impact scenarios; distinguishes between proceedings where CPS has structural leverage vs ones where it is a price-taker
  • Bad answer signature: "We work constructively with UKE and KRRiT" — deflects without specifics; cannot quantify the impact range
  • Follow-up probe: "Has any regulatory body opened a formal investigation into your pricing practices or market dominance in the last 12 months?"
  • Diagnostic note: Regulatory proceedings are systematically under-disclosed in CPS public communications; a management team that can name the specific articles and quantify the risk is a qualitatively better information source than sell-side analysis on this topic

Q7.2 — Leverage covenant stress test

  • Question: "Bear case: your EBITDA declines 15% for two consecutive years — walk us through the covenant breach scenario and your available remedies"
  • Expected answer: Management will characterise a 15% EBITDA decline as highly unlikely; will reference covenant headroom without specifics
  • Good answer signature: Engages with the scenario; provides specific covenant thresholds; describes lender relationship and waiver/amendment precedent; identifies assets that could be monetised
  • Bad answer signature: "That scenario is not realistic" — refuses to engage; cannot name specific covenants or headroom
  • Follow-up probe: "Have you stress-tested your covenants with lenders in the last 6 months — and if so, what was the outcome?"
  • Diagnostic note: Management unwillingness to engage with a downside scenario is itself diagnostic — it suggests either denial or a covenant position tighter than disclosed; bears cross-reference this with Q19 pre-mortem "leverage trap" scenario

Q7.3 — Pricing fragility

  • Question: "If a competitor launched an aggressive convergent bundle at a 20% price discount to your equivalent product, what is your retention toolkit and what would it cost you in ARPU terms?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite content differentiation and bundle lock-in as the primary defences
  • Good answer signature: Describes a specific tiered-response toolkit; quantifies the ARPU cost of the defensive response; references historical competitive response data
  • Bad answer signature: "Our content and brand are differentiated enough to retain customers" without a quantified toolkit
  • Follow-up probe: "Has a competitor tried this in the last 3 years, and what happened to your net adds in the following two quarters?"
  • Diagnostic note: Price competition in Polish convergent telecom is a live risk (P4/Iliad strategy); a management team without a quantified retention toolkit is exposed to a faster-than-modelled churn event

Q7.4 — False-friend probe: convergence moat durability

  • Question: "Devil's advocate: the convergence bundle moat in Poland may already be fully priced into your customer base — what evidence do you have that cross-sell rates are still rising vs plateauing?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite cross-sell momentum; will resist characterising the moat as plateauing
  • Good answer signature: Provides specific cross-sell penetration rates by product pair with trend data; acknowledges where penetration is high and growth is decelerating
  • Bad answer signature: Cites overall bundled-customer growth without penetration rate decomposition; cannot distinguish new-to-bundle vs existing customers re-packaged
  • Follow-up probe: "What percentage of your customer base holds 3+ products — and what was that figure 2 years ago?"
  • Diagnostic note: If 3+ product penetration is high and rising slowly, the incremental bundling upside is largely extracted — directly challenges the bull-case narrative; this is the most important false-friend probe in the memo

Sceptical close — Q19 ACH gap check:

  • NOT covered by any question in this memo: currency/FX sensitivity on PLN/EUR content rights contracts; climate-related infrastructure risk (data centre energy transition costs); management succession risk if Zygmunt Solorz-Żak's health or holding-company restructuring forces a corporate event; Polish macroeconomic slowdown impact on B2B/ICT public-sector spend cycle — the analyst should consider adding probes on at least the succession and PLN/EUR content cost items if meeting time permits

10. §8 — Cross-Cutting Meta + Non-Obvious Questions (LOAD-BEARING)

J-front verdict: The questions management WON'T volunteer answers to are on holding-company alignment, the real leverage trajectory, and whether the ICT growth story is recurring revenue or a contract-cycle artefact — surface these explicitly

Non-obvious angle #1 — The Solorz discount probe ⚡

  • Question: "Institutional investors apply a meaningful holding-company discount to CPS — what is your own estimate of that discount, and what would you do to close it?"
  • Why unconventional: No public transcript shows this question asked directly to CPS management; the consensus interview avoids it because it feels confrontational
  • Expected answer: Management may acknowledge the discount exists but attribute it to market structure; unlikely to have a specific closure strategy
  • Good answer signature: Acknowledges the discount candidly; describes specific governance steps being taken to close it; references timeline
  • Bad answer signature: Denies the discount exists or attributes it entirely to leverage/macro
  • Diagnostic note (Concept A): Management's emotional response to this question is as diagnostic as the verbal answer — discomfort or evasion confirms that governance premium is NOT on their priority list

Non-obvious angle #2 — The metric that worries management most ⚡

  • Question: "If you had to name the single internal metric whose trend worries you most right now, what would it be — and what is its current direction?"
  • Why unconventional: No earnings call or investor day transcript for CPS contains this question; it forces the management team to reveal its own internal early-warning system
  • Expected answer: Management may deflect or name a benign metric (e.g. customer satisfaction)
  • Good answer signature: Names a specific, non-obvious metric (e.g. ICT contract pipeline win rate, standalone pay-TV monthly churn in the 45–60 demographic, or energy cost per terabyte)
  • Bad answer signature: Names a metric they're winning on, not losing on; or deflects to "all our metrics are healthy"
  • Diagnostic note: The WH Smith CEO archetype — a management team that can candidly name its own vulnerability has high self-awareness and communication quality (Concept A credibility signal); one that cannot is either overconfident or concealing

Non-obvious angle #3 — The capex triage question

  • Question: "If you had to cut total capex by 20% tomorrow, which projects survive and which are cancelled — and what does the triage reveal about your true strategic priorities?"
  • Why unconventional: Forces explicit ranking of strategic priorities; reveals whether the stated strategy and the funded strategy are identical
  • Expected answer: Management will resist the hypothetical; will likely reference all investments as strategically critical
  • Good answer signature: Engages with the triage; identifies discretionary vs non-discretionary capex clearly; reveals which segments are truly high-conviction vs optionality
  • Bad answer signature: "We wouldn't make that cut" — refuses to engage; cannot distinguish must-have from nice-to-have
  • Diagnostic note: If management identifies content capex as cuttable but network as non-cuttable, the strategic narrative around media differentiation may be weaker than communicated

Multiple-truths probe (Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 3):

  • Question: "The bull case says your convergence bundle creates a durable moat and pricing power; the bear case says you are over-levered and the pay-TV base is structurally declining — which element of the bear case do you think is most underappreciated by the bull-case investors who own your stock?"
  • Design intent: Explicitly invites the "and yet" answer — acknowledges the bull case AND asks management to steelman the bear case; management cannot dodge into either-or framing
  • Good answer signature: Identifies a specific bear-case element they believe is real but manageable (e.g. "pay-TV decline is real, but we think the broadband offset is undermodelled"); gives quantified reasoning
  • Bad answer signature: Restates the bull case entirely; refuses to engage with the bear-case premise
  • Diagnostic note: Management that can steelman the bear case has intellectual honesty (Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 3); management that deflects into pure bull-case narrative is either overconfident or optimising for the meeting, not for the relationship

Cross-roads question — Succession and control:

  • Question: "Zygmunt Solorz-Żak controls CPS through the holding structure — has the board formalised a succession framework, and how would a change in ultimate control affect the CPS group strategy?"
  • Design intent: Surfaces the governance risk that is most systematically avoided in public forums
  • Diagnostic note: A non-answer here (or deflection to "this is a shareholder matter") confirms that the governance risk is live and unmanaged from the minority perspective

Edge-detection probe — ICT margin reality check:

  • Question: "ICT segment appears to generate lower EBITDA margins than your core telecom/media — can you give us segment-level EBITDA margin for ICT and explain the trajectory to group-average margins?"
