EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (BLUF)

Cenergy operates a highly profitable two-divisional energy transition model, with Cables and Steel Pipes leveraging capacity expansions to capture high-margin European and US grid investments. The company is uniquely positioned as a fleet-light specialist manufacturer serving the unprecedented subsea transmission bottleneck, delivering sector-leading capital returns while maintaining exceptional pricing power through multiyear utility framework agreements and strict technical qualification barriers.

Business model and organizational structure

Cenergy operates as a Belgian holding company with full subsidiary control of Hellenic Cables (100%) and Corinth Pipeworks (100%), manufacturing power cables and high-specification steel pipes. The group delivers integrated turnkey submarine cable engineering, procurement, and installation through its subsidiary Fulgor, which operates one of only a few facilities globally producing ultra-high-voltage subsea cables. Cenergy entered 2026 with a robust order backlog of EUR 3.3 billion, securing strong medium-term execution visibility across European and global utilities. Geographic revenue concentration shifted dramatically, with Europe expanding from 42% to 66% in 2025 while Greece shrank to just 7%, reflecting aggressive international diversification. The group

prioritizes value over volume, executing higher-margin, highly complex energy projects while constructing a Baltimore land cables plant expected operational by 2027. 1

Industry structure and structural tailwinds

The global cables and wires market expanded to USD 350-370 billion, compounding at 6-7% CAGR, while the power cable segment reached USD 195.8 billion with 4.7-5.5% long-term growth projected to USD 296.7 billion by 2034. European grid investments must exceed EUR 580 billion by 2030, driving power transmission markets to expand at 10% annually to EUR 26 billion by 2028, with regional interconnectors representing a cumulative EUR 2.7 billion opportunity. The subsea transmission market requires installing approximately 44,200 km of high-voltage cables by 2036, with the North Sea representing 54.70% of capacity and global offshore wind expanding from 85 GW in 2024 to 240 GW by 2030. High-voltage cables suffer from severe demand-supply imbalances where demand vastly exceeds industry capacity, with offshore wind cables recording the longest lead times of all products globally. The subsea cables sector operates as a global oligopoly excluding Chinese players, with only five to six companies capable of manufacturing UHV cables, and competitive intensity declining as utilities black-list underperforming vendors, limiting bidding to four to six seasoned firms.

Value chain positioning and margin capture

Cenergy acts as a highly specialized downstream player focusing capital on high-value subsea armoring and steel pipe welding, while avoiding capital-intensive liquid steel production and sourcing HRC from global partners. Unlike integrated peers Prysmian and Nexans with dedicated installation fleets, Cenergy relies on selective installation partnerships, reducing vessel ownership drag and supporting sector-leading Return on Capital Employed. Specialized subsea cable manufacturing and pipe coating stages deliver exceptional profitability, with typical EBIT margins of 12-16% versus standard manufacturing at 5-8% and upstream commodity sourcing at just 2-4%. Technological barriers and strict utility certifications limit the certified supplier pool globally, with severe subsea bottlenecks reinforcing vendor pricing leverage and extending Cenergy's total order backlog to EUR 3.3

billion. Corinth Pipeworks completed its Concrete Weight Coating facility in 2025 and acquired the Hartlepool LSAW facility for GBP 10 million to double LSAW pipe capacity, offering customers integrated manufacturing and coating under one roof.

Customer concentration and contract protection

Cenergy maintains a highly diversified blue-chip utility customer base, with management enforcing a policy where no single customer exceeds 10% of total annual group sales to minimize concentration risk. Major European utility customers include E.ON, TenneT, Terna, Energinet, and IPTO, alongside offshore wind partnerships with Ocean Winds and marine operators like DEME, plus steel pipe clients including Woodside Energy, Snam, and OMV Petrom. Onshore land cable sales are secured through highly predictable multi-year framework contracts with distribution operators, including a multi-year agreement with Alliander N.V. leveraging doubled Thiva plant capacity. Hellenic Cables completely transfers copper and aluminum price risks to customers on customized projects via explicit contract pass-through clauses and centrally hedges residual time gaps via LME derivatives. Technical switching barriers create severe customer lock-in because certified product qualifications require years of testing and design compliance, forcing utilities to prioritize pre-qualified suppliers for major power grid tenders.

Supplier dynamics and raw material hedging

Raw materials represent approximately 76% of the cost of goods sold within Hellenic Cables' manufacturing operations, with copper cathodes serving as the main component and hot-rolled steel coils constituting primary inputs for Corinth Pipeworks. Cables projects completely transfer metal price risks to customers via contract pass-through clauses and back-to-back LME matching, while catalogue adjustments for onshore low-voltage cables exhibit brief timing lags relative to volatile metal movements. Corinth Pipeworks utilizes natural hedging because liquid financial derivatives do not exist for steel, signing hot-rolled steel procurement contracts simultaneously with customer orders via quasi-consortium bidding arrangements. Unlike peers, Cenergy lacks its own copper rod mill, creating long-term raw material sourcing risks, while Nexans maintains superior vertical integration via dedicated metallurgy plants and Prysmian's Encore Wire acquisition added proprietary copper rod mills lowering costs. Cenergy utilizes supplier finance letters of credit to extend payment due dates to 120-210 days, with outstanding supplier finance liabilities reaching EUR 331.67 million at year-end 2025. 26
