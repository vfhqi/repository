Section 3 – What the company does 15 findings

Section summary
  1. The business model occupies a highly consolidated, profit-favoured niche in the global energy transmission value chain with multi-year contract visibility
  2. Cenergy Holdings operates a highly integrated industrial model with exceptional visibility backed by a €3.38bn backlog across structurally growing energy markets

Divisional Operations and Revenue Sourcing

1.
Dominant cables segment: Premium high-voltage power interconnections are manufactured by Hellenic Cables, representing €1,464m or 71% of FY25 group revenues, and serving as the primary growth engine for the entire company
2.
Repositioned pipes segment: Advanced energy transition pipelines are produced by Corinth Pipeworks, representing €597m or 29% of FY25 revenues, and shifting from legacy oil projects to hydrogen-ready and carbon capture steel tubes
3.
Bespoke contract model: Highly predictable revenue streams are secured through multi-year bespoke engineering and construction contracts, which form 100% of the massive €3.38bn group order backlog as of late FY25
4.
Favourable value chain position: Direct tier-one relationships allow Cenergy to bypass intermediaries, securing approved supplier status with blue-chip global energy majors including BP, Shell, and Total, which creates immense customer switching costs
5.
Integrated coastal infrastructure: Low-cost maritime export advantages are unlocked by the Corinth plant's deep-water port, enabling minimum transit delays and lower freight rates for exporting heavy offshore subsea cable reels
6.
onshore and offshore mix: Diversified subsea cables backlog of €2.66bn in Q1 2026 is structurally split between 44% grid interconnections and 29% offshore wind, reducing single-market regulatory exposure

Moat Dynamics and Industry Structure

7.
Steep technological moats: Immense pre-qualification barriers for 320kV DC and 525kV extra-high-voltage cables protect Cenergy from low-cost Asian competitors who lack the long-term track record required by European national grids
8.
Oligopolistic cables industry: Consolidated global competitive landscape is dominated by only four major European players including NKT, Prysmian, Nexans, and Cenergy, which preserves strong pricing power and highly favorable project contract terms
9.
Strict regional protection: Stringent security requirements prevent non-US and non-European manufacturers from bidding on national power grids due to security concerns, effectively locking out Chinese suppliers from core utility tenders
10.
Asset-light maritime flexibility: Low-risk fleet coordination model relies on deep partnerships with multiple specialized maritime operators rather than owning capital-intensive cable-laying vessels, protecting Cenergy from high fixed shipping overheads
11.
Highly creditworthy customer base: Sovereign-backed national grid operators like IPTO in Greece and Alliander N.V. in the Netherlands dominate the Cables order book, virtually eliminating payment default risks
12.
Growing energy transition demand: Favourable European grid investments continue to expand, with the European Hydrogen Backbone and carbon capture initiatives requiring over 50,000 km of new and repurposed large-diameter steel pipelines by 2040
13.
side of the story: Raw material supply leakage represents a key vulnerability as tighter European copper scrap markets could disrupt regional smelter supply if OECD waste regulations fail to stop outflows to China

Section 5 – The possible investment case 12 findings

Section summary
  1. The forward investment case is highly compelling because Cenergy is doubling high-margin European capacity while establishing a protected US manufacturing footprint
  2. Earnings visibility remains outstanding as a €3.38bn backlog and 20% EPS CAGR back the medium-term EBITDA target of €370-400m

Primary Capacity and Market Drivers

1.
Completed Corinth subsea expansion: Doubled offshore manufacturing capacity at the Corinth plant was operational in January 2025, allowing Cenergy to execute on high-margin subsea cable projects at twice its historical rate
2.
Onshore capacity doubling: Growing land power backlog is supported by a €50m investment program at Thiva, set to complete by end of 2025, which adds new production lines for high-voltage land cables
3.
Strategic US greenfield entry: Premium land cables plant costing ~$200m in Baltimore, Maryland, is underway with operations starting in late 2027, allowing Cenergy to establish local supply in a highly protected market
4.
Substantial federal tax incentives: Alleviated US CapEx requirements are secured via a Qualifying Advanced Energy Project transferable tax credit of up to $58m under Section 48C, directly reducing US project risks
5.
Structural shift in steel pipes: Favourable cleaner energy projects representing over 40% of the Corinth Pipeworks orderbook are now tied to high-value carbon capture and hydrogen pipelines, boosting segment profitability

Case Plausibility and Success Dynamics

6.
Unprecedented sector-leading compounding: Explosive medium-term growth is projected with a 20% adjusted EBITDA and EPS CAGR over 2023-2027, representing the highest expansion rate in the European cables sector
7.
Visible financial ambitions: Highly credible medium-term targets include adjusted EBITDA of €380-420m and top-line annual growth of over 12%, which are strongly supported by five years of backlog coverage
8.
Independent segment drivers: Insulated investment case execution means the European onshore and offshore grid expansions can deliver the bull case independently of US Baltimore plant schedules
9.
Stable backlog foundation: Superb medium-term visibility is anchored by the €3.38bn group order book, ensuring high utilization and protective pricing clauses through the next 18 to 36 months
10.
Other side of the story: US pre-operating delays could result in strained capital efficiency in late 2027 if Baltimore land plot development faces local environmental or construction bottlenecks

Section 7 – Key risks and the other side 10 findings

Section summary
  1. The risk profile is structurally insulated by five-year backlogs and oligopolistic supply, although labor scarcity and vessel dependence require close monitoring
  2. Sovereign listing and parent concentration constraints may persist as watchpoints, though operational momentum and grid security barriers remain highly defensive

Operational Bottlenecks and Execution Risks

1.
Skilled blue-collar scarcity: Critical labor availability constraints represent the primary operational limit, as wage inflation normalizes but technical personnel remain scarce
2.
Raw material price volatility: Managed copper margin risk primarily affects low-voltage cables, though mitigated by passing price adjustments directly to customers through distribution price lists
3.
Marine fleet dependency: Fleet deployment coordination risks exist because Cenergy owns no cable-laying vessels and depends on third-party operators for offshore cable installations
4.
Greenfield execution hurdles: Potential US construction cost overruns represent a key risk at the Baltimore plant, where any local permitting or technical delays could inflate the $200m budget

Structural and Governance Exposures

5.
Dominant parent ownership: High shareholder concentration risk persists with parent Viohalco SA holding a 71.46% stake, which could restrict minority shareholder voting influence on major corporate decisions
6.
Deceptive short-term backlog signals: Volatile steel pipe orderbooks may create false negative backlog signals, masking the strong underlying transition to hydrogen
7.
Leading award indicator: Critical contract win tracking should monitor ongoing IPTO and 525kV tender results to verify the next multi-year phase of Cables backlog growth
8.
side of the story: Defensive asset-light fleet model protects Cenergy from prohibitive vessel maintenance and idle-time costs, allowing the company to maintain high capital efficiency compared to vessel-owning peers
