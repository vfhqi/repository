Section 3 – What the company does 13 findings

Section summary
  1. The company's niche engineering focus on fluid mechanics delivers high switching costs and robust pricing power through a highly profitable aftermarket business.

Divisional Structure and Value Chain

1.
Diverse segment mix: IMI operates two main platforms, Automation and Life Technology, which are further divided into five distinct sector business units. These comprise Process Automation and Industrial Automation under the Automation banner, and Climate Control, Transport, and Life Science & Fluid Control under Life Technology
2.
Process engineering dominance: The largest division is Process Automation, generating £1,006.00m of sales in FY25, representing an 11.04% increase compared to £906.00m in FY24. This division designs severe-service valves and actuators for critical power, liquefied natural gas, nuclear, and petrochemical plants
3.
Hydronic climate control: The Climate Control segment generated £410.00m of revenue in FY25, up 5.40% compared to £389.00m in FY24, delivering energy-efficient solutions for water-based building heating and cooling
4.
Transactional revenue streams: IMI's business model is split between original equipment construction projects and highly profitable, recurring aftermarket parts and upgrades. Original equipment orders are often lumpy, whereas the aftermarket represents c. 45.00% of total sales
5.
High aftermarket density: In Process Automation, aftermarket sales represent c. 60.00% of revenue, and in Climate Control they represent c. 75.00%, shielding margins during capital expenditure downcycles
6.
Favoured chain position: IMI sits in a profit-favoured position in the value chain by dealing directly with major global original equipment manufacturers and end-users, ensuring high customer intimacy

Competitive Moat and Industry Structure

7.
Substantial customer base: IMI serves over 35,000.00 customers globally, which prevents any significant customer concentration risks and enhances the predictability of cash flows
8.
High switching barriers: The primary competitive moat is high switching costs, as IMI's severe-service valves operate under extreme pressure where replacement with non-qualified parts risks catastrophic failure
9.
Consolidated peer group: The niche fluid engineering industry is highly consolidated, with IMI competing directly against premium players like Rotork, Spirax Group, Crane Co, and Flowserve
10.
Superior pricing power: Strong pricing power is demonstrated by high gross margins, enabling IMI to fully pass on inflationary raw material and logistics costs to end customers
11.
Digital competitive advantage: The company leverages proprietary digital systems like the Growth Hub, which actively tracks over 200,000.00 installed valves to proactively target high-margin aftermarket upgrade campaigns
12.
Other side of the story: Competitors argue that IMI has historically lagged behind in its electrical actuation and advanced digital portfolios compared to highly focused peers like Rotork. This technological gap could allow competitors to capture market share in high-growth electric factory automation and smart grid niches if IMI's R&D efforts fail to keep pace

Section 5 – The possible investment case 8 findings

Section summary
  1. The investment case is exceptionally strong, supported by non-discretionary energy transition demand and rapid growth in liquid cooling for data centres.

Primary Growth Engines

1.
Energy transition tailwinds: Over half of IMI's sales are directly supported by rising global energy demand and decarbonisation mandates. This has driven conventional power aftermarket order growth of 16.00% and new construction power order growth of 32.00% in Process Automation
2.
Data centre liquid cooling: The rapid expansion of artificial intelligence data centres represents a massive high-margin growth adjacency for the Climate Control segment. Order intake more than doubled to £18.00m in FY25 and is projected to expand by a further 50.00% in FY26
3.
Nuclear market opportunities: The rising structural focus on energy security has unlocked a robust nuclear project pipeline. This helped drive a record Group order book of £875.00m in FY25, up 2.00% from £857.00m in FY24
4.
Growth Hub commercialization: The company's unique test-and-learn innovation engine has successfully scaled custom client solutions. Growth Hub orders reached £206.00m in FY25, up 38.25% from £149.00m in FY24, demonstrating direct monetization of R&D

Portfolio Evolution and Profitability

5.
Defensive aftermarket expansion: Management can see a visible path to expand high-margin aftermarket revenues to 65.00% of Process Automation sales over the next five years, up from c. 60.00% today
6.
Independent growth drivers: The bull case does not require all divisions to fire simultaneously; for example, Process Automation and Climate Control are growing rapidly despite temporary cyclical declines in Industrial Automation and Transport
7.
Other side of the story: The primary mechanism of failure for this thesis is a severe contraction in global capital goods budgets, which would delay critical liquefied natural gas and nuclear infrastructure projects, causing new construction orders to fall sharply. Under this bear scenario, original equipment revenues would contract, and IMI's high fixed-cost manufacturing base would suffer material negative operating leverage
