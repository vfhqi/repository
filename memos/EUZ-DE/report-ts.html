Section 3 – What the company does 13 findings

Section summary
  1. The company commands a durable, high-barrier competitive advantage by controlling key supply bottlenecks of rare, medical-grade radioisotopes globally.

Core Segment Breakdown

1.
Dual segment structure: Operating business is managed via the Medical and Isotope Products segments, targeting distinct clinical, research, and industrial customer bases
2.
Highly profitable medical: The Medical segment manufactures radioactive ingredients for diagnostics and therapeutic oncology, CDMO services, and brachytherapy seeds
3.
Stable isotope products: Isotope Products manufactures metrological radiation sources, industrial measurement isotopes, and provides low-level radioactive waste recycling services
4.
Strong generator leadership: The company is the undisputed global market leader in Gallium-68 generators, serving as a critical diagnostic companion for PET scans
5.
Evolving therapeutic portfolio: EUZ is rapidly expanding its GMP-compliant production of next-generation therapeutic beta-emitter Lutetium-177 and targeted alpha-emitter Actinium-225
6.
Profitable service integration: CDMO and CMO activities allow the company to lock in long-term supply contracts by supporting pharmaceutical customers from preclinical phases

Customer Concentration and Moats

7.
Diversified customer base: The company supplies a broad range of Big Pharma players and early-stage biotech innovators, reducing reliance on any single drug candidate
8.
Significant customer win: A long-term Lutetium-177 supply contract with Eli Lilly, worth up to 100.00m EUR, validates the company's leading supply chain competence
9.
Decades long regulatory moat: High barriers to entry are maintained through stringent handling permits, specialized global facilities, and regulatory lock-in effects
10.
Extreme switching costs: Once a pharmaceutical product is approved using a specific radioisotope supplier, changing suppliers requires costly and lengthy regulatory re-approval
11.
Bespoke manufacturing partnerships: The company serves as a primary supply and manufacturing partner for innovative clinical developers, including a close partnership with Bicycle Therapeutics
12.
Other side of the story: Emerging cyclotron-based isotope production methods could eventually bypass generator-based pathways, disrupting the economics of the company's core GalliaPharm generator business

5.

Section 7 – Key risks and the other side 8 findings

Section summary
  1. The risk profile is moderate but highly complex, dominated by strict radioactive transport logistics and potential customer clinical trial failures.

Regulatory and Operational Vulnerabilities

1.
Extreme regulatory oversight: Handling and processing radioactive isotopes requires extensive licenses that can be withdrawn or become significantly more expensive to maintain
2.
Strict transport logistics: Global shipping of radioactive material relies on specialized hazardous goods carriers, making the business highly vulnerable to global logistics disruptions
3.
Long term waste liabilities: The decommissioning and remediation of radioactive waste sites represent a highly sensitive, long-term cost exposure that could increase under new regulations
4.
Severe reputational risk: Any decontamination or radiation safety event would trigger devastating reputational damage and lead to immediate facility closures
5.
Potential clinical delays: Short-term earnings phased delays remain possible if high-margin license deals or clinical trials from partners face execution delays
6.
False friend signals: A short-term surge in diagnostics equipment demand may mask a slower commercial therapeutic isotope uptake, skewing underlying growth visibility
7.
Other side of the story: The company's diversified isotope-agnostic portfolio minimizes structural binary risks, as a failure in one drug target is easily offset by other therapies
