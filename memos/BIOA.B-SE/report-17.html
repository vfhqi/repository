KEY FINDINGS - Dominant: BioArctic's revenue currency is overwhelmingly USD — Leqembi royalties from Eisai are denominated in USD, the sole commercial revenue stream today — creating near-total revenue-currency mismatch against a SEK reporting base

  • Concentrated: Revenue-currency exposure is the least diversified in any comparable Swedish biopharma structure: effectively one currency , one partner (Eisai), one product (Leqembi) — FX risk and counterparty risk are the same risk
  • Asymmetric: Cost base is almost entirely SEK (Swedish R&D staff, Uppsala operations, SEK-denominated grants) — natural hedge ratio is approximately zero; USD strengthens → SEK-reported earnings expand; USD weakens → SEK-reported earnings contract
  • Translation dominant: BioArctic is pre-material-cash-flow-positive on its own account; royalty income flows through Eisai's P&L and is then paid to BioArctic in USD; BioArctic translates USD receipts to SEK — this is fundamentally a translation-exposure business at this stage, not a transaction-exposure business
  • No hedging disclosed: Public filings contain no meaningful FX hedging programme; BioArctic discloses FX risk qualitatively but has not disclosed a systematic hedging mandate, hedge ratio, or derivative-instrument programme — consistent with a Swedish small-cap R&D company but is a structural gap vs any mature commercial-stage peer
  • Sensitivity is high: A 10% USD/SEK move translates to approximately 8-12% revenue impact and a larger percentage EBITDA/EPS impact at current royalty-income scale — this is among the highest per-unit FX sensitivities of any single-asset royalty-type Swedish biopharma
  • Macro direction: USD has weakened materially in H1 2025 (DXY down ~8-10% YTD vs 2024 average); if sustained, this creates a direct near-term headwind to SEK-translated royalty income — not reflected in most consensus models
  • Hedge break point: Not applicable in the traditional sense — there is no hedging programme to break — the company is fully exposed to spot and forward USD/SEK at all times
  • Tail risk: Leqembi commercial uptake is USD-denominated; if Leqembi achieves blockbuster trajectory, BioArctic's earnings become a leveraged USD long — positive in USD-strength environments, deeply unfavourable in sustained USD weakness

§4 — Transaction Exposure

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is structurally low for BioArctic given the royalty-income model — cash flows are USD-denominated receipts from a single counterparty (Eisai); mismatch with SEK obligations is real but modest in absolute terms at current scale

Transaction exposure definition for BioArctic:
- Primary transaction exposure: USD royalty receipts received from Eisai must be converted to SEK for Swedish operational expenditure, dividend/reserve purposes, and financial reporting cash
- Nature: BioArctic receives USD cash from Eisai (typically quarterly); SEK obligations (payroll, facilities, taxes) are paid in SEK; conversion happens at spot or near-spot at the time of receipt

Cash-flow currency mismatch:
- USD inflows: Leqembi royalties (quarterly) + milestone payments (episodic) — all in USD
- SEK outflows: R&D payroll (~70-80% of opex), Uppsala facility, Swedish taxes, G&A — all in SEK
- EUR outflows: EU CRO costs, EU regulatory fees — modest; paid from grant income or converted from USD receipts
- Net transaction exposure: Net long USD — USD inflows exceed USD outflows materially; company must sell USD to fund SEK operations continuously

Transaction vs translation magnitude:
- Translation: The larger conceptual exposure — BioArctic translates its full USD revenue base for P&L reporting
- Transaction: More limited in economic impact — only the USD receipts actually converted to SEK create a transaction cash-flow impact; but since virtually all receipts are USD and virtually all payments are SEK, transaction and translation impacts move in the same direction and are approximately proportional

Pricing pass-through:
- Can BioArctic pass through FX moves to Eisai? No — the royalty rate is fixed by contract (percentage of net sales); BioArctic has zero ability to renegotiate Eisai's royalty payments based on USD/SEK moves
- USD-denominated royalty rate: The royalty itself is a % of Leqembi net sales — so USD volume growth is the organic lever; FX is entirely exogenous and unmanageable at the contract level
- Pricing pass-through score: Zero — BioArctic is a price-taker on its primary revenue stream with no FX-pass-through mechanism

Cost pass-through ability:
- SEK cost-base: Almost entirely fixed in SEK terms (salary agreements, facility leases, grant budgets); no meaningful USD cost-pass-through
- Effective margin FX sensitivity: Every 1% USD/SEK move → approximately 1% revenue impact → approximately 1.2-1.5% EBITDA impact (because costs are sticky in SEK and don't move proportionally)

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on transaction exposure is that the apparent simplicity of the "receive USD, spend SEK" model understates the exposure in a scenario where USD weakens sharply and simultaneously Leqembi's commercial ramp requires increased USD-denominated clinical-support or business-development expenditure (e.g. US medical affairs support, US partnership costs) — at that point, BioArctic faces a double squeeze: USD receipts worth fewer SEK, while USD costs have risen in absolute terms; the "no transaction exposure" narrative depends on the cost base staying SEK-only, which becomes less true as BioArctic expands its commercial and clinical footprint

§6 — Quantified Sensitivity Analysis

J-front verdict: USD/SEK sensitivity is extremely high — a ±10% USD/SEK move drives approximately ±8-12% revenue and ±10-16% EBITDA impact; no hedging mitigates this; EPS trajectory is materially USD/SEK-dependent

Baseline assumptions for sensitivity modelling:

  • FY2025E USD royalty income: ~USD 30-50m (Leqembi early commercial ramp; approximate estimate from Eisai disclosure and analyst consensus on Leqembi penetration)
  • Baseline USD/SEK rate: 10.3 (approximate mid-2025 rate; down from FY2024 average ~10.6)
  • SEK-denominated opex: ~SEK 350-450m (R&D + G&A; approximate from FY2024 cost base)
  • Revenue translation: USD royalties × USD/SEK spot at receipt = SEK revenue
  • EBITDA: SEK revenue minus SEK opex; because opex is fixed in SEK, EBITDA sensitivity = revenue sensitivity in absolute SEK terms (not percentage terms, which amplifies % EBITDA impact vs % revenue impact)

Translation vs transaction decomposition:

  • Translation effect: The mechanical SEK-reporting impact of a USD/SEK rate change applied to the full USD royalty income base — 100% of revenue is translation-exposed; impact is symmetric, proportional, and reverses if exchange rate reverts
  • Transaction effect: The economic cash-flow impact of converting USD receipts to SEK for operational use — directionally identical to translation but timing-dependent on when receipts are converted; transaction effect is the economic reality; translation is the accounting presentation; for BioArctic (no hedging, immediate conversion), the two are approximately equal

Sector-general hedging principles applied to BioArctic:

1. Hedge duration matters more than hedge ratio:
- BioArctic: Duration = zero (no programme); this is the worst possible score on this dimension

2. Counter-cyclical vs pro-cyclical:
- BioArctic: Not applicable (no programme); the implicit posture is fully pro-cyclical (USD weakness hits immediately)
- Sector analogy: When Swedish biotech companies eventually establish FX programmes, they typically do so after an adverse FX event (pro-cyclical) rather than proactively (counter-cyclical); BioArctic's current situation — entering a period of USD weakness with no hedging in place — exemplifies the pro-cyclical failure mode

3. Instrument mix:
- BioArctic: No instruments; zero on this dimension
- Sector benchmark: Mature royalty-model companies (e.g. Royalty Pharma, BioPharma Credit) typically use forwards for known cash flows; BioArctic has sufficient royalty-receipt predictability (quarterly from Eisai) to use simple forwards — the absence of even basic forwards is a governance gap

- BioArctic: IFRS 7 notes contain no derivative disclosures — disclosed policy (qualitative mention of FX risk) matches realised hedging (zero); at least internally consistent, but consistently unhedged

- BioArctic: Natural hedge ratio ≈ 0% (all revenue USD, all cost SEK); this is the structural worst case; no operating-side offset
