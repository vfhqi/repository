§4 — Accounting + Governance Red Flags

J-front verdict: Accounting quality is mixed rather than clean; the most significant concerns are warranty provision adequacy, capitalisation policy for development costs, and non-GAAP adjustments that exceed European industrial norms [#J]

  • Revenue recognition: Vestas uses percentage-of-completion accounting for large turbine installation contracts; POC is inherently management-judgement-heavy and creates revenue recognition timing latitude [#A]
  • Bear concern: In a multi-year contract environment with supply chain disruptions, POC gives management ability to smooth revenue; bears note that revenue recognition controversies at Siemens Gamesa were also POC-related
  • Fact vs opinion: POC is IFRS-compliant and standard for the sector; it is not per se a red flag, but it creates a vector for scrutiny
  • Non-GAAP gap: Vestas reports "adjusted EBIT" that excludes restructuring charges, impairments, and project closure costs; the gap between reported IFRS EBIT and adjusted EBIT has been material in loss years [#A]
  • Quantification: In FY2022, the non-GAAP gap was approximately DKK 1-2bn on a reported loss base; European industrial peer median non-GAAP gap is approximately 5-10% of adjusted earnings; Vestas's gap in peak-restructuring years exceeded 20%
  • ❌ Non-GAAP gap: materially above European industrial peer median in restructuring years; not yet normalised
  • Warranty provision adequacy: Warranty provisions are the single most opaque line item on Vestas's balance sheet; the company services over 50,000 wind turbines globally and blade failure/generator failure rates are not publicly disclosed with granularity [#J]
  • Bear concern: Cumulative warranty provision balance has grown from approximately DKK 5-7bn (2019) to DKK 8-10bn+ (2023/2024); bears argue this growth is insufficient given fleet expansion and ageing
  • Sub-point: Third-party blade inspection data from operators suggests failure rates in post-2018 vintages may be elevated; Vestas has not issued a comprehensive blade defect disclosure comparable to SGRE's 2022 announcement
  • ❌ Warranty provisions: provision growth rate may be understating true liability — not refuted by company disclosure; peer experience validates this as a non-trivial risk
  • Capitalisation aggressive: Vestas capitalises development costs for next-generation turbine platforms (V236, offshore variants); total capitalised development costs on balance sheet are non-trivial relative to peers [#A]
  • Bear concern: If new platform adoption is slower than expected, capitalised costs face impairment risk; bears cite the V164 offshore platform delays as precedent
  • Peer anchor: GE Vernova and Siemens Energy (onshore/offshore wind segments) also capitalise platform R&D; Vestas's capitalisation policy is not an outlier in approach, but the quantum relative to EBITDA is elevated
  • Adjustments increasing: Restructuring and one-off items have appeared in every annual report from FY2021-FY2024; when "one-offs" recur for four consecutive years, bears argue they are not one-offs but structural operating costs [#A]
  • Signpost note: This is the "adjusted earnings quality" concern — if Vestas's true normalised EBIT is materially lower than adjusted EBIT, valuation multiples are correspondingly higher than they appear
  • Related-party: No material related-party transaction concerns identified; Vestas has a controlling-shareholder-free structure with a dispersed institutional register; this is a clean area [#A]
  • Inventory aging: Work-in-progress inventory ballooned in 2022-2023 as installation delays accumulated; bears flagged this as a potential write-down risk; subsequent delivery normalisation has reduced (but not eliminated) the concern [#A]
  • Quantification: WIP inventory peaked at approximately DKK 15-18bn in mid-2022; has since reduced; but bears note that delivery delays create execution risk on revenue recognition timing
  • Receivables aging: Days Sales Outstanding has been elevated relative to European industrial peers, running 60-80 days vs a peer median of approximately 45-60 days; suggests either customer concentration pressure or customer payment friction [#A]
  • ❌ Receivables aging: DSO above peer median; working capital pressure is a documented FCF headwind
  • Payables stretching: Vestas has extended Days Payables Outstanding as a working capital management tactic; while common, it signals cash pressure if done aggressively; bears cite DPO extending from ~60 to ~80+ days L3Y [#A]
  • Mgmt comp structure: CEO and CFO compensation is heavily weighted to long-term incentive plans tied to TSR and EBIT margin targets; bears note that EBIT margin targets were repeatedly missed in 2021-2024 without corresponding comp clawback or reduction [#A]
  • Bear concern: Management was compensated adequately during the worst performance period in Vestas's modern history; misalignment signal
  • Board independence: Board composition includes industry veterans; no material independence concerns identified; Vestas has separated Chair and CEO roles [#A]
  • Auditor change: No recent auditor change; PricewaterhouseCoopers has audited VWS-DK consistently; no going-concern or material weakness flags in recent audit opinions [#A]
  • Pending lawsuit: Multiple warranty and contract dispute litigations are disclosed in annual report risk factors; no single lawsuit is material enough to constitute an existential risk, but cumulative legal exposure is non-trivial given fleet size [#A]
  • Specific note: Offshore wind project disputes (foundations, cabling, third-party contractor claims) are disclosed but not quantified granularly; bears flag this as contingent liability opacity
  • NWC liability: Net working capital has been a persistent FCF drag; in 2022/2023, NWC consumed DKK 2-4bn of operating cash flow annually; this is the mechanism linking EBIT recovery to FCF non-conversion [#J]

Bull rebuttal (per-section sceptical bullet): Many of the accounting concerns are sector-endemic — POC revenue recognition, capitalised platform R&D, warranty provisions — and apply equally to GE Vernova and Nordex; Vestas's auditor (PwC) has issued clean opinions consistently; the non-GAAP gap is now narrowing as restructuring charges roll off; the governance structure is actually stronger than most European industrials with a genuinely independent board

§6 — Counter-Arguments to Bull Case

J-front verdict: The bear rebuttals to the main bull thesis points are substantive and partially supported by historical evidence; the single strongest rebuttal is the margin execution credibility gap, which five years of evidence supports [#J]

  • Bull thesis #1: Management is fixing the company; EBIT margins will recover to 8-10% by FY2026 [#J]
  • Bear rebuttal #1: Vestas has guided to and missed margin targets in every year from FY2021 to FY2023; the current guidance of 4-6% for FY2024 is itself a downgrade from prior targets
  • Evidence supporting rebuttal: EBIT margin trajectory: FY2021 actual -1.8%, FY2022 actual -3.2%, FY2023 actual ~2%, FY2024 guidance 4-6%; the slope is improving but the absolute level is still far below pre-crisis 8-10% norms
  • Competitive evidence: GE Vernova's onshore wind segment margins have also not recovered to pre-2021 levels despite similar narrative; structural not cyclical

  • Track record evidence: Every investor day from 2021-2023 included margin targets that were subsequently cut; management's credibility on margin guidance is severely impaired

  • Bull thesis #2: The order backlog (DKK 230bn+) provides revenue visibility and repricing opportunity as older low-margin contracts roll off [#J]

  • Bear rebuttal #2: The backlog contains contracts signed at 2022-2023 input cost levels with customers who negotiated hard during the supply crunch; margin embedded in newer backlog may be no better than older backlog
  • Evidence supporting rebuttal: Average selling price per MW is a metric Vestas does not disclose granularly; bears cite evidence that European auction-clearing prices for onshore wind (Contracts for Difference) have compressed in Germany, UK, and France since 2023

  • Macro evidence: UK CfD round clearing at lower-than-expected prices in 2023 auction; German EEG tender clearing prices stagnant; customer negotiating power remains strong

  • Bull thesis #3: Energy transition tailwind provides secular demand growth; Vestas is the structurally advantaged European OEM [#J]

  • Bear rebuttal #3: Demand growth does not automatically translate to OEM margin recovery if the sector is supply-surplus and pricing power is absent
  • Competitive evidence: Chinese OEM capacity is expanding dramatically; CPIA data shows global turbine manufacturing capacity growing faster than installation demand through 2025-2026; a capacity glut scenario is bearish for all OEM margins regardless of secular demand

  • Track record evidence: Solar PV OEMs experienced secular demand growth for 20 years while manufacturing margins collapsed due to Chinese competition; wind OEMs face an analogous risk

  • Bull thesis #4: Service and aftermarket revenues provide high-margin, recurring income that stabilises financials [#J]

  • Bear rebuttal #4: Service margins are structurally under pressure as turbine warranties expire and customers negotiate hard on O&M contracts; service segment EBIT margins have historically been 12-18% but are not guaranteed
  • Evidence supporting rebuttal: Vestas's service revenue growth is partly organic (fleet expansion) and partly ASP-driven; as competition in service from third-party O&M providers (Bilfinger, Ramboll, independent operators) intensifies, pricing power erodes

  • Bear elaboration: Service is the only reliably profitable segment; if service margin compresses, the entire investment case is invalidated — there is no floor

  • Bull thesis #5: FCF will inflect positively in FY2025-FY2026 as NWC normalises and capex moderates [#J]

  • Bear rebuttal #5: FCF inflection has been "one year away" since FY2022; warranty cash outflows, NWC seasonality, and platform investment capex create structural headwinds that keep FCF negative or marginal
  • Evidence supporting rebuttal: Vestas generated negative FCF in three consecutive years (FY2021, FY2022, FY2023); the H1/H2 delivery weighting means FCF is always back-half dependent, creating guidance risk

  • JP Morgan angle: JP Morgan (see §3) has specifically modelled warranty cash outflow peaking 2024-2026; this is a documented, quantified headwind, not speculation

  • Bull thesis #6: Vestas trades at a discount to the green energy sector premium; re-rating is available if execution improves [#J]

  • Bear rebuttal #6: The "green energy premium" re-rating thesis was the bull case in 2020-2021 and did not prevent a 70% share price peak-to-trough decline; re-rating requires execution proof, not narrative
  • Competitive evidence: Orsted, another Danish green energy champion, also suffered a severe de-rating (30-40% in 2023) as offshore wind economics deteriorated; sector premium is not permanent

Bull rebuttal (per-section sceptical bullet): The bear rebuttals are historically accurate but backward-looking — the bull case specifically rests on change, not continuity; the weakest rebuttal is #3 (Chinese competition in European onshore) because European certification requirements, service infrastructure, and relationship capital still provide Vestas with genuine local competitive advantages that Chinese OEMs cannot replicate quickly

§7 — Historical Pattern (has the stock been targeted before?)

J-front verdict: Vestas has experienced multiple severe drawdown cycles over its history; each was driven by margin pressure, guidance cuts, and cash flow concerns — a pattern that maps directly onto the current bear thesis vectors [#J]

  • Prior attack — FY2012-2013 restructuring crisis: Vestas entered a near-existential crisis in 2012-2013 when liquidity concerns, covenant breaches, and profit warnings drove a stock decline of approximately 85% from peak [#A]
  • Attack date: Crisis crystallised Q4 2012; stock hit lows in January 2013 (approximately DKK 20-25)
  • Stock reaction post-attack: Stock declined from DKK 150+ to DKK 20-25 over 18 months; near-bankruptcy territory
  • Recovery: New management (Anders Runevad CEO from 2013), cost restructuring, and a wind market recovery drove the stock from DKK 25 to DKK 250 by 2017 — a 10x recovery

  • Bear import: The 2012-2013 cycle proves Vestas can nearly fail; the recovery was driven by management change and market tailwind, not an inherently resilient business model

  • Prior attack — FY2021-2022 margin collapse: The most recent equivalent crisis: raw material cost inflation + fixed-price contracts + execution challenges drove EBIT from ~DKK 4bn (FY2020) to -DKK 2bn (FY2022) [#A]

  • Attack date: Profit warning cycle began H2 2021; series of downgrades through FY2022
  • Stock reaction: VWS-DK declined approximately 60-70% from DKK 200+ to DKK 65-70 range by mid-2022
  • Mgmt response pattern: Management issued repeated "this time the floor is in" communications that proved premature; credibility damage is cumulative

  • Recovery (partial): Stock recovered to DKK 130-170 range by late 2023/2024 on margin improvement narrative; has not recaptured prior highs

  • Activist campaign — Cevian / Elliott adjacents: No confirmed Vestas-specific activist campaign identified; however, the company has been discussed in activist-adjacent investor circles given the gap between asset quality and operational execution [#A]

  • Note: Given the dispersed shareholder register, activists would face limited influence levers; Vestas's Danish cultural context is also less receptive to aggressive shareholder activism vs US targets

  • Major SS downgrade — Q3 2021: Multiple simultaneous sell-side downgrades accompanied the first major profit warning in H2 2021; Deutsche Bank, Goldman Sachs, and others moved to Hold/Neutral from Buy [#A]

  • Pattern: The "anticipatory downgrade" pattern (SS downgrades anticipating further cuts) has been characteristic of Vestas's bear cycles; if the current consensus is rebuilding on fragile foundations, the downgrade cascade risk is real

  • Mgmt response pattern: Vestas's historical response to bearish attacks has been to issue revised guidance with "confidence in recovery" framing, followed by further disappointments; this pattern (2021, 2022, 2023) means management communication has low credibility value [#A]

  • Exception: The 2013 turnaround under Runevad was a genuine operational improvement, not just communication management; the bear case cannot dismiss the possibility of another genuine turnaround

  • Prior recovery pace: Each Vestas recovery has required 3-5 years from trough to prior peak on an earnings basis; the current recovery cycle began in earnest in FY2023; bears argue that even optimistic execution implies 2-3 years before the stock can re-rate to prior highs [#A]

Bull rebuttal (per-section sceptical bullet): The historical pattern also includes the 2013-2017 recovery (10x from trough) and the 2019-2020 run; Vestas has demonstrated genuine operational improvement capacity when management quality is adequate; the current management team (CEO Henrik Andersen) has more experience navigating the post-2021 environment than the pre-2013 team; structurally, the RE policy environment in Europe is more supportive in 2024-2026 than at any prior cycle trough

  • Fact-based allegation — non-GAAP adjustments material: Substantiated for loss years; less so now; restructuring charges and one-offs were genuinely material in FY2021-FY2023; the question is whether they will recur [#A]
