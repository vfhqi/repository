3. PART A — FAILURE NARRATIVES

§2 — Failure Narrative 1: Single-Customer Royalty Concentration Collapse

Specific failure narrative: Nanoco's near-entirety of current revenue derives from a single licensing relationship (Samsung settlement-derived royalties or equivalent successor arrangement). If Samsung — or whichever single counterparty dominates the royalty stack — renegotiates terms, terminates, or simply pivots its display technology roadmap away from cadmium-free QD, Nanoco's revenue drops by ≥80% in a single quarter. Transmission mechanism: customer pivot → royalty stream zeroed → operating losses resume at full cash-burn rate → covenant breach or distressed equity raise within 12 months.

Early warning signals:
- Samsung Display's annual report references to QD material sourcing or alternative display technology investment increasing
- Any public statement by Samsung on MicroLED or perovskite-based display commercialisation timeline acceleration
- Nanoco management commentary becoming evasive on "royalty visibility" or shifting from absolute royalty figures to percentage growth language
- Absence of new licensing deals announced 12+ months post-settlement — signals customer replication has stalled
- Nanoco cash position declining faster than operating cost rate implies — suggests royalty receipts are below guidance

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — precedent base rate for single-customer revenue concentration failure in IP-licensing pure-plays is well-documented; Acacia Communications (2019 Huawei concentration), InterDigital chronic single-counterparty renegotiation cycles, and Tessera Technologies' repeated royalty cliff events all demonstrate this failure mode activates with high frequency in the sector

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: Samsung has full contractual leverage at renegotiation date; has own materials R&D capability
- Motive: cost reduction pressure on display segment; technology migration to alternative display architectures reduces QD dependency
- Opportunity: any contract renewal event or product-generation transition creates a natural renegotiation window
- All three legs present — full activation risk

Disconfirming evidence: A signed multi-year, fixed-royalty contract with Samsung that has no volume-tiering or technology-substitution exit clauses; public disclosure of ≥2 additional licensing counterparties generating material revenue; Samsung's display roadmap explicitly committing to cadmium-free QD for 5+ product generations

Monitoring checklist:
- Quarterly royalty revenue absolute value vs. prior period (not just YoY growth)
- Samsung Display Technology segment capex allocation in Samsung Electronics annual report
- Number of distinct licensing counterparties disclosed by Nanoco
- Nanoco cash position trajectory vs. operating cost run-rate
- Any Samsung IP filings referencing alternative QD materials or perovskite display materials

Interaction with other failure modes: Directly correlated with FM3 (cash burn governance) and FM6 (technology substitution) — if Samsung pivots due to technology substitution, FM1 and FM6 co-activate simultaneously, compounding severity

Sceptical bullet: The disconfirming evidence — a long-dated fixed royalty contract with no substitution exit — has not been publicly disclosed; absence of that disclosure is itself a mild confirming signal for this failure mode

§3 — Failure Narrative 2: Royalty Rate Compression on Re-Licensing

Specific failure narrative: Even if the existing Samsung relationship is stable, Nanoco's expansion thesis depends on signing new licensing deals with additional display/LED manufacturers at royalty rates comparable to the Samsung settlement-implied rate. New counterparties — LG, BOE, Sharp/Foxconn — negotiate from a position of awareness of the Samsung precedent and from a technology landscape where cadmium-free QD is no longer novel. Transmission mechanism: new licensee signs at materially lower royalty rate → revenue per-deal economics disappoint → consensus EPS forecast requires ≥3× more deals to achieve the same absolute revenue — deal cadence assumptions become unrealistic.

Early warning signals:
- First new licensing deal announced at a royalty rate/structure that implies materially lower per-unit economics than the Samsung deal
- Management commentary on "tailored licensing structures" or "flexible royalty models" — language that historically signals rate compression
- Time-to-close on new licensing negotiations extending beyond 18 months from first public disclosure of pipeline
- New licensee is a Tier-2 manufacturer (regional Chinese panel maker) rather than a Tier-1 — implies Nanoco accepting lower-quality counterparty at lower rates
- Sell-side consensus revenue forecasts revised down for Year 2+ post first new deal close

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — royalty rate compression on second-deal licensing is well-documented in semiconductor IP (ARM's historical licensing rate dynamics post-initial anchor deals; Rambus's repeated rate compression battles); however, cadmium-free QD still faces limited alternative supply, which partially sustains Nanoco's negotiating leverage

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: any Tier-1 display manufacturer has legal resources to negotiate aggressively and awareness of Samsung deal economics
- Motive: cost minimisation on materials licensing is standard procurement behaviour
- Opportunity: each new licensing negotiation is a discrete opportunity to renegotiate the effective rate floor
- All three legs present — full activation risk, modestly tempered by QD supply alternatives being limited today

Disconfirming evidence: Second licensing deal closes at royalty rate within 20% of Samsung-implied rate; Nanoco discloses a "standard licensing rate card" rather than bespoke deal structures; independent expert commentary confirms cadmium-free QD supply alternatives remain commercially unviable within 24 months

Monitoring checklist:
- Revenue per licensing counterparty disclosed in annual report
- Management language in earnings calls on "licensing pipeline" vs "signed agreements"
- Time elapsed from pipeline announcement to deal close on each new negotiation
- Any public IP challenges to Nanoco's core QD patent portfolio
- Competitor patent filings in cadmium-free QD synthesis space

Interaction with other failure modes: Correlated with FM1 (concentration) — lower rates on new deals make FM1 (Samsung dependence) proportionally more dangerous, not less, as diversification is less economically powerful than assumed; also linked to FM8 (M&A integration failure is N/A here, but FM7 management credibility)

Sceptical bullet: The disconfirming evidence — a standard rate card — is specifically NOT how Nanoco has disclosed its licensing approach historically; bespoke deal framing is itself a warning

Specific failure narrative: Nanoco operates in a post-litigation phase with a fundamentally different cash profile than a mature royalty business — R&D must continue to defend the patent estate and develop next-generation materials, while commercial headcount scales to support new licensing. If royalty receipts are lumpy (milestone-based, not quarterly) and operating costs are fixed, the company burns through cash reserves during deal-gap periods. Transmission mechanism: multi-quarter deal gap → cash below covenant threshold or board-directed equity raise → dilution event → per-share EPS trajectory collapses regardless of aggregate royalty economics.

Early warning signals:
- Cash position falls below £10m with no new deal signed — signals runway pressure
- Any RNS referencing "strategic review of financing options" — historically precedes equity raise in UK small-cap IP businesses
- R&D headcount reductions — signals Nanoco cutting investment to preserve cash, which accelerates IP obsolescence risk
- Auditor commentary on going concern qualification risk (even as a "remote" mention)
- Capex guidance being reduced in consecutive periods — operational contraction signal

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — post-litigation cash profile is inherently binary (lumpy receipts, fixed costs); analogues include Imagination Technologies (2017 Apple withdrawal triggered rapid cash deterioration) and InterDigital's periodic covenant stress during prolonged licensing renegotiations

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: operating cost base is real and largely fixed in short term; deal receipts are variable and counterparty-controlled
- Motive: not a "motive" failure — this is a structural governance/timing risk
- Opportunity: any deal-gap period creates the runway risk window
- Two-of-three (means + opportunity; motive N/A for structural risk) — downgrade to watch-only; plausibility remains real but is contingent on a specific timing gap

Disconfirming evidence: Nanoco holds ≥18 months of operating costs in unencumbered cash; royalty receipts are structured as quarterly fixed minimums (not milestone-contingent); new deal close rates are sufficiently rapid to prevent multi-quarter revenue gaps

Monitoring checklist:

- Structure of royalty payment schedule (fixed quarterly vs. milestone-based) — from RNS disclosures
- R&D headcount trend (absolute number in annual report)
- Any debt facility announcements
- Auditor's report language on going concern

Interaction with other failure modes: Directly amplified by FM1 (if Samsung royalty stream gaps) and FM5 (if management credibility triggers investor concern and blocks equity access); compound risk is non-linear

Sceptical bullet: Nanoco's post-Samsung settlement cash position was materially improved — but the adequacy of that position relative to the commercial build-out required is not publicly quantified with sufficient granularity to dismiss this scenario

§5 — Failure Narrative 4: Technology Substitution — Perovskite / MicroLED Displacement

Specific failure narrative: Cadmium-free quantum dots face a credible technology substitution threat from two vectors: (a) perovskite-based light emitters, which offer comparable colour gamut without the QD supply-chain complexity, and (b) MicroLED displays, which eliminate the need for a colour-conversion layer altogether. If either technology reaches commercial manufacturing yield thresholds within 36 months, the addressable application base for Nanoco's IP shrinks materially before the licensing portfolio has been fully monetised. Transmission mechanism: perovskite/MicroLED commercial viability confirmed → licensees accelerate technology migration → renewal rates on existing licences fall → new licensing pipeline collapses → long-term EPS terminal value destroyed.

Early warning signals:
- Apple, Samsung, or LG public statements confirming MicroLED commercial production timelines for ≥50" displays
- Perovskite display panel demonstrations at CES/Display Week achieving >90% of current QD colour gamut benchmarks
- Any major Tier-1 display manufacturer exiting QD materials procurement agreements
- University/research publications reporting perovskite EL lifetime >50,000 hours (the commercial viability threshold)
- Nanoco management reducing forward R&D pipeline guidance on next-generation QD applications

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility with 24-36 month materialisation lag — MicroLED has been "24 months away" commercially for five years and faces yield/cost barriers; perovskite faces cadmium contamination and lifetime challenges; however, base rate for technology substitution in display materials over a 5-7 year horizon is meaningful (plasma → LCD → OLED transitions all occurred faster than consensus expected)

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: both perovskite and MicroLED are scientifically viable, with well-funded development programs at tier-1 manufacturers
- Motive: display manufacturers want to reduce IP licensing cost and supply-chain dependency
- Opportunity: each product generation cycle (typically 2-3 years) is a technology selection decision point
- All three legs present — full activation but timeline uncertainty defers immediate risk; monitoring priority, not action priority

Disconfirming evidence: MicroLED yield rates remain below 99.999% (required for commercial viability) with no public milestone achieved; perovskite lifetime under operating conditions remains below 20,000 hours; Nanoco patents extend into perovskite or alternative QD architectures, capturing substitution upside

Monitoring checklist:
- Annual Display Week conference — technology demonstration milestones for MicroLED and perovskite
- Samsung Display / LG Display capex allocation to QD vs. alternative display technology segments
- Nanoco patent filings — are they expanding into perovskite or alternative colour-conversion materials?
- Academic publication rate on perovskite EL lifetime (Web of Science / Scopus proxy)
- Any Tier-1 manufacturer announcing a QD-free display product roadmap

Interaction with other failure modes: Structurally correlated with FM1 (Samsung concentration) and FM2 (royalty rate compression) — substitution risk accelerates both; most dangerous if it occurs simultaneously with FM3 (cash burn), creating a "no-exit" scenario

Sceptical bullet: This is the failure mode least visible in near-term financial data — it will not appear in quarterly revenues until it's nearly irreversible, making it Nanoco's structurally hardest-to-monitor existential risk

- Competitor or potential licensee patent filings that appear to design around Nanoco's core synthesis claims

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — IP challenge is a standard tactic by well-resourced counterparties post-licensing demand; Samsung's willingness to settle rather than challenge through IPR is itself evidence of portfolio strength, but that inference has limits; precedent base rate for successful IPR challenges in materials science patents is approximately 30-40% partial invalidation rate at PTAB

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:

- Motive: avoiding royalty payments on a licensing obligation with growing revenue base
- Opportunity: any new licensing negotiation where a counterparty resists creates the filing opportunity
- All three legs present

Disconfirming evidence: Samsung settlement terms include explicit patent validity acknowledgement; Nanoco has proactively filed continuation patents extending core synthesis claims; no IPR petitions filed against core portfolio patents as of last public disclosure

Monitoring checklist:

- EPO eRegister opposition filings

Interaction with other failure modes: High severity interaction with FM1 (Samsung concentration) — if Samsung uses IPR challenge as renegotiation leverage, both fail simultaneously; partially offsetting FM4 (technology substitution) since substitution reduces the target's motive to challenge IP it no longer needs

Sceptical bullet: Samsung's settlement rather than continued litigation is the strongest available evidence of portfolio validity — but it's also a one-data-point inference from a counterparty with unique strategic dynamics

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: management has stated commercial expansion ambitions; the cost items are real and incrementally funded

- Opportunity: every half-year reporting period where deals are delayed creates the "cost ahead of revenue" dynamic

Disconfirming evidence: Nanoco operating cost base has remained flat or declined post-settlement; headcount growth is specifically in licensing/commercial roles with explicit deal pipeline justification; CFO guidance on "EBIT break-even" timeline is consistent with analyst consensus

§9 — Failure Narrative 8 (FALSE FRIEND): Samsung Settlement Economics Create an Artificially Flattering Revenue Base

Specific failure narrative (FALSE FRIEND): The Samsung litigation settlement produced a lump-sum or back-royalty payment that is effectively non-recurring, but is being received over multiple reporting periods in a manner that optically resembles a healthy recurring royalty stream. Analysts modelling forward revenue off this base are extrapolating a structural annuity from what is actually a catch-up payment. Transmission mechanism: settlement receipts complete → ongoing royalty rate (if any) is materially lower than implied by settlement-period revenue → next 4-6 quarters show sharp revenue decline → consensus misses → de-rating of the "licensing royalty business" multiple.

Early warning signals:
- Nanoco management failing to explicitly separate "settlement-related receipts" from "ongoing contractual royalty" in revenue disclosure
- Revenue profile showing a smooth stream but with accounting footnotes referencing "catch-up" or "prior period" components
- Any guidance on "normalised royalty run-rate" that is materially below current reported revenue
- Analyst notes referencing "post-settlement revenue cliff" as a known risk but not modelling it into base case
- Royalty revenue growth rate suddenly negative in the first clean-run period post-settlement

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — this is the canonical false-friend pattern for litigation-origin revenue; Vringo/FORM Holdings, Acacia Research, and ParkerVision all demonstrated this exact profile; the settlement payment structure in UK IP cases frequently blends back-royalties, current-period royalties, and lump-sum components in a way that is disclosure-dependent for accurate modelling

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: accounting treatment of settlement receipts is opaque in standard UK IFRS reporting; disaggregation is not mandated
- Motive: management has no adversarial motive — but natural optimism bias and desire for "clean" revenue narrative creates de facto obscurity
- Opportunity: every reporting period before the post-settlement clean run is a window where this false-friend operates
- All three legs present

Disconfirming evidence: Nanoco annual report explicitly disaggregates settlement back-payment from ongoing royalty entitlement; management provides a "normalised royalty run-rate" guidance figure; auditors have reviewed and confirmed the revenue recognition basis does not include non-recurring catch-up elements

Monitoring checklist:
- Revenue recognition accounting policy note (annual report) — specifically for multi-period settlement payment treatment
- Guidance language: "recurring royalty" vs. "settlement receipts"
- Comparison of reported revenue to any analyst "normalised" revenue figure
- Revenue trajectory in first 2 quarters post-settlement completion date
- CFO commentary on revenue sustainability at results calls

Interaction with other failure modes: Most dangerous interaction: this false friend masks FM1 (concentration) — if the revenue base is inflated by settlement catch-up, the concentration risk is even higher than raw revenue figures suggest; amplifies FM3 (cash burn) since forward cash planning may be over-optimistic

Sceptical bullet: If this failure mode is active, it will not be visible in audited accounts — it requires analyst-level disaggregation of the settlement terms, which are not public

§11 — Failure Narrative 10: Regulatory / ESG-Driven Demand Compression

Specific failure narrative: Nanoco's commercial narrative is partly premised on the regulatory tailwind from RoHS (Restriction of Hazardous Substances) directives that restrict cadmium-based QDs and therefore create demand for Nanoco's cadmium-free technology. If the regulatory environment shifts — either through RoHS exemption extensions for cadmium QDs (as has occurred previously) or through a broader display technology transition that makes the cadmium/cadmium-free distinction commercially irrelevant — the differentiation premium embedded in Nanoco's licensing fees erodes. Transmission mechanism: RoHS exemption extended → cadmium QD manufacturers retain market position → display makers have no regulatory compulsion to adopt cadmium-free QD → Nanoco's negotiating leverage weakens → royalty rate pressure on new deals.

Early warning signals:
- Any European Commission consultation document on RoHS Annex exemption for cadmium QDs — publicly available in EU EUR-Lex
- Cadmium QD manufacturers (Nanosys, Samsung SDI) increasing production capacity rather than transitioning to cadmium-free
- Display manufacturers publicly citing "regulatory uncertainty" as a reason to delay cadmium-free QD adoption
- Nanoco management language shifting away from "regulatory tailwind" framing
- Any industry body (Display Supply Chain Consultants, DSCC) publishing forecasts showing cadmium-free QD adoption slower than prior consensus

Plausibility verdict: Low to medium plausibility — the RoHS trajectory has been broadly adverse to cadmium QD over the medium term, and the political environment around hazardous substances in consumer electronics has moved in Nanoco's favour; however, regulatory timelines are slow and uncertain, and the market has historically tolerated cadmium QD exemptions longer than Nanoco's commercial narrative implies

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: European Commission has full authority to extend RoHS exemptions through standard derogation process
- Motive: industry lobbying by cadmium QD incumbents (well-funded) provides pressure for extension
- Opportunity: each RoHS review cycle (approximately every 5 years) is an extension decision point
- All three legs present — but timeline uncertainty makes this a medium-term rather than immediate risk

Disconfirming evidence: European Commission has formally declined to extend cadmium QD exemptions in the most recent review; Nanoco's patents cover technology applications beyond RoHS-regulated applications; major Tier-1 display makers have made public commitments to cadmium-free QD independent of regulatory mandate

Monitoring checklist:
- EU EUR-Lex RoHS exemption review documents (specifically Annex III/IV exemption for cadmium in display applications)
- Cadmium QD production capacity announcements by Nanosys, Samsung SDI, QD Vision successors
- DSCC and IHS Markit (now S&P Global) QD display adoption forecasts
- Nanoco management commentary on regulatory developments in annual reports
- EU Green Deal and Circular Economy regulation progress — directionally supportive of cadmium restriction

Interaction with other failure modes: Moderate correlation with FM2 (royalty rate compression) — regulatory delay reduces Nanoco's pricing power; partially independent of FM1 (Samsung concentration) since Samsung's QD usage is commercial-driven, not primarily regulatory

Sceptical bullet: The RoHS regulatory machinery has been moving in Nanoco's direction for years — the risk here is pace, not direction

Plausibility verdict: Low plausibility in current base case, but medium plausibility conditional on FM3 (cash burn) materialising — the distressed M&A scenario is path-dependent, not unconditional

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
