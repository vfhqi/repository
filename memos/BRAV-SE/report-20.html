4. §2 — THEME 1: REVENUE DRIVERS + VISIBILITY

J-front verdict: Revenue-side uncertainty is HIGH — backlog gives partial visibility, but the installation/service revenue split trajectory and pricing discipline in a softening construction market are unverified; these questions are most diagnostic on the bull/bear discriminant

Q1-A — Backlog visibility:

"What percentage of next 12 months' revenue is covered by signed contracts or framework agreements today, and how does that compare to 24 months ago?"

  • Expected answer: ~50-60% of rolling 12M revenue is contracted; management will likely cite backlog growth in SEKbn
  • Good answer: specific number with trend line; differentiated by segment (installation vs service); commentary on framework agreement terms and re-pricing clauses
  • Bad answer: vague reference to "strong backlog"; inability to split installation vs service visibility; no comment on re-pricing
  • Follow-up probe: "Of the contracted backlog, what share has fixed-price terms vs cost-plus, and how has that ratio shifted since 2022?"
  • Diagnostic note: Fixed-price share rising in an inflationary labour environment = margin compression risk (bear); cost-plus shift with re-pricing clauses = pricing power resilience (bull); no change narrative in a changed cost environment = management not tracking it (false-friend)

Q1-B — Volume vs price split:

"In H2 2024, how much of reported revenue growth was volume, how much was price, and how much was acquisition contribution?"

  • Expected answer: management will cite overall growth; unlikely to volunteer a clean three-way split without prompting
  • Good answer: explicit organic volume / price / M&A bridge with segment detail; acknowledgement of where volume was negative
  • Bad answer: total growth cited without bridge; "we don't manage the business that way"; blended answer that conflates price and volume
  • Follow-up probe: "In segments where volume was negative, did you hold margin or did you chase revenue to cover fixed costs?"
  • Diagnostic note: Volume-chasing in a downturn to cover fixed-cost base is the bear-case mechanism; margin discipline in volume decline is the bull-case attribute

Q1-C — End-market segmentation:

"What share of 2024 service revenue was genuinely recurring — multi-year maintenance contracts — versus project-linked service call-outs?"

  • Expected answer: management will claim strong recurring share; the honest number is likely lower than framing suggests
  • Good answer: specific percentage with churn rate on multi-year contracts; average contract duration cited; renewal rate disclosed
  • Bad answer: "service is our growth engine" without recurring/non-recurring split; conflation of service segment revenue with recurring revenue
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the average contract length and renewal rate on the top-50 service contracts by revenue?"
  • Diagnostic note: If >40% of service revenue is genuinely multi-year contracted, the revenue-quality bull case holds; if most service revenue is call-out or project-linked, the defensive narrative is overstated (false-friend)

Q1-D — Customer concentration:

"Does any single customer represent more than 5% of group revenue, and what is the top-10 customer concentration as a share of total?"

  • Expected answer: Bravida's model is geographically and customer-diversified; top-10 probably <25% of revenue; management will present this as a strength
  • Good answer: specific numbers; trend in concentration; comment on whether large public-sector / infrastructure customers are growing or shrinking in share
  • Bad answer: "we are very diversified" without numbers; inability to quantify top-10 share
  • Follow-up probe: "Are any of your largest customers in sectors that are structurally reducing their facilities footprint — e.g. retail, office?"
  • Diagnostic note: High public-sector share is a recession hedge (bull); high exposure to commercial real estate construction is a cyclical risk (bear)

Q1-E — Forward order book:

"How has order intake momentum changed quarter-on-quarter in Q1 2025, and are you seeing any project deferrals or cancellations in the installation pipeline?"

  • Expected answer: management will be cautious here; likely to cite resilience in infrastructure / energy-transition-related orders
  • Good answer: quantified order intake trend; identification of the specific end markets where deferrals are occurring; honest commentary on cancellation rate
  • Bad answer: "order book is healthy" without numbers; no acknowledgement of any deferrals
  • Follow-up probe: "What share of 2025 installation revenue plan is covered by signed contracts today versus this time last year?"
  • Diagnostic note: Gap between this year's coverage vs prior year is the single most honest leading indicator of revenue risk

Sceptical bullet: if management answers Q1-A through Q1-E fluently and consistently, the analyst should note that backlog and pipeline metrics are the most-prepared answers in any services company meeting; fluency here signals competent IR preparation, not necessarily business resilience; push to Q1-E (order intake momentum) where the current-period data is less rehearsed

5. §3 — THEME 2: MARGIN TRAJECTORY + LEVERS

J-front verdict: Margin uncertainty is the HIGHEST-CONVICTION question in this DD — EBITA margin guidance of ~6% group is below implied peer-best practice; the diagnostic question is whether the gap is structural or transient; management's answer to Q3-C (acquired company margin trajectory) is load-bearing

Q2-A — GM driver:

"Walk me through the gross margin bridge from 2022 to 2024 — what drove the improvement or deterioration, by segment, in basis points?"

  • Expected answer: labour cost inflation partially offset by pricing; service mix helping; installation margin under pressure
  • Good answer: explicit bps-level bridge by driver; identification of the single largest margin headwind; forward view on whether labour inflation has peaked
  • Bad answer: "margins have been resilient"; no bps-level detail; inability to identify the primary driver
  • Follow-up probe: "In the installation segment specifically, what is your subcontractor vs employed-labour split, and how does that affect your ability to flex costs?"
  • Diagnostic note: High employed-labour share in installation = operating leverage cuts both ways (bull in growth, bear in contraction); subcontractor-heavy model = more variable cost but thinner control margins

Q2-B — Op leverage / organic margin bridge:

"What is your incremental EBITA margin on organic revenue growth — and has that number been stable or compressing over the last three years?"

  • Expected answer: management will cite 6-7% EBITA margin target; incremental margin question may not be prepared
  • Good answer: explicit incremental margin number (e.g. "on organic growth above X%, we drop Y% to EBITA"); trend direction stated honestly
  • Bad answer: deflection to reported margin; no incremental margin concept offered; confusion between gross and EBITA margin
  • Follow-up probe: "At what level of organic revenue decline does EBITA margin go negative — what is your fixed-cost floor?"
  • Diagnostic note: This is the single most important structural margin question — the fixed-cost floor defines the downside protection of the thesis; a management team that cannot answer this is not managing the business analytically

Q2-C — Acquired company margin trajectory (LOAD-BEARING):

"For acquisitions made in 2020-2022, what is the average EBITA margin of those companies today versus at acquisition — and are any still below group average?"

  • Expected answer: management will claim integration is on track; specific margin data by acquisition cohort is unlikely to be volunteered
  • Good answer: cohort-level margin data; identification of one or two acquisitions that underperformed; honest commentary on root cause
  • Bad answer: "acquired companies integrate well" without data; no cohort visibility; claim of uniform improvement
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the average time from acquisition close to reaching group EBITA margin — and what drives the variance between faster and slower integrations?"
  • Diagnostic note: If acquired companies average 3+ years to reach group margin, the accretion narrative is delayed and RONIC is lower than headline IRR suggests (bear); if integration is demonstrably faster, the compounding M&A engine is credible (bull)

Q2-D — Mix shift:

"If the group achieves your stated service/installation revenue mix target in 3 years, what does the blended group EBITA margin look like — can you walk the mix arithmetic?"

  • Expected answer: management will describe service as higher-margin; the arithmetic of the mix shift will likely not be explicit
  • Good answer: segment margin differentials stated explicitly (e.g. service EBITA 8%, installation EBITA 5%); mix arithmetic walked; implied group margin stated
  • Bad answer: "service is higher margin so mix shift is positive" without quantification; inability to state segment EBITA margins
  • Follow-up probe: "Is the service margin differential structural (contract type, labour intensity) or is it currently inflated by COVID-era deferred maintenance demand?"
  • Diagnostic note: If the service margin premium is structural, mix shift is a genuine margin catalyst (bull); if it is cyclical, the mix-shift margin narrative is overstated (false-friend)

Q2-E — Pricing-cost spread:

"How quickly can you pass through a 5% increase in electrician/plumber labour rates to customers — and what is the contractual mechanism?"

  • Expected answer: framework agreements have re-pricing clauses tied to industry wage indices; pass-through is 3-6 month lag
  • Good answer: specific contractual mechanism cited; re-pricing clause coverage quantified; lag time stated; examples of successful pass-through
  • Bad answer: "we are good at pricing" without mechanism; no comment on coverage of re-pricing clauses; no lag time given
  • Follow-up probe: "In 2022-2023, when labour cost inflation was acute, what was the bps impact of pricing lag on EBITA — and how would you handle a repeat?"
  • Diagnostic note: If pricing pass-through has a structural 6+ month lag with no index clause, margin is vulnerable in any future labour cost spike (bear risk that management may have learned from but not disclosed)

Sceptical bullet: management teams in installation/service businesses reliably cite "mix shift to service" as the margin catalyst; the question is whether this is a deliberate capital allocation choice or a post-hoc narrative on what the market happened to deliver; Q2-D forces the quantification that separates intentional strategy from lucky outcome

6. §4 — THEME 3: CAPITAL ALLOCATION + BALANCE SHEET

J-front verdict: Capital allocation is HIGH UNCERTAINTY — Bravida's serial acquisition model requires RONIC discipline that is difficult to verify from public filings; the questions below probe whether management has genuine returns discipline or is running a volume-growth roll-up

Q3-A — RONIC discipline:

"What is the pre-tax ROIC on your acquisition cohort from 2019-2023, and how does that compare to your stated acquisition IRR hurdle at the time of purchase?"

  • Expected answer: management will cite a hurdle rate (likely 12-15% IRR pre-tax); actual cohort ROIC vs hurdle is unlikely to be disclosed without pressure
  • Good answer: explicit cohort ROIC with vintage detail; honest acknowledgement of where hurdles were missed; commentary on what changed in the underwriting model as a result
  • Bad answer: restatement of hurdle rate without cohort outcome data; "our M&A track record has been strong" without numbers
  • Follow-up probe: "Can you name one acquisition that did NOT meet its IRR hurdle, and what did you learn from the underwriting miss?"
  • Diagnostic note: The ability to cite a failed acquisition with specific lessons is a strong signal of analytical rigour (bull); inability or deflection is a yellow flag on capital discipline (false-friend)

Q3-B — M&A philosophy:

"How do you decide between a bolt-on that strengthens an existing geographic position versus a new-market entry — and what has been the ROIC differential?"

  • Expected answer: management will claim disciplined geographic focus; ROIC differential by acquisition type unlikely to be pre-prepared
  • Good answer: explicit framework for bolt-on vs new-market; data on ROIC differential; acknowledgement that new-market entries carry higher risk
  • Bad answer: "we like both types"; no framework; no ROIC differentiation
  • Follow-up probe: "What is your current pipeline skew — more bolt-ons or more new-market entries — and does that reflect valuation or strategic intent?"
  • Diagnostic note: Pipeline skew toward new-market entries in a high-multiple environment = risk of RONIC dilution (bear); bolt-on discipline in a softening market = compounding flywheel intact (bull)

Q3-C — Leverage target + M&A capacity:

"At current leverage, what is your remaining M&A capacity in 2025-2026, and at what net debt/EBITDA level would you pause acquisitions?"

  • Expected answer: management will cite a target leverage range (likely 1.5-2.5x net debt/EBITDA); pause threshold will be around 3x
  • Good answer: explicit numbers; current position vs range stated; confirmation that organic FCF generation rate is sufficient to de-lever within 12-18 months of any large deal
  • Bad answer: "we are comfortable with our leverage"; no specific threshold for pausing
  • Follow-up probe: "If EBITDA declined 15% in a construction downturn, where does leverage go, and what is the covenant headroom?"
  • Diagnostic note: Stress-testing leverage in a downturn scenario is the load-bearing test of whether the M&A engine is resilient or fragile; a management team that cannot walk the covenant headroom under stress is not running the balance sheet defensively

Q3-D — Working capital management:

"What is your cash conversion cycle trend over the last three years, and does the acquisition pipeline affect working capital predictability?"

  • Expected answer: services businesses typically have low working capital; management will cite strong cash conversion; acquisition integration can temporarily disrupt WC
  • Good answer: explicit days-based metrics (DSO, DPO, inventory days); trend direction; commentary on integration disruption and how quickly it normalises
  • Bad answer: "cash conversion is strong" without metrics; no commentary on acquisition WC dynamics
  • Follow-up probe: "In your largest acquisition in the last 3 years, how much working capital did you have to inject post-close, and was that in the underwriting model?"
  • Diagnostic note: WC surprises post-acquisition are a hidden source of IRR dilution that rarely surfaces in headline metrics; management awareness of this is a signal of underwriting quality

Sceptical bullet (sceptical meta): management will frame capital allocation as disciplined; the test is not whether they have a framework but whether they can describe a situation where the framework said no and they listened; the inability to cite a declined deal is a yellow flag

7. §5 — THEME 4: STRATEGY + COMPETITIVE POSITION

J-front verdict: Strategic uncertainty is MODERATE — Bravida's pan-Nordic expansion and energy-transition positioning are credible narratives, but competitive differentiation from regional independents and pan-European peers (Imtech, Caverion, Assemblin) requires probing; win-rate data is the most diagnostic element

Q4-A — Win condition:

"In a competitive tender for a new multi-year service contract, what is your win rate, and what are the top three reasons you lose?"

  • Expected answer: management will cite brand, scale, and geographic coverage as differentiators; win rate likely 30-40% on contested tenders
  • Good answer: explicit win rate; honest top-3 loss reasons with examples; commentary on whether loss reasons are structural or addressable
  • Bad answer: "we have strong competitive positioning"; no win rate; no honest loss reasons
  • Follow-up probe: "Has the win rate changed materially in the last 18 months, and do you attribute that to your own capability or competitor behaviour?"
  • Diagnostic note: Declining win rate is an early signal of competitive erosion (bear); stable or improving win rate with pricing discipline is a moat signal (bull)

Q4-B — Moat trajectory:

"What does Bravida have today that a well-funded regional independent cannot replicate in 3-5 years — specifically, not assets, but capabilities?"

  • Expected answer: management will cite scale in procurement, brand, cross-selling, data systems; the honest answer is that capabilities are partially replicable
  • Good answer: specific capability named (e.g. digital service delivery platform, technician training and retention system); evidence of how that capability creates measurable pricing or cost advantage
  • Bad answer: generic "scale and brand" answer; no capability specificity; no measurable advantage cited
  • Follow-up probe: "If a private-equity-backed regional roll-up offered technicians 20% more salary, how many would leave, and what does that tell you about your moat?"
  • Diagnostic note: Technician retention is the load-bearing moat question for a labour-intensive services business; a management team that has not stress-tested this is not thinking clearly about competitive risk

Q4-C — Energy transition adjacency:

"What share of 2024 revenue was directly attributable to energy transition projects — heat pumps, EV charging, grid upgrades — and what is your targeted share in 2027?"

  • Expected answer: management will claim energy transition exposure as a tailwind; specific revenue attribution may not be tracked granularly
  • Good answer: explicit revenue share; growth rate of the sub-segment; commentary on whether margin in energy-transition projects differs from base business
  • Bad answer: "we are well-positioned for energy transition" without revenue data; conflation of all electrification work with energy-transition
  • Follow-up probe: "In energy-transition projects, are you primarily a subcontractor or the lead contractor, and does that affect your margin capture?"
  • Diagnostic note: Lead contractor status = higher margin and stickier customer relationship (bull); subcontractor status = commodity labour with no differentiation premium (risk that energy-transition narrative is not margin-accretive)

Q4-D — Disruption risk:

"Where are you most vulnerable to technology-enabled competition — remote monitoring, IoT-enabled service dispatch, or AI-assisted diagnostics — and who is doing it better than you today?"

  • Expected answer: management will acknowledge digital transformation; likely to claim they are investing; unlikely to name a competitor doing it better
  • Good answer: honest identification of a capability gap; specific competitor or start-up named; investment roadmap to close the gap
  • Bad answer: "we are digitising the business"; no acknowledgement of a gap; no external reference point
  • Follow-up probe: "Has any customer in the last 12 months reduced their service contract scope because of improved in-house monitoring capability — even one example?"
  • Diagnostic note: Even one example of customer scope reduction due to technology substitution is a leading indicator of the long-term disruption risk that is currently underweighted in consensus models

Sceptical bullet: the energy-transition narrative is consensus in the Nordic services sector; the diagnostic question is not whether Bravida has exposure but whether that exposure is margin-accretive or margin-dilutive (commodity labour risk); Q4-C is designed to force the arithmetic

8. §6 — THEME 5: MANAGEMENT QUALITY + OPERATOR TRACK RECORD

J-front verdict: Management quality signal is MIXED — long CEO tenure is positive; the incentive structure (LTI tied to EPS + ROCE) is directionally aligned but vesting parameters need verification; the operator test is whether management can demonstrate analytical rigour under pressure, not in prepared remarks

Q5-A — Operator track record:

"What is the single biggest operational mistake you have made in the last four years, and what did the P&L look like before and after you fixed it?"

  • Expected answer: management will offer a carefully-chosen, minor-seeming example; the instinct is to under-disclose
  • Good answer: specific operational failure with P&L quantification; clear root-cause analysis; evidence of systemic fix (not just outcome); no hedging language
  • Bad answer: "we have learned a lot"; vague reference to "integration challenges"; failure example that is actually a macro event (COVID, supply chain) rather than an operational decision
  • Follow-up probe: "If you faced the same situation again today, what would be the first metric that would alert you — and are you tracking it?"
  • Diagnostic note: Specificity of the failure + quality of the systemic fix = analytical culture indicator; vagueness or macro-attribution = culture that does not learn from operational data (false-friend signal)

Q5-B — Sandbagging discipline:

"Looking back at the last four years, in how many of those years did reported EBITA exceed your internal budget at the start of the year — and by how much?"

  • Expected answer: management will not have this number immediately; the question is deliberately uncomfortable; they may reframe to external guidance
  • Good answer: honest acknowledgement of sandbagging tendency or its absence; specific data points; comment on whether budget methodology has changed
  • Bad answer: deflection to external guidance; "we don't share internal budgets"; reframing without answering
  • Follow-up probe: "If your internal budget was consistently exceeded, what does that tell you about the quality of your forecasting process — is that a feature or a bug?"
  • Diagnostic note: Persistent beat vs internal budget = either good sandbagging (analyst-friendly, bull) or poor forecasting culture (operational quality risk); the follow-up probe forces management to characterise their own forecasting discipline

Q5-C — Power of incentives — LTI structure (MANDATORY per Q20-MMO):

"Walk me through your LTI vesting conditions — which metric has the highest weight, what is the performance range, and when was it last recalibrated?"

  • Expected answer: LTI tied to EPS growth and/or ROCE; typical vesting period 3 years; recalibration happens at board discretion
  • Good answer: explicit metric weights; specific threshold/maximum performance ranges; honest commentary on whether the targets were set at stretch or were likely to vest in base case
  • Bad answer: "our LTI is aligned with shareholder value"; no metric weights; no performance range detail; no comment on stretch
  • Follow-up probe: "In 2022-2024, did the LTI vest at threshold, target, or maximum — and what would need to go wrong for it NOT to vest in 2025?"
  • Diagnostic note (power of incentives): If LTI targets are set so that they vest in base case without stretch, the incentive structure reinforces the status quo rather than driving outperformance; if LTI did NOT vest in a period of apparent operational progress, the targets are set at genuine stretch (bull signal on alignment)

Q5-D — Communication clarity + crisis playbook:

"In the event of a major project loss or quality incident, describe the sequence of decisions you made in your most recent significant operational crisis."

  • Expected answer: management will describe the process correctly in the abstract; the question seeks a specific real example
  • Good answer: specific incident named; decision sequence described with timeline; honest commentary on what they would do differently
  • Bad answer: hypothetical framing ("if we had a crisis, we would…"); no real example offered; PR-polished language
  • Follow-up probe: "Who in the organisation knew about the problem first, and what was the time lag before it reached you — and is that lag acceptable?"
  • Diagnostic note: Time lag from problem identification to CEO awareness is a culture indicator; long lags with hierarchical filtering = culture that hides bad news (bear on management quality); short lags with open reporting = learning culture (bull)

Q5-E — Hiring posture:

"How many of your top-20 managers were promoted internally versus hired externally in the last three years — and what does that tell you about your talent pipeline?"

  • Expected answer: Bravida will likely claim strong internal promotion culture; the honest data may show external hiring pressure in technical / digital roles
  • Good answer: specific split; honest commentary on where internal pipeline is thin; investment in training and development cited with metrics
  • Bad answer: "we develop talent internally"; no data; no acknowledgement of pipeline gaps
  • Follow-up probe: "In digital transformation and data analytics roles specifically, are you winning the talent war or losing it — and to whom?"
  • Diagnostic note: External hiring pressure in digital/analytics roles signals a capability gap that could delay technology-enabled competitive response (bear risk on strategy execution)

Sceptical bullet: management quality questions are the most rehearsed in any CEO/CFO meeting; the diagnostic signal is NOT what they say about themselves but the ratio of specific data to abstract language in their answers; count the specific numbers; a management team that cannot answer Q5-B (sandbagging discipline) with internal budget data is not running an analytically rigorous business

9. §7 — THEME 6: RISK + DOWNSIDE (Q19 PRE-MORTEM CROSS-REF)

J-front verdict: Downside risk is ELEVATED on two vectors — construction cycle exposure in installation and labour cost inflexibility; the pre-mortem failure modes are (1) organic revenue decline in installation with fixed cost base, (2) M&A RONIC below WACC, and (3) technician attrition spike in a tight labour market

Q6-A — Bear-case probe:

"If Nordic construction activity declines 15% in 2026, walk me through what happens to group EBITA — segment by segment."

  • Expected answer: management will cite service/maintenance as a buffer; installation will take the hit; they will quantify partially
  • Good answer: explicit segment-level sensitivity; confirmation of fixed vs variable cost split; stated floor margin in a severe downturn
  • Bad answer: "service provides resilience" without quantification; no segment-level sensitivity
  • Follow-up probe: "In a 15% construction decline, do you flex headcount or accept margin erosion — and how quickly can you flex?"
  • Diagnostic note: Headcount flexibility is the load-bearing operational question in a labour-intensive business; a management team that cannot answer this has not stress-tested the downside (bear flag)

Q6-B — Capex-cut prioritisation (LOAD-BEARING diagnostic):

"If you had to cut 20% of discretionary capex and investment spending tomorrow, which projects survive and which get cut first — walk me through the priority list."

  • Expected answer: management will resist the premise ("we don't think about it that way"); if pressed, they will prioritise digital and safety capex
  • Good answer: explicit priority order; reasoning linked to ROIC and strategic value; honest identification of nice-to-haves vs must-haves
  • Bad answer: "we maintain discipline on all investments"; inability to rank; deflection to "the board sets capex"
  • Follow-up probe: "If the projects that get cut first are the ones building long-term competitive capability, what does that tell you about the balance sheet fragility?"
  • Diagnostic note: ⚡ This question is the most operationally diagnostic question in the memo — management's instinctive ranking of capex priorities reveals the true strategic hierarchy, independent of the strategy deck; the answer management resists most is the most informative

Q6-C — Pricing fragility:

"In 2023-2024, did you lose any significant contracts specifically on price, and what was the margin of the competitor who won them?"

  • Expected answer: management will acknowledge some price competition; they will not know the competitor's margin
  • Good answer: specific contract loss examples; honest commentary on the pricing gap; evidence that they chose not to match rather than could not match
  • Bad answer: "we are not the cheapest but we win on quality"; no specific losses cited; no pricing-gap data
  • Follow-up probe: "Is price competition intensifying or moderating in 2025, and in which geographies or segments is it most acute?"
  • Diagnostic note: Intensifying price competition in a softening market is the demand/supply imbalance bear case; management that cannot cite specific loss examples is not tracking competitive dynamics rigorously

Q6-D — Failure mode test — technician attrition:

"What is your technician attrition rate today versus three years ago, and what would a 5 percentage-point increase in attrition do to service delivery capacity and margin?"

  • Expected answer: management will cite below-industry attrition; will not have the margin sensitivity pre-prepared
  • Good answer: explicit attrition rate with trend; margin sensitivity quantified; retention investment described with ROI framing
  • Bad answer: "our people are loyal"; no attrition data; no margin sensitivity
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the average time to train a replacement technician to full productivity, and what does that cost — in direct terms and in service quality terms?"
  • Diagnostic note: Technician attrition + long training lead times = margin fragility in tight labour markets; this is the highest-plausibility operational failure mode in the Q19 pre-mortem and management's answer here is the most direct test of whether they have quantified it

Q6-E — False-friend probe — goodwill and acquisition write-off risk:

"How much of the balance sheet is goodwill and acquired intangibles, and under what scenario would you expect to take an impairment charge in the next 2 years?"

  • Expected answer: management will acknowledge goodwill concentration; impairment scenario will be framed as highly unlikely
  • Good answer: explicit goodwill/equity ratio; CGU-level breakdown; honest description of the headroom in the impairment test; scenario in which impairment could trigger
  • Bad answer: "impairment is a non-cash item"; dismissal of scenario as unlikely; no CGU headroom data
  • Follow-up probe: "At what discount rate assumption does your largest CGU go below carrying value — and how does that compare to current WACC?"
  • Diagnostic note: Goodwill impairment in a serial acquirer is the false-friend destroyer — it does not affect FCF but it signals that the acquisition machine has been destroying value; management that cannot describe the impairment headroom has not thought rigorously about balance-sheet risk
