D'IETEREN GROUP: CEO/CFO MEETING MEMO

Questions and Analysis for Board Discussion

Date: April 9, 2026
Company: D'Ieteren Group (Euronext Brussels: DIE)
Prepared by: [Portfolio Manager]
Purpose: Advance preparation for meeting with CEO and CFO

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

D'Ieteren Group delivered mixed 2025 results characterised by underlying operational growth masked by significant headwinds from debt refinancing and foreign exchange volatility. Adjusted profit before tax declined 10% to €955.6 million, but underlying growth excluding FX and refinancing costs was 3.8%. The group enters 2026 with a portfolio in transition: Belron approaching potential IPO, Moleskine stabilising after leadership changes, and D'Ieteren Automotive facing structural Belgian market headwinds. Key areas for discussion centre on: (1) Belron's capital structure optionality and IPO timing/valuation; (2) path to margin expansion (Belron targeting 25%+ by 2028); (3) cash flow normalisation post-refinancing; (4) cyclical lows in D'Ieteren Automotive and TVH, and recovery timing; (5) management's philosophy on capital allocation given extraordinary dividend executed and continued leverage constraints; (6) European expansion opportunities for Belron given lower North American penetration rates; (7) working capital management through 2026 tariff environment. The portfolio manager holds a 12-18 month holding period and seeks significant changes in financial trajectory and multiple re-rating potential.

PRIMARY QUESTIONS FOR CEO/CFO (Ordered by Materiality)

1. BELRON IPO: TIMING, VALUATION, AND CAPITAL STRUCTURE IMPLICATIONS

Why this matters: Belron represents ~80% of group value and carries €9-10bn of debt. An IPO would be the most consequential transaction for D'Ieteren in a decade, reshaping leverage, capital allocation, shareholder returns, and group strategic optionality.

Secondary questions:
- Rothschild has been engaged to explore options including an IPO. What is the current timeline (2026, 2027, later)? What hurdle rates or valuation thresholds must be met for an IPO to proceed?
- Current market estimates value Belron at €27-32bn in an IPO. How does management's internal valuation compare, and what are the key value drivers (margin expansion, ADAS penetration, ROIC assumptions)?
- If IPO proceeds, how much Belron equity would D'Ieteren retain, and what is the post-IPO leverage target for Belron (standalone) and D'Ieteren Group?
- Would an IPO trigger changes in Belron's capital allocation (dividends, buybacks, M&A spend) versus current parent-directed free cash flow sweeps?
- If an IPO does not proceed in 2026, what are the alternative monetisation or refinancing scenarios being modelled?

Supporting analysis:
- Belron raised significant debt in Q4 2024 (senior secured notes) to refinance existing debt and pay a special dividend. This increased leverage to 5.2x at end-2024, improving to 4.7x by end-2025 through EBITDA growth and FCF application.
- An IPO would allow Belron to refinance its debt independently at lower cost, potentially unlocking value through multiple expansion (Belron likely would be rated investment grade).
- D'Ieteren Group has demonstrated willingness to execute large capital actions: in 2024, the family executed a €4 billion extraordinary dividend (€74/share), funded by €850m existing liquidity plus €1bn new debt facility.
- Retaining >50% of Belron post-IPO would maintain strategic control and significant dividend upside, while reducing D'Ieteren's leverage materially.

Expected management response: Management will emphasise they are "exploring options" and "Very happy with Belron share," signalling no forced sale. Likely to highlight IPO valuation upside potential and that any transaction would prioritise value maximisation. If IPO delayed, will discuss alternative refinancing efficiency gains.

2. BELRON MARGIN EXPANSION: 2028 TARGET >25%, PATH AND RISK FACTORS

Why this matters: Belron's adjusted operating margin of 23.0% in 2025 sits between 2025 ambition (23%) and 2028 target (>25%). Margin expansion directly drives group profitability; 200bps improvement = ~€100m PBT upside. Changes in pricing power, ADAS penetration, and labour cost inflation will materially affect feasibility.

Secondary questions:
- Break down the 200bps margin expansion from 23% to >25% by driver: (a) ADAS recalibration penetration growth, (b) pricing/mix progression, (c) labour productivity gains, (d) operating leverage from scale, (e) other. What is the expected contribution of each?
- ADAS recalibration achieved 42% penetration in 2024. What is 2025 penetration and price per recalibration? What is the long-term saturation point (is 70-80% realistic)?
- What is management's labour cost inflation assumption through 2028, particularly for North America where Safelite operates? How does this compare to pricing power and productivity roadmap?
- How much of margin expansion depends on Belron's geographic mix shift (e.g., increasing cash job contribution in North America vs. insurance-driven work)? What is the risk if mix remains weighted to insurance?
- Are there technology investments required (e.g., ADAS equipment, fleet services infrastructure) that would materially slow margin expansion?

Supporting analysis:
- Belron organic sales grew 7.1% in 2025 (at constant FX). Adjusted operating profit grew 15.7%, implying significant operating leverage and margin expansion.
- ADAS represents a high-margin service: 42% of jobs already included recalibration by end-2024. This is a direct pricing/mix driver; recalibration adds significant per-job value.
- Cash jobs in the US represent only 27% of work vs. insurance (33%) and commercial (40%), suggesting significant upside if penetration accelerates. Management highlighted US cash jobs as "key priority with largest addressable market."
- Margin expansion is not dependent on significant volume growth; implied 2026-2028 revenue growth is mid-to-high single digit, which is moderate. Operating leverage comes from mix and scale.

Expected management response: Management will provide detailed waterfall showing ADAS contribution (likely 100+ bps), pricing progression (50-75 bps), and productivity (25-50 bps). Will acknowledge labour cost inflation but emphasise pricing discipline and productivity offsets. May position 2025 23% as "ahead of trajectory."

3. NORTH AMERICA GROWTH DYNAMICS: SAFELITE POSITIONING, MARKET SHARE, AND COMPETITIVE PRESSURE

Why this matters: Safelite generates >50% of Belron's revenue. North American growth is moderating (claims avoidance in early 2025, though easing by year-end). Understanding Safelite's competitive positioning, pricing power, and market share trajectory is critical to Belron's 2026-2028 value creation.

Secondary questions:
- What is Safelite's current market share in US vehicle glass repair/replacement, and how has it evolved over 2024-2025? Competitors include Glass America, Auto Glass Now, and many regional players. Is Safelite gaining or losing share?
- Safelite acquired TruRoad Holdings in 2025. What did this acquisition cost, what synergies are targeted, and how does it fit into Safelite's geographic or service expansion strategy?
- What percentage of Safelite revenue is insurance-driven vs. cash jobs vs. commercial contracts? Is there a strategic target mix for 2028, and what pricing/margin implications does a shift to cash jobs entail?
- How is Safelite positioned against disruptors or alternative service models (e.g., mobile services expanding, insurance partnerships consolidating, OEM-directed repair networks)? What are key competitive advantages Safelite is leveraging?
- What is the underlying organic growth rate for Safelite in 2026E, excluding M&A? Is 3-5% organic realistic given market maturity, or should it be lower?

Supporting analysis:
- Safelite's parent, Belron, performed 17.1 million jobs globally in 2025, up 3.2% YoY. This is modest volume growth, suggesting market saturation or claims avoidance (weather-related reductions in 2024-early 2025).
- North America is ~55% of Belron's sales (€3.5bn estimated). If Belron is 20% of US market pre-acquisition (as reported in 2007 and likely higher now), Safelite is a dominant incumbent.
- Cash jobs carry higher margins than insurance work (less administrative overhead, direct pricing power). US cash jobs are only 27% of volume, vs. 33% commercial and 40% insurance, suggesting significant operating leverage if mix shifts.
- Mobile recalibration units and in-situ ADAS systems are new competitive advantages Belron is deploying. These improve convenience and attachment rates.

Expected management response: Management will emphasise Safelite's market position and pricing discipline. Will highlight TruRoad as strategic (likely geographic fill or service line expansion). May be cautious on cash job mix targets due to insurance relationship dependencies, but will note C&I (commercial) upside. Will acknowledge competition but stress Belron's scale and brand advantages.

4. D'IETEREN AUTOMOTIVE: STRUCTURAL HEADWINDS AND PROFITABILITY FLOOR IN DECLINING BELGIAN MARKET

Why this matters: D'Ieteren Auto is 10-12% of group PBT (~€100-130m) but is in structural decline due to falling Belgian car registrations. Management's ability to stabilise margins and manage fixed costs will determine if it remains a strategic asset or a drag on group returns.

Secondary questions:
- Belgian new car market registrations are expected to decline to ~415k by 2028 (from higher levels today). What is management's assumption for D'Ieteren Automotive market share, and how does this translate to unit sales and revenue?
- D'Ieteren Automotive achieved a 22.8% market share and had 1.4m VW-branded vehicles on the road in 2025. Is this market share defensible, or is there structural loss risk due to competing retailers or direct-to-consumer models by VW Group?
- Management targets adjusted operating profit margin of ~4% by 2028 for D'Ieteren Auto. Current actual margin is not clearly disclosed but implied to be in mid-single digits (H1-2025 segment profit was depressed due to new car sales decline). What is the FY 2025 actual margin, and how does 4% target compare to historical baselines?
- What revenue diversification beyond new car sales is being pursued? After-sales services, financing (VDFin), digital/mobility offerings, used car operations—which are growth drivers?
- The company achieved EcoVadis Platinum certification (top 1% ESG). Is this a competitive differentiator for corporate fleet customers, and is there pricing premium attached?

Supporting analysis:
- D'Ieteren Auto's H1-2025 adjusted PBT declined 27.1% YoY, mainly driven by lower new car sales following exceptional 2024 (record year).
- Belgian market registrations are declining due to regulatory headwinds (EU emissions regs), EV transition costs, and macro weakness. Long-term shrinkage is structural, not cyclical.
- VW Group has 10-11 brands under D'Ieteren distribution (VW, Audi, Porsche, Bentley, Lamborghini, Cupra, Skoda, Rimac, Bugatti, Maserati, Microlino). Distribution breadth provides some resilience vs. single-brand peers.
- D'Ieteren Auto has access to captive finance (VDFin, joint venture with VW Finance) and after-sales services (parts, service, collision repair). These provide margin stability vs. pure retail.

Expected management response: Management will note that 4% target is achievable through: (1) market share stabilisation at 22-23%, (2) revenue mix shift to after-sales and services, (3) fixed cost deleveraging. Will likely characterise auto distribution as "stable cash-generative" vs. growth business. If asked about VW OEM pressure or direct-sales models, will emphasise long-term distribution contracts and strategic value of VW brand in Belgium.

5. FREE CASH FLOW TRAJECTORY AND WORKING CAPITAL NORMALISATION

Why this matters: Free cash flow of €374m in FY 2025 is strong but down from exceptional €441m in 2024, partly due to additional financial charges from refinancing (€140-160m in P&L interest, plus cash interest leakage). FCF trajectory through 2026-2028 underpins leverage reduction, shareholder returns, and M&A capacity.

Secondary questions:
- H1-2025 FCF was €257m (vs. €363m in H1-2024). The decline was driven by higher cash interest (~€98m additional financial charges), lower working capital inflow, and higher capex. Is this seasonal (H2 typically stronger) or a structural step-down?
- D'Ieteren Automotive working capital swung negative in H1-2025 (-€47m) due to inventory normalisation after exceptional 2024 levels. Is this a one-time normalisation, or is working capital a structural headwind going forward?
- PHE (Parts) working capital swung significantly negative in H1-2025 (-€46m swing vs. H1-2024 +€116m), driven by Top Part acquisition and inventory build ahead of US tariff implementation. How much of this is transient (tariff-driven inventory build) vs. structural?
- What is management's 2026 FCF guidance, and what are the key assumptions (EBITDA growth, cash interest, capex spend, working capital)?
- Moleskine FCF swung from -€8m (H1-24) to -€5m (H1-25) as inventory management improved. Is Moleskine close to FCF break-even, or will it remain a modest cash consumer?

Supporting analysis:
- Full-year 2025 FCF of €374m is stated as "remaining strong" despite refinancing headwinds. This implies management expected a larger decline and achieved better-than-feared cash generation.
- The €500m bridge loan was repaid ahead of schedule in H1-2025 (not in guidance window), suggesting cash discipline and desire to reduce debt quickly.
- Group gross external debt stands at €500m post-bridge repayment (excluding Belron's ~€9bn, which is now semi-autonomous). This is very low relative to group EBITDA, creating refinancing flexibility.
- Working capital is a lever: D'Ieteren Automotive inventory normalisation was expected; PHE tariff inventory build is transient; Moleskine inventory stabilisation is improving.

Expected management response: Management will likely guide to FCF of €350-400m in 2026 (normalised from 2025 exceptional level but still strong). Will emphasise cash interest reduction post-refinancing margin compression and outline path to leverage reduction (targeting Belron 4.0-4.5x by 2028, group net cash by end-2028). Will characterise H1 working capital swings as timing-related and normalising.

6. CAPITAL ALLOCATION PHILOSOPHY: DIVIDENDS, LEVERAGE TARGETS, AND M&A APPETITE POST-EXTRAORDINARY DIVIDEND

Why this matters: D'Ieteren executed a €4bn extraordinary dividend (€74/share) in 2024, funded by €850m liquidity + €1bn new debt. This signal is material: the family prioritised shareholder returns over fortress balance sheet. Understanding post-dividend capital allocation philosophy (ordinary dividends, buybacks, leverage tolerance, M&A) is critical to valuation expectations.

Secondary questions:
- The €2.00 ordinary dividend for FY 2025 (up from €1.60 in 2024) signals progressive dividend policy. What is the long-term ordinary dividend payout target as % of PBT or FCF? Is there appetite for further dividend growth as leverage normalises?
- Are there plans for share buyback programmes? (Management has previous buyback authority renewed through May 2028). Does management view the stock as undervalued or prefer organic growth reinvestment?
- What is the group's net leverage target through the planning horizon (2026-2028)? Belron is targeting 4.0-4.5x net leverage; what about the consolidated group including cash/debt at corporate level?
- Given capital allocation for shareholder returns (€4bn extraordinary + ordinary dividends), is there material capacity for add-on M&A, or is capital discipline tilted toward debt reduction?
- The extraordinary dividend was funded by new debt. Was this a "one-time" event, or does the family's approach to capital allocation suggest openness to further financial engineering (e.g., dividend recap) if equity is undervalued?

Supporting analysis:
- The €4bn extraordinary dividend ($74/share) in 2024 was one of the largest capital returns by D'Ieteren in years. This suggests the family views equity as undervalued or portfolio "mature" (unlikely to require fresh capital for transformation).
- Ordinary dividend has grown: €1.60 (2024) to €2.00 (2025), a 25% increase. If this growth rate continues, dividend will be €2.50+ by 2027, implying payout ratio of 20-25% of reported PBT (higher if underlying growth is higher).
- Belron is self-funding from operations; consolidating multiple leverage targets may be complex, but "fortress balance sheet" is not management's stated priority (evidenced by extraordinary dividend).
- Group has capacity for material M&A: if FCF stabilises at €350-400m/year and leverage is managed, €200-300m acquisition capacity is plausible.

Expected management response: Management will state commitment to progressive ordinary dividend, strong but sustainable leverage (Belron 4-4.5x, group below 3x net leverage by 2027-2028), and opportunistic M&A if strategic fit found. Will likely characterise extraordinary dividend as reflecting family's confidence in portfolio and value creation, not desperation for capital.

7. MOLESKINE TURNAROUND: LEADERSHIP CHANGE, CHANNEL MIX, AND PATH TO PROFITABILITY

Why this matters: Moleskine is a small business (~€80-100m revenue estimated) but notable underperformer, with adjusted PBT of -€8m in FY 2025 and -€7.6m in H1-2025. Leadership changes (CEO Archaimbault departed; co-CEOs Boucar and Browne appointed) signal urgency. Turnaround timing and success will influence group narrative around "portfolio quality."

Secondary questions:
- What is the turnaround plan under new co-CEOs? Focus areas: DTC channel expansion (higher margin), wholesale stabilisation, cost structure optimisation, product innovation?
- Wholesale channel weakness (inventory caution at key accounts) was cited in H1-2025. Is this demand-driven (structural wholesale softness) or execution-driven (lost shelf space)? How is management rebuilding wholesale relationships?
- Management guided to "low- to mid-single-digit growth" in 2026 sales and "growth in adjusted operating result" (implying return to profitability). What does breakeven/profitability look like in PBT terms, and by what year is it targeted?
- Moleskine's DTC channels grew in H1-2025, but wholesale decline was steeper. What is the target mix (DTC vs. wholesale vs. partnerships) by 2027-2028, and what margin progression does this imply?
- Are there M&A synergies or portfolio opportunities (e.g., combining Moleskine with other luxury goods, expanding into adjacent categories) being explored, or is this a pure organic turnaround play?

Supporting analysis:
- Moleskine revenue declined 2.2% organically in FY 2025 (and 3.6% in H1-2025), reflecting wholesale weakness despite DTC growth. This is material headwind vs. group growth.
- Adjusted operating result of €7.7m (FY 2025) fell to -€8.0m after interest on shareholder loan, indicating shareholder loans are a major PBT drag (~€15m annual interest).
- New leadership (Boucar and Browne as co-CEOs) is a signal of change; prior CEO Archaimbault's departure suggests previous strategy or execution was misaligned.
- Moleskine is a "heritage brand" in premium stationery, operating globally with strong brand recognition. Turnaround likely involves: (1) DTC expansion (e-commerce, flagship stores), (2) product innovation (beyond core notebooks), (3) wholesale channel recovery or consolidation.

Expected management response: New leadership likely not present, but CFO will note that co-CEOs bring operational expertise and that 2026 outlook for "sales growth + operating result growth" is achievable through channel optimisation. Will likely acknowledge wholesale was a headwind but emphasise DTC momentum and that shareholder loan interest structure may be re-evaluated if turnaround progresses.

8. TVPARTS PROFIT DECLINE AND MARKET RECOVERY TIMING

Why this matters: TVH is a €1.4bn revenue business that posted a 26.3% adjusted profit decline in FY 2025, driven by "challenging market conditions" in parts and accessories distribution (material handling, construction, industrial equipment). Understanding if this is cyclical (recovery expected 2026-2027) or structural (market share loss) is material to group profit guidance.

Secondary questions:
- What markets/regions are most affected by TVH's 2025 weakness? Is it global, or concentrated in specific geographies (North America, Europe, Asia)? What is your assessment of recovery timing by region?
- TVH maintained 13.4% adjusted operating margin despite profit decline, suggesting cost discipline. Is there material headcount reduction, asset restructuring, or cost actions taken in 2025 that position TVH for faster recovery if markets improve?
- TVH achieved 0.2% organic sales growth in FY 2025 despite market weakness, suggesting it is losing share or facing sector headwinds. What is organic growth assumption for 2026-2027, and how does this rebound to "mid-single-digit" long-term target?
- Management targets "mid-single-digit organic sales growth" through 2028 (per investor day). Does this assume markets recover to growth, or is this structural market share gain? How achievable is this given current market conditions?
- TVH invested in digital platforms, network footprint, and customer experience (e.g., 55m known parts references, 94% same-day shipment). Are these investments yielding return (customer retention, pricing power), and what is the ROI timeline?

Supporting analysis:
- TVH H1-2025 revenue grew 0.2% YoY (extremely modest), while adjusted operating profit declined 30.8% YoY. This implies margin compression, not pure volume loss.
- H1-2025 adjusted operating margin was 14.3%, down from implied 20%+ in prior year, suggesting operational deleverage in softening market.
- Adjusted operating margin of 13.4% (full-year) is healthy for distribution and suggests the business is not in distress. Cost structure is flexible enough to not require restructuring.
- Markets served by TVH (material handling, construction equipment, industrial) are cyclical, often correlated with capex cycles and building activity. 2025 softness likely reflects construction slowdown and capital conservatism across industrial.

Expected management response: Management will characterise 2025 as "cyclical trough" and outline recovery assumptions in 2026 guidance. Will highlight margin protection and operational discipline, and note that digital investments position TVH to capture market share in recovery. If pressed on growth targets, will likely concede to "mid- to low single digit" rather than defend "mid-single digit" through 2028.

9. CURRENCY AND INTEREST RATE HEADWINDS: 2026 SENSITIVITY AND MITIGATION

Why this matters: FY 2025 was heavily impacted by €30.6m FX headwinds (primarily USD weakness) and €140-160m additional financial charges from refinancing. USD exposure is material (Belron North America, Safelite, TVH US operations). Interest rate and FX outlook will significantly influence 2026 PBT guidance.

Secondary questions:
- What is the current FX hedging policy for USD exposure, and what proportion of North American cash flows are hedged for 2026? If EUR/USD remains at 1.17 (Dec 2025 level), does this imply continued FX headwinds, or is 2026 guidance based on stable rates?
- Belron refinancing achieved a 75bps interest margin reduction on USD and EUR term loans. What is the all-in weighted average cost of debt for Belron post-refinancing, and how does this compare to pre-refinancing?
- How much of the €140m additional financial charges in 2025 are from Belron debt vs. group corporate debt, and is the €40m corporate-level interest sustainable through 2026-2027?
- If interest rates decline by 100-150bps through 2026-2027, how much PBT benefit would D'Ieteren realise through mark-to-market and refinancing opportunities?
- Is there appetite to swap variable-rate debt to fixed, or will management maintain exposure to potential rate declines?

Supporting analysis:
- €30.6m FX headwind in 2025 (on €956m reported PBT) is material (~3% impact). If EUR/USD averages 1.15-1.20 in 2026, this could be repeated or worse.
- Belron's senior secured net leverage of 4.7x implies ~€9-10bn of debt. At 4-5% all-in cost of debt (post-refinancing), annual cash interest is €360-500m (gross), with interest expense in P&L running higher due to amortisation and refinancing fees.
- Group corporate debt post-bridge repayment is only €500m, minimising corporate-level interest. This is a key difference vs. Belron, which carries most of the leverage.
- USD weakness is a headwind for earnings translation but supportive of Safelite competitiveness (lower cost of labour relative to local pricing). Management may view this as a long-term positive despite near-term P&L drag.

Expected management response: Management will note that 2026 guidance of "low- to mid-single digit PBT growth" already assumes FX at Dec 31 rates (EUR/USD 1.17). Will emphasise debt refinancing achievements and lower margin on new facilities. If FX improves, will characterise upside as "beat"; if rates fall, will reference potential refinancing benefit. Likely to state "no active hedging beyond natural hedges" for most of USD exposure.

10. COMPETITIVE INTENSITY IN VEHICLE GLASS AND PARTS DISTRIBUTION: MARKET CONSOLIDATION RISKS

Why this matters: Both Belron (vehicle glass) and TVH (parts distribution) operate in fragmented markets with M&A and consolidation trends. Competitive pricing pressure, potential new entrants, and customer consolidation (e.g., insurance companies, OEMs, large fleets) could erode pricing power and margins.

Secondary questions:
- In vehicle glass repair/replacement, are there competitive threats emerging from: (a) OEM-integrated repair networks, (b) insurance company consolidation into captive repair networks, (c) new mobile service models, or (d) regional/local competitors? How is Belron positioned against each?
- Safelite's competitors (Glass America, Auto Glass Now, regional players) are typically smaller and fragmented. Is there risk of consolidation among Safelite competitors, creating a stronger #2 or #3 player? Has Belron evaluated acquisitions of regional competitors?
- In parts distribution , what is the competitive landscape (local vs. international players, OEM distributors, online aggregators)? Is Amazon or other platforms creating disintermediation risk?
- Are insurance companies and large fleet customers increasingly demanding exclusive arrangements, preferred vendor rates, or in-house capabilities that could reduce Belron's/TVH's bargaining power?
- What is management's M&A strategy for both Belron and TVH through 2026-2028? Are there bolt-on acquisition targets identified?

Supporting analysis:
- Belron operates in 40 countries with ~30,000 employees and brands including Carglass, Safelite, Autoglass, O'Brien. Global scale and brand portfolio are competitive advantages, but regional players can be competitive on service/pricing.
- TruRoad acquisition by Safelite in 2025 is an example of bolt-on consolidation; likely a regional player or service line acquisition. This suggests Belron's consolidation appetite is active.
- Vehicle glass is a high-frequency, low-average-transaction-value service (per vehicle, but recurring across fleet). Insurance relationships are critical; direct B2B relationships with large fleets (Uber, Amazon, delivery services) are growing.
- Parts distribution is fragmented globally but increasingly contested by online platforms (eBay, Amazon, specialty e-commerce) and direct OEM channels. TVH's scale and service model (same-day shipment to 90k customers) is a defensible moat but not invulnerable.

Expected management response: Management will emphasise Belron's unmatched global scale, brand recognition, and operational excellence as competitive moats. Will note TruRoad acquisition as part of organic consolidation strategy but likely won't disclose specific targets. For TVH, will highlight digital investments and customer intimacy as protections vs. online disintermediation. May acknowledge insurance consolidation but characterise D'Ieteren's partnerships as long-term and resilient.

11. EBITDA MARGIN PROGRESSION AND OPERATIONAL LEVERAGE OPPORTUNITY

Why this matters: Management has guided to consolidated EBITDA progression, but underlying operating margins (operating profit / revenue) are the truest measure of operational health and pricing power. Understanding EBITDA vs. operating profit dynamics, amortisation run-rate, and working capital efficiency reveals quality of earnings.

Secondary questions:
- What is the 2025 consolidated EBITDA (group share), and what is the EBITDA/PBT bridge (depreciation, amortisation, other non-cash items, interest, tax)? Is amortisation expected to increase (new intangibles) or decrease (past intangibles fully written off)?
- EBITDA margin (EBITDA / revenue) for each segment in 2025: Belron, D'Ieteren Auto, TVH, Moleskine, other. Which segments are expanding margins, and which are contracting?
- Is operating leverage from revenue growth (holding margins flat or expanding) the primary source of PBT growth, or is this driven by financial engineering (leverage reduction, interest savings)?
- What capex intensity (capex / revenue) is required to support growth and margin expansion through 2028, and is this embedded in FCF guidance? Does Belron require material capex for ADAS equipment, infrastructure, or is this more maintenance-level?

Supporting analysis:

- Belron's adjusted operating margin improved to 23.0% (from 20.5% in 2023), a 250bps step-up in two years. This is significant operational leverage, driven by price/mix and ADAS.
- D'Ieteren Automotive and TVH are both lower-margin businesses (mid-single digits and mid-teens, respectively), so group margin profile is weighted to Belron's expansion.
- Depreciation and amortisation run-rate is likely €150-200m annually (Belron + TVH intangibles). If this is stable, EBITDA margin progression should track operating profit margin progression.

Expected management response: Management will highlight Belron's 250bps margin expansion as evidence of operational execution and provide 2028 outlook for margin progression by segment. Will note that depreciation is normalising and won't be a headwind to PBT conversion from EBITDA. Will likely emphasise "underlying growth and margin expansion" as beating "reported" numbers, which are depressed by refinancing costs.

12. PORTFOLIO STRATEGY AND ASSET ALLOCATION: CORE VS. PERIPHERAL, FUTURE EXITS

Why this matters: D'Ieteren is a diversified holding company with stakes in five operating businesses (Belron ~80% of value, others ~20%). Understanding which assets are "core" vs. "for sale" informs strategic flexibility, capital allocation optionality, and M&A appetite.

Secondary questions:
- Is Belron D'Ieteren's sole long-term core holding, with D'Ieteren Auto, TVH, Moleskine, and Immo as "complementary" or "peripheral"? Or does management view all assets as long-term holds?
- D'Ieteren Immo (real estate) was recently assigned to new CEO Steven Boel (appointed in 2025). What is the strategy for this asset? Is it a long-term hold, or is there a plan to monetise (REIT, sale)?
- Are there any businesses for which D'Ieteren would actively seek buyers if valuations were attractive (e.g., Moleskine, TVH, D'Ieteren Auto)? What valuation or ROIC thresholds would trigger a sale?
