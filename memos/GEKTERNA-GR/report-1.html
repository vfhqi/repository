§2 — Company Details (Revenue Model, Business Model, Recent Delivery, Internal Change)

Net signal: Revenue model combines regulated/quasi-contracted cash flows (concessions, renewables PPAs) with lumpy project-execution revenues (construction); the contracted layer provides genuine predictability but is currently overshadowed by the growth capex cycle, obscuring FCF quality [#J]

Revenue Model

  • Revenue source: Three segments — Construction (~55%), Renewables/Energy (~25%), Concessions (~20%); exact proportions shift with project completions and energy prices [#I]
  • Sub: Construction revenue is project-milestone driven — lumpy, not recurring
  • Sub: Concessions revenue (Attiki Odos toll + Makedonia Airport landing fees) is volume-linked but contractually underpinned with 25-40 year concession terms
  • Sub: Renewables revenue is partially PPA-contracted and partially merchant; Greek wholesale power prices add volatility to the merchant portion
  • Geography: Primarily Greece; international exposure growing via concessions bids in SE Europe and Middle East but still <15% of group revenue [#I]
  • Sub: Greek concentration is both an advantage (first-mover in RRF spending) and a risk (sovereign/political exposure, market size ceiling)
  • Sub: Makedonia Airport (Thessaloniki) serves ~7m passengers pa; international tourism recovery post-COVID has been strong tailwind
  • Cyclicality: Construction is cyclical; concessions are quasi-defensive (motorway volumes correlated with GDP but with contractual floors); renewables are structural/secular [#A]
  • Sub: 2020 demonstrated the downside case: group revenue fell ~7% but was cushioned by concession contracts; a severe recession would expose the construction book more severely
  • Predictability: Order backlog is the primary visibility mechanism for construction; as of early 2025, construction backlog reportedly ~€3.0-3.5bn (2.0-2.5x annual construction revenue) — above the European contractor peer median of ~1.5-2.0x [#A]
  • Sub: Backlog quality matters: RRF-funded projects carry sovereign backstop; private-sector and municipal contracts carry higher payment-risk profile
  • Driver count: Revenue is driven by multiple largely independent drivers (construction: project awards; concessions: traffic/passenger volumes; renewables: wind/solar output + power prices) — moderate diversification within the portfolio [#A]

Business Model Strength

  • Competitive advantage: Vertical integration is the primary structural advantage — Gek Terna can bid construction + operate concession + own renewable energy on the same asset, reducing overall project return requirements vs fragmented competitors [#A]
  • Sub: This is rare in European infrastructure; Vinci and ACS have similar models at larger scale
  • Sub: Greek market incumbency and regulatory relationships built over 40+ years provide a durable entry barrier for new entrants
  • Customer behaviour: B2G (business-to-government) dominated; Greek state, Eurozone public entities, and airport authorities are primary customers; switching is effectively nil once a concession is awarded [#A]
  • Sub: Concession contracts are non-competitive once awarded — no churn risk during concession term
  • Sub: Renewables PPAs with state offtakers (HEDNO/DAPEEP) provide medium-term contracted cash flow certainty
  • Lock-in/switching costs: Concessions provide 20-40 year lock-in by contractual definition; construction lock-in is zero but recurring project awards (framework agreements, preferred-contractor status) create de facto stickiness [#A]
  • Scale economies: Scale advantages in construction (equipment, procurement, labour pools) are relevant but not exceptional — construction is inherently local, limiting national-to-international scale transfer [#A]
  • Sub: Renewables scale creates procurement advantages (panel/turbine pricing, grid connection queue management) — increasingly important in a competitive auction market
  • Sub: Concession scale (Attiki Odos being Europe's busiest toll road by per-km volume) generates data/operational advantage for future concession bids

Recent Delivery — Last Three Years

  • Recent delivery: FY2022-24 saw meaningful revenue and EBITDA growth, driven by RRF construction awards ramp and Terna Energy renewables commissioning [#A]
  • Sub: FY2022 EBITDA margin improvement (+60bps YoY) driven by higher-margin RRF projects displacing legacy municipal contracts
  • Sub: FY2023 saw Makedonia Airport passenger volumes recover to pre-COVID levels (~7m pa), boosting concessions EBITDA contribution
  • Sub: Terna Energy added ~400MW of wind/solar capacity in 2022-23, growing installed base from ~1.2GW toward ~1.6GW
  • Backlog execution: Backlog grew faster than revenue in 2022-24, indicating strong commercial momentum; as of late 2024, backlog coverage was 2.0-2.5x vs 1.5x European median [#A]
  • Sub: Selectivity in bidding (margin-accretive RRF projects vs low-margin municipal) is a positive signal on operator discipline
  • Renewables ramp: Terna Energy's commissioning of ~200-300MW pa in 2023-24 against a pipeline of ~3GW is the dominant near-term growth engine [#A]
  • Sub: GEK Terna-level benefit is proportional to ~46% stake in Terna Energy; full consolidation would require buy-in of minority

Internal Change

  • Leadership change: No CEO change noted in the last 3 years; Giorgos Peristeris remains Group CEO with longstanding presence; family-linked governance is stable but raises management succession risk questions [#A]
  • Sub: Board composition includes independent directors but family influence is structurally dominant — governance discount vs best-in-class European peers
  • Strategic shift: Pronounced pivot toward renewables as the primary growth pillar is the most significant internal change of the last 3 years; construction is now positioned as an execution engine for the renewables/concessions business rather than the standalone profit centre [#A]
  • Sub: This strategic evolution (contractor → integrated infrastructure platform) mirrors Vinci's 1990s transformation — a credible analogue but one that took 15+ years to fully value-create
  • M&A / asset activity: Gek Terna increased its stake in Terna Energy and has participated in international concession bids (Middle East, SE Europe); no major transformative acquisition in the last 3 years [#I]

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on GEK Terna's revenue model is that the concession and renewables "quality" is partially illusory — Attiki Odos concession expires in 2024-2035 window depending on extension terms, Makedonia Airport volumes depend on Greek tourism (seasonally concentrated, geopolitically sensitive), and Terna Energy's merchant renewables portion is exposed to collapsing Greek power prices if and when the grid adds significant baseload; the apparent predictability could prove cyclically dependent [#J]

§3 — Industry Context (Peer Comparison, Structure, Macro + Non-Macro Change)

Net signal: GEK Terna operates in a structurally supportive environment — Greece's RRF pipeline is a once-in-a-generation public investment cycle, but competitive intensity in renewables auctions is rising rapidly, and the motorway concession model faces secular pressure from EV penetration and traffic-pattern change [#J]

Industry Structure and Value Chain

  • Market structure: Greek construction/infrastructure is a oligopoly; GEK Terna (via Terna S.A.) is the market leader alongside Intrakat, Avax, and Aktor — top-4 contractors hold an estimated 60-70% of public infrastructure contract awards [#A]
  • Sub: Top-5 share >60% is consistent with a concentrated structure that supports rational pricing and MOL stability
  • Sub: International contractors (Hochtief, Bouygues) have made limited inroads into the Greek public works market, partly due to regulatory and relationship barriers
  • Concentration: GEK Terna's position as #1 Greek contractor (by revenue and backlog) gives it structural advantage in bidding on the largest RRF-funded projects (minimum scale requirements filter out smaller competitors) [#A]
  • Competitive intensity: Renewables auction competition is intensifying — Greek DAPEEP auctions now attract pan-European developers (Iberdrola, EDP Renováveis, RWE) alongside domestic players; auction clearing prices declining [#A] ❌
  • Sub: New wind/solar auction IRRs reportedly compressing from ~10-12% to ~7-9% unlevered as European capital floods Greek renewables market
  • Sub: Terna Energy's advantage lies in grid connection queue position and permitted pipeline — building these positions takes 3-5 years, creating a structural lag for new entrants
  • BtE (Barriers to Entry): Construction barriers to entry in Greece are moderate (local presence, bonding capacity, equipment); concession barriers are very high (long-term sovereign contracts, historical incumbency); renewables barriers are moderate and declining [#A]
  • Sub: The combination is more defensible than any single segment — a pure contractor or pure renewable developer cannot replicate the vertically integrated model easily

External Macro Changes

  • Macro shift — RRF: Greece's €17.8bn EU Recovery and Resilience Facility allocation (one of the highest per capita in the EU) is the dominant macro tailwind; Gek Terna is structurally positioned as the primary execution vehicle [#A] ⚡
  • Sub: RRF disbursement timeline runs to 2026-27 (deadline extended post-COVID); estimated €5-8bn in infrastructure projects requiring contractor-scale execution
  • Sub: This is not cyclical public spending — it is EU-guaranteed capital injection with virtually no sovereign credit risk to Gek Terna's receivables
  • Macro shift — interest rates: Rising EuroZone rates in 2022-24 increased project finance costs for renewables SPVs and concession refinancing; a rate-cut cycle (2024-26) would materially improve project economics [#A]
  • Sub: Every 100bps reduction in project finance rates adds ~€15-25m to Terna Energy's annual FCF on its current debt stack (estimated €1.5-2bn renewable project debt)
  • Sub: GEK Terna's concession SPV debt is largely floating-rate or refinancing in 2025-27 — rate sensitivity is a live two-sided lever
  • Macro shift — Greek GDP: Greece has outgrown the EU average since 2022, driven by tourism, shipping, and FDI; construction activity is correlated with GDP growth and is supportive of Gek Terna's backlog execution [#A]

External Non-Macro Changes

  • Regulatory shift — renewables: Greek government has accelerated renewable licensing (Law 4685/2020 reformed permitting) and targets 65% renewable penetration by 2030; this structurally grows the Terna Energy TAM [#A]
  • Sub: Simplified licensing reduces development lead times from 7-10 years to 3-5 years — a meaningful pipeline velocity improvement
  • Political shift: Greece's New Democracy government (Kyriakos Mitsotakis, re-elected 2023) is explicitly pro-investment and pro-infrastructure; political support for RRF execution and privatisation of state assets (airports, ports) is GEK Terna-positive [#A]
  • Sub: Airport privatisation pipeline (beyond Makedonia) may create additional concession opportunities for GEK Terna in its home market
  • Disruption — EV / traffic patterns: Long-term secular risk to motorway toll revenues as EV penetration rises and autonomous vehicle/ride-sharing changes travel patterns; Attiki Odos concession (expiry mid-2030s) spans the critical EV transition window [#A]
  • Sub: Near-term (5-year) impact is minimal; 10-year impact is a legitimate discounting question for concession terminal values
  • Peer context note: No external cohort or sector peer research was provided for this run; industry benchmarks above are sourced from AS research on GEK Terna coverage and general sector knowledge. Peer context gaps flagged in QC commentary.

Sceptical bullet: The bearish cross-cutting read on industry context is that the RRF tailwind is a one-time stimulus that creates a backlog bubble rather than a structural re-rating of Greek contractor competitive positions; once the RRF window closes (~2027), GEK Terna reverts to a sub-scale Greek contractor in a structurally challenged construction market, while simultaneously facing intensifying pan-European competition in the renewables auctions that underpin its growth narrative [#J]
