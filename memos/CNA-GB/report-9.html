§1.2 — Trajectory Bullets

  • L3Y revenue CAGR: Revenue grew from £15.5bn (FY2021) to £26.2bn (FY2022) to £22.0bn (FY2023) to ~£20.0bn (FY2024); the FY2021–FY2024 CAGR of ~+9% pa is entirely commodity-price-driven, not volume or structural [#A]

  • Revenue at FY2024 levels (~£20bn) is declining toward FY2021 base as energy prices mean-revert; organic revenue (ex-commodity pass-through) is roughly flat over L3Y [#A]

  • L3Y EPS CAGR: Adjusted EPS moved 6.1p (FY2021) → 20.3p (FY2022) → 21.5p (FY2023) → ~9.7p (FY2024); peak-to-trough decline of ~55% in a single year destroys any simple CAGR framing [#A]

  • FY2021-to-FY2024 adjusted EPS CAGR is approximately +17% pa — but this masks the spike-reversion; a more honest FY2019-to-FY2024 normalised CAGR would be materially lower [#I]

  • Inflection point (positive): FY2022 was the clear positive inflection driven by the UK energy price crisis (gas/power market dislocations benefiting Centrica's upstream Spirit Energy and British Gas retail supply margin); this was NOT a management-execution inflection but a macro tailwind [#A]

  • Inflection point (negative): H1 2024 marked the negative inflection with energy retail margins compressing toward normalised levels and British Gas Services underperforming; FY2024 adjusted EPS of ~9.7p approximately doubled FY2019 levels (~4–5p), suggesting some genuine structural improvement beneath the noise [#J]

  • Operating leverage: Adjusted EBITDA margin expanded from ~7% (FY2021) to ~13% (FY2022) and has since compressed to ~8% (FY2024); margin expansion was entirely commodity-cycle-driven, not cost-structure or scale-driven [#A]

  • Trajectory verdict: The L3Y trajectory is characterised by a one-period extraordinary spike (FY2022), a partial persistence (FY2023), and a sharp normalisation (FY2024); this is a commodity cycle, not a compounding earnings story [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: What might invalidate this trajectory read? If Centrica's "structural" businesses (BG Services, nuclear, optimisation) are in fact performing better than the commodity noise suggests, the true underlying CAGR could be understated; conversely, if FY2024's ~9.7p adjusted EPS still embeds residual commodity windfall (above-normalised Spirit E&P margins), the true trough is below current consensus [#J]

§2 — Quality of Earnings

J-front verdict: Earnings quality is mixed; commodity FV movements dominate reported earnings and the gap between reported and adjusted EPS is material; cash conversion was strong in FY2022–FY2023 but partly for structural reasons that will not repeat [#J]

  • Non-GAAP gap: FY2022 reported EPS (~24p) vs adjusted EPS (20.3p); the £800m+ commodity fair-value movement reversal creates a persistent and significant gap between reported and adjusted figures across all periods [#A]

  • The adjustment philosophy strips out commodity FV movements, exceptional restructuring, nuclear decommissioning provisions, and pension charges/credits; these are directionally defensible but cumulatively large [#A]

  • Adjustments trend: ❌ The total magnitude of adjustments has been consistently large (£500m–£2bn range) across all L3Y periods, with the direction flip-flopping (FV gains in FY2022, FV losses as prices reversed in FY2023–FY2024); no visible trend toward cleaner reported vs adjusted convergence [#A]

  • Investors must rely on management-defined adjusted EPS as the "real" earnings number, which is an above-average reliance on non-GAAP metrics vs typical FTSE 100 industrials [#I]

  • Recurring vs one-off: British Gas restructuring costs have appeared in multiple periods (FY2022, FY2023, FY2024); these are treated as exceptional/one-off by management but represent an ongoing cost of operating a large-scale workforce in a regulated service market [#J]

  • Spirit Energy E&P asset impairment/reversal has also recurred; not truly one-off at the portfolio level [#A]

  • Cash conversion: FY2022 operating cash flow approximately £3.3–3.5bn vs adjusted operating profit ~£2.9bn; cash conversion >100% reflecting substantial working-capital release as energy payables settled and prepayments unwound [#A]

  • FY2024 operating cash flow ~£1.2–1.4bn vs adjusted operating profit ~£1.3bn; cash conversion ~90–100%; still adequate but the extraordinary NWC tailwind of FY2022 is definitively absent [#A]

  • NWC tailwind (FY2022): ⚡ RARE: During FY2022, Centrica benefited from an estimated £400–500m working capital release as British Gas customers prepaid (direct debit catch-up) and energy credit balances unwound; this was a genuine one-period structural tailwind that inflated FY2022 operating cash flow well beyond underlying earnings quality [Cross-ref: §1]

  • NWC headwind (FY2024): In FY2024, the reverse occurred — rising British Gas Services receivables and slower collections as customer volumes declined; NWC became a modest headwind, reducing cash conversion below the FY2022–FY2023 extraordinary levels [#A]

  • D&A trend: Depreciation and amortisation has been broadly stable (£400–500m pa range); no aggressive accounting acceleration or deceleration visible; Spirit E&P depletion is declining as the asset base shrinks, which will eventually reduce D&A and thus flatter future EBITDA-to-EBIT conversion [#I]

  • Pension: Centrica carries a large legacy defined-benefit pension surplus/deficit position; pension credits have contributed modestly to reported net income in FY2022–FY2023 (~£150–250m range), inflating reported EPS vs cash earnings; these credits are excluded from adjusted EPS but affect the reported/adjusted gap [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: What's the bearish read on earnings quality? The adjusted EPS figure management promotes strips out items that recur with notable frequency (restructuring, FV movements, impairments); if an analyst applies a more conservative "normalised" P&L, stripping recurring exceptionals over a 5-year cycle, the true earnings power may be 15–20% below management's preferred adjusted metric [#J]

§3 — Revenue vs Profit Growth (Margin Sustainability)

J-front verdict: Margin expansion was cyclical not structural; the FY2022–FY2023 EBITDA margin uplift is fully explained by commodity pricing and will not structurally repeat; the true structural margin question sits in BG Services, optimisation, and nuclear [#J]

  • Revenue CAGR vs Profit CAGR: FY2021–FY2023 revenue CAGR ~+26% pa; adjusted EBITDA CAGR ~+57% pa; EBITDA grew materially faster than revenue, implying operating leverage — but this leverage was commodity-pricing not scale or cost efficiency [#A]

  • The operating leverage mechanism was energy supply margin: as wholesale prices rose faster than retail tariff resets, Centrica's bought-forward hedges allowed it to capture the spread; this is the opposite of durable pricing power — it's a lag-effect windfall [#A]

  • Gross margin: FY2021 ~17%; FY2022 ~21%; FY2023 ~17%; FY2024 ~13%; gross margin is now below FY2021 levels, suggesting the structural gross margin of the business sits in the 13–17% range, not the 20%+ achieved in FY2022 [#A]

  • EBITDA margin: FY2021 ~7%; FY2022 ~13%; FY2023 ~11%; FY2024 ~8%; the FY2024 normalised EBITDA margin of ~8% is above FY2021 (~7%), suggesting modest genuine structural improvement (+100bps over 3 years) [#A]

  • This +100bps structural improvement is potentially attributable to BG Services restructuring benefits, HVAC/boiler services pricing, and efficiency in the energy supply operations; but it is modest [#I]

  • Cost cut driver: British Gas has executed significant workforce reductions (engineers, contact centre) over FY2021–FY2024; restructuring has delivered genuine OpEx savings, likely £100–200m pa cumulative, contributing to the structural margin floor being higher than FY2019–FY2020 [#J]

  • Mix shift: The revenue mix is shifting away from commodity-heavy supply toward services (BG Services), optimisation (energy management), and nuclear (Hinkley Point B completion, upcoming EDF partnership revenues); this is directionally margin-improving if executed, but L3Y data does not yet demonstrate the mix shift's margin impact cleanly [#I]

  • Sustainability assessment: The ~8% EBITDA margin in FY2024 is the honest starting point for sustainability analysis; a bull case sees this expand to 10–11% by FY2026 via BG Services re-pricing, nuclear output normalisation, and optimisation growth; a bear case sees further compression to 6–7% if energy retail margins continue declining [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: What's the bearish read on margin sustainability? British Gas residential energy supply margins are now at or below normalised levels, with Ofgem price cap dynamics limiting upside; BG Services is facing margin pressure from wage inflation and competition in the boiler/HVAC market; nuclear is subject to output variability; there is a plausible path to FY2025–FY2026 EBITDA margins of 6–7%, materially below FY2024 [#J]

§4 — Seasonal Patterns

J-front verdict: Centrica shows pronounced H1 weighting (winter energy consumption) in the British Gas residential supply business; this is a stable, structural seasonal pattern embedded in UK utility economics [#J]

  • H1 vs H2 pattern: British Gas Energy Supply margin is strongly H1-weighted given UK winter heating demand (Oct–Mar); H1 consistently contributes 55–65% of full-year adjusted operating profit in energy supply [#A]

  • H1 2022 adjusted EPS (~14p) vs H2 2022 implied (~6p from FY total of 20.3p) confirms this weighting pattern; H1 2023 (~10p) vs H2 2023 implied (~11.5p) shows some seasonal narrowing in the commodity normalisation phase [#A]

  • Q1 pattern: Centrica does not report Q1 standalone; H1 results (released ~July) capture the winter heating season and are typically the stronger half [#I]

  • Seasonality direction: The seasonal H1 bias is structural and embedded; it reflects UK energy consumption patterns, not a transitional feature [#A]

  • Seasonality changing: As Centrica's mix shifts toward BG Services (more even seasonal spread) and energy optimisation (trading-driven, less seasonal), the H1 bias should gradually moderate; L3Y data shows some early signs of this but the signal is weak [#I]

  • Cause — weather: Winter 2022–2023 was average; Winter 2023–2024 was warmer than average (contributing to H1 2024 revenue miss); weather is a genuine swing factor of ±5–10% on H1 energy supply margin year-over-year [#A]

  • Implications for investor: The pronounced H1 weighting means H1 results carry disproportionate earnings momentum significance; a warm winter is a material H1 headwind that consensus struggles to pre-price accurately; FY 2024 was partly impacted by exactly this dynamic [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: What's the bearish read on seasonal patterns? If UK winters continue warming structurally (climate trend), the H1 energy consumption bias will erode over a 5–10 year horizon, creating a secular headwind for the British Gas Supply margin component; this could represent a slow-burn derating of earnings predictability [#J]

§5 — Beat/Miss Pattern + Management Credibility

J-front verdict: Management has been a credible communicator throughout the commodity windfall period, consistently flagging normalisation and avoiding earnings extrapolation; beat/miss pattern favours modest beats in commodity-up periods and in-line to slight miss in the normalisation phase [#J]

  • Beat/miss frequency: Of 6 reporting periods L3Y (FY2021 base through FY2024): 4 periods saw adjusted EPS beat consensus (FY2022, H1 2022, H1 2023, FY2023); 1 period broadly in-line (H1 2024); 1 period slight miss (FY2024) [#J]

  • Beat rate ~67% of periods (4 of 6); miss rate ~17% (1 of 6); this is modestly above the FTSE 100 large-cap utility peer base rate of ~55–60% beat frequency [#I]

  • Beat magnitude (commodity windfall periods): FY2022 beat of ~+3.3p (+19%) and FY2023 beat of ~+2.5p (+13%) were substantial in absolute terms; these beats reflect management's deliberate refusal to upgrade guidance mid-year during the energy price spike, creating sandbagging-like outcomes without explicit sandbagging intent [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: FY2024 miss of ~-0.3p (-3%) on adjusted EPS is modest; the miss was driven by BG Services margin shortfall and nuclear output variability; management flagged both risks explicitly at H1 2024, reducing the element of negative surprise [#A]

  • Sandbagging signal: Centrica management does not provide explicit quarterly/half-year EPS guidance; they provide directional language ("normalisation expected," "above-normalised conditions persisting") rather than point estimates; this reduces formal sandbagging opportunity but also limits precision of consensus anchoring [#I]

  • Guidance accuracy: On structural metrics (BG Services operating profit, nuclear output, Spirit production guidance), management accuracy has been mixed; BG Services has missed guidance on margin delivery in 2 of the last 3 years (FY2022, FY2024), which is a specific credibility concern on the key structural division [#J]

  • Spirit Energy production guidance has been broadly accurate; nuclear output has been subject to unplanned outages creating forecast errors [#A]

  • Sell-side revision pattern (post-FY2023): Following FY2023 results, consensus FY2024 EPS was revised down modestly (approximately -5 to -8%) by covering brokers including Barclays [AS-Barclays] and RBC [AS-RBC], reflecting early recognition that normalisation would be sharper than prior estimates [#J]

  • Post-FY2024 results, consensus FY2025E EPS was revised down approximately -5 to -10% by Jefferies [AS-Jefferies] and Morgan Stanley [AS-MS], reflecting BG Services disappointment [#A]

  • Trend in accuracy: Management guidance accuracy on group-level earnings was highest during the commodity windfall (when they correctly refused to extrapolate peak margins); accuracy on structural divisional targets (BG Services margin improvement trajectory) has been below-expectation; the most important forward credibility question is whether BG Services can deliver the margin recovery management describes [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: What's the bearish read on management credibility? The L3Y beat track record was manufactured by extraordinary external conditions, not management insight; the periods where management had genuine control (BG Services margin restoration, nuclear reliability) show below-guidance delivery; sandbagging "cushion" in the commodity-windfall period is now exhausted, and the structural business credibility has not been established on a multi-year basis [#J]

  • Mgmt commentary: FY2025 guidance framed as "meaningful improvement" in BG Services; nuclear contribution expected to grow (Hinkley Point C JV progression); language is constructive but lacks specificity on magnitude [#J]
  • Sceptical bullet: Across the per-period narrative, the dominant bearish thread is that every positive surprise in FY2022–FY2023 was commodity-driven and every structural target (BG Services margin, nuclear reliability) has delivered below plan; the forward bull case is therefore being built on the divisions with the worst recent L3Y track record [#J]

  • Pre-release revision (FY2023 cycle): Going into FY2023 results, consensus was pulled back modestly (-5 to -10% on FY2023E EPS) through H2 2023 as energy prices declined; analysts correctly anticipated direction of travel but under-estimated pace of normalisation [#A]

  • Estimate dispersion (FY2024 cycle): Going into FY2024 results, analyst estimate dispersion was higher than typical; range of FY2024 adjusted EPS estimates was approximately 8p–13p, reflecting genuine uncertainty about pace of BG Services recovery and nuclear output; this wide range signals analyst uncertainty, not consensus [#A]

  • Post-FY2024 revision: Following FY2024 results (~9.7p adjusted EPS), FY2025 consensus was revised down approximately -5 to -10% by covering brokers; the direction of revisions is negative but magnitude contained — analysts still expect BG Services recovery in FY2025 [#A]

  • Revision pattern assessment: Sell-side analysts covering Centrica are reactive to commodity prices (as expected for a utility) but have been systematically slow to reflect structural divisional underperformance (BG Services); this creates a mild downward revision risk if BG Services margin recovery disappoints again in FY2025 [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: What's the bearish read on sell-side revision pattern? If BG Services is structurally challenged (competitor pressure, wage inflation, customer churn) rather than cyclically delayed, the ~2–3p of EPS improvement embedded in FY2025–FY2026 consensus from BG Services recovery will be revised out, creating a further -10 to -15% downgrade cycle in consensus estimates [#J]

§8 — Forward Plausibility

J-front verdict: The L3Y track record provides weak support for a forward EPS recovery thesis; the required forward trajectory (stabilisation at 10–14p range) is plausible but rests on divisions that have consistently underdelivered structural targets [#J]

  • Track-record support: Adjusted EPS FY2024 ~9.7p; consensus FY2025E approximately 10–12p; the forward thesis requires ~0–25% EPS growth from FY2024 levels — modest in absolute terms but requires BG Services margin recovery that has not yet been demonstrated [#A]

  • Required CAGR: For a 25% IRR at ~130p share price, Centrica would need either: (a) EPS of ~16–18p by FY2027 (+17–22% pa CAGR from FY2024 base) or (b) significant re-rating from current ~13x forward P/E; neither L3Y EPS track record nor current structural momentum clearly supports the ~17–22% CAGR scenario [#J]

  • Achievable from L3Y: The L3Y track record shows Centrica CAN generate 15–21p adjusted EPS but only under extraordinary commodity conditions; the structural earnings base (absent commodity windfall) is best evidenced by FY2024's ~9.7p — and the bull case requires doubling this in ~3 years [#J]

  • Acceleration required: Achieving ~16–18p by FY2027 requires simultaneously: BG Services margin restoration to ~6–8% (from current ~3–4%), nuclear output growth (Hinkley Point C contribution), Spirit E&P maintenance above decline, and optimisation profit growth; all four must deliver concurrently [#A]

  • Margin runway: From FY2024's ~8% EBITDA margin, structural improvement to ~10–11% is theoretically achievable but would represent the highest sustained non-commodity margin in Centrica's post-demerger history; comparable UK utility peers (SSE, National Grid) trade at higher multiples partly because they have demonstrated this level of margin stability [#I]
