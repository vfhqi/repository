2. KEY FINDINGS - Predominantly SEK-reporting, multi-currency-revenue business: Volati reports in SEK but earns meaningful revenue in EUR, NOK, DKK, and smaller exposures in USD and GBP; the company is therefore a net importer of non-SEK revenue translated into a volatile reporting currency, creating material translation exposure that management does not fully hedge

  • Natural hedging is partial and segment-uneven: Several business areas (e.g. Tornum export sales, Dampf/industrial segments with EUR-denominated customers) have revenue in EUR or NOK but localised SEK cost bases, creating genuine transaction exposure; by contrast, Stala/Consumer Products has more local Scandinavian revenue-cost matching; the portfolio aggregation masks the segment-level mismatches

  • Translation is the dominant exposure channel: Volati is a Swedish acquirer of predominantly Nordic/European businesses; translation of non-SEK subsidiaries into SEK is the primary FX channel, not transactional hedging of cross-border invoicing; this means SEK strengthening is a direct top-line and EBITDA headwind with limited financial-hedge offset

  • No published per-currency EPS sensitivity: Management does not provide a consolidated currency-sensitivity table in the annual report; RESEARCHER has triangulated sensitivity from geographic revenue disclosure and estimated margin profiles — these are analyst estimates, not company-disclosed figures

  • Hedging policy is modest and transaction-focused: Volati's stated policy addresses transaction exposures at the subsidiary level; there is no disclosed group-level translation hedge; hedge ratio, horizon, and instrument detail are not quantified in public filings — a transparency gap relative to sector best practice

  • SEK/EUR is the single most important currency pair: EUR revenues estimated at 15-20% of group, NOK at ~15%, DKK at ~8-10%; combined non-SEK revenue is estimated at 40-50% of group; this means a 10% move in the SEK trade-weighted basket against EUR/NOK/DKK has a meaningful ~4-5% impact on reported revenue and ~3-4% on reported EBITDA

  • FX has been a modest net tailwind in 2022-2023, turning more neutral in 2024: SEK weakness against EUR/USD in 2022 and early 2023 inflated translated non-SEK earnings; the SEK recovery in late 2023-2024 has partially unwound this; the 2025-2026 macro outlook for SEK is mildly SEK-strengthening (rate convergence, current-account normalisation), which represents a modest headwind vs the 2022-2023 tailwind base

  • Thesis FX-resilience is medium: The Volati thesis rests on organic + acquisitive compounding; FX adds ~±3-5% annual earnings variability around the organic growth trend; this is manageable but not trivial — at the margin, a sustained 10-15% SEK appreciation would subtract ~1.5-2.5 SEK/share from EPS trajectory in the near term

  • Key risk is a structural SEK appreciation scenario: If the Riksbank's rate normalisation proceeds faster than ECB's, SEK could appreciate materially against EUR/NOK; this is the single tail risk that would pressure both the revenue translation line and the acquisition-price arbitrage (Volati acquires Nordic businesses using SEK equity — a stronger SEK improves acquisition purchasing power but also pressures reported results if EUR-denominated EBITDA shrinks on translation)

  • Disclosure quality is below best practice: Volati does not publish a consolidated currency sensitivity table, does not disclose hedge ratios or durations at group level, and does not separately quantify FX-driven vs organic growth contributions in management commentary — all three are standard practice among comparable European diversified industrials

Revenue Currency Analysis

  • Concentrated in Nordic basket: ~37% of revenue is in the NOK/DKK/EUR Nordic-bloc currencies, which are themselves partially correlated (DKK is EUR-pegged; NOK has historically tracked EUR with commodity overlay); this means a broad SEK appreciation against the Nordic basket is the primary FX channel, not a diversified multi-currency scenario

  • EUR is the most economically significant single foreign currency: At ~18% of group revenue, a 10% EUR/SEK move translates to ~1.8% reported revenue move (translation-only, before transaction effects); EUR exposure is rising as Volati expands into European export markets through Tornum and acquires businesses with EU customer bases

  • NOK is the second-largest exposure: Norway represents ~14% of revenue; NOK/SEK is a historically volatile pair (range ~0.90-1.05 NOK/SEK L5Y); a reversion to cycle-low NOK (as in 2020 commodity shock) would represent a ~15% NOK/SEK decline, implying a ~2.1% reported revenue headwind on this segment alone

  • Mix trend is toward higher non-SEK over time: L3Y data suggests a ~1pp/year drift toward non-SEK revenue, driven entirely by acquisitions; organic growth is broadly currency-neutral within segments; this means the FX exposure slowly increases as the acquisition programme continues — a feature of the business model worth monitoring

  • Geographic mix L3Y: Sweden has declined from ~58% to ~55% of revenue; Nordic-ex-Sweden (NO+DK) stable at ~22%; Continental Europe (EUR bloc) rising from ~16% to ~18%; tiny but growing USD/GBP; the acquisition pipeline suggests continued drift toward EUR and potentially CEE

  • FX-driven vs organic growth L3Y: In FY2022, SEK weakened ~8% vs EUR and ~4% vs NOK; this provided an estimated ~2.5-3.0% FX tailwind to reported group revenue that year; in FY2024, partial SEK recovery reduced this tailwind to an estimated ~0.5-1.0%; Volati does not separately quantify FX-driven growth contributions in management commentary, which is a disclosure gap

  • Constant-currency growth: Is not disclosed by Volati; RESEARCHER triangulates: FY2023 reported revenue growth ~+15% (acquisition-driven); estimated FX contribution ~+1.5%; constant-currency organic growth ~+8-9%; management's "organic growth" metric excludes acquisitions but does not exclude FX — a subtle presentation point that slightly flatters organic growth figures in a SEK-weak year

  • USD exposure is small but disproportionately important for Tornum: Tornum's grain-handling equipment has global export customers who may invoice in USD or EUR; even at ~3% of group revenue, USD/SEK volatility can affect Tornum's order book attractiveness to international customers; USD at current levels (~10.5 SEK) represents a moderate tailwind for Tornum export competitiveness

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on revenue-currency mix is that the apparent "Nordic diversification" (NOK + DKK) is not true diversification — DKK is pegged to EUR and NOK is highly correlated with EUR in non-crisis periods, meaning a broad SEK appreciation event hits all three simultaneously; the effective EUR-bloc exposure is closer to ~40% of revenue (EUR + DKK + correlated NOK), not the ~18% pure-EUR figure, and a simultaneous EUR/NOK weakness against SEK is precisely the scenario that would occur if a Scandinavian macro shock elevated safe-haven SEK demand

Cost Currency Analysis

  • Personnel is the largest cost category and is well-naturally-hedged: The majority of Volati's ~3,400 employees (AR24) are located in the same geographies as their revenue-generating operations; Norwegian staff serve Norwegian customers; this geographic matching provides natural hedging on the largest single cost line

  • COGS is partially mismatched: Some subsidiaries source materials cross-border — industrial components from German/EU suppliers (EUR-denominated COGS against SEK or NOK revenue) and imported raw materials where USD is the reference price; this creates modest but real transaction exposure, particularly in industrials businesses

  • Tornum is the most exposed business unit on a transaction basis: Tornum manufactures grain-handling equipment in Sweden (SEK costs: labour, Swedish steel, domestic suppliers) but sells to export customers in EUR, USD, and other currencies; this is a genuine SEK-cost/EUR-revenue mismatch; when SEK strengthens, Tornum's export competitiveness and margin deteriorate simultaneously — a classic transaction exposure

  • Consumer Products (Stala) is the best naturally-hedged segment: Stala predominantly serves Swedish customers with Swedish manufacturing costs; limited FX transaction exposure; the main FX risk here is translation of any small non-Swedish operations, not transaction

  • Natural hedge ratio — group level: RESEARCHER estimates the overall natural hedge ratio at ~70-75% of foreign-currency revenues covered by matching foreign-currency costs (primarily because international subsidiaries are self-contained); the residual ~25-30% represents genuine transaction exposure (Tornum exports + centrally-purchased materials) — this is a manageable but non-trivial ~10-12% of group revenue exposed transactionally

  • Capex currency mismatch: Capital equipment is often EUR-denominated even for Swedish operations; this creates periodic EUR/SEK transaction exposure at investment phase; given Volati's active acquisition programme, integration capex can temporarily elevate transaction exposure in post-acquisition years

  • Group overhead is a SEK black hole relative to non-SEK earnings: Stockholm HQ costs (~SEK 80-100m estimated from AR24 central costs) are entirely SEK; when non-SEK subsidiaries earn EUR/NOK/DKK that is translated to SEK and then absorbed by SEK overhead, the overhead coverage ratio improves in a SEK-weak environment and worsens in SEK-strength — a second-order effect but notable

  • Input cost inflation via USD-denominated commodities: Several Volati subsidiaries (industrial, construction-adjacent) use steel, energy, and chemicals where global pricing references USD; even if invoiced in SEK domestically, the underlying cost driver is USD-linked; this creates a USD correlation in COGS that is not captured in a simple geographic cost analysis

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on cost-currency exposure is that the "natural hedge" narrative overstates protection at the segment level because Tornum — arguably Volati's highest-margin, highest-organic-growth business — is precisely the unit with the worst natural hedge (SEK costs vs EUR/USD revenues); the well-hedged segments (Stala/Consumer) have lower margins and lower growth, meaning the FX-exposed segment contributes disproportionately to the earnings growth story; a sustained SEK appreciation therefore hits the highest-quality earnings within the portfolio hardest

§3 — Translation Exposure

J-front verdict: Translation is the dominant FX channel for Volati; estimated FX translation added ~2-3% to reported revenue in FY2022 (SEK weakness), moderated to ~1% in FY2023, and turned broadly neutral to modestly negative in FY2024 as SEK partially recovered; translation has been a net contributor to the 2022-2023 EPS growth narrative

Translation Exposure Analysis

  • Reported FX impact L1Y (FY2024): RESEARCHER estimates translation added approximately +0.5% to group revenue in FY2024 as the SEK remained mildly weak vs EUR/NOK basket on a full-year average basis vs the prior year; the EBITDA translation impact is similar in proportional terms, suggesting ~SEK 30-50m of EBITDA was FX-translation-driven in FY2024

  • Reported FX impact L3Y summary:

  • Translation magnitude in an average year: Based on ~45% non-SEK revenue, a 5% SEK appreciation against the non-SEK basket reduces reported revenue by approximately 2.2% and EBITDA by approximately 2.0% (partially offset by non-SEK cost translation); a 10% SEK appreciation would reduce reported revenue by ~4.3% and EBITDA by ~3.8%

  • Reporting currency strength — the asymmetric risk: The 2022-2023 earnings narrative was partially flattered by a weak SEK environment; consensus EPS estimates may implicitly embed a continued-weak-SEK assumption; a mean reversion of SEK toward PPP-implied levels vs EUR (which are ~8-12% stronger than current spot) would mechanically subtract from reported EPS with no operational change at the subsidiary level

  • Balance sheet translation exposure (net assets): Non-SEK net assets translate into SEK equity on the balance sheet; a 10% SEK appreciation would reduce consolidated net assets by approximately SEK 600-900m (estimated ~35% of group net assets in non-SEK subsidiaries at book value); this is not a P&L exposure unless goodwill is impaired, but it affects the equity base and could have leverage ratio implications if net assets decline while SEK-denominated debt remains

  • Goodwill and acquisition multiples: Volati has made numerous acquisitions priced at EUR/NOK-denominated enterprise values; these goodwill amounts are now translated at closing-rate into SEK balance sheet; a sustained SEK appreciation does not automatically trigger impairment (the underlying businesses still earn local-currency returns) but makes the goodwill SEK-equivalent appear to decline, which is technically neutral but creates optics risk in capital-intensive periods

  • Management's disclosure on translation: Volati provides a qualitative note in the annual report acknowledging "significant exposure to movements in EUR, NOK and DKK" against the SEK; the company does not provide a quantitative sensitivity table on translation effect in SEK terms, nor does it separately disclose FX contribution to reported growth — a transparency gap that forces analyst triangulation

  • Translation vs acquisition-driven growth confusion risk: In years of SEK weakness + high acquisition activity, translation tailwinds are bundled into reported growth alongside acquisition contributions; constant-currency organic growth is materially lower than headline reported growth in such years; FY2022 is the clearest example — reported revenue growth of ~+35% included acquisitions (~+25pp), some translation (~+3pp), and organic (~+7pp)

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on translation is that three years of mild-to-moderate SEK weakness have embedded an earnings level (not just growth rate) that is ~5-8% above where it would be in a normalised SEK environment; if SEK appreciates 8-10% in 2025-2026 (within consensus range), the translation effect doesn't just slow growth — it actively reverses a multi-year embedded tailwind, creating a situation where reported EBITDA could decline even with mid-single-digit organic growth; this is the binary-event risk: a sharp SEK re-rating (e.g. triggered by Riksbank hawkishness or a normalisation of the SEK risk premium) could make FY2025 reported numbers look like a deceleration even if the underlying businesses are healthy

§4 — Transaction Exposure

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is real but concentrated in Tornum (export revenue) and selected COGS inputs (EUR-denominated materials for Swedish factories); the economic magnitude is manageable (~10-12% of group revenue at risk) but asymmetric — Tornum is the highest-quality earnings contributor, so transaction risk hits disproportionately where it hurts most

Transaction Exposure Analysis

  • Transaction exposure definition applied: Cash flows where Volati invoices customers in one currency but incurs costs in another currency, or where committed cross-border payables/receivables exist; distinguished from translation (which is purely a reporting-currency re-expression of foreign subsidiary earnings)

  • Primary transaction exposure points:

Exposure point Revenue currency Cost currency Estimated annual exposure Mechanism
Tornum export orders EUR / USD / SEK SEK (Swedish manufacturing) SEK 150-250m Swedish-made equipment sold to EU/global grain-handling customers
EU-sourced materials (industrial segments) SEK / NOK EUR (German/EU suppliers) SEK 80-120m German precision parts, EU industrial components
Intra-group service fees (Sweden HQ to subs) SEK NOK / DKK / EUR (subsidiary P&Ls) SEK 40-60m Group overhead allocation
Minor USD commodities (steel, chemicals) SEK USD-referenced SEK 30-60m Pass-through pricing to Swedish customers lags FX moves

Total estimated net transaction exposure: SEK 300-490m annually (~8-12% of group revenue). These are RESEARCHER estimates; Volati does not publish a transaction exposure quantum.

  • Tornum is the most significant single transaction exposure source: RESEARCHER estimates Tornum contributes ~SEK 150-250m of EUR/USD-invoiced export revenue against an almost entirely SEK cost base; at ~15% EBITDA margin for Tornum, a 10% SEK appreciation would reduce Tornum EBITDA by approximately SEK 15-25m (assuming no immediate price pass-through) — material relative to a group EBITDA of ~SEK 700-800m (AR24 estimate)

  • Pricing pass-through — Tornum: Grain-handling equipment is a project-based business with long order cycles (6-18 months between quote and delivery); FX moves during the project cycle cannot be fully passed through to customers; Tornum likely uses forward contracts on specific project cash flows (RESEARCHER cannot confirm from public disclosures) but the nature of the business creates irreducible transaction exposure on contracts in backlog

  • Pricing pass-through — industrial segments with EU-sourced materials: Volati's industrial subsidiaries (e.g. Swedish manufacturers with German component supply) have somewhat more flexibility to pass through input cost increases, as pricing is often annual or semi-annual; the lag is ~6-12 months; during a rapid SEK depreciation event (which inflates EUR-denominated material costs in SEK terms), there is a margin squeeze in the gap before repricing

  • Transaction vs translation magnitude comparison: Transaction exposure (SEK 300-490m of annual cash flows at risk) is smaller than translation exposure (SEK 3.0-3.5bn of non-SEK revenues translated annually) but the economic quality of the impact differs: transaction exposure directly affects cash margins and cannot be offset by cost-side translation; translation exposure affects reported numbers but the economic cash generation at subsidiary level is unaffected

  • Cost pass-through ceiling — competitive dynamics: In segments where Volati competes with European manufacturers pricing in EUR, a SEK depreciation actually improves Volati's competitive position (cheaper SEK-priced products vs EUR competitors); conversely, SEK appreciation erodes this competitive advantage; the pass-through ceiling in those scenarios is set by competitive pricing pressure, not customer willingness

  • Aggregate transaction exposure vs hedging programme: Given that Volati's disclosed hedging policy is modest and subsidiary-level, RESEARCHER estimates that perhaps 30-50% of identified transaction exposure is actively hedged at any point (forward contracts on confirmed export orders; see §5 for hedging policy detail); the residual 50-70% of transaction exposure (~SEK 150-340m) is unhedged at any time

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on transaction exposure is that pass-through pricing will fail precisely when FX moves are largest and fastest — a 15-20% SEK appreciation compressed into 6-9 months would create a margin squeeze at Tornum (project backlog invoiced in EUR with SEK cost overruns), simultaneous input cost pressure at industrial segments (EUR-material buyers), and a competitive disadvantage in export markets, all before any pricing adjustment could take effect; the company's project-based revenue at Tornum means the "repricing at next contract" safety valve operates on an 18-month cycle, not a 3-month cycle, compounding the margin impact timeline

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Volati's hedging policy is partial and operationally delegated — subsidiary-level transaction hedging is practiced but not quantified in group disclosures; translation hedging is absent at group level; hedge ratio, duration, and instrument mix are not disclosed; this places Volati in the lower tier of European industrials on hedging transparency

Hedging Policy Analysis

  • Disclosed policy — group level: Volati's annual report financial risk section states the group is exposed to currency risk through its international operations and that subsidiaries are expected to manage their own transactional FX risks; the group Treasury function sets policy guidelines but specific ratios and horizons are not publicly disclosed

  • Translation hedging — explicitly not done: Consistent with most European industrials of comparable size, Volati does not hedge translation exposure; the rationale is standard — translation hedging of equity is expensive and reverses over time; this is a deliberate policy choice, not a gap; the consequence is that the SEK/non-SEK translation effect flows through reported results unmitigated

  • Transaction hedging — subsidiary-level, forward contracts: The annual report notes the use of forward exchange contracts at subsidiary level to hedge committed or highly probable transaction cash flows; the specific businesses most likely to use these are Tornum (project-based export) and any subsidiary with significant cross-border supply chains

  • Hedge horizon — not disclosed: Volati does not publish a hedge horizon; RESEARCHER infers a typical horizon of 3-12 months for transaction hedging based on the nature of Tornum's project cycles and standard Nordic industrial practice; this is short relative to sector best practice (18-24 months for European industrials with material FX exposure)

  • Hedge ratio — not disclosed: Group hedge ratio against transactional exposure is not quantified; RESEARCHER estimates 30-50% of identified transaction exposure based on subsidiary-level implementation and the absence of a centralized treasury mandate; this estimate carries significant uncertainty

  • Hedge instruments — forwards only (inferred): The AR24 notes refer to forward exchange contracts; there is no mention of options, collars, or other derivative instruments; a forwards-only approach is cost-efficient but limits upside optionality in a favourable FX scenario (i.e. if SEK weakens after a forward is placed to sell EUR, Volati misses the upside)

  • Hedge effectiveness L3Y: Volati's IFRS notes (AR22-AR24) disclose realised and unrealised gains/losses on derivative instruments; from available disclosures:

  • FY2022: Net derivative gain — modest positive (SEK depreciation environment made EUR-sell forwards profitable for those covering EUR costs; those covering EUR revenues less relevant in a depreciating SEK)
  • FY2023: Net derivative position — small and within normal bounds
  • FY2024: Net derivative position — not material in disclosed notes

  • Assessment: The L3Y realised derivative P&L is small relative to the scale of non-SEK revenues, consistent with a partial/moderate hedge programme; there is no evidence of large hedge gains or losses that would indicate either aggressive hedging or significant hedge misalignment

  • Hedge cost — not separately disclosed: Forward contract costs (bid-ask spreads + rate differential from interest rate parity) are embedded in the effective exchange rate achieved; given modest hedge ratios, total annual hedge cost is likely sub-SEK 10-15m — immaterial at group level

  • Treasury centralisation — limited: Volati operates a decentralised conglomerate model; there is no evidence of a Group Treasury with sophisticated centralised hedging capability comparable to, say, a Sandvik or Atlas Copco; the CFO function likely coordinates policy but execution is subsidiary-level; this decentralisation is consistent with Volati's operating model but means hedging is less optimised than a centralised treasury

  • Disclosure against "boring hedger" standard: Applying the sector-general principle that "the boring hedger wins" (publishes quarterly hedge-position summaries, names horizon, reports realised effectiveness ratios), Volati scores poorly: no quarterly hedge summary, no named horizon, no realised effectiveness ratio disclosure; the FX risk note is qualitative-dominant with limited quantitative backing

  • Counter-cyclical vs pro-cyclical assessment: Cannot be determined from public disclosures; RESEARCHER notes that the absence of a centralised treasury mandate makes counter-cyclical hedging (buying protection when FX is cheap) less likely than reactive/pro-cyclical hedging (increasing cover when FX begins to move adversely)

  • Peer hedging philosophy comparison (no D-BATCH-25 peers available; named-sector analogues used): Comparable European diversified industrials with similar revenue-mix profiles (e.g. Indutrade, a Swedish diversified industrial comparable) disclose more on hedging; Indutrade's annual report includes geographic revenue breakdown by currency with clearer identification of hedged vs unhedged exposure; Lagercrantz Group (another Swedish serial acquirer comparable) similarly identifies its most significant currency pairs; Volati's disclosure lags both on transparency

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on hedging discipline is that "subsidiary-level hedging with group guidelines" is a description that can mean anything from a sophisticated, well-executed programme to a series of ad hoc forward contracts placed when subsidiaries feel exposed; the absence of any disclosed hedge ratio, duration, or effectiveness metric means Richard cannot verify that the programme is functioning as intended; in a stress scenario (e.g. rapid SEK appreciation), the fragility of a decentralised hedging programme — where different subsidiaries may have different hedge ratios, different counterparty banks, and different horizons — would become apparent precisely when centralised coordination is most needed
