§1 — General (Description, Financials, Peer Comparison)

Net signal: SES is a large-cap European satellite operator (~€2.5-3bn market cap) with an above-median EBITDA margin profile but below-median FCF yield due to structural capex intensity; financial profile shows legacy-revenue headwinds offsetting MEO growth, producing near-flat organic revenue — a classic transition-period financial signature [#J]

Description: SES operates two complementary satellite constellations — GEO (Geostationary Earth Orbit, ~50 satellites) and MEO (Medium Earth Orbit, the O3b and O3b mPOWER fleets) — serving broadcast/video distribution and data/connectivity customers globally [#I]
- Sub: GEO fleet carries broadcast/DTH (direct-to-home) channels and provides fixed/maritime/aeronautical data capacity globally
- Sub: MEO fleet (O3b acquired 2016, mPOWER constellation currently deploying) targets high-throughput, low-latency enterprise data, cruise/maritime, government, and telecom backhaul markets

Organisation: Two reporting segments since 2022 restructuring — (1) Networks (formerly Video+Networks combined) and (2) Space Infrastructure Services — though SES also reports the Intelsat merger as a distinct corporate track [#I]
- Sub: Networks = the revenue-generating satellite services business (Video + Data/Connectivity sub-segments)
- Sub: Space Infrastructure Services = a newly-created segment offering hosted payload / government capacity — smaller contributor at present

Listing: Euronext Paris and Luxembourg Stock Exchange; A-share ordinary + B-share depositary receipts (FDRs); EUR-reporting; Luxembourg legal domicile; majority-owned by Luxembourg government (~19%) + other governmental entities [#I]
- Sub: Government ownership structure provides a degree of political protection but historically constrains capital allocation flexibility vs pure-play public operators
- Sub: Market cap approximately €2.4-2.7bn as of Q1 2026 (Euronext Paris); EV approximately €4.5-5.5bn including net debt post C-Band proceeds

§2 — Company Details (Revenue Model, Business Model, Recent Delivery, Internal Change)

Net signal: SES's revenue model combines high-predictability GEO broadcast contracts (long-term, inflation-linked transponder leases) with less-mature MEO connectivity revenues — the mix is shifting adversely as Video declines faster than MEO ramps, producing a period of negative organic growth that breaks the predictability thesis temporarily [#J]

Revenue source: Core revenue = transponder/capacity leases (multi-year contracts, typically 3-10 year terms) — highly predictable in isolation; total backlog ~€4-5bn as of FY2024 (approximately 2.5x annual revenue) [#I]
- Sub: Backlog provides near-term revenue visibility but backlog roll-off in Video accelerating as contract renewals come at lower rates or non-renewal
- Sub: MEO backlog build slower than initially guided; mPOWER commercial service launch delayed multiple times (initial target 2022, actual ~late 2023/2024 partial)

Revenue mix — Video vs Data: Video (GEO broadcast, DTH distribution) ~35% and declining; Data/Connectivity ~50% and growing modestly; Government ~12% and stable-to-growing [#A]
- Sub: Video revenue is structurally pressured — European and US pay-TV ecosystems shrinking; channel capacity demand declining; renewal rates coming in at 15-25% below expiring contract rates per industry estimates
- Sub: Data revenue must grow ~8-12% pa to offset Video decline and produce flat group revenue — an ambitious internal offset rate ❌

Geography: Revenue roughly 1/3 Americas, 1/3 EMEA, 1/3 APAC+MEA+Gov; US government is largest single end-market (via O3b + GEO DoD contracts) [#I]
- Sub: US government concentration (~15-20% of revenue) provides some stability; US DoD satellite spending growing but O3b/mPOWER must win competitive procurement against ViaSat, Hughes, and emerging LEO providers

Predictability: GEO-Video contracts are highly predictable (long-term, high renewal switching cost for broadcast operators); MEO-Data contracts shorter and more competitive [#A]
- Sub: Weighted contract tenor ~3-5 years for Data vs ~7-10 years historically for Video; declining average contract length is a structural negative for forward visibility

Cyclicality: Low cyclicality in Video (secular decline, not cyclical); moderate cyclicality in Government (budget cycles); moderate cyclicality in Cruise/Maritime (economically-sensitive) [#I]

Customer behaviour: GEO Video customers (broadcasters, DTH platform operators) have high switching costs — renegotiating orbital slot, transponder frequency plan, and ground infrastructure is expensive; but contract non-renewal is rising as video volumes decline [#A]
- Sub: MEO Data customers (ISPs, mobile backhaul, maritime operators) have lower switching costs — contracts are shorter, multiple vendors available (including Starlink for maritime)
- Sub: NPS data not publicly disclosed; churn in Video not separately reported but implied by declining Video revenue at ~5-8% pa organically

Lock-in / switching costs: GEO broadcast: high — orbital position, frequency, ground system calibration; MEO data: moderate — terminal equipment lock-in but not as deep as broadcast; emerging LEO alternatives reducing MEO lock-in over time ❌ [#A]

Scale economics: SES's scale (50+ GEO satellites, 20+ MEO O3b/mPOWER satellites) creates fixed-cost absorption advantages in ground network operations, orbital management, and customer support [#A]
- Sub: However, scale advantages in satellite capacity are being eroded by LEO entrants whose cost-per-bit economics will improve materially over 2025-2030 (SpaceX Starlink capacity ~40-60 Tbps vs SES O3b mPOWER ~10 Tbps total throughput capacity)

Secular trend: DTH/broadcast (Video) is a secular-decline market (OTT streaming, cord-cutting); enterprise data connectivity is secular-growth (cloud, IoT, remote operations); cruise/maritime connectivity is secular-growth post-COVID recovery [#A]
- Sub: SES is positioned on both sides simultaneously — declining and growing verticals; the net trajectory depends on which side grows faster

Recent delivery — L3Y (FY2022-FY2024):
- Delivery — C-Band: Successfully executed FCC Accelerated Relocation of C-Band spectrum (phases 1+2 complete); received ~$3.97bn gross proceeds; delivered on time, a genuine execution success [#A] [CQ10 cross-ref]
- Delivery — mPOWER: ❌ O3b mPOWER ramp delayed — commercial service launch originally guided 2022, pushed to late 2023/early 2024; capacity utilisation ramp still in early innings as of FY2024 [#A]
- Delivery — Video: Managed Video decline broadly in line with or slightly better than guided; no major unexpected contract losses reported but renewal pricing drag ongoing [#I]
- Delivery — Intelsat merger: Announced merger agreement December 2023; regulatory review (EU, US FCC, other jurisdictions) ongoing as of Q1 2026; deal not yet closed; meaningful execution and integration uncertainty [#A]

Internal change — L12M and N3Y:
- Leadership change: Steve Collar, CEO since Feb 2018; long-tenure satellite executive (previously SES Chief Strategy Officer and prior O3b Networks). No CEO change flagged L12M [#I]
- CFO: Andrew Browne, CFO; relatively stable leadership team [#I]
- Strategic pivot: 2022 strategic announcement — "SES as a Networks company" — pivoting from satellite-asset operator toward managed solutions / end-to-end network services framing; attempting to capture more value-chain revenue [#A]
- Intelsat merger: Transformational if completed — would create a combined global satellite infrastructure group; strategic rationale = combined GEO + MEO fleet, cost synergies, combined US government position [#A]
- mPOWER commercial launch: Partial launch FY2024; full commercial service ramp target FY2025 — the single most important near-term internal execution milestone [#J]

Sceptical bullet: Bearish read — Video decline is irreversible and accelerating; MEO competitive moat is structurally weaker than management's "next-generation networks" narrative implies; Starlink/Kuiper are cost-per-bit disruptors that will compress Data pricing before mPOWER reaches full utilisation; Intelsat merger creates integration distraction at exactly the moment mPOWER ramp needs CEO focus; "managed services" pivot risks adding SG&A without proportionate revenue lift [#J]

§3 — Industry Context (Peer Comparison, Structure, Macro + Non-Macro Change)

Net signal: The commercial satellite industry is undergoing structural disruption from LEO entrants; GEO operators face a dual pressure of Video secular decline and data pricing compression; MEO (SES's differentiation) is a middle ground under squeeze from both GEO and LEO; industry structure has historically been oligopolistic but LEO entry is materially changing concentration dynamics [#J]

Market structure: Global commercial satellite services market (~$130-150bn total market including launch, manufacturing, services) is bifurcating — traditional GEO FSS (Fixed Satellite Services) ~$13-15bn pa revenue pool declining ~2-3% pa; broadband satellite including LEO growing rapidly [#I]
- Sub: GEO FSS industry top 5 historically = SES, Intelsat, Eutelsat, Telesat, ABS/Arabsat/HellasSat — but Intelsat restructured 2020, Eutelsat acquiring OneWeb 2023; industry is consolidating rapidly
- Sub: SpaceX Starlink now generates reportedly ~$6-8bn revenue (2024 estimates) and is the largest single satellite connectivity revenue generator globally — a ⚡ structural disruption that was not in SES consensus models 5 years ago

Concentration: GEO FSS top-5 share historically ~70-75% of GEO FSS revenue; but LEO entrants (Starlink primarily) are growing the total addressable market while simultaneously competing for data customers; effective concentration in the broader satellite connectivity market is falling sharply [#A]

Peer divergence: Eutelsat (post-OneWeb) has taken on significant leverage (~5-6x EBITDA) to fund LEO buildout; Telesat (Lightspeed LEO) has struggled with financing; Viasat operates a high-throughput GEO broadband niche but faces Starlink competition in consumer broadband; SES's MEO (O3b mPOWER) strategy is differentiated from both pure GEO and full LEO approaches [#A]
- Sub: SES's MEO low-latency positioning (~150ms RTT vs GEO ~600ms) is a genuine technical differentiator vs legacy GEO; but Starlink LEO (~20-40ms RTT) is lower latency still, undermining MEO's latency advantage in some applications

Competitive intensity: Rising materially — Starlink available in 100+ countries as of 2025; maritime and aviation Starlink service gaining share against SES's O3b maritime and aviation verticals; Amazon Kuiper anticipated at scale 2025-2026 [#A]

New entrant: ⚡ RARE: SpaceX Starlink represents the most structurally significant new entrant in GEO/MEO satellite history — cost-per-bit trajectory on LEO will decline ~70-80% by 2028 (SpaceX internal + industry estimates) vs SES O3b mPOWER's cost structure; SES's core competitive advantage in low-latency MEO narrows materially over the planning horizon [Cross-ref: §4.2 MOAT, §4.3 Competition]

Barriers to entry — GEO: High — orbital slot filing/coordination (ITU process), spectrum licensing, satellite manufacturing/launch cost ($200-500m per GEO satellite); regulatory approvals; SES holds strong orbital arc positions (e.g. 19.2°E, 28.5°E prime European arcs) [#A]
- Sub: BtE in LEO significantly lower per capacity unit — SpaceX's vertical integration (Falcon 9, Starship, in-house satellite manufacturing) reduces cost-per-satellite ~10-30x vs traditional operators

Barriers to exit: Low for contracts (transponder leases can be non-renewed); moderate for satellites themselves (stranded asset risk if revenue erodes before end of satellite life); orbital assets cannot be readily liquidated [#I]

Macro shift — macro tailwinds: Global enterprise connectivity demand growing ~10-15% pa; maritime broadband growth post-COVID; government/defence satellite spend growing (NATO commitments, Ukraine conflict demand, Space Force) [#A]
- Sub: USD strength benefits SES as significant revenue is USD-denominated (particularly US government, maritime); EUR/USD FX a modest tailwind in recent periods

Macro shift — macro headwinds: European broadcasting market contraction accelerating (streaming shift); emerging market telecom backhaul customers (a core O3b market) increasingly considering Starlink as alternative; inflation in launch costs (partially mitigated by SpaceX Falcon 9 reusability but Boeing/Arianespace cost increases) [#A]

Regulatory shift: EU orbital debris / space sustainability regulations tightening (ESA/EU Space Agency guidance); ITU spectrum coordination becoming more competitive as LEO constellations file for new spectrum; FCC's C-Band reallocation (completed for SES) was a specific US regulatory windfall unlikely to repeat [#I]
- Sub: US Space Force and allied government procurement rules increasingly favour multi-orbit solutions (GEO + LEO + MEO combined), which plays to SES + Intelsat combined if merger closes

Political shift: Luxembourg government's ~19% stake creates some insulation from hostile M&A but limits capital allocation dynamism; EU digital sovereignty initiatives support European satellite operators as strategic infrastructure [#I]

Thematic trend: "Multi-orbit" satellite solutions becoming the procurement norm for large enterprise and government customers — favours operators with both GEO and MEO/LEO assets; SES's mPOWER + GEO combination positions it as a multi-orbit provider without requiring LEO investment [#A]
- Sub: This multi-orbit thesis is SES's central marketing narrative for the N3Y — the credibility of this thesis vs Starlink's LEO-only simplicity is the key battleground for investor thesis evaluation

Sceptical bullet: Bearish cross-cutting read — industry structural dynamics are moving faster against SES than consensus prices; Starlink's cost trajectory will make MEO uncompetitive in broad enterprise connectivity by 2027-2028; the window for mPOWER to establish strong utilisation before LEO price competition arrives is 24-36 months at most; Eutelsat's failure to fund OneWeb effectively is a cautionary peer precedent for satellite transition overreach [#J]

§4.2 — Advantaged Business + SRCA (BB#2 TC2)

MOAT — Customer moats: GEO Video customers face high switching costs — orbital position, transponder frequency plan, ground station infrastructure; long-term contracts (7-10yr); DTH broadcasters deeply embedded [#A] [CQ16]
- Sub: MEO/O3b Data customers have lower switching costs — 3-5yr contracts, terminal hardware lock-in modest; Starlink maritime/aviation terminals increasingly competitive alternative
- Sub: Government customers have moderate switching costs (security-cleared infrastructure, procurement process lock-in) but budget cycles introduce risk

MOAT — Production moats: GEO orbital slots are ITU-coordinated scarce resources — SES's prime European arcs (19.2°E, 28.5°E) and US orbital positions are near-impossible to replicate [#A] [CQ17]
- Sub: O3b mPOWER operates in MEO spectrum that SES licenced early — a first-mover regulatory position; but LEO operators have filed ITU spectrum filings for large new constellations, increasing future coordination complexity
- Sub: Ground network infrastructure (15+ TT&C stations globally) represents production asset depth difficult for new entrants to replicate quickly

MOAT — Scale advantage moats: SES operates >50 GEO satellites + 20+ MEO satellites — scale in orbital management, ground operations, and customer support is real [#A] [CQ18]
- Sub: However, SpaceX operates ~6,000+ Starlink satellites (2025); absolute scale in LEO now completely dwarfs GEO/MEO operators; scale advantage is orbital-position specific, not capacity-volume based
- Sub: Combined SES+Intelsat GEO fleet would be ~100+ satellites — the largest combined GEO fleet globally; meaningful scale in GEO, but GEO is the declining segment

MOAT — Regulatory moats: ITU spectrum/orbital rights are a hard regulatory moat — filing process takes years; coordination agreements require incumbent consent [#A] [CQ19]
- Sub: C-Band spectrum held by SES (and Intelsat) was a specific US regulatory asset that generated the FCC windfall; no equivalent windfall identified in current asset base
- Sub: EU space sustainability rules and orbital debris regulations increasingly favour incumbents with compliant satellites over new entrants

MOAT — Culture moats: Engineering-first satellite operations culture; 35+ year operating history; relationships with ESA, European governments, and major broadcasters [#I] [CQ20]
- Sub: Not a top-tier culture moat (no Ryanair/Avanza-style outlier culture signal); competent and stable, not exceptional

MOAT — Relative scale: SES is #2 global GEO satellite operator by revenue (~€1.85bn) behind Intelsat (~$2.0bn pre-restructuring) and broadly comparable to Eutelsat (~€1.5-1.8bn post-OneWeb consolidation) [#A] [CQ21]

MOAT — TAM: GEO FSS market ~$13-15bn pa; MEO/satellite connectivity total addressable market (enterprise + government + maritime) potentially ~$30-50bn pa over 10-year horizon; but SpaceX + Amazon entering this TAM simultaneously [#A] [CQ22]

OPERATE (RA5) — high-level: Pricing power in GEO Video moderately positive (inflation-linked contract structures) but trend negative on renewal; in MEO Data, pricing is competitive and under pressure from Starlink; SCU analysis ESA-depth — Q8 deepens [CQ23-CQ26]
