BP has undergone material strategic and governance transformation over the past 36 months, marked by three CEO transitions since 2020 , the sudden dismissal of Chair Albert Manifold in May 2026 due to serious governance concerns, an aggressive $20bn divestment program , a fundamental reset away from energy transition back to hydrocarbons, and $6.04bn in low-carbon impairments exposing severe historical capital destruction. These cascading changes signal profound organizational disruption at a critical inflection point. The entry of Elliott Investment Management with a 5% stake worth $4.70bn in late 2024, demanding $20bn free cash flow by 2027 and structural separation into upstream and downstream units, has forced management to accelerate portfolio simplification and balance sheet repair. Management implemented a fundamental strategic pivot in February 2025, slashing transition capex from $7bn to $1.5-2.0bn annually while raising oil and gas spending to $10bn and cancelling 70% of pre-FID hydrogen projects . The cumulative effect represents a complete inversion of the prior decade's strategic direction, creating both execution opportunity and profound uncertainty around long-term competitiveness.

Materiality of Change Signal

BP has experienced frequent executive transitions with three CEOs since 2020 , including Murray Auchincloss's unexpected resignation in December 2025 and the appointment of Meg O'Neill as BP's first-ever external CEO in April 2026, representing the company's third chief executive in six years and indicating persistent top-level instability. The board unanimously dismissed Chair Albert Manifold in May 2026 following serious concerns regarding governance standards, oversight, and conduct , causing BP's American Depositary Share price to fall 3.85% to $42.65 and severely damaging investor confidence in board stability.

Multiple senior directors departed in April 2026 , including Melody Meyer, Karen Richardson, and Simon Henry, which deprives the board of energy and technology expertise at a time when activist pressure demands strategic accountability. Management announced an accelerated corporate restructuring in June 2026, shifting to two distinct operating segments (Upstream and Downstream) from the prior three-segment model, signaling a fundamental simplification of organizational complexity under the new CEO.

Expert calls covering BP and peers remain thin with only 3 calls across the 36-month window , and former directors noted that continuous reorganizations have significantly hindered operational agility and employee morale , raising culture execution risks.

Elliott Investment Management built a significant 5% stake worth $4.70bn by February 2025, demanding BP boost free cash flow to $20bn by 2027 through cost and spending cuts and advocating for structural separation into upstream and downstream units .

BP implemented a fundamental reset back to hydrocarbons in February 2025, slashing planned transition spending to $1.5-2.0bn annually (12% of overall capex) from $7bn previously while raising annual oil and gas capex to $10bn and cancelling 70% of pre-FID hydrogen projects and 40% of biorefineries .

Management is executing an aggressive $20bn divestment target by 2027 to strengthen the balance sheet, having already secured or completed $11bn in disposals including the Castrol stake sale and multiple refining asset exits.

BP recognized $6.04bn in low-carbon impairments in 2025, representing the largest transition write-down in the sector and exposing severe historical capital destruction from the abandoned green energy strategy.
