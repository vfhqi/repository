Section B; Investment case — Executive summary

BLUF. Hexatronic is a genuine, large change case on the right theme, attached to a weak-to-medium foundation, and it earns a Deep Dive rather than conviction. A structurally-growing Data Center / AI-fibre and defence engine, now the single largest profit contributor, is being built on top of a deliberately-shrunk legacy fibre cash cow, by a decisive new management team that has delivered its cost programme ahead of plan. The change is real and beginning to show in the numbers, which clears the Early-Stage Assessment test on the change leg. The foundation leg does not yet clear it. The business is structurally sub-scale with no upstream glass and a permanent margin ceiling; the growth is bought rather than earned, dependent on three to six acquisitions a year and on a roughly one-in-three joint probability across the company's own five drivers; the operator is unproven, a new chief executive with no fibre and no prior public-company-chief-executive record, and there is no anchor shareholder; and the outputs are bottom-quartile against the cohort on a single-broker coverage base. The recommendation is to progress to Deep Dive to stress-test exactly those three mediocrity risks against the gate, not to back the case here. This is a B-on-change, C-on-bankability profile, and conviction is medium.

The case in one line: the right theme and a real, large change, sitting on a business and an operator that have not yet earned the benefit of the doubt, so the Deep Dive, not the Early-Stage Assessment, is where it is won or parked.
  • The change is large and on the right theme, the single strongest point in the case: Data Center grew +62% organically in Q4-25 and +20% in Q1-26 and is now the largest absolute EBITA contributor at SEK 73m, on an AI-fibre tailwind (72-GPU clusters need 16x more fibre, data-centre capacity 2.7x to 219GW by 2030) that is one of the more durable in the universe, and the change is internal as well as external: a new chief executive, a reset strategy, a delivered cost programme, and an explicit M&A engine
  • The base the change acts on is impaired, which is why the change is not yet bankable: group adjusted EBITA margin has fallen from 15.4% (2023) to 8.4% (2025), legacy Fiber Solutions sits at 6.2% with the European region down 31% organically in Q1-26, and the recovery to a 12% group margin by FY27 depends on the cost savings sticking and the legacy cash holding up while the mix shifts
  • The growth is bought, not earned, and that is the core reservation: the SEK 3bn 2028 Data Center target needs three to six acquisitions a year at 5 to 7x funded by a declining legacy and recurrent equity issuance (SEK 600m in May-26), while consensus organic growth is only +5 to 7%, so the gap to the ambition is M&A that has to execute flawlessly, what the Business Foundation report itself calls growth that is "heroic, not mechanical"
  • The operator is the swing variable and the evidence is thin: the new chief executive (from March 2025, ex-Ansell, no fibre and no prior listed-company-chief-executive role) has delivered the cost programme decisively and allocates capital with discipline (deals at 5 to 7x, transparent three-segment reporting), but there is no anchor shareholder above ~10% of votes, board and management own only ~13%, and the supporting expert validation is all 2023-vintage, so conviction on the management leg is medium not high
  • The business has a real niche moat but a structural ceiling, and the Deep Dive must weigh the two: the customer lock-in is genuine and expert-confirmed (a 20 to 30% installed-labour saving, blown-fibre training stickiness, Harsh-Environment certification lock-in, embedded Day-2 Data Center teams), but Hexatronic owns no upstream glass, is ~17 times smaller than Corning, and competes from the lower-margin assembly and integration layer of the value chain
  • The outputs are bottom-quartile versus the cohort and the coverage is thin, which caps conviction: Hexatronic was the only peer with twelve-month consensus revisions down, its FY26E EBITDA margin of 9.6% sits roughly 840 basis points below the sector at ~18%, and the only price targets in the corpus are from a single sponsored broker (Redeye), with no street consensus and no rating mix to triangulate against
  • The valuation skew is positive but unbankable here: at ~SEK 31.5 the stock sits below Redeye's SEK 37 base and well below the SEK 62 bull (+97%), with a SEK 19 bear (-39%), a wide and positively-skewed range if the pivot proves out, but already up ~90% off its low to near the top of its range with no dividend to underpin total return while a holder waits
  • Action: progress to Deep Dive; the Deep Dive must resolve the operator track record (E9), the business-quality and scale ceiling (E10/E12) and the output predictability and false-friend test (E2/E7) against the mediocrity-shunning gate, and a C or below on any of those parks the case there

The cohort read. Graded against the cables and fibre-infrastructure cohort, Hexatronic is the high-change, low-quality member. Prysmian, Nexans and NKT are scaled, vertically-integrated, and expanding margins into the same supercycle (Prysmian grew +5% organically with an 11.5% EBITDA margin and saw its FY27 EBITDA estimates raised 15.5%; Corning grew +13% with a 22% margin and owns the glass), while Hexatronic was the only peer in the set with twelve-month consensus revisions down and sits ~840 basis points below the sector EBITDA margin. On Pillar I (the scale of the change) Hexatronic arguably screens better than the cohort, because its transformation from FTTH laggard to Data Center infrastructure name is more dramatic than the cohort's steady compounding. On Pillar II (the bankability of the change) it screens worse on almost every dimension that matters, which is the whole tension of the case and the reason the cohort itself may be the cleaner way to own the identical theme.

The bull and the bear, stated together. The bull, essentially Redeye's, is that the market is still anchored on the old FTTH-laggard regime and is mispricing a genuine re-rating: the Data Center engine is compounding at +62% and +20% organically, the cost programme is delivered, the mix shifts to 50% high-margin sales by 2028, and the bull SEK 62 implies +97% if the pivot proves out. The bear, essentially Deutsche Bank's and the Risks report's, is that the bull rests on a structurally declining legacy generating enough cash to fund three to six acquisitions a year while a fibre-inexperienced chief executive integrates them, on a ~33% joint probability across the company's own five drivers, with the entire price-target structure resting on one sponsored broker. Both are credible; the memo's read is that the bull is a real, positively-skewed path that is not yet bankable, which is exactly a Deep Dive, not an investment.

What would move the view. The grades lift if the Deep Dive's full Leadership and Risks reports show the chief executive's record and the board are stronger than the incomplete current evidence implies, if the niche lock-in proves durable enough to defend margins against the no-glass ceiling, and if independent coverage confirms the Data Center re-rating; on those, the case could clear the gate and progress to a position. The grades fall, and the case parks at the Deep Dive, if the false-friend signature dominates (delivery shown to lean on lowered expectations and acquired growth), if the legacy cash generation that funds the M&A engine deteriorates further, or if Data Center organic growth slips below the 15% level that makes the 2028 target unreachable without even more acquisitions.

Conviction and sizing read. Conviction is medium and deliberately so: the theme and the change are high-conviction positives, the foundation and the operator are medium-to-low-conviction unknowns, and the netting of the two is a case worth the next stage's work but not yet worth capital. If the Deep Dive clears the gate, the right entry is a small, thesis-confirmation-led tranche rather than a momentum-led one, because the stock has already rallied ~90% off its low to near the top of its range and the upside the bull relies on is unbankable until the street, not one broker, confirms it.
