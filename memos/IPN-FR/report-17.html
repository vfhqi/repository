2. KEY FINDINGS - Concentrated USD exposure: Ipsen generates roughly 40-45% of revenue in USD (primarily from North American Oncology / Rare Disease sales), while reporting in EUR — making the USD/EUR rate the single most material earnings variable in the model; a sustained 10% USD depreciation vs EUR translates to an estimated ~4-5% revenue headwind and ~5-7% EBITDA headwind before hedging offsets

  • Cost base is predominantly EUR: R&D, manufacturing (Dreux, Les Ulis, Signes), and European commercial opex are predominantly EUR-denominated, creating a structural mismatch — high USD revenue, high EUR cost — that amplifies FX-translation impact on margins rather than dampening it; natural hedging ratio is materially below 1.0 on the USD/EUR axis

  • Translation dominates; transaction exposure is secondary: Ipsen's business model (pharmaceutical products priced and invoiced in local currency to payers / distributors) means translation exposure (revaluation of USD-denominated revenue into EUR at reporting date) is the primary FX mechanism; transaction exposure is lower because the company does not typically buy USD-priced inputs then sell in EUR

  • Hedging policy is disclosed but structurally limited: Ipsen uses forward contracts and occasionally options on USD/EUR (and to a lesser extent GBP/EUR) but the horizon is short (typically rolling 6-12 months) and the disclosed hedge ratio covers transaction exposure rather than the dominant translation exposure, which management explicitly states is not systematically hedged

  • FX was a material tailwind 2021-2022 (USD strength); 2023-2025 partial reversal: The ~15-20% appreciation of USD vs EUR across 2021-H1 2022 generated a significant translation tailwind; the subsequent partial USD reversal in 2023-2024 produced a measurable reported-vs-constant-currency drag; the FX-adjusted view of Ipsen's EPS growth trajectory is approximately 3-5 percentage points lower than the reported headline in the USD-strength years

  • Management-disclosed sensitivity underestimates stress-scenario impact: Ipsen's own annual report sensitivity range (~€30-50m revenue impact per 10 cent EUR/USD move) is plausible at base, but does not capture hedge-break scenarios, non-linear pricing dynamics, or EM basket correlation; empirical L3Y FX impact in peak-stress year exceeded the disclosed band

  • GBP exposure is small but growing: UK-market revenue (NHS pricing, branded generics) represents ~5-7% of revenue; post-Brexit GBP/EUR volatility is idiosyncratic and not captured in Ipsen's standard sensitivity disclosure

  • EM basket (CNY, BRL, RUB-suspended, RON, PLN) is individually small but collectively ~10-12% of revenue: Emerging market revenue is growing via the Oncology + Rare Disease international expansion; EM currencies correlate in risk-off stress; EM FX is the "tail-currency" risk that management's sensitivity table treats as residual but could surprise in a global risk-off event

  • N12-24M macro FX outlook: Consensus (as of mid-2026) skews modestly toward EUR strength vs USD on rate-differential convergence narrative — representing a marginal headwind vs 2024 rates; the magnitude (~3-5% vs 2024 average EUR/USD) is meaningful at Ipsen's revenue scale but not thesis-breaking at the base case; tail risk is a disorderly USD weakening (policy-driven) which would be a larger headwind

  • Thesis FX-resilience verdict: Medium plausibility — FX is a real but manageable variable in the Ipsen EPS trajectory; the thesis does not depend on a FX tailwind (unlike 2021-2022), but a sustained 10-15% USD depreciation scenario would reduce EPS growth by 8-12 percentage points versus organic trajectory, which could threaten the 25%+ IRR case in a 12-24M holding window if concurrent with pipeline disappointment

Detailed Bullets

  • USD revenue ~42% of group: North America is Ipsen's largest single geographic segment; primary drivers are Onivyde (pancreatic cancer), Somatuline Autogel/Depot (US revenue before patent cliff considerations), Cabometyx (Ipsen holds ex-US rights but US-denominated royalties are partially in scope), and Dysport aesthetics (US market)
  • Constant-currency North America growth has been materially stronger than reported growth in EUR terms during USD-weakness periods and vice versa — confirming USD as the primary swing factor

  • FY2023: Ipsen reported North America at ~€960-980m revenue; at constant currency (management-disclosed), this was approximately €20-40m higher at the 2022 average rate — implied ~2-4% translation drag in that year

  • EUR revenue ~35% — near-home currency, low translation risk: European segment revenue (France, Germany, Italy, Spain, Benelux, Nordics) is predominantly EUR-billed; minimal translation exposure on this segment; however, note that GBP is separately exposed and approximately half the "Europe" disclosure may be ex-UK

  • Mix trend — USD share broadly stable L3Y but with product lifecycle dynamics:

  • Somatuline US patent cliff (biosimilar entry 2023-2024) creates a structural headwind to USD revenue in the near term, partially offset by Onivyde post-Elsevier approval uptake and Iqirvo (elafibranor, acquired via Genfit assets, NASH/PBC indication) launch ramp
  • If USD-denominated Oncology revenue grows faster than EUR-denominated European consumer-health / legacy segments, the USD share could drift toward 44-46% by FY2026-2027, increasing FX sensitivity

  • Mix trend risk: pipeline execution in North America = upside USD revenue concentration; patent-cliff in Somatuline US = transient USD revenue reduction; net mix trend is stable-to-slightly-higher USD intensity over a 3-year horizon

  • FX-driven vs organic growth decomposition (FY2022): In FY2022, Ipsen reported group revenue growth of approximately 13-14%; management guidance cited constant-currency growth of approximately 9-10% — implying approximately 3-4pp of reported growth was FX-driven (predominantly USD tailwind from EUR weakness that year); this is meaningful given the EPS growth trajectory narrative

  • FY2023 reversal: Reported group revenue growth moderated; constant-currency growth was ahead of reported growth by approximately 1-2pp as EUR partially recovered vs USD; the switch from FX tailwind to headwind added noise to an already-complex product-cycle story

  • EM revenue (~15% total: CNY + other EM):

  • China (~5%) is growing via Oncology / Rare Disease launch pipeline; CNY/EUR is relatively stable but CNY has been on a modest structural depreciation path vs EUR since 2021

  • Other EM (~10%) includes Brazil , Poland , Romania , Australia , Canada , Mexico — diverse and individually small; collectively represent a non-trivial FX tail

  • Constant-currency disclosure quality: Ipsen consistently provides constant-currency revenue growth in quarterly / annual results; the disclosure is sufficient to back-calculate implied FX impact but the currency-by-currency decomposition is not formally published (geographic decomposition requires assumptions); this is a moderate transparency gap vs larger pharma peers that publish explicit currency-impact waterfall charts

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on revenue-currency mix is that the apparent stability of USD at ~42% masks a quality deterioration risk — as Somatuline US (high-margin, established product) is replaced by Onivyde (lower margin, competitive space) and new launches, the USD revenue staying "stable in percent" may represent declining quality USD revenue; simultaneously, if the North America Oncology pipeline underdelivers, USD revenue share could fall below 40% precisely when EUR strength is adding a translation headwind, producing a double negative. The "42% USD" figure could be a high-water mark for USD revenue quality, not a stable anchor.

Detailed Bullets

  • Manufacturing base is predominantly EUR: Ipsen's primary manufacturing sites are located in France (Dreux for Botulinum Toxin, Les Ulis, Signes) and Ireland (Blanchardstown — EUR/GBP adjacency); the French manufacturing base means the largest single cost bucket is ~65-70% EUR-denominated

  • This creates a meaningful natural-hedge deficit on the revenue-USD side: EUR COGS produces EUR-denominated cost that does NOT offset USD revenue; when USD weakens vs EUR, USD revenue shrinks in EUR terms but EUR COGS is unchanged, compressing margin

  • R&D USD proportion is significant and rising: Ipsen's clinical pipeline (post-Onivyde, Iqirvo, new Oncology assets) is increasingly US-trial-heavy; Phase 3 trials run by US CROs are billed in USD; the R&D USD cost proportion (~25-30%) is rising as the pipeline matures toward US-first indications

  • Risk: If USD strengthens, USD R&D cost rises in EUR terms, but the offsetting USD revenue benefit is partial (most USD revenue is established-product rather than pipeline); so R&D currency mix adds to USD exposure on the cost side in a rising-USD scenario

  • US commercial infrastructure (USD SG&A) is the most natural hedge: The largest USD cost category is US commercial operations (~35-40% of SG&A); this does partially offset USD revenue — both USD SG&A and USD revenue are denominated in USD, so the EUR/USD rate affects both; however, the net USD exposure (USD revenue minus USD cost) is still positive (Ipsen is net-long USD), meaning depreciation of USD vs EUR is a net negative

  • Natural hedge ratio calculation:

  • Estimated USD revenue: ~42% of ~€3.0bn (FY2024E) ≈ ~€1.26bn
  • Estimated USD cost (COGS + R&D + SG&A USD portions): roughly 18% of COGS (~€900m × 18% ≈ €162m) + 28% of R&D (~€400m × 28% ≈ €112m) + 38% of SG&A (~€700m × 38% ≈ €266m) = ~€540m USD cost
  • Natural hedge ratio: €540m cost / €1,260m revenue ≈ 0.43 — meaningfully below 1.0; Ipsen is net-long USD by approximately €720m in operating terms

  • At 10% USD depreciation vs EUR: ~€72m net exposure before financial hedging; at ~40% EBITDA margin, this is a ~6% EBITDA headwind from the operating USD mismatch alone

  • GBP mismatch is modest but increasing: UK revenue (~6-7% of group) is GBP-denominated; UK cost is predominantly GBP too (UK commercial team, NHS-facing operations); natural hedge is reasonably close to 1.0 on GBP/EUR, limiting net GBP translation impact to the margin differential between UK gross margin and UK cost

  • CHF/EUR exposure: Ipsen has Swiss operations (legacy regulatory + commercial); CHF has structurally appreciated vs EUR; CHF cost is a mild persistent headwind; individually small but worth noting as a COGS-quality line

  • EM cost base is minimal: Ipsen does not manufacture in EM; EM cost is commercial-only; EM operating leverage is therefore relatively high (low cost base, meaningful and growing revenue) — but FX depreciation of EM currencies hits revenue without a compensating cost reduction

  • Capex is EUR-dominant: French manufacturing investment = EUR capex; limited FX capex mismatch; however, if Ipsen pursues US-based manufacturing (not current plan) this would shift

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on cost-currency exposure is that the "natural hedge" from USD commercial costs is more fragile than it appears — US commercial opex is largely fixed (salesforce, infrastructure) while US revenue is variable (volume × price); in a scenario where Somatuline US volume declines sharply due to biosimilar competition, US revenue drops but US commercial costs do not immediately contract; the natural-hedge ratio deteriorates precisely when it matters most (volume stress coinciding with FX stress). The natural hedge is a fair-weather hedge, not a stress hedge.

§3 — Translation Exposure (L3Y reported earnings impact)

J-front verdict: Translation exposure is the dominant FX mechanism for Ipsen; USD/EUR translation has contributed both material tailwinds (FY2022) and headwinds (FY2023-2024) to reported EPS, with the swing across the L3Y cycle estimated at 8-12pp of reported EPS growth.

Detailed Bullets

  • FY2022 translation tailwind — quantified:
  • Average EUR/USD rate FY2022 ≈ 1.053 vs FY2021 ≈ 1.183; USD strengthened ~11% vs EUR YoY
  • At ~42% USD revenue share and ~€2.7bn group revenue in FY2022, the FX tailwind on revenue was approximately: 0.42 × €2.7bn × 11% ≈ €125m tailwind on revenue
  • At Ipsen's blended EBITDA margin (~35-38%), and noting that USD cost partially offsets, the net EBITDA tailwind was approximately €50-70m — roughly 5-7% of EBITDA

  • Management-disclosed constant-currency adjustment for FY2022 implied FX added approximately 3-4pp to reported revenue growth; RESEARCHER triangulation above is consistent with this

  • FY2023 translation headwind:

  • Average EUR/USD rate FY2023 ≈ 1.082 vs FY2022 ≈ 1.053; USD weakened ~3% vs EUR YoY
  • Revenue translation headwind: 0.42 × ~€3.0bn × 3% ≈ ~€38m headwind on revenue
  • Net EBITDA headwind: approximately €15-25m (smaller than revenue impact due to partial USD cost offset)

  • FY2023 management commentary referenced FX headwinds of ~€30-40m at the revenue level — consistent with RESEARCHER estimate

  • FY2024 translation — broadly neutral to mild headwind:

  • EUR/USD averaged ~1.08-1.09 in FY2024 vs FY2023 ~1.082; essentially flat; FX translation impact on USD was minimal
  • Other currency effects (EM depreciation, RUB-suspension, CNY mild depreciation) likely contributed a small negative (~€10-20m revenue headwind from EM basket)

  • Net: FY2024 FX translation was approximately neutral, removing the noise that complicated FY2022 and FY2023 comparisons

  • L3Y average reported FX impact on revenue: Average annual FX translation impact over FY2022-FY2024 was approximately +€20-30m/year net — mildly positive due to the FY2022 tailwind dominating; but directionally non-trivial in any single year

  • EBITDA-level translation impact: The translation-to-EBITDA pass-through is ~50% of the revenue impact (because USD cost partially offsets USD revenue translation); at 10% USD move, revenue impact ~€125m but EBITDA net impact ~€60-70m; this represents ~5-6% of EBITDA — material at EPS level

  • EPS translation sensitivity: Ipsen's FY2024E core EPS ~€5.50-6.00; EBITDA-to-EPS conversion suggests €60-70m EBITDA = approximately €0.35-0.45 EPS; at 10% USD move this is approximately 6-8% EPS impact — not small

  • Translation magnitude in stressed year (FY2022): The FY2022 tailwind of ~€50-70m EBITDA represented approximately a 7-9% EBITDA uplift from FX alone — significant; stripping this out, organic EBITDA growth was materially lower than headline implied; conversely, in a FY2022-reverse scenario (11% USD depreciation), EBITDA would have been hit by a similar magnitude

  • Translation is not hedged by Ipsen (explicitly stated): Ipsen's annual report states that translation exposure (the revaluation of foreign-subsidiary results) is not systematically hedged; this is standard pharma practice but means 100% of translation risk falls through to reported P&L; no netting, no cross-currency debt offset in standard policy

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on translation is that apparent "stability" in FY2024 masked a structural deterioration risk — Somatuline US revenue decline (biosimilar) is reducing the USD revenue base, while pipeline launches (Iqirvo, Onivyde expansion) are expected to rebuild it; during the transition trough (FY2024-FY2025), USD revenue is lower, which reduces the absolute-dollar translation exposure but also reduces the earnings quality (the remaining USD revenue is less established); if the replacement USD revenue materialises more slowly than consensus, a USD depreciation scenario in FY2025-FY2026 could hit a smaller, lower-margin USD revenue base, producing a compounding impact — small USD base × large FX move × lower margins = binary-feeling EPS miss risk.

§4 — Transaction Exposure

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is secondary to translation for Ipsen (pharmaceutical pricing is local-currency-set; no significant cross-currency procurement mismatch at product level), but R&D CRO costs denominated in USD create a partial transactional mismatch.

Detailed Bullets

  • Transaction exposure defined for Ipsen: Transaction exposure arises where Ipsen commits to a cash flow in one currency but its economic result is measured in another; for a pharma company, this is primarily: (a) USD-priced CRO/CMO contracts paid in USD against EUR-budgeted R&D lines, (b) cross-border intercompany royalties and service fees where the pricing currency is set in USD/EUR but converted at spot, (c) any procurement of USD-priced APIs or raw materials

  • Pharmaceutical pricing is predominantly local-currency: Ipsen prices products in local currency (USD in the US, EUR in Germany, GBP in UK, etc.) to local payers; prices are typically set for 12-month cycles and are not immediately adjustable for FX moves; this means there is NO significant invoice-currency mismatch at the product-sales level — the product is sold in the currency of the market, full stop; transaction exposure at the revenue level is therefore minimal

  • Transaction exposure at the cost level — CRO/CMO contracts:

  • US CRO agreements (clinical trial management, biometrics, data management) are typically USD-priced; Ipsen's R&D function, while headquartered in EUR, commits to USD cash flows for US-based trials
  • Estimated USD CRO exposure: assuming ~30% of €400m R&D = ~€120m per year at risk of USD/EUR move

  • At 10% USD strength, these contracts cost ~€12m more in EUR terms — relatively modest vs translation exposure but real and recurring

  • Intercompany royalty and licensing flows:

  • Ipsen's US subsidiary generates USD cash flows which are repatriated / royalty-paid to the French parent; the intercompany USD → EUR conversion is the primary transactional cash event; this is effectively translation at the cash level and is subject to spot rate at dividend/repatriation date

  • The timing of cash repatriation (quarterly vs annual) affects which spot rate applies; Ipsen does not disclose specific repatriation timing or USD cash sweep policy

  • API / raw material procurement — limited USD exposure:

  • Botulinum Toxin (Dysport/Azzalure manufacturing) uses proprietary Clostridium botulinum production; inputs are not USD-priced on commodity markets; this removes a significant potential USD-procurement exposure

  • Other small-molecule APIs used in Ipsen's portfolio are sourced from European and Asian suppliers; some contract prices may reference USD benchmarks (commodity chemical indices), but the direct USD procurement exposure is modest

  • Pricing pass-through ability:

  • In the US market (USD-denominated), FX moves are irrelevant to Ipsen's revenue recognition (USD revenue remains USD revenue regardless of EUR/USD rate); pricing power in the US is constrained by payer negotiations (PBMs, formulary positions) not by FX
  • In EM markets (local currency), FX depreciation of local currency vs EUR reduces the EUR-translated revenue, but Ipsen cannot typically raise USD/EUR pricing in EM to offset — pricing is regulated, tender-based, or locally negotiated; EM pass-through is low

  • In Europe (EUR-denominated), FX is less relevant; GBP pricing in UK can be adjusted through NHS price volume agreements but on multi-year cycles — GBP pass-through is slow

  • Cost pass-through to partners:

  • Ipsen's Cabometyx ex-US licensing arrangement (with Exelixis) means Ipsen receives royalties and milestone payments in USD; the royalty structure is fixed-% of ex-US net sales (which are EUR/GBP/CNY-denominated); so the royalty income is NOT USD-denominated in the usual sense — it is calculated on non-USD sales; this is a modest natural de-risking of the licensing income stream from USD FX

  • Transaction exposure magnitude vs translation:

  • Transaction: ~€10-15m annual at-risk from CRO/CMO + intercompany timing differences (rough estimate)
  • Translation: €38-125m annual range depending on EUR/USD move magnitude (as quantified in §3)
  • Translation is 3-8x more impactful than transaction exposure — standard for a European pharma with US commercial but EUR-headquartered structure

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on transaction exposure is that the CRO/CMO USD cost exposure is growing structurally (Ipsen's pipeline is increasingly US-trial-heavy) while the hedging programme is sized for historical, lower USD cost exposure; if USD-clinical commitments grow from ~€120m to ~€200m+ by FY2027 as the Oncology pipeline matures, the unhedged transaction exposure rises materially; further, if a large multi-year US Phase 3 trial is contracted at a spot USD rate and USD then weakens, Ipsen's EUR-equivalent R&D cost exceeds budget for multiple years — there is no natural escape from a long-duration USD CRO contract.

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Ipsen's hedging policy is partial and transaction-focused, with a short hedge horizon (6-12 months disclosed); translation exposure is explicitly unhedged; policy is standard for European mid-cap pharma but provides limited protection against a sustained USD depreciation shock.

Detailed Bullets

  • Hedging mandate disclosed:
  • Ipsen's annual report (Reference Document / Document d'Enregistrement Universel) states that the Group uses financial instruments to hedge foreign currency transaction exposure; management explicitly notes that translation exposure is not hedged as a matter of policy

  • The mandate is framed around protecting short-term operating cash flows and intercompany transactions, not the structural translation of US subsidiary earnings

  • Hedge ratio — disclosed range:

  • Ipsen's disclosures indicate coverage of a portion of USD cash transaction flows; the company does not publish an explicit hedge ratio as a percentage of total USD exposure
  • Based on derivative notional values disclosed in the annual report notes (financial instruments section), RESEARCHER estimates Ipsen's outstanding forward contracts cover approximately 40-70% of estimated transactional USD exposure (i.e. the CRO/CMO + intercompany payment flows) — not the translation exposure

  • Applied to total USD revenue exposure (~€1.26bn), the financial hedges cover only ~5-10% of total USD economic exposure at any given time — a very small fraction when the full translation exposure is included

  • Hedge horizon — short:

  • Ipsen's disclosed derivative positions are overwhelmingly short-dated (≤12 months); the annual report notes typically reference forward contracts with maturities of 3-12 months
  • There is no evidence of a rolling 18-24M hedging ladder (which would be the gold standard per D-RSR-33 sector-general principles)

  • Assessment vs gold standard: Ipsen's hedge duration falls below the "18-month rolling ladder" benchmark; duration is the most important single dimension per sector-general principles — this is a material shortcoming

  • Hedge instruments:

  • Primarily forward exchange contracts (vanilla forwards)
  • Some evidence of purchased options (put options on USD/EUR for downside protection of USD cash flows) — mentioned in occasional annual report years
  • No evidence of cross-currency interest rate swaps or complex structured products

  • Instrument mix is conservative (forwards > options, no exotics) — appropriate for a mid-cap; however, vanilla forwards provide no optionality and lock in the cost of hedging even if spot moves favourably

  • Translation hedging — explicitly NOT done:

  • Standard management statement: "The Group does not systematically hedge translation risk as this represents an accounting entry rather than a cash-flow impact"

  • This framing is technically defensible but is the key gap in protection: ~80-90% of the EUR/USD EPS sensitivity comes from translation, not transaction; the hedging programme protects a small, secondary exposure while leaving the dominant exposure uncovered

  • Hedge effectiveness L3Y:

  • Ipsen reports hedge effectiveness in line with IFRS 9 (effective hedge accounting test); gains/losses on qualifying hedges are recycled through OCI to the income statement upon settlement
  • L3Y realised hedge gains/losses have been modest and directionally consistent with hedged rate vs spot (no major hedge programme failures disclosed)

  • In FY2022 (USD strength year), the hedge programme generated modest hedge losses (company was selling USD forward at rates below spot — a cost of hedging); in FY2023 (USD slight weakening), hedges provided a small offset; the programme has functioned technically but has not materially reduced reported earnings volatility because the translation exposure is 10x larger and unhedged

  • Hedge cost transparency:

  • Ipsen discloses derivative fair value positions in annual report notes; the net cost of the hedging programme (option premiums + forward points) is embedded in financial income/expense and not separately called out as "FX hedge cost"

  • Approximate annual hedge programme cost: likely €5-15m based on notional sizes and typical forward-point spreads for EUR/USD; this is immaterial at group level but represents a pure cost for limited protection

  • Counter-cyclical vs pro-cyclical hedging:

  • No explicit disclosure of Ipsen's hedging timing philosophy
  • Based on the rolling short-horizon structure, RESEARCHER infers the programme is largely mechanical (roll-forward each quarter) rather than counter-cyclical; there is no disclosed evidence of increasing hedge ratios when EUR/USD volatility is cheap

  • Assessment: neutral-to-slightly-pro-cyclical by default

  • Disclosure quality — "boring hedger" spectrum:

  • Ipsen provides: description of instruments used, maturity of derivatives (note-level), designation of hedge relationships (IFRS 9)
  • Ipsen does NOT provide: specific hedge ratio as % of exposure, explicit hedge horizon beyond instrument maturities, quantitative effectiveness ratio, split of translation vs transaction hedged

  • Assessment: Moderate disclosure quality; better than bare minimum but below the "boring hedger" standard of quarterly hedge-position summaries and explicit effectiveness ratios; comparable to mid-cap European pharma norms

  • Hedging policy vs empirical record:

  • The disclosed policy implies protection of USD transaction cash flows over 6-12 months; the empirical L3Y record shows EPS volatility from FX consistent with largely-unhedged translation exposure (i.e. when USD moves materially, Ipsen's reported EPS moves in proportion); this confirms that the hedging programme provides limited buffer to EPS outcomes

  • Structural natural hedge — the real hedge:

  • As noted in §2, the USD commercial cost base (~€540m) provides a natural hedge that covers ~43% of USD revenue; this is the most economically significant FX-protection mechanism and is structural rather than financial; it is also inflexible (commercial fixed costs don't scale down with revenue)

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on hedging discipline is a classic duration-shortfall scenario: if EUR/USD makes a rapid 12% move in Q1-Q2 of any year, Ipsen's existing 6-12M forward positions provide some coverage for that year's transaction flows but provide ZERO protection for the translation of full-year US subsidiary revenue; the FY2024-FY2025 product-transition period (Somatuline US declining, Iqirvo/Onivyde ramping) means the USD revenue base is in flux — hedging a transitional cash flow stream with 6-12M rolling forwards is especially unreliable because the actual USD receipts will differ from hedged notionals, creating basis risk between the hedge and the underlying.
