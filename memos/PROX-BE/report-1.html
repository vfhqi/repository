KEY FINDINGS

  • Dividend shock destroys income thesis near-term. PROX slumped ~16% on 27-Feb-26 after cutting gross DPS from EUR 1.20 to EUR 0.60 (2025), stepping to EUR 0.30 → 0.40 → 0.50 through 2028; yield now sub-5% at current price (~EUR 6.60), removing the core reason most investors owned the stock. [#J]
  • Domestic business is genuinely solid. Belgian domestic EBITDA grew +3.1% like-for-like in FY2025, ARPU is rising (broadband +4.2% YoY Q1-25), convergence book growing +5.3% YoY — the underlying telco franchise is performing. [#J]
  • Proximus Global is the open wound. EUR 275m goodwill impairment on BICS/TeleSign/Route Mobile in Q4-25; Global EBITDA -19.4% YoY in Q4; SMS/voice wholesale revenues in structural decline; integration execution problems cited. This segment absorbed ~EUR 3bn+ in capital and is now impaired. ❌ [#J]
  • Fibre overbuild is half-done and expensive. ~2.5m+ homes passed (~50% Belgium) at end-2025; target 4.2m by 2028 and 95% by 2032. Capex ~EUR 1.2-1.25bn guided for 2026 (vs revenue base of ~EUR 6.3bn), generating negligible organic FCF (~EUR 100m in 2026). Payback is post-2028. [#A]
  • Digi is the structural wildcard. Entered Belgium Dec-24 with EUR 5/month mobile; ended 2025 with only 91k subs but already drove mobile service revenue -1.8% in Q4-25. Belgium's high historical ARPU (among Europe's highest) makes it a prime target for further disruption. ❌ [#J]
  • New CEO, blank-sheet strategy. Stijn Bijnens (CEO from Sep-25) has a tech/PE background, not telecoms. Boutin left for Vodafone. "Amplify" domestic + "Elevate" global strategy presented Feb-26 CMD — early days, credibility unproven. [#I]
  • State ownership (53.5%) simultaneously protects and constrains. Belgian government backstops balance sheet and historically required high dividends; the dividend reset to EUR 0.30/share implies state's income need is now subordinated to capex cycle — a structural shift worth tracking. [#A]
  • Leverage at 2.8x ND/EBITDA (2026 guide) is manageable but not comfortable given capex intensity and weak FCF; covenant headroom exists but leaves little room for M&A or error during the build phase. [#A]
  • The investment case requires two things going right simultaneously: domestic ARPU uplift from fibre migration AND Proximus Global returning to growth (guided 2027+). If either disappoints, the stock faces further re-rating. [#J]
  • FCF inflection is the keystone. Organic FCF guided ~EUR 400m by 2030 vs ~EUR 130m in 2025 and ~EUR 100m in 2026. If credible, this is a 3x FCF inflection over 4 years supporting a re-rating; but it requires Proximus Global recovery + domestic fibre ARPU uplift + capex normalisation post-2028. [#J]

§1 — BUSINESS OVERVIEW & STRUCTURE

Verdict: PROX is a mid-size EU incumbent telco (~EUR 6.3bn revenue) with a structurally sound Belgian domestic franchise but an internationally diversified global segment that has destroyed value. The two-segment structure creates analytical complexity and competing capital demands. [#J]

Scale: Group revenue EUR ~6.3bn FY2025, Domestic EUR ~4.8bn, Global EUR ~1.5bn; Group EBITDA EUR ~1.75bn; ~3m mobile postpaid customers in Belgium; listed Euronext Brussels (PROX.BR). [#I]

  • Segment structure: Two reported divisions — Domestic (Belgium consumer + enterprise) and Proximus Global (international wholesale/CPaaS/digital identity). [#I]
  • Domestic contributed ~76% of FY2025 group underlying revenue; Global ~24% — but Global share falling as SMS/voice wholesale declines. [#A]

  • Domestic EBITDA ~EUR 1.71bn FY2025 (est); Global EBITDA ~EUR 150m — stark margin asymmetry in favour of domestic. [#A]

  • Domestic consumer segment: Fixed broadband (~1.5m subs), mobile postpaid (~3m), fixed voice (declining), convergent bundles (1.17m as of FY2024). [#I]

  • Consumer (Residential) contributes the majority of domestic revenue; Enterprise/B2B is the growth vector but smaller. [#A]

  • Domestic enterprise segment (B2B): Connectivity, mobile, IT services, cloud, cybersecurity, IoT. Won Belgium's largest public cybersecurity contract (EUR 100m+, 7-year, federal government). [#I]

  • B2B total revenue -0.8% YoY Q3-25 due to fixed voice headwinds; ICT/IT services +2.8% — structural pivot underway. [#A]

  • Proximus Global — BICS: International wholesale carrier (voice, data, signalling, IoT connectivity roaming). High-volume, low-margin business facing SMS-to-OTT substitution. [#I]

  • BICS revenue structural pressure from declining A2P SMS and wholesale voice; competitive CPaaS market intensifying. ❌ [#A]

  • Proximus Global — TeleSign: Digital identity/authentication (phone verification, 2FA, fraud prevention). Acquired for ~USD 1bn (2022); serves major US tech platforms. [#I]

  • TeleSign is the highest-quality asset in Global but SMS verification faces substitution threat from passkeys/biometrics long-term. [#A]

  • Proximus Global — Route Mobile: India-headquartered CPaaS provider; acquired May-24 for ~EUR 570m (majority stake). Adds India/emerging-market CPaaS volume. [#I]

  • Route Mobile acquisition added revenue scale but integration challenged; contributed to EUR 275m goodwill impairment alongside broader Global write-down in Q4-25. ❌ [#A]

  • Proximus Global rebrand (Dec-24): BICS + TeleSign + Route Mobile unified under "Proximus Global" umbrella; EUR 3.1bn equity value claimed at creation — now impaired by EUR 275m within 6 months. ❌ [#J]

  • Ownership: Belgian State 53.51%; Proximus treasury shares 4.56%; free float ~42%. State ownership implies political oversight of pricing, employment, and dividend policy. [#I]

  • ⚡ RARE: Belgium is one of very few Western EU states still majority-owning its incumbent telco — Telecom Italia (government stake ~23%), Deutsche Telekom (Federal Republic ~32%) are smaller stakes; 53.5% is exceptional and constrains strategic optionality materially. [#J]

  • Sceptical note: The "Proximus Global" rebrand was executed simultaneously with underlying financial deterioration; the strategic narrative (digital comms leader) arrived faster than the financial results. This is a yellow flag on execution credibility. [#J]

§2 — BUSINESS MODEL & ECONOMICS

Verdict: Belgian domestic unit economics are sound and improving (ARPU rising, convergence growing, margins expanding); the problem is the capex-to-revenue ratio is elevated and FCF is near-zero during the overbuild phase. Global economics are deteriorating. [#J]

Revenue mix: Fixed broadband and mobile postpaid services dominate domestic; within Global, BICS wholesale voice/data is majority but declining; TeleSign CPaaS is growing. [#A]

  • Fixed-mobile convergence: 1,172,000 convergent households as of FY2024, +5.3% YoY; convergence penetration ~47% of Proximus fixed base. Convergence customers show lower churn than single-product customers (company-stated). [#I]
  • Belgium FMC household penetration ~58% (2022 base); Proximus targeting further gains as fibre footprint expands — fibre acts as upsell vehicle into convergent packages. [#A]

  • Convergence = defensive necessity in Belgian market as Telenet (cable) and Orange Belgium (MVNO/own-network) also push bundled offers; not a Proximus-exclusive moat. [#J]

  • ARPU trajectory — residential:

  • Broadband ARPU: EUR 49.9 Q1-25, up +4.2% YoY from EUR 48.5 Q1-24 — driven by January 2025 price indexation + fibre upsell. [#I]
  • Mobile postpaid ARPU residential: ~EUR 19/month (single-SIM); Group ARPC (convergent) ~EUR 58.5 Q3-25, +1.2% YoY. [#I]
  • Fixed voice ARPU: EUR 28.1 Q4-24, +1.7% YoY — 7 consecutive quarters of growth, entirely index-linked, not volume. [#I]

  • L3Y assessment: ARPU trajectory is positive but driven primarily by price indexation (Belgian CPI-linked contracts) rather than volume/product upsell. This is structural-regulatory rather than demand-pull — classifies as partially demand-pulse. [#A]

  • ARPU trajectory — enterprise B2B mobile:

  • Business mobile postpaid ARPU EUR 18.7 Q2-25, -2.6% YoY. ❌ [#I]

  • Reflects competitive pressure on mobile + reduced out-of-bundle spend. Enterprise mobile under pressure as Digi's entry forces incumbents to hold/reduce pricing. [#A]

  • Fibre overbuild — scope and timeline:

  • Total Belgian premises: ~5.4m. Proximus target: 4.2m passed by 2028 (~78%), 95% by 2032. [#I]
  • Progress: ~2.5m passed by end-2025 (~46% Belgium); deploying via own network + JVs (Fiberklaar in Flanders, target 1.5m; Unifiber in Wallonia, target 0.6m by 2028). [#I]
  • Fiberklaar: Proximus acquired 100% ownership in Jul-24, consolidating JV onto balance sheet — capex now fully Proximus-borne. [#I]
  • Fibre-related capex: ~30% of total group capex in 2025; FY2025 capex EUR 1,249m (vs EUR 1,356m guided, EUR 107m underspend). [#I]

  • ⚡ RARE: Proximus connecting a home to fibre "every 15 seconds" at peak pace — this is one of Europe's faster FTTH deployment velocities for an incumbent of this size, though Belgium started late vs Netherlands/Portugal. [#A]

  • Capex intensity: FY2025 capex/revenue ~20% (EUR 1.25bn / EUR 6.3bn) — elevated vs EU telco average of ~15-17%. Will remain EUR 1.2-1.25bn in 2026 guidance. Capex normalisation expected post-2028 as overbuild completes. [#A]

  • EBITDA margin profile:

  • Domestic EBITDA margin: estimated ~35-36% (domestic EBITDA ~EUR 1.71bn / domestic revenue ~EUR 4.82bn). [#A]
  • Group EBITDA margin: ~28% (est EUR 1.75bn / EUR 6.3bn) — diluted by low-margin Global segment. [#A]
  • EU telco peer comparison: Swisscom domestic EBITDA margin ~40%+; Telenet ~50%+ (pre-Liberty merger); Orange Belgium ~25%; PROX domestic margin sits mid-range for a converged incumbent. Swisscom's superior margin reflects Swiss market pricing power and less competitive intensity. [#A]

  • Global segment EBITDA margin: ~10% (EUR 150m / ~EUR 1.5bn) — typical for wholesale/CPaaS, and declining. ❌ [#A]

  • B2B/Enterprise margin vs consumer:

  • B2B connectivity margins broadly comparable to consumer; IT services typically lower margin but growing — net effect is B2B margin slightly dilutive vs pure consumer connectivity. [#A]

  • ICT revenue growth (+2.8% Q3-25) partially offsets legacy connectivity decline; full year B2B trend negative at revenue level but improving at margin via mix shift. [#A]

  • Flywheel vs treadmill assessment: Belgian domestic shows embryonic flywheel characteristics (fibre upsell → ARPU increase → convergent bundle → churn reduction) but it is expensive to build (capex-heavy) and the flywheel is not yet self-funding — still a treadmill in FCF terms through 2027. [#J]

  • Sceptical note: ARPU growth is predominantly index-linked (CPI-based contractual escalators), not genuine pricing power from product differentiation. When/if Belgian inflation moderates, the ARPU tailwind diminishes — the structural ARPU growth case rests on fibre upsell which is real but slow to materialise at scale. [#J]

§3 — COMPETITIVE DYNAMICS

Verdict: Belgian market has the worst possible timing for PROX — fibre mid-build cost phase colliding with the entry of a disruptive 4th mobile operator (Digi) in a market historically characterised by high incumbent pricing. Domestic competitive moat is under more pressure than at any point in the past decade. [#J]

Mobile market structure: 3-player established market (Proximus, Telenet/Base, Orange Belgium) becoming 4-player post Digi entry Dec-24. [#I]

  • Market shares (mobile, approx): Proximus ~43% total penetration (largest), Telenet ~35%, Orange Belgium ~24%; Digi sub-1% after first year (91k subs) but exerting disproportionate pricing pressure. [#I]
  • Proximus mobile market leadership held through network quality premium; not at risk of structural share loss near-term but pricing ceiling lowered. [#A]

  • Mobile service revenue Belgium market -1.8% YoY Q4-25 — Digi effect already measurable within 12 months of launch. ❌ [#A]

  • Digi disruption specifics:

  • Launched Dec-24: EUR 5/month (15GB mobile) and EUR 10/month (500Mbps fibre). Belgian incumbent mobile ARPU was EUR 25+ — a 5x+ gap. [#I]
  • Belgian mobile ARPU among Europe's highest (historically); c.13x France's per-GB cost — makes Belgium structurally attractive for disruptors. [#A]
  • Incumbents' response: raise data allowances at unchanged prices rather than cut headline tariffs; Proximus launched budget sub-brand alternatives. [#I]
  • Digi's 91,000 subs after one full year vs Belgian market of ~13m SIMs — market share impact modest but pricing reference point permanently lowered. [#A]

  • Risk: Digi builds MVNO scale, improves coverage (using shared infrastructure), and accelerates assault on mid-market segment by 2026-27. [#J]

  • Fixed competition — cable (Telenet/Liberty Global):

  • Telenet is Belgium's dominant cable operator; recently absorbed by Liberty Global (majority owner). Covers ~70%+ of Flanders and Brussels with cable HFC/DOCSIS 3.1 network. [#I]
  • Telenet is Proximus' primary fixed competitor; Proximus overbuilding on top of Telenet cable footprint with FTTH — classic fibre-vs-cable overbuild dynamic. [#A]
  • Proximus fibre where deployed demonstrably superior on speed/latency vs Telenet cable; but Telenet upgrading to multi-gig DOCSIS 3.1 to compete. [#A]

  • Cable alternative-network risk in Wallonia partially addressed via Unifiber JV with Orange (rolling out fibre in areas where cable is weaker). [#I]

  • Alt-net overbuilder risk assessment:

  • Belgium has limited independent alt-net risk vs UK/Netherlands; Digi is building on shared infrastructure (MVNO model for mobile; own fibre footprint limited). [#A]
  • Primary competitor on fixed is Telenet (incumbent cable), not new-entrant altnets; the dynamic is fibre-vs-cable not fibre-vs-fibre in most areas. [#A]

  • Altnet-style threat is Digi attempting to replicate Iliad (France) playbook; structurally plausible but facing Belgium's regulatory complexity and existing infrastructure density. [#J]

  • International segment competitive dynamics — BICS:

  • BICS competes in wholesale A2P SMS, voice termination, and IoT connectivity; market undergoing structural decline as OTT messaging (WhatsApp, iMessage) displaces traditional SMS. [#I]
  • A2P SMS market had artificial resilience from 2FA/authentication (TeleSign's domain) but faces long-run substitution via passkeys and app-native auth. [#A]

  • BICS revenue trajectory: declining YoY; -22.2% Global revenue Q4-25 (driven by currency + structural). ❌ [#A]

  • International — TeleSign:

  • Digital identity/phone verification; key customers are large US tech platforms and financial services. [#I]
  • TeleSign is high-quality sticky B2B SaaS-adjacent business but scale is limited (~EUR 500m revenue est); margin profile better than BICS. [#A]

  • Substitution risk: passkeys/biometric auth adoption by Apple/Google platforms represents a 5-10 year risk to SMS-based 2FA volumes. [#J]

  • Regulatory environment:

  • Belgian regulator BIPT historically strict on wholesale access obligations; Proximus must provide wholesale broadband access to resellers — limits pricing freedom. [#I]
  • BIPT has not imposed onerous fibre unbundling on new FTTH network (vs some EU states); this is a regulatory tailwind — Proximus has limited obligation to share new fibre at regulated prices for now. [#A]

  • Spectrum: Proximus holds 5G spectrum licences; 5G rollout effectively complete for population coverage (99%+ stated by management). [#I]

  • Sceptical note: The Belgian competitive dynamic is worsening structurally — a 4th MNO entered at the worst possible time (mid-fibre overbuild, elevated capex). Even if Digi remains small, it has permanently shifted the pricing reference frame. The risk is not market share loss but ARPU compression that invalidates the fibre payback model. [#J]

§4 — FINANCIAL PROFILE

Verdict: Proximus domestic is financially resilient with improving EBITDA; the group-level FCF story is bleak near-term due to capex intensity and Global drag. The dividend reset was necessary but the quantum of cut signals management itself has limited confidence in near-term FCF recovery. [#J]

Revenue:

  • FY2025 Group underlying revenue: ~EUR 6.3bn (domestic EUR 4.82bn; Global EUR ~1.5bn). [#I]
  • Domestic revenue: -0.1% YoY FY2025 — effectively flat; Global revenue decline -22.2% in Q4 (full-year Global down significantly due to wholesale/SMS decline). [#I]
  • Domestic services revenue trajectory: broadly stable FY2026 guided; CAGR +1% target 2026-2028. [#I]

  • 2026 guidance: Domestic services revenue broadly stable; Global EBITDA EUR 100-130m. [#I]

  • FY2025 EBITDA:

  • Domestic EBITDA: grew +3.1% like-for-like FY2025 — positive despite revenue headwinds from cost efficiency. [#I]
  • Global EBITDA: EUR 150m est FY2025, -19.4% YoY Q4 alone; full-year deterioration meaningful. ❌ [#I]

  • Group EBITDA: broadly stable YoY (domestic growth offset Global decline). [#A]

  • FCF:

  • Organic FCF FY2025: EUR 130m (outperformed guidance by EUR 30m). [#I]
  • Reported FCF FY2025: EUR 480m (includes working capital and non-recurring items). [#I]
  • Organic FCF 2026 guidance: "up to EUR 100m" — actually guided lower than 2025 actuals. ❌ [#I]

  • FCF 2030 ambition: EUR 400m — implying 3x+ inflection over 4 years; requires capex normalisation, domestic ARPU growth, Global recovery. [#A]

  • Net debt / EBITDA:

  • FY2025 ND/EBITDA: ~2.7-2.8x (company target comfort zone 2.5-3.0x). [#I]
  • 2026 guidance: ND/EBITDA c.2.8x — leverage stable but not declining during peak capex. [#I]
  • Peer comparison: Swisscom ND/EBITDA ~1.5-2.0x (conservative Swiss operator); Telenet ~4.5x (highly levered, LG-owned); Orange Belgium ~1.5x; PROX at 2.8x sits in the middle but on an upward trend during build phase. [#A]

  • ❌ Leverage trajectory is the wrong direction (rising) while FCF is near-zero — less margin for error than peers who have completed their build cycles. [#J]

  • Capex cycle:

  • FY2025 capex: EUR 1,249m — EUR 107m below prior guidance (positive surprise). [#I]
  • FY2026 guided capex: EUR 1,200-1,250m — similar level; capex plateau extends at least through 2027. [#I]
  • Peak capex year: likely 2024-2026 band; post-2028 normalisation expected as FTTH build completes. [#A]

  • Capex/revenue ratio ~20% — at the upper end of EU incumbent telco peers (~15-18% median); Belgium's late-start FTTH build drives elevated intensity. [#A]

  • Dividend policy:

  • Historical DPS: EUR 1.20/share for several years; cut to EUR 0.60 for FY2025 result (50% cut). ❌ [#I]
  • New policy: EUR 0.30 (FY2026) → EUR 0.40 (FY2027) → EUR 0.50 (FY2028). [#I]
  • Yield at EUR 6.60 share price: ~4.5% on FY2025 DPS (EUR 0.60); ~4.5% on FY2026 (EUR 0.30) — yield halved in real terms as policy resets. ❌ [#I]

  • State dividend obligation: at 53.5% ownership, Belgian state received ~EUR 340m/year at EUR 1.20 DPS; new policy reduces that to ~EUR 85m on EUR 0.30 — a massive reduction in government income. The state's acceptance of this cut signals political support for the overbuild investment cycle. [#A]

  • FCF cover of dividend:

  • Organic FCF 2026: guided "up to EUR 100m". Dividend payout 2026: ~EUR 80m (EUR 0.30 × ~265m shares). FCF cover ~1.25x — barely covered. [#A]

  • If Proximus Global disappoints further or domestic ARPU stalls, FCF cover breaks. Risk is non-trivial. [#J]

  • Consensus estimates (FY2026/27E, market):

  • FY2026E: Revenue ~EUR 6.1bn; EBITDA ~EUR 1.75bn; EPS ~EUR 0.30-0.35. [#I]
  • FY2027E: Revenue broadly flat; EBITDA modest improvement; FCF recovery. [#I]

  • EV/EBITDA: At ~EUR 6.60 share price, market cap ~EUR 1.75bn (free float portion), enterprise value ~EUR 7-8bn → EV/EBITDA ~4-4.5x — cheap vs EU incumbent peers (typically 5-7x) but optically distorted by Global impairment overhang. [#A]

  • Sceptical note: The EUR 400m FCF by 2030 ambition is arithmetically plausible (capex normalises ~EUR 900m post-build, EBITDA stable/growing, interest costs manageable) but requires zero further deterioration at Global AND Belgian domestic competitive environment holding. The prior management team set similarly ambitious targets that were missed. [#J]

§5 — MANAGEMENT & CAPITAL ALLOCATION

Verdict: Management credibility is low after the combination of CEO departure, goodwill impairment, and dividend cut within 12 months. New CEO Stijn Bijnens brings fresh perspective but zero telecoms operating experience — this is a high-stakes appointment at a sensitive inflection point. [#J]

CEO — Stijn Bijnens (from Sep-25):

  • Background: Computer science, KU Leuven; led LRM (Limburg development agency) from 2008; CEO of Cegeka (Belgian IT services/cloud company) from 2019. [#I]
  • Cegeka is a mid-size European IT services group — relevant experience in B2B digital services, cloud, systems integration. [#I]
  • Zero telecoms operating experience; no track record running a regulated incumbent with network capex cycle, mobile/fixed convergence strategy, or international wholesale assets. [#J]

  • ❌ "Animal CEO" test: Bijnens scores adequately on B2B/digital credentials; scores poorly on telecoms network/capital allocation expertise and international M&A track record. The most complex decisions (Global segment restructuring, fibre ROI) require expertise he doesn't demonstrably have. [#J]

  • Guillaume Boutin (departed May-25, joined Vodafone):

  • Architect of the "inspire2025" strategy including fibre overbuild commitment and Global acquisitions (TeleSign 2022, Route Mobile 2024). [#I]
  • Left mid-strategy, before the investments he initiated generated returns — this is a significant governance concern. Management departure mid-cycle leaves capital allocation decisions without their architect. [#J]

  • Joining Vodafone Investment & Strategy suggests he saw the domestic Belgian picture as less attractive than an international role. [#J]

  • Capital allocation — assessment:

  • Fibre overbuild (EUR 3bn+ total commitment): correct long-term strategic decision; Belgium needed to upgrade from copper; timing was industry-consensus. Execution within capex budget in 2025 (underspent by EUR 107m) is a positive sign. [#A]
  • TeleSign acquisition (USD ~1bn, 2022): price paid was high for a CPaaS business entering a structural headwind from passkey/biometric substitution. EUR 275m impairment (on combined Global book) partially attributable to TeleSign over-valuation. ❌ [#J]
  • Route Mobile acquisition (EUR ~570m majority stake, May-24): acquisitive expansion into lower-quality India/EM CPaaS at peak of the deal cycle, 6 months before industry headwinds accelerated. Timing was poor. ❌ [#J]

  • Pattern: Boutin-era capital allocation was aggressive in Global while the domestic overbuild was consuming capital — too many fronts simultaneously. [#J]

  • State ownership constraint:

  • Belgian government as 53.5% owner has board representation and historically required high dividend payouts (EUR 1.20/share = EUR 320m/year to state). [#I]
  • State acceptance of 75%+ dividend cut signals alignment with long-term investment cycle — this is actually a positive shift in the state-ownership dynamic. [#A]
  • Political constraints: employment levels (Proximus employs ~11,000 in Belgium), Belgian coverage obligations, and BIPT relationship all constrained by government involvement. Network investment decisions require political buy-in. [#I]

  • State ownership limits hostile M&A risk (no takeover premium available to minority shareholders) but also limits strategic optionality (sale/merger of Global assets requires political approval). [#J]

  • Sceptical note: The CMD "Amplify + Elevate" strategy was presented by a CEO (Bijnens) who had been in post only 5 months. Strategy documents authored under this compressed tenure deserve lower credibility weighting — the real test is 12-18 months of execution evidence, not another slide deck. [#J]

§6 — GROWTH OUTLOOK & OPTIONALITY

Verdict: The domestic fibre payback is real but back-ended (post-2028); Global recovery is speculative; the 25%+ IRR investment thesis requires a 4-year holding period with multiple variables going right. At current valuation (~4x EV/EBITDA), the market is pricing meaningful execution risk. [#J]

Fibre overbuild — ARPU uplift timing:

  • Fibre migration drives ARPU uplift via: (1) customers upgrading to higher-speed tiers, (2) convergent bundle take-up anchored on fibre, (3) price indexation applied to new fibre contracts. [#A]
  • Proximus broadband ARPU +4.2% Q1-25 already partly reflects fibre mix shift. [#I]
  • At 50% fibre coverage (2025) → target 78% by 2028 → material ARPU uplift from fiber upsell not fully reflected until 2026-2028. [#A]
  • Churn dynamics: fibre customers show lower churn than DSL (company-stated; logical given switching friction and speed advantage). Convergent fibre customer is the "gold standard" low-churn customer. [#A]

  • ARPU uplift realisation risk: Digi's entry creates pricing ceiling; if incumbents cannot raise convergent package prices, fibre ARPU uplift comes from mix shift not pricing — slower and less certain. [#J]

  • TeleSign / BICS growth trajectory:

  • TeleSign: digital identity market secular growth (digital onboarding, fraud prevention); addressable market expanding with digital/financial services growth; but SMS 2FA substitution is a multi-year headwind. [#A]
  • TeleSign mid-term growth: company targets high single-digit revenue growth if SMS volumes stabilise; realistic but unproven given accelerating passkey adoption by Apple/Google from 2025. [#J]
  • BICS: wholesale voice declining at ~5-10%/year structurally; IoT connectivity growing but small relative to voice/SMS decline. BICS is a managed-decline asset requiring cost restructuring not growth capital. [#J]
  • Route Mobile: India/EM CPaaS growth rates higher but margin thin; strategic value is diversification from TeleSign's US-centric book, but integration execution poor thus far. [#A]

  • Proximus Global return to growth guided "2027+" — this is a 2+ year waiting period during which the segment will continue destroying value. ❌ [#J]

  • B2B digital services growth:

  • Belgium's largest public cybersecurity contract (EUR 100m, 7 years) demonstrates government B2B credibility. [#I]
  • Hybrid cloud positioning: Proximus + hyperscaler partnerships (AWS, Azure) for sovereign cloud — Belgium's GDPR sensitivity makes domestic cloud hosting valuable. [#A]
  • ICT/IT services growing at ~3% in a market with single-digit overall revenue; not a high-growth engine but a margin-accretive mix-shift. [#A]

  • B2B digital services as % of total B2B revenue: still minority; the growth is real but from a small base and insufficient to offset mobile/voice headwinds at group level. [#A]

  • Consensus EPS trajectory and IRR thesis:

  • FY2026E EPS: ~EUR 0.30-0.35 (depressed by capex cycle + Global drag). [#A]
  • FY2028E EPS: consensus likely EUR 0.60-0.80 as capex normalises and FCF grows. [#A]
  • At EUR 6.60 share price, if Proximus achieves EUR 400m FCF by 2030 and re-rates to 5x EV/FCF, equity value could be materially higher — but the uncertainty band is wide. [#J]
  • 25%+ IRR plausibility: requires share price ~EUR 12-13 by 2029 (from EUR 6.60 today, ~4 years, ~20% CAGR) + dividends (EUR 0.30 → 0.50). Not impossible but requires best-case execution on all three vectors: domestic fibre, Global recovery, capex normalisation. [#J]

  • ❌ Base case is more modest: 10-15% CAGR from current trough if execution is average. 25%+ IRR requires multiple expansions + earnings recovery — hard to achieve with Belgian market structural pressures mounting. [#J]

  • Football rights — a specific note:

  • Proximus lost Belgian football broadcasting rights — cited by analysts as a subscriber retention risk for the consumer segment. Pay-TV churn risk is a near-term headwind not fully quantified. [#I]

  • Loss of sports content removes a key reason for customers to stay on premium Proximus bundles vs migrating to Telenet/Digi alternatives. [#J]

  • Sceptical note: The "Elevate" strategy for Global has no operational credibility yet. The EUR 3.1bn equity valuation assigned to Proximus Global at Dec-24 rebrand was EUR 275m impaired by Feb-26 (2 months later).
