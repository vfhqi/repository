3. §1 — EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (Top-3 most-diagnostic questions)

  • DIAGNOSTIC: The three questions that most sharply separate the bull from the bear are the FTTP unit economics probe (§3), the compensation-structure probe (§6), and the leverage-ceiling probe (§7)
  • Bull thesis dependency: FCF re-rating story depends on FTTP capex peaking in FY26, EBITDA stabilising in Consumer post-migration, and BT Sport-exit / non-core disposals funding the balance sheet — all three are management-execution-dependent, not market-structure-dependent
  • Bear thesis dependency: capex overrun, BT Business revenue erosion faster than cost saves, and pension cash drain crowding out FCF growth — none of these are fully testable from filings alone
  • False-friend risk: management could present a coherent FTTP story (fibre build ahead of schedule, take-up rates on plan) while the economics per-passing deteriorate — the false-friend question probes whether headline milestones are decoupled from per-unit margin
  • Pre-meeting posture: assume management will lead with fibre build progress and cost-transformation update — push past these to the FCF bridge and the post-FY27 capital intensity question
  • ⚡ Sceptical entry: the most useful pre-meeting assumption is that management have NOT been asked "what is your revenue per fibre premises connected, by cohort vintage, and how does the FY23 cohort compare to the FY25 cohort?" — if they have a clean answer, that is genuinely good signal; if they deflect, that is the most important piece of information in the meeting

Sceptical closing bullet: The question the analyst does NOT want to ask is whether BT's pension deficit can be structurally refinanced — it feels too blunt and too political. Per Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 1, that is exactly why it should be asked.

4. §2 — THEME 1: Revenue Drivers + Visibility

VERDICT: Revenue-side uncertainty is HIGH — FTTP take-up rates are disclosed as headline figures but cohort-level ARPU uplift is not; BT Business migration trajectory carries the highest diagnostic weight for the 18M FCF bridge

Q2.1 — FTTP take-up cohort ARPU

  • Question: "What is the average monthly ARPU for a premises connected to FTTP vs a copper-line equivalent, and how has that gap trended over the last four quarters?"
  • Expected answer: Management will give headline take-up rate (currently ~26–28% of premises passed) and note ARPU uplift of "mid-single digits" — they will resist giving a per-cohort number
  • Good answer signature: Specific ARPU delta (e.g. "£4–5/month uplift on FTTP vs copper-equivalent") with direction of travel improving as early cohorts mature
  • Bad answer signature: Deflection to aggregate Consumer revenue per user or to gross additions — avoidance of per-cohort vintage data
  • Follow-up probe: "Are the FY23 premises-connected cohorts showing better or worse ARPU retention at 18 months than your original business case assumed?"
  • Diagnostic note: Good answer = bull case intact (FTTP revenue per connected premises is expanding); bad/deflected answer = potential false-friend (building ahead of schedule but monetisation lagging plan); cross-refs Q19 ACH failure mode #2 (take-up rate ≠ revenue yield)

Q2.2 — BT Business migration attachment

  • Question: "What percentage of BT Business SME customers are actively migrating to full-fibre products, and what is the net revenue effect per migrated customer vs legacy leased-line equivalents?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite broad migration-to-Ethernet/fibre progress but resist a clean net-revenue-per-customer figure; likely to package as "simplification savings offsetting revenue headwind"
  • Good answer signature: Explicit net-positive revenue per migrated SME customer with specific £/customer number or direction; acknowledgement of where negative mix exists
  • Bad answer signature: Reference to total BT Business revenue trajectory without per-customer migration economics — conflates product-mix with volume
  • Follow-up probe: "Is the revenue headwind from legacy product run-off in BT Business already at its widest point, or do you expect it to widen further in FY26?"
  • Diagnostic note: If BT Business revenue erosion is still accelerating in FY26, the EBITDA bridge is at risk — this is the highest-probability bear-case catalyst in the 12–18M window; cross-refs Q19 ACH failure mode #1 (legacy erosion faster than fibre ramp)

Q2.3 — Openreach wholesale pricing

  • Question: "Under the Ofcom WFTMR pricing framework, where do you see the biggest pricing risk to Openreach's regulated rental income over the FY26–FY28 period?"
  • Expected answer: Management will note that WFTMR pricing is indexed and relatively predictable; likely to point to competitive pricing from alt-net build as the bigger risk than regulatory price reductions
  • Good answer signature: Specific articulation of the pricing control mechanism, which products are most exposed, and a quantified sensitivity (e.g. "1% move in regulated rental = £Xm EBITDA")
  • Bad answer signature: "Regulatory framework is well understood and supportive" with no specific sensitivity — this is a rehearsed deflection
  • Follow-up probe: "If Ofcom tightens the charge control on Physical Infrastructure Access in the next review, what is the financial exposure?"
  • Diagnostic note: Bull case requires Openreach pricing to remain stable or improve; any signals of upcoming downward regulatory reset would materially alter the Openreach EBITDA contribution, which underwrites the FCF re-rating

Q2.4 — Consumer churn post-price rises

  • Question: "Following the above-inflation price increases in FY24–FY25, what has been the net churn effect in Consumer broadband, and is churn stabilising or re-accelerating?"
  • Expected answer: Management will report churn within "normal range" and note structural stickiness from FTTP upgrade; they will not volunteer if churn is at the top of the normal range
  • Good answer signature: Explicit churn rate (expressed as monthly or annual percentage), trend direction, and segmentation between copper and fibre customers
  • Bad answer signature: "Churn is within our expectations" without a number — the absence of a number when asked directly is a flag
  • Follow-up probe: "Is the churn rate for FTTP-connected customers materially lower than copper-connected customers, and what is the delta?"
  • Diagnostic note: Discriminates between "pricing power is real and sustainable" (bull) and "we are harvesting copper customers before they churn anyway" (false-friend); latter scenario means Consumer ARPU growth is front-loaded and not repeatable

Sceptical closing bullet: Fluent answers on churn and pricing should be treated as rehearsed — BT has disclosed pricing-uplift data in four consecutive earnings calls. The diagnostic signal is the answer to the cohort-vintage question (Q2.1), which requires real-time recall rather than a prepared number.

5. §3 — THEME 2: Margin Trajectory + Levers

VERDICT: Margin trajectory is the LOAD-BEARING uncertainty — the gap between EBITDA guidance and normalised FCF is large and contested; capex phasing is the single biggest swing variable in the 18M window

Q3.1 (MOST DIAGNOSTIC) — FTTP per-passing cost and FCF bridge

  • Question: "What is the all-in cost-per-passing for FTTP build today vs your FY22 business case, and how does that roll into your FY26 FCF guidance?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite per-passing cost in the "£250–350" range and note they are on or slightly below original plan; they will not volunteer deterioration vs the original business case baseline
  • Good answer signature: Specific cost-per-passing with directional trend (declining as scale builds), explicit bridge from capex peak to FCF conversion, with sensitivity disclosed
  • Bad answer signature: "Cost-per-passing is tracking to plan" without a specific number, or refusal to bridge capex reduction to FCF — this is the most important deflection to detect
  • Follow-up probe: "If cost-per-passing comes in 10% above your FY22 business case across the remaining 7–8m premises, what is the FCF impact in absolute terms?"
  • Diagnostic note: This is the single question most likely to surface the false-friend scenario — where FTTP build is progressing but the IRR is below what was assumed in the re-rating story; cross-refs Q19 ACH matrix most-plausible failure mode

Q3.2 — Cost transformation programme (5Y £3bn)

  • Question: "Of the £3bn five-year cost programme, how much has been delivered to date, and what percentage of remaining savings depend on headcount reduction vs network automation vs procurement?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite delivery to plan (FY24/25 run-rate savings) and describe a mix of savings — likely to emphasise automation and procurement as "clean" savings while being vague on headcount timing
  • Good answer signature: Specific cumulative delivery (£Xm of £3bn), explicit split by lever, and timeline for when remaining headcount-dependent savings hit the P&L
  • Bad answer signature: "Programme on track" with aggregate delivery figures only — the important question is whether the back-loaded savings (FY26–FY28) are more or less certain than front-loaded ones
  • Follow-up probe: "Which of the three savings levers carries the highest execution risk, and what contingency do you have if it slips?"
  • Diagnostic note: If savings are disproportionately back-loaded and headcount-dependent, the margin ramp in FY26–FY27 is higher-risk than management's language implies

Q3.3 — EBITDA to FCF conversion

  • Question: "Walk me from your FY26 EBITDA guidance to normalised FCF — specifically pension cash payments, spectrum, and working capital — and where is the biggest swing item?"
  • Expected answer: Management will give a consolidated FCF guidance number and reference the "£1bn+ FCF" medium-term target; they will likely not break out the EBITDA-to-FCF bridge components explicitly
  • Good answer signature: Explicit bridge (EBITDA → interest → tax → pension → capex → working capital → FCF), with each line item quantified and directional
  • Bad answer signature: Reference to the medium-term FCF target without the bridge — the bridge is where risk is hidden; if management cannot articulate it in the meeting, that is signal
  • Follow-up probe: "Is the pension cash drain (deficit payments) expected to be materially higher or lower in FY27 vs FY26, and has the triennial valuation changed your view?"
  • Diagnostic note: Pension cash drain is the most under-discussed FCF headwind in BT's financials; if the 2023 triennial valuation has moved the required deficit contribution, this materially affects the FCF re-rating timeline

Q3.4 — Openreach EBITDA margin sustainability

  • Question: "Openreach EBITDA margins have been above 40% — is that level sustainable as you absorb higher fibre maintenance costs and the copper-switch-off cost programme?"
  • Expected answer: Management will note that Openreach margins benefit from scale and that fibre has lower long-run maintenance costs vs copper; they may acknowledge near-term dilution from switch-off costs
  • Good answer signature: Explicit near-term vs long-term margin trajectory for Openreach (e.g. "margins dip to 38% in FY26 before recovering to 42%+ by FY28"), with specific cost drivers named
  • Bad answer signature: "Openreach margins are structural and long-run supportable" without addressing the near-term copper switch-off cost burden
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the total cost of the copper switch-off programme, and how much of that is already in your FY25–FY28 capex and opex guidance?"
  • Diagnostic note: Copper switch-off is a one-time but material cost item — if it is not fully provisioned in current guidance, FY26 margin could disappoint vs consensus

6. §4 — THEME 3: Capital Allocation + Balance Sheet

VERDICT: Capital allocation is the highest-stakes non-operational question — the three-way tension (dividend vs deleveraging vs FTTP build) is unresolved post-FY27, and management's answer to what comes AFTER peak capex is the most revealing statement they can make

Q4.1 — Post-FY27 capital allocation priority

  • Question: "Post capex peak in FY26, how do you rank the three competing uses of incremental FCF: dividend growth, balance sheet deleveraging, and potential reinvestment or buybacks?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely give a formulaic answer — "dividend first, leverage target second, then optionality" — without explicit ranking between deleveraging and dividend growth
  • Good answer signature: Explicit priority ordering with a leverage target range stated (e.g. "we want to be at 2.0–2.5x net debt/EBITDA before considering accelerated capital returns"), and a timeline
  • Bad answer signature: "We remain committed to the progressive dividend and responsible balance sheet management" — this non-answer signals management is not yet ready to commit to a post-FY27 framework
  • Follow-up probe: "At what net debt/EBITDA level would you consider initiating a share buyback programme?"
  • Diagnostic note: The answer to this question is the most important forward-looking capital allocation statement management can make; bear case is that the balance sheet absorbs most incremental FCF for longer than the market expects, compressing the re-rating timeline

Q4.2 (MOST DIAGNOSTIC) — Leverage ceiling and covenant flexibility

  • Question: "What is the actual leverage ceiling you manage to — the level at which you would cut the dividend or delay investment — and how far is current net debt from that ceiling?"
  • Expected answer: Management will reference credit rating BBB- anchor and "comfortable headroom"; they will not volunteer the covenant-level ceiling
  • Good answer signature: Specific net debt/EBITDA range at which capital allocation decisions would be forced, with current headroom quantified
  • Bad answer signature: Investment-grade rating aspiration with no specific ceiling — the absence of a ceiling number should be flagged as a refusal to commit
  • Follow-up probe: "If EBITDA fell 10% below your FY26 plan, what is the first capital allocation lever you would pull?"
  • Diagnostic note: This is the bear-case stress test — management's answer reveals whether the balance sheet is genuinely managed or is managed appearance; if they cannot answer this, the balance sheet risk is higher than disclosed

Q4.3 — M&A philosophy and non-core disposal

  • Question: "Is there a non-core asset or business unit you would be willing to dispose of in the next 24 months to accelerate deleveraging, and what is the threshold IRR you require to retain vs divest?"
  • Expected answer: Management will note the BT Sport / EETV transaction as evidence of disposal discipline and be vague about further disposals; they may reference "strategic optionality"
  • Good answer signature: Specific articulation of which assets are held for strategic reasons vs financial reasons, and a returns hurdle for retention (e.g. "any business unit below X% ROCE is a disposal candidate")
  • Bad answer signature: "We regularly review our portfolio" — the absence of a specific hurdle or candidate is a flag
  • Follow-up probe: "Is the Global/Enterprise division's current ROCE above or below your cost of capital, and if below, what is the path to closing that gap?"
  • Diagnostic note: BT Enterprise / Global has been a persistent ROCE underperformer — if management cannot articulate a path to ROCE accretion, disposal optionality is the bull-case hedge

Q4.4 — Pension deficit management

  • Question: "After the 2023 triennial valuation, has the required deficit repair contribution schedule changed, and what is the total pension cash outflow expected over FY25–FY30?"
  • Expected answer: Management will note the deficit has narrowed and contributions are agreed with trustees; they will likely cite the IAS 19 surplus without disclosing the funding basis (which drives actual cash payments)
  • Good answer signature: Specific annual cash contribution schedule, clear distinction between IAS 19 (accounting) and funding basis (cash), and confirmation of when deficit payments end
  • Bad answer signature: Reference to IAS 19 surplus and "constructive trustee relationship" without cash flow specifics
  • Follow-up probe: "Under what interest-rate or inflation scenario does the pension cash drain re-accelerate, and have you hedged against that scenario?"
  • Diagnostic note: Pension cash is the most opaque FCF headwind — the IAS 19/funding basis gap is where analysts most consistently underestimate BT's true FCF yield; a specific answer here is genuinely bullish signal

7. §5 — THEME 4: Strategy + Competitive Position

VERDICT: Strategic positioning is reasonably clear at the Openreach infrastructure level but is HIGH UNCERTAINTY at the BT Business/Global level — the competitive response to alt-net build and the long-run role of BT in enterprise connectivity are the two unresolved strategic questions

Q5.1 — Alt-net competitive response and Openreach pricing

  • Question: "As alt-net competitors pass 8–10m premises with FTTP, what is Openreach's pricing and contract strategy to retain wholesale customers who could switch to a rival physical infrastructure?"
  • Expected answer: Management will note Openreach's structural advantages (national coverage, reliability, service levels) and the regulatory protections against anti-competitive pricing; they may reference long-term wholesale agreements
  • Good answer signature: Specific retention mechanisms (volume commitments, pricing tiers, SLA differentiation), with data on wholesale customer lock-in rates and contract tenor
  • Bad answer signature: "Openreach's national coverage is our moat" without addressing the specific economics of retaining a Virgin or Sky on a long-term contract when alt-net offers price parity
  • Follow-up probe: "If CityFibre or Nexfibre offered BT's top-three wholesale customers a 15% price discount on comparable FTTP access, how confident are you that those customers would stay?"
  • Diagnostic note: Alt-net competition is the highest-credibility bear-case scenario for Openreach's long-run ARPU — management fluency here is meaningful, but so is their willingness to price to defend

Q5.2 — BT Business strategic pivot

  • Question: "In three years, what percentage of BT Business revenue should come from managed services vs pure connectivity, and what is the gross margin differential between those two product sets today?"
  • Expected answer: Management will articulate the shift toward managed services as a strategic priority and cite wins in public sector or enterprise managed networking — they will likely not give a gross margin comparison
  • Good answer signature: Specific product mix target (e.g. "managed services to represent 40% of BT Business revenue by FY28 vs 25% today"), with gross margin differential quantified
  • Bad answer signature: "We are pivoting to higher-value managed services" without a specific margin or mix target — this is the most common deflection in telco strategy presentations
  • Follow-up probe: "Is the managed services business currently margin-accretive or margin-dilutive vs your connectivity business on a like-for-like basis?"
  • Diagnostic note: If managed services currently carries lower gross margins than connectivity (which is common in early managed services transitions), the strategic pivot is margin-dilutive before it is margin-accretive — this discriminates between "genuine value creation" and "narrative covering a revenue decline"

Q5.3 — Disruption from fixed-wireless and 5G substitution

  • Question: "What percentage of your Consumer broadband customers have either trialled or switched to fixed-wireless as a substitute, and how does that churn channel compare to broadband-to-broadband switching?"
  • Expected answer: Management will minimise fixed-wireless substitution risk and note that FTTP speeds and reliability are structurally superior; they may cite low single-digit percentage of subscribers using fixed-wireless
  • Good answer signature: Explicit FWA substitution churn data, with a view on whether this channel is growing or stable, and pricing response
  • Bad answer signature: "Fixed-wireless is not a meaningful substitute for FTTP broadband" — this answer, while potentially accurate, is the answer most likely to be wrong in 24 months
  • Follow-up probe: "If BT Mobile were to aggressively push a fixed-wireless home broadband bundle, would that cannibalise your Openreach economics?"
  • Diagnostic note: Internal EE/Openreach tension on FWA is a genuinely non-obvious strategic conflict within BT Group — an honest answer about internal pricing tension between EE and Openreach would be meaningful bullish signal on management quality

8. §6 — THEME 5: Management Quality + Operator Track Record

VERDICT: Management quality is MIXED on observable evidence — Allison Kirkby has communicated a clearer capital allocation framework than prior CEOs, but execution on cost transformation and the FCF bridge has yet to be stress-tested in a revenue headwind scenario

Q6.1 (MOST DIAGNOSTIC) — Compensation structure and FCF incentive alignment

  • Question (Power-of-incentives probe): "What proportion of your annual variable compensation is linked to FCF generation vs EBITDA vs FTTP build milestones — and which metric most directly drives your team's day-to-day decisions?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite the Remuneration Report structure; for the CEO, LTIP is linked to a mix of TSR, FCF, and strategic KPIs — but the specific weighting and what constitutes a "beat" on FCF vs EBITDA will likely be disclosed only at a surface level
  • Good answer signature: Explicit FCF as a primary LTIP metric with a meaningful weighting (>30%), with confirmation that FCF is defined as post-pension, post-spectrum normalised
  • Bad answer signature: "Our incentive structure is fully disclosed in the Remuneration Report and is aligned with shareholder value" — absence of a substantive answer on which metric actually drives behaviour is itself signal
  • Follow-up probe: "If FTTP build milestones were running ahead of plan but FCF was below target, which direction would you lean in resource allocation decisions?"
  • Diagnostic note (Concept A): The answer to this question predicts behaviour more reliably than strategy presentations — if the CEO's LTIP is more sensitive to EBITDA or build milestones than to normalised FCF, the FCF re-rating thesis requires a management incentive realignment that may not occur in the 18M investment window

Q6.2 — Sandbagging discipline and guidance track record

  • Question: "In each of the last three financial years, did you start the year expecting to beat, meet, or miss your own EBITDA guidance — and what was the actual outcome?"
  • Expected answer: Management will note that guidance was delivered or modestly beat; they will not volunteer years where guidance was cut mid-year
  • Good answer signature: Honest acknowledgement of at least one miss or guidance trim, with a clear explanation of what caused it and what changed in the planning process as a result
  • Bad answer signature: "We have consistently delivered on or above our guidance commitments" — a claim of consistent guidance delivery from BT over a multi-year period should be tested against the actual record (which includes multiple guidance reductions under prior management)
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the single biggest assumption in your FY26 EBITDA guidance that, if wrong, would cause you to miss by the most?"
  • Diagnostic note: Sandbagging discipline is a leading indicator of management quality — management that cannot name a specific risk to their own guidance (without prompting) is either overconfident or concealing

Q6.3 — Crisis playbook and decision under pressure

  • Question: "If your FTTP cost-per-passing comes in 20% over plan in FY26, walk me through the decision sequence — what do you cut, what do you protect, and at what threshold do you revise public guidance?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely give a thoughtful answer on capital prioritisation (highest-IRR premises first, slow lower-density build) but will resist naming a specific threshold for guidance revision
  • Good answer signature: Explicit decision rule (e.g. "if cost-per-passing exceeds £X for two consecutive quarters, we slow the rural build programme and redirect to urban dense areas"), with the guidance revision trigger named
  • Bad answer signature: "We have strong cost controls in place and do not expect that scenario" — refusal to engage with the stress test is itself a culture signal
  • Follow-up probe: "Has the board formally stress-tested the balance sheet against a 20% capex overrun scenario, and what was the output?"
  • Diagnostic note: The willingness to engage with a specific stress scenario is the single most reliable indicator of management culture in investor meetings — management that can walk through a crisis scenario in detail is more credible than management that dismisses it

Q6.4 — Hiring posture and talent pipeline

  • Question: "Where is the talent constraint in executing the cost transformation — is it technical (network engineers), commercial (managed services salesforce), or digital/AI capability?"
  • Expected answer: Management will describe a broad talent strategy and cite progress on internal upskilling; they may note specific hires in AI or digital
  • Good answer signature: Specific identification of the bottleneck function, with a quantified target (e.g. "we need 2,000 additional fibre engineers by FY26 and are currently 400 ahead of plan")
  • Bad answer signature: "We are investing in our people and capability" — a non-specific answer about talent signals that management has not stress-tested the execution capacity constraint
  • Diagnostic note: BT's cost transformation and FTTP build are simultaneously people-intensive — if the talent constraint is not named and managed, execution risk on both programmes rises; this is a low-probability but high-impact diagnostic flag

9. §7 — THEME 6: Risk + Downside (Pre-mortem cross-ref)

VERDICT: Downside scenarios are concentrated in three vectors — FTTP economics underperforming, pension cash drain re-accelerating, and BT Business revenue declining faster than cost saves — each of these is partially testable in a management meeting

Q7.1 (MOST DIAGNOSTIC) — Balance sheet stress and FCF floor

  • Question: "What is the minimum EBITDA level at which BT's balance sheet remains self-funding — i.e. no equity raise, no covenant breach — and how much EBITDA buffer do you currently have above that floor?"
  • Expected answer: Management will not give a specific floor number; they will reference investment-grade credit rating and headroom vs financial covenants
  • Good answer signature: Specific EBITDA floor (or net debt/EBITDA ceiling) at which management would be forced to act, with current headroom quantified in absolute terms
  • Bad answer signature: "We are comfortably within our financial covenants and have strong liquidity" — this answer, while potentially accurate, does not answer the question and signals management is not willing to discuss stress scenarios
  • Follow-up probe: "In the 2020–2022 period when EBITDA was under pressure, how close did BT come to triggering any balance sheet constraint, and what was the decision that prevented it?"
  • Diagnostic note: Historical stress-test references are the most reliable way to understand the actual (vs stated) leverage ceiling — management that can recount a specific near-miss and the resolution is demonstrably more credible than management giving forward guidance only

Q7.2 — Alt-net overbuild acceleration risk

  • Question: "If the four largest alt-net operators each raise £1bn+ in the next 12 months and accelerate their overbuild into BT's highest-revenue-density urban premises, what is the impact on Openreach wholesale revenue in a three-year scenario?"
  • Expected answer: Management will minimise this risk by noting capital market stress on alt-nets and Openreach's structural advantages; they will not give a specific revenue sensitivity
  • Good answer signature: Explicit sensitivity (e.g. "if alt-nets capture 15% of the top-500-urban premises within 5 years, our wholesale revenue impact is £Xm") with a stated confidence level
  • Bad answer signature: "Alt-nets are facing significant capital and operational challenges and we do not expect them to be a material threat to Openreach's position"
  • Follow-up probe: "Which specific alt-net operator do you consider the most competent and best-capitalised threat, and why?"
  • Diagnostic note: The answer to the specific naming of the most dangerous alt-net competitor is highly diagnostic — a management team that names a specific competitor and articulates why is more credible than one that references "the broader alt-net market"

Q7.3 — Regulatory deterioration risk

  • Question: "If the next Ofcom WFTMR review moves against BT on Physical Infrastructure Access pricing, what is the earnings impact and how would you respond strategically?"
  • Expected answer: Management will note the constructive regulatory relationship and the alignment between Ofcom's universal service objectives and BT's FTTP build programme
  • Good answer signature: Specific earnings sensitivity to a 5–10% regulated price change, with a named strategic response (e.g. "we would slow the rural build programme and redirect capital to unregulated services")
  • Bad answer signature: "Regulatory risk is well understood and we have a constructive relationship with Ofcom" — this is the most rehearsed deflection in BT investor meetings
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the single regulatory decision in the next 24 months that could most negatively affect BT's economics, and what is your assessment of its probability?"
  • Diagnostic note: Probing for a specific probability estimate on a named regulatory risk forces management out of generic reassurance and into a position — management who can give a probability (even a range) have actually done the work

Q7.4 — Pension re-acceleration

  • Question: "Under a scenario where interest rates fall 200bps from current levels, what happens to your pension funding deficit on a cash basis, and what is the required catch-up contribution?"
  • Expected answer: Management will note the pension is well-hedged with LDI (Liability Driven Investment) strategy and that a rate fall is largely neutralised; they may reference hedging ratio
  • Good answer signature: Specific hedging ratio (e.g. "85% hedged") and residual sensitivity (e.g. "200bps rate fall increases deficit by £Xbn on funding basis with £Ym incremental annual contribution required")
  • Bad answer signature: "The pension is well-hedged and we are not concerned about interest rate sensitivity" without a specific hedging ratio or residual exposure
  • Follow-up probe: "Post-LDI crisis in September 2022, has BT increased or decreased its hedging ratio on the pension, and by how much?"
  • Diagnostic note: The September 2022 LDI crisis is directly relevant to BT — a management team that has a precise answer on post-crisis hedging ratio adjustment is demonstrably more engaged with this risk than one that gives a generic hedging reassurance

Sceptical closing bullet (Q19 ACH cross-ref): The Q19 ACH items NOT covered by questions in this memo include: (1) the long-run marginal cost of BT's network if fibre depreciation cycles are extended, and (2) the regulatory risk of BT being required to structurally separate Openreach (a low-probability but high-impact scenario). The second omission is by design — the question is almost always deflected and the public position is well-known — but Q8.3 below partially addresses it.

10. §8 — CROSS-CUTTING META + NON-OBVIOUS QUESTIONS

VERDICT: The questions management WON'T volunteer answers to are more revealing than the ones they will — the three non-obvious angles are capex IRR ranking, internal EE/Openreach pricing tension, and structural separation risk assessment

Q8.1 (⚡ NON-OBVIOUS) — Capex IRR ranking

  • Non-obvious angle: "If you had to rank your five largest capex programmes by expected internal rate of return, what would the order be — and which single programme has the widest range of plausible outcomes?"
  • Expected answer: Management will resist an explicit IRR ranking; they may note all programmes are "above hurdle rate"
  • Good answer signature: Explicit ranking attempt (even qualitative — "FTTP urban dense-area build is our highest-conviction IRR, followed by...") with the widest-range programme named
