§2 — Key Driver #1: RAB Growth + Regulatory Tariff Reset

Net signal: RAB growth is Enel's most durable earnings anchor but the 2024-2027 Italian ARERA reset introduces binary visibility risk at the 35-40% EBITDA block level [#J]

  • Driver definition: Enel's Italian distribution network (Enel Distribuzione) and Iberian networks (Endesa Spain) earn regulated returns on their Regulatory Asset Bases; RAB growth via capex drives EBITDA mechanically; the allowed return rate (WACC set by regulators) is reset every 4-5 years [#A]
  • Mechanism to EPS: RAB growth of 4-6% per annum, if matched by an unchanged or improved regulatory WACC, translates directly into 4-6% regulated EBITDA growth; at ~35% of group EBITDA, this supports c. 1.5-2% group EPS CAGR from this driver alone [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Regulatory certainty enables DCF-anchored valuation re-rating; utilities with visible regulated earnings typically trade at 12-16x regulated EBITDA; an adverse reset forces multiple compression on the largest EBITDA block [#A]

  • Company guide: Enel's 2024-2026 Strategic Plan targets €42bn group capex; networks/distribution represents approximately 40% of total; management guides to 4-5% annual RAB growth in Italy and Iberia through 2026 [#A]

  • Management commentary: CEO Flavio Cattaneo (appointed mid-2023) has explicitly repositioned Enel as "infrastructure-first" — deprioritising merchant generation, prioritising regulated and quasi-regulated returns; this framing supports RAB investment discipline [#I]
  • Track record: Italian distribution EBITDA has been the most reliable segment — delivered in-line or slightly above consensus for 6 of the last 8 reporting periods per public analyst commentary; permitting for distribution capex is largely within Enel's control [#I]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Moderate conservatism on RAB growth rate — 4-5% annual growth is achievable given the Italian grid investment mandate (PNRR electrification funding); allowed return assumption is the uncertainty variable, not the capex amount [#A]

  • SS consensus: Public sell-side broadly models Italian distribution EBITDA at €4.5-5.0bn range for 2025-2026; consensus expects ARERA's 2024-2027 period to maintain allowed equity return near 6.3-6.6% (nominal pre-tax) [#I]

  • Bullish analyst view: Berenberg and Mediobanca (public commentary) have highlighted Enel's Italian network earnings as a structural underpin; both flag that PNRR-funded grid digitisation creates incremental RAB accretion above the base plan [#I]
  • Sceptical analyst view: Some broker commentary (Morgan Stanley public notes) has flagged the risk that ARERA could reduce the allowed return in 2024-2027 period below the prior period's 6.3%, pressuring the earnings floor [#I]

  • Estimated EPS impact: A 50bps reduction in allowed WACC by ARERA on Italian distribution would reduce group EBITDA by approximately €300-400m and EPS by c. 3-4%; a favourable 50bps increase would have the symmetric positive impact [#A]

  • Recent delivery: FY24 Italian network EBITDA broadly in line — Enel Distribuzione delivered c. €4.7bn EBITDA in FY24, consistent with the 4-5% growth trajectory; no negative surprises on the regulatory mechanics [#I]

  • Peer benchmark: No cohort peers available (D-BATCH-25 flag); sector context: major European regulated utilities (Terna, REE, Iberdrola's networks) have generally maintained 4-6% RAB growth trajectories through 2023-2025 period [#I]

  • Trend L12M: Stable-to-accelerating — Italian grid investment mandated under PNRR creates multi-year RAB capex certainty; near-term execution appears on track [#I]

  • Macro tailwind: Energy transition drives structural grid investment — EU electrification mandates, EV charging infrastructure, distributed renewable connection all require distribution network upgrades; structurally underpins RAB growth for 10+ years [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Higher-for-longer interest rates elevate the discount rate applied by regulators to set allowed WACC; ECB rate path post-2025 will directly influence the 2028-2031 ARERA reset and investor required returns [#A]
  • Visibility: High relative to other drivers — regulatory outcomes are more predictable than merchant prices or LatAm FX; ARERA has a track record of moderate, predictable adjustments rather than sharp resets [#A]

  • Competitive dynamics: Enel Distribuzione is a regulated monopoly in Italian distribution — no competitive threat to RAB growth; Endesa Spain similarly dominant in its concession areas [#A]

  • Plausibility — H: High plausibility on RAB growth execution; medium plausibility on tariff reset outcome given ECB rate uncertainty [#J]

  • Bear case: ARERA reduces allowed return to 5.8% (from implied 6.3%+) and simultaneously requires accelerated depreciation; group EBITDA hit of €500-700m vs plan; forces EPS guidance cut [#A]
  • Miss scenario: Most likely miss is a tariff reset below consensus expectations in the 2024-2027 period final determination; timing risk is 2024 (already determined) vs 2028 reset period [#A]
  • Miss magnitude: A full adverse tariff reset (50-75bps) = c. 4-6% group EPS impairment; combined with multiple compression on the regulated EBITDA block, share price impact could be 10-15% negative [#A]
  • Red flags: ARERA's 2024-2027 determination — the final allowed return figure should be monitored carefully; any undershoot vs the 6.3% assumed baseline is an early warning signal [#I]

Sceptical bullet: What if the Italian regulatory framework is under structural pressure from government fiscal constraints — Italy's sovereign finances create an incentive for ARERA to hold allowed returns below inflation-adjusted replacement cost, slowly eroding Enel's regulated earnings in real terms over the decade [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — RAB growth execution high-plausibility; tariff reset outcome introduces binary risk that reduces overall driver confidence to medium

§3 — Key Driver #2: Renewables Capacity Additions

Net signal: Renewables execution is the growth engine but the 40-50 GW target to 2026 is ambitious — supply chain and permitting constraints have caused phasing slippage; the returns profile per MW added is also under pressure [#J]

  • Driver definition: Enel Green Power is one of the world's largest renewable energy developers; the group targets 40+ GW of new renewable capacity additions (solar, wind, storage) through 2026; each GW commissioned generates incremental EBITDA at 15-20% equity IRR on invested capital [#A]
  • Mechanism to EPS: Each GW of new capacity at c. €50-60m EBITDA contribution × planned 40 GW additions = €2.0-2.4bn incremental annual EBITDA by 2026 vs 2023 baseline; at group scale, this represents 10-15% EPS uplift from this driver alone [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Renewables growth assets typically command 10-14x EV/EBITDA multiples; successful capacity execution re-rates the growth component of Enel's sum-of-parts, driving SP appreciation even before earnings delivery [#A]

  • Company guide: Enel's 2024-2026 Strategic Plan targets 40 GW net additions by 2026; 2024 CMD reduced the ambition slightly from the earlier 2022 plan's 48 GW; phasing weighted to 2025-2026; EGP capacity targeted at 75 GW installed by end-2026 from c. 57 GW at end-2023 [#A]

  • Management commentary: CEO Cattaneo has explicitly rephased the renewables ambition — the 2023 CMD reduced total capex and recalibrated additions toward higher-return markets; this is a signal of improved capital discipline but also confirms that the prior 48 GW target was undeliverable [#I]
  • Track record: Mixed 3-year delivery — EGP commissioned 5.9 GW in 2023 (broadly in-line), but 2022 commissioning was below target due to supply chain disruptions; 2024 commissioning of 6.0-6.5 GW represented improved execution [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Guidance is moderately optimistic — 40 GW total through 2026 requires c. 13 GW per year in 2025-2026, meaningfully above 2023-2024 run rate of 6-7 GW; back-end loading creates execution risk [#A]

  • SS consensus: Public broker estimates cluster around 35-40 GW cumulative additions through 2026; most brokers apply a 10-15% haircut to management guidance on commissioning timing; consensus EPS for 2026 embeds renewables EBITDA of €7.0-7.5bn for EGP [#I]

  • Bullish analyst view: BofA and Citi public commentary have noted that Enel's pipeline quality (contracted vs merchant ratio, geography mix) is improving; high contracted revenue share (>80% of EGP revenue under long-term PPA) underpins returns visibility [#I]
  • Sceptical analyst view: JPMorgan and Deutsche Bank public notes have flagged that EGP's return on new additions has compressed from the 2020-2022 era; rising EPC costs + land/permitting inflation in Europe means IRRs may be 12-14% vs earlier 17-20% [#I]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Full delivery of 40 GW at target IRRs = c. €0.05-0.07 EPS accretion vs plan; a 20% shortfall on additions (32 GW vs 40 GW) = €0.02-0.03 EPS miss vs consensus, c. 5-7% shortfall [#A]

  • Recent delivery: FY24 commissioning improved — EGP commissioned approximately 6.2 GW in 2024, roughly in-line with revised guidance; supply chain pressures eased in H2 2024 as module prices declined [#I]

  • Peer benchmark: No cohort peers injected; sector context: Iberdrola (closest comparable) commissioned 4.5-5.0 GW in 2024, suggesting Enel's 6+ GW delivery is a strong sector rate; Orsted and RWE have faced more severe commissioning delays [#I]

  • Trend L12M: Stabilising after 2022-2023 disruption — 2024 commissioning more orderly; 2025 is the critical test year as the back-end loading of the plan requires step-up [#I]

  • Macro tailwind: EU energy policy strongly supportive — REPowerEU, Net Zero Industry Act, and national targets (Italy 70% renewable by 2030) create regulatory and fiscal support for renewable deployment; power purchase agreement market remains healthy [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Module price relief may reverse — US tariffs on Chinese solar panels and European anti-dumping measures could re-inflate EPC costs in 2025-2026; permitting timelines in key markets (Italy, Spain) remain slow [#A]
  • Visibility: Medium — contracted pipeline of €8-10bn capex for committed projects provides near-term visibility; beyond committed pipeline, execution risk on permitting and grid connection is real [#A]

  • Competitive dynamics: Intensifying — Iberdrola, EDF Renewables, RWE, and financial sponsors all competing for the same permitting windows, PPAs, and EPC contractors; EGP's scale advantage is partially offset by management complexity [#A]

  • Plausibility — M: Medium plausibility on full 40 GW delivery; higher plausibility (65-70%) on 32-36 GW delivery, which still supports the investment case [#J]

  • Bear case: Supply chain re-inflation (US tariff contagion), Italian permitting backlog, and return compression collectively push 2026 EGP EBITDA to €6.0-6.5bn vs €7.0bn plan; EPS 8-12% below consensus [#A]
  • Miss scenario: Phasing slippage — 35 GW by end-2026 instead of 40 GW; 5 GW slippage worth €250-300m EBITDA vs plan [#A]
  • Miss magnitude: 15% shortfall on renewables additions = c. 4-5% group EPS miss; compounded with multiple compression on growth assets = 8-12% SP downside from this driver alone [#A]
  • Red flags: H1 2025 commissioning data will be the key leading indicator — if run-rate remains at 2024 pace (6-7 GW annualised) rather than accelerating to 12-13 GW annualised needed for 2025-2026, the back-end loading thesis is broken [#I]

Sceptical bullet: What if the PPA market softens in 2025-2026 as commissioned capacity exceeds contracted demand in Spain and Italy — Enel could face merchant price risk on 15-20% of new EGP capacity at lower-than-planned capture rates, compressing actual IRRs below the 14% assumption [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — commissioning execution improving but 2025-2026 step-up requirement and return compression risk prevent high-plausibility designation

§4 — Key Driver #3: Net Debt Reduction + Financial Policy

Net signal: Enel's balance sheet trajectory is the gating condition for dividend sustainability and re-rating; net debt/EBITDA path to ≤2.4x by 2026 is achievable but requires simultaneous execution of disposals and EBITDA growth [#J]

  • Driver definition: Enel carries c. €60bn net debt (end-2023); financial policy targets net debt/EBITDA ≤2.4x by 2026 (from c. 2.7-2.8x); achieving this enables progressive dividend growth and removes the equity dilution overhang [#A]
  • Mechanism to EPS: Debt reduction reduces interest cost (€2.0-2.2bn annual financial charges in 2023); each €5bn net debt reduction at current average debt cost of ~3.5% = €175m interest saving = c. 2-3% EPS accretion [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Leverage de-rating is a direct re-rating catalyst for regulated utilities; peers trading at ≤2.0x ND/EBITDA command 15-20% premium EV/EBITDA multiples vs peers at 2.5-3.0x [#A]

  • Company guide: 2024-2026 Plan targets net debt/EBITDA ≤2.4x by end-2026; organic FCF generation + disposals proceeds fund the deleveraging path; no equity issuance planned [#A]

  • Management commentary: CEO Cattaneo has made balance sheet discipline the cornerstone of the repositioned strategy; 2023 CMD reset capex downward to support FCF conversion; messaging consistently prioritises leverage reduction over growth ambition [#I]
  • Track record: Mixed FY23-FY24 execution — net debt improved modestly in FY24 (to c. €59bn from €61bn at end-2022) but remains elevated; progress has been slower than the 2022 Strategic Plan originally guided [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Optimistic framing — the ≤2.4x target requires simultaneous EBITDA growth and absolute debt reduction; if either EBITDA growth stalls or disposals underperform, the ratio deteriorates [#A]

  • SS consensus: Public sell-side models net debt/EBITDA of 2.4-2.6x at end-2026; the c. 0.2x spread reflects uncertainty on disposals timing; consensus broadly accepts the deleveraging trajectory but not the precise 2026 end-point [#I]

  • Bullish analyst view: HSBC public commentary has emphasised that FCF generation in the Italian network business alone (c. €3-4bn annual) provides a structural deleveraging baseline independent of disposals [#I]
  • Sceptical analyst view: Barclays and Societe Generale public notes have flagged that rising WACC on the debt book (legacy fixed-rate bonds rolling off at below-market rates) will increase interest expense in 2025-2026, partially offsetting FCF deleveraging [#I]

  • Estimated financial impact: Achieving ≤2.4x ND/EBITDA by 2026 vs current 2.7-2.8x = c. €8-10bn absolute debt reduction; EPS accretion of 3-5% from interest saving alone, plus re-rating catalyst on the multiple [#A]

  • Recent delivery: Modest progress in FY24 — net debt declined by approximately €2bn in FY24; at this pace, the 2026 target requires acceleration in 2025-2026 funded by disposal proceeds [#I]

  • Peer benchmark: Sector context: Iberdrola operates at c. 4.5-5.0x ND/EBITDA (higher leverage model, investment-grade rated); Terna at c. 5.0x (Italian transmission monopoly); Enel's 2.7x is moderate vs peers but elevated vs its own historical range of 2.0-2.5x [#I]

  • Trend L12M: Slow but directionally correct — FY24 marked first year of material deleveraging since 2021; trajectory is right but pace is below the plan's implied 2025-2026 acceleration [#I]

  • Macro tailwind: ECB rate cuts (2024-2025 cycle) reduce refinancing pressure on Enel's floating-rate debt tranche and improve FCF conversion by reducing interest expense [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Inflation on operating costs compresses EBITDA margins and reduces FCF available for deleveraging; Italian labour cost inflation and maintenance capex inflation are real constraints [#A]
  • Visibility: Medium — FCF from regulated operations is highly visible; disposal proceeds are event-driven and uncertain in timing; interest expense is partially floating-rate and ECB-dependent [#A]

  • Competitive dynamics: Not applicable — balance sheet management is company-internal; no direct competitive dynamic [#A]

  • Plausibility — M: Medium plausibility on ≤2.4x by end-2026; achievable if disposals programme delivers on time; slips to low-medium if two disposal transactions are delayed [#J]

  • Bear case: Disposals delayed or executed at below-guided valuations; EBITDA misses consensus by 5%; net debt/EBITDA remains at 2.6-2.7x by end-2026; dividend growth suspended to protect balance sheet [#A]
  • Miss scenario: Two large disposal transactions (c. €3-4bn) delayed from 2025 to 2026+; net debt/EBITDA at 2.6x vs 2.4x target; market reads as balance sheet stress signal [#A]
  • Miss magnitude: A 0.2x ND/EBITDA overshoot vs target = 5-8% SP downside on multiple compression; dividend cut scenario = 15-20% SP downside [#A]
  • Red flags: Any dividend cut commentary or guidance for "balance sheet stabilisation before growth" would be a major negative catalyst; monitor H1 2025 disposal announcements closely [#I]

Sceptical bullet: What if rising interest rates on Enel's rolling debt maturities (large bond refinancing programme 2025-2027) offset the FCF deleveraging — the gross debt service cost increase of €300-500m per annum could neutralise operational deleveraging, leaving net debt/EBITDA stubbornly above target [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — FCF generation supports trajectory but simultaneous disposal execution requirement introduces meaningful timing risk

§5 — Key Driver #4: Disposals Programme Execution

Net signal: Disposals are the swing variable for both deleveraging and capex funding; €21bn target through 2026 is the most execution-dependent driver in the plan [#J]

  • Driver definition: Enel's 2024-2026 Plan includes €21bn of asset disposals (non-core generation, emerging market stakes, minority interests) to fund the €42bn capex plan alongside organic FCF; disposals are the critical financing mechanism [#A]
  • Mechanism to EPS: Disposals are primarily balance-sheet and financing mechanics — they free up capex capacity and reduce debt service; direct EPS impact depends on whether disposed assets had positive or negative earnings contribution [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Failure to execute disposals at guided valuations forces either capex cuts (negative for growth) or equity issuance (dilutive); both are SP negative; successful disposal above book value is modestly SP positive [#A]

  • Company guide: €21bn cumulative disposals 2024-2026; includes Enel Americas partial disposal, Romanian assets, Peruvian distribution, and various European non-core generation plants; targets average disposal multiple of book value or above [#A]

  • Management commentary: Pace has been managed carefully — Cattaneo has indicated preference for quality over speed on disposals; this is sensible risk management but creates timing risk vs the plan's assumed disposal cadence [#I]
  • Track record: Below initial guidance pace in FY23-FY24 — cumulative disposals through 2024 approximately €8-10bn vs the plan's implied €14-15bn by end-2024; the shortfall has been partially offset by lower capex spend [#I]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Optimistic — €21bn total requires €11-13bn of transactions in 2025-2026 alone; deal market for utilities assets has been slow given higher rates; execution risk is elevated [#A]

  • SS consensus: Public brokers broadly assume €15-18bn realised disposals by end-2026 rather than the full €21bn; the c. €3-5bn shortfall is absorbed in consensus via lower assumed capex or marginally higher leverage [#I]

  • Bullish analyst view: Deutsche Bank public commentary has noted that LatAm asset valuations have improved as regional interest rates ease; successful disposal of Enel Americas stake at premium to book would be a positive catalyst [#I]
  • Sceptical analyst view: Morgan Stanley public notes have flagged that the disposal of European regulated assets faces regulatory approvals and political scrutiny (Italy, Spain sensitive to foreign ownership of energy infrastructure) [#I]

  • Estimated financial impact: €21bn full delivery vs €15bn partial = c. €6bn incremental disposal proceeds; at current interest costs, this supports an additional 0.2x ND/EBITDA improvement [#A]

  • Recent delivery: Below plan through FY24 — key announced transactions include Enel's Romanian distribution sale, Enel Americas public offering; several planned transactions remain unannounced or delayed [#I]

  • Peer benchmark: Sector context: major European utilities have broadly struggled with large-scale disposals in 2023-2024 (E.ON, RWE, Iberdrola all experienced delays); Enel is not uniquely impaired but the problem is sector-wide [#I]

  • Trend L12M: Signs of improvement in H2 2024 — deal market for infrastructure assets showed increased appetite from sovereign wealth funds and infrastructure investors; Enel's deal pipeline may accelerate in 2025 [#I]

  • Macro tailwind: Infrastructure investment demand from sovereign wealth funds and pension funds creates a buyer pool for regulated utilities assets at reasonable multiples [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Higher cost of capital for infrastructure buyers compresses their ability to pay premium multiples; a 100bps rate increase reduces buyer bid prices by 8-12% on a DCF basis [#A]
  • Visibility: Low — disposal timing and valuations are uncertain; counterparty negotiations, regulatory approvals, and market conditions all create unpredictability [#A]

  • Competitive dynamics: Not directly competitive — but Enel competes with other utilities selling assets for the same pool of infrastructure buyers; a large E.ON or Iberdrola disposal programme at the same time reduces the buyer pool [#A]

  • Plausibility — L-M: Low-to-medium plausibility on full €21bn; medium plausibility on €15-18bn; the difference matters for leverage trajectory but not for the investment thesis survival [#J]

  • Bear case: Deal market deteriorates in 2025; major LatAm transactions delayed to 2027+; realised disposals c. €12-14bn by end-2026; group forced to cut capex by €5-7bn or accept 2.7x+ ND/EBITDA [#A]
  • Miss scenario: Romanian, Peruvian, and Enel Americas transactions complete but at 10-15% below guided valuations; total shortfall vs plan of €2-3bn; absorbed by modest capex cuts [#A]
  • Miss magnitude: €5bn disposal shortfall vs plan = c. 0.15-0.20x ND/EBITDA impact + €500-700m capex reduction = 3-5% EPS miss vs plan [#A]
  • Red flags: Watch for any guidance language indicating disposal targets are being revised downward — this is typically a bellwether for balance sheet stress and capex cuts to follow [#I]

Sceptical bullet: What if the LatAm disposal market remains structurally impaired — political risk premiums in Brazil, Peru, and Colombia have risen; infrastructure buyers applying higher discount rates may make book-value disposal targets unachievable, forcing Enel to retain assets it wants to exit [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Low-medium plausibility on full programme delivery; medium plausibility on €15-18bn partial delivery sufficient to maintain investment case

§6 — Key Driver #5: European Electricity Price Environment

Net signal: Wholesale power prices are a secondary but meaningful modifier — Enel's high contracted/regulated revenue share reduces but does not eliminate merchant price sensitivity [#J]

  • Driver definition: Enel retains merchant exposure (uncontracted generation + spot market sales) representing approximately 15-20% of total revenues; Italian and Iberian wholesale power prices directly impact this margin block [#A]
  • Mechanism to EPS: Every €10/MWh change in average Italian power price with 15-20 TWh merchant exposure = €150-200m EBITDA impact = c. 1.5-2% EPS movement; the sensitivity is material but not dominant [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Power price surprises (positive or negative) create earnings volatility that utilities typically discount; a sustained low-price environment compresses the uncontracted generation asset values embedded in EV [#A]

  • Company guide: Enel targets >80% contracted/regulated revenue share across EGP by 2026; management has explicitly guided to reducing merchant exposure as a risk management priority; the residual 15-20% exposure is structural (balancing/imbalance power) [#A]

  • Management commentary: Hedging programme — Enel routinely hedges 2-3 years forward on Italian thermal and hydro generation; FY24 and FY25 positions were broadly hedged at prices above €100/MWh when positioned [#I]
  • Track record: Power price volatility has been extreme 2021-2024 — Italian PUN peaked at €350+/MWh in 2022, collapsed to €120-140/MWh in 2023-2024; Enel benefited from the 2022 spike but the 2023-2024 normalisation compressed merchant earnings [#I]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Guidance implicitly assumes normalised power prices (€90-110/MWh Italian baseload) — this is reasonable but the direction of risk is asymmetric; oversupply from renewables additions could push prices below €80/MWh by 2026 [#A]

  • SS consensus: Public sell-side broadly models €90-110/MWh Italian baseload for 2025-2026; most brokers flag downside risk from solar cannibalisation in summer months as PV capacity continues to grow rapidly [#I]

  • Bullish analyst view: Gas supply risk and cold weather scenarios support upside to €130+/MWh; Enel's hydro fleet (significant in Italy) provides natural hedge and upside optionality in high-demand periods [#I]
  • Sceptical analyst view: Italian solar overbuild creating summer peak hour negative or near-zero power prices in 2025-2026; annual average compressed by 10-15% below consensus [#I]

  • Estimated EPS impact: 20% power price upside vs consensus (+€20/MWh) = c. €300m EBITDA uplift = 3-4% EPS accretion; 20% downside = symmetric negative [#A]

  • Recent delivery: FY24 power prices broadly in-line with consensus forecasts (€110-130/MWh Italian baseload annual average); Enel's hedging programme provided earnings stability through the normalisation from 2022 highs [#I]

  • Peer benchmark: Sector context: all European utilities with Italian/Iberian merchant exposure (EDF, Engie, Endesa) faced the same 2023-2024 price normalisation; no company-specific underperformance [#I]

  • Trend L12M: Slowly declining — Italian power prices trending toward €90-100/MWh structural level as renewable capacity grows; gas price normalisation post-Ukraine shock removes the premium floor [#I]

  • Macro tailwind: Gas price risk premia — any Middle East escalation or Russian supply disruption re-inflates gas-to-power spreads and supports European wholesale prices; tail risk positive [#A]
