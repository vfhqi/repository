Delivery Hero: Modest Growth Amid Mounting Legal and Leverage Risks
Key Takeaways

Slowing Growth Trajectory: Management expects 2026 GMV growth of just 8-10% and revenue growth of 14-16%, representing a significant deceleration from the 23.1% revenue growth achieved in 2025, raising questions about the sustainability of the company's expansion narrative.

Massive Off-Balance Sheet Risk: The company carries €0.8-1.3 billion in contingent liabilities, primarily from Spanish rider reclassification disputes (€520-860 million exposure), with Glovo already paying or guaranteeing €524 million, creating an existential threat to the balance sheet that could wipe out significant cash reserves.

Profitability Plateau Concerns: 2026 EBITDA guidance of €910-960 million represents minimal growth from 2025's €903 million, with Morgan Stanley cutting estimates by 3% citing aggressive reinvestment in South Korea and the Middle East, suggesting margin expansion has stalled despite structural cost improvements.

Intensifying Competitive Threats: Coupang controls roughly one-third of South Korean market share while Meituan's Saudi Arabia entry threatens MENA margins through aggressive pricing, forcing costly defensive investments that suppress near-term profitability.

European Margin Collapse: Spain's rider employment transition added €100 million in annual structural costs and triggered an €183 million Italian provision, with Glovo now planning to cut 750 riders, demonstrating the devastating regulatory risk across core European markets.

Expensive Debt Refinancing: The new $1.4 billion term loan carries SOFR+500bps pricing through 2032 to repay 1.00% convertible bonds, guaranteeing a massive increase in cash interest burden that undermines free cash flow generation despite extended maturities.

Taiwan Sale Execution Risk: The $600 million Grab transaction at just 0.33x EV/GMV requires approval from the same Taiwan regulator that blocked the previous Uber sale in 2024, creating significant uncertainty around the critical deleveraging catalyst.

FY26 Guidance: Management expects 2026 GMV to grow between 8% and 10% on a like-for-like basis.

Top-line Expectations: The 2026 revenue guidance is set at a 14% to 16% year-over-year like-for-like growth rate.

1.3 Demand Drivers and End-Market Dynamics (Rating: Green)

Quick Commerce: The company expects its quick commerce GMV to increase from €7.5 billion in 2025 to nearly €10 billion in 2026.

AdTech Momentum: Advertising revenue reached €1.5 billion in 2025 as the company targets a long-term goal of exceeding 4% of total group GMV.

1.4 Risks, Competition, and Macro Headwinds (Rating: Red)

Foreign Exchange: Severe depreciation of the U.S. dollar and the Korean won acts as a persistent headwind against reported top-line growth.

Asian Competition: Coupang remains a formidable threat in the highly competitive South Korean market, controlling roughly a third of the region's gross transaction value.

Middle Eastern Disruptors: The entry of Meituan into Saudi Arabia threatens to capture market share among cost-sensitive consumers through aggressive promotional pricing.

European Slowdown: Transitioning the Spanish rider fleet to an employment-based model temporarily dampened GMV growth across the European segment in late 2025.

Divestiture Impacts: The $600 million sale of the Foodpanda Taiwan business to Grab removes an asset that generated €1.5 billion in GMV during 2025.

Regulatory Contingencies: The company faces substantial off-balance sheet contingent liabilities ranging from €642 million to €1 billion related to rider reclassification risks in Spain.

Macroeconomic Pressures: A slowing global macroeconomic environment remains a distinct downside risk to achieving expected order frequencies and overall revenue expansion.

Guidance: Management has set the 2026 adjusted EBITDA guidance at a range of €910 million to €960 million.

Reinvestment: This relatively modest projected year-over-year growth reflects a deliberate strategy to reinvest in the customer proposition and sustain top-line momentum.

Long-Term: Executives maintain a highly ambitious long-term profitability target of reaching a 5% to 8% adjusted EBITDA-to-GMV margin by 2030.

Break-Even: The Integrated Verticals segment, which includes the capital-intensive dark store network, reached full-year adjusted EBITDA break-even for the first time in 2025.

2.2 Cost of Goods Sold and Gross Profit Dynamics (Rating: Orange)

COGS: Cost of sales surged by 18.5% year-over-year to €10.62 billion in 2025.

Delivery: This increase was primarily driven by higher delivery expenses associated with the aggressive rollout of own-delivery services.

Fleet: Total delivery expenses comprised €6.75 billion for external riders and €507.3 million allocated directly to the company's own rider fleet.

Gross: Despite the rising absolute cost of sales, the gross profit margin calculated as a percentage of Total Segment Revenue expanded to an all-time high of 8.3% in the fourth quarter of 2025.

AdTech: Gross profit expansion was heavily subsidized by high-margin advertising technology revenues, which are projected to eventually exceed 4% of total Group GMV.

Seasonality: Management notes that gross profit margins face natural seasonal pressures during the first and third quarters due to adverse weather conditions impacting rider supply and delivery efficiency.

2.3 Operating Costs and AI-Driven Efficiencies (Rating: Green)

Marketing: Marketing expenses decreased by 2.1% in 2025 to €1.42 billion, reflecting a highly disciplined approach to customer acquisition and retention.

Ratio: Marketing spend as a percentage of GMV compressed slightly from 3.0% in 2024 to 2.9% in 2025.

Technology: IT expenses fell by €46.8 million year-over-year to €485.0 million, driven by the successful centralization of the global tech platform.

SG&A: The combined ratio of Selling, General & Administrative (SG&A) and marketing costs dropped steeply from 7.2% of GMV in early 2023 to just 6.0% by the third quarter of 2025.

mation: These structural cost reductions are directly attributed to the rollout of agentic AI and machine learning tools across customer service and vendor management operations.

2.4 Sell-Side Expectations and Consensus Adjustments (Rating: Orange)

Revisions: Morgan Stanley recently cut its 2026 adjusted EBITDA estimate by 3% to €944 million, positioning it near the bottom end of management's guidance range.

Rationale: Morgan Stanley explicitly cited the company's decision to heavily reinvest in the South Korean and Middle Eastern markets as a primary headwind to near-term margin expansion.

Consensus: The broader market consensus for 2026 adjusted EBITDA sits at €951 million, reflecting mild disappointment that the inflection curve is flattening due to competitive realities.

Activism: Jefferies highlights that activist investors on the shareholder register will likely continue pressuring management to extract deeper cost savings from centralized corporate functions to protect bottom-line margins.

2.5 Risks to Margin Expansion and Operational Headwinds (Rating: Red)

Reclassification: The company absorbed massive margin hits in Europe, including a €183 million provision related to the legal reclassification of gig-economy riders in Italy.

Spain: Transitioning the Spanish rider fleet to an employment-based model added approximately €100 million in structural annual costs to the European segment.

Redundancies: In response to these crushing labor cost dynamics, the Glovo subsidiary is reportedly launching a redundancy plan affecting up to 750 delivery riders in Spain to salvage unit economics.

Liabilities: Morgan Stanley warns that the company carries an estimated €1.3 billion in legal provisions and an additional €440 million to €770 million in off-balance-sheet contingent liabilities related to rider disputes.

Competition: Aggressive pricing wars and the scale-up of own-delivery operations in South Korea against Coupang are temporarily suppressing the region's otherwise highly lucrative gross margins.

Dark Stores: While the Quick Commerce vertical reached break-even, the absolute costs of running urban warehouses (Dmarts) leave the margin profile highly vulnerable to any sudden macroeconomic shock that depresses basket sizes.

Currency: Severe foreign exchange volatility, particularly the depreciation of the US Dollar and Korean Won against the Euro, routinely forces management to downgrade absolute EBITDA expectations regardless of local operational success.

Interest, Tax, and Other P&L Line Items Below EBIT
3.1 Interest Expense and Debt Refinancing Dynamics (Rating: Orange)

Interest: Net interest result for the full year 2025 deteriorated to negative €283.1 million, representing a 15.8% increase in costs compared to 2024.

Amortization: This rise was primarily driven by the amortization of financial liabilities measured at amortized cost, which reached negative €298.8 million due to recent term loan syndications.

Refinancing: In March 2026, the company successfully allocated a new $1.4 billion senior secured term loan facility maturing in June 2032.

Pricing: This new facility is subject to a relatively high annual interest rate equivalent to the secured overnight financing rate plus 500 basis points.

Strategy: Management intends to utilize these proceeds to repay its 2026 convertible bonds and repurchase its 2027 convertible bonds, effectively extending the maturity profile.

Income: Interest and similar income provided a partial offset, generating €98.9 million in 2025, supported by gains from partial convertible bond buybacks and interest on cash balances.

Risk: Despite extending maturities, structurally higher "higher-for-longer" interest rates permanently elevate the company's cash interest burden, acting as a direct headwind to unlevered free cash flow conversion.

3.2 Tax Rates and Below-EBIT P&L Items (Rating: Yellow)

Taxes: The consolidated income tax expense for 2025 decreased significantly to €230.7 million, down from €339.3 million in 2024.

Effective Rate: The effective tax rate remains volatile and heavily influenced by non-deductible operating expenses, which amounted to €102.9 million in 2025.

Disallowance: Tax provisions include specific risks regarding the partial disallowance of costs for tax purposes in the MENA region, estimated at €34.2 million.

Investment Result: The investment result saw a massive year-over-year decrease of €1.75 billion, primarily due to the absence of the prior year's €1.9 billion gross proceeds from the Talabat IPO.

Breakup Fee: Non-operating income in recent periods was heavily bolstered by a €211.9 million cash inflow from a breakup fee received from Uber upon the termination of the Taiwanese business sale agreement.

Other Financials: The "Other Financial Result" line item swung to a negative €28.8 million in 2025, compared to a positive €44.8 million in the prior year, driven by adverse foreign currency movements.

3.3 Hyperinflation Accounting Impacts (Rating: Yellow)

IAS 29: The company's operations in Argentina and Türkiye qualify as hyperinflationary economies, necessitating the application of IAS 29 accounting standards.

Restatement: Financial information for these subsidiaries is restated on a historic cost approach using the consumer price index before translation into Euros.

Tailwind: In the fourth quarter of 2025, hyperinflation accounting actually provided a positive impact on reported GMV, Revenue, and adjusted EBITDA.

Dynamics: This optical benefit occurred because the monthly CPI percentage increase in these markets temporarily outpaced the monthly foreign exchange devaluation against the Euro.

3.4 Contingent Liabilities and Legal Provisions (Rating: Red)

Contingencies: The company carries a massive off-balance sheet contingent liability burden estimated between €0.8 billion and €1.3 billion.

Spain: The primary driver of this exposure is the legal classification of delivery riders at the Glovo subsidiary in Spain, carrying a contingent risk of €520 million to €860 million.

Provisional Payments: As of the end of 2025, Glovo had already provisionally paid or guaranteed approximately €524 million related to these specific Spanish social security liabilities.

Italy: In 2024, the company was forced to book a €183 million non-cash provision following a court ruling reclassifying its riders in Italy, covering potential social security contributions and fines dating back to 2016.

Antitrust: The company successfully settled a major European Commission antitrust investigation, which required a cash outflow of €329 million in 2025.

Korea: Furthermore, Delivery Hero remains under active investigation by the Korean Fair Trade Commission, with potential material fines payable in 2026.

Activism: Activist investor Aspex Management has publicly demanded a special auditor to investigate the CEO's handling of these "existential" legal liabilities that exceed €1.5 billion in total gross exposure.

3.5 Sell-Side EPS Expectations and Risks (Rating: Red)

EPS Cuts: Morgan Stanley explicitly noted that their adjusted EPS estimates were cut by outsized amounts because net income is hovering near break-even, making it highly sensitive to the recently elevated interest costs.

Downgrades: Jefferies recently downgraded its 2026 EPS estimates by a staggering 56%, reflecting the brutal combination of negative foreign exchange translations and rising below-the-line costs.

Low Base: Both analysts highlight the "low base effect," warning that while EBITDA is growing, the bottom-line net income remains razor-thin and highly vulnerable to any unexpected tax provisions or interest rate spikes.

Flexibility: The overall liquidity profile is further supported by an undrawn revolving credit facility of €840 million that was recently extended to mature in May 2028.

Maturity: This new term loan serves to materially extend the aggregate debt maturity profile, with the principal coming due in June 2032.

Pricing: The extended duration comes at a steep cost, as the new facility carries an expensive floating interest rate equivalent to the Secured Overnight Financing Rate plus 500 basis points.

Optimization: The company successfully executed massive working capital optimization initiatives throughout 2025 to protect its liquidity profile.

Underlying: When excluding the extraordinary cash impacts of the Uber breakup fee and the Glovo Spain legal payments, the underlying change in working capital was actually a positive €138 million in 2025.

Inventories: Management exerted strict discipline over Dmart inventory management, focusing on accelerating the cash conversion cycle and strictly limiting the time products sit on warehouse shelves.

Terms: The treasury team successfully negotiated extended payment terms with various payment service providers, structurally retaining cash within the business for longer periods.

Receivables: Gross carrying amounts of post-paid consumer receivables increased slightly to €31.8 million in 2025, but management views the associated credit risk as highly immaterial.

5.3 Capital Expenditure and Reinvestment (Rating: Orange)

CapEx: Total capital expenditures for the group increased to €319.6 million in 2025, up from €271.2 million in 2024.

Intangibles: A significant portion of this investment was directed toward internally generated intangible assets, capitalizing €147.5 million of development costs for the centralized global tech platform.

Dmarts: Management explicitly warned that capital expenditures are expected to rise again in 2026 as the company aggressively builds out new urban dark stores (Dmarts) to support the Quick Commerce expansion.

Leases: Alongside physical CapEx, the aggressive expansion of the Dmart footprint structurally inflates cash lease payments, which already grew to €164.4 million in 2025.

Taxonomy: Delivery Hero identified that nearly 37.6% of its 2025 CapEx was EU taxonomy-eligible, primarily driven by investments in its vehicle fleet and data processing infrastructure.

Risk: The commitment to ramp up capital intensity for the Quick Commerce segment leaves the free cash flow profile highly sensitive to any sudden drop in consumer demand or basket sizes.

5.4 M&A Strategy, Divestitures, and the Taiwan Sale (Rating: Orange)

Taiwan: In March 2026, Delivery Hero signed a definitive agreement to sell its entire foodpanda Taiwan business to Grab for $600 million in cash.

Valuation: The transaction was executed on a cash-free, debt-free basis and represents a relatively modest 0.33x EV/GMV multiple based on 2025 actuals.

Support: Following the expected close in the second half of 2026, Delivery Hero is contractually obligated to provide transition support services to Grab for up to 12 months, carrying hidden operational integration costs.

Deliveroo: Earlier in 2025, the company spent $22.5 million (€20.1 million) to acquire select customer and merchant assets from Deliveroo in Hong Kong to consolidate its local market leadership.

Exits: Continuing its ruthless approach to underperforming geographies, Delivery Hero fully ceased operations in Thailand in May 2025.

Analyst: Morgan Stanley notes that the $600 million Grab deal marks the first major crystallization of value since the board faced intense shareholder pressure to divest assets in late 2025.

Broker: Jefferies views the Taiwan sale positively but points out that activist shareholders may still demand further divestitures of the "family silverware" to repair the balance sheet.
