§2 — Switching Costs + Pricing Leverage

  • Overall verdict: Switching costs are structurally above-average for gaming-device hardware due to regulatory certification requirements, floor-management system integration, and operator staff training — this is Roko's strongest value-chain positive [#J]

  • Switching cost — qualification: Gaming devices must obtain certification from national gambling regulators (Spelinspektionen in Sweden; equivalent bodies in other jurisdictions) before floor deployment; re-certifying a competitor's device typically takes 6-18 months and incurs direct cost of SEK 0.5-2m per jurisdiction per device type — meaningful friction for operators at scale [#J]

  • Sub: IGT and Aristocrat investor materials cite regulatory qualification cycles as a primary switching-cost driver — consistent with Roko's market context

  • Switching cost — integration: Roko devices likely integrate with operator floor-management systems (accounting, jackpot, CRM), creating integration switching costs; replacing one device supplier often requires middleware reconfiguration or full FMS vendor involvement [#J]

  • Sub: in Nordic gaming halls, FMS integration is a known complexity — Novomatic, IGT, and Konami all reference this in competitive materials

  • Switching cost — training: Operator floor staff require training on device interfaces and maintenance protocols; for smaller gaming-hall operators, this is non-trivial; for major casino chains with dedicated technical teams, this cost is lower and switching friction is correspondingly reduced [#A]

  • Switching cost — product: Roko's product differentiation relative to global gaming-device suppliers (IGT, Aristocrat, Konami) is not fully assessable from available data; if Roko competes primarily on price/value rather than unique game IP, product-level switching costs are lower than certification/integration costs alone would imply [#A]

  • Pricing pass-through: No direct evidence of Roko successfully passing price increases to customers; gaming-device replacement decisions are capex-cycle driven, and operators typically have multi-year capex budgets that limit tolerance for mid-cycle price escalation [#A]

  • Sub: sector norm for gaming-device hardware is modest pricing power — 1-3% annual ASP increase at most, with volume dependent on operator floor refresh cycles

  • Customer pushback: No evidence of overt customer pricing pushback in available materials; but the absence of evidence is not evidence of absence given the thin disclosure environment for this name [#A]

  • Renewal pricing: Service/maintenance contract renewal pricing is likely more stable than capital equipment pricing — but the relative weight of recurring service revenue vs capex equipment revenue in Roko's mix is not clearly disclosed, limiting this analysis [#A]

  • Competitive bidding: Major European gaming operators routinely run competitive procurement processes for device refresh cycles, particularly post-reregulation when new entrant device suppliers seek to establish installed base; Roko faces competitive RFP exposure at major account renewal events [#J]

  • Sub: scientific Games and Aristocrat have explicitly cited Nordic/Northern European expansion as a strategic priority in recent investor materials — directly relevant to Roko's competitive bidding exposure

  • ⚡ RARE — Switching cost: The regulatory qualification + FMS integration + multi-jurisdiction certification combination for gaming-device hardware creates a switching-cost stack that is genuinely unusual relative to general B2B hardware; estimated 12-24 month total switching timeline for a large Nordic operator to fully migrate a device portfolio is at the top quartile of B2B hardware switching-cost intensity [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: What is the bearish read on switching costs and pricing leverage? The bear mechanism is scale asymmetry: Roko's switching costs protect it against casual displacement, but a large global incumbent (Aristocrat, IGT) with the capital to offer subsidised transition support, free certification services, and operator-side FMS integration assistance can effectively absorb switching costs on behalf of the operator. If a global player decides Nordic market share is strategically important and offers a "migration package" to a major operator, Roko's certification-cycle moat can be neutralised by a well-resourced competitor willing to invest in conquest. The pricing power read is also fragile — if Roko's game-content library is thinner than global incumbents, operators at renewal may accept 6-month switching friction in exchange for accessing superior game IP [#J]

  • Supply chain risk: Geopolitical exposure to Taiwan (display panels, advanced ICs) and China (PCB assembly, mechanical components) is a structural background risk; no acute crisis signal in available data, but tail risk is non-zero and is not Roko-specific — sector-wide for gaming-device hardware [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: What is the bearish read on supplier dynamics? The bear mechanism is an allocation-crunch scenario triggered by a geopolitical shock or next-generation semiconductor demand surge (AI hardware, automotive electrification) that causes component allocation tightening. In that scenario, Roko — as a low-volume, non-strategic buyer — would face extended lead times, forcing either production delays (revenue miss) or spot-market procurement at significantly elevated cost (margin miss). A second, more proximate bear: if certified gaming-grade RNG chips are sourced from a limited pool of qualified suppliers, even a temporary supply disruption at one supplier could halt regulatory-certified production runs [#J]
