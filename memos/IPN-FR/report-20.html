2. KEY FINDINGS - Most diagnostic: Three questions crystallise the bull/bear discrimination — palonopeptide launch trajectory vs consensus, Sohonos/fibrodysplasia approval pathway durability, and CFO capital-allocation discipline on BD/M&A versus buyback optionality

  • Revenue-side: Cabozantinib royalty cliff is the single most consensus-acknowledged risk; the diagnostic question is whether management has quantified the de-risked revenue bridge beyond 2026 at deal-economics level rather than at slide-deck level
  • Margin-side: The core uncertainty is whether ongoing R&D step-up (to fund mid-stage pipeline) compresses margins structurally or is a temporary investment-cycle trough — management's specific answer to opex phasing is load-bearing
  • Capital allocation: Ipsen's net cash / low leverage position combined with a historically BD-heavy posture creates a high optionality / high-execution-risk binary; the CFO's authentic answer on hurdle rates and deal-screen discipline is the most-informative data point of any meeting
  • Management quality: CEO David Loew joined 2020; CFO Aymeric Le Chatelier since 2018; the tenure window covers both the Cabometyx royalty renegotiation, Sohonos acquisition, and palonopeptide in-licensing — track record is assessable, not speculative
  • Downside probes: Palonopeptide label risk (acromegaly breadth vs narrow label), Sohonos ultra-rare launch execution, and European pricing/AMNOG-equivalent pressure on oncology are the three highest-plausibility failure modes per Q19 pre-mortem logic
  • Non-obvious: ⚡ One outlier angle — management's private view on competitive intelligence from the CAB + cabo combo data, and whether Ipsen sees Exelixis as a long-term partner or long-term adversary post-2026

Sceptical meta-bullet (§1 close): The questions the analyst does NOT want to ask include: "Are you worried about the cabo cliff?" (too consensus, invites the rehearsed pivot) and "Is the pipeline enough?" (too soft, binary answer always yes). Per Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 1 — the most-different question is: "Walk me through the quarter in which you would first see an EPS line that is lower YoY, and what does the P&L look like in that quarter?" That question is uncomfortable precisely because it forces a time-stamped honest answer rather than a narrative answer.

3. §1 — TOP-3 MOST-DIAGNOSTIC QUESTIONS (EXECUTIVE SUMMARY)

J-front verdict: Three questions separate the 25%+ IRR thesis from a value-trap; all three probe EPS trajectory specificity rather than narrative

Q-D1: Revenue Bridge Specificity

"Can you walk me through the revenue line in 2027 — product by product — assuming cabozantinib royalties step down to zero? What does the bridge look like?"

  • Expected answer: Management references palonopeptide launch ramp, Onivyde growth, and Sohonos as the offset; provides directional framing but avoids specific numbers
  • Good answer signature: Gives product-by-product contribution, acknowledges specific royalty dollar quantum, provides numeric bridge with stated assumptions
  • Bad answer signature: Pivots to total portfolio growth narrative without specifying the cabo offset quantum; uses "we're confident" language without arithmetic
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the specific royalty figure in your 2026 base plan, and what does it look like at the low end of your royalty renegotiation sensitivity?"
  • Diagnostic note: Bull case requires Ipsen's own launches to replace ~€250-300m+ in high-margin cabo royalties; fluent narrative answer without numbers is a false-friend signal — it means management is NOT yet ready to disclose the bridge math publicly, which raises uncertainty on EPS trough depth

Q-D2: Palonopeptide Commercial Confidence

"What is your internal probability-weighted peak sales estimate for palonopeptide, and what is the single biggest commercial risk to that estimate?"

  • Expected answer: Management gives a headline peak sales range (consensus circa €500-800m) with reference to label breadth; names HCP adoption rate or market access as the main risk
  • Good answer signature: Specific peak sales number, segment-level breakdown (acromegaly vs further indications), explicit acknowledgement of reimbursement-pathway risk in France/Germany
  • Bad answer signature: "We're very excited about the profile" with no number; deflects to phase 3 data without engaging the commercial build
  • Follow-up probe: "What is your reimbursement assumption in Germany — SMC/AMNOG pathway — and what is the timeline?"
  • Diagnostic note: Palonopeptide is a next-generation somatostatin analogue competing against established lanreotide/octreotide generics; the bear case is that label is narrow and pricing is challenged by generic pressure — management's commercial confidence level (and whether they can quantify it) is highly diagnostic

Q-D3: Capital Allocation Revealed Preference

"If no M&A deal clears your return hurdle in the next 12 months, what is the specific deployment plan for the free cash flow?"

  • Expected answer: References buyback programme, progressive dividend, maintains deal optionality language
  • Good answer signature: Explicit FCF quantum stated, specific buyback pace given (e.g. "€Xm per quarter"), states the opportunity cost of holding cash vs returning it
  • Bad answer signature: "We have a strong pipeline of BD opportunities" without engaging the no-deal scenario; pivots back to M&A optionality without addressing the cash accumulation question
  • Follow-up probe: "Your net cash position has been building — at what point does accumulated cash become an ROE drag rather than optionality?"
  • Diagnostic note: Capital allocation discipline is the single most operator-differentiating variable in mid-cap specialty pharma; management that can NOT answer the no-deal scenario fluently is revealing that BD optionality is being used as a shield against shareholder distribution discipline

4. §2 — THEME 1: REVENUE DRIVERS + VISIBILITY

J-front verdict: Revenue uncertainty is high and concentrated — cabo royalty cliff + palonopeptide ramp timing create a 2026-2027 EPS trough risk that is not fully reflected in consensus

Q2.1 — Cabozantinib Royalty Mechanics

Backlog visibility:

"Walk me through the exact royalty rate structure on cabozantinib — is it tiered by sales volume, and does the renegotiation with Exelixis create a step-down at a specific revenue threshold?"

  • Expected answer: Management acknowledges tiered structure; may not disclose specific rates; references prior renegotiation history
  • Good answer: Provides the specific threshold trigger, confirms or denies a cliff vs gradual step-down, characterises the royalty revenue as fully modelled in guidance
  • Bad answer: "We don't disclose the contractual mechanics" without any directional framing — this leaves the analyst modelling a wide range
  • Follow-up probe: "Is the royalty income line in your 2025 guidance based on Exelixis's consensus cabo sales — or your own internal forecast?"
  • Diagnostic note: Bear case — cabo royalties step down more sharply and sooner than modelled; bull case — Exelixis cabo indication expansion (HCC, RCC label maintenance) sustains royalty run-rate longer than the cliff narrative implies; management's royalty assumption is a binary discriminator

Q2.2 — Palonopeptide Launch Sequencing

Volume vs price split:

"In the palonopeptide launch, is your revenue model more dependent on price per prescription or on penetration rate among somatostatin-naive versus somatostatin-experienced patients?"

  • Expected answer: Both matter; management frames it as penetration in treatment-naive first then switching
  • Good answer: Specific penetration assumption for year-1, year-2; acknowledges the switching barrier (patients on LAR generics are sticky)
  • Bad answer: "It's a highly differentiated product" without engaging the penetration vs price trade-off — this is a red flag for commercial planning immaturity
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the net price you're targeting relative to current-generation LAR formulations — and is there a pricing corridor below which the commercial case doesn't work?"
  • Diagnostic note: Bull case requires premium price AND penetration; false-friend risk is that price is achieved but volume disappoints (physician inertia in acromegaly is high — patients are often stable on existing regimens and HCPs resist switching)

Q2.3 — Sohonos/FOP Commercial Trajectory

End-market segmentation:

"Fibrodysplasia ossificans progressiva has a global diagnosed population of roughly 800 patients — what is your current number of patients on therapy, and what is the realistic ceiling at full penetration?"

  • Expected answer: ~200-350 patients currently on drug; global prevalence ceiling acknowledged; framing as a grow-via-diagnosis platform
  • Good answer: Specific current patient count, specific diagnosis rate improvement assumption, reference to whether EU launch is adding incremental patients vs cannibalising US
  • Bad answer: "FOP is a growing market" (it is not — it is fixed prevalence, ultra-rare); failure to state patient count is a bad-answer red flag
  • Follow-up probe: "If the diagnosed FOP population is essentially captured by 2026, what is the next indication for palovarotene, and when would you expect an IND filing?"
  • Diagnostic note: Sohonos revenue durability depends entirely on whether Ipsen extends palovarotene into adjacent fibro/bone conditions; the bear case is that FOP revenue plateaus at ~€150-200m with no adjacency expansion — this tests the pipeline-dependent revenue-growth narrative

Q2.4 — Onivyde Geographic Expansion

Share gain / loss:

"Onivyde's label was recently expanded to first-line pancreatic cancer in combination — what market share are you targeting in that setting versus gemcitabine/nab-paclitaxel combinations, and in which geographies first?"

  • Expected answer: References NAPOLI-3 data; targets first-line as a significant expansion; US + EU sequencing; share gain framing
  • Good answer: Specific market share target, timeline for EU first-line reimbursement, acknowledgement of FOLFIRINOX as competitive benchmark
  • Bad answer: "It's a meaningful opportunity" without share or market sizing
  • Follow-up probe: "Is the bottleneck on Onivyde growth market access/reimbursement or HCP adoption — which is the rate-limiting step?"
  • Diagnostic note: First-line pancreatic is a larger market but also more competitive; bull case is Onivyde becomes a €400m+ product in first-line; bear case is that reimbursement agencies apply cost-effectiveness scrutiny and restrict to second-line-or-later, capping growth

Sceptical bullet (§2 close): A fluent answer to the palonopeptide penetration question should not generate comfort — management teams with weak commercial plans are often the most fluent at repeating phase 3 data points as a substitute for commercial detail. The diagnostic discriminator is NOT narrative quality — it is whether management can state year-1 prescription numbers, not peak sales ranges.

5. §3 — THEME 2: MARGIN TRAJECTORY + LEVERS

J-front verdict: Core margin uncertainty is the R&D investment cycle — whether the opex step-up is temporary and bounded or structurally higher given mid-stage pipeline density

Q3.1 — R&D Spend Phasing

R&D adequacy:

"Your R&D/sales ratio has been rising — at what revenue level does R&D spending as a percentage of sales plateau, and what is the specific trigger for opex leverage to inflect?"

  • Expected answer: Management references pipeline progression milestones as the driver; states R&D will grow at a lower rate than revenue once palonopeptide launches
  • Good answer: Specific R&D ratio target (e.g. "25-27% normalised"), specific year when phasing peaks, acknowledgement of which programmes are the margin-dilutive spend items
  • Bad answer: "We're investing for long-term growth" without a stated ceiling or inflection trigger
  • Follow-up probe: "Which single programme, if discontinued, would most reduce your R&D burn — and have you run that scenario?"
  • Diagnostic note: Bear case — R&D spend remains elevated as new BD assets require clinical development investment simultaneously with commercial launches; EPS trough is deeper and longer than consensus assumes; good answer gives analyst confidence in the operating leverage recovery timeline

Q3.2 — SG&A Efficiency in a Multi-Launch Environment

Op leverage:

"You are simultaneously launching palonopeptide in acromegaly and supporting Sohonos/FOP and Onivyde first-line — is your commercial infrastructure sized for all three, or do you need to hire materially?"

  • Expected answer: References shared commercial infrastructure; some hires needed but scalable; oncology sales force is already in place from Onivyde
  • Good answer: Specific headcount add expected, specific SG&A absolute level in year-1 post-launch, acknowledgement of whether rare-disease and oncology sales forces are structurally separate
  • Bad answer: "We have an efficient commercial model" — no numbers, no acknowledgement of incremental cost
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the SG&A-to-revenue ratio you are targeting for 2026 and 2027, and what assumptions underpin those ratios?"
  • Diagnostic note: Multi-product launch periods in mid-cap pharma are the highest-SG&A-risk window; consensus may underestimate the commercial cost in 2025-2026

Q3.3 — Gross Margin Mix + Royalty Fade

GM driver:

"When cabozantinib royalties (near-100% gross margin) fade, what is the gross margin of the replacing products — and what is the net gross margin impact of the mix shift?"

  • Expected answer: Royalties are acknowledged as super-margin; replacement products (palonopeptide, Onivyde) are manufactured; GM compression is expected but partially offset by scale
  • Good answer: Product-by-product gross margin stated (or bracketed); net GM impact of mix shift quantified; specific year when GM trough occurs
  • Bad answer: "Our gross margins are strong and sustainable" without engaging the royalty-fade math
  • Follow-up probe: "Is palonopeptide manufactured in-house or under a CMO arrangement — and what is the cost-of-goods structure?"
  • Diagnostic note: Royalty income is 100% gross margin; manufactured drug products carry COGS; the royalty-to-product mix shift structurally compresses gross margin regardless of revenue trajectory — this is a consensus blind spot in bottom-up models

Q3.4 — FX Exposure Management

FX pass-through:

"With material USD revenue (cabozantinib royalties denominated in USD) and EUR-based cost base, what is your natural hedge ratio and what is the EPS sensitivity to a 10% EUR/USD move?"

  • Expected answer: Acknowledges USD/EUR as primary FX pair; references some natural hedge; may use financial derivatives
  • Good answer: Specific EPS sensitivity stated (€Xm per 1% EUR/USD move), specific hedge ratio, maturity of existing hedges
  • Bad answer: "We manage FX exposure prudently" with no quantification
  • Follow-up probe: "As USD-denominated royalties fade, does your FX exposure profile structurally change — and have you adjusted hedging strategy accordingly?"
  • Diagnostic note: EUR strengthening in 2024-2025 is an already-materialised risk; management that cannot quantify FX sensitivity is either not managing it or is choosing not to disclose it — both are signals

6. §4 — THEME 3: CAPITAL ALLOCATION + BALANCE SHEET

J-front verdict: Capital allocation is the highest-variance operator variable — Ipsen's BD history is mixed and the no-deal cash accumulation scenario is under-interrogated by consensus

Q4.1 — M&A Return Hurdle Discipline

RONIC discipline:

"What is your internal IRR hurdle for M&A — and can you walk me through the last deal you passed on and why it didn't clear the hurdle?"

  • Expected answer: References a "mid-teens" IRR hurdle; may not disclose a specific passed deal; references strategic fit as a co-criterion
  • Good answer: Specific IRR number stated, willingness to describe a deal type that fails the screen (e.g. too large, wrong stage, wrong therapeutic area), acknowledgement that financial discipline and strategic fit are sequential not simultaneous gates
  • Bad answer: "We are selective and disciplined acquirers" — no number, no negative example; this is the most common BD non-answer in mid-cap pharma
  • Follow-up probe: "If palonopeptide launches successfully and generates significant FCF, does that increase your deal-size appetite — or does the FCF lower your urgency to buy pipeline?"
  • Diagnostic note: False-friend probe — Ipsen has a history of paying up for rare-disease assets (Sohonos acquisition at a significant premium); the question tests whether the IRR framework is genuinely binding or is narrative decoration

Q4.2 — Shareholder Return Framework

SBB cadence:

"What is the specific buyback pace in 2025 — euros per quarter — and at what share price or leverage ratio does buyback accelerate versus pause?"

  • Expected answer: References existing buyback programme; provides general envelope; links buyback to cash generation
  • Good answer: Specific quarterly buyback quantum, stated trigger for acceleration (e.g. if no BD deal closes by Q3), acknowledgement of the leverage ratio cap
  • Bad answer: "We return capital to shareholders in a balanced way" — no specifics on pace, no trigger framing
  • Follow-up probe: "Is there a board-level cap on buyback as a percentage of average daily volume — and what is it?"
  • Diagnostic note: Buyback optionality is a bull-case support mechanism; if management can't state the pace, the optionality is theoretical not real; consensus may be embedding buyback accretion that management is not actively committed to delivering

Q4.3 — Working Capital Dynamics Post-Launch

Working capital management:

"As palonopeptide and Onivyde first-line launch simultaneously, what is the working capital build you're modelling — and is FCF conversion lower in 2025-2026 than in prior years as a result?"

  • Expected answer: Acknowledges inventory build and trade receivables increase as launches ramp; provides directional FCF conversion guidance
  • Good answer: Specific working capital build estimate, FCF conversion ratio stated for 2025/2026 vs historical 90%+ conversion
  • Bad answer: "We have strong cash generation" without engaging the launch-period working capital drag
  • Follow-up probe: "Is there any milestone payment associated with the palonopeptide licence that would hit FCF in 2025-2026?"
  • Diagnostic note: Launch-period working capital absorption plus milestone obligations can create a transient FCF trough that looks like earnings weakness — consensus models often miss the cash conversion timing impact

7. §5 — THEME 4: STRATEGY + COMPETITIVE POSITION

J-front verdict: Strategic positioning is coherent but dependent on three concurrent execution bets — any one of palonopeptide, Sohonos adjacency, or Onivyde market-share gain missing creates a gap in the post-cabo growth narrative

Q5.1 — Exelixis Relationship Post-2026

Competitive response:

"After the cabozantinib royalty arrangement normalises, what is your strategic relationship with Exelixis — partnership for new cabo indications, competitor, or arms-length licensor?"

  • Expected answer: References the ongoing relationship; suggests collaboration on new indications is possible; frames Exelixis as a key partner not a competitor
  • Good answer: Specific indications where Ipsen and Exelixis are co-invested, clarity on whether Ipsen participates in RCC combo data (cabo + immunotherapy), acknowledgement of where interests diverge
  • Bad answer: "We have a strong relationship with Exelixis" — any failure to engage the post-royalty strategy is avoidance
  • Follow-up probe: "Is there any scenario in which Ipsen pursues a direct oncology programme that competes with Exelixis's pipeline?"
  • Diagnostic note: The cabo relationship is both Ipsen's biggest current revenue driver and potentially a strategic constraint on oncology independence — this is the most underexplored strategic tension in Ipsen's positioning

Q5.2 — Rare Disease Platform Depth

Adjacency expansion:

"Beyond FOP, what is the next rare disease indication in the pipeline — and is the Sohonos acquisition team still intact and generating new programme ideas?"

  • Expected answer: References early-stage fibro/bone programmes; may cite internal discovery work; affirms rare disease as a strategic pillar
  • Good answer: Names specific next-indication candidate, states development timeline, confirms whether Sohonos scientific team is retained and what they're working on
  • Bad answer: "Rare disease is a key pillar" without naming a specific next programme — this suggests the rare disease platform is FOP-only, not a genuine multi-programme platform
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the R&D headcount at the legacy Sohonos team today versus at acquisition, and what is their current programme focus?"
  • Diagnostic note: Bear case is that Sohonos was a one-product acquisition with no follow-on pipeline; bull case is that the scientific platform generates a second FOP-adjacent rare disease candidate within 3-5 years

Q5.3 — Regulatory Positioning + Label Expansion

Regulatory positioning:

"For palonopeptide, are you planning a sNDA for broader neuroendocrine tumour indications — and what is the regulatory pathway and timeline if so?"

  • Expected answer: References acromegaly as the launch indication; may mention NETs as a longer-term target; regulatory timeline uncertain
  • Good answer: Specific indication expansion plan, named regulatory pathway (FDA/EMA), timeline for data package, acknowledgement of whether Phase 3 data is needed vs supplemental data
  • Bad answer: "We're focused on the acromegaly launch" — entirely reasonable but if this is the only answer, it means the label expansion optionality is speculative not programmatic
  • Follow-up probe: "Is the NET indication opportunity a data-generation question or a commercial-strategy question — i.e. do you have the data already?"
  • Diagnostic note: Palonopeptide's differentiated pharmacology (once-weekly injectable vs monthly LAR) could support a broader somatostatin-receptor-positive tumour strategy — if management has a clear NET expansion plan, the peak sales ceiling is materially higher

8. §6 — THEME 5: MANAGEMENT QUALITY + OPERATOR TRACK RECORD

J-front verdict: CEO/CFO tenure covers the pivotal 2020-2025 BD and pipeline reconstruction cycle — track record is assessable; incentive structure alignment is the load-bearing question

Q6.1 — Sandbagging Discipline and Guidance Track Record

Sandbagging discipline:

"Over the last four years, has Ipsen's guidance been consistently met, beaten, or missed — and which product line has generated the most guidance variance?"

  • Expected answer: Defends overall guidance track record; may acknowledge COVID-period miss; attributes any variance to external factors
  • Good answer: Specific quantification of guidance variance (beat/miss distribution), acknowledgement of a specific product that caused variance, statement of the guidance-setting methodology (conservative vs mid-case)
  • Bad answer: "We have a strong track record of delivery" — generic claim without accepting the empirical question
  • Follow-up probe: "What is your guidance methodology — do you set guidance at a mid-case scenario or at a conservative floor?"
  • Diagnostic note: Power of incentives — management teams compensated on relative TSR or absolute EPS will systematically under-guide to manage miss risk; knowing the guidance philosophy predicts future guidance reliability

Q6.2 — Compensation Incentive Structure (Power of Incentives)

Power of incentives:

"What are the specific KPIs in the CEO's long-term incentive plan — and what percentage of LTI is tied to absolute EPS versus relative TSR versus pipeline milestones?"

  • Expected answer: References annual report compensation section; likely a mix of revenue growth, EPS, and rTSR; may provide breakdown
  • Good answer: Specific KPI weightings stated, acknowledgement of which metric is most influencing current decision-making, statement of whether pipeline milestones are binary (hit/miss) or partial-credit
  • Bad answer: "Our compensation is disclosed in the annual report" — this is technically true but unhelpful; the diagnostic question is which KPI is behaviorally dominant
  • Follow-up probe: "If the LTI were 100% rTSR rather than absolute EPS, would you be making different capital allocation decisions right now?"
  • Diagnostic note: This is the Concept A power-of-incentives probe — compensation structure predicts capital allocation posture; a CEO heavily weighted on rTSR has a different M&A risk appetite than one weighted on absolute EPS

Q6.3 — Crisis Playbook and Adverse-Event Management

Crisis playbook:

"When the FDA issued the Complete Response Letter on a prior submission, how was the internal response organised — who owned the CRL response, and what did you learn about your regulatory process?"

  • Expected answer: References the specific regulatory history; may be defensive; will likely reference cross-functional teams and FDA engagement
  • Good answer: Names the person who owned the response, acknowledges the specific process failure, states what changed structurally in the regulatory function post-CRL
  • Bad answer: "We take regulatory compliance very seriously" without engaging the specifics of what changed
  • Follow-up probe: "Is the regulatory team today more internally resourced or more dependent on external consultants than before the CRL experience?"
  • Diagnostic note: How management handled a past adverse event is a direct window into the crisis-management culture — organisations that internalise regulatory failure and change process are structurally lower-risk than organisations that attribute failure to external factors

Q6.4 — Communication Clarity on Pipeline Uncertainty

Communication clarity:

"What is the programme in your pipeline right now where your confidence in Phase 3 success is lowest — and what is the go/no-go decision trigger?"

  • Expected answer: May cite an early-stage programme; deflects to "all our programmes have strong scientific rationale"
  • Good answer: Names a specific programme, quantifies the probability of success internally (even as a range), states the specific trial endpoint that would trigger discontinuation
  • Bad answer: Any formulation of "all our programmes have strong potential" — this is management signalling that they won't publicly differentiate risk within the portfolio, which is itself a signal about communication quality
  • Follow-up probe: "When do you expect to make a go/no-go decision on [named programme] — and what does a go decision look like operationally?"
  • Diagnostic note: Management willingness to rank pipeline uncertainty is a primary quality signal; unwillingness to rank is consistent with the false-friend profile where the portfolio is managed for optionality presentation rather than capital discipline

9. §7 — THEME 6: RISK + DOWNSIDE

J-front verdict: Three high-plausibility failure modes from Q19 pre-mortem logic — cabo royalty cliff deeper/sooner than modelled, palonopeptide commercial miss, and European pricing compression — are the diagnostic risk questions

Q7.1 — Cabo Cliff + EPS Trough Depth

Bear-case probe:

"In your stress scenario — cabo royalties step down 40% in 2026, palonopeptide year-1 volume comes in at 50% of plan — what does EPS look like, and is there a year in which EPS is materially below 2024 levels?"

  • Expected answer: Declines to give the stress scenario number; references portfolio resilience; pivots to base case
  • Good answer: Acknowledges the scenario is modellable, gives even a directional answer ("EPS would be under pressure but we'd still be profitable and cash-generative"), acknowledges the trough year
  • Bad answer: "We don't give stress-case guidance" — completely legitimate but if this is the only answer, the analyst should assume the stress scenario is worse than management wants to acknowledge
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the minimum EPS level at which you would pause the buyback programme — is there a floor EPS below which capital return policy changes?"
  • Diagnostic note: The ACH matrix from Q19 pre-mortem — the bear case requires simultaneous cabo cliff AND palonopeptide miss; the question probes whether management has modelled that intersection or is relying on portfolio resilience narrative

Q7.2 — European Pricing Pressure

Pricing fragility:

"In Germany and France, what pricing assumptions underpin your palonopeptide revenue model — and what is the revenue impact if you receive a 30% pricing discount versus your base case?"

  • Expected answer: References AMNOG process in Germany, transparency pricing in France; may decline to give specific discount assumption
  • Good answer: Specific base-case net price stated, specific price corridor tested (e.g. best-case, base, worst-case), acknowledgement of which market is the pricing risk
  • Bad answer: "We are working constructively with HTA agencies" — this is the most common EU pricing non-answer and is diagnostically useless
  • Follow-up probe: "If Germany grants a non-benefit (Zusatznutzen nicht belegt) status to palonopeptide, what is your commercial plan — re-label, re-price, or withdraw?"
  • Diagnostic note: EU HTA reform (EUTR from 2025) is restructuring the joint clinical assessment landscape; palonopeptide as a new somatostatin analogue in a market with generic alternatives is a high HTA-scrutiny target; this is an under-asked question in consensus analyst Q&A

Q7.3 — Pipeline Dependency Post-Cabo

Failure mode test:

"If palonopeptide approval is delayed 12 months, and Onivyde first-line reimbursement is delayed in Europe 12 months, what does your 2026 P&L look like — and which cost lines are fixed versus discretionary?"

  • Expected answer: Acknowledges delay scenarios are possible; references cost flexibility; may note that multiple products reduce single-event dependency
  • Good answer: Acknowledges fixed cost base (R&D commitments, field force already hired), identifies specific cost lines that are discretionary (third-party R&D, BD pipeline investment), provides directional margin impact
  • Bad answer: "We have a diversified portfolio so a single delay wouldn't be catastrophic" — true but non-responsive; the diagnostic value is in which costs are truly fixed
  • Follow-up probe: "How many months of delay would trigger a structural cost-reduction programme — do you have a contingency plan that's been stress-tested at the board level?"
  • Diagnostic note: Fixed vs discretionary cost structure determines the operational leverage of the downside; a high fixed-cost base combined with delay risk is the core mechanics of the bear case

Q7.4 — False-Friend Probe — Rare Disease Label Durability

False-friend probe:

"Sohonos/palovarotene received an FDA approval with a specific age restriction and administration requirement — have there been any adverse events or post-marketing signals that concern you about the label durability?"

  • Expected answer: References the FDA-approved label; notes the age-restriction reflects clinical trial design; states post-marketing surveillance is ongoing
  • Good answer: Specific post-marketing data update, acknowledgement of any label-related constraint on prescriber behaviour, proactive reference to patient registry data
  • Bad answer: "The product has a well-characterised safety profile" — this is the exact formulation used when management is managing a regulatory communication rather than disclosing a commercial insight
  • Follow-up probe: "Is there any ongoing FDA dialogue that could result in a label amendment — and in which direction (broader or narrower)?"
  • Diagnostic note: The FDA age restriction on palovarotene is a known commercial constraint; any signal of label broadening is a positive catalyst; any signal of label narrowing or post-marketing safety concern is an asymmetric downside that is not in consensus models

Sceptical bullet (§7 close): Q19 ACH diagnostic items NOT covered by any question in this memo: (a) the detailed mechanics of the Exelixis royalty renegotiation (rates, floors, caps) are not explicitly addressed in a single question — this gap should be closed; (b) the post-market pharmacovigilance profile of Onivyde in first-line pancreatic is not probed — first-line expansion introduces a new patient population that may have a different safety signal; (c) the European regulatory approval timeline for palonopeptide (EMA vs FDA sequential vs parallel) is assumed but not directly probed.

10. §8 — CROSS-CUTTING META + NON-OBVIOUS QUESTIONS

J-front verdict: Management will have rehearsed answers to all pipeline and royalty questions — the diagnostic edge is in questions that require on-the-fly arithmetic or require management to contradict their own public narrative

Non-obvious questions + WH Smith probe angles

Non-obvious angle — internal P&L fluency:

"On the call with your CFO: if palonopeptide generates €300m in year-3 revenue, walk me through the actual operating profit contribution — COGS, SG&A allocation, R&D attribution — and what's the product-level EBIT margin?"
- Why non-obvious: Consensus models top-down group margin; the diagnostic value is in whether management knows the product-level margin structure, which is a proxy for how actively they manage portfolio-level capital allocation
- Good answer signal: CFO can do this arithmetic fluently, even with some approximation
- Bad answer signal: Defers to group margin disclosure without engaging product-level margin — indicates the company is managed as a portfolio narrative rather than a capital-allocation portfolio

Non-obvious angle — competitor intelligence:
⚡ "What is your commercial team learning from early Exelixis RCC combination data — and are there signals in that data that change your view of how long the cabo royalty tail lasts?"
- Why outlier: This question puts Ipsen on the other side of the Exelixis telescope — as a royalty holder with an information asymmetry.
