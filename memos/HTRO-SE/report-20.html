§1 — Executive Summary: Top-3 Most-Diagnostic Questions

[J] Three questions determine whether HTRO is a compounding platform or a cyclical recovery story with a 2028 target that cannot be reached.

Most-diagnostic question #1 — DC Microduct Switching Cost:
"When a hyperscaler renews a DC campus microduct contract, what is the average cost of switching to a competing supplier, expressed as a percentage of the original contract value — and can you give us a specific customer example?"
This question cuts directly to whether DC revenue has durable moat or is vulnerable to bid-out on renewal. The expected answer should include reference to recabling cost, integration standards, and proprietary connector/coupler system dependency. A vague answer about "relationships" or "service quality" is a bad answer.

Most-diagnostic question #2 — M&A Machine Replicability:
"Fröberg ran 20+ acquisitions at Ahlsell — an industrial wholesale distributor. HTRO's acquisition targets are mostly fiber connectivity specialists. What is the single most important thing you have learned at HTRO that was NOT transferable from Ahlsell, and how has it changed your acquisition criteria?"
This directly tests whether Fröberg's M&A track record is portable to a structurally different business. A specific, candid answer signals self-awareness and genuine learning. A confident dismissal of the distinction is a significant warning sign.

Most-diagnostic question #3 — FTTH Order Book Visibility:
"What percentage of your projected 2026 FTTH North America revenue is currently covered by signed contracts or committed purchase orders — as of today?"
The BEAD rollout narrative is ubiquitous in sell-side coverage. This question forces the CFO to quantify committed backlog vs. pipeline, separating signal from noise.

Sceptical bullet: The questions the analyst does NOT want to ask are: (a) whether Weterings represented a systematic failure of acquisition due diligence or a one-off, because a systematic failure would invalidate the M&A machine thesis entirely; and (b) whether DC growth is partly pulled-forward demand from hyperscalers pre-building capacity ahead of tariff uncertainty — because if so, the 2028 trajectory is flattered by timing, not structural demand. The analyst avoids these because the answers could force a fundamental re-rating of the investment case rather than a refinement.

§2 — Theme 1: Revenue Drivers + Visibility

Q1 — DC Contract Structure and Renewal Dynamics

Verbatim question (≤30w): "When a DC campus microduct contract comes up for renewal, what share of your customers have re-bid to alternative suppliers in the last three years, and what was the outcome?"

Expected answer: Management should know the re-bid rate. For a high-moat product, re-bid rate should be low (sub-20%) and win rate on re-bids should be very high (above 80%). They should reference integrated microduct-connector-coupler systems that create recabling complexity.

Good answer signature: Specific re-bid data with win rate; reference to integration lock-in at the hardware/connector level; examples of customers who attempted to switch and reversed course; commentary on total switching cost including labour.

Bad answer signature: Vague reference to "long-term relationships" and "customer satisfaction"; inability to quantify re-bid rate; deflection to pipeline growth rather than renewal retention.

Follow-up probe: "Are any of your top-5 DC customers currently in an active re-bid or RFP process for their next campus build-out, and can you tell us which suppliers are in the room?"

Diagnostic note: Tests ACH-DC-1 (DC moat is structural) vs. ACH-DC-2 (DC revenue is relationship-dependent with no switching cost moat). This is the single highest-leverage question in the meeting. A good answer is consistent with KD#1 bull case (YELLOW to GREEN); a bad answer forces KD#1 to RED.

Q2 — FTTH North America Order Book

Good answer signature: Specific percentage with breakdown between firm POs and framework agreements; reference to BEAD state approval milestones and how these translate into customer commitment; named (or described) contractor relationship depth.

Bad answer signature: "We don't disclose order book figures" without any directional guidance; conflation of BEAD program size with HTRO's addressable share; inability to distinguish between awarded and committed.

Follow-up probe: "Blue Ridge industry experts suggest 25-35% of installation crews in BEAD states are currently microtrench-capable — how does this labour bottleneck affect your delivery timeline assumptions for 2026?"

Diagnostic note: Tests KD#3 (FTTH recovery) and cross-references Q15 Blue Ridge expert finding. If management cannot quantify backlog and deflects the crew-capacity question, the BEAD recovery narrative is sell-side-driven, not management-validated. Adjust FTTH recovery probability downward.
