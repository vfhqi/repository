§2 — D1 Deep Dive: AI & Cloud / Optical-IP (Infinera)

D1 is the load-bearing driver for the bull case. At ESA depth, the evidence base is substantially stronger than at Triaging. Three new hard data points distinguish ESA from Q7: the named Google switching design win, the NVIDIA $1bn equity investment, and the confirmation that Nokia is now #1 in optical market share.

Mechanism to EPS: #A Optical Networks and IP Routing are housed within the Network Infrastructure segment. At ~45%+ gross margins, incremental optical/IP revenue flows through to operating profit at approximately 2.5x the rate of Mobile Infrastructure revenue. The mechanism is: hyperscaler AI data centre buildout → demand for 400G/800G coherent optics and IP routing → Nokia order intake → revenue recognition 6-12 months later → EPS. Management raised FY-26 optical/IP combined growth guidance to 18-20% CAGR vs prior 10-12%, implying €700m+ incremental revenue over the year at these margins.

Q1-26 delivery evidence: #I Net sales from AI & Cloud customers grew 49% YoY. Total Optical Networks net sales grew 20% YoY. AI & Cloud accounted for 8% of group net sales. Q1-26 AI & Cloud order intake was €1bn — roughly equal to the entire segment's quarterly revenue run-rate, implying book-to-bill well above 1.0. Group comparable operating profit increased 54% YoY to ~€560m, with Network Infrastructure the primary contributor.

Named hyperscaler anchor — Google: #I Jefferies identifies a firm Q1-26 IP Networks switching design win, believed to be Google, with shipments beginning Q2-26. This is the most specific order-level evidence available for the AI & Cloud thesis and represents a named large-enterprise commitment that is not yet fully reflected in consensus revenue models. Google Cloud's infrastructure capex is multi-year and locked into supply agreements — this is not a one-quarter order.

⚡ RARE — NVIDIA $1bn equity investment: #J NVIDIA made a $1bn equity investment in Nokia in late 2025. Strategic equity investments by GPU leaders in telecom/networking infrastructure companies are rare events. This validates the GPU-based AI-RAN architecture thesis and aligns NVIDIA's commercial interest directly with Nokia's execution. A $1bn equity commitment signals multi-year strategic intent, not a marketing arrangement. NVIDIA does not make equity investments in companies whose execution they doubt.

Infinera integration mechanics: #A Nokia completed the Infinera acquisition on 28 February 2025 for $2.3bn. Infinera's photonic integrated circuit technology, combined with Nokia's digital signal processing expertise, creates a vertically integrated optical platform that competitors cannot replicate quickly. Ciena (the primary pure-play competitor) lacks the PIC-DSP integration; Lumentum and Coherent are component suppliers without end-to-end system capability. Nokia's #1 optical market share in Q4-25 (displacing Ciena for the first time) is the direct output of this integration. The synergy target of >€200m net comparable operating profit by 2027 equates to >10% comparable EPS accretion — at scale, this is the difference between a 12% and 14% group operating margin.

Market structure — optical competitive dynamics: #A The optical networking market is a duopoly-plus-fringe: Nokia/Infinera + Ciena hold the majority of coherent optical systems revenue. Huawei is largely excluded from Western data centre customers. Nokia's vertical integration (PIC manufacturing capability at Infinera) is a structural advantage that takes 3-5 years for a competitor to replicate. This is not a commoditised market — bespoke coherent optical systems require deep technical relationships with hyperscalers. Nokia's Google design win is evidence that these relationships are being built.

Hyperscaler capex context at ESA depth: #I Aggregate hyperscaler AI capex expectations (Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta) increased from ~$540bn (Nov-25 estimates) to over $700bn by early 2026. Nokia raised its addressable AI & Cloud market CAGR estimate to 27% (2025-2028) from prior 16%. The data centre interconnect and campus spine networking market — Nokia's primary addressable slice — is growing faster than total hyperscaler capex because it represents a disproportionate share of AI-specific new builds (inference clusters require dense optical interconnect within and between DCs).

Product cycle visibility: #I Nokia has launched coherent optical pluggable transceivers and transponder modules for campus, metro, and regional data centre networks, with general availability targeted for H2-2027. This means the current 2026 optical/IP revenue is hardware system sales; the 2027-28 revenue adds the pluggable transceiver market (higher volume, lower ASP but broader deployment). The product roadmap is visible and the next cycle is already in development.

Peer/base-rate benchmarking: #A Ciena (pure-play optical) trades at ~20-22x EV/EBITDA on mid-cycle consensus. Nokia's Network Infrastructure segment, if valued at Ciena-comparable multiples, would imply an enterprise value for the segment alone approximating Nokia's current group EV. This is the sum-of-parts re-rating argument: the market may be on the cusp of valuing Nokia as a sum-of-parts rather than a blended telecom equipment multiple, which would be structurally supportive of further de-conglomeration optionality.

Bear case on D1 — timing and concentration: #J Hyperscaler capex guidance is public and well-telegraphed — it is possible the market has already fully priced the 2026-27 optical/IP growth trajectory. Nokia's revenue recognition lags order intake by 6-12 months, meaning a slowdown in orders in H2-2026 would appear in 2027 financials, creating a lag effect that could cause earnings disappointment 12-18 months after any order slowdown. Additionally, AI & Cloud remains 8% of group sales — if Nokia guides to a one-quarter order normalisation (even from a high base), the market reaction would be disproportionate given the current multiple.

❌ Single-segment concentration risk: #A AI & Cloud contributing 8% of group sales while arguably driving 50%+ of the current market multiple creates a fragile valuation architecture. The 2027-28 financials need to show AI & Cloud scaling to 15-20% of group sales (requiring roughly 2x further from here) for the current valuation to be supported by fundamentals rather than narrative.

❌ Infinera integration execution risk: #A The >€200m net synergy target requires Nokia to simultaneously integrate Infinera's manufacturing, R&D, and sales organisations while accelerating revenue growth into a hot market. Integration typically absorbs management bandwidth and can cause temporary customer satisfaction issues. Nokia has not disclosed whether Infinera's customer relationships (primarily with US carriers and cable operators) have been retained without disruption post-acquisition.

Upside scenario on D1: #J If hyperscaler AI data centre buildout extends to 2028 at current pace AND Nokia converts the Google design win into a multi-year platform relationship AND the Infinera synergies deliver on schedule, Network Infrastructure operating margins could reach 17%+ (top of 2028 guidance range) by 2027, implying approximately €1.6-1.8bn of operating profit from the segment alone. This would represent ~60% of the current 2028 group OP midpoint target from a single segment.

Plausibility verdict at ESA: High plausibility. The evidence base is now materially stronger than Triaging: named hyperscaler orders, NVIDIA equity commitment, #1 optical share, and a visible product roadmap. The primary assumption requiring ongoing monitoring is hyperscaler capex trajectory. If this holds, D1 is the most powerful driver in Nokia's architecture.

§3 — D2 Deep Dive: Mobile Networks RAN Recovery

D2 has been downgraded from Medium to Low-Medium plausibility at ESA depth. New data points — Verizon capex cuts, continued global share erosion, and the structural AT&T loss — materially constrain the recovery trajectory compared to the Q7 Triaging assessment.

Mechanism to EPS: #A Mobile Networks (Radio Access Networks) serves MNOs globally with 5G macro base stations, Massive MIMO radios, and baseband units. At current gross margins of ~44-46%, Mobile Infrastructure is lower-margin than optical/IP. The mechanism is: carrier capex recovery → Nokia RAN orders → revenue recognition (2-3 quarter lag) → EPS contribution, but with lower flow-through than optical because of competitive pricing pressure.

Trough calibration: #I Nokia's Mobile Networks business entered a severe cycle trough in 2023-24. Primary drivers: Reliance Jio first-wave 5G rollout completion (removed ~€500m+ annual revenue), AT&T contract loss to Ericsson (€14bn deal), and broad global carrier capex digestion. Mobile Networks net sales fell 25% YoY at the trough in Q2-24. This was the steepest single-segment decline Nokia had experienced in a decade.

Recovery evidence — what is confirmed: #I Nokia regained approximately 18,000 net base station sites since the start of 2024. Mobile Networks grew 6% in Q4-25 — the first positive YoY growth after several quarters of decline. Nokia held flat for full year 2025 against a market that declined 2%, representing ~2pp of relative outperformance. T-Mobile multi-year extension signed for baseband and radio technologies supporting 5G SA deployment. Nokia's North American net sales grew double digits in Q4-24.

AT&T structural loss — permanent cap on recovery: #J The AT&T $14bn Ericsson network deal (2023) removed Nokia from the second-largest US carrier. This is a structural, not cyclical, headwind. US carrier networks represent Nokia's historically highest-margin geography. The AT&T loss caps Nokia's North American RAN market share recovery and creates a permanent €300-500m+ annual revenue hole relative to pre-2023 run rates. There is no plausible path to AT&T reversal within a 24-month horizon.

Verizon capex cuts — new 2026 headwind: #J Verizon has signalled significant capex reductions for 2026. Verizon is Nokia's largest remaining major US carrier customer. If Verizon reduces RAN capex materially, Nokia's North American mobile revenue — the primary recovery narrative in 2024-25 — faces renewed pressure in 2026.

Global RAN share deterioration — the hard data: #J Nokia's global RAN market share fell from 19.5% (2023) to 17.6% (2024). This occurred despite Huawei being legislatively excluded from multiple large markets. The implication is that Nokia is not winning the Huawei displacement sites — Samsung and Ericsson are. This is a structurally negative data point that fundamentally challenges the Huawei displacement narrative (D5) and the broader RAN recovery thesis (D2). If Nokia cannot gain share when Huawei is banned, it is losing share to Western competitors on technology and price grounds.

5G-Advanced and AI-RAN — timing constraints: #I Nokia's AI-RAN product (GPU-based radio access node using NVIDIA GPUs) targets field trials in Q4-26 and commercial availability from end-2027. This is a 2028+ revenue driver. For the 24-month investment horizon, AI-RAN is a strategic optionality and a re-rating catalyst (if trials succeed and operators commit), but it is not a 2026-27 revenue contribution. Nokia's AI-RAN architecture (GPU-based, NVIDIA partnership) is differentiated from Ericsson's ASIC approach — the strategic bet is that software-defined GPU architecture is more future-flexible, which operators will value as 6G approaches.

Huawei displacement — carrier upgrade cycle overlap: #A Nokia's share erosion coincides with a period when European carriers should theoretically be replacing Huawei equipment with Nokia or Ericsson. That Nokia is losing share during this period suggests either: (a) carriers are choosing Ericsson or Samsung over Nokia for Huawei replacements, or (b) carriers are delaying Huawei replacement and keeping incumbent kit longer than legislated deadlines. Both interpretations are bearish for D2 within the 24M horizon.

Dell'Oro market projection: #I Dell'Oro projects global RAN market to remain "mostly stable" in 2026. This means Nokia's RAN recovery depends entirely on market share gains — not market growth. Nokia needs to recapture share that is declining. This is a structurally harder ask than participating in a market recovery.

Bear case on D2 — realistic scenario: #J If Verizon cuts 2026 capex 20%, Nokia's North American mobile revenue could decline 15-20% vs 2025. If global RAN share continues declining (17.6% → 16.5%), Nokia loses incremental volume across the remaining customer base. The 2028 Mobile Infrastructure gross margin target of 48-50% requires both cost delivery AND pricing power — the latter is being compressed by Samsung competition. D2 could be a drag on group earnings in 2026-27 while D1 is the growth engine, resulting in a bifurcated P&L that is harder to value.

❌ Samsung competitive threat: #A Samsung has been the primary beneficiary of Huawei displacement in Europe and Asia. Samsung's Open RAN-native architecture is winning deals in Deutsche Telekom and other tier-1 carriers where Nokia expected to benefit. This is a structural competitive dynamic, not cyclical — Samsung has made sustained investments in RAN technology and is now a credible tier-1 alternative to Nokia and Ericsson in markets where Huawei is excluded.

Upside scenario on D2: #J If the T-Mobile/Nokia AI-RAN Innovation Center produces compelling field trial results in 2026-27, operators could begin pre-committing to Nokia's 5G equipment ahead of AI-RAN commercial launch (logic: install Nokia 5G now to position for AI-RAN upgrade path with minimal stranded asset cost). This is the Jefferies thesis and is the most credible upside scenario for D2 re-rating within 24 months. Probability: medium-low at ESA stage, but worth monitoring as AI-RAN trial results emerge.

Plausibility verdict at ESA: Low-Medium. Stabilisation is confirmed but recovery is structurally constrained by AT&T loss, Verizon capex cuts, global share erosion, and a flat RAN market. D2 is not a near-term catalyst — it is a monitoring item that needs to stop deteriorating before it becomes an investment thesis.

§4 — D3 Deep Dive: Nokia Technologies Licensing

Nokia Technologies is the company's most distinctive competitive asset and is consistently underweighted in sell-side analysis. At ESA depth, the contracted revenue structure and automotive/IoT trajectory warrant a full-section treatment.

Mechanism to EPS: #A Nokia Technologies generates royalty income on standard-essential patents (SEPs) across 2G-5G wireless standards. Revenue carries near-100% gross margin — effectively every €1 of licensing revenue flows directly to gross profit with minimal variable cost. Technologies contributes approximately €1.0-1.3bn in gross margin per year, representing ~20-25% of Nokia's total gross margin from approximately 5-8% of total revenue. The mechanism to EPS is direct: stable/growing licensing revenue at near-100% margin is the highest-quality earnings component in Nokia's portfolio.

Contracted revenue floor: #I Nokia has secured over €800m of contracted recurring net sales annually through to 2030. This means approximately 60%+ of Technologies revenue is contractually locked — not subject to renewal negotiation or litigation risk — for the next four years. This is a structural earnings floor that provides EPS visibility absent from the equipment segments. The contracted-through-2030 structure is rare in the technology sector — most IP licensing businesses face renewal cliffs every 3-5 years.

Smartphone renewal cycle completion — a critical de-risking event: #J Nokia completed its smartphone patent licence renewal cycle (2021-2024) with agreements signed with Apple, Samsung, Huawei, OPPO, vivo, and Honor. This resolved a multi-year uncertainty that caused Nokia to miss its 2023 Technologies guidance. The renewal completion means Nokia enters 2026-2030 with the world's largest smartphone manufacturers locked in as paying licensees, with contracted rates that reflect current 5G royalty structures.

Automotive expansion — structural growth overlay: #I Nokia has licensed WLAN and cellular connectivity technology to five major automakers, including Mercedes-Benz. Four Chinese OEMs are licensed in the automotive sector. The connected vehicle market adds approximately 80-100m new devices annually to Nokia's addressable licensing pool, each with wireless connectivity requirements under Nokia's SEP portfolio. The automotive licensing revenue run-rate is now exceeding €200m annually (per company disclosures), growing from near-zero in 2020.

IoT and consumer electronics expansion: #I 2025 saw new licensing agreements with three consumer electronics companies and a notable Amazon deal covering video technologies in streaming services and connected devices (March 2025). Amazon's scale (100m+ Echo devices, Fire TV units, Kindle) represents a meaningful new royalty stream. IoT device proliferation is in early innings — Nokia's 7,000+ declared 5G patent families will remain relevant as IoT devices migrate to 5G connectivity through 2030.

Peer anchoring — Qualcomm comparison: #A Qualcomm derives approximately $9bn annually from its technology licensing business , operating at ~70% operating margins. Nokia at €1.3bn is subscale by comparison but the architecture is identical — SEP licensing based on declared essential patents to wireless standards. The key difference is that Qualcomm has additional leverage through its baseband chip relationships (licensee volume = chip customer volume), which Nokia lacks. However, Nokia's trajectory (automotive, IoT expansion) mirrors Qualcomm's historical licensing growth arc from 2010-2020 when Qualcomm expanded beyond smartphones into automotive and IoT.

Earnings anchor — Ericsson comparison: #A Ericsson has no equivalent licensing segment of comparable scale or margin quality. Nokia's Technologies segment provides approximately €1.0-1.3bn in gross margin contribution regardless of equipment cycle — a structural resilience floor that Ericsson cannot replicate. In a scenario where RAN markets remain flat and AI/optical spending moderates, Nokia's earnings floor is materially higher than Ericsson's.

Litigation risk — the structural constraint: #J SEP licensing is inherently contested. Hold-out by licensees (OPPO and vivo disputes in prior years caused €100-200m revenue delays) is an ongoing risk. EU SEP Regulation discussions (potential FRAND rate-setting framework) represent a regulatory overhang that could reduce Nokia's leverage in future negotiations. Nokia's ability to enforce licences through import injunctions (particularly in the US) is a key tool — any legislative weakening of injunction rights would reduce hold-out costs for licensees.

Automotive licensing TAM expansion: #A The total automotive connected device TAM for Nokia's licensing is difficult to size precisely, but the structural direction is clear: every new car produced globally has 5G or WiFi connectivity (or both), and both are subject to Nokia's SEP portfolio. Global vehicle production is approximately 85m units annually. If Nokia's automotive licence rate per vehicle is €2-5 (consistent with peer SEP licensing economics), the steady-state automotive licensing revenue potential is €170-425m annually — compared to the current €200m+ run-rate that includes both automotive OEMs and IoT. Growth from here is structural and multi-year.

Bear case on D3: #J EU SEP Regulation, if enacted in its more aggressive form (mandatory FRAND rate-setting with independent FRAND arbitration), could reduce Nokia's negotiating leverage and compress royalty rates on new agreements post-2028. Chinese OEM holdouts (in the context of broader geopolitical deterioration) could delay recognition of €200-300m in annual revenue, as experienced in 2023. IoT licensing scale-up requires each new device category to be litigated or negotiated separately — the path from €200m automotive/IoT to €400m is not linear.

Plausibility verdict at ESA: High plausibility. The contracted floor is locked, the smartphone cycle is complete, automotive expansion is delivering, and the IoT pipeline is building. Key monitoring items: EU SEP Regulation progress; any major Chinese OEM dispute; rate structures in 2028 renewal cycle negotiations.

§5 — D4, D5, D6: Supporting Drivers

D4 — Cost Savings + Margin Expansion

Programme delivery status: #I Nokia has achieved approximately €800m of the targeted €1.2bn gross savings by early 2026. Two-thirds delivery is confirmed. The remaining €400m targets 2026 completion through further headcount rationalisation, real estate consolidation, and procurement optimisation.

2028 financial targets — the loaded bet: #A The 2028 OP guidance range of €2.7-3.2bn (midpoint €3.0bn) from a 2025 base of approximately €2.0bn requires both cost delivery AND revenue growth. D4 alone (cost savings without revenue growth) can contribute approximately €400-600m of the gap. The remaining €400-600m must come from optical/IP revenue leverage and Mobile Infrastructure margin expansion. D4 and D1 are co-dependent — neither achieves the 2028 targets without the other.

Operating leverage math: #A Nokia's comparable OpEx base is approximately €4.5-5.0bn (2025). The 2028 central cost target of €150m (from €350m current) implies a €200m reduction in the corporate centre alone. Combined with segment-level cost reductions, the total gross savings programme is targeting a structurally lower fixed cost base — which means that D1 revenue growth flows through to operating profit at a higher conversion rate in 2027-28 than in 2024-25.

FCF quality: #A FY-25 FCF of €1.5bn (72% conversion) is strong by telecom equipment sector standards. Ericsson typically converts 50-60% of operating profit to FCF. Nokia's higher conversion reflects structural improvements in working capital management and reduced restructuring cash costs. The AGM Apr-26 approved a €0.14/share dividend and 550m share buyback valid through Oct-27 — both funded from FCF without incremental debt.

❌ Reinvestment tension: #A Nokia is simultaneously targeting cost savings and accelerating R&D spend for AI-RAN and next-gen optical products (€2bn+ annual R&D). This creates a tension in the cost programme — gross savings may be partially offset by higher R&D and product development costs, and the net OpEx reduction could disappoint vs the gross savings headline. Nokia has not disaggregated net vs gross savings targets publicly.

Plausibility verdict at ESA (D4): Medium-High. The programme is two-thirds delivered and structurally credible. The 2028 OP targets require D1 to materialise. Primary monitoring: whether gross savings are being offset by R&D reinvestment, and whether the Nokia Technologies cost allocation is distorting the true segment margin picture.

D5 — Huawei Displacement (EU + Global Restricted Markets)

Market opportunity size: #I Nokia CEO Hotard estimates Huawei's directly competing European business at €2.0-2.5bn annually. Chinese vendors (Huawei + ZTE) still hold an estimated 33-40% of the European RAN market despite multi-year security legislation. This is the most important contextual fact: the displacement has not happened yet at scale.

Confirmed wins: #I Nokia has displaced Huawei at 3,000+ Deutsche Telekom 5G sites. Deutsche Telekom's stated assessment: Nokia is "as good as or better than Huawei" — a direct commercial validation. Germany mandates core network Huawei/ZTE removal by end-2026, RAN management system replacement by end-2029. Nokia is positioned as the primary beneficiary at DT, Europe's largest carrier by network scale.

The share erosion problem: #J Nokia's global RAN share fell from 19.5% to 17.6% in 2024 — during the period when Huawei displacement was supposed to be adding volume. The interpretation at ESA depth is unambiguous: Nokia is not capturing the Huawei displacement volume proportionate to its starting market share. Samsung is winning a disproportionate share of Huawei replacement contracts, particularly in Germany and Asia. This is a hard data point that downgrades D5 from "a medium-confidence catalyst" to "a slow-burn with uncertain Nokia share of the prize."

Legislative framework — incomplete execution: #J German telcos received deadline extensions. UK RAN deadline is 2027 (core completed). Belgium, France, Sweden have varying frameworks. European carriers have been slow to execute because Huawei displacement carries stranded asset costs and requires network management platform migration — not just hardware swap. Nokia's opportunity is real but the timeline to revenue recognition is 2027-2029, not 2026.

❌ Samsung structural gain: #A Samsung's Open RAN-native architecture is winning European displacement contracts. Nokia's more traditional RAN architecture requires operators to adopt Nokia-specific management platforms; Samsung offers greater flexibility for operators pursuing Open RAN strategies. This is a structural competitive disadvantage in the displacement market specifically.

⚡ RARE market structure data: #J Huawei maintaining 33-40% European RAN market share despite multi-year security legislation is an extraordinary persistence of incumbency. When displacement does accelerate — driven by hard legislative deadlines or geopolitical events — the order surge could be rapid and concentrated. Nokia is positioned to benefit, but only if it can compete with Samsung for the sites.

Plausibility verdict at ESA (D5): Medium-Low (downgraded from Triaging). The opportunity is real but Nokia's ability to capture it proportionately is unproven given the 2024 share data.

D6 — India 5G / Airtel Multi-Billion Extension

Airtel contract confirmation: #I Nokia won a multi-billion multi-year extension from Bharti Airtel in November 2024 covering 4G modernisation and 5G deployment across key Indian cities/states. The contract includes AirScale portfolio, Massive MIMO radios, ReefShark SoC, and MantaRay NMS. Airtel awarded additional Nokia contracts in February 2025 for 5G Fixed Wireless Access device expansion. India 5G penetration has reached approximately 42% (Jio 210m subscribers, Airtel 153m) — densification and FWA expansion will drive continued radio unit deployments.

Jio absence as structural ceiling: #J Reliance Jio (India's largest 5G operator by subscriber count at 210m) has no confirmed Nokia contract for wave 2 deployment. Nokia's India recovery is Airtel-dependent. This creates single-carrier concentration risk within India: if Airtel delays deployments due to spectrum cost pressures, ARPU headwinds, or regulatory factors, Nokia's India revenues cannot be offset by Jio volume.

Pricing structure: #A India contracts are consistently described by analysts as "bargain deals" — Nokia wins volume at compressed margins vs its European and North American contracts. India revenue is important for overhead absorption and Mobile Networks segment throughput, but it does not improve Nokia's group gross margin profile materially. India revenue growth is volume-accretive, not margin-accretive.

❌ Low-margin volume trap: #A Nokia's India business contributes revenue that helps cover fixed costs but at below-group-average margins. Rapid India volume growth without margin improvement could dilute Nokia's group gross margin trajectory at the same time as the cost programme is delivering savings — partially offsetting the margin expansion thesis.

Plausibility verdict at ESA (D6): Medium. Airtel contract is confirmed and multi-year. Jio absence caps upside. India is a volume story, not a quality-earnings story.

§6 — Interaction Effects & Scenario Matrix

The six drivers are not independent. ESA analysis reveals three important interaction clusters:

Cluster 1 — Correlated bull (D1 + D4 + Multiple): #J D1 optical/IP revenue growth enables operating leverage through the P&L (D4), which drives FCF quality and supports the 2028 OP targets, which in turn sustains the current 26x EBITDA multiple. All three move together in the bull case. But they also move together in the bear case: if D1 disappoints (optical orders normalise), D4 loses its revenue lever, and the multiple compresses from 26x toward 12-15x. This cluster amplifies both upside and downside.

Cluster 2 — Decoupled anchor (D3 + floor): #J D3 (Nokia Technologies) is structurally independent of D1, D2, D4, D5, D6. It provides a €1.0-1.3bn gross margin floor that acts as a valuation backstop regardless of equipment cycle.
