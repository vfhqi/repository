  • Verdict — EUR/PLN is the load-bearing currency pair: A 10% PLN depreciation against EUR translates to an estimated 2–4% EBITDA headwind via cost inflation (content, capex, debt service), with EPS impact amplified by the leverage effect on net finance costs; PLN/EUR is the single number to watch

  • Verdict — USD/PLN secondary but compounding: USD exposure is primarily through handset procurement and selected content rights; estimated at 1–2% EBITDA sensitivity per 10% PLN/USD move; not independently thesis-threatening but compounds EUR/PLN stress

  • Verdict — macro FX backdrop is moderately adverse N12-24M: Consensus has PLN gradually appreciating vs EUR on NBP-ECB rate differential normalisation and EU funds inflows, which is a mild tailwind on the cost side; but Poland's current account and geopolitical risk premium create downside tail, and PLN remains a EM-proxy currency in risk-off episodes

  • Verdict — FX is not the primary EPS risk for CPS: The thesis is more exposed to domestic revenue trajectory (cord-cutting, ARPU compression, Polsat Box subscriber dynamics) and leverage trajectory than to FX; FX is a second-order modifier, not a first-order driver

  • Verdict — hedging transparency is a weak signal: CPS does not publish quarterly hedge-position summaries, does not name hedge counterparties, and does not disclose realised hedge effectiveness ratios consistently; on the "boring hedger" transparency spectrum, CPS sits in the lower quartile of European large-cap telecoms

  • Verdict — FX sensitivity is manageable in base case: Under base-case PLN stability (±5%), EBITDA impact is sub-1%; under stress (PLN -20% vs EUR), EBITDA impact is estimated 4–8%; under tail-stress (PLN -40%), hedge-break scenarios become material and non-linear cost pass-through failures emerge

§1 — Revenue by Currency

J-front verdict: Revenue is overwhelmingly PLN-denominated (~95%+), making CPS one of the most domestically-concentrated revenue profiles in European telecom; foreign-currency revenue is negligible and not a primary analytical driver

Revenue mix characterisation:
- Domestic concentration: CPS operates exclusively in Poland across its integrated telecom (Polkomtel/Plus network), pay-TV (Polsat Box / Canal+), and media (Telewizja Polsat) segments; all customer-facing revenue — subscriber fees, advertising, B2B telecom contracts — is contracted and invoiced in PLN
- No material USD/EUR revenue streams: CPS does not have international operations generating non-PLN revenue; there are no disclosed export revenue lines, no international content licensing fees received in foreign currency, and no cross-border B2B contracts of material scale
- Advertising revenue (PLN only): Telewizja Polsat's advertising revenue (~PLN 1.2–1.4bn annually) is entirely PLN-denominated, priced against Polish media market CPMs; no FX linkage
- Subscriber revenue (PLN only): Both telecom (Plus) and pay-TV (Polsat Box) ARPU is set in PLN; contracts are PLN-denominated; there is no EUR- or USD-linked pricing in the consumer segment
- B2B/wholesale revenue (predominantly PLN): Polkomtel's enterprise/wholesale segment is PLN-denominated; international roaming settlements involve minor EUR/USD flows but are de minimis relative to total revenue (~PLN 13–14bn group total in FY2023)
- FX-driven growth contribution L3Y: Near-zero — revenue growth has been driven by organic subscriber trends, ARPU changes, and consolidation/organic growth in B2B, not by FX translation effects
- Constant-currency growth = reported growth: Because PLN is the near-exclusive revenue currency, constant-currency and reported revenue growth are functionally identical; management does not report a constant-currency revenue bridge because the adjustment would be immaterial

§2 — Cost by Currency

J-front verdict: Cost base carries meaningful EUR (~20–30%) and USD (~5–10%) exposure, creating a structural mismatch against the PLN revenue base; natural hedging is minimal; this is where FX risk actually lives for CPS

COGS currency mix:
- Content rights — EUR and USD dominant: CPS's largest cost variable outside personnel is content acquisition — sports rights (primarily for Polsat Sports channels and Polsat Box), premium entertainment content, and programming from international distributors; these contracts are typically EUR- or USD-denominated, consistent with industry practice where international rights are cleared in major currencies; estimated EUR/USD share of content cost: 40–60% of the content rights line
- Handsets and devices — USD/EUR denominated: Device procurement for telecom (Plus network handset subsidies, CPE for Inea broadband) is sourced from Samsung, Apple, Huawei, and other OEMs whose wholesale prices are USD- or EUR-referenced; PLN depreciation directly inflates device cost and pressures the subsidy economics in postpaid mobile
- Network equipment capex — EUR dominant: Infrastructure capex (4G/5G radio equipment from Ericsson/Nokia/Huawei, fibre optic cable, core network) is primarily EUR-priced with some USD elements; CPS's ongoing 5G rollout and fibre densification programme (including Inea integration capex) creates a sustained EUR capex flow
- Energy costs — PLN but commodity-linked: Electricity, the largest energy input, is procured domestically in PLN but tracks European energy markets (EUR-denominated TTF/spot); an indirect EUR FX sensitivity exists through energy price linkage, though this is a commodity risk more than a direct FX risk

Opex currency mix:
- Personnel costs — PLN: Wages and salaries for ~20,000+ CPS group employees are PLN-denominated; this is the largest single opex line and is PLN-denominated
- Network maintenance — mixed: Third-party network services involve some EUR-denominated vendor contracts (Nokia/Ericsson managed services) but the majority is PLN-denominated domestic suppliers
- Interconnection and roaming — EUR: International interconnection settlements are EUR/USD-denominated per industry standard; modest but non-zero
- Lease liabilities — predominantly PLN: Tower leases and real estate are PLN-denominated; IFRS 16 lease liabilities are PLN
- Debt service — EUR significant: This is the most material non-revenue FX item; post-Polkomtel acquisition, CPS carries EUR-denominated debt (including Eurobond tranches and EUR-denominated bank facilities under the group refinancing); interest and principal service on EUR debt creates a recurring transaction exposure that is directly sensitive to PLN/EUR — see §4

Capex currency mix:
- EUR share of capex: Estimated 30–50% of total capex (PLN ~3–4bn annually in recent years) has EUR or USD content through equipment procurement; this is consistent with European telecom sector norms where radio access, core, and transmission equipment is European/Asian OEM-priced
- Natural hedge ratio — capex: Zero; capex is funded from PLN operating cash flows, creating a clean mismatch

Natural hedge assessment:
- Natural hedge ratio: Approximately 2–4% (revenue in EUR/USD is ~3%; cost in EUR/USD is ~28–33%); the mismatch is significant and directional — CPS is a structural PLN-earner with structural EUR/USD cost exposure
- Mismatch — revenue vs cost: Approximately 25–30 percentage points of cost are in non-PLN vs approximately 3% of revenue in non-PLN; every PLN depreciation is asymmetrically bad — costs rise faster than revenues

Sceptical bullet — bearish read on cost-currency exposure: The "natural hedge" narrative for domestic telecoms breaks down for CPS because content rights, the most strategically critical cost line (determining the attractiveness of Polsat Box and Polsat Sports to subscribers), are structurally EUR/USD-denominated, and unlike network equipment capex (a one-time capex cycle cost), content rights renew periodically — creating a recurring, rolling transaction exposure that cannot be deferred; in a sustained PLN depreciation episode, CPS faces a binary choice: absorb margin compression or renegotiate/drop content rights, either of which damages the competitive position of the pay-TV business; the "natural hedge from domestic operations" framing that management occasionally implies is technically correct but analytically misleading

§3 — Translation Exposure

J-front verdict: Translation exposure is immaterial for CPS — near-100% PLN revenue means there is essentially no foreign-currency revenue to translate; translation risk is a non-issue; the real FX risk channel is transactional, not translational

Translation mechanism for CPS:
- Translation exposure defined: Translation exposure arises when a company consolidates foreign-currency subsidiaries — their P&L and balance sheet must be translated at prevailing exchange rates, creating FX movements in the consolidated accounts without any underlying cash impact
- CPS subsidiaries — PLN dominant: The operating subsidiaries (Polkomtel, Telewizja Polsat, Polsat Box, Inea, Cyfrowy Polsat proper) are all Polish legal entities with PLN functional currencies; no material foreign-currency-functional subsidiaries exist
- Translation exposure magnitude: Near-zero for revenue and EBITDA; CPS's reported revenue in PLN is essentially the same as the "underlying" revenue because all operating subsidiaries function in PLN
- Reported FX impact L3Y on revenue: Management has not disclosed meaningful translation FX adjustments in revenue bridges in FY2021, FY2022, or FY2023 because there is nothing to translate; this is analytically confirmed, not a disclosure gap
- Translation on balance sheet: The balance sheet does carry some FX translation effect on EUR-denominated debt (the EUR debt is denominated in EUR but the group reports in PLN; the PLN carrying value of EUR debt changes as PLN/EUR moves); this is not a P&L translation effect but a balance sheet revaluation that flows through IFRS profit/loss (net finance costs) or OCI depending on whether the debt is designated as a hedge
- Reported FX impact L1Y (FY2023): The FX line in net finance costs reflects primarily the revaluation of EUR-denominated borrowings; in FY2023, with PLN broadly stable to slightly appreciating vs EUR (EUR/PLN moved from ~4.69 at end-2022 to ~4.31 at end-2023), this would have generated a positive (income) FX revaluation on EUR debt; the magnitude depends on the EUR debt quantum — estimated PLN ~10–14bn of EUR-denominated debt outstanding implies approximately PLN 1.5–2.5bn of FX revaluation gain in FY2023 from PLN appreciation (rough: EUR/PLN from 4.69 → 4.31 = ~8% PLN appreciation × estimated EUR ~2.5–3.5bn EUR debt = approximately PLN 1.0–1.5bn gain)
- This is the key translation nuance: CPS's translation exposure is almost entirely on the liability side (EUR debt revaluation), not the revenue/EBITDA side; this makes reported net income volatile relative to EBITDA in a way that is mechanically driven by PLN/EUR movements — a commonly misunderstood feature of leveraged European operators with EUR-denominated acquisition financing

EBITDA vs net income divergence:
- Translation effect on EBITDA: Zero — EBITDA is unaffected by debt revaluation
- Translation effect on net income/EPS: Significant and volatile, driven entirely by EUR debt revaluation; this means CPS's reported EPS can swing materially even in years of stable EBITDA, creating noise in the earnings trajectory that is FX-driven but not operationally meaningful
- Analytical implication for Richard: When modelling EPS, the EUR debt revaluation line must be isolated and treated as below-EBITDA FX noise, not as an indicator of operating performance; EPS trajectory analysis should normalise for this effect

Sceptical bullet — bearish read on translation: The apparent "translation stability at EBITDA level" is genuine, but the below-EBITDA FX revaluation on EUR debt creates a risk that is non-trivially large relative to net income (PLN net income in recent years approximately PLN 0.5–1.5bn, meaning a PLN 500m–1.5bn FX revaluation swing can dominate the reported bottom line); in a sustained PLN depreciation scenario — for example, a risk-off episode driven by Poland's geopolitical premium or NBP dovish policy error — the EUR debt revaluation loss can wipe out reported net income in a single year, generating a misleading EPS collapse that is technically non-cash but will be read by the market as a genuine earnings deterioration; this creates a binary headline-risk even when underlying EBITDA is stable

§4 — Transaction Exposure

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is the dominant FX risk channel for CPS — EUR and USD cost contracts create real cash-flow mismatches with PLN revenues; the debt service channel is particularly consequential

Transaction exposure points:

  • Channel 1 — Content rights renewal: Rights to international sports content (football leagues, Eurosport-adjacent content, Formula 1, international boxing) and premium entertainment (HBO content, international studio output) are negotiated in EUR or USD; at each renewal cycle, if PLN has depreciated, the PLN-equivalent cost is higher even at unchanged EUR/USD headline price; management cannot defer this exposure — the renewal is binary (pay the rate or lose the content)
  • Estimated annual content rights cash flow in EUR/USD: Not disclosed with precision; based on CPS cost disclosures, total programming and content costs are approximately PLN 2–3bn annually; assuming 40–50% is EUR/USD-denominated, this implies EUR/USD transaction exposure of approximately EUR 200–350m equivalent annually at current rates

  • Pricing pass-through ability: Weak — CPS operates in a competitive Polish pay-TV market (Canal+, Netflix, Disney+, HBO Max all present); subscriber ARPU is PLN-denominated and PLN-sensitive; there is limited ability to raise PLN subscriber fees to offset a sudden EUR/USD-driven content cost increase without subscriber churn risk

  • Channel 2 — Handset and device procurement: Device subsidies for the Plus postpaid base require PLN-to-USD/EUR conversion at point of procurement; PLN depreciation makes the subsidy budget more expensive in PLN terms, pressuring postpaid gross margins

  • Estimated annual device procurement cash flow: EUR/USD equivalent approximately EUR 100–200m annually (based on Plus subscriber additions/upgrades and industry-average subsidy levels)

  • Pass-through ability: Partial — handset prices can be adjusted, but in a competitive Polish mobile market (Orange, T-Mobile, Play also present), aggressive device subsidy adjustment risks churn

  • Channel 3 — EUR-denominated debt service: This is the largest single transaction exposure in absolute PLN terms; CPS carries significant EUR-denominated debt from the Polkomtel acquisition financing (total group net debt approximately PLN 15–18bn, with a material EUR tranche); interest is paid in EUR, principal is repaid in EUR

  • Annual EUR interest payments: At approximately EUR 200–400m equivalent (estimated from disclosed debt schedules and EUR-denominated tranche sizing), this is a direct PLN-to-EUR conversion requirement annually
  • Refinancing risk: When EUR debt is refinanced, the principal is repaid in EUR; at PLN-depreciation, the PLN cost of repayment rises proportionally; this is a genuine cash-flow risk, not a book entry

  • Pass-through ability: Zero — debt service is contractual and non-negotiable

  • Channel 4 — Network equipment capex: EUR-priced Ericsson/Nokia/Huawei equipment procured for 5G rollout; capex budgets in PLN are eroded by PLN depreciation; at a PLN 3–4bn annual capex run rate with ~35% EUR content, approximately EUR 250–330m of equipment capex is EUR-denominated annually

  • Pass-through ability: Indirect — CPS can defer or slow capex in a PLN stress scenario, but this has strategic implications for 5G rollout competitiveness

Cash-flow currency mismatch:

Pricing pass-through assessment:
- Consumer pricing: CPS's subscriber pricing is set annually (or per contract term) in PLN; rapid pass-through of FX-driven cost increases requires either renegotiating subscriber contracts (rare mid-term) or absorbing until next annual cycle; in practice, pass-through lag is 6–18 months minimum
- B2B pricing: Enterprise telecom contracts may include EUR-indexation clauses for multinational clients, providing some limited pass-through, but this is a minority of revenue
- Advertising pricing: Zero FX pass-through — TV advertising is priced against PLN-denominated GRP value; advertisers will not accept EUR-linked ad rates in the Polish market

Sceptical bullet — bearish read on transaction exposure: The pass-through failure mechanism is most dangerous for content rights: unlike devices (can be cheapened/removed from subsidy) or capex (can be deferred), premium content (sports rights, HBO) is a subscriber retention anchor that CPS cannot easily abandon without triggering subscriber churn in an already-competitive pay-TV market; a sustained PLN depreciation of 15–20% would create a content cost overhang of approximately PLN 150–300m annually that cannot be passed through without churn risk, and cannot be eliminated without strategic retreat from premium content — a bind that no amount of financial hedging can fully resolve because the operational hedge (content investment) and financial hedge (short EUR forwards) work in opposite directions from a strategic optionality perspective

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Hedging programme is partially disclosed, appears to focus on EUR-denominated debt exposure and selected transaction items, but lacks the transparency, duration clarity, and realised-effectiveness disclosure of best-practice European telecoms; assessed as partial/reactive rather than systematic

Disclosed hedging policy:
- Policy source: CPS annual reports include a "Financial risk management" note (typically Note 38 or equivalent in IFRS financial statements) addressing currency risk, interest rate risk, and liquidity risk; the currency risk section is the primary disclosure
- General policy statement: CPS discloses that it "identifies and monitors foreign currency risk" arising from EUR and USD-denominated liabilities and procurement; the company "uses derivative financial instruments to hedge certain exposures" — standard IFRS boilerplate without specificity on ratio, duration, or instrument mix
- Hedging mandate: Not disclosed with board-level specificity; there is no published hedging policy document equivalent to what larger European multinationals publish; no published hedge ratio target, no published horizon mandate

Translation hedging:
- Translation hedging policy: CPS does not hedge translation exposure (EBITDA-level) because there is essentially no foreign-currency EBITDA to hedge; this is the correct policy given the revenue mix and is consistent with standard practice
- EUR debt translation: The EUR debt revaluation (discussed in §3) creates P&L volatility that CPS does not structurally hedge via net investment hedges; this is a disclosure gap — it is unclear whether any EUR debt is designated as a net investment hedge under IFRS 9

Transaction hedging:
- EUR debt service hedging: CPS has disclosed use of cross-currency interest rate swaps (CCIRS) to hedge EUR-denominated debt interest payments into PLN equivalents; this is the most clearly disclosed hedging instrument; specific hedge ratios and remaining duration of CCIRS not publicly precise but implied to cover a significant portion of the EUR debt service programme
- Content rights hedging: No explicit disclosure of forward contracts or options on EUR/USD for content rights procurement; it is possible that CPS enters into short-dated EUR forwards for specific large rights deals, but this is not confirmed in public filings
- Device procurement hedging: No explicit disclosure; industry practice for European telecoms is to partially hedge 3–6 months forward on device procurement; CPS likely follows a similar practice but does not confirm in public disclosures
- Capex hedging: No explicit disclosure; European infrastructure vendors often allow EUR-PLN pricing adjustment windows, providing a contractual form of partial hedging

Hedge instruments disclosed:
- CCIRS (Cross-Currency Interest Rate Swaps): Confirmed — used for EUR debt service conversion
- Forward FX contracts: Likely used but not explicitly confirmed at program scale in public filings; referenced obliquely in risk management notes
- FX options/collars: No disclosure; likely not used at scale
- Natural hedging (operating): Minimal, as established in §2

Hedge effectiveness:
- IFRS 9 effectiveness disclosure: CPS's annual report includes effectiveness testing language for designated hedges but does not publish a comprehensive hedge effectiveness ratio in a user-friendly format; realised gain/loss on derivative instruments can be derived from the financial statements but requires careful note reconciliation
- L3Y realised hedge gain/loss: Not explicitly published in a consolidated format by CPS; the FY2023 finance costs note would show realised and unrealised FX derivative settlements — based on available disclosures, CPS has not reported material hedge-programme failures, but the opacity makes independent verification difficult

Hedge horizon:
- Disclosed horizon: Not explicitly stated; based on CCIRS maturities referenced in debt notes (which align with the EUR debt repayment schedule, typically 3–7 year tranches from the original Polkomtel financing), the CCIRS hedges have multi-year horizons matching debt duration — this is structurally sound for the debt service channel
- Operating hedges (content, devices): Likely short-duration (3–12 months) based on industry practice and the absence of any disclosure suggesting long-duration program hedging

Hedge cost:
- CCIRS cost: Not broken out explicitly; embedded in reported interest expense; given Polish rates have been materially above EUR rates, PLN-for-EUR CCIRS (paying fixed PLN, receiving EUR) would have carried positive carry in the 2021–2023 period of rate divergence
- Forward contract cost: Not separately disclosed; would be embedded in procurement cost settlements

"Boring hedger" transparency score for CPS:
- Does CPS publish quarterly hedge-position summaries? No
- Does CPS name its hedge counterparties? No
- Does CPS publish hedge ratios by category? No
- Does CPS publish realised hedge effectiveness ratios? No
- Does CPS confirm instrument mix beyond CCIRS? No, oblique only
- Does CPS disclose horizon by category? No; only implied from debt schedules
- Assessment: CPS is a low-transparency hedger on the "boring hedger" spectrum; the minimum required IFRS disclosure is present but the additive analytical disclosure (ratios, horizons, effectiveness) is absent

Sceptical bullet — bearish read on hedging discipline: The combination of (a) no disclosed hedge ratio for non-debt transaction exposures (content rights, devices), (b) no publicly confirmed forward-contract programme for recurring EUR/USD procurement, and (c) no realised effectiveness data means there is a plausible scenario where CPS's operating transaction exposures (content + devices, estimated PLN 1.4–2.4bn annually) are effectively unhedged or only spot-hedged — meaning that in a fast-moving PLN depreciation episode (as occurred in H1 2022 when EUR/PLN moved from ~4.55 to ~4.83), CPS could face the full spot-rate cost increase on those procurement lines with zero hedge offset; the CCIRS on debt service is reassuring for the debt channel but does nothing for the operating cost channels which collectively represent a larger EBITDA risk

§6 — Quantified Sensitivity Analysis

J-front verdict: A 10% PLN depreciation vs EUR is estimated to reduce EBITDA by approximately 2–4% and EPS by approximately 4–8%, with non-linear amplification beyond ±20% as hedge break and content-rights repricing effects compound; EUR/PLN is the single most consequential FX variable in the model

Analytical framework:
- Base assumptions for sensitivity: FY2023 revenue ~PLN 13.5bn; EBITDA ~PLN 4.7bn; net finance costs ~PLN 1.5bn (FX-affected portion ~PLN 1.0bn for EUR debt service); net income ~PLN 1.0–1.5bn; EUR-denominated cost base ~PLN 3.0–3.5bn annually (content + devices + capex + debt service)
- Translation vs transaction decomposition: For PLN/EUR sensitivity, essentially all impact is transactional (cost-side and debt-service cash outflows increase in PLN terms); translational impact on revenue is zero; on the net income line, the EUR debt revaluation creates an additional non-cash "translation" effect that is directionally opposite to the transactional effect — PLN depreciation increases PLN cost of EUR liabilities but also increases the PLN book value of EUR debt outstanding (a below-EBITDA headwind in addition to the above-EBITDA cost headwind)
- Hedge offset assumption for sensitivity: CCIRS assumed to offset approximately 60–70% of EUR debt service FX risk (as per implied coverage from disclosed CCIRS instruments); operating transaction exposure (content, devices) assumed 0–30% hedged (low confidence — disclosed hedge ratio absent)

Sector hedging principles applied to CPS (per D-RSR-33):

  • Hedge duration matters more than hedge ratio: CPS's CCIRS covers the debt service channel with multi-year duration (structurally sound), but the operating exposure channels (content, devices) appear to have short-duration or absent hedging; on this dimension CPS is below best practice — a 100% short-duration hedge on operating exposures is inferior to 70% hedged across 18 months
  • Counter-cyclical vs pro-cyclical: No evidence from CPS's disclosures that it engages in counter-cyclical hedging (increasing hedge ratios when PLN is strong and FX hedging is cheap); the absence of a published counter-cyclical mandate suggests reactive/pro-cyclical positioning, which is the inferior approach
  • Instrument mix: CCIRS confirmed (appropriate for debt); forwards likely but unconfirmed for operating exposures; options/collars not disclosed; instrument concentration in CCIRS for the single debt channel is appropriate but lacks the collar-structure optionality that best-practice peers use for operating hedges

  • Natural hedging: CPS has minimal natural hedging (discussed in §2); it is structurally disadvantaged vs a peer that generates, say, 20% of revenue in EUR and 20% of cost in EUR (a natural match); CPS's 3% EUR revenue vs 20–25% EUR cost creates an inherently asymmetric exposure profile

§8 — Macro FX Outlook (N12-24M)

J-front verdict: The macro FX backdrop for PLN/EUR is moderately constructive N12M (PLN stable to slightly appreciating on EU funds, NBP rate normalisation, current account) but the N24M outlook carries geopolitical tail risk and NBP policy divergence risk; net assessment: modest FX tailwind in the base case, with a skewed downside tail

EUR/PLN consensus outlook (N12-24M):
- Base case (consensus, mid-2024 vintage): EUR/PLN gradually declining from ~4.30–4.35 toward ~4.10–4.20 range over 12–18 months; PLN mild appreciation driven by EU cohesion fund inflows (Poland among largest EU fund recipients), post-election political normalisation (Tusk government EU relationship reset), and current account improvement
- NBP policy: NBP cut rates 75bps in late 2023 (September–October 2023); rate differential vs ECB has narrowed; further NBP cuts in 2024 would compress the PLN's carry advantage and potentially weaken PLN; consensus in mid-2024 sees NBP on hold or modest additional cuts, ECB also cutting — differential stable-to-compressing, mildly PLN-negative on carry basis
- Current account: Poland's current account has moved into approximate balance (2022 energy shock-driven deficit correcting); balanced-to-improving current account is PLN-supportive structurally
- Rate differential: At mid-2024, NBP reference rate approximately 5.75%; ECB deposit rate approximately 3.75% (following June 2024 cut); 200bp differential is PLN-supportive but narrowing; FX consensus models this differential fading toward 100–150bp by end-2025 — mildly PLN-negative on carry but offset by structural EU fund inflows

- USD/PLN: Linked primarily to EUR/USD (Poland trades EUR more than USD); if EUR strengthens vs USD (consensus modestly EUR-positive N12M on ECB-Fed rate differential normalisation), USD/PLN would soften (PLN appreciates vs USD); for CPS, a stronger EUR vs USD also means some USD-denominated content costs (denominated in USD but linked to EUR procurement budget) become relatively cheaper in EUR terms — a second-order minor benefit
- USD outlook: Fed rate cuts expected 2024–2025; dollar broadly expected to modestly weaken N12–18M vs EUR in consensus; this is mildly favourable for USD-denominated procurement costs in PLN terms

Range of forecasts (consensus dispersion):
- EUR/PLN N12M range: ~4.10–4.50; relatively tight consensus with modest PLN-appreciation central tendency; upside risk to 4.50–4.70 in geopolitical stress; downside risk to 4.00 in risk-on/EU-normalisation scenario
- Consensus dispersion: Moderate; PLN is a liquid EM-adjacent currency with reasonably priced options; implied volatility on EUR/PLN 3M options approximately 6–8% (annualised) — modest relative to EM peers but meaningful for a company with 20–25% EUR cost exposure

Key macro drivers:
- Positive for PLN: EU fund absorption acceleration under Tusk government; political normalisation premium; continued current account improvement; positive risk sentiment in CEE post-Ukraine ceasefire speculation
- Negative for PLN: NBP rate cuts compressing carry advantage; Poland's external debt (EUR-denominated corporate and sovereign); geopolitical premium if Russia-Ukraine conflict escalates or extends; broader EM risk-off episodes (PLN correlates with EM basket in risk-off)

§9 — FX as Tailwind/Headwind for Thesis + Sceptical Synthesis

J-front verdict: FX is a second-order modifier on the CPS EPS thesis — not a primary driver, but the asymmetric PLN/EUR cost exposure means it can become a first-order risk in stress; base case is mildly supportive; stress case introduces a multi-channel simultaneous squeeze that amplifies leverage risk

Net FX vs thesis:
- Primary EPS drivers (FX-independent): Domestic revenue trajectory (subscriber counts, ARPU, B2B penetration), EBITDA margin trajectory (content cost discipline, personnel costs, energy), and leverage trajectory (deleveraging pace toward 2–3x net debt/EBITDA); these are the thesis-defining variables and are PLN-denominated throughout
- FX contribution to EPS base case: In the base case of PLN stable to mild appreciation vs EUR, FX contributes approximately +0.5–2% annual EPS support vs a flat-PLN counterfactual; this is real but not material relative to the organic operating drivers
- FX as a thesis-modifier vs thesis-driver: FX is a modifier — it can take a 10% organic EPS growth trajectory to 12% (mild PLN strength) or 7% (mild PLN weakness) but is unlikely, in isolation, to reverse an otherwise-sound thesis in the base case

FX thesis-dependence quantification:
- If PLN depreciates 10% vs EUR (outside base, within plausible 2Y range): EBITDA impact approximately -4–7% → if thesis assumes 12% EBITDA growth, realised EBITDA growth would be approximately 5–8%; EPS impact approximately -8–16% → a 12% EPS growth thesis becomes approximately -4% to +4% EPS growth; thesis robustness is moderate but not destroyed
- If PLN depreciates 20% vs EUR (stress scenario, comparable to 2022 episode magnitude): EBITDA impact approximately -8–14%; EPS impact approximately -16–32%; the thesis is materially impaired for the year of the shock; in a sustained 20% depreciation environment, the investment thesis would require a complete rewrite
- Leverage multiplier: The most critical interaction is FX × leverage — a PLN depreciation simultaneously (a) increases operating costs (EBITDA headwind), (b) increases PLN equivalent of EUR debt (leverage ratio worsens in PLN terms), (c) increases PLN interest cost, and (d) potentially triggers covenant testing; all four channels move adversely simultaneously in a PLN depreciation shock; this compounding is not captured in a simple "EPS sensitivity" table

Reverse-engineered FX bear case:
- Bear case trigger: PLN depreciates 15–20% vs EUR within 18M (triggered by: NBP cut-cycle acceleration + geopolitical premium + EM risk-off; each individually a 10–20% probability event; joint probability lower but non-negligible given current Russia-Ukraine conflict persistence)
- Bear case P&L impact: EBITDA -6–11%; net income -15–25% (including EUR debt revaluation loss); reported EPS potentially negative in a severe year if revaluation losses dominate
- Bear case leverage impact: Net debt/EBITDA ratio rises 0.3–0.7x in PLN terms (PLN value of EUR debt increases while PLN EBITDA is compressed); CPS's current leverage of approximately 2.5–3.0x could move toward 3.0–3.7x — approaching or breaching typical covenant levels
- Bear case conclusion: FX-driven covenant breach is the "interesting" bear-case mechanism — it's not that FX destroys the business, it's that FX depreciation could force a covenant renegotiation, triggering refinancing at worse terms, undermining the free cash flow trajectory that supports the deleveraging story and hence the equity re-rating thesis

Single largest FX risk for the thesis:
- The single largest FX risk is not EBITDA margin compression (manageable and partially reversible) but EUR debt revaluation intersecting with covenant pressure — a 15–20% PLN depreciation creating a simultaneous EBITDA compression + leverage ratio blow-out that forces a refinancing conversation; this is the scenario where FX transitions from second-order modifier to first-order thesis-breaker

Synthesis — what FX adds to the investment case:
- Tail upside: Continued PLN appreciation (N12M base case) provides an underappreciated 3–6% annual EPS tailwind via EUR cost base deflation in PLN terms; this is not in consensus EPS models which typically assume flat FX; in a PLN-appreciation scenario, CPS's reported EPS growth could exceed organic growth by 3–6 percentage points — a genuine tailwind that is currently priced-in only partially
- Base case: FX is neutral to mildly supportive; the thesis is driven by deleveraging, ARPU recovery, and content investment ROI, not by FX
- Stress case: FX becomes a lever that amplifies the leverage risk already embedded in a highly leveraged operator; the FX risk and the leverage risk are not independent — they are positively correlated in a PLN depreciation scenario; stress-testing the thesis requires a joint FX × leverage scenario, not isolated sensitivity analysis

Sceptical synthesis — what the bulls are getting wrong on FX:
- Bull narrative error: "CPS is a domestic PLN business so FX doesn't matter" — this ignores the structurally mismatched EUR/USD cost base and the EUR debt leverage amplifier
- What's actually true: CPS earns in PLN and spends EUR and USD in significant amounts; the one-sided nature of this exposure (no EUR revenue to offset) means every PLN depreciation is a pure cost increase with no automatic revenue offset; the thesis requires being right on PLN trajectory AND domestic subscriber trajectory AND ARPU trajectory simultaneously — the FX is an additional uncorrelated risk rather than a diversifying factor
- The hidden interaction: Content rights are competitively strategic and EUR/USD-priced; deleveraging requires EBITDA growth; if FX compresses EBITDA at the same time deleveraging is the thesis catalyst, the timeline for the equity re-rating extends — potentially from 18M to 36M+, materially reducing IRR

Confidence in FX-resilience of thesis: Medium plausibility — FX is manageable in base case but introduces a non-trivial conditional risk that the thesis is right-directionally but wrong-on-timing if PLN depreciates and extends the deleveraging timeline
