§1 — Executive Summary: Driver Map and Cross-Cutting Verdict

  • J-front verdict: BBY's driver map is credible for a 25%+ IRR over 18–36 months, but depends critically on US margin normalisation materialising as management guides. [#J]
  • Thesis coherence: Three independent but reinforcing drivers — UK public sector conversion, capital return acceleration, margin recovery — provide genuine asymmetry at current 11x forward PE. [#J]
  • Valuation entry point: BBY trades at 11.2x forward PE vs construction sector median 14.2x — a 21% peer discount despite record £22.7bn order book; re-rating alone provides ~25% upside. [#A]
  • [AS·ARS]: Sell-side models a 1.54:1 upside/downside skew at 13.5x FY2026 estimated EPS and 7.1% free cash flow yield — establishing asymmetric upside with quantified downside protection.
  • [AS·Jefferies]: Bull case targets 1200p, driven by 15% EBITDA growth and elevated buybacks; bear case 600p if construction margins revert to 1% — a 2:1 price range from current levels.
  • [AS·DB]: Deutsche Bank models 8% EPS CAGR through 2027, underpinned by UK power transmission doubling and continued buyback accretion.

Driver Map — Ranked by EPS/Share Price Impact Potential

  • Driver 1 — UK public sector contract conversion: order book up 23% YoY; Sizewell C £3bn anchor now in backlog; defence and energy frameworks accelerating. [#I]
  • Plausibility verdict: high; UK government's £725bn infrastructure pipeline and Sizewell C financial close reduce conversion risk materially. [#J]
  • Driver 2 — US construction revenue growth: US revenue £4.5bn (+25% YoY); data centre and healthcare buildings pipeline expanding; Texas civils one-off cleared. [#I]
  • Plausibility verdict: medium; US Buildings revenue growth is real, but thin 0.6% PFO margin in 2025 means earnings leverage depends on normalisation. [#J]
  • Driver 3 — Construction margin recovery: UK construction PFO margin hit 3.5% in 2025, above 3% target; US margin recovery to normalised level is the swing factor. [#I]
  • Plausibility verdict: medium-high; UK is already delivering; US normalisation management-guided but one project overrun already cost credibility. [#J]
  • Driver 4 — Capital return programme: £200m buyback announced for 2026; cumulative returns since 2021 exceed £1.2bn; total 2026 shareholder cash ~£267m vs £189m in 2025. [#I]
  • Plausibility verdict: high; returns are mechanically executable from cash generation and PPP proceeds; not contingent on earnings acceleration. [#J]
  • Driver 5 — Order book quality and EPS upgrade cycle: 88% of UK orders on target-cost/cost-plus terms vs 79% in 2024; risk profile improving alongside scale; analyst consensus buy at 881p. [#I]
  • Plausibility verdict: medium-high; order book quality improvement is verifiable and creates conditions for estimate upgrades, but consensus EPS growth only 8.9% CAGR. [#J]
  • Driver 6 — Nuclear and defence megaproject revenue ramp: Sizewell C civil works £2–3bn contract scope; Rolls-Royce nuclear fissile framework (exclusive); AWE Aldermaston programme ongoing. [#I]
  • Plausibility verdict: medium; long-duration programmes reduce near-term EPS impact; revenue recognition lags contract award by 12–24 months. [#J]

Dominant Driver Cluster

  • Infrastructure spend conversion + capital returns is the dominant cluster: order book visibility provides EPS confidence; buyback directly compresses share count for per-share uplifts. [#A]
  • US margin recovery is the single swing variable — contributes disproportionately to bull vs bear EPS spread. [#J]
  • [AS·ARS]: 90%+ of UK Construction revenues tied to government funding commitments, creating cross-driver dependency — any infrastructure budget deferral stalls power frameworks, civil engineering conversions, and margin recovery simultaneously.

Sceptical Synthesis — Single Pivotal Assumption

  • Pivotal assumption: US construction PFO margin normalises from 0.6% in 2025 toward the 2%+ level implied by peers; without this, EPS growth stalls at low single digits and re-rating stalls. [#J]
  • [AS·ARS]: The sceptical synthesis identifies a second pivotal assumption — unwavering commitment and fiscal capacity of the UK government to fund unprecedented power grid expansion without deferrals; both conditions must hold for the full bull case to materialise.

§2 — Driver 1: UK Public Sector Contract Conversion

  • J-front verdict: This is the highest-quality driver in the set — evidence already on the scoreboard, policy support unusually explicit, contract risk profile the best in BBY's history. [#J]

Definition

  • Mechanism — framework utilisation and award timing: BBY wins work primarily through DIO regional prime contracts, National Grid frameworks, National Highways, and now Sizewell C civil works alliance. [#I]
  • Spending review context: UK government committed to £725bn infrastructure pipeline with NISTA (National Infrastructure and Service Transformation Authority) providing multi-year programme certainty. [#I]
  • Four chosen growth markets: UK energy transition and security, UK transport, UK defence, US buildings — stated explicitly in FY2025 results as the primary allocation lens. [#I]

Company Guidance

  • Order book target — explicit guidance: management guided approximately 20% order book growth for 2025; actual out-turn was 23% growth to record £22.7bn (FY2024: £18.4bn). [#I]
  • Sizewell C inclusion: approximately £3bn of Sizewell C civil works entered the order book following financial close in November 2025 — this is crystallised, not pipeline. [#I]
  • Further pipeline cited: management highlights UK power transmission schemes, major defence contracts, and UK transport as 2026–2027 pipeline sources. [#I]
  • Support Services margin guidance raised: support services target raised to >8%, supporting further earnings growth in 2026 and 2027. [#I]
  • [AS·DB]: Power margins expected to remain structurally robust supporting the division's 6–8% long-run margin target — a significant step above the current UK Construction 3.5%.
  • [AS·Jefferies]: UK Power forecast to contribute approximately 50% of BBY's ~8% EPS CAGR through 2027; revenues projected to reach £700m by 2027, more than doubling 2023 levels.

Recent Delivery Evidence

  • UK Construction PFO margin — fifth consecutive year of improvement: FY2025 PFO margin 3.5% vs 3.0% target; PFO grew 22% to £110m (FY2024: £81m). [#I]
  • Order book risk profile improvement: 88% of UK orders on target-cost or cost-plus terms in 2025 vs 79% in 2024 — a material derisking of earnings quality. [#I]
  • UK energy specifically: over £3.5bn of new UK power generation orders won in 2025; this category drove order book expansion above guidance. [#I]
  • Revenue delivery: group revenue up 8% to £10.77bn in 2025; UK construction was a primary contributor alongside US buildings. [#I]
  • [AS·ARS]: 84% of the order book is tied to public sector or regulated clients — exceptional defensive quality vs mid-tier peers where private-sector cyclicality is higher.
  • [AS·ARS]: National Grid £59bn High Voltage Direct Current framework secured with two framework places; £833m Net Zero Teesside contract signed with Technip Energies — specific, material wins beyond Sizewell C headline.

Bear Case

  • ❌ Fiscal constraint re-emergence: Labour government spending review could defer infrastructure capital; NISTA framework is not legally binding contract awards. [#A]
  • Procurement delay risk: major programmes routinely slip 12–24 months between framework inclusion and active revenue recognition; Sizewell C civil works peak ramp is 2028–2032. [#A]
  • Competitive underbidding: large-scale frameworks attract Kier, Morgan Sindall, and European entrants; price compression risk on framework renewals. [#A]
  • Sceptical note: the order book figure includes awarded-not-started work; £22.7bn backlog converts to revenue over 3–4 years — the near-term EPS uplift per year is more modest than headline suggests. [#J]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — order book already in record position and UK contract risk profile demonstrably the best since 2019. [#J]

§3 — Driver 2: US Construction Revenue Growth

  • J-front verdict: US revenue growth is real and accelerating, but thin margins make this a volume-without-value story in 2025; the thesis requires margin normalisation to convert revenue scale into EPS. [#J]

Definition

  • US scale: US revenue £4.5bn (approximately $6bn) in 2025, up from £3.6bn ($4.8bn) in 2024 — a 25% revenue increase in one year. [#I]
  • Mix shift — Buildings vs Civils: BBY's US strategy deliberately concentrates on Buildings (commercial, data centres, healthcare, government buildings) over Civils; Civils exposure is being managed down. [#I]
  • Data centre positioning: management explicitly targeting more US data centre work; record backlog cited as the springboard; no disclosed revenue quantum for this sub-segment. [#I]
  • [AS·ARS]: US Buildings now represents 91% of divisional revenues — a deliberate structural de-risking away from fixed-price civil engineering that is measurable and materially advanced.

Company Guidance

  • 2026 outlook: management expects further Buildings revenue growth in 2026, driven by data centres, healthcare, and government buildings. [#I]
  • Margin trajectory — management language: "completion of challenging US projects" (specifically the Texas highways overrun) is expected to normalise margins by end of 2026. [#A]
  • US construction target margin: management has not stated an explicit US PFO margin target in recent communications, a gap vs UK where 3% was a stated floor. [#A]
  • Growth markets focus: US buildings is the only US growth market in BBY's stated four-market framework — Civils is not a growth priority. [#I]

Recent Delivery Evidence

  • ❌ 2025 US PFO margin — 0.6%: a single problem project in Texas highways "more than offset" a strong US Buildings performance; peer US contractors average 2–3% construction margins. [#A]
  • Revenue momentum positive: £4.5bn US revenue is approximately 42% of group total; demonstrating the US is now a critical earnings contributor even at thin margins. [#I]
  • Data centre pipeline credibility: Construction Dive (March 2026) reports BBY targeting more US data centre jobs from a record backlog position — corroborates management's stated strategy. [#I]
  • US Civils legacy — risk not fully resolved: the Texas project which caused the 2025 overrun appears to be in run-off; no new large Civils exposures disclosed as of March 2026. [#A]
  • [AS·MS]: Morgan Stanley projects US Buildings revenue resetting toward $6bn medium-term, supporting a divisional margin of 1.5–2.0% — providing a quantified analyst anchor absent from management guidance.
  • [AS·Jefferies]: Jefferies models approximately 80bps of US margin improvement by 2027 as loss-making Civils contracts roll off; secured major data centre orders in Virginia, Oregon, plus a $200m data centre contract in Washington D.C.
  • [AS·DB]: Deutsche Bank forecasts US margin improvement in the 1–2% range as the workload definitively shifts from Civils to Buildings — the most modest recovery scenario of the three brokers.

Macro Context — US Infrastructure Law and IRA

  • Bipartisan Infrastructure Law relevance: BBY's US Buildings focus means direct BIL exposure is limited; the company benefits primarily through data centre electrification demand and government buildings rather than road/bridge contracts. [#A]
  • Data centre demand — structural tailwind: hyperscaler and co-location data centre capex remains at elevated run-rate; BBY's positioning in this vertical is a genuine structural tailwind. [#I]
  • IRA manufacturing reshoring: some US healthcare and advanced manufacturing facility construction benefits indirectly from IRA-driven reshoring; BBY does not call this out explicitly. [#A]
  • FX risk — GBP/USD: BBY reports in sterling; a weakening dollar reduces reported US revenue and PFO in sterling terms. [#A]

Bear Case

  • ❌ Margin compression risk: US Buildings market is competitive; data centre construction is attracting all major US contractors; pricing discipline cannot be assumed. [#A]
  • Concentration — Texas civils precedent: one project caused the entire US division to report 0.6% margin; a single large fixed-price Civils commitment could again wipe US profitability. [#A]
  • FX headwind: dollar/sterling rate adverse to BBY if sterling recovers; a 10% sterling appreciation against dollar reduces US PFO sterling equivalent by ~10%. [#A]
  • Sceptical note: 25% US revenue growth in 2025 was partially driven by revenue recognition timing on large Buildings awards; sustainable organic growth rate is likely 10–15%, not 25%. [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — US revenue growth is genuine, but EPS contribution depends entirely on margin normalisation management has guided but not yet delivered. [#J]

§4 — Driver 3: Construction Margin Recovery

  • J-front verdict: UK margin recovery is already delivered and verifiable; US margin recovery is the credible next leg and the single largest source of EPS upgrade potential. [#J]

Definition

  • UK baseline to target — already exceeded: UK Construction PFO margin was 3.5% in FY2025 vs the stated 3% long-term target; the target was formally surpassed for the first time. [#I]
  • US baseline — deeply depressed: US Construction PFO margin was approximately 0.6% in FY2025; peer group average is 2–3%; gap of 140–240bps is the EPS leverage opportunity. [#A]
  • Group underlying PFO — already improving: underlying PFO from earnings-based businesses £293m, up 16% in 2025; this includes both UK Construction and Support Services. [#I]
  • Mechanism — contract mix improvement: moving more US work to Buildings (vs Civils); increasing UK cost-plus exposure; reducing legacy fixed-price exposure. [#I]

Company Guidance

  • UK margin — raised ambition: having exceeded 3%, management raised support services margin guidance to >8% and flagged potential for UK Construction margin to sustain above target. [#I]
  • US margin normalisation — management commitment: management explicitly linked improved 2026/2027 US margins to completion of the challenging Texas project — a specific, verifiable commitment. [#A]
  • Group EPS guidance — 47.6p in 2025, growth expected: consensus EPS forecast for FY2026 approximately 49p — implies only 3% growth; analysts appear sceptical on US margin recovery pace. [#A]
  • Group target language: "further profitable growth in 2026 and 2027" — directional rather than quantified; no explicit group-level PFO margin target set. [#A]

Recent Delivery Evidence

  • UK — fifth consecutive year of margin improvement: UK Construction PFO: FY2021 sub-2%, FY2022 ~2.5%, FY2023 ~2.8%, FY2024 ~3.0%, FY2025 3.5% — a consistent improvement curve. [#I]
  • Support Services — margin leadership: support services PFO has been above 7% in recent years; the >8% new target reflects genuine operational leverage in this segment. [#I]
  • ❌ US margin — inconsistent delivery: FY2024 US PFO margin was also thin (~1%); FY2025 at 0.6% is a deterioration not an improvement, despite increased revenue. [#A]
  • Group PFO composition — UK doing the heavy lifting: UK Construction £110m PFO vs US Construction implied ~£27m (0.6% on £4.5bn); UK carries the group. [#A]
  • [AS·DB]: Deutsche Bank normalised EBIT model projects approximately £100m for UK Construction based on consistent 3.0% margin delivery — the conservative anchor below current 3.5% achieved.
  • [AS·Jefferies]: Jefferies models Support Services trending toward 9% margins and UK Construction sustaining 3.5% over the medium term — the most constructive of the sell-side scenario set.
  • [AS·MS]: Morgan Stanley expects Support Services sustainably above 8%, driven by high-margin power transmission mix — convergent with Jefferies on the floor but less optimistic on the ceiling.
  • [AS·DB]: Peer context: BBY's UK Construction 3.5% margin still trails Morgan Sindall's 5.1%, but the upward momentum from energy mix shift is narrowing the gap more rapidly than prior cycles.

Bear Case

  • Labour and materials inflation: UK construction wage inflation running above CPI; steel and concrete input costs volatile; cost-plus contracts mitigate but do not eliminate this. [#A]
  • Competitive pricing pressure: large framework renewals attract price competition; sustained 3.5%+ UK margin depends on maintaining pricing discipline. [#A]
  • Mix deterioration: Sizewell C civil works ramp involves complex enabling works and tunnelling; early-phase large programmes often compress margins vs steady-state. [#A]
  • ❌ US margin — no floor established: without a stated US margin target, the market has no anchor; a second US overrun event would severely damage BBY re-rating prospects. [#J]
  • Sceptical note: 140–240bp US margin gap vs peers sounds like easy recovery, but US Civils projects are inherently volatile; BBY may need two or three years of clean delivery before the market credits the improvement. [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium-high plausibility — UK margin is confirmed and sustainable; US recovery is management-guided and plausible but not yet evidenced in results. [#J]

§5 — Driver 4: Capital Return Programme

  • J-front verdict: Capital returns are the most mechanically certain driver — the programme is funded, committed, and track-record confirmed since 2021; this is the near-term floor on total shareholder return. [#J]

Definition

  • 2026 buyback — £200m committed: announced at FY2025 results on 11 March 2026; this is incremental to dividends and PPP disposal proceeds. [#I]
  • Dividend growth — 12% increase: full-year dividend 14p per share for FY2025 vs 12.5p in FY2024; 12% DPS growth is well above FTSE 100 median (~5%). [#I]
  • Total 2026 shareholder cash — ~£267m: combining buyback, final FY2025 dividend, and 2026 interim dividend — up from £189m total cash return in 2025. [#I]
  • Programme history: cumulative returns since 2021 capital allocation framework launch exceed £1.2bn; programme has not been interrupted by project overruns or macro shocks. [#I]
  • [AS·ARS]: Average monthly net cash surged nearly 60% year-over-year to £1.21bn in 2025; management has guided £1.3–1.5bn average monthly net cash for 2026 — mechanically funds the £200m buyback with headroom.

Company Guidance

  • Commitment framing: management language is "significant ongoing shareholder returns" with "confidence in continued ability to deliver" — explicit multi-year commitment language. [#I]
  • PPP disposal gains — guided lower: FY2025 disposal gains were £36m on 12 asset sales (above guidance); FY2026 disposal gain guidance is £5–15m — a step down. [#A]
  • Balance sheet — funded: net cash position and PPP portfolio valuation of £1.1bn support buyback execution without leverage; no new debt required. [#I]
  • Buyback mechanics: £200m at approximately 860p current price implies approximately 23m shares retired — around 7% of diluted share count. [#A]

Recent Delivery Evidence

  • Programme execution — consistent: every year since 2021 the buyback has been executed as communicated; no deferral, no dilutive acquisition interruption. [#I]
  • PPP portfolio — disciplined recycling: 12 assets sold for £120m in FY2025, generating £36m of gains; portfolio directors' valuation remains at £1.1bn — no sign of distressed selling. [#I]
  • Dividend cover — adequate: 47.6p EPS vs 14p DPS implies 3.4x cover; low payout ratio by UK mid-cap standards provides headroom for further dividend growth. [#A]
  • No acquisition disruption: management has not signalled M&A appetite that would divert capital return firepower; this distinguishes BBY from peers (Kier, Costain) that have diluted via acquisitions. [#I]
  • [AS·DB]: Deutsche Bank forecasts an 8% distribution yield for shareholders — significantly above the sector median of approximately 6% — placing BBY in the top quartile of total return generators in UK construction.
  • [AS·Jefferies]: Jefferies' bull case incorporates an elevated £200m buyback per annum from 2027, sustaining EPS accretion through share count reduction even if earnings growth moderates.
  • [AS·ARS]: BBY's £189m cash return in 2025 significantly outpaces Kier's initial £20m buyback and Morgan Sindall's internal reinvestment focus — peer context confirms BBY's capital return programme is a structural outlier.

Bear Case

  • Balance sheet stress — cyclical risk: a severe UK recession or large US overrun could erode operating cash flow; £200m buyback represents approximately 6–7 months of group operating cash flow. [#A]
  • ❌ PPP disposal gains — declining: FY2026 guidance of £5–15m vs £36m in 2025 and £25m in 2024 signals that the easy PPP recycling gains are largely captured. [#A]
  • Acquisition appetite re-emergence: a new CEO or strategic review could shift capital allocation priorities; risk is low but non-zero. [#A]
  • PPP market conditions: if interest rates spike materially, secondary market PPP valuations compress and disposal proceeds fall. [#A]
  • Sceptical note: a 7% share count reduction from a single £200m buyback is meaningful but the per-share EPS contribution is roughly 3–4p — impressive but not a standalone 25% IRR driver. [#J]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — capital return programme is funded, committed, and has four years of uninterrupted delivery; the only credible interruption is a large project loss. [#J]

§6 — Driver 5: Order Book Quality and EPS Upgrade Cycle

  • J-front verdict: Order book quality is improving measurably; however, analyst consensus upgrades have been modest — the market has partially priced in the order book expansion but not yet the EPS leverage. [#J]

Definition

  • Order book — record £22.7bn: up 23% YoY from £18.4bn; represents approximately 2.1x FY2025 revenue — strong multi-year visibility. [#I]
  • Quality dimension — contract terms: 88% of UK orders on target-cost or cost-plus in FY2025 vs 79% in FY2024 — genuinely reduced earnings volatility. [#I]
  • Bid pipeline: management cites significant further pipeline in UK power transmission, major defence contracts, and US buildings — suggesting order book growth can continue into 2026. [#I]
  • EPS upgrade cycle mechanism: as revenue converts from backlog and margin improves, EPS beats would prompt estimate revisions upward, compressing PE and expanding multiple. [#A]
  • [AS·ARS]: Management cites a further £20bn pipeline of awarded-but-not-yet-contracted work underpinning future order book growth — a forward-fill runway approximately equal to the current order book.

Company Guidance

  • 2026 and 2027 growth outlook: management explicitly anticipates "further profitable growth in 2026 and 2027" supported by order book and pipeline. [#I]
  • Bid pipeline — qualitative: pipeline described as significant in UK power transmission, DIO/DNE defence contracts, and US buildings; no precise quantum beyond what has entered the order book. [#I]
  • Award rates — not guided: construction industry bid-hit rates typically run 20–35% of submitted tenders; BBY does not disclose this metric. [#A]

Recent Delivery Evidence

  • EPS trajectory — improving: FY2023 underlying EPS approximately 39p; FY2024 approximately 43.7p; FY2025 47.6p — compound growth of approximately 10% CAGR over two years. [#I]
  • ❌ Analyst consensus — modest upgrade cycle: FY2026 consensus EPS approximately 49p — implies only 3% growth; management's 9–16% PFO growth trajectory has not fully flowed into outer-year estimates. [#A]
  • PE rating — structural discount: BBY at 11.2x forward PE vs sector median 14.2x; if this gap closed to neutral, the share price would be approximately 1,050p (~22% above current 860p). [#A]
  • ⚡ RARE — re-rating potential: if order book quality metrics sustain and US delivers, BBY's sector discount (21% below median) is one of the widest in the UK construction universe. [#J]
  • [AS·DB]: Deutsche Bank recently upgraded FY2026 and FY2027 EPS forecasts by 7% and 14% respectively — the most concrete evidence of the upgrade cycle beginning to materialise, though from a conservative base.
  • [AS·Jefferies]: Jefferies lifted target price to 985p following broad-based order intake strength; backlog margins near record levels supporting further upward consensus revisions.
  • [AS·MS]: Morgan Stanley trimmed PFO estimates by 3% for 2025, awaiting clearer visibility on US non-residential building orders and citing an impending lull in traditional US commercial markets — the contrarian broker voice within an otherwise constructive consensus.

Bear Case

  • Project delays — backlog conversion risk: a large project in the order book delayed 12–24 months compresses revenue recognition and delays EPS upgrade catalyst. [#A]
  • Competitive intensity — framework renewals: when current NEC4 frameworks come up for renewal, pricing competition could compress margins. [#A]
  • Macro shock — UK recession: a UK recession in 2026–2027 could trigger government infrastructure programme deferrals, reducing order book conversion. [#A]
  • Sceptical note: 2.1x revenue cover sounds robust, but construction backlog always includes projects delayed, descoped, or cancelled at 5–15% margins of total; effective "hard" cover is closer to 1.8x. [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium-high plausibility — order book quality metrics are the best in BBY's post-2017 history; EPS upgrade cycle is underway but requires US clean delivery to accelerate. [#J]

§7 — Additional Drivers

  • J-front verdict: Two additional credible drivers exist — nuclear/defence megaproject revenue ramp and PPP portfolio re-rating; both are lower probability in the 18–36 month window but warrant monitoring. [#J]

Driver 6: Nuclear and Defence Megaproject Revenue Ramp

  • Definition: Sizewell C civil works (£2–3bn scope, BBY-led JV with Laing O'Rourke and Bouygues), Rolls-Royce fissile construction framework (exclusive contractor), AWE Aldermaston ongoing programme. [#I]
  • Company guidance — Sizewell C timing: civil works enabling phase 2026–2027; main works ramp 2027–2032; near-term (2026) contribution limited to enabling works and mobilisation. [#I]
  • Evidence base: Programme Alliance Agreement signed June 2025; approximately £3bn now in BBY order book; Rolls-Royce appointment as exclusive nuclear contractor announced separately. [#I]
  • Macro support: UK government committed £14.2bn toward Sizewell C; Strategic Defence Review (June 2025) declared defence as an engine for growth — political commitment at highest level. [#I]
  • Bear case: nuclear projects carry schedule risk, regulatory risk, and cost overrun exposure historically greater than conventional construction; Hinkley Point C is the cautionary precedent for cost escalation. [#A]
  • ❌ Near-term EPS impact — limited: Sizewell C main works peak revenue is 5–10 years out; the 18–36 month window sees mobilisation and early enabling works only. [#A]

Plausibility verdict: Low-medium plausibility in 18–36 month window; high strategic value but revenue recognition lags are too long for near-term IRR thesis. [#J]

Driver 7: PPP Portfolio Re-rating or Discount Narrowing

  • Definition: BBY's investments portfolio at directors' valuation of £1.1bn; market capitalisation ~£2.8bn implies limited sum-of-parts credit for this portfolio. [#A]
  • Mechanism: a secondary market re-rating of infrastructure PPP assets (driven by rate cuts or institutional demand) increases directors' valuation and could prompt a special distribution. [#A]
  • Evidence base: portfolio generated £36m disposal gains in FY2025 above guidance; portfolio represents approximately 39% of market cap at directors' valuation. [#A]
  • [AS·MS]: Morgan Stanley applies a 20% discount to Directors' Valuation in base case SOTP and 25% in the bear case due to US Military Housing regulatory overhangs — implying the portfolio contributes approximately £855m–£858m to intrinsic value, not the £1.07bn face.
  • [AS·Jefferies]: Jefferies applies only a 10% discount — closer to fair value — suggesting a £963m implied portfolio contribution; the spread between broker assumptions (0%–25%) is itself an indication of valuation uncertainty.
  • [AS·DB]: Deutsche Bank includes the full £1.07bn Directors' Valuation at 0% discount — the most constructive interpretation, contingent on US Military Housing monitorship resolution.
  • Bear case: rising long-term gilt yields compress PPP net present values; FY2026 disposal gain guidance of £5–15m signals gains are reducing, not accelerating. [#A]
  • ❌ Rate environment risk: Bank of England rate path uncertain; a "higher for longer" UK rates scenario is structurally negative for PPP secondary market pricing. [#A]

Plausibility verdict: Low plausibility as a primary driver; PPP recycling is contributing but at declining gain rates; sum-of-parts re-rating requires institutional catalyst not yet visible. [#J]

Thin Drivers — Explicitly Not Pursued

  • US defence exposure: BBY has no significant US defence business; US revenue is Buildings-dominated; no credible driver here. [#I]
  • AI/digital infrastructure premium re-rating: data centre construction is a real revenue line but does not command a "tech" PE multiple; construction is construction regardless of end-use. [#A]
  • ESG premium: nuclear energy and infrastructure are increasingly considered ESG-positive, but insufficient evidence that ESG re-rating adds measurable multiple to UK construction peers. [#A]

§8 — Cross-Driver Dependencies and Correlation Risks

  • J-front verdict: The BBY thesis is moderately robust — three drivers (UK contract conversion, capital returns, order book quality) are largely independent; US margin recovery is correlated with multiple drivers and is the common failure mode. [#J]

Correlation Analysis

  • UK public sector conversion + order book quality — highly correlated: both depend on the same government infrastructure spending commitment and NISTA pipeline; a spending review deferral hits both simultaneously. [#A]
  • Capital returns + earnings growth — partially correlated: the £200m buyback is funded by operating cash flow; a large loss event could interrupt both earnings and buyback simultaneously. [#A]
  • US margin recovery + EPS upgrade cycle — highly correlated: the estimate revision upside is almost entirely contingent on US margin normalisation; without US clean delivery, consensus stays at ~3% growth. [#A]
  • Nuclear/defence ramp — largely independent: Sizewell C is funded by government commitment (£14.2bn committed); it is not sensitive to construction market cycles in the short term. [#I]
  • PPP portfolio — countercyclical to some extent: PPP valuations are rate-sensitive but not correlated to BBY's operating performance; provides a partial hedge. [#A]
  • [AS·DB]: 25–30% of new power generation wins are expected to directly utilise UK Construction division capabilities — creating execution dependency that could trigger simultaneous margin contraction across both divisions if labour or supply chain stress emerges.
  • [AS·DB]: A 1% increase in the inflation assumption adds 11% to the Directors' Valuation — a positive correlation between inflation and PPP value that partially offsets the negative impact of inflation on construction margins.

Robustness Assessment

  • Independent driver count: Drivers 1, 4, and 5 are partially correlated but not the same variable; Drivers 2 and 3 are effectively the same variable (US margin); Driver 6 is largely independent. [#A]
  • Effective independent drivers — 3 to 4: not 6; the headline driver count overstates diversification. [#J]
  • Fragility identification: concentration on UK government spending follow-through and US margin normalisation means a coordinated disappointment on both could leave the thesis with only the mechanical capital return floor. [#J]

Simultaneous Disappointment Scenario

  • Scenario — all top-3 drivers disappoint: UK order book conversion slows to 10% (vs 23% base), US margin stays at 0.6% (vs ~1.5% bull), capital return reduces to £100m (half guidance). [#A]
  • EPS downside estimate: base 47.6p FY2025; UK PFO growth halved ~£15m less PFO; US flat — no incremental contribution; buyback reduced — fewer share count benefits; implied EPS circa 43–45p. [#A]
  • Share price downside: at 10x depressed multiple on 44p EPS = approximately 440p — a 49% drawdown from current 860p. [#A]
  • Probability of simultaneous disappointment: low — UK public sector spending has strong political commitment and BBY's backlog is locked, not bid-stage. [#J]
  • [AS·Jefferies]: Jefferies explicitly models the full simultaneous disappointment case — projecting a 33% downside to a 500p target price if construction margins revert to 1% and Supply Services growth stalls to low single digits; the broker-modelled downside (500p) is slightly less severe than the Claude-modelled 440p, but directionally aligned.

Sceptical Note on Cross-Driver Risk
