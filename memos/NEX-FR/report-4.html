§7 — Sector & Value Chain Context

Verdict: NEX-FR's outperformance is approximately 50:50 company-specific vs sector tailwind — HV/subsea is a genuine structural supercycle, but Nexans' execution within it and restructuring contribution are company-specific [#J]

  • Sector revenue: The electrical cable and connectivity sector is experiencing bifurcated growth — energy infrastructure (strongly growing) vs commercial/industrial cable (flat to modest) vs enterprise networking (soft) [#A]
  • This bifurcation means the peer group is not homogeneous; peers' growth rates reflect their exposure to different sub-segments more than their competitive execution

  • Nexans' ~30-35% HV exposure gives it the best positioning among its peers (excluding Prysmian)

  • Sector margin: Sector-wide EBIT margins are generally expanding in energy infrastructure sub-segments due to capacity scarcity and project pricing — compressing in commodity distribution (pricing competitive) and enterprise networking (volume pressure) [#A]

  • Sector revisions: The electrification/grid sector is experiencing positive consensus revision cycles — driven by accelerating government grid investment announcements (EU, US IRA, UK transmission investment) creating demand visibility [#A]

  • This positive revision cycle benefits all energy-infrastructure players including Nexans and Prysmian

  • The scale of grid investment required means this is a multi-year revision tailwind, not a single-quarter event

  • Supplier signals: Cable-grade copper and aluminium supply is adequate at current price levels — no supply crunch signals that would cause input cost escalation beyond commodity price movements [#I]

  • Polymer/compound suppliers: broadly stable, some supply normalisation post-2022 squeeze

  • Customer signals: Nexans' primary customers (TSOs — Transmission System Operators, offshore wind developers, EPC contractors) are signalling strong multi-year demand [#A]

  • Ørsted, RWE, Vattenfall offshore wind programmes: active procurement
  • National Grid, RTE, Elia interconnector programmes: active procurement

  • Data centre power infrastructure customers: early-stage but growing demand signal

  • End-market growth: European grid interconnector investment is estimated to require €600bn+ through 2030 (European Commission estimates) — this provides secular demand that dwarfs current cable industry capacity [#A]

  • HV cable sector capacity constraint is genuine — 2-3 year lead times for new manufacturing investment create a sustained pricing and volume discipline

  • Competitive intensity: In high-voltage/subsea, competitive intensity remains low due to barriers to entry (capex, certifications, installation vessel fleet) [#J]

  • Chinese players (ZTT, Far East Cable) have limited European market penetration on major HV projects due to regulatory and certification barriers

  • Prysmian and Nexans maintain effective pricing discipline without formal coordination — a natural oligopoly reinforced by long project lead times

  • Cyclical vs structural: The energy-transition demand driver is structural, not cyclical — independent of economic cycle to a significant degree [#J]

  • The data centre power infrastructure demand is incremental and emerging — not yet material to Nexans but growing

  • Risk: if government policy reverses (EU offshore wind permitting delays, US IRA modification), the structural tailwind could be interrupted, but is not eliminated

  • Distributor signals: No significant distributor channel for HV/subsea (direct project sales) — in commodity distribution cable, some destocking signals visible in 2023-24 in European distribution channels [#A]

  • This destocking in commodity cable was a modest headwind to Nexans' distribution segment in 2023, now broadly complete

  • Sceptical bullet: The structural tailwind narrative may be obscuring a cyclical component — grid investment programmes are policy-dependent, and European fiscal constraints (post-2024 government formation challenges in France, Germany) could delay or slow transmission investment programmes; if even 20-30% of the anticipated grid capex programme slips by 18-24 months, Nexans' HV backlog conversion timeline and revenue growth rate face meaningful risk [#J]
