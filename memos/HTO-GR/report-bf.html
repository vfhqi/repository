EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (BLUF)

Business foundation and revenue model

OTE transformed into a streamlined Greek pure-play with highly predictable cash generation, leveraging structural prepaid-to-postpaid migrations and broadband upselling to drive durable returns. The business exited its final international market in Romania in late 2025 to become a streamlined Greek pure-play operator, while management recently absorbed the COSMOTE subsidiary directly into OTE, creating a leaner, more agile operating model with reduced management layers. Deutsche Telekom's 54.6% stake provides access to scale economies, shared IT platforms, and the global Telekom brand identity, ensuring robust governance oversight and procurement advantages that structurally lower supplier leverage.

The mobile division drives growth through a structural migration from prepaid to postpaid contracts, achieving nine consecutive years of postpaid expansion, with prepaid customers still representing 56% of the mobile base, well above European averages near 40%, providing a durable upselling runway. The fixed-line model relies on migrating legacy copper customers to higher-ARPU FTTH connections, supported heavily by government voucher subsidies, while following the Terna Fiber acquisition, OTE aims to pass 3.5 million homes by 2030, capturing over 70% of the country's lines. The system solutions division acts as a major systems integrator, delivering exceptional 57% year-on-year growth in late 2025, with management expecting private sector digitalization to seamlessly replace public spending as EU recovery funds taper.

Beyond core telecoms, OTE successfully operates the Payzy digital wallet, Cosmote Insurance, and the BOX food delivery platform, where BOX serves over one million users, providing meaningful customer retention benefits that structurally lower core telecom churn levels. However, the vaunted mobile "more-for-more" strategy relies heavily on a largely unpenetrated prepaid base; if macroeconomic pressures stall this transition, growth could stagnate rapidly.

Financial profile and peer benchmarking

Despite reported return on net operating assets consistently exceeding 25 percent annually, the stock trades at 4.5x to 5.1x forward EV/EBITDA, representing a massive 30 to 37 percent discount versus the European sector average that persists largely due to fears over PPC's disruptive fiber network buildout. Nevertheless, the exceptional margins and free cash flow generation reflect a protected incumbent market that PPC's aggressive new fiber rollout could permanently erode.

Industry structure and competitive position

The Greek mobile market structurally outpaces European peers, with average revenue growing 2.0% versus flat wider-European trends over five recent quarters, driven by a durable, multi-year runway for ARPU-accretive postpaid migrations following historical financial crisis patterns that left Greece with exceptionally high prepaid penetration. Vodafone consistently initiates inflation-linked price hikes, providing crucial cover for OTE to elevate its own tariffs safely, while OTE and Nova cross-license premium sports broadcasting rights, eliminating destructive bidding wars and structurally boosting pay-TV retention.

The state power utility PPC entered the retail broadband market with aggressive discounts up to 50% below established incumbent pricing, yet despite severe discounting, the utility secured only 13,000 to 20,000 retail connections by early 2026, failing to dent OTE's base because the utility lacks retail telco distribution, mobile offerings, or pay-TV products, severely limiting its converged bundling capabilities. Critically, the utility's leadership explicitly and repeatedly rules out entering the mobile market, containing the disruption vector exclusively to fixed-line infrastructure, though the utility competitor possesses vast capital and aerial infrastructure; acquiring a mobile challenger would rapidly shatter the established oligopoly pricing discipline.

Value chain position and margin distribution

OTE operates as a fully integrated incumbent across the profit-heavy infrastructure and retail stages, combining highly profitable passive infrastructure, active networks, and retail services under one corporate umbrella. The company recently spun off its 3,800 mobile towers into a 100% owned subsidiary, retaining full control of these high-margin assets, while maintaining full ownership of the tower subsidiary allows the operator to avoid the margin leakage associated with leasing back sites from independent TowerCos. The operator actively consolidates its network layer, recently acquiring Terna Fiber to fully control the Ultra-Fast Broadband deployment in rural Greece, ensuring the company will not have to pay wholesale fees to third parties in those regions, structurally protecting its retail margins.

Global radio access network spend is slowing abruptly, transferring significant bargaining power from equipment vendors like Ericsson and Nokia back to telecom operators, while OTE utilizes long-term Power Purchasing Agreements and fixed contracts to effectively shield its cost base from energy inflation over the next three years. The Greek market exhibits exceptionally strong retail pricing power, with standard 10-15GB mobile plans costing 20-30 euros monthly, well above European averages, and the company successfully utilizes adjacent services, such as the BOX food delivery platform and Payzy digital wallet, to increase retail customer stickiness and elevate switching costs.

Customer and supplier dynamics

OTE faces zero material customer concentration risk within its core consumer segment, while the mobile division drives retention through a continuous nine-year migration of users from prepaid to stickier postpaid contracts, with postpaid subscribers now accounting for 45% of the total mobile base, steadily rising from 38% in 2023. Approximately 70% to 75% of fixed-line customers also take mobile or pay-TV services, creating significant churn barriers, while OTE recently introduced "MagentaONE" to unify fixed and mobile plans, providing major discounts that structurally penalize partial switching.

OTE successfully implemented a 2.6% CPI-linked monthly fee increase across 2 million postpaid customers, averaging roughly €0.50 per user, and management unilaterally raised the minimum offline prepaid top-up from €13 to €15, forcing higher minimum commitments without triggering mass churn. A former OTE Group manager highlights that competitors cannot currently surpass COSMOTE's network reliability, cementing its pricing power, though the pricing leverage relies entirely on Vodafone and Nova remaining rational; if macroeconomic pressure forces them to discount, OTE's premium ARPU would compress.

OTE leverages Deutsche Telekom's scale to negotiate network equipment contracts, structurally diluting the bargaining power of individual hardware vendors, while total 2025 purchases from Deutsche Telekom group companies reached 571.9 million euros, representing massive indirect supply chain dependency. OTE owns and operates the "Germanos" retail network, ensuring distributors cannot force unfavorable pricing, exclusivity terms, or margin squeezes, with management aggressively pushing online sales through the centralized Cosmote App, structurally bypassing physical retail costs and accelerating direct-to-customer transitions.

Foundation quality and operator strength

Incoming CEO Kostas Nebis assumed leadership in mid-2024 with a formidable track record of accelerating structural growth at Hrvatski Telekom, where under his prior leadership, the Croatian telco delivered increasingly stronger operating performance and financials across a five-year executive tenure. Deutsche Telekom's 54% majority ownership provides strict oversight, anchoring OTE's corporate governance to premium German institutional standards, while deep operational integration with the German parent grants OTE access to advanced cybersecurity platforms, AI tools, and massive procurement scale.

OTE operates as the undisputed apex incumbent, capturing 65% of sector revenue and an overwhelming 93% of operating free cash flow, while consistent recognition as the fastest national network structurally lowers churn, ensuring the company retains high-end postpaid and enterprise clients. An industry expert explicitly confirms that there is currently no competitor capable of surpassing COSMOTE's network reliability. The company routinely exercises its pricing leverage, recently raising the minimum offline prepaid top-up by 15% without triggering mass churn, while management successfully implemented CPI-linked monthly fee increases across 2 million postpaid customers, proving the absolute inelasticity of its premium subscriber base.

The mechanical nature of Greek product penetration catch-up, combined with 0.3x leverage and Deutsche Telekom governance, creates a highly robust foundation, though the single largest risk remains a complete breakdown of Greek telecom pricing discipline driven by aggressive cross-selling and infrastructure overbuilding from the state utility PPC, with accelerating prepaid-to-postpaid churn rates or a documented inability to seamlessly pass through standard CPI-linked annual pricing increases serving as early warning signals.

Disruption forces and long-term growth

Building nationwide fibre and mobile networks requires vast upfront capital, structurally deterring asset-light entrants in the Greek market, while the state utility PPC aggressively overbuilds fixed-line fibre but explicitly rules out entering the highly profitable mobile sector. The Greek mobile market is a mature three-player oligopoly, securing long-term pricing discipline compared to fragmented four-player European peers, while telecom operators are structurally regaining pricing leverage against equipment vendors as the peak 5G capital expenditure cycle subsides globally.

OTE serves as a premier systems integrator for European and public sector digitalisation, driving exceptional 57% year-on-year systems solutions growth, while the operator successfully launched GPU-as-a-Service capabilities, directly positioning its data centres to capture surging enterprise AI workloads. Greek postpaid penetration sits unusually low, offering a highly predictable, multi-year runway for ARPU-accretive prepaid-to-postpaid migrations, while the government will abolish the 10% special pay-TV tax in 2026, mechanically stimulating legitimate subscriber growth against piracy.

Major rating agencies are upgrading Greece to Developed Market status by 2027, structurally expanding the addressable institutional investor base, while as peak fibre buildouts complete toward the end of the decade, free cash flow generation will mechanically expand. However, the state utility's massive fibre rollout could permanently commoditise the fixed-line access stage, eroding OTE's most durable competitive advantage over the long term.
