3. THEME §1 — Revenue Drivers + Visibility

J-front verdict: Revenue-side uncertainty is HIGH — destocking signal is visible in H2 FY24 and early FY25 results, but whether order book recovery is real or management-guided optimism is unresolved; questions below are designed to stress-test backlog claims

Q1 — Divisional revenue bridge: volume vs price vs mix

Question: "Can you walk me through each division's revenue change year-on-year — splitting volume, price, and mix — for the most recent half?"

  • Expected answer: Management confirms price was broadly flat to marginally positive; volume down in Distribution, roughly flat in Systems; mix negative due to lower-margin product categories running hot
  • Good answer signature: Specific numbers per division, unprompted acknowledgement of which mix headwinds are structural vs transient
  • Bad answer signature: Blended group-level answer, inability to split volume/price, or "pricing held up well" without quantum
  • Follow-up probe: "For the volume decline in Distribution — is that destocking at the customer, or end-market demand genuinely down?"
  • Diagnostic note: If volume is down but price is held, bull case (destocking cycle, recoverable) is more plausible; if price is also softening, bear case (competitive pressure, share loss) is more plausible; cross-ref Q19 ACH matrix destocking vs structural-demand failure mode

Backlog visibility:

Q2 — Order book cover and quality

Question: "What is your current order book coverage for the next 12 months, and how does order quality — firm vs conditional — compare to 18 months ago?"

  • Expected answer: Order book coverage for Systems is 6-9 months (typical for project-based businesses); Distribution is shorter; some deterioration vs peak FY23
  • Good answer signature: Explicit firm/conditional split, acknowledgement of any push-outs or cancellations, specific sector composition (defence vs industrial vs energy)
  • Bad answer signature: No firm/conditional distinction, or "the order book is healthy" without data
  • Follow-up probe: "Of the conditional orders, what conversion rate do you apply internally — and has that rate changed?"
  • Diagnostic note: Stable or improving firm order coverage = bull; rising conditional proportion or declining conversion assumption = bear/false-friend; this is the cleanest leading indicator available

Customer concentration:

Q3 — Top-5 customer revenue and contract renewal calendar

Question: "What percentage of group revenue comes from your top 5 customers, and when do those contracts or frameworks come up for renewal?"

  • Expected answer: Top-5 likely 25-40% of revenue given the business model mix; some multi-year framework agreements in defence
  • Good answer signature: Specific renewal dates flagged, no single customer above 15%, at least one multi-year framework recently renewed
  • Bad answer signature: Refusal to quantify, or single customer materially above 20% without a long-term contract in place
  • Follow-up probe: "Have any top-10 customers reduced their annual spend with you in the last 12 months — and if so, why?"
  • Diagnostic note: Customer concentration + churn is the false-friend risk — the company appears diversified but if 2-3 large framework customers pause ordering simultaneously, the revenue line is more volatile than it looks

End-market segmentation:

Q4 — Defence vs commercial mix trend

Question: "Defence has been cited as a tailwind — what proportion of group revenue is now defence-derived, and how has that changed over 3 years?"

  • Expected answer: Defence has grown as a proportion, now likely 30-45% of group; Management will cite UK/NATO procurement as a driver
  • Good answer signature: Specific percentage with a directional trend, differentiation between direct prime-contract defence vs dual-use industrial
  • Bad answer signature: Vague "significant defence exposure" without a number, or conflation of dual-use industrial with pure-play defence to inflate the headline figure
  • Follow-up probe: "Of the defence revenue, what proportion is on frameworks with committed call-off schedules vs discretionary project orders?"
  • Diagnostic note: Framework/call-off defence revenue is much higher quality than discretionary — this question discriminates between a durable defence-growth story (bull) and a marketing narrative that overstates defence exposure (false-friend)

Sceptical bullet: Fluent answers on revenue visibility from a management team with three recent integrations may reflect rehearsal rather than genuine granularity — probe for specifics the IR script wouldn't contain (specific customer names if permissible, specific contract renewal dates, specific conversion rates)

4. THEME §2 — Margin Trajectory + Levers

J-front verdict: Margin trajectory is the HIGHEST-uncertainty item — gross margin compression in Distribution was visible in FY24; recovery path depends on mix, pricing recovery, and integration synergy realisation; questions below test whether management has a credible margin bridge

GM driver:

Q5 — Gross margin bridge: Distribution division

Question: "Distribution gross margin compressed in FY24 — can you quantify the drivers and tell me what proportion of that compression is reversible in 12 months?"

  • Expected answer: Compression driven by product mix (more commodity components vs value-added), some pricing pressure, and inventory-related costs; management will claim partial reversibility
  • Good answer signature: Explicit quantification (e.g., "150bps from mix, 50bps from pricing, 30bps from inventory costs"), clear mechanism for recovery, honest acknowledgement of what is structural
  • Bad answer signature: "Margins will improve as volumes recover" without a mechanism, or inability to split the compression into components
  • Follow-up probe: "Is there a floor gross margin in Distribution below which you would exit or restructure product lines?"
  • Diagnostic note: A management team that can't bridge their own gross margin compression is not running the business at the operator-detail level the thesis requires; this is a quality-of-management diagnostic as much as a margin diagnostic

Mix shift:

Q6 — Systems vs Distribution margin mix and trajectory

Question: "Systems margins are structurally higher than Distribution — are you actively shifting group mix toward Systems, and what does that mean for capex and headcount?"

  • Expected answer: Yes, mix-shift toward Systems is stated strategy; implies more engineering headcount and longer project cycles; capex relatively light (service/engineering business)
  • Good answer signature: Specific Systems revenue growth targets, acknowledgement of the operational trade-offs (longer cycle, more working capital, more key-person risk)
  • Bad answer signature: "Systems is a great business" without a resource allocation plan, or capex/headcount targets that don't support the stated ambition
  • Follow-up probe: "How much of Systems revenue growth in the last 2 years was organic vs acquired, and what is the organic growth rate of legacy Systems?"
  • Diagnostic note: If Systems organic growth is low despite M&A, the acquisition strategy may be masking organic stagnation — false-friend risk

Op leverage:

Q7 — Fixed cost base and operating leverage at various revenue scenarios

Question: "If group revenue grew 10% from here, what would the EBIT flow-through rate be — and how does that change at +20%?"

  • Expected answer: Meaningful operating leverage exists given largely fixed central overhead; management will likely claim 30-40% incremental margins, possibly higher in Systems
  • Good answer signature: Specific incremental margin assumption per division, acknowledgement of which cost lines are genuinely variable vs semi-fixed
  • Bad answer signature: "We have good operational leverage" without a number, or a flow-through rate that implies margins already at normalised levels (no upside)
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the single biggest cost line you cannot reduce quickly if revenue disappointed — and what is its magnitude?"
  • Diagnostic note: Operating leverage quantification separates a business with genuine earnings power at normalised revenue from one where cost creep has absorbed the theoretical leverage; cross-ref with the FY24 overhead absorption problem

R&D adequacy:

Q8 — R&D spend adequacy for competitive positioning

Question: "What percentage of revenue is invested in product/technology development, and is that sufficient to maintain your technical edge in 3 years?"

  • Expected answer: R&D as disclosed is relatively modest for a technology-adjacent business; management may broaden to include engineering-bid costs; likely 2-4% of revenue formally classified as R&D
  • Good answer signature: Honest acknowledgement if R&D intensity is below what the competitive position requires, with a plan to address; or evidence that the value-added is in systems integration (less R&D-intensive)
  • Bad answer signature: Conflation of bid costs with genuine R&D, or claim that current R&D is fully adequate without referencing what competitors spend
  • Follow-up probe: "What technology shift — AI-embedded components, software-defined systems, something else — most threatens your current product portfolio in the next 5 years?"
  • Diagnostic note: R&D adequacy is the long-duration bear case for a distributor/systems integrator — not a near-term risk but a compounding structural one; the answer reveals how far ahead management is thinking

Sceptical bullet: A management team that presents a clean margin-recovery story without acknowledging the structural mix pressure from Distribution should trigger scepticism — the most credible answer includes genuine tension between near-term recovery optimism and structural headwinds

5. THEME §3 — Capital Allocation + Balance Sheet

J-front verdict: Capital allocation is a KEY discriminator — post-acquisition leverage and integration execution determine whether the M&A programme creates or destroys value; questions below probe RONIC discipline and balance sheet headroom

RONIC discipline:

Q9 — Returns hurdle for acquisitions and post-acquisition ROCE

Question: "What ROCE do the three recent acquisitions (Willow, Pacer, ICS) generate today, and how does that compare to your stated hurdle rate at acquisition?"

  • Expected answer: Management will likely deflect to synergy-adjusted or run-rate ROCE; actual achieved ROCE likely below hurdle given integration costs and revenue softness
  • Good answer signature: Honest admission of where ROCE currently sits vs hurdle, specific synergy delivery milestones, timeline to hurdle-rate achievement
  • Bad answer signature: "These are strategic acquisitions" (code for: returns below hurdle and no timeline to recovery), inability to give a ROCE number
  • Follow-up probe: "If one of these acquisitions is underperforming on ROCE, at what point would you consider disposal?"
  • Diagnostic note: RONIC discipline is the single best predictor of long-run value creation in a serial-acquirer; an honest answer that includes current-below-hurdle + credible path is actually more bullish than a claim of immediate hurdle-rate achievement

Leverage target:

Q10 — Net debt trajectory and leverage ceiling

Question: "What is your net debt/EBITDA target, how long to reach it, and what is the maximum leverage you would tolerate for a compelling acquisition?"

  • Expected answer: Current leverage likely 1.5-2.5x EBITDA post-acquisitions; management will cite a target of ≤2x; ceiling for a strategic deal likely stated at 3x
  • Good answer signature: Specific deleveraging timeline with FCF assumptions, honest acknowledgement of covenant headroom, clear ceiling with rationale
  • Bad answer signature: "We are comfortable with current leverage" without a target or timeline, or a ceiling that implies another acquisition before deleveraging is complete
  • Follow-up probe: "What does your FCF generation look like in a flat-revenue scenario — is deleveraging still on track?"
  • Diagnostic note: Leverage in a revenue-soft environment is a binary risk; if FCF is insufficient to deleverage on schedule, either the dividend or the M&A programme is constrained — this discriminates between a self-funding growth story and a balance sheet management story

Working capital management:

Q11 — Inventory days and cash conversion cycle

Question: "Inventory days have increased — what is the current level, what is the normalised level, and when does it normalise?"

  • Expected answer: Inventory days elevated due to Distribution build-up and destocking cycle; management will cite normalisation in 12-18 months
  • Good answer signature: Specific inventory days number (current vs normalised), breakdown by division, explicit slow-moving/excess inventory quantum
  • Bad answer signature: "Inventory is being worked down" without a number or timeline, inability to give a slow-moving inventory quantum
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the carrying value of inventory that has been on the balance sheet for more than 12 months — and what is your write-down policy for that cohort?"
  • Diagnostic note: This is the balance-sheet bear-case probe; a significant slow-moving inventory balance that hasn't been written down is a hidden P&L risk; the answer is diagnostic for the CFO's conservatism

Sceptical bullet: Post-acquisition capital allocation narratives tend to be framed around synergy delivery on management's own timeline; the diagnostic test is whether management can articulate what would cause them to accelerate disposal or restructuring — not just what would cause the synergies to arrive faster

6. THEME §4 — Strategy + Competitive Position

J-front verdict: Strategic positioning is MODERATELY HIGH uncertainty — the stated strategy (higher Systems mix, defence tailwind, value-added distribution) is coherent, but whether SOLI has genuine moat in any of these areas vs scale-advantaged competitors is unresolved

Moat trajectory:

Q12 — Sustainable differentiation in Systems integration

Question: "In Systems integration, what specifically prevents a larger competitor — a defence prime or a specialist integrator — from taking your customers?"

  • Expected answer: Management will cite relationships, niche technical expertise, approved-supplier status, speed of delivery; these are real but fragile moats
  • Good answer signature: Specific barriers cited with examples (e.g., certifications that take 3 years to replicate, long-term framework agreements, proprietary IP in integration processes), honest acknowledgement of where moat is thin
  • Bad answer signature: "We have strong customer relationships" without evidence of stickiness (contract duration, switching cost quantification, customer NPS or retention data)
  • Follow-up probe: "Have you lost a significant tender in the last 12 months to a competitor — and if so, who won it and why?"
  • Diagnostic note: Lost tenders are the clearest signal of competitive position deterioration; management willingness to discuss this is itself a quality-of-management signal

Adjacency expansion:

Q13 — Next adjacency and strategic optionality

Question: "If you made one more acquisition in the next 18 months, what capability or market access would it add that you don't have today?"

  • Expected answer: Management will likely cite either geographic expansion (US/Europe) or deepening in a specific vertical (space, CBRN, autonomy)
  • Good answer signature: Specific and coherent rationale for the adjacency, acknowledgement of integration capacity constraints given recent M&A
  • Bad answer signature: A generic "bolt-on that fits our model" answer, or a response that reveals the next deal is already in the pipeline before current integrations are complete
  • Follow-up probe: "Do you have the management bandwidth to integrate another acquisition right now, or does the next 18 months need to be consolidation-focused?"
  • Diagnostic note: Management that is already hunting for the next deal before current integrations are proven is exhibiting acquisition-addiction behaviour — this is a capital-destruction risk; the question is designed to surface it

Regulatory positioning:

Q14 — Export control and compliance posture

Question: "How significant are export control and ITAR/EAR compliance obligations for your business, and have you had any compliance incidents in the last 3 years?"

  • Expected answer: Export control is moderately significant given dual-use and defence content; management will likely have a compliance function; no disclosed incidents
  • Good answer signature: Evidence of a mature compliance programme, proactive investment in compliance infrastructure, honest disclosure of any minor incidents resolved
  • Bad answer signature: Downplaying of export control complexity, or a compliance answer that sounds like it's being delivered for the first time
  • Follow-up probe: "As the defence proportion of your revenue grows, how are you scaling the compliance function — what is the headcount and budget?"
  • Diagnostic note: Export control is a silent bear risk for a defence-adjacent distributor/integrator; the question surfaces whether management is ahead of or behind the regulatory curve

Sceptical bullet: The defence tailwind narrative is structurally compelling given the macro environment, but the quality of defence exposure (framework call-offs vs discretionary project, prime vs sub-prime) determines how much of the tailwind actually flows through to SOLI — fluent defence-growth narrative without distinguishing these is a rehearsed answer, not a revealing one

7. THEME §5 — Management Quality + Operator Track Record

J-front verdict: Management quality is UNCERTAIN — the team has executed a multi-acquisition programme under complexity, but integration track record is unproven at this scale; questions below probe operator depth, incentive alignment, and sandbagging discipline

Sandbagging discipline:

Q15 — Track record on guidance vs outcome

Question: "Over the last 5 years, how often has the business met, beaten, or missed its own internal targets — not just consensus, but internal?"

  • Expected answer: Management will default to consensus-beat narrative; internal targets are rarely disclosed; the question is designed to probe whether they sandbag externally
  • Good answer signature: Willingness to discuss internal vs external guidance discipline, acknowledgement of years where the business missed its own plan, specific examples
  • Bad answer signature: Deflection to consensus beats only, refusal to discuss internal targets, or a "we always deliver" answer that doesn't pass the plausibility test given FY24 revenue softness
  • Follow-up probe: "In FY24, what were your internal revenue and EBIT targets at the start of the year — how did you finish versus those?"
  • Diagnostic note: Sandbagging discipline discriminates between a management team that manages expectations conservatively (safe for the concentrated long) and one that guides optimistically and delivers disappointment; FY24 is the test case

Power of incentives (Concept A — mandatory):

Q16 — LTIP metrics, vesting criteria, and alignment

Question: "What are the primary metrics in your LTIP, what are the vesting thresholds, and have any grants lapsed unvested in the last 3 years?"

  • Expected answer: LTIP likely references EPS growth and/or TSR; vesting thresholds likely disclosed in annual report but rarely probed in meetings; no disclosed lapses
  • Good answer signature: Management can describe LTIP metrics without consulting notes, alignment with ROCE or FCF per share (not just EPS), acknowledgement if any grants are at risk
  • Bad answer signature: CEO/CFO unsure of their own LTIP metrics, or LTIP exclusively referencing revenue growth (misaligned with capital efficiency)
  • Follow-up probe: "Would you personally be willing to increase your equity holding in the business at current prices — and have you done so recently?"
  • Diagnostic note (Concept A): If LTIP is EPS-based only, management has an incentive to pursue EPS-accretive but ROCE-dilutive acquisitions — this is the mechanism through which M&A value destruction happens; the incentive structure predicts the next capital allocation decision

Culture archetype:

Q17 — Talent retention and key-person risk post-acquisitions

Question: "Of the senior people who came with the Willow, Pacer, and ICS acquisitions, what proportion are still with the business — and why did any leave?"

  • Expected answer: Some attrition is normal; management will claim retention was good; the real number may be higher than disclosed
  • Good answer signature: Specific retention rate, honest acknowledgement of 1-2 departures and the reasons, evidence that the cultural integration was deliberate (not assumed)
  • Bad answer signature: "Retention has been excellent" without data, or deflection to general HR policies
  • Follow-up probe: "Is there a single person in the acquired businesses whose departure would meaningfully damage revenue — and what retention mechanisms are in place for them?"
  • Diagnostic note: Key-person risk in acquisitions is a latent bear case for small-cap integrators; the question surfaces it in a way that management has to respond to specifically

Sceptical bullet: A CEO who speaks fluently about culture and retention without having concrete retention data is exhibiting a common small-cap management pattern — the culture is described in the CEO's image but not evidenced by process; the diagnostic test is whether HR metrics exist, not whether the CEO values culture

8. THEME §6 — Risk + Downside (Pre-Mortem Cross-Ref)

J-front verdict: Downside risks are INADEQUATELY PRICED by a simple destocking-recovery narrative — the bear case includes structural demand softness, integration failure, and inventory write-down; questions below are designed to surface each failure mode explicitly

Inventory at risk:

Q18 — Slow-moving inventory and write-down policy (⚡ non-obvious)

Question: "What is the carrying value of inventory lines where you haven't shipped in 6 months — and what triggers an automatic write-down?"

  • Expected answer: Management will likely have a 12-month slow-moving policy; specific carrying value of 6-month-stale inventory is rarely volunteered
  • Good answer signature: Specific number, clear policy mechanism (automatic write-down at X months), evidence the CFO runs this report regularly
  • Bad answer signature: "We review inventory regularly" without a number or policy specifics, or evidence that write-down policy is more aggressive in theory than practice
  • Follow-up probe: "In the last 2 reporting periods, what was the aggregate inventory write-down number — and is the pipeline of write-downs growing or shrinking?"
  • Diagnostic note: A growing slow-moving inventory balance without proportional write-downs is a hidden P&L risk that will eventually crystallise; this is a false-friend probe — the business looks stable on the surface but has a silent balance-sheet deterioration
  • ⚡ Outlier flag: This question is non-standard for investor meetings with small-cap management; most meetings don't get to this level of inventory granularity; if the CFO can't answer, it is more diagnostic than if they can

Bear-case probe:

Q19 — Revenue floor test

Question: "If defence procurement slowed materially and Distribution destocking continued for another 12 months simultaneously, what would group revenue and EBIT look like?"

  • Expected answer: Management will resist the double-adverse scenario; may offer individual sensitivity but not the compound scenario
  • Good answer signature: Willingness to stress-test in real time, acknowledgement that the compound scenario produces a materially worse outcome than consensus, and that contingency planning exists
  • Bad answer signature: "We don't see that as a realistic scenario" without engaging with the numbers, or deflection to the more-likely central case
  • Follow-up probe: "At what revenue level would you need to restructure the cost base — and what does that restructuring look like?"
  • Diagnostic note: The compound-adverse scenario is the Q19 ACH matrix highest-plausibility bear case; management's willingness to engage with it (vs deflect) is a quality signal

False-friend probe:

Q20 — Organic growth rate isolation

Question: "Stripping out all acquisitions made since FY21, what is the organic revenue growth rate of the legacy business over 3 years?"

  • Expected answer: Organic growth rate likely modest (3-6% CAGR); recent headline growth has been acquisition-driven; management may resist the isolation
  • Good answer signature: Management provides the number, acknowledges organic growth is below the headline rate, and articulates why the acquisitions are additive to organic growth going forward
  • Bad answer signature: Inability or unwillingness to isolate organic growth, or conflation of acquisition-driven revenue with organic growth
  • Follow-up probe: "Is the organic growth rate of the legacy business accelerating, decelerating, or flat over the last 3 years?"
  • Diagnostic note: This is the false-friend discriminator — a business that appears to grow at 12% CAGR headline but grows at 3% organically is a fundamentally different investment proposition; acquisition-driven growth without organic growth acceleration is a value-destruction treadmill

Sceptical bullet — uncovered ACH matrix items: The questions above cover destocking duration, integration risk, inventory write-down, and defence-demand sensitivity; NOT explicitly covered: (a) supplier concentration and component availability risk (a single-source supplier failure could cascade through Distribution margin), (b) the FX sensitivity of non-UK sourcing in Distribution, (c) the cyber/IP risk in Systems (a data breach on a defence project would have asymmetric reputational consequences) — these are surfaced for §8

9. CROSS-CUTTING META + NON-OBVIOUS QUESTIONS (LOAD-BEARING)

J-front verdict: The consensus interview asks about revenue recovery and margin trajectory; the most-diagnostic questions are the ones management WOULDN'T volunteer — the questions below are designed to surface the edges, incentives, and failure modes that the IR script doesn't reach

Non-obvious angle:

  • "Single-metric worry" probe (Q2 of BLUF): "What single metric, if it moved against you, would most concern you — and what is the current trend on that metric?" — this is the CFO's internal dashboard question; the answer reveals which risk they actually manage against
  • Expected: CFO names order intake or gross margin; Good: specific number + trend disclosed; Bad: generic answer or deflection to a metric that sounds good
  • Diagnostic: the metric they name is the one they're most exposed on — this is a rare case where a candid answer is more informative than a rehearsed one

WH Smith probe:

  • ⚡ "Cutting 20% of capex" test: "If you had to cut 20% of your investment budget tomorrow, which projects would you protect and which would you cut?" — this reveals the genuine priority stack, not the stated strategy
  • Expected: Management protects Systems engineering capability and cuts Distribution IT/automation; Good: clear prioritisation with rationale; Bad: "we wouldn't cut anything" or a list that preserves everything
  • Diagnostic: a management team that can't rank their own investment priorities hasn't made the difficult trade-offs; the answer discriminates between a management team with genuine conviction and one executing strategy-by-PowerPoint

Cross-roads question:

  • "Most surprising customer feedback" probe: "What is the most surprising piece of customer feedback you have received in the last 6 months — something that changed how you think about the business?" — this is the unconventional question that surfaces unconventional information
  • Expected: Something about delivery speed, technical support quality, or product availability; Good: a candid answer that reveals a gap between management's self-image and customer perception; Bad: a polished answer that is clearly designed to reassure
  • Diagnostic: the quality and candour of the answer reveals how close management is to the customer vs insulated by the IR function

Edge-detection probe:

  • "What keeps you up at night" — asked LAST: Save this for the end of the meeting when defences are lower — "If you imagine looking back in 3 years and the business hasn't achieved its targets, what was the reason?" — this is the pre-mortem question directed at the CEO/CFO
  • Expected: Management names a generic macro risk (defence procurement cuts, supply chain); Good: a specific and candid answer that names an internal risk; Bad: a generic answer that could have been scripted
  • Diagnostic: specific internal risk named = genuine self-awareness; generic macro risk only = management is not running a pre-mortem internally

Multiple-truths probe (Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 3):

  • "And yet" question on destocking: "You're right that destocking is cyclical and should normalise — and yet, if a portion of the Distribution volume decline is structural end-market share loss, what does that portion look like and how are you addressing it?" — this question explicitly acknowledges the bull case and simultaneously asks for the bear-case nuance, preventing management from dodging into either-or framing
  • Expected: Management acknowledges some structural element, quantifies it (or can't), and articulates the response
  • Good answer: "We estimate 30% of the volume decline is structural and here's what we're doing about it"; Bad answer: "It's all cyclical" — this is an implausible answer for a business with three divisions in different end-markets

Incentive probe:

  • Stock ownership question: "What is your current beneficial equity holding as a percentage of your annual salary — and has it increased or decreased in the last 12 months?" — aligns with Concept A power-of-incentives; management with growing equity holdings are demonstrating the strongest form of conviction
  • Expected: CEO has meaningful equity holding; CFO less so; recent net selling is a signal worth probing
  • Good: Both have increased holdings; Bad: net selling in the last 12 months without a declared plan

Diagnostic crystalliser:

  • "What has changed in the last 6 months that I don't know from the published results?" — a deliberately open question asked at the end; management will either (a) volunteer nothing (rehearsed), (b) flag something minor (testing), or (c) reveal a genuine update that hasn't been published (informative); the analyst's job is to probe category (c) further
  • Diagnostic: this is the "information not in filings" gate from the MISSION; the question literally asks for it; management's willingness to engage is itself diagnostic

Stewing trigger:

  • "If your largest customer called you tomorrow and told you they were moving 50% of their spend to a competitor, could you replace that revenue within 24 months?" — this is a stewing question; management will be uncomfortable; the discomfort is the signal
  • Good: "Yes, here's how — we have X customers we're not yet fully serving"; Bad: "That wouldn't happen" (non-answer) or visible discomfort without recovery

Sceptical bullet — questions the analyst is NOT asking because they feel uncomfortable: The question the analyst is most avoiding is "Are you losing Systems integration tenders to lower-cost or better-resourced competitors?" — it implies the core differentiation thesis is wrong; it feels like an accusation; it is the most important question in the meeting if the analyst holds the bear case with any weight; the obstacle is the way; it should be asked, probably in the form of Q12's follow-up ("have you lost a significant tender in the last 12 months and who won it?"), but the analyst should be prepared to press further if the first answer is deflective

10. AGGREGATE WEAK SIGNALS / DOWNSIDE (❌)

  • ❌ Inventory write-down risk: slow-moving inventory balance is unquantified in public filings; if the CFO cannot give a specific number in the meeting, the risk is not yet managed
  • ❌ Organic growth isolation: if organic revenue growth of the legacy business is below 5% CAGR, the acquisition programme is masking stagnation, not accelerating growth
  • ❌ LTIP misalignment: if LTIP is EPS-only (not ROCE or FCF/share), the incentive structure predicts continued acquisition activity regardless of integration quality — this is a compounding value-destruction risk
  • ❌ Key-person attrition post-acquisition: if senior attrition from acquired businesses is above 25%, integration has damaged the asset being acquired, not just the integration costs
  • ❌ Defence exposure quality: if >50% of "defence" revenue is dual-use industrial rather than true defence frameworks, the defence tailwind narrative is overstated
  • ❌ Compound-adverse scenario not modelled: if management cannot engage with the compound (defence slowdown + extended destocking) scenario, the company may not have stress-tested its covenant compliance under that scenario
