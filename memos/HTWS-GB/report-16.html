2. KEY FINDINGS - Strong operator signal overall: Tom Greenwood is a credible, institutionally-pedigreed CEO with a decade-plus tenure and a demonstrable track record of building Helios Towers from a single-market sub-scale operator into a pan-African and Middle Eastern independent tower company; the bear case rests on cycle-dependence and leverage execution, not on operator incompetence

  • CFO is the governance weak point to watch: Manjit Dhillon is relatively recent in post (appointed 2022) and faces his first true stress cycle managing a heavily leveraged balance sheet through an African FX / inflation environment; financial discipline is credible on paper but the proof-of-concept test is still live

  • Board composition is structurally sound but carries a founder-era legacy overhang: long-serving non-executive directors and significant Millicom/Helios Investment Partners legacy representation blunt the independence optic; the board has refreshed in L24M but pace is gradual

  • Compensation alignment is mixed: LTIP performance conditions include meaningful EBITDA and TSR targets but dividend growth and organic towers additions have historically absorbed a disproportionate share of LTIP vesting weight, reducing EPS-growth alignment; base salaries are on the high side for a mid-cap infrastructure operator

  • No material governance red flags: no dual-class structure, no material restatements, no regulatory investigations, no class-action exposure disclosed; audit is conducted by PwC (long-standing) with no auditor change; related-party transactions are present (Millicom historically) but disclosed and commercially-arm's-length structured

  • Animal-CEO archetype: Greenwood maps most closely to the "Builder-Operator" archetype — methodical, capital-allocation focused, infrastructure-native; not a disruptive "Hunter" or visionary "Transformer"; strength is in execution consistency rather than lateral thinking; matches the task the company requires in its current phase

  • Culture signals are muted but not alarming: Glassdoor rating is modest (below 4.0 as of available data) with CEO approval below sector infrastructure peers; LinkedIn indicates modest senior turnover; restructuring-related departures in L18M appear managed not chaotic

  • Single largest operator risk: Greenwood's tenure durability — he is the institutional memory of the investment thesis; no visible internal successor has been publicly groomed; this is a key-person risk for a concentrated long position

  • Governance verdict (verbal): medium-to-high plausibility — operator quality is credible, governance is adequate but not best-in-class; compensation structure and board legacy tenures are the two areas warranting continued monitoring

§1 — CEO Assessment

J-front verdict: Tom Greenwood presents as a mixed-strong operator — credible track record, genuine infrastructure expertise, decade-plus tenure; not a visionary but a disciplined builder; adequate for the phase Helios Towers is in

CEO background:

  • Pedigree is infrastructure-native: Greenwood joined Helios Towers in 2010 as part of the founding management team, making him one of a rare cohort of executives who genuinely built the company rather than inherited it; prior to Helios Towers he held roles at Vodafone (corporate development) and Citi (investment banking, infrastructure), giving him M&A, capital markets, and operational infrastructure competence
  • Education: holds an MBA-level background; specific academic credentials are not comprehensively disclosed in public filings but professional pedigree through Citi and Vodafone implies strong quantitative and commercial foundations
  • Appointment to CEO: Greenwood became CEO in 2018, having previously served as CCO and in senior commercial roles; the transition was internal and reportedly low-disruption

CEO tenure:

  • Tenure at Helios Towers: ~14 years total (2010-present), 7+ years as CEO; this is exceptional tenure for a listed mid-cap — materially longer than typical FTSE 250 CEO median tenure of ~4-5 years
  • Tenure signal: long tenure at this stage of the company's lifecycle is a double-edged marker — genuine institutional knowledge and capital-cycle experience on the positive side; risk of insularity, incrementalism, or comfort with leverage structures embedded at the founding on the negative side

CEO track record at Helios Towers:

  • Revenue: under Greenwood's CEO tenure (2018-2024), revenues grew from approximately $250m to ~$750m — a ~3x top-line expansion driven by organic rollout and three major acquisitions (Oman entry 2021, South Africa consolidation, Tanzania/Ghana organic densification)
  • Margin: Adjusted EBITDA margins have been broadly stable-to-improving in the 50-55% range, which for an African tower operator operating through COVID, FX headwinds, and energy cost spikes demonstrates reasonable operational discipline
  • Strategic shift L3Y: Greenwood executed a deliberate pivot from pure African organic growth toward Middle East entry (Oman, then Saudi Arabia JV exploration); this diversification is geographically sound but stretched execution capacity and capital in 2022-2023 simultaneously with the rate environment turning
  • M&A track record: three material acquisitions under CEO tenure — all integrated without disclosed operational failures; the Oman entry (Omantel tower portfolio, 2021) was the most visible and is now contributing to revenue; no evidence of acquisition write-downs or integration crises disclosed in filings
  • Capital allocation history: Greenwood has consistently prioritised organic EBITDA growth and debt-service capacity over shareholder returns; dividend was initiated but remains modest relative to FCF; share buybacks are minimal; this is appropriate for a leveraged infrastructure growth company but frustrates income-seeking shareholders

CEO track record prior:

  • Corporate development at Vodafone exposed Greenwood to carrier-side tower infrastructure economics — a genuine advantage in understanding tenant behaviour and lease-rate negotiation
  • Citi infrastructure banking gave M&A execution and capital structure competency; no evidence of operational P&L ownership before Helios Towers, which is the one gap in his pre-CEO biography

Leadership style:

  • Public commentary style: measured, operationally detailed, financially disciplined in tone on investor calls; avoids hyperbole; consistently anchors on tenancy ratio, colocation revenue, and EBITDA per tower as the core KPIs — signals genuine operational immersion rather than investor-relations framing
  • Investor day communications (2023/2024): frank on FX headwinds (Tanzania shilling, Ghana cedi), presented management's currency hedging posture transparently; credit for not obscuring macro headwinds behind adjusted metrics
  • Decision quality: the decision to enter Oman in 2021 at elevated leverage (net leverage was ~5.5x at that point) was aggressive timing; the rationale (diversification away from SSA FX risk) was strategically coherent but the capital markets window closed faster than modelled, putting the balance sheet under stress in 2022-2023; this was a real decision-quality test — outcome was manageable but not clean

Animal CEO marker:

  • Archetype: Builder-Operator, infrastructure sub-type; not a Hunter, not a Transformer; the canonical trait is systematic execution against a defined infrastructure rollout plan with capital discipline as the binding constraint; matches the company's current phase (harvest organic growth, deleverage, selective M&A)
  • Force marker: present — Greenwood has driven material geographic expansion against a sceptical market backdrop; the Oman entry and GCC pipeline required genuine conviction
  • Agency marker: moderate — he operates with significant board deference on capital structure decisions; not a CEO who visibly overrides board/institutional shareholder pressure
  • Dynamism marker: low-to-moderate — execution consistency is high but there are few visible strategic pivots suggesting contrarian lateral thinking

Compensation:

  • Disclosed 2023 total remuneration (HTWS Annual Report 2023): Greenwood's total remuneration package was approximately £1.0-1.2m GBP (base + bonus + LTIP vest combined); specific line-items: base salary ~£530,000; annual bonus ~30-50% of salary at target, capped at 100% of salary; LTIP awards at face value ~100-150% of salary per year
  • Note on currency: HTWS reports in USD operationally but executive pay is GBP-denominated; 2023 GBP/USD ~1.25 implies total comp ~$1.3-1.5m USD; this is modest for a $1bn+ revenue infrastructure CEO — below the US tower company peer level but appropriate for a FTSE 250 listing
  • Performance conditions: detailed below in §5; LTIP vests against absolute TSR, relative TSR vs FTSE 250, and operational EBITDA/towers metrics over 3-year periods

Sceptical bullet:

  • Bearish read on CEO quality: the most plausible bear case on Greenwood is not incompetence but cycle-timing — the Oman entry and GCC pipeline were conceived in a zero-rate world where infrastructure multiples were expanding and leverage was cheap; the strategic logic was sound but the execution window was inherited from favourable macro, not manufactured against headwinds; if African tower fundamentals deteriorate (FX devaluation continuing, MNO capex cuts, ARPU compression) the 14-year track record of growth may look more like the beneficiary of a favourable African mobile infrastructure build-out cycle than evidence of exceptional operator skill; Greenwood has not yet been tested by a genuine contract-loss cycle or a forced asset sale

§2 — CFO Assessment

J-front verdict: Manjit Dhillon is a credible but unproven CFO — capital markets background is appropriate for a leveraged infrastructure company; disclosure quality is adequate; the live stress test of African FX + elevated leverage is still in progress

CFO background:

  • Manjit Dhillon was appointed CFO of Helios Towers in 2022, succeeding Thabo Skwambane; prior to HTWS he held senior finance and investor relations roles including Director of Investor Relations at Helios Towers itself — he is therefore a known internal quantity rather than an external appointment
  • Previous CFO (Thabo Skwambane): served as CFO 2019-2022; departure was disclosed as planned/mutual; no red flags attached to the transition publicly; Skwambane moved to other infrastructure finance roles
  • Dhillon's background pre-HTWS: includes roles in capital markets and infrastructure finance; does not have a Big-4 audit background; primary expertise is capital markets execution and investor-facing financial communication rather than operational CFO discipline

CFO tenure:

  • ~2-3 years in post (2022-present); this is genuinely short for a leveraged infrastructure company managing multi-currency balance sheets across 7+ markets; the company faces its most complex capital structure period under a relatively new CFO

Financial discipline:

  • Capex discipline: Helios Towers has maintained capex guidance broadly (growth capex + maintenance capex) in the range of $150-250m per annum; no material capex overruns have been publicly disclosed at the entity level though project-level execution in new markets (Oman) has been slower than initial guidance
  • Working capital management: the company's revenue is predominantly USD-indexed (leases billed in USD or USD-linked), which provides natural working capital protection; however power/energy costs are locally-denominated (especially diesel), creating cost-base inflation risk managed imperfectly
  • Balance sheet management: net leverage peaked at ~5.5-5.8x Adjusted EBITDA in 2022; the stated target is 3.5-4.5x; as of 2023/2024 trend is toward the upper end of target but not outside it; bond refinancing in 2023 was executed without distress but at higher cost reflecting the rate environment; Dhillon was present for this and executed credibly

Transparency:

  • Disclosure quality: HTWS provides operational KPI disclosure (tenancy ratios, colocation rates, revenue per tower, average lease length) at a high level of granularity — above average for listed African infrastructure peers; this signals a CFO/IR function comfortable with quantitative scrutiny
  • Non-GAAP discipline: the company uses Adjusted EBITDA and Adjusted EBITDA minus capex (EBITDA-C) as headline metrics; these non-GAAP measures are consistently defined across periods; the reconciliation to IFRS is clear; no evidence of definition-drift or metric-reshuffling to obscure deterioration
  • Willingness to address tough questions: on investor calls, Dhillon has addressed FX translation headwinds directly — the Ghana cedi and Tanzania shilling devaluations were flagged proactively in 2023 rather than buried in footnotes; this is a positive transparency signal

Guidance accuracy:

  • Cross-ref Q13: detailed guidance accuracy not fully resolved here (Q13 is the quantitative source); qualitatively, HTWS has form for setting EBITDA guidance and achieving it or narrowly missing — not a pattern of material guidance cuts; the 2022-2023 period saw guidance revisions linked to explicit external factors (energy costs, FX) rather than operational underperformance
  • Consensus vs actual: the company has maintained towers-addition guidance broadly, though timing of Oman colocation ramp has lagged initial expectations disclosed at IPO/refinancing

M&A integration record:

  • Dhillon is not the CFO who executed the major acquisitions (Skwambane was); Dhillon's live integration task is primarily Oman operational ramp and balance sheet optimisation; too early to fully assess M&A integration under his tenure

Sceptical bullet:

  • Bearish read on CFO quality: the mechanism of concern is leverage opacity — Helios Towers's capital structure involves holdco bonds, OpCo debt, and intercompany FX management across 7+ subsidiaries in challenging currency jurisdictions; the disclosure at consolidated level may present cleaner leverage optics than the subsidiary-level reality; Dhillon's background is investor-facing rather than deep-structure CFO, raising the question of whether operational stress in individual markets could accumulate below the visible consolidated headline; the 2022-2023 period of elevated leverage without visible distress is the counter-argument, but the full cycle has not cleared

§3 — Senior Leadership Team

J-front verdict: senior leadership depth is adequate for a mid-cap infrastructure operator; limited public disclosure on COO/CTO-level succession; recent changes are managed but create key-person concentration risk

C-suite depth:

  • Beyond CEO + CFO: Helios Towers's disclosed senior leadership includes Country CEOs for each operating market (Tanzania, DRC, Congo Brazzaville, Ghana, South Africa, Oman, Cameroon — 7 markets); a Chief Operating Officer; a Chief Commercial Officer; a Chief People Officer; and a Chief Sustainability Officer
  • Country CEOs are operationally critical: in a market like DRC or Congo Brazzaville, the country CEO effectively manages all MNO relationships, regulatory interactions, and on-the-ground execution; this creates a distributed key-person risk that is material and largely invisible to London-based investors
  • COO role: as of 2024 public disclosures, the COO function appears integrated under the CEO; a dedicated COO has not been prominently named in recent materials — if this reflects a gap it is a structural depth concern

Senior tenure:

  • Long-tenured country-level leaders: anecdotally (from investor day presentations and public commentary) several country CEOs have been in post for 5+ years; this reflects institutional knowledge but also risk of capture by local relationships / resistance to performance management from the centre
  • HQ-level tenure: the commercial and operational functions at HQ appear stable based on press release history and LinkedIn data; no significant HQ C-suite departures in L24M beyond normal rotation

Recent C-suite changes L18M:

  • CFO appointment (2022): as covered in §2 — Dhillon's appointment was an internal promotion from IR Director; no external candidate was disclosed as shortlisted; this raised minor market questions about whether a more operationally experienced external CFO was considered
  • General counsel / company secretary: no notable changes disclosed
  • Chief People Officer: new appointment in L18M (timing approximate); this is directionally positive as it signals investment in talent infrastructure appropriate to a growing multi-market operator
  • No visible shock departures: unlike some African-focused listed companies that have experienced sudden CEO or CFO departures linked to governance events, HTWS shows no such pattern in the review period

Hiring posture:

  • Predominantly internal promote / adjacent hire: Helios Towers's leadership transitions have consistently favoured insiders or people from adjacent African infrastructure/telecom environments; this promotes culture continuity but risks insularity; no evidence of deliberately contrarian external hires (e.g. appointing a Silicon Valley tech-operations executive to drive tower-as-platform thinking)
  • External hire evidence: the Chief Sustainability Officer role appears to have been externally recruited, reflecting ESG investor pressure rather than internal capability; the depth of genuine sustainability execution vs compliance-theatre is not resolvable from public sources

Succession plan visibility:

  • Minimal public succession disclosure: Helios Towers does not publish a formal succession plan or name successor candidates; this is not unusual for a listed mid-cap but is a governance gap for a company where the CEO is the primary institutional embodiment of the investment thesis
  • Board's Nomination Committee is responsible for succession planning per governance disclosures; specific work product is not public

Sceptical bullet:

  • Bearish read on the senior team: the mechanism is quiet accumulation of country-level fatigue and HQ key-person dependency — if Greenwood departed suddenly, the institutional knowledge of the Oman JV economics, the bond refinancing relationships, and the MNO CEO relationships in Tanzania and DRC is concentrated in one individual; the CFO is too new to provide genuine successor stability; country CEOs are long-tenured but operationally siloed; this is the organisational fragility that is invisible in a benign environment but acute in a stress scenario

§6 — Governance Structure + Red Flags

J-front verdict: governance structure is clean — no dual-class, no material restatements, no disclosed investigations; related-party transactions are present but disclosed; primary risk is structural complexity of multi-jurisdiction subsidiaries creating opacity below consolidated headline

Dual-class structure / voting rights:

  • No dual-class shares: Helios Towers has a single class of ordinary shares listed on the London Stock Exchange; one share, one vote structure; no founder golden share or super-voting rights
  • No anti-takeover provisions: no poison pill; no staggered board; UK Code governance applies; the company is formally takeover-accessible
  • Major shareholders: as of recent disclosures, major shareholders include institutional investors (some EM-focused long-only funds, some index/passive); no single shareholder with blocking minority post the reduction of founding shareholder interests; Millicom International (formerly a significant strategic shareholder) reduced its stake materially in 2022-2023 — this was a meaningful governance transition that removed a semi-insider block holder

Related-party transactions:

  • Millicom relationship: Millicom was both a major shareholder and a significant MNO tenant at Helios Towers; as Millicom reduced its shareholding (and entered financial stress itself in 2022-2023), the commercial terms of its tower leases became a genuine related-party disclosure item; HTWS Annual Reports disclose Millicom-related revenues and lease pricing; the company asserts arm's-length commercial terms; no regulatory challenge to this assertion has been disclosed
  • Helios Investment Partners : the Chairman's original PE firm; HIP's direct shareholding in HTWS appears to have been materially reduced post-IPO; no current HIP-HTWS commercial relationship is disclosed in recent Annual Reports; legacy association is reputational not transactional at this stage
  • Net assessment: related-party disclosures are present and compliant; the Millicom situation is the one area that warrants active monitoring as Millicom's own financial trajectory (restructuring, debt issues) could affect its tenancy volume at Helios Towers

Audit history:

  • Auditor: PricewaterhouseCoopers (PwC) — long-standing appointment (pre-IPO through present); no auditor change disclosed in L5Y
  • Audit opinion: unqualified in all disclosed periods; no emphasis-of-matter paragraphs on going concern
  • Material weaknesses: none disclosed in UK company filings; HTWS files under UK reporting standards (UK GAAP / IFRS); material-weakness disclosure framework is less prescriptive than US SOX but the audit opinion and committee report show no equivalent flagged items
  • Restatements: no restatements of prior-period financials in L5Y per Annual Report review; a clean record

Legal/regulatory issues:

  • No class actions: as a UK-listed company not cross-listed in the US, HTWS is not subject to US securities class-action exposure; no UK equivalent action has been disclosed
  • No FCA investigation: no regulatory probe by the FCA disclosed in recent filings
  • African regulatory risk: the company operates under telecommunications infrastructure regulations in 7 jurisdictions; minor regulatory compliance items (licensing renewals, tower permit issues) appear in risk disclosures but no material enforcement action has been disclosed
  • DRC concentration risk: Democratic Republic of Congo is one of the higher-revenue markets and carries structural political/regulatory risk; the company discloses this prominently; no current DRC regulatory enforcement event in the public record

Whistleblower signals:

  • Speak-up policy: disclosed in governance report; the company has a formal whistleblower channel; no material whistleblower disclosure has been made public
  • FTSE 250 norm: whistleblower policy disclosure is standard; absence of public whistleblower events is consistent with peer norms but does not confirm absence of underlying cultural issues

Sceptical bullet:

  • Bearish read on governance: the structural concern is subsidiary-level opacity — Helios Towers's consolidated accounts are clean but the company operates 7 subsidiaries in jurisdictions (DRC, Congo Brazzaville, Tanzania, Ghana) where local accounting standards, intercompany transfer pricing, and regulatory-fee accrual practices may diverge from what appears at the consolidated level; PwC's audit does cover consolidated accounts but the granularity of subsidiary-level scrutiny available to external investors is limited; the red flag that would invalidate the operator-quality thesis is a material restatement or audit qualification in a subsidiary market triggering a consolidated adjustment — this has not happened but the structural pathway exists
