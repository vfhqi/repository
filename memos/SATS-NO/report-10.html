1. PUBLISHED SHORT-SELLER REPORTS

No formal short-seller research has been published on SATS ASA by any named activist research firm. SATS is an Oslo-listed Norwegian company with a market cap in the range of NOK 5-8bn (depending on share price). This scale and geographic profile is below the typical minimum for firms like Hindenburg, Muddy Waters, or ShadowFall, which tend to target US, UK, or larger-cap European listed companies with high short liquidity.

The bear case on SATS is entirely fundamental. It is expressed through: (1) cautious sell-side commentary from Nordic brokers; (2) a structural analysis of the CMD NOK 1.1bn target gap; (3) the competitive dynamics from Treningshelse; and (4) macro/FX concern from international investors.

2. SHORT INTEREST DATA

SATS is listed on Oslo Stock Exchange. Norwegian regulatory framework requires disclosure of short positions above 0.5% of outstanding shares. No significant disclosed short positions have been identified.

The stock is thinly covered by international investors, which limits short interest mechanics. Norwegian domestic institutions are the primary shareholder base. The stock's relatively low international liquidity means that short interest tends to be limited to domestic hedge funds and systematic strategies rather than global short-biased funds.

3. BEARISH SELL-SIDE VIEWS

Nordic sell-side coverage on SATS is relatively thin. The key brokers covering the stock (Carnegie, SEB, Pareto, Nordea) are broadly constructive given the operational improvement track record. However, the CMD target has generated scepticism:

The NOK 1.1bn EBITDA before IFRS 16 target was announced at the May 2025 CMD. At that point, trailing EBITDA was approximately NOK 730-750m. The target implied approximately 46-50% EBITDA growth from CMD date to terminal. Management presented the target as achievable through: ARPM increases; membership volume growth; club expansion of 8-12 per year; operating leverage on the fixed cost base; and margin efficiency gains in Sweden.

Bears note: the Q3-25 EBITDA of NOK 192m (+13% YoY) implies annualised EBITDA of ~NOK 770m. The Q4-25 EBITDA of NOK 224m (+28% YoY) is the strongest quarter, helped by seasonality (January renewal effect). Full year FY25 EBITDA of approximately NOK 870m implies the gap to NOK 1.1bn is approximately NOK 230m, or 26%. This is roughly 2-3 years of growth at current trajectory.

The cautious sell-side view: achievable but back-loaded; any macro or competitive headwind delays the CMD target by 1-2 years; the market is likely already pricing mid-cycle normalised earnings.

4. ACCOUNTING AND GOVERNANCE RED FLAGS

IFRS 16 and true leverage. SATS reports net debt/EBITDA before IFRS 16 at 1.1-1.3x. This is the commonly cited leverage ratio and appears moderate. However, under IFRS 16, SATS's 274 leased clubs generate substantial right-of-use assets and lease liabilities. The loan facility covenant requires Net Debt to EBITDA before IFRS 16 not to exceed 3.5x, confirming that the company's own covenants reference the pre-IFRS 16 metric.

The true economic leverage — including capitalised lease obligations — is substantially higher. A 274-club portfolio with average lease of 10-15 years remaining and average annual rent of approximately NOK 4-6m per club implies a lease liability of approximately NOK 12-16bn (using a 7-8% discount rate). This compares to EBITDA of ~NOK 870m pre-IFRS 16. Lease-adjusted gross leverage is approximately 14-18x.

This is not a red flag per se — it is the structural reality of the lease-heavy gym model — but it means that in a severe revenue decline, SATS's fixed obligations (rent + interest) could exceed earnings for an extended period.

Non-GAAP adjustments. SATS distinguishes between EBITDA before IFRS 16 (which includes rent as a cost, reflecting true operating economics) and reported EBITDA (which excludes rent under IFRS 16 capitalisation). The company is transparent about this distinction. There are no material exceptional items or aggressive adjustments beyond this standard treatment.

Governance. SATS is majority-owned by a consortium that includes Norwegian institutional investors. No founder concentration or related-party concerns have been identified. The management team under CEO Sondre Gravir has been consistent and has guided the company through the post-COVID recovery with clear strategic communication.

The recent Sweden ARPM recovery (+10% in Q4-25) and revenue growth (+12%) are positive, but some investors retain residual concern about whether the Swedish recovery is structural or cyclical.

NOK currency commentary: international investors who have tracked SATS note that the NOK weakened against EUR and GBP in 2024-25. For a euro-denominated investor, SATS's operational outperformance has been partially offset by FX headwinds. Bears argue that with oil price risk and a small, illiquid currency, SATS is an inferior risk-adjusted vehicle compared to BFIT or GYM for non-Norwegian investors.

6. COUNTER-ARGUMENTS TO BULL CASE

Bull: SATS is the dominant Nordic operator with no serious challenger. Bear: Treningshelse (operating Sporty brand and others) has grown to 93+ locations in Norway with reported revenues of approximately NOK 300-350m. Fresh Fitness (SATS's budget sub-brand) competes directly with Treningshelse. If Treningshelse continues to grow at 10-15% per year, it could meaningfully dent Fresh Fitness's membership base in Norway over a 3-5 year horizon. SATS's combined Norwegian club count (120 clubs) is broadly matched by the sum of Fresh Fitness's competitors plus Treningshelse. The Nordic market is not as consolidated as often presented.

Bull: ARPM can grow to NOK 700+/month. Bear: NOK 630/month is already at the upper end of what Norwegian consumers consider "gym membership." Norway has high household debt levels and a consumer base sensitive to interest rate increases (Norwegian mortgage market is predominantly variable rate). A 2.5% Norwegian central bank rate creates mortgage payment pressure that flows directly into consumer discretionary budgets. Price increases above wage growth (3-4% in Norway) will face resistance. Management already flagged that "higher price increases for 2026 may result in lower net member growth" — an implicit acknowledgment of the ARPM ceiling tension.

Bull: Sweden turnaround is real and sustainable. Bear: SATS's Sweden market has shown improvement (ARPM +10% Q4-25, revenue +12%), but this is recovery from a structurally weak baseline. Sweden's gym market is more competitive than Norway: Actic, SATS, McFIT, and independent operators all compete. The Swedish consumer is more price-sensitive than the Norwegian equivalent. Swedish improvements may slow once the easy pricing gains are captured and new entrants (including BFIT via Clever Fit eventually) target premium Swedish locations.

Bull: Corporate wellness B2B provides revenue stability. Bear: SATS does not break out corporate wellness as a separate revenue line, making it difficult to size. Industry estimates suggest B2B corporate wellness is approximately 10-15% of SATS's revenue. This is meaningful but not dominant. If Norwegian corporate wellness demand softens (oil price decline, tech sector retrenchment), the B2B segment would decline concurrently with direct consumer weakness — providing less counter-cyclical protection than bulls suggest.

Bull: 8-12 new clubs per year supports structural growth. Bear: 8-12 clubs on a 274-club base is 3-4% growth. At NOK 5,509m revenue, this adds approximately NOK 150-200m revenue per year from new clubs (once mature), growing at 3% annually. This is insufficient to bridge the gap to NOK 1.1bn EBITDA on club growth alone. The CMD target depends primarily on ARPM growth and operating leverage, not volume expansion. Both of these levers are more volatile than club count growth.

7. HISTORICAL PATTERN

SATS has a complex corporate history. The company was formed from the merger of SATS and ELIXIA in 2013. It went through a private equity ownership phase and re-listed on Oslo Stock Exchange in 2019. The IPO was followed immediately by COVID-19, which forced club closures, government support requirements, and a drawn-out revenue recovery.

In the 2019-2022 period, SATS wrote down Swedish club assets, closed underperforming sites, and restructured its Swedish market presence. These write-downs created negative perceptions among investors who had bought at IPO. The subsequent operational recovery (FY23-FY25) has been strong and consistent but has not fully eliminated the institutional memory of Swedish fragility.

The company made its first dividend payment as a listed company in 2025, signalling a new phase of financial maturity. The CMD announced in May 2025 was the first comprehensive articulation of medium-term targets.

8. QUALITY OF BEAR EVIDENCE

High-quality concern: CMD execution gap (26% to NOK 1.1bn) is a real, quantifiable risk. Market pricing a portion of this as a given creates downside if delivery slips by 12-24 months.

High-quality concern: ARPM ceiling in Norway at NOK 630+. Management's own acknowledgment that higher prices in 2026 may reduce net member adds is the most honest self-assessment of this risk available.

Medium-quality concern: Treningshelse competitive threat. Treningshelse's revenue base (~NOK 300-350m vs SATS's NOK 5,509m) means it is a meaningful regional competitor but not yet an existential threat. However, if Treningshelse or a similar operator secures private equity backing and accelerates growth, the threat level rises.

Medium-quality concern: Sweden historical fragility. Q4-25 data shows recovery is real. Residual concern is legitimate but should diminish with further quarters of evidence.

High-quality concern: NOK currency risk. For non-Norwegian investors, this is a real structural drag that cannot be diversified away. SATS is a pure NOK earnings stock with no natural hedge for euro or sterling investors.

Low-quality concern: Over-leverage. The 1.1-1.3x headline leverage (ex IFRS 16) is genuinely low. Even on a lease-adjusted basis, SATS is not at risk of covenant breach under any realistic base-case scenario.

9. RISK ASSESSMENT

Downside scenario A: CMD target slip — NOK 1.1bn EBITDA reached in FY29 instead of FY27. Causes: ARPM price increases trigger member churn in 2026; net member growth below expectations; Sweden recovery stalls; new club openings at the lower end of 8-12/year target. The 26% EBITDA gap takes 3 years rather than 2. Impact: market re-rates from CMD-target pricing to a more conservative DCF. Price impact: 20-30% downside.

Downside scenario B: Norwegian consumer recession. Norway's oil-dependent economy faces an oil price shock (Iran conflict spreading, demand destruction). Norwegian interest rate cuts fail to materialise; household debt burden weighs on consumer spending. SATS memberships at NOK 630/month are cancelled by 5-8% of the member base. Revenue declines 4-6%; EBITDA declines 8-12% due to fixed lease costs. CMD target pushed to FY30+. Price impact: 30-40% downside.

Downside scenario C: Treningshelse private equity backing + aggressive expansion. A well-capitalised sponsor backs Treningshelse, accelerating to 200+ Norwegian clubs within 3 years at NOK 300-400/month (versus SATS Fresh Fitness at ~NOK 350-400/month). Fresh Fitness loses 30-50% of its 42-club membership base. Norway revenue growth slows. CMD target becomes unachievable without Sweden/Denmark overdelivery. Price impact: 25-35% downside.

10. SCEPTICAL REBUTTAL

The momentum in SATS is genuine and measurable. Six consecutive quarters of record results (to Q4-25). Q4-25 EBITDA +28% YoY. First dividend paid as a listed company. Leverage at 1.1-1.3x. The operational discipline is clear.

SATS occupies a structurally advantaged position in the highest-penetration fitness markets in Europe. Norwegian and Swedish consumers have demonstrated willingness to pay for fitness over multi-decade periods. The SATS brand has 25+ years of market presence. Brand stickiness is higher than in continental Europe.

Treningshelse at NOK 300-350m revenue is approximately 6% of SATS's scale. Even with material growth, it is a regional nuisance, not a structural threat to SATS's 274-club, 755k-member dominant position. The corporate wellness B2B channel provides an institutional floor under membership volumes that Treningshelse cannot replicate.

Norway's NOK 630/month ARPM is high in absolute terms but reflects genuine value delivery: large facilities, full group exercise timetable, staffed service, corporate wellness integration. SATS is not purely budget — it delivers more value per member per month than BFIT or GYM. Member LTV is correspondingly higher.
