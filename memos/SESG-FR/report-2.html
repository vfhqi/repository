§7 — Value Chain: Competition

J-front verdict: Competitive intensity has materially increased in L3Y with Starlink's commercial scaling; SES is not yet in existential competitive threat but its pricing power and market share trajectory in commercial connectivity are under genuine structural pressure [#J]

  • Industry structure: Satellite connectivity was historically a near-oligopoly (SES, Intelsat, Inmarsat, Viasat, SES O3b) with high barriers to entry (launch cost, spectrum rights, orbital slots); Starlink has fundamentally disrupted this structure [#A]

  • New competitor — Starlink: SpaceX Starlink has >5,000 satellites operational, falling ASP (from ~$500/month to ~$120-150/month for consumer; enterprise pricing still premium but declining), and is actively targeting maritime, aviation, and government segments [#I]

  • Starlink Maritime launched commercially 2022; Starlink Aviation 2023; Starlink Gov (higher security tier) pursuing US DoD contracts

  • Disruption pace: faster than most incumbents including SES anticipated in 2021-22

  • New competitor — Amazon Kuiper: Amazon has FCC licence for 3,236-satellite LEO constellation; commercial launch expected 2025-26; represents a second well-capitalised LEO competitor [#I]

  • Kuiper differentiator: deep integration with AWS infrastructure; could appeal to enterprise customers running cloud workloads

  • Not yet operational — lead time before competitive impact meaningful gives SES a window

  • Eutelsat/Oneweb: Eutelsat acquired Oneweb (LEO constellation, ~600 satellites) and rebranded as a LEO+GEO combined operator — a direct strategic response to Starlink [#I]

  • Eutelsat/Oneweb is a direct peer competitor; their LEO trajectory matters to SES

  • Eutelsat's financial distress (high debt post-Oneweb acquisition) could benefit SES if Eutelsat proves unable to execute

  • Inmarsat: Acquired by Viasat (completed 2023); combined Viasat/Inmarsat entity is a significant competitor in aviation and maritime — with GEO+LEO hybrid ambitions [#I]

  • BtE: Traditional barriers to entry (spectrum rights, orbital slots, capex) remain meaningful; no new GEO/MEO entrant is feasible; LEO is where BtE has structurally lowered [#A]

  • SES's MEO orbital slots and O3b spectrum rights are legitimate barriers in the MEO layer

  • Market structure: Moving from stable oligopoly toward a three-tier structure: LEO (Starlink, Kuiper, Eutelsat/Oneweb) / MEO (SES mPOWER) / GEO (SES, Intelsat, Inmarsat/Viasat) — with MEO occupying a technically differentiated but commercially contested middle layer [#J]

  • Competitor health: Eutelsat is financially stressed (debt-heavy post-Oneweb); Intelsat pre-acquisition was emerging from Chapter 11; Viasat/Inmarsat is integration-heavy — paradoxically, SES's competitors are mostly financially constrained or distracted, which is a relative positive [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish competitive read: Starlink's cost curve continues declining faster than SES's mPOWER pricing can follow; Starlink Gov achieves DoD accreditation at higher security tier, directly attacking SES's most profitable segment; the mechanism is that government buyers — who have historically paid premium for MEO reliability — begin accepting LEO with supplemental redundancy, eliminating SES's pricing justification; Kuiper compounds pressure by 2027

§8 — Value Chain: Disruption

J-front verdict: Disruption is the most underweighted risk in SES's investor narrative — the LEO cost curve is deflationary and structural, and mPOWER's technical advantages may have a shorter shelf life than the 10-15 year asset life of the satellites implies [#J]

  • Technology disruption — LEO: Starlink's LEO constellation is a genuine technology disruption, not a mere competitive threat [#J]
  • Cost structure: SpaceX's reusable launch capability means Starlink's marginal cost of adding capacity is a fraction of SES's satellite procurement cost

  • The adoption curve: Starlink went from 0 to >3 million subscribers in ~3 years — adoption is faster than satellite incumbents' historical product cycles

  • Disruption pace: Faster than SES management's public narrative suggests; SES has consistently described Starlink as "complementary" and serving "different segments" — this framing is increasingly untenable as Starlink moves up-market [#J]

  • Maritime evidence: Starlink Maritime is winning commercial shipping contracts that would previously have been GEO/MEO

  • Incumbent response: Al-Saleh's response is "MEO differentiation + software layer" — positioning mPOWER as the premium, low-latency, high-SLA option vs LEO's "good enough" proposition [#A]

  • This is a defensible positioning for the next 3-5 years in government and premium enterprise

  • It is not a long-term structural answer if LEO latency continues improving with more constellation density

  • Disruption cost: SES does NOT have a credible LEO response — mPOWER is MEO; the company lacks the capital and launch cadence to build a competitive LEO constellation [#J]

  • Intelsat acquisition adds GEO scale, not LEO capability

  • This is a structural gap: SES is a MEO/GEO operator in a world moving toward LEO

  • New entrant — innovative: Eutelsat/Oneweb, Telesat Lightspeed (Canadian MEO/LEO hybrid), and AST SpaceMobile (direct-to-device LEO) represent additional innovative entrants that could further commoditise connectivity [#I]

  • Adoption curve: Government customers lag commercial in technology adoption — this is SES's temporal moat; DoD procurement cycles are 3-7 years, giving mPOWER a protected window [#A]

  • Commercial maritime and aviation adoption of Starlink is already faster than government

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish disruption read: the "government as protected moat" thesis is correct for 2025-28 but structurally time-limited; the mechanism is that as Starlink achieves DoD and NATO accreditation (actively pursuing), the procurement cycle advantage compresses; by 2028-30 the pricing differential between LEO and MEO for government could force MEO premium compression; SES's capex cycle for mPOWER replacements in the early 2030s will then face a radically different competitive price environment

§9 — Value Chain: Political and Regulatory

J-front verdict: Regulatory environment is mixed — C-Band windfall was a one-time positive now exhausted; US government customer relationships are a genuine structural tailwind; spectrum regulatory risk and satellite orbital slot governance are manageable but not negligible [#J]

  • Regulatory shift — C-Band: US C-Band spectrum repack (FCC-mandated, completed by 2024) delivered SES ~$3.7bn in proceeds — the defining regulatory event of the last 5 years; it is now over [#I]

  • This regulatory windfall is not a change force for the next 3 years; its absence is the forward regulatory context

  • Political actor — US government: US DoD and civilian agencies are increasing satellite bandwidth spending; JADC2 (Joint All-Domain Command and Control) and proliferated military space are structural demand tailwinds for secure MEO connectivity [#I]

  • SES O3b/mPOWER has FedRAMP-equivalent accreditation for government use; Starlink Gov is pursuing similar

  • Geography — Luxembourg: SES is Luxembourg-domiciled; Luxembourg government is a substantial shareholder; this creates a quasi-sovereign entity dynamic that could slow aggressive strategic pivots but provides implicit state backing [#I]

  • Trade policy: US-EU trade tensions could affect SES's US government contract eligibility if foreign ownership thresholds tighten; Luxembourg domicile has not historically been an issue but regulatory nationalism in satellite (a dual-use technology) is increasing [#A]

  • Regulatory shift — orbital governance: ITU spectrum and orbital slot filing regime is becoming more contested as LEO constellations multiply; SES's MEO orbital slots and frequency assignments are valuable and protected but require ongoing regulatory management [#I]

  • Environmental regulation: Satellite operators face increasing scrutiny on orbital debris; mPOWER deorbit plans and debris mitigation are regulatory compliance issues rather than strategic threats [#I]

  • Compliance cost: No specific material compliance cost increases identified in public record [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish regulatory read: US government customer relationships are at risk if the incoming administration (post-2024 US election) shifts satellite procurement toward domestic operators (SpaceX has US-only ownership advantage over Luxembourg-domiciled SES); the mechanism is that Congressional or DoD procurement rules prioritise US-incorporated primes, disadvantaging SES on new contract awards; the Intelsat acquisition — bringing US-incorporated Intelsat into the SES group — could partially mitigate this risk but adds integration complexity

  • This concentration contributed to mPOWER delay risk; Boeing's satellite manufacturing division has been under financial and operational pressure

  • Supplier health: Boeing's satellite and space division has faced restructuring; its financial health is relevant to SES's future fleet procurement [#I]

  • For next-generation fleet decisions, SES may need to diversify supplier base

  • Launch: Launch market has improved structurally — SpaceX Falcon 9 and Falcon Heavy provide SES competitive launch options; Arianespace (SES's historical primary launch provider) has faced capacity constraints [#I]

  • SES has used SpaceX launches — a pragmatic response to Arianespace delays; this is a positive supply-side development

  • Vertical integration: SES does not manufacture satellites; it is entirely dependent on external prime contractors [#I]

  • Supply risk: Ground segment and network operations technology — SES's software/platform ambition requires partnerships with terrestrial technology providers; these relationships are less visible in public disclosures [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish supply-side read: Boeing's continued satellite manufacturing problems could delay any mPOWER follow-on procurement or additional spacecraft; the mechanism is that SES's next fleet generation faces both a more competitive market and a more constrained reliable supplier base; if Boeing exits satellite manufacturing (a non-zero risk given its division's restructuring), SES loses its primary technical relationship and faces a supplier search at a critical fleet-planning moment
