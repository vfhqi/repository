EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (BLUF)

Airtel Africa operates a structurally advantaged dual-engine telecommunications and mobile money platform across fourteen sub-Saharan African countries, demonstrating robust operator strength through superior margin expansion and strong free cash flow conversion versus regional peers, though severe foreign exchange volatility persistently obscures underlying organic outperformance. The business successfully captures high-growth data and fintech demand while offsetting voice commoditisation, positioning itself as the primary challenger to market leaders MTN and Vodacom across its operational footprint. The company commands a hyper-fragmented, prepaid-dominant customer base with proven pricing pass-through capabilities and deepening ecosystem lock-in via mobile money integration. Sub-Saharan telecom and fintech markets operate as rational oligopolies with high barriers to entry, structurally supporting robust margin pools despite persistent currency devaluation cycles. Management has demonstrated exceptional through-cycle execution, maintaining sector-leading profitability while systematically de-risking the balance sheet and returning capital to shareholders through progressive dividends and active buyback programs. 2 Airtel Africa operates a dual-engine platform combining traditional mobile telecommunications with a rapidly scaling digital mobile money ecosystem, providing voice, data, and enterprise connectivity to 184 million active subscribers while the fintech arm delivers banking, payment, and micro-loan services to 54.1 million previously unbanked users. The group organises telecommunications operations into three highly distinct regional reporting segments based on market maturity and currency dynamics, with the Nigeria segment operating exclusively within Africa's most populous market and heavily influencing total group financial performance, East Africa comprising mature operations in Kenya, Malawi, Rwanda, Tanzania, Uganda, and Zambia, and Francophone Africa encompassing diverse markets including Chad, DRC, Gabon, Madagascar, Niger, Republic of the Congo, and Seychelles. Airtel Money Commerce BV functions as a distinct holding company controlling all fintech operations outside Nigeria, with this segregated corporate structure isolating fintech cash flows and facilitating targeted external capital allocation while enabling management to prepare the mobile money segment for a planned standalone public listing. 5 Mobile data revenue surpassed legacy voice in FY26 to become the single largest contributor to group earnings, reaching $2.53 billion and representing a formidable 35.2 percent constant currency growth rate, with this mix shift structurally improving group margins as incremental data traffic requires minimal marginal operating expenditure. Traditional voice connectivity remains a massive but steadily commoditising cash generator, generating $2.32 billion in FY26 but growing at a significantly slower 12.8 percent constant currency rate as structural interconnect rate reductions and shifting consumer habits persistently pressure regional voice average revenue per user. The African telecom and mobile money market serves a rapidly expanding demographic base, with Sub-Saharan Africa accounting for 55% of global mobile money customers and 74% of transaction volumes, supported by the world's largest emerging consumer base with working-age population growing 2.7% annually. The structural migration from feature phones to smartphones accelerates data consumption systemically, with Sub- Saharan smartphone penetration projected to rise from 48% to 64% by 2025, while African telecom markets fundamentally operate as tight oligopolies or duopolies due to massive capital requirements with most operating markets containing only two to four major mobile network operators. Nigeria's massive market is dominated by two primary players controlling over 85% of active GSM subscriptions, with MTN leading at 51.6% market share followed directly by Airtel Africa at 34.3%. Mobile money operations represent the most lucrative stage of the African telecom value chain, with standalone mobile money return on invested capital reaching extraordinary levels estimated around 275% for market leaders.

Airtel Africa commands a highly integrated, dual-engine position spanning high-barrier connectivity and lucrative fintech stages, structurally securing outsized profit pools through a multi-stage platform encompassing both active network connectivity controlling spectrum, switching routing, and direct customer billing relationships across 14 markets, while the fintech arm acts as a proprietary banking layer managing digital wallets, merchant payments, and micro-lending services. Management has structurally divested passive tower infrastructure to independent operators, deliberately reducing upstream capital intensity by relying on long-term lease

agreements with major tower companies, while aggressively pursuing active network sharing agreements with regional peers including partnerships to share fibre networks and tower infrastructure with Vodacom Group in Tanzania and the Democratic Republic of Congo and similar sharing frameworks with MTN Group covering operations in Uganda and Nigeria. The company actively manages an exceptionally dense physical distribution footprint to bypass fragmented third-party retail channels, controlling 49,000 exclusive retail outlets and establishing a profound last-mile physical moat against asset-light digital competitors, with the non-exclusive agent network expanding by 39 percent year-on-year to further consolidate direct consumer access. 24

The massive consumer base of 183.5 million individual subscribers completely eliminates any material top- customer revenue concentration risk, with this extreme fragmentation isolating the group from single-account churn events in a manner similar to regional peer MTN Group which likewise exhibits negligible top-account concentration. Group-level monthly churn remains structurally high but stable at approximately 4.2% to 4.3%, with East Africa exhibiting slightly higher monthly churn at 4.4% reflecting a highly competitive multi-operator environment while Nigeria demonstrates superior retention with monthly churn holding noticeably lower at 2.1%. Bundling core connectivity with Airtel Money creates profound structural lock-in and mitigates prepaid churn, with mobile money active users reaching 54.1 million representing nearly 30% of the total telecom subscriber base, while multi-service users deliver significantly higher ARPU and exhibit much lower churn compared to single-service customers. Airtel Africa demonstrated exceptional pricing power by successfully passing through massive inflation-driven tariff hikes without breaking demand, with a 50% Nigerian tariff adjustment substantially absorbed by consumers with highly manageable volume elasticity as Nigerian data usage actually accelerated to 11.0 GB per month despite these steep unit price increases.

Airtel Africa relies heavily on a duopoly of passive infrastructure providers primarily American Tower Corporation and IHS Towers, having structurally divested passive tower assets to these independent TowerCos across the continent to reduce capital intensity, with tower lease agreements recently renewed for extended durations of 10 to 12 years that secure long-term site availability but legally bind Airtel to prolonged, inflexible operational expenditure commitments. TowerCos exert immense pricing leverage through inflation-linked and frequently USD-pegged lease escalators built into long-term infrastructure contracts, with recent contract renewals triggering an immediate $1.2 billion increase in capitalised lease liabilities across the group and this massive lease liability recognition mechanically driving group reported leverage up by 0.6x to 2.3x at the time. Reliable network operations fundamentally depend on massive diesel consumption due to chronically unreliable regional electricity grids, with energy expenses representing the single largest operational cost lever consuming an estimated 30% to 40% of total network operating costs as Nigerian telecommunications operators collectively consume over 480 million litres of diesel annually. Airtel bypasses fragmented third-party retail by actively controlling a staggering proprietary distribution network of 49,000 exclusive retail outlets, while the broader non-exclusive distribution channel relies on an immensely fragmented base of over 2.4 million individual Airtel Money agents with the group adding over 660,000 new agents and 130,000 customer-activating outlets in the last twelve months alone driving a 39% increase in the non-exclusive agent base.

Airtel operates in consolidated oligopolies, using network-sharing and satellite partnerships to counter TowerCo leverage and defend its robust challenger position against incumbents MTN and Vodacom who act as primary pan-African market leaders boasting superior aggregate scale and historical network investment advantages, while Airtel Africa occupies the primary challenger position in most markets competing aggressively on pricing, coverage expansion, and differentiated mobile money use cases by relying on shared tower networks to maintain lower-cost infrastructure partially offsetting scale advantages enjoyed by larger peers. Airtel maintains a solid second place in Nigeria with 33.9% subscriber share capitalising on MTN's recent market share losses linked to SIM barring, with MTN Nigeria recently losing 140 basis points of subscriber market share over the first quarter of 2024 following a reduction in capital expenditure. Operators generally exhibit rational pricing discipline focusing on value repair rather than destructive volume acquisition, with Airtel Africa successfully passing through significant tariff adjustments in Nigeria driving constant currency revenue growth without triggering severe customer churn, while rival operators aggressively pursue active network sharing agreements to eliminate duplicate capital investments with Airtel and Vodacom signing strategic infrastructure sharing agreements covering Tanzania, the Democratic Republic of Congo, and Mozambique following similar frameworks with MTN Group covering Uganda and Nigeria. 38 Management demonstrates exceptional through-cycle execution maintaining sector-leading margins and deleveraging the balance sheet despite extreme regional currency volatility, targeting massive structural unbanked and unconnected demographics through a six-pillar strategy prioritising network excellence and Airtel Money acceleration, with the dual-engine approach directly aligning with the continent's low smartphone and banking penetration ensuring structural demand capture. The operator delivered a 24.0 percent constant currency revenue expansion in FY26 easily outpacing regional peer Vodacom's historical top-line compounding, while management successfully expanded consolidated EBITDA margins to a record 50.3 percent in the final quarter of FY26 as the internal efficiency programme systematically eliminates non-growth expenditure creating positive operating leverage despite severe regional inflation. The tight integration of daily telecommunications utility with Airtel Money banking services creates formidable switching barriers as multi-service users exhibit materially higher average revenue per user and significantly lower churn profiles than single-service subscribers, while the company operates a vast proprietary network encompassing 49,000 exclusive retail outlets and over 2.4 million mobile money agents with this physical last-mile density virtually impossible for digital-only fintech challengers to replicate across fourteen fragmented African jurisdictions. The operator delivered 24.0 percent constant currency revenue growth in FY26 materially exceeding Vodacom's historical mid-single-digit equivalent trajectory, with basic earnings per share before exceptional items surging by 127.7 percent to 18.6 cents fuelled by margin expansion and foreign exchange gains while the business achieved a 23.1 percent return on capital employed in FY26 rebounding sharply from 19.6 percent in the prior year.

The structural evolution of African telecommunications heavily favours Airtel Africa's dual-engine model supported by powerful demographic and digital tailwinds, as replicating a pan-African core network infrastructure demands billions of dollars and nearly a decade to deploy with operators facing capital intensity ratios structurally hovering between 13% and 17% of revenue just to maintain network parity, effectively eliminating the threat of new terrestrial mobile network entrants. Low-earth orbit satellite constellations like Starlink represent a complex, evolving technological disruption vector, with Airtel Africa initially partnering with SpaceX to utilise Starlink's Direct-to-Cell capabilities to address remote rural coverage gaps, though over a five to ten year horizon direct-to-

device satellite connectivity could evolve into a substitute that bypasses traditional terrestrial tower networks entirely. Sub-Saharan Africa features the world's largest emerging consumer base driving immense structural volume expansion, with the region's unique mobile subscriber penetration forecast to grow to 53% by 2030 while data traffic across the continent currently surges at an incredible 50% compound annual growth rate. The transition from feature phones to smartphones fundamentally reshapes industry economics, with regional smartphone penetration projected to surge from 54% in 2024 to 81% by 2030 mechanically driving higher data consumption as smartphone users consume roughly 10.9 gigabytes monthly.

The principal risk confronting Airtel Africa's investment case centres on whether sustained hyper-inflation and currency devaluation cycles will eventually break the demonstrated consumer pricing elasticity , forcing prepaid customers to fundamentally curtail daily data and fintech usage despite the essential utility nature of these services. While headline USD revenue compounding persistently fails to materialize when local currency devaluations continuously outpace aggressive organic growth, potentially rendering the high margins illusory in hard currency terms, the operational strength of the underlying business model remains validated by sector- leading constant currency performance. Management explicitly warned that ongoing geopolitical events driving energy cost inflation will continue to pressure near-term consolidated EBITDA margins, with Deutsche Bank highlighting group guidance that EBITDA margins could compress by 250-300 basis points year-on-year if fuel prices remain elevated. Secondary structural risks include the credibility of independent TowerCos extracting all future profitability via aggressive lease escalators , particularly if renewable energy transitions fail to materialize and diesel inflation persists, alongside potential regulatory intervention forcibly capping mobile money take-rates before the platform achieves standalone public valuation or mandating structural unbundling of the highly profitable fintech segment from core connectivity networks.
