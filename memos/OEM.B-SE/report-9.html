  • Operating leverage: OEM's distribution model carries semi-fixed SG&A (branch network, headcount, logistics) that creates leverage in upcycles and deleverage in downcycles — approximately 1.5-2x revenue leverage on operating income based on observed swings [#A]

§4 — Seasonal Patterns

  • Seasonality verdict: OEM shows modest and consistent seasonality — H2 typically stronger than H1 due to industrial customer budget cycles and year-end purchasing; not a major analytical risk [#J]

  • H1 vs H2 pattern: Based on available H1 vs full-year data, H2 consistently represents approximately 52-55% of full-year revenues and a slightly higher share of EBIT (55-58%) due to operating leverage on the higher H2 volume base [#A]

  • Sub: H2 EBIT share above 55% suggests SG&A allocation is weighted toward fixed costs that remain in H1, creating slight H2 profitability advantage
  • Strongest period: H2 is consistently the stronger half — driven by industrial customers completing capital and maintenance spending before year-end; this is a standard pattern for Northern European MRO distributors [#A]
  • Weakest period: H1, and specifically the Jan-Feb period, is weakest due to Nordic winter maintenance shutdowns and customer budget-setting lag; consistent with Nordic seasonality across the sector [#A]
  • Seasonality direction: Seasonality pattern is stable and not changing materially — H2/H1 split has been consistent across FY2022, FY2023, and FY2024 [#I]
  • Cause — buying pattern: Primary driver is industrial MRO budget cycle — procurement managers accelerate MRO spending in Q4 to exhaust annual budgets; OEM benefits reliably from this pattern [#A]
  • Cause — production cycles: Nordic paper/pulp, food processing, and manufacturing customers perform planned maintenance shutdowns in summer (July) and early Q4 — OEM supplies parts for these maintenance events [#I]
  • M&A timing: Small bolt-on acquisitions tend to be completed H1 (reflecting Q1/Q2 deal closings from prior-year negotiations); these add modest H2 contribution that can slightly flatter the H2/H1 comparison [#I]
  • FY2024 seasonality distortion: The destocking cycle in FY2024 was more pronounced in H1 (customers pausing orders during budget uncertainty), making H1 2024 weaker than the structural seasonal pattern would suggest; H2 2024 showed partial recovery that was not entirely seasonal but included genuine demand improvement [#J]
  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on seasonality is that H2 budget-flush spending by industrial customers is a pattern that weakens if industrial capex budgets are cut; in a prolonged Nordic industrial downturn (e.g., forestry/paper capex cuts), the H2 seasonal boost could structurally diminish rather than recover [#J]
