2. KEY FINDINGS - Dominant finding: Diploma is a GBP-reporting company that earns ~60-65% of revenue in non-GBP currencies (predominantly USD and CAD), creating a structurally meaningful translation exposure that has swung reported EPS by an estimated ±3-5% in individual years over L3Y — not a trivial rounding error for an investor modelling 12% EPS CAGR

  • USD is the dominant exposure vector: North America contributes ~55-60% of group revenue and the vast majority is USD/CAD-denominated; GBP/USD is therefore the single highest-leverage rate for modelling purposes, with a 10% GBP strengthening against USD reducing reported revenue by approximately £90-110m at FY2024 scale and EBITDA by approximately £25-35m (translation, pre-tax)

  • Natural hedging is structurally partial but meaningful: North American business segments (primarily Seals, Controls, Interconnect) source a significant portion of COGS and operating costs locally in USD/CAD, reducing transaction exposure vs a pure export model; however the GBP-denominated central cost base (PLC overhead, UK-booked acquisition costs, GBP dividend obligations) creates a persistent structural mismatch

  • Transaction exposure is modest relative to translation: Diploma is predominantly a value-added distributor operating through locally-incorporated subsidiaries; each subsidiary transacts in local currency and consolidates upward — the group-level transaction exposure (genuine cash-flow currency mismatch on inter-company flows and UK-booked costs) is smaller than headline translation risk

  • Hedging policy is minimal and translation-agnostic: Diploma's stated policy does not hedge translation exposure (standard for most European industrials); forward contracts are used selectively for transaction exposure on specific known inter-company and procurement flows; disclosed hedge ratios and durations are not granularly disclosed in recent filings, creating transparency opacity

  • FX has been a modest tailwind for L3Y reported numbers: GBP weakness vs USD across FY2022-FY2023 created translation tailwinds; FY2024 saw partial reversal; consensus N12-24M outlook (GBP recovering modestly vs USD) implies translation headwind risk at the margin for FY2025-FY2026 reported numbers

  • Sensitivity calibration: A sustained 10% GBP appreciation vs USD (the single biggest risk) would reduce group EBITDA by approximately £25-35m, or ~5-7% of consensus FY2025 EBITDA — material enough to reduce reported EPS by ~7-9p per share (est.) against FY2024 underlying EPS of ~127p, a ~6-7% EPS headwind from a single currency move

  • ⚡ Non-obvious risk — acquisition-integration FX basis: Diploma's M&A programme creates a lagged FX basis risk — targets acquired at high-GBP/low-USD prices carry a structurally different cost basis than if acquired at today's rates; the goodwill/intangibles recorded in GBP expand/contract with translation, and the amortisation charge denominated in GBP varies with the FX rate at the time of acquisition, creating non-obvious EPS volatility from the amortisation line

  • Thesis FX verdict: FX is a ~medium-weight risk factor for the thesis — not a binary threat, but capable of shaving 5-8% off reported EPS in a 10% sustained GBP-strengthening scenario; the organic EPS growth must be stressed by ~5-8% in the FX bear case; thesis remains viable but FX-dependent growth attribution requires monitoring

§1 — Revenue by Currency (Geographic Breakdown + Mix Trends)

J-front verdict: Diploma's revenue is predominantly non-GBP (~60-65% USD/CAD/EUR), making it a structurally GBP-translation-exposed company with concentrated USD/North America risk — diversified vs UK peers but USD-concentrated vs global industrials

Reporting currency: GBP sterling

  • USD/North America dominance: Diploma generates approximately 55-60% of group revenue from North America (US + Canada), essentially all billed in USD or CAD; at FY2024 reported revenues of approximately £1,285m, this implies ~£700-770m of USD/CAD-denominated revenue; USD alone is estimated to represent ~45-50% of group revenue, CAD ~8-10%

  • EUR exposure is secondary and declining as share: European operations (primarily UK/Europe Controls and Seals sub-segments) contribute approximately 15-20% of group revenue; a portion of this is GBP (UK end-markets) and a portion EUR (Continental European customers); EUR share of total group revenue is estimated at ~10-14%; the share has been diluted over L3Y as North American M&A (notably Windy City Wire FY2022, Cablecraft FY2023 and numerous bolt-ons) grew the USD base faster than EUR

  • GBP (home currency) is a minority of revenue: UK-origin revenue is approximately 20-25% of the group; the implication is that ~75-80% of revenue is booked in currencies other than the reporting currency — an above-average translation exposure position for a UK-listed industrial

  • Mix trend L3Y — USD/CAD share is rising: North American revenue as a % of group revenue has increased from approximately 47% in FY2021 to approximately 55-58% in FY2024, driven primarily by M&A (Windy City Wire was the step-change in FY2022, adding ~£340m of USD revenue); this structural shift means FX sensitivity to GBP/USD is directionally increasing, not decreasing, as the strategy plays out

  • Constant-currency vs FX-driven growth decomposition (L3Y):

  • FY2022: reported revenue growth ~50%+ (Windy City Wire consolidation); FX contributed an estimated +5-6% tailwind (GBP/USD moved from ~1.37 average to ~1.24 average over the fiscal year, a ~10% GBP weakening)
  • FY2023: reported revenue growth ~28%; underlying growth ~20%+; FX tailwind estimated +3-4% (GBP/USD averaged ~1.23 in FY2023 vs ~1.37 pre-Windy City acquisition)

  • FY2024: reported revenue growth ~8%; underlying growth ~6-7%; FX headwind estimated -1 to -2% (partial GBP recovery); net FX effect more muted as GBP/USD oscillated in a tighter band (~1.25-1.28)

  • Organic growth ex-FX: across L3Y, organic growth ex-FX has been meaningfully below reported growth — the FX tailwind from GBP weakness inflated reported performance; investors modelling forward from L3Y reported numbers risk embedding a FX-flattery base in the organic growth estimate

  • FX-driven growth (L3Y cumulative): FX is estimated to have contributed approximately +6-9% cumulative to reported revenue growth over FY2022-FY2024 combined, weighted by the step-change GBP/USD depreciation following Brexit/energy shock cycles; this is a material FX-flattery in the reported track record

§2 — Cost by Currency (Natural Hedges + Mismatches)

J-front verdict: Cost base is broadly matched to revenue geography at the operating subsidiary level — North American COGS and opex are largely USD/CAD, creating a meaningful but incomplete natural hedge; GBP-denominated central costs and acquisition amortisation create residual structural mismatch

  • COGS currency mix — primarily local: Diploma's value-added distribution model means COGS (predominantly inventory/product purchases) is sourced locally within each geography; North American Seals, Controls and Interconnect divisions purchase from local and international suppliers but transact in USD; this provides a natural hedge at the gross margin line

  • Estimated USD/CAD COGS: ~50-55% of total group COGS (consistent with North American revenue share)

  • Estimated GBP COGS: ~20-25% (UK subsidiaries)

  • Estimated EUR COGS: ~10-15% (European subsidiaries)

  • Opex currency mix — partially mismatched: Operating expenditure includes:

  • Local subsidiary opex (wages, rent, local overheads) — currency-matched to revenue geography (~natural hedge)
  • Central/PLC-level costs (Board, investor relations, UK-based senior management, auditor fees, listed-company costs) — predominantly GBP; estimated ~£30-50m per annum; this is a GBP cost against a ~75-80% non-GBP revenue base and is therefore a structural mismatch
  • Group IT/ERP infrastructure costs — increasingly USD (cloud/SaaS vendor contracts often USD-denominated); small but growing

  • Acquisition-related earnout payments and deferred consideration — often denominated in the local currency of the acquired entity (USD for US acquisitions) and therefore partially hedged on a natural basis

  • Capex currency mix: Diploma is asset-light; capex is low (typically ~1-2% of revenue); capex currency broadly follows geographic footprint — USD/CAD-weighted given North American scale; not a material FX risk line in isolation

  • Goodwill and intangibles amortisation — the hidden FX mismatch: Acquired intangibles (customer relationships, trade names) are initially recognised in GBP at the prevailing exchange rate; amortisation is then booked in GBP at the historical acquisition-rate GBP/equivalent; this creates a non-economic FX effect in the P&L amortisation line — when the USD strengthens, new acquisitions bring in higher GBP-equivalent intangible values, and the amortisation charge is subsequently higher (in GBP terms) than if the same business had been acquired at a stronger USD; conversely, GBP strengthening post-acquisition reduces the GBP-equivalent carrying value at retranslation; this is an ⚡ outlier risk — flagged in aggregate block

  • Natural hedge ratio — estimation:

  • At North American segment level: estimated natural hedge ~75-85% (USD/CAD revenue vs USD/CAD cost)
  • Residual transaction exposure at group level: ~15-25% of non-GBP revenue has a GBP cost counterpart (central costs, UK-booked acquisition charges)

  • This is a reasonably good natural hedge ratio for a UK-listed industrial with North American operations — better than a UK exporter selling in USD but sourcing in GBP

  • Margin FX sensitivity — differential: Because COGS is more currency-matched than opex, FX movements affect gross margin less than they affect EBIT margin; a 10% GBP appreciation vs USD:

  • Reduces USD revenue in GBP by ~10% (translation)
  • Reduces USD COGS in GBP by ~10% (translation) — gross profit impact is smaller in % terms than revenue impact
  • Does NOT reduce GBP central costs — EBIT margin compresses because the fixed GBP cost base is diluted by a smaller translated-GBP revenue base

§3 — Translation Exposure (Reported Earnings Impact L3Y)

J-front verdict: Translation has contributed meaningfully to reported results L3Y — GBP weakness in FY2022-FY2023 provided a tailwind that has partially reversed in FY2024; translation risk is the dominant FX channel for Diploma, not transaction

  • Reported FX impact L1Y (FY2024):
  • Diploma AR2024 reports a modest FX headwind on revenue of approximately £(15-25)m vs prior year (management reference to "adverse currency movements" in the strategic report)
  • Average GBP/USD for FY2024 (Oct 2023-Sep 2024): approximately 1.268 vs FY2023 average approximately 1.238 — a ~2.4% GBP appreciation against USD
  • Revenue translation headwind (mechanically): ~2.4% × ~50% USD revenue share × FY2024 revenue ~£1,285m ≈ £(15-19)m revenue headwind

  • EBITDA translation headwind: approximately £(5-10)m (EBITDA margin ~15-16% on USD revenues, adjusted for natural hedge at operating cost level)

  • Reported FX impact L2Y (FY2023):

  • FY2023 was a tailwind year: GBP/USD average ~1.238 vs FY2022 average ~1.243 (small difference in full-year average) but year-on-year comparison benefits from Windy City Wire now fully consolidated; the dominant effect in FY2023 was the comparison-base effect from GBP weakness in the prior period
  • GBP/USD had fallen sharply to a low of ~1.07 in Sep 2022 (Truss/Kwarteng mini-budget), recovering to ~1.23-1.27 by FY2023 end; the fiscal-year average masked intra-year volatility

  • Reported FX contribution to FY2023 revenue growth: approximately +2-3% (positive translation)

  • Reported FX impact L3Y (FY2022):

  • FY2022 was the peak FX tailwind year for Diploma: GBP weakened significantly against USD (average ~1.24 vs ~1.37 prior year, ~10% GBP depreciation); Windy City Wire was consolidated from November 2021 bringing large USD revenue
  • Combined effect: GBP depreciation × enlarged USD base = a c.+5-7% FX tailwind to revenue growth; this is the single largest FX-flattery year in recent history

  • Management did not explicitly isolate FX from Windy City Wire consolidation effect in FY2022 reporting — the commingling makes precise FX attribution difficult; this is a disclosure quality concern

  • Translation magnitude — average year: In a "normal" year (2-3% GBP/USD movement), translation impact on Diploma reported revenue is approximately ±£20-35m; on EBITDA approximately ±£5-12m; on EPS approximately ±3-6p per share (against FY2024 underlying EPS of ~127p — a ~2-5% EPS swing per 2-3% GBP/USD move)

  • Translation magnitude — stressed year (±10% GBP/USD): A 10% GBP appreciation vs USD would reduce reported revenue by approximately £(80-100)m and EBITDA by approximately £(25-35)m — a ~6-8% EBITDA headwind on a base of ~£410-430m FY2025E EBITDA; on a post-tax, EPS basis this translates to approximately £(18-24)m net income headwind, or approximately £(15-20)p per share (against ~127p FY2024 underlying EPS), a ~12-16% EPS hit

  • Reporting currency strength vs thesis: The thesis relies on EPS growing from ~127p (FY2024) toward ~180-200p+ over a 3-5Y horizon; a sustained GBP strengthening vs USD represents the largest single FX risk to reported EPS growth; the organic EPS growth must be approximately 10-12% per annum; a persistent FX headwind of 3-5% per annum would reduce reported EPS CAGR to 5-7% — insufficient for a 25%+ IRR target on a reasonable P/E entry multiple

  • Balance sheet translation: USD/CAD net assets translate into GBP for balance sheet purposes; as GBP strengthens, the GBP-equivalent of net assets shrinks, reducing reported net assets and NAV per share; goodwill and intangibles on acquired US businesses are retranslated at closing rate — a 10% GBP/USD appreciation reduces the balance sheet value of US goodwill by approximately £(90-120)m given ~£900-1,200m of US-denominated goodwill; this flows through Other Comprehensive Income , not P&L, but affects the reported book value and leverage ratios (net debt / EBITDA expressed in GBP)

Sceptical bullet — bearish read on translation: Apparent translation stability over L3Y (modest annual effects, partially offsetting) masks a binary-event risk: the FY2022 GBP mini-budget shock (GBP/USD to ~1.07) created a fortuitous +5-7% translation tailwind; if the next macro shock is inflationary (US exceptionalism sustained, Fed higher-for-longer), GBP/USD could move to 1.10-1.15 and create a REVERSE binary tailwind of similar magnitude, but the direction would be a headwind; the L3Y average translation impact therefore understates the tails, and investors anchoring on "stable FX effects" risk being surprised by a +8-10% translation hit in a single fiscal year

§4 — Transaction Exposure (Economic Impact)

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is modest relative to translation — Diploma's locally-incorporated subsidiary model structurally limits inter-company cash-flow mismatches; residual transaction risk lies in central funding flows and acquisition-related payments

  • Transaction exposure definition for Diploma: Transaction exposure arises where Diploma entities receive/pay in a currency other than their functional currency; for a locally-incorporated subsidiary model, this primarily arises from: (i) UK parent cash repatriation / dividends from subsidiaries (USD dividend to GBP parent), (ii) inter-company funding arrangements (loans from UK parent to US subsidiaries, or vice versa), (iii) specific procurement contracts where a North American subsidiary sources product in a currency other than USD/CAD, (iv) earn-out and deferred consideration payments on acquisitions denominated in local currency but funded from UK parent GBP resources

  • Magnitude vs translation: Transaction exposure is estimated to be significantly smaller than translation exposure for Diploma; the subsidiary model means most cash flows are transacted locally; the primary transaction exposure is at the UK parent level when it receives USD/CAD dividends or inter-company interest and converts to GBP for UK-booked expenses, dividends to shareholders, and GBP interest payments

  • Cash-flow currency mismatch — key points:

  • UK parent pays GBP dividends to shareholders (~£50-60m per annum FY2024); these must ultimately be funded from USD/CAD cash generated by subsidiaries; the conversion of USD/CAD operating cash flow to GBP dividend is a transaction exposure
  • UK parent services GBP-denominated revolving credit facility interest (partially); if the revolving credit facility has USD tranches (likely for cross-currency facility), the interest payments may be USD-denominated, naturally matching USD cash inflows — natural hedge

  • Acquisition earn-outs: Diploma has a regular M&A programme with earn-out structures; these are typically denominated in the local currency of the acquisition (USD for US deals); funded from the revolving credit facility (mixed-currency); creates episodic transaction exposure at deal close

  • Margin economic impact from transaction exposure:

  • Estimated gross transaction exposure (net inter-company cash flows requiring conversion): £50-120m per annum at the UK parent level
  • At a 10% GBP/USD move, this creates a transactional P&L impact of approximately £5-12m — smaller than the translation impact but real and cash-affecting

  • This does NOT appear in the standard geographic revenue analysis and is therefore a hidden cash-flow risk layer

  • Pricing pass-through — Seals segment: In the Seals segment (~35-40% of group revenue), Diploma's distribution agreements with principal suppliers (fluid power component manufacturers) typically include agreed price-list mechanisms; USD pricing changes from US-based principals (e.g. Parker Hannifin equivalents) flow through to customer pricing; in a USD-strengthening scenario, US-sourced components cost more in GBP but Diploma passes the USD price list to customers — this is an effective pass-through mechanism and reduces transaction risk at gross margin level in Seals; however, the pass-through operates with a lag (typically at the next price-list update, 6-12 months)

  • Pricing pass-through — Controls and Interconnect segments: Less systematic pass-through; Controls and Interconnect (particularly Interconnect post-Windy City Wire, which is a US-centric business selling in USD to US customers) are primarily USD-in/USD-out with minimal transaction exposure at the subsidiary level; the FX risk for these segments is purely translational at the group level

  • Debt currency matching: Diploma's revolving credit facility (£650m facility as at FY2024) is a multi-currency facility; the ability to draw in USD means interest costs can be currency-matched to USD cash inflows; this is a sensible treasury practice that partially eliminates transaction exposure on the debt servicing side

Sceptical bullet — bearish read on transaction exposure: Apparent pass-through pricing protection may fail in a real FX shock because customer contracts in the Seals and Controls segments have fixed-price windows; a 15-20% rapid GBP appreciation would impose a significant margin headwind during the 6-12 month lag before price lists can be renegotiated; additionally, in a stress scenario where GBP appreciates sharply on a UK rate shock (e.g. UK fiscal consolidation), Diploma's USD earnings would fall in GBP while its GBP costs (PLC overhead, GBP interest, GBP dividends) remain constant — the pricing pass-through from USD-list suppliers does NOT protect the GBP margin when the direction of FX pressure is GBP-strengthening (making USD-denominated supply chains cheaper in GBP, not more expensive) — this is the wrong direction for pricing pass-through to help; transaction exposure from GBP-strengthening compresses the GBP-equivalent revenue without a corresponding pricing benefit

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Hedging policy is minimal-and-translation-agnostic — Diploma uses selective forward contracts for transaction exposure but explicitly does not hedge translation exposure; disclosed hedge granularity is sparse, preventing rigorous hedge-effectiveness quantification from public filings

  • Hedging mandate — disclosed policy:
  • Diploma's annual report states that the group does not hedge translation exposure (retranslation of foreign-currency-denominated assets/liabilities) — consistent with the majority of European industrials at this scale
  • Transaction exposures (specific identified cash-flow mismatches) are hedged selectively using forward foreign exchange contracts and currency options

  • The board treasury policy mandates managing "material" transaction exposures; "material" is not defined quantitatively in recent filings — a transparency gap

  • Translation hedging (not done):

  • Standard practice for UK-listed industrials with diversified geographic operations
  • Rationale: translation exposure is non-cash (does not affect the underlying economic earnings of subsidiaries); the cost of hedging translation (e.g. rolling currency options on the full notional revenue base) would be prohibitive and would not protect economic value

  • Implication for modelling: Full exposure to translation swing; reported EPS is entirely dependent on GBP/USD average for the fiscal year

  • Transaction hedging (done selectively):

  • Forward contracts are the disclosed primary instrument
  • Currency options disclosed as occasionally used for specific large transactions (e.g. M&A-related currency conversion)
  • Hedge horizon: not explicitly stated in recent disclosures; based on the nature of Diploma's business (distribution, short-cycle), hedging is likely to be shorter-duration (3-12 months); longer-duration hedges (18-24M) are not evidenced in public disclosures

  • Hedge ratio: not quantified as a percentage of exposure in the AR2024 IFRS 7 note — a material disclosure gap relative to peers who often state "we hedge X% of the next 12M net exposure"

  • IFRS 7 / IFRS 9 derivative disclosure — AR2024:

  • AR2024 does disclose the fair value of derivative financial instruments outstanding at year-end; the notional amounts and fair values are disclosed but the exact instruments and expiry profiles are in aggregate form
  • FY2024 derivative assets/liabilities are modest in absolute terms (sub-£5m fair value net position implied from recent disclosures) — consistent with a light hedging programme rather than a comprehensive rolling hedge ladder

  • The modesty of the derivative position confirms that Diploma is NOT running a comprehensive forward-cover programme across its ~£700-770m of non-GBP revenues

  • Hedge effectiveness L3Y:

  • IFRS 9 requires disclosure of hedge effectiveness where hedge-accounting relationships are formally designated; Diploma's disclosures suggest limited formally-designated hedges (the majority of derivative use appears to be economic hedging without formal hedge-accounting designation — meaning fair value movements flow through P&L rather than OCI)
  • The absence of large OCI hedge-effectiveness entries in L3Y results is consistent with either: (i) highly effective designated hedges (small ineffectiveness), or (ii) limited designated hedge relationships — (ii) is more consistent with the disclosed policy of selective transaction hedging

  • Realised gain/loss on FX derivatives L3Y: small and inconsistent year-over-year; not a material driver of P&L — consistent with selective rather than comprehensive hedging

  • Hedge cost / income recognition:

  • Forward contract costs are embedded in the forward premium/discount vs spot; for GBP/USD, the GBP-forward typically trades at a premium to spot (positive interest rate differential historically when UK rates > US rates, or vice versa depending on the period); hedging costs are not explicitly disclosed as a separate P&L line

  • Options premium, where used, would be charged to P&L on inception; not material in L3Y disclosures

  • Disclosure quality assessment:

  • Diploma's FX hedging disclosure does not meet the "boring hedger wins" standard: no stated hedge ratio as % of exposure, no stated hedge horizon in months, no explicit hedge-ladder maturity profile, no explicit instrument breakdown (forwards vs options vs collars vs swaps)
  • This disclosure level is below the gold standard (e.g. a Rolls-Royce or a large FMCG with detailed FX tables in the annual report) but is not unusual for a mid-cap industrial of Diploma's size

  • Counter-check: the modest fair value of derivatives outstanding suggests the disclosed policy of "selective transaction hedging" is genuine — if there were a large rolling programme, the notional derivative positions would be larger

  • Debt-hedge matching as a natural transaction hedge:

  • The multi-currency revolving credit facility (draw in USD/GBP/EUR) functions as a partial natural hedge for transaction exposure on the debt side; when USD operational cash flows service USD-drawn debt, the currency loop closes without FX conversion
  • This is a sensible treasury practice but is not the same as a formal hedge programme; it reduces structural transaction exposure rather than hedging it

Sceptical bullet — bearish read on hedging discipline: The disclosed hedging policy is materially less detailed than what a rigorous investor would want to verify hedge effectiveness — no stated ratio, no stated horizon, no ladder profile; the genuine risk is that Diploma's "selective transaction hedging" means the treasury function hedges only episodically (around specific known events like M&A funding, dividend upstreaming) rather than running a continuous rolling programme; if this is correct, then in a sustained 12-18M GBP-strengthening scenario, Diploma would have zero financial hedging protection on translation (by policy) and minimal financial hedging protection on transaction (by practice), meaning the full economic impact of GBP strength would flow through reported EPS with no mitigation from the hedging programme; the transparency opacity makes it impossible to verify from public disclosures whether this risk is real or overstated
