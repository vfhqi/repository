§3 — Industry Context

Net signal: Metlen operates across three structurally different industry contexts — Greek merchant power (concentrated, politically volatile), European renewables development (fragmented, capital-intensive race), European aluminium (oligopolistic, structural capacity attrition tailwind) — each with distinct competitive dynamics [#J]

Market structure — Greek power: Greek electricity market is relatively concentrated — top 3 generators (Metlen, PPC/DEI, Elpedison) account for ~70-75% of installed thermal capacity [#I]
- Sub-bullet: PPC (Public Power Corporation, semi-state) remains market leader; Metlen is clear #2 in private generation; Elpedison (Shell + Hellenic Petroleum JV) is #3
- Sub-bullet: High concentration is partially offset by Greek government's willingness to intervene on pricing — retroactive windfall levies in 2022-2023 demonstrated that high-concentration does not insulate against political margin compression

Market structure — European renewables development: Highly fragmented — top 10 European developers (Orsted, Iberdrola, EDP, NextEra, Engie, RWE, Terna Energy, BayWa r.e., Lightsource, Statkraft) collectively hold perhaps 20-25% of visible pipeline [#I]
- Sub-bullet: Metlen's global pipeline (~4-5 GW) is significant at national level but sub-scale vs Iberdrola (>60 GW in development) or EDP (>30 GW) — a mid-tier developer
- Sub-bullet: Consolidation pressure is real: smaller developers (<1 GW pipeline) are being absorbed by larger balance sheet players; Metlen has scale to consolidate but also to be acquired

Market structure — European aluminium: ❌ Extreme concentration by default — European primary aluminium smelter base has collapsed from ~3.5m tonnes/year capacity in 2008 to ~1.8m tonnes/year by 2024, with Metlen/AoG among largest remaining [#J]
- Sub-bullet: Norsk Hydro, Speira (Trimet + Rusal assets), AoG (Metlen), and Alro Romania represent the near-entirety of European primary capacity — oligopolistic by attrition
- Sub-bullet: CBAM (EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism, fully operational 2026) creates structural import cost uplift for non-EU aluminium — direct tailwind for AoG's European premium

Competitive intensity — power generation: Greek merchant power market is moderately competitive but politically managed; Greek regulatory authority sets capacity market parameters that support base-load gas plants [#A]
- Sub-bullet: New entrants (renewables) effectively compete by displacing fossil generation in merit order — Metlen faces the unusual dynamic of its own renewables assets competing with its gas generation
- Sub-bullet: No material new gas-fired capacity planned in Greece — natural barriers to new thermal entrants given decarbonisation trajectory

New entrant threat — renewables: High new entrant activity in European renewables (financial sponsors, infrastructure funds, Asian manufacturers with captive EPC); permitting bottlenecks are the primary barrier [#A]
- Sub-bullet: Greece's permitting reform (2023 fast-track for renewables) could paradoxically reduce Metlen's incumbency advantage by easing entry for new developers
- Sub-bullet: International markets (Australia, UK) have even lower barriers to renewable development entry — Metlen enters as a challenger, not an incumbent

Disruption force — energy storage: BESS (battery energy storage) is simultaneously a product Metlen is developing (positive) and a technology eroding the value of gas peaking capacity (negative) [#A]
- Sub-bullet: Gas CCGT plants face declining utilisation as storage + renewables absorb more of the intraday flexibility premium — medium-term structural headwind for Power & Gas EBITDA
- Sub-bullet: Metlen's strategy of building BESS alongside renewables is logical hedge — whether execution in competitive BESS markets delivers the same returns as Greek incumbent generation is unproven

Macro shift — European decarbonisation: EU Green Deal + RePowerEU accelerate renewables deployment, create CBAM tailwinds for European aluminium, and set lignite phase-out timelines that structurally change Metlen's asset base [#A]
- Sub-bullet: Greek government decarbonisation target (carbon-neutral grid by 2028) directly mandates Metlen's lignite exit — policy is execution risk (asset retirement costs) but also strategic clarity
- Sub-bullet: European energy transition spending ~€500bn+/year estimated by EC through 2030 — Metlen's renewables business is embedded in a structural demand super-cycle

Macro shift — aluminium demand: EV + renewable energy infrastructure (grid cables, solar frames, EV battery housings) structurally elevate aluminium demand growth toward 3-5% CAGR through 2030 vs historical 2-3% [#A]
- Sub-bullet: European-origin premium aluminium (low-carbon certified, CBAM-insulated) commands 10-15% premium over imported LME equivalent — growing sustainability procurement trend
- Sub-bullet: Aluminium demand tied to Chinese industrial cycle remains the largest price setter; European producers are price-takers on LME but premium-setters on European premia

Regulatory shift: Greek government's 2022-2023 windfall levy (retroactive on energy generators above a strike price) is the key regulatory risk precedent — demonstrated willingness to claw back supernormal profits [#A]
- Sub-bullet: EU-level energy price cap interventions (2022-2023 temporary) have since lapsed; but political precedent is set and Metlen must price regulatory intervention risk into Greek power strategies
- Sub-bullet: Positive regulatory: Greek RES (renewable energy source) feed-in premium contracts (DAPEEP auctions) provide 20-year revenue visibility on contracted capacity — direct support for Renewables segment valuation

Value chain dynamics — supplier side: Gas procurement is Metlen's largest input cost in Power & Gas — exposed to LNG spot market and pipeline gas pricing (TTF-linked) [#A]
- Sub-bullet: AoG's bauxite supply (for alumina) comes primarily from imported West African sources — commodity supply chain with no proprietary sourcing advantage
- Sub-bullet: EPC costs for renewables construction have inflated 15-25% (2021-2023) due to turbine/panel supply chain tightness; moderating as supply chains normalise but still elevated vs 2020 benchmarks

Sceptical bullet: The bearish cross-cutting read on industry context is that Metlen operates in three sectors none of which is structurally benign — Greek power is politically vulnerable, European renewables development is commoditising (margin compression as more capital chases the same permits), and aluminium is a structurally cyclical commodity with limited pricing power on the LME; the CBAM tailwind and European smelter attrition story is real but may be priced in, and regulatory risk in power markets is under-estimated by current consensus [#J]
