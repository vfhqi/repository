Section 1: Brief company description

The Gym Group is a pioneer and a leading operator in the UK low-cost health and fitness market, running 260 sites nationwide as of December 2025.

The company's operations are entirely focused on the United Kingdom, with no current international footprint.

Map of The Gym Group's locations across the United Kingdom

It serves a massive consumer base of over 923,000 active members, generating approximately 70 million individual gym visits annually.

The core consumer proposition is built around 24/7 access, flexible no-contract memberships, and high-quality equipment at highly affordable price points.

This low-cost positioning translates to an average standard monthly membership price of just £25.64 as of the end of 2025.

The group maintains a structural pricing advantage, sitting significantly cheaper than mid-market operators, which charge an average premium of 60% compared to The Gym Group.

Operations are underpinned by a highly advantaged, labour-light business model that fundamentally disrupts traditional full-service gym economics.

A typical site operates with only two full-time employees, relying heavily on digital customer interactions and entirely stripping out traditional sales teams and receptionists.

The gyms are intentionally "kit-led," meaning they focus entirely on strength and cardio equipment while actively avoiding high-cost, high-maintenance facilities like swimming pools, saunas, and cafes.

Personal training operates on a rental model, where freelance trainers pay rent to the company to access its members, effectively offsetting the fixed wage costs of the full-time site management.

Revenue is generated almost entirely through three main channels: core membership income, personal trainer rental income, and minor ancillary income.

Membership income is by far the largest component, encompassing standard monthly direct debits, premium 'LIVE IT' upgrades, non-refundable joining fees, and longer-term student or corporate passes.

Ancillary revenues, while a small percentage of the total, represent high-margin additions such as vending machine sales, day passes, and advertising income from in-gym media screens.

The business is actively broadening its reach into B2B channels, recently completing a nationwide rollout with Wellhub to access a pool of 1.5 million corporate employees.

The customer base skews remarkably young, with Gen Z representing 44% of the total average membership in 2025.

The overarching corporate purpose is defined as "breaking down barriers to fitness for all," aiming to democratise access to health facilities across socioeconomic lines.

Given its uniform national rollout strategy, the Board of Directors manages and evaluates the business as a single cohesive operating segment.

The company heavily leans into its sustainability credentials as a differentiator, operating as the UK's first carbon-neutral gym chain with science-based net-zero targets.

Jefferies notes that The Gym Group represents a scaled leader in a growing market, utilising a disruptor model that takes outsized market share from traditional mid-market operators.

However, Jefferies also highlights that the company's strict UK-only focus may structurally restrict its valuation multiples when compared to multi-country peers like Basic-Fit.

Section 2: Key components summary

Leadership and Management Changes

Chief Executive Officer Will Orr, who joined in late 2023, has firmly established his "Next Chapter" growth strategy, accelerating the company's expansion plans and achieving midterm return targets ahead of schedule.

Chief Financial Officer Luke Tait, appointed in late 2022, has successfully overseen a period of significant deleveraging, bringing the company's Adjusted Leverage down from 2.0x in 2022 to 1.0x by the end of 2025.

Board composition is evolving, with Non-Executive Director Wais Shaifta stepping down at the May 2026 AGM after five years of service, prompting the Nomination Committee to seek succession for his roles chairing the Remuneration and Sustainability Committees.

Informal commentary from a former Head of Marketing indicates strong confidence in the CEO and CFO's financial acumen, though they noted some doubts regarding the broader central office executive team's capabilities.

The management team is heavily investing in operational leadership, upskilling gym managers to handle the transition from a freelance personal trainer model to a directly employed workforce.

Cultural and employee metrics remain robust under the current leadership, with employee engagement scores maintained at a high 9 out of 10, placing The Gym Group in the top 5% of consumer services businesses.

Margin Evolution and Financial Profile

The company has demonstrated significant operating leverage over the last 12 months, with FY25 Group Adjusted EBITDA Less Normalised Rent increasing by 19% to £56.7m, significantly outpacing the 8% growth in total revenue.

A major operational milestone was achieved as the Return on Invested Capital (ROIC) for mature sites increased by 2 percentage points to 27% in 2025, hitting the company's initial 25% target early and tracking well toward the new 30% hurdle rate.

If the 13 legacy workforce-dependent gyms are excluded from the calculation, the mature site ROIC already sits at the 30% target level.

Jefferies highlights that The Gym Group's management has guided FY26E EBITDA less normalised rent to the top end of market forecasts at £60.7m, demonstrating confidence in carrying recent momentum into the next financial year.

Shareholder Returns and Capital Allocation

In a major shift for capital returns, the Board announced the commencement of a £10m share buyback programme in January 2026, signalling confidence in the company's surplus free cash flow generation.

The buyback replaces the immediate prospect of a dividend, aligning with a capital allocation policy that prioritises maintaining leverage below 2.0x and fully funding organic site expansion before returning excess capital to shareholders.

Demand and Macroeconomic Dynamics

Member demand has proven highly resilient to cost-of-living pressures, with January and February 2026 peak trading showing a 9% year-on-year revenue increase and membership numbers climbing 8% to 999,000.

Gen Z consumers now represent 44% of the company's average members, a demographic that views fitness as a non-discretionary necessity and ranks it as a top priority for their spending.

The rising use of GLP-1 weight-loss medications (estimated at 2 million users in the UK) is acting as a structural demand tailwind, as clinical guidance heavily recommends resistance training to prevent the loss of lean muscle mass associated with these drugs.

Consumer behaviour is shifting toward higher engagement, with the proportion of members visiting the gym four or more times a month increasing by 150 basis points in 2025, reaching 54.6%.

Section 4: Change in strategy/structure
The 'Next Chapter' Strategic Framework

Under the leadership of CEO Will Orr, The Gym Group has formally transitioned its corporate strategy into a three-pillared framework known as the 'Next Chapter' growth plan.

The first strategic pillar, "Strengthen the Core," focuses intensely on increasing returns from the existing mature estate by driving like-for-like revenue growth through yield management and retention initiatives.

The second pillar, "Accelerate Rollout of Quality Sites," utilises the free cash flow generated by the strengthened core to fund a rapid, self-sustaining expansion of the physical gym network.

The final pillar, "Broaden our Growth," represents a strategic shift toward identifying adjacent revenue streams and new channels, moving the company beyond its traditional direct-to-consumer subscription model.

A key manifestation of this third pillar is the recent nationwide rollout of a strategic partnership with Wellhub, a B2B2C corporate wellness platform that provides the company access to a pool of over 1.5 million employees across 400 UK corporate clients.

To support these structural changes, the company has undertaken a major technological overhaul, investing heavily in replacing legacy member management and payment systems to unlock capabilities like referral programmes and dynamic retention strategies.

Evolution of Pricing Architecture and Premium Tiers

The company has structurally overhauled its membership architecture, moving away from a binary model to a flexible three-tier pricing system across the entire estate.

This new architecture consists of an entry-level 'Off-Peak' product (starting from approximately £13.99), a 'Standard' membership (replacing the legacy 'DO IT' tier), and a premium 'Ultimate' product (replacing the 'LIVE IT' tier).

The introduction of the Off-Peak tier was strategically designed to attract highly price-sensitive consumers and older demographics who prefer to exercise during the day, effectively serving as a safety net to prevent churn among financially constrained members.

Jefferies analysts note that the increased flexibility provided by this new three-tier pricing structure is a core driver of the company's ability to close the price gap with competitors while protecting overall membership volumes.

The premium 'Ultimate' tier has maintained robust penetration, hovering around 29% to 31% of the total membership base throughout 2024 and 2025, demonstrating strong consumer willingness to pay for multi-site access and added perks.

To further drive yield without forcing members into higher tiers, the company has structurally introduced unbundled "add-on" products, such as standalone Guest Passes and Multi-site access, which have proven highly popular among Standard tier members.

A former Head of Marketing at the company noted that pricing discipline involves balancing the standard monthly price point against introductory offers, with the premium tier playing a vital role in maintaining consistent EBITDA margins across the portfolio.

Accelerated Opening Targets and Geographic Strategy

The company has significantly upgraded its structural growth ambitions, abandoning its previous cautious post-Covid rollout pace in favour of aggressive, self-funded expansion.

Initially targeting 50 new sites over a three-year period, management accelerated this target in early 2026 to approximately 75 new high-quality sites, fully funded by internal free cash flow.

Geographically, while Greater London remains a highly attractive market for new openings, the company is increasingly utilising bespoke machine-learning data models to identify untapped "white space" in smaller urban residential areas and provincial towns.

A former director in the UK gym industry observed that The Gym Group is actively shifting its property strategy to target smaller towns where it can operate as the sole low-cost provider, leveraging smaller box formats to fit into non-traditional retail spaces.

To cater to varying geographic catchments, the company is testing structural format innovations, ranging from compact footprint gyms in suburban locations (e.g., Hendon) to massive 20,000 square foot "destination" facilities in larger markets (e.g., Norwich).

All new site openings are now governed by a strict capital allocation framework that demands a 30% Return on Invested Capital (ROIC), ensuring that aggressive geographic expansion does not dilute overall corporate profitability.

Furthermore, the physical structure and aesthetic of the gyms are evolving; new and refurbished sites feature a more premium, on-trend design with dedicated spaces for socialising and a heavier weighting toward strength equipment, specifically tailored to attract Gen Z consumers.

Section 5: Change in priorities/ambition

Under the leadership of CEO Will Orr, who joined in late 2023, The Gym Group’s overarching ambition has definitively shifted from post-pandemic recovery to aggressive, self-funded operational acceleration.

Orr explicitly articulated this shift during recent earnings calls, stating that his mandate has "not been to reinvent the wheel" but rather to drastically "accelerate performance and increase shareholder returns."

The management team is now intensely focused on executing the 'Next Chapter' growth strategy, which is designed to build upon existing strengths while selectively introducing new, high-upside initiatives.

A key element of this heightened ambition is the strict, unwavering commitment to driving sustained organic growth entirely from internally generated free cash flow.

This intense focus on cash generation is already yielding results, with the company delivering a 39% year-on-year increase in free cash flow to £37.5 million in early 2025.

Operationally, the leadership team is demonstrating a ruthless prioritization of yield management over pure membership volume growth.

Orr noted that the company’s principal focus will be towards yield and solving to maximize revenue growth, taking advantage of a structural pricing gap against mid-market competitors.

This yield-focused ambition is supported by the national rollout of a new three-tier pricing architecture, which management believes provides the necessary flexibility to price aggressively across the core membership base.

In terms of site expansion, the company has radically upgraded its mid-term ambitions, accelerating its organically funded rollout target from 50 to approximately 75 new gyms over the next three years.

This upgraded target demands a high level of operational intensity, requiring the company to open at least 20 to 22 new sites in 2026 alone.

Despite the rapid pace of expansion, management is enforcing absolute capital discipline, insisting that all new site openings meet a strict 30% Return on Invested Capital (ROIC) hurdle rate.

To ensure this 30% ROIC target is met amidst inflationary pressures, the property and development teams are employing intense value engineering during gym fit-outs.

Cost-saving initiatives include utilizing the exposed structural shells of buildings and utilizing darker paintwork, which structurally reduces the frequency and cost of ongoing maintenance.

Marketing ambitions have also been heavily restructured, pivoting away from broad national campaigns toward highly targeted, localized dominance.

Orr emphasized this new intensity, stating that the company will not "get distracted by the expense of national awareness," but instead aims to definitively "be #1 locally and win around each of our 233 sites."

At the gym level, management is aggressively optimizing its labor-light operating model to combat National Living Wage increases.

Rather than utilizing a rigid staffing structure, operational intensity now involves deploying flexible cluster managers and dual-site managers based on the specific complexity and scale of local catchments.

Technological ambition is receiving unprecedented investment, with the company undertaking a massive, phased replacement of its legacy member management and payment systems across 2025 and 2026.

This tech overhaul is expected to unlock entirely new operational capabilities, including sophisticated member-get-member referral programs and dynamic retention interventions.

While the executive team remains laser-focused on the core estate and near-term rollout, they are concurrently laying the groundwork for a broader growth ambition.

Orr has confirmed that the company is actively investigating adjacent revenue streams and new B2B channels that align closely with The Gym Group's core competencies.

Finally, the initiation of a £10 million share buyback program signals a major maturation in the Board's financial ambition, proving their willingness to aggressively return surplus capital once target leverage ratios are comfortably achieved.

Jefferies analysts view this refined ambition extremely positively, noting that the new management's straightforward plan to focus on core performance and self-funded unit rollouts has driven consistent delivery and an ongoing valuation re-rating.

Section 6: Financial profile

Robust Revenue Acceleration: The Gym Group has demonstrated a highly resilient top-line trajectory, with total revenue for the 2025 financial year increasing by 8% to £244.9 million. This growth was structurally balanced, driven equally by a 4% increase in average member volumes to 945,000 and a 4% uplift in average revenue per member per month (ARPMM) to £21.60. On a like-for-like basis, revenue grew by 3%, entirely fulfilling management's internal targets and demonstrating pricing power in a mature market. The momentum has aggressively continued into the critical 2026 New Year peak recruitment period, with January and February revenue surging by 9% year-on-year.

Operating Leverage and Margin Expansion: The company's underlying profitability metrics have significantly outpaced top-line growth, showcasing the immense operational leverage inherent in its labour-light business model. Group Adjusted EBITDA Less Normalised Rent (the Board's preferred metric for underlying cash profitability) increased by an impressive 19% to £56.7 million in 2025. This margin expansion was largely driven by the maturation of site cohorts opened in previous years, alongside stringent site-level cost controls. Statutory profit before tax similarly surged by 196% to £7.4 million, confirming that the operational gains are flowing cleanly through to the bottom line.

Structurally Advantaged Cash Conversion: The Gym Group operates with a highly advantageous negative working capital model that structurally generates cash as the business scales. Because members pay their subscriptions upfront at the beginning of the month, while the company pays its suppliers on 30 to 45-day terms, organic growth intrinsically produces working capital inflows. This dynamic helped drive a 10% year-on-year increase in Free Cash Flow to £38.3 million in 2025. Consequently, the business is now in a position where it can fully self-fund its aggressive site expansion purely from internal cash generation.

Capital Expenditure Profile: The cash demands of the business are clearly bifurcated between maintenance and expansionary requirements. Maintenance capital expenditure, which ensures the existing estate remains competitive and fresh, runs at approximately 6% of total revenue, costing £17.3 million in 2025. Conversely, expansionary capital expenditure stepped up significantly to £33.9 million in 2025, funding the fit-out of 16 new gyms and a major ongoing overhaul of the company's proprietary member management technology platform. For 2026, total capital expenditure is guided to rise to between £60 million and £65 million to support an accelerated target of at least 20 new site openings.

Hidden Tax Shield Valuation Support: A unique and highly valuable component of The Gym Group's medium-term financial profile is its accumulated tax shields. Due to substantial statutory losses incurred during the Covid-19 pandemic lockdowns, the company has built up significant tax offsets. Management has confirmed that these historical losses will shelter the business from paying any material cash corporate tax until at least 2027. This multi-year tax holiday structurally inflates the company's free cash flow conversion profile relative to peers, providing excess liquidity to either accelerate site rollouts or compound shareholder returns.

Aggressive Deleveraging Trajectory: The balance sheet has undergone a rapid and highly successful deleveraging phase over the past 24 months. The company's Adjusted Leverage ratio (defined as Non-Property Net Debt divided by EBITDA Less Normalised Rent) was compressed down to 1.0x by the end of 2025, halving from a peak of 2.0x in 2022. Total Non-Property Net Debt now stands at just £59.3 million. This 1.0x ratio sits incredibly comfortably below the stringent banking covenant threshold of 3.0x, completely removing any lingering market concerns regarding capital structure fragility.

Liquidity and Debt Facilities: To support its newly accelerated "Next Chapter" growth plans, the company proactively secured an extension and upsizing of its debt facilities. In mid-2025, management negotiated a combined £102 million facility, comprised of a £45 million Term Loan and a £57 million Revolving Credit Facility. Crucially, the maturity profile of this debt was successfully pushed out to June 2028, securing long-term operational runway. With £43 million of absolute headroom remaining on these facilities at the end of 2025, the company possesses immense liquidity buffers against macroeconomic shocks.

Inflection to Capital Returns (Buybacks): In a watershed moment for the company's financial maturity, the Board initiated a £10 million share buyback programme in January 2026. This move perfectly aligns with the stated capital allocation framework, which permits the return of excess capital to shareholders once organic growth is fully funded and leverage is reliably maintained below 2.0x. The pivot to a buyback over a traditional dividend suggests the Board views the current equity valuation as fundamentally disconnected from the intrinsic cash-generating power of the mature estate.

Unit Economics - The 30% ROIC Hurdle: The most critical leading indicator of the company's financial health is the Return on Invested Capital (ROIC) generated by its mature sites. In 2025, mature site ROIC expanded by 200 basis points to reach 27%, comfortably surpassing the initial 25% target. Even more impressively, if the legacy 13 "workforce-dependent" sites (which suffer from post-Covid structural shifts in office commuting) are excluded, the core residential and provincial estate is already hitting the aggressive 30% target hurdle rate. This 30% ROIC confirms that new capital deployed into fresh site openings is highly accretive.

Jefferies Research View on Valuation: Jefferies analysts argue that The Gym Group's valuation multiples are fundamentally suppressed, trading at just 7.0x EV/EBITDA for FY26E despite forecasting an 18% three-year EBITDA compound annual growth rate. They explicitly expect the valuation to re-rate and expand as confidence builds in the new management team's execution of self-funded rollouts. Jefferies maintains a DCF-derived price target of 250p, implying substantial upside, noting that variable pricing effectively optimizes unit returns and acts as a major catalyst for margin protection.

Morgan Stanley View on Global Multiples: Morgan Stanley highlights that while The Gym Group and Basic-Fit are the most comparable global peers, GYM trades at a relative discount because it operates exclusively in the highly competitive U.K. market, whereas Basic-Fit benefits from multi-country scale. Morgan Stanley's proprietary analysis indicates that well-positioned global fitness operators should theoretically command EV/EBITDA multiples in the 9x to 12x range, underscoring the potential for significant multiple expansion if The Gym Group can consistently sustain its current 25%+ organic growth trajectory.

Sector Benchmarking vs PureGym: Expert commentary from a former PureGym Divisional Director reveals distinct differences in capital deployment strategies between the UK's top two low-cost operators. PureGym demands an absolute, cast-iron return on investment—historically targeting 35% to 40% ROC—before authorizing any major £500k site refurbishments. Conversely, The Gym Group is perceived as being more willing to accept slightly lower baseline returns (closer to 20% on refurbs) to ensure defensive footprint maintenance and continuous aesthetic upgrades, reflecting a more holistic approach to long-term estate health rather than pure near-term capital extraction.

Sector Benchmarking vs Basic-Fit: Basic-Fit, the European market leader, operates on a vastly different scale, managing over 1,660 gyms and generating €1,415m in FY25 revenue. Despite Basic-Fit's dominance and its recent Clever Fit acquisition in Germany, The Gym Group generates highly competitive unit economics. Industry experts point out that Basic-Fit relies heavily on rigid, fixed-price banding across its massive network, whereas The Gym Group utilizes highly localized, dynamic variable pricing mechanisms that allow it to extract superior yield from highly subscribed urban catchments without deterring volume in poorer provincial towns.

Inflation Mitigation via Fixed Utilities: The company has structurally derisked a highly volatile component of its P&L by successfully locking in long-term utility hedges. Electricity rates, historically a major pressure point for 24/7 gym operators, have been fully fixed until October 2027. This extraordinary foresight protects the bottom line from European energy market shocks and virtually guarantees that mid-term LFL cost inflation will remain capped at an easily manageable 3% to 4% range, allowing pricing yield to drop cleanly to EBITDA.

EBITDA Forecast Upgrades: Given the robust volume and yield metrics achieved in early 2026, sell-side consensus has structurally shifted upward. Management has confidently guided that FY26E EBITDA Less Normalised Rent will reach the absolute top end of market forecasts at £60.7 million. This sustained earnings momentum practically eliminates the equity risk premium associated with the previous turnaround phase, shifting the financial narrative purely toward compounding growth.

Refinement of Variable Pricing: Pricing elasticity has proven to be incredibly robust. The introduction of the 'Off-Peak' tier successfully stabilized churn among lower-income members, while simultaneous increases to the standard tier drove ARPMM up without triggering mass cancellations. A former Head of Revenue Management at PureGym estimated that best-in-class UK operators require this dynamic yielding to defend against aggressive entrants like JD Gyms, validating GYM's sophisticated machine-learning approach to price setting.

CapEx Inflation Realities: While returns are stellar, the absolute cost of building a gym has undeniably inflated. An industry expert noted that pre-Covid, a standard low-cost gym cost roughly £1.2m to £1.35m to fit out. Building materials and specialized HVAC requirements have pushed these initial capital outlays higher across the sector. The fact that The Gym Group can still clear a 30% ROIC hurdle rate despite these inherently higher baseline construction costs demonstrates immense pricing power and strict value-engineering during the build phase.

Valuation Penalty of a UK-Only Strategy: Despite pristine financial mechanics, the company's geographic concentration remains a structural anchor on its valuation multiple. Sell-side analysts explicitly warn that unless The Gym Group attempts to broaden its growth internationally, it will likely forever trade at a discount to Basic-Fit, which commands a premium for its diversified, multi-country European rollout runway.
