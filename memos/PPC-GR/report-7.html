KEY FINDINGS - Verdict: PPC-GR presents a multi-variable thesis with one dominant fulcrum — the renewable capacity build-out — supported by a credible regulatory and tariff backdrop, but the case requires at least four of six drivers to deliver simultaneously [#J]

  • Top driver: Renewable capacity additions (target ~9 GW by 2026E, from ~3 GW in 2022) are the single highest-impact lever, linking directly to EBITDA margin expansion and re-rating [#J]
  • Execution risk concentration: Two drivers — RES capex execution and debt/leverage trajectory — are correlated; a capex slippage hits both EBITDA and balance-sheet optics simultaneously [#A]
  • Regulatory anchor: Greece's RAB-linked distribution tariff mechanism and the EU decarbonisation funding stack (RRF, REPowerEU) provide structural visibility that peers in less-supportive regulatory regimes lack [#I]
  • Earnings delivery: PPC-GR moved from net-loss territory (2021) to positive EBITDA of ~€1.4bn in 2023, with consensus pointing toward €1.7-1.9bn by 2025E — a trajectory that is real but consensus-embedded [#A]
  • Bear case fulcrum: Wholesale power price normalisation is the single biggest case-level risk; PPC-GR's merchant exposure means a sustained retreat in Greek/South-East European power prices compresses generation margin regardless of renewable additions [#J]
  • Leverage overhang: Net debt/EBITDA of ~3.5-4.0× (2023 reported) is elevated vs European utility median of ~2.5-3.0×; improvement is a necessary condition for de-rating risk to recede [#I]
  • Valuation context: PPC-GR has historically traded at a discount to Western European utility peers on EV/EBITDA; the discount is partly justified by country risk and leverage, but a re-rating toward 6-7× EV/EBITDA (from ~5× in 2024) is embedded in sell-side bull cases [#A]
  • Setup archetype: "Capex-cycle compounder + regulated-income growth" — analogous to CEZ or Enel in their early-2010s pivot periods, but with higher execution risk and a smaller free-float [#I]
  • Go/no-go signal: The fulcrum driver (RES additions) is plausible but back-end loaded; the 24-month window catches only part of the value crystallisation, creating a timing risk that reduces but does not eliminate the IRR case [#J]

§2 — Key Driver #1: RES Capacity Build-Out + EBITDA Margin Expansion

J-front verdict: Highest-impact single driver; PPC-GR is mid-cycle in a credible 7-10 GW RES expansion programme, but commissioning cadence is back-end loaded and 24M window captures only partial delivery [#J]

  • Driver definition: PPC-GR's strategy targets exit from lignite generation (completed Megalopolis closure 2023) and replacement with wind, solar, and hydro capacity; incremental RES MWh carries materially higher EBITDA margin than legacy thermal [#I]
  • Mechanism to EPS: Each GW of commissioned RES adds ~€80-120mn EBITDA (indicative, based on Greek RES contract structures and spot-merchant mix); EBITDA flows to EBIT after D&A step-up, then to EPS after interest costs [#A]
  • Mechanism to SP: Higher EBITDA → lower net debt/EBITDA → re-rating from ~5× toward 6-7× EV/EBITDA; analogous to Iberdrola's re-rating as RES mix shifted from ~30% to ~55% (2010-2018, EV/EBITDA moved from ~6× to ~9×) [#I]

Company guidance & management view:
- Company guide: Management 2023-2026 business plan targets ~9 GW total RES installed by 2026E (vs ~3 GW in 2022), implying ~6 GW net additions over four years [#A]
- Management commentary: CEO Giorgos Stassis has consistently characterised the RES programme as the "transformation anchor"; licensing and grid connection agreements cited as de-risking factors [#A]
- Track record: 2023 additions were reportedly behind the linear schedule by one quarter; management revised commissioning timelines modestly at H1-2023 results — mild sandbagging pattern, not a red flag yet [#A]
- Conservatism vs optimism: Guidance appears mildly optimistic given Greek permitting complexity; historical Greek RES industry commissioning has lagged licenced capacity by 12-18 months on average [#I]

Sell-side consensus:
- SS consensus: Public broker coverage (Eurobank Equities, Axia Capital, Optima Bank, Alpha Finance, National Bank Securities among Greek brokers; Jefferies and Berenberg among international) broadly accepts the 9 GW target but discounts it by 10-20% for delays [#A]
- Consensus changes: EPS estimates revised upward L12M on stronger-than-expected 2023 EBITDA; 2025 estimates reflect ~€1.7-1.9bn EBITDA, implying RES contribution of ~€400-500mn [#A]
- Estimated EPS impact: Full 6 GW addition (if on schedule) would add ~€480-720mn EBITDA cumulatively by 2026E; partial delivery (4 GW by end-2026E) adds ~€320-480mn — the difference is ~25% of total consensus 2026E EBITDA [#I]

Recent performance & baseline:
- Recent delivery: PPC-GR ended 2023 with approximately 4.4 GW of RES installed (wind + solar + small hydro); this is ahead of 2022 base of ~3 GW, confirming positive momentum [#A]
- L3Y baseline: Lignite exits (2021-2023) created a temporary EBITDA trough; RES additions are refilling that gap — the trajectory is real, not cosmetic [#I]
- Peer benchmark: CEZ (Czech Republic) achieved comparable RES mix shift over 2015-2022 with 8-12% EBITDA CAGR during the capex cycle; PPC-GR's guided EBITDA trajectory (~15% CAGR 2022-2026E) is at the aggressive end of that peer range [#A]
- Trend: Accelerating commissioning in H2-2023 and early 2024, with solar projects benefiting from faster grid connection under the Greek fast-track licensing regime [#I]

Macro & end-market context:
- Macro tailwind: REPowerEU and Greece's National Recovery and Resilience Plan are channelling €3-4bn in subsidised financing toward Greek RES; PPC-GR is the primary beneficiary as the dominant incumbent [#A]
- Structural driver: European power market decarbonisation is structural, not cyclical; Greek renewable resource quality (solar irradiance, wind load factors) is among the best in EU [#I]
- Competitive dynamics: Greek independent power producers (Terna Energy, Heron, Mytilineos Power) are also expanding RES; PPC-GR's scale and balance sheet give it preferential grid connection, but competition is intensifying for best-in-class wind/solar sites [#A]
- Visibility: Medium-high — licensing pipeline is public, EPC contracts are largely signed for near-term projects; variability sits in commissioning timing, not in whether projects will be built [#I]

Plausibility assessment:
- Key assumptions: (1) Permitting delays stay within 12-18 month tolerance; (2) EPC costs remain within budget (steel/copper inflation is a risk); (3) grid connection milestones met on schedule [#I]
- Magnitude if delivered: 6 GW on-schedule → ~25-30% EPS uplift by 2026E vs consensus; partial delivery (4 GW) → ~15% EPS uplift [#A]
- Bear case risk factor: A 2-year wholesale price collapse would reduce merchant RES economics materially even on already-commissioned capacity [#J]

Sceptical bullet: Greek permitting remains notoriously slow; the fast-track licensing regime was introduced but faces legal challenges from environmental groups; a 12-18 month systematic delay across the RES pipeline would reduce 2026E EBITDA by ~10-15% vs consensus [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — commissioning pipeline is real and partially de-risked, but back-end loading and permitting complexity constrain 24M delivery confidence

§3 — Key Driver #2: Wholesale Power Price Trajectory (Greek/SEE Market)

J-front verdict: Single largest external amplifier; PPC-GR retains meaningful merchant exposure that makes EPS highly sensitive to Greek wholesale prices — a factor largely outside management's control [#J]

  • Driver definition: Greek wholesale electricity prices (HENEX Day-Ahead) determine the revenue realisation on PPC-GR's unhedged generation output; this includes residual thermal (gas CCGT), hydro dispatch value, and merchant RES output above any fixed-price RES support contracts [#I]
  • Mechanism to EPS: Each €10/MWh move in average realised price across PPC-GR's ~15-20 TWh of merchant-equivalent exposure translates to ~€150-200mn EBITDA; at ~30% effective tax rate, this is ~€0.20-0.27 EPS per €10/MWh [#A]
  • Mechanism to SP: Power price expectations are a leading indicator for generation-heavy utility multiples; the 2022 price spike drove PPC-GR's EV/EBITDA to its peak; any sustained reversion compresses the multiple before it compresses reported EPS [#J]

Company guidance & management view:
- Company guide: Management does not provide explicit power-price forecasts; 2023-2026 business plan assumes "normalised" prices above long-run cost (~€70-80/MWh average), which is broadly consistent with forward curves at the time of publication [#A]
- Management commentary: Management has emphasised progressive hedging policy and growing contracted/regulated revenue share as a path to reducing merchant exposure over time [#I]
- Conservatism vs optimism: The €70-80/MWh assumption is optimistic relative to 2019 pre-crisis levels (~€50/MWh) but reasonable given structural gas-price floor effects and continued EU carbon pricing support [#A]
- Track record: PPC-GR benefited materially from 2021-2022 price spikes (Greek wholesale peaked >€300/MWh in 2022) and retained some elevated realisation into 2023 [#I]

Sell-side consensus:
- SS consensus: Brokers embed Greek wholesale prices of €70-90/MWh for 2024-2026E in base cases; bear-case sensitivities typically show €50-60/MWh scenarios reducing 2025E EBITDA by 15-25% [#A]
- Consensus changes: Post-2022 normalisation narrative has modestly reduced SS price assumptions vs mid-2022 peak expectations; 2024E estimates were trimmed ~5-10% in H2-2023 as gas prices retreated [#A]
- Divergence: Bull analysts (e.g., Axia Capital, Eurobank Equities) embed ~€85-90/MWh; more cautious international brokers use ~€70-75/MWh — a 15-20% spread that drives significant EPS divergence [#A]

Recent performance & baseline:
- Recent delivery: Greek Day-Ahead average in 2023 was approximately €100-110/MWh (retreating from 2022 peaks but still elevated); 2024 YTD has trended toward €70-85/MWh as gas prices normalised [#I]
- L3Y baseline: Price trajectory: ~€50/MWh (2019-2020) → ~€150/MWh (2021) → ~€280/MWh (2022 peak) → ~€105/MWh (2023) → ~€75-85/MWh (2024E) [#A]
- Peer benchmark: Iberian utilities (Endesa, EDP) with comparable merchant exposure saw EBITDA variance of 20-35% around power price cycles; PPC-GR's merchant leverage is at the higher end of European utility peers given smaller hedged/contracted proportion [#A]
- Trend: Decelerating from elevated post-crisis levels; the direction is toward normalisation, which is the base case [#I]

Macro & end-market context:
- Macro headwind: European gas price normalisation (TTF ~€30-35/MWh in 2024 vs €200+ in 2022) is structurally reducing the marginal cost of generation and compressing power price floors [#J]
- Structural support: EU ETS carbon prices (~€50-70/tCO₂ range) provide a structural floor that supports power prices above pre-2019 levels even in a low-gas-price environment [#I]
- SEE market integration: Increasing interconnector capacity between Greece, Bulgaria, and Romania is partially arbitraging away the Greek premium pricing; this is a medium-term structural headwind for elevated Greek prices [#A]
- Visibility: Low — power prices are driven by weather, gas markets, and EU carbon policy, all of which have high forecast error over 24 months [#J]

Plausibility assessment:
- Key assumption: Greek wholesale prices hold at €70-80/MWh through 2025-2026E — consistent with current forward curves but sensitive to gas-price and carbon-price movements [#I]
- Magnitude if delivered: Price staying at €80/MWh vs consensus €75/MWh adds ~€50-100mn EBITDA — modest upside [#A]
- Magnitude if missed: Price falling to €55/MWh removes ~€200-300mn EBITDA (15-20% of 2025E consensus) — material downside [#J]

Sceptical bullet: The asymmetry is unfavourable: upside from prices staying elevated above consensus is limited (~€50-100mn EBITDA) while downside from price normalisation to 2019 levels is ~€200-300mn EBITDA — making power prices a capped upside/open downside driver from current levels [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — forward curves support the base case, but merchant exposure creates a structurally asymmetric risk profile that is not fully priced into consensus

§4 — Key Driver #3: Leverage Reduction + Credit Profile Improvement

J-front verdict: Necessary condition for multiple expansion; PPC-GR's elevated leverage (net debt/EBITDA ~3.5-4.0×) is the primary reason it trades at a discount to Western European utility peers, and deleveraging is the gating factor for re-rating [#J]

  • Driver definition: PPC-GR's net debt stood at ~€4.5-5.0bn at end-2023 against EBITDA of ~€1.4bn; the path to re-rating requires net debt/EBITDA approaching 3.0× by 2026E, which requires both EBITDA growth and capex discipline [#I]
  • Mechanism to EPS: Debt reduction lowers interest expense (each €500mn debt reduction at ~4% average cost saves ~€20mn pre-tax); combined EBITDA growth + interest saving creates a double-lever EPS uplift [#A]
  • Mechanism to SP: Credit rating improvement (currently sub-investment grade / high-yield BB range) would expand the investor universe, reduce refinancing costs, and support multiple re-rating toward investment-grade utility comps at 6-8× EV/EBITDA [#J]

Company guidance & management view:
- Company guide: Management targets net debt/EBITDA of ~2.5-3.0× by 2026E; this requires EBITDA growing to ~€1.7-1.9bn while net debt stays flat or declines modestly [#A]
- Management commentary: CFO has emphasised use of EU-subsidised green financing (ESG bonds, EIB facilities) to reduce average cost of debt and extend maturities; 2023 refinancing reportedly improved average maturity profile [#I]
- Conservatism vs optimism: The leverage target is achievable only if EBITDA growth is on-schedule; if RES additions slip (Driver #1 risk), the leverage target slips simultaneously — a correlated risk [#A]
- Track record: PPC-GR reduced net debt from ~€5.5bn (2021) to ~€4.5-5.0bn (2023) — modest progress; the pace of deleveraging has been slower than initial guidance [#J]

Sell-side consensus:
- SS consensus: Brokers broadly model net debt/EBITDA reaching 2.8-3.2× by 2026E; some more cautious analysts flag that capex requirements for RES expansion may prevent meaningful absolute debt reduction [#A]
- Estimated financial impact: Investment-grade upgrade (S&P/Moody's) would reduce blended cost of debt by ~50-100bps; on ~€4.5bn gross debt this is €22-45mn annual interest saving, ~€0.05-0.10 EPS [#I]
- Multiple expansion: Re-rating from 5.0× to 6.5× EV/EBITDA on €1.8bn 2026E EBITDA implies ~€2.7bn incremental enterprise value, or ~€8-10/share incremental (on ~300mn shares) [#A]

Recent performance & baseline:
- Recent delivery: Refinancing activity in 2022-2023 extended maturities; no near-term debt maturity wall visible; however, absolute net debt has not declined materially [#A]
- L3Y baseline: Net debt/EBITDA deteriorated 2020-2022 as EBITDA fell during lignite exit; 2023 marked the inflection point back toward deleveraging [#I]
- Peer benchmark: European utilities median net debt/EBITDA is ~2.5-3.0× (e.g., Enel ~3.0×, Engie ~2.7×, Verbund ~1.5×); PPC-GR at 3.5-4.0× is at the high end of investment-grade comparables and above many IG peers [#A]
- Trend: Improving directionally, but improvement pace (0.3-0.5× per year) is gradual; a 24M window gets leverage to ~3.0-3.5×, not yet at the 2.5× level that would trigger IG upgrade [#I]

Macro & end-market context:
- Macro tailwind: EU green financing programmes (EIB, EBRD, RRF) provide access to below-market-rate debt specifically for RES/grid projects; this reduces interest burden and supports faster deleveraging [#A]
- Macro headwind: Rising European risk-free rates (2022-2024 cycle) have increased refinancing costs for any existing floating-rate or maturing fixed-rate debt; this partially offsets green-financing benefits [#I]
- Visibility: Medium — EBITDA trajectory is visible; the key uncertainty is whether capex for RES expansion consumes all incremental cash flow, preventing absolute debt reduction [#A]

Plausibility assessment:
- Key assumptions: (1) EBITDA reaches €1.7bn by 2025E; (2) RES capex programme does not require incremental debt beyond current facilities; (3) no one-off Greek government cash calls (special levies, recapitalisation demands) [#I]
- Magnitude if delivered: IG upgrade + 6.5× multiple → ~€8-10/share incremental; ~25-35% SP upside from leverage re-rating alone [#A]
- Miss scenario: EBITDA misses €1.5bn in 2025E and capex overruns → net debt/EBITDA stays above 3.5× → no IG upgrade → discount to peers persists [#J]

Sceptical bullet: The deleveraging timeline is tightly coupled to EBITDA delivery; every 6-month RES delay pushes the IG-upgrade trigger point out by approximately 12 months, meaning the 24M window may not capture the multiple expansion event at all [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — trajectory is correct but pace is slower than management guides; 24-month window likely insufficient for full leverage re-rating crystallisation

§5 — Key Driver #4: Regulated Networks (DEDDIE/HEDNO) RAB Growth

J-front verdict: Most visible and least volatile driver; HEDNO (the distribution network subsidiary) provides a growing regulated earnings stream that anchors the base case and reduces overall earnings volatility [#J]

  • Driver definition: HEDNO (Hellenic Electricity Distribution Network Operator), PPC-GR's 51%-owned distribution subsidiary, operates under a RAB-linked regulatory framework; tariff resets and RAB investment drive predictable EBITDA growth independent of power prices [#I]
  • Mechanism to EPS: RAB grows as HEDNO invests in grid modernisation; regulatory return on RAB (WACC-linked, currently ~6-7%) translates to EBITDA; HEDNO distributes dividends to PPC-GR parent [#A]
  • Mechanism to SP: Regulated EBITDA reduces earnings volatility, justifies a higher-quality earnings multiple, and provides a floor to EPS estimates if generation earnings disappoint [#J]

Company guidance & management view:
- Company guide: HEDNO capex plan targets €1.5-2.0bn over 2023-2026E for grid upgrades, smart metering, and EV charging infrastructure; RAB growth of ~8-10% p.a. is guided [#A]
- Management commentary: Management consistently highlights HEDNO as the "stable platform" supporting the group's transformation; regulatory reset (RAR IV period, 2024-2027) is expected to maintain the current WACC framework [#I]
- Track record: HEDNO has delivered regulated EBITDA growth in line with guidance historically; this is the most reliable earnings line within PPC-GR [#A]
- Conservatism vs optimism: Conservative to realistic — RAB-based earnings have high visibility; the main risk is a regulatory tariff reset delivering a lower WACC, not a capex miss [#I]

Sell-side consensus:
- SS consensus: Broadly aligned; HEDNO EBITDA growing from ~€400mn (2022) toward ~€550-600mn (2026E) is embedded in most models [#A]
- Estimated EPS impact: HEDNO contributes ~25-30% of group EBITDA; growth from €400mn to €580mn adds ~€180mn EBITDA at group level, contributing ~€0.25-0.35 EPS cumulatively by 2026E [#I]
- Consensus changes: Stable; no material revisions to HEDNO estimates in L12M [#A]

Recent performance & baseline:
- Recent delivery: HEDNO EBITDA grew ~12% in 2022-2023 per public reporting, in line with RAB growth trajectory [#A]
- Peer benchmark: Comparable regulated distribution operators in Southern Europe (e.g., Rede Elétrica Nacional in Portugal, TERNA Energia's network business in Italy) have delivered 5-9% EBITDA CAGR under similar RAB frameworks; PPC-GR/HEDNO is at the upper end [#I]
- Trend: Stable and accelerating with smart-metering investment cycle [#A]

Macro & end-market context:
- Structural tailwind: Greece's grid requires significant modernisation to accommodate RES integration and EV adoption; EU funds (RRF + Structural Funds) support HEDNO capex, reducing equity intensity [#I]
- Regulatory visibility: RAR IV period (2024-2027) is confirmed; WACC under review but RAE (Greek regulatory authority) has historically been supportive [#A]
- Visibility: High — regulatory framework is published; capex programme is approved; earnings are largely insulated from power-price movements [#J]

Plausibility assessment:
- Key assumptions: (1) RAR IV WACC not cut materially; (2) HEDNO capex executes on schedule; (3) no regulatory interference from Greek government on distribution tariffs [#I]
- Magnitude if delivered: ~€180mn incremental EBITDA by 2026E; ~€0.30-0.40 EPS additive; anchors group floor even if generation disappoints [#A]

Sceptical bullet: The main bear case on HEDNO is a RAE regulatory reset delivering a lower WACC (e.g., 5% vs current 6-7%), which would reduce HEDNO EBITDA by ~€50-80mn annually; historically rare but not impossible in an era of lower risk-free rates [#J]

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — regulated framework provides the strongest earnings visibility of any PPC-GR driver; execution and regulatory risk are both low relative to generation drivers

§6 — Key Driver #5: Retail Market Share Defence + Customer Margin Expansion

J-front verdict: Stabilising but contested; PPC-GR's retail franchise is structurally eroding as Greek market liberalisation progresses, but customer base remains large and margin recovery is occurring as government tariff interventions unwind [#J]

  • Driver definition: PPC-GR serves ~5 million electricity customers in Greece, retaining dominant market share despite liberalisation; retail margin is the spread between wholesale procurement cost and customer tariffs, net of supply costs [#I]
  • Mechanism to EPS: Retail EBITDA contributes ~€100-200mn; a 10% market share loss (from ~60% to ~54%) reduces retail EBITDA by ~€20-30mn; conversely, margin recovery as government subsidies unwind adds €50-100mn [#A]
  • Mechanism to SP: Retail earnings provide dividend-supporting cashflow; the narrative of "successful market defender vs entrants" supports the investment thesis of durable competitive positioning [#J]

Company guidance & management view:
- Company guide: Management targets retention of ~55-60% residential market share through service quality, loyalty programmes, and bundled energy/telecom offers [#A]
- Management commentary: CEO has acknowledged structural market-share erosion as an accepted cost of liberalisation; focus is on maintaining profitability per customer, not share [#I]
- Track record: PPC-GR lost ~15pp of market share 2019-2023 (from ~75% to ~60%); the rate of loss has slowed as the most price-sensitive customers have already switched [#A]
- Conservatism vs optimism: Moderately optimistic — a further 5-10pp loss over 2024-2026E is probable as remaining price-sensitive segments switch [#I]

Sell-side consensus:
- SS consensus: Broadly assumes stable retail EBITDA at €100-150mn through 2026E; some upside modelled if government tariff interventions (introduced 2022-2023) are fully withdrawn [#A]
- Estimated EPS impact: Full withdrawal of government retail subsidies and return to market pricing could add €50-100mn EBITDA; continued intervention extending beyond 2024 would reduce that by ~€30-50mn [#I]

Recent performance & baseline:
- Recent delivery: Greek government maintained retail electricity subsidies through most of 2022-2023, suppressing PPC-GR's retail margin below normalised levels; partial unwinding began in H2-2023 [#A]
- Trend: Improving as subsidies unwind; underlying market share loss continues but at a slower pace [#I]

Macro & end-market context:
- Macro tailwind: Lower wholesale prices (2024E) make customer retention easier as the incentive to switch for price is reduced [#A]
- Competitive headwind: Mytilineos Energy, Elpedison, and NRG are all investing in customer acquisition; digital customer platforms are lowering switching friction [#I]
- Visibility: Medium — retail margin is partly policy-determined (government tariff intervention risk remains) [#A]

Plausibility assessment:
- Key assumptions: (1) Government tariff interventions do not re-emerge at 2022-scale; (2) market share stabilises above 55%; (3) retail unit margins recover to €25-30/customer annually [#I]
- Magnitude if delivered: ~€50-80mn incremental EBITDA from margin normalisation; modest but earnings-quality positive [#A]

Sceptical bullet: Greek government retail tariff interventions are an endemic political risk — they were introduced with little warning in 2021-2022 and re-emergence ahead of any electoral cycle cannot be excluded; this is an earnings volatility risk that is hard to model [#J]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — market share stabilisation is credible, but retail margin recovery depends on sustained government non-intervention, which has a poor track record in Greece

§7 — Key Driver #6: EU Funding + RRF Capex Cost Subsidy

J-front verdict: Underappreciated enabler; EU funding programmes (RRF, CEF, EIB) are materially reducing PPC-GR's equity capex intensity for the RES programme, improving FCF generation and reducing dilution risk [#J]

  • Driver definition: Greece's National RRP allocates ~€3-4bn to energy transition, with PPC-GR as the primary institutional recipient; EU grants and concessional loans reduce the all-in capex cost of RES projects by an estimated 15-25% vs pure-market financing [#I]
  • Mechanism to EPS: Lower effective capex per GW → higher FCF → lower incremental debt per GW of RES added → faster deleveraging path; additionally, grant portions are non-dilutive to equity [#A]
  • Mechanism to SP: EU funding provides a capex-intensity advantage over unsubsidised IPP competitors; it underpins the leverage reduction trajectory (Driver #3) and accelerates the RES buildout (Driver #1) [#J]

Company guidance & management view:
- Company guide: Management has guided that EU funding will cover ~€1.0-1.5bn of the total RES capex programme (approximately 20-25% of the €5-6bn total investment plan) [#A]
- Management commentary: CEO and CFO have explicitly credited EU funding as enabling the capex programme without requiring equity issuance; this is a key differentiator vs regional IPP peers [#I]
- Conservatism vs optimism: Conservative — EU RRP disbursement has generally been slower than planned across all EU member states; Greece specifically has accelerated disbursement relative to EU average, so risk is to the upside on timing [#A]

Sell-side consensus:
- SS consensus: Most brokers embed EU funding as a capex assumption rather than as a separate driver; those who model it explicitly note it reduces net capex by €200-300mn annually [#A]
- Estimated financial impact: €1.0-1.5bn in total EU grants/concessional loans over 2023-2026E reduces net debt by €250-375mn vs the no-subsidy scenario — equivalent to ~0.15-0.20× net debt/EBITDA improvement [#I]

Recent performance & baseline:
- Recent delivery: Greece had drawn approximately €4.5bn of its RRP allocation by end-2023 (energy projects and infrastructure); PPC-GR confirmed receipt of first tranches of EIB green bond facilities in 2023 [#A]
- Trend: Accelerating — EU disbursements are tracking ahead of original 2021 projections for Greece specifically [#I]
