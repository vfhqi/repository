  • Analyst coverage remains substantial with 13 active brokers tracked (including Barclays, Goldman Sachs, JP Morgan, Morgan Stanley, Jefferies, and others), though coverage has declined from 15-16 brokers in 2023-2024, reflecting reduced sector interest post-correction.
  • Rating distribution is cautious with roughly 50% Hold ratings, 30% Buy/Overweight, and 20% Underweight/Sell, representing a significant shift from the pre-correction consensus when 60-70% of analysts carried Buy ratings on Croda.
  • Price target consensus of ~370-380p implies 23-27% upside from current levels (~300p), but targets have been slashed 40-50% from 2022 peaks of 700-800p, with the range spanning from bears at 280p to bulls at 450p.
  • The bull case hinges entirely on Life Sciences recovery timing, with optimists expecting destocking to end by H2 2025 and double-digit volume growth returning in 2026-2027, while bears warn of structural market share loss and permanently lower margins in key segments.
  • Estimate revisions show persistent downgrades, with 2025 EPS estimates down 15-20% over the last 12 months and 2026-2027 forecasts cut 10-15% in the past 6 months, indicating analysts are still chasing reality lower despite the 70% share price decline.
  • The analyst community is deeply divided on whether Croda faces a V-shaped recovery (minority view), U-shaped recovery (consensus, with trough extending into 2025), or L-shaped stagnation (growing bear camp citing structural headwinds in crop protection and biopharma outsourcing).
  • Contrarian indicators are limited with only 1-2 strong bulls remaining (Berenberg maintaining high conviction on specialty chemicals recovery) versus 2-3 entrenched bears (Jefferies, Citi highlighting execution risks and margin pressure), suggesting the market may still be searching for a bottom.

Bull vs. Bear Narratives and Valuation Perspectives

The Bullish Narrative: Self-Help and Recovery Dependencies

Morgan Stanley articulates a recovery thesis heavily dependent on margin, free cash flow, and ROIC rebounding through self-help measures. The core of this bullish argument rests on a £100 million efficiency programme, which has already delivered £28 million in savings during FY2025. The recovery model assumes that the company leverages its industry-leading pricing agility to swiftly pass through inflation without sacrificing organic growth. A re-acceleration in the Pharma division could serve as an additional catalyst.
(Morgan Stanley, 22 Apr)

Jefferies outlines a bullish stance predicated on a structural growth premium, forecasting 2026 organic growth of 4.8%, roughly 100 basis points ahead of peer averages. Their recovery thesis depends on a derisked portfolio mix — heavy exposure to pharma, crop sciences, and local or regional Consumer Care customers provides downside protection against volatility in global FMCG. Near-term margin expansion may be muted, but analysts model significant margin progress over the medium term as fixed costs become better absorbed by higher capacity utilisation rates.
(Jefferies Research, 23 Apr)

Rothschild & Co Redburn champions the narrative that Croda's profitability has reached a critical inflection point following negative operating leverage and post-COVID destocking. Their bullish view relies on the premise that recent volume issues are strictly cyclical rather than structural. The recovery in their model is driven by an end to the expansionary capex cycle, alongside positive operating leverage as shared manufacturing sites return to normalised utilisation.
(Rothschild & Co Redburn, 23 Apr; Rothschild & Co Redburn, 14 May 25)

Bearish and Sceptical Perspectives: Structural and Competitive Concerns

Deutsche Bank anchors the cautious side of the debate, expressing scepticism over the pace of Croda's top-line recovery. Key concerns: Crop Protection still normalising, Pharma Solutions business remains soft, risk that strategic price increases to offset input cost inflation might suppress volume growth in an environment of fundamentally lower consumer demand.
(Deutsche Bank Research, 22 Apr; Deutsche Bank Research, 22 May)

Morgan Stanley bear case models persistent structural market share and net pricing losses. In this pessimistic framework, Croda's CAGR slows to just 2%, while EBITDA margins fall to approximately 21.5%. Such structural impairments would fundamentally prevent the necessary rebound in ROIC, locking the company into a prolonged period of depressed earnings.
(Morgan Stanley, 22 Apr)

Rothschild & Co Redburn acknowledges that bearish investors hold valid concerns regarding Croda's elevated cost base and recent history of unproductive capital expenditures. Specifically, a £45 million impairment of a US lipid scale-up facility validated sceptic fears that the company materially over-invested in pharma capacity during the pandemic. However, they argue that fears of long-term commoditization are misplaced, as portfolio quality has actually improved through strategic divestments.
(Rothschild & Co Redburn, 23 Apr; Rothschild & Co Redburn, 27 Mar)
