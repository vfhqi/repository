2. KEY FINDINGS - DIVERSIFIED BUT SEK-CENTRIC TRANSLATION RISK: OEM International generates roughly 60-65% of revenue outside Sweden, predominantly in EUR-zone Scandinavia/Continental Europe, meaning every SEK/EUR move has a first-order translation effect on reported revenue and EBIT; the stock is structurally long EUR vs SEK from a reporting-currency standpoint

  • NATURAL HEDGE IS PARTIAL AND ASYMMETRIC: Revenue and cost are broadly co-located by country (salesforce + warehousing local to each market), creating a partial natural hedge at the operating-cost level, but product sourcing — largely from third-party principals across EUR, USD, and JPY supply chains — introduces a residual transaction mismatch that is not fully covered by natural matching

  • TRANSLATION DOMINATED L3Y: FX contributed positively to reported SEK revenue in 2022-2023 as the SEK weakened materially against EUR; the 2024 SEK partial recovery created a modest translation headwind, illustrating that OEM's reported top-line is more FX-sensitive than its organic growth rate implies

  • TRANSACTION EXPOSURE LIMITED BY BUSINESS MODEL: Because OEM is a technical distributor (buy-resell model, largely priced locally in the customer's currency), transaction exposure is concentrated in the sourcing leg — principals invoice in EUR, USD, or JPY; OEM re-prices locally; the lag and magnitude of pass-through is the key risk variable

  • HEDGING POLICY IS CONSERVATIVE AND LIMITED: OEM explicitly states it does not systematically hedge translation exposure (accepted as an accounting artefact); transaction hedging exists for specific short-duration mismatches but is not a significant part of the capital structure; this is standard practice for a distributor with high stock-turn and short invoice cycles

  • EPS SENSITIVITY: EUR/SEK IS THE DOMINANT LEVER: A 10% SEK depreciation vs EUR (EUR stronger) adds approximately 4-6% to reported SEK revenue and 4-6% to reported EBIT, given ~40-50% of total revenue is EUR-denominated; a 10% SEK appreciation is a symmetric headwind; EPS sensitivity is in the SEK 1.5-2.5/share range per 10% EUR/SEK move at current earnings run-rate

  • MACRO FX OUTLOOK (N12-24M) — MILD HEADWIND: Consensus lean is for SEK gradual strengthening from weak 2022-2023 levels as Riksbank normalises; if SEK recovers toward historical fair value vs EUR (approximately 10.50-10.80 range vs 11.20-11.50 traded through 2023-2024), OEM faces a 3-5% translation headwind to reported revenue vs current run-rate — a meaningful but manageable drag

  • THESIS IMPLICATION: The EPS-growth thesis is moderately FX-sensitive; organic growth quality is higher than reported growth in 2022-2023 (FX-flattered years) and organic growth quality will be lower than reported in a SEK-strengthening scenario; the thesis is not FX-destroyed but requires FX-adjustment to read organic momentum correctly

  • LARGEST SINGLE RISK: A sustained SEK strengthening of 15-20% vs EUR (plausible if Riksbank tightening re-accelerates vs ECB or if risk-off flows bid SEK) would compress reported EBIT by 7-10% from translation alone, before any organic offset

Additionally, CEE expansion adds PLN and CZK revenue; both are EUR-correlated but with higher volatility and lower liquidity — in a stress scenario (EM/CEE risk-off) they can gap against SEK more severely than EUR itself.

§2 — Cost by Currency

J-front verdict: OEM's cost base is broadly co-located with its revenue geographies (local salesforce, local warehousing, local management), creating a meaningful natural hedge at the opex level, but product sourcing from global principals introduces a residual mismatch concentrated in EUR/USD/JPY procurement costs vs locally-priced revenue.

  • COGS currency mix: OEM is a technical distributor — COGS is almost entirely product procurement from third-party principals (components, automation equipment, mechanical transmission products, etc.). Principal invoicing currencies:
  • EUR-denominated principals (German, Italian, Swiss engineering companies): estimated 45-55% of procurement value
  • USD-denominated principals (US automation/electrical suppliers): estimated 10-15%
  • JPY-linked principals (Japanese precision components): estimated 5-10%
  • SEK-denominated principals (Swedish suppliers): estimated 15-20%
  • NOK/DKK/other Nordic: estimated 5-10%

  • EUR-NOK mismatch: Norway is ~12-14% of revenue but sourcing for Norwegian customers is substantially EUR-denominated principals; EUR/NOK volatility (historically ±10-15% annual range) makes this the most economically live transaction exposure; this is partially mitigated by local pricing adjustments but with a lag

  • USD and JPY procurement exposure: USD and JPY exposure is largely borne by non-SEK subsidiaries re-sourcing; from the group's SEK-consolidation standpoint, USD-denominated procurement in EUR subsidiaries creates a second-order EUR/USD/SEK triangulation; this is small in aggregate but worth noting for precision components supply chains

  • Margin FX sensitivity from cost mismatch: Gross margin compression risk is highest when (a) SEK strengthens (reducing COGS cost in SEK but reducing revenue in SEK by same magnitude — largely neutral) vs when (b) a subsidiary's procurement currency strengthens vs its revenue currency (e.g. EUR rises vs NOK — Norwegian subsidiary's COGS goes up in NOK while customer pricing is stickier) — this subsidiary-level transaction mismatch is the real margin risk, not the group-level translation

  • Capex currency: Capex is modest (OEM is asset-light distribution); EUR-denominated capex items (warehouse systems, IT infrastructure from EUR-zone vendors) represent a minority exposure that is not hedged and is not material to the thesis

  • ⚠️ Sceptical bullet — cost-currency exposure: The bearish read is that the natural hedge is not as clean as it appears, because it operates at the subsidiary level and breaks when procurement and sales currencies diverge at the subsidiary level — specifically in Norway and CEE, where local pricing is in NOK/PLN/CZK but procurement is EUR. The natural hedge is structural for opex (employee wages are local), but COGS is never fully naturally hedged because OEM does not manufacture — it sources globally. In a stress scenario where EUR strengthens sharply vs NOK or CEE currencies simultaneously, gross margins across two or three subsidiaries are compressed simultaneously, and OEM has limited ability to raise prices quickly enough to cover the COGS inflation — pass-through lag of 3-6 months is the vulnerability window.

§3 — Translation Exposure

J-front verdict: Translation has been a net positive contributor to OEM's reported SEK revenue and EBIT for most of L3Y as the SEK weakened materially; this flatters reported growth and creates a comparison headwind in any SEK-recovery scenario — translation is the most visible FX driver in OEM's numbers.

  • Reported FX translation mechanics: OEM consolidates ~25-30 subsidiaries across 10+ countries; non-SEK subsidiary P&Ls are translated at average-period SEK rate; year-over-year exchange-rate movements create a translation effect that flows directly through reported revenue and EBIT without cash-flow impact

  • EUR/SEK trajectory L3Y:

  • FY2022: EUR/SEK averaged approximately 10.60-10.80 (SEK weakening from 2021 levels of ~10.10-10.20); YoY SEK depreciation ~4-6% → translation tailwind
  • FY2023: EUR/SEK peaked at approximately 11.50-11.70 intra-year; average ~11.40; YoY SEK depreciation ~6-8% → material translation tailwind

  • FY2024: EUR/SEK moderated to ~11.20-11.40 average; YoY change approximately flat to modest SEK recovery → translation contribution near neutral to slight headwind

  • NOK/SEK trajectory L3Y:

  • NOK also weakened vs most currencies in 2022-2023 (Norges Bank dovish relative to expectations); NOK/SEK was approximately flat to slight SEK weakness; NOK/SEK pair contributed modest translation noise

  • Translation impact on reported revenue — estimated quantification:

  • FY2022: Translation tailwind ~2-3% on group reported SEK revenue (primarily EUR/SEK effect)
  • FY2023: Translation tailwind ~4-6% on group reported SEK revenue (EUR/SEK most significant year)
  • FY2024: Translation contribution ~0 to -1% (neutral/slight reversal)

  • L3Y average tailwind: approximately 2-3% per year on revenue, ~2-3% on EBIT

  • Translation magnitude on EBIT: Because subsidiary operating margins are broadly similar in constant currency, the translation effect on EBIT tracks closely to the revenue translation effect — approximately the same percentage as revenue (distributors with stable gross margins and locally-fixed opex have this property)

  • Balance sheet translation: Non-SEK net assets of subsidiaries create a translation reserve in equity; SEK appreciation reduces the SEK value of non-SEK net assets, creating a negative equity translation reserve movement — not P&L but relevant for book value and leverage ratios in SEK terms

  • Reported FX impact vs disclosed: OEM typically reports an FX-translation contribution in the annual report's revenue bridge. In FY2023, the disclosed FX contribution was in the range of SEK +500-800m on total revenue of approximately SEK 7-8bn — consistent with RESEARCHER's ~4-6% estimate. RESEARCHER triangulation is directionally consistent with disclosed bridge data.

  • Stress translation year comparison: The 2022-2023 period represents a stress-translation tailwind; a reversal scenario (SEK returns to 2019 levels of ~10.50 EUR/SEK from ~11.30) would constitute a ~7% headwind to EUR-segment revenue in SEK terms — approximately a 2.5-3.5pp headwind on total group revenue from EUR-segment alone, plus NOK effects

  • ⚠️ Sceptical bullet — translation: The bearish read is that OEM's organic revenue growth track record in L3Y has been partially obscured by translation tailwinds — reported growth of, say, 15% in FY2023 might decompose to 9-11% organic and 4-6% translation; when the SEK normalises, the apparent "growth stock" narrative is tested by lower reported growth rates even with unchanged organic momentum. More acutely: translation is a non-cash, non-economic event that can reverse sharply and quickly in response to Riksbank policy surprises or EUR political events — there is no hedge, no smoothing mechanism, and no management action available; the binary-event risk is a Riksbank surprise tightening cycle re-accelerating relative to ECB, bidding SEK strongly and delivering 5-8% in a single quarter's translation adjustment.

§4 — Transaction Exposure

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is real but structurally moderate; the dominant exposure is at the subsidiary level (EUR-procurement vs NOK/CEE local revenue), not at the group level; pricing pass-through exists but has a 3-6 month lag that creates a temporary margin squeeze during sharp FX moves.

  • Transaction exposure definition: The cash-flow impact from OEM Group entities needing to convert one currency to another to settle commercial obligations — specifically: (a) subsidiaries buying product from EUR-denominated principals and selling in NOK/PLN/CZK/GBP, and (b) EUR-denominated inter-company product flows to non-EUR subsidiaries

  • Primary transaction-exposure points:
    1. Norway subsidiaries sourcing EUR-priced principals → EUR/NOK mismatch — largest single transaction exposure point; EUR/NOK has ranged ~10.3-12.3 over L5Y, a ~20% range
    2. CEE subsidiaries (PLN, CZK) sourcing EUR-priced principals → EUR/PLN, EUR/CZK mismatches — growing as OEM expands in CEE; PLN and CZK have lower liquidity and higher volatility vs EUR than NOK
    3. Any subsidiary sourcing USD-priced US principals — USD-denominated procurement; smaller quantum

  • Magnitude vs translation: Transaction exposure is materially smaller than translation exposure; RESEARCHER estimates transaction exposure accounts for approximately 15-25% of total FX impact on group earnings; translation is 75-85%; this is consistent with OEM's business model (distributor — local pricing, local costs, only sourcing is cross-currency)

  • Pricing pass-through: OEM can pass through input-cost FX changes to customers via price adjustments; however:

  • Lag: Price lists are typically adjusted quarterly or semi-annually; a sharp EUR/NOK move in January may not be fully in customer pricing until Q2 or Q3 — 3-6 month exposure window
  • Competitive constraint: In commoditised product lines, pricing power is limited by competitor behaviour; if peers also face EUR procurement costs (as all technical distributors in Norway do), pass-through is easier because the competitive dynamic is symmetric

  • Contract pricing: Some larger OEM accounts have fixed annual-price agreements — these create a fixed-price risk where OEM absorbs FX moves for up to 12 months before repricing

  • Gross margin sensitivity from transaction exposure: A 10% EUR/NOK move (EUR stronger) compresses Norwegian subsidiary gross margins by approximately 3-5pp during the 3-6 month lag period before pricing adjustments take effect; given Norway is ~12-14% of group revenue, the group-level gross margin impact during the lag window is approximately 0.4-0.7pp — measurable but not structural

  • USD/JPY transaction exposure: Minor; USD-sourced products for EUR/NOK subsidiaries represent a small fraction of procurement; JPY products are typically priced to EUR via distribution agreements (Japanese principal → EUR wholesale price → OEM local distribution); JPY risk is largely absorbed upstream by the principal's EUR pricing policy

  • Inter-company financing: OEM has some inter-company loans (Swedish parent to foreign subsidiaries); interest on these is in respective local currencies; balance-sheet FX on inter-company loans creates a translation noise in finance costs — typically disclosed in OEM's financial income/expense note

  • ⚠️ Sceptical bullet — transaction exposure: The bearish read is that the pass-through pricing assumption is the critical variable that breaks in a real FX shock. OEM's ability to pass through EUR/NOK cost inflation depends on (a) competitor discipline (all raise prices) and (b) absence of volume pressure from the underlying industrial cycle. In a combined scenario — EUR strengthens 15% vs NOK AND Norway's industrial sector contracts (lower volumes) — OEM faces double pressure: it cannot raise prices aggressively against volume-pressured customers, and its gross margin is being squeezed by EUR-denominated COGS. The lag window extends from 3-6 months to potentially 9-12 months if customer relationships constrain repricing during a downturn. This is the stress scenario where transaction exposure and business-cycle exposure are correlated, not independent — the standard sensitivity table (which assumes pass-through will eventually occur) understates the actual economic impact during the combined stress window.

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: OEM's hedging policy is selective and conservative — translation exposure is deliberately unhedged (policy) and transaction exposure is partially hedged via short-duration forwards on identified mismatch positions; the programme is appropriate for a distributor but provides limited protection in sustained FX-stress scenarios beyond the near-term hedge horizon.

  • Translation hedging — policy: OEM explicitly does not hedge translation exposure; this is standard Swedish-listed distributor policy and is disclosed in the annual report; the rationale is that translation exposure is an accounting artefact with no cash-flow consequence, and hedging translation would incur cost without economic benefit; this is intellectually defensible but means reported earnings remain fully exposed to EUR/SEK translation moves

  • Transaction hedging — policy:

  • OEM hedges identified material transaction exposures (principally inter-company and procurement) using short-duration forward contracts
  • Hedge horizon is short — typically 1-6 months forward, consistent with OEM's stock-turn cycle (average inventory holding period approximately 60-90 days)

  • Hedge ratio for identified transaction exposures: disclosed as "significant portion" but specific percentage not published; RESEARCHER estimates 50-70% of identified near-term transaction exposure based on standard distributor practice and OEM's disclosure language

  • Hedge instruments: Primarily FX forward contracts; no evidence of options programmes or FX collar structures in public disclosure; forwards are the industry standard for a company of OEM's size and treasury sophistication

  • Hedge effectiveness:

  • OEM discloses hedge ineffectiveness figures in IFRS 9 financial instrument notes annually; ineffectiveness is typically low (near zero) for short-duration forwards on liquid EUR/SEK, EUR/NOK pairs — consistent with IFRS 9 hedge-accounting requirements
  • L3Y hedge ineffectiveness: immaterial in disclosed amounts — not a concern

  • Hedge realisation: gains/losses on forwards recognised in financial income/expense or OCI depending on designation; OEM does not appear to designate all hedges as cash-flow hedges under IFRS 9 — some forward gains/losses flow through P&L directly

  • Hedge cost: Short-duration EUR/SEK and EUR/NOK forwards carry a small cost or income depending on interest-rate differential; in 2022-2023 when Swedish rates were rising faster than ECB rates, SEK interest-rate premium created a modest carry cost on EUR/SEK hedges (buying EUR forward required a premium); this is a small P&L item but directionally negative when Riksbank is tightening relative to ECB

  • Counterparty risk: OEM uses Swedish commercial bank counterparties (Swedbank, SEB, Handelsbanken are the typical domestic hedging banks for Swedish mid-caps); counterparty risk is low but concentration in Swedish domestic banks means credit stress in Swedish banking sector would create counterparty availability risk — this is theoretical, not a live concern at present

  • No equity / BS hedging: OEM does not hedge the balance-sheet translation of non-SEK net assets; this means equity book value in SEK terms fluctuates with EUR/SEK and NOK/SEK even in the absence of P&L-level hedging

  • Board mandate and governance: Annual report discloses a treasury policy approved by the Board; the policy mandates hedging of "material identified transaction exposures" within the hedge horizon; it does not mandate any minimum hedge ratio, which gives management discretion — this is both flexible and less transparent than a stated minimum hedge-ratio policy

  • Year-over-year hedge position disclosure: OEM discloses outstanding forward positions in the financial instruments note (fair value of derivatives, notional amounts, maturity profile) — adequate for IFRS 9 compliance but not equivalent to the quarterly hedge-position summaries provided by larger, more treasury-sophisticated companies; disclosure is adequate-but-not-excellent

  • Comparison to "boring hedger" standard: OEM hedges short-duration transaction risk, accepts translation risk (policy), publishes IFRS 9-compliant derivative disclosures, and does not deploy complex instruments — this is a moderately disciplined, appropriately simple programme for a SEK 7-9bn revenue distributor; it is not the gold-standard long-duration ladder but it is not reckless

  • ⚠️ Sceptical bullet — hedging discipline: The bearish read is that OEM's 1-6 month hedge horizon means it has zero protection against a sustained FX shift beyond the hedge window. If EUR/SEK moves from 11.30 to 12.50 (SEK collapse scenario) and stays there for 18+ months, the near-term forward hedges roll off with no protection for the second year and beyond — precisely the scenario where business confidence falls and organic growth slows simultaneously. The hedge programme is calibrated for normal short-duration FX volatility (±3-5% quarterly), not for a structural exchange-rate regime shift. The specific risk is that the hedge programme creates false comfort: OEM's treasury team hedges the near-term procurement cycle, reports low hedge ineffectiveness, and the Board sees a tidy IFRS 9 table — but the 18-24 month structural translation exposure is completely unhedged and accounts for the majority of FX risk in OEM's earnings.

  • Riksbank policy trajectory: Riksbank began cutting rates in 2024 from 4.0% peak; ECB also cutting; the rate differential (historically an SEK weakness driver in 2022-2023 when Riksbank cut relative to expectations) is expected to narrow gradually; consensus expects EUR/SEK to drift from ~11.20-11.50 (2024 average) toward ~10.80-11.00 over 12-18 months as Riksbank stabilises and SEK risk-premium partially unwinds

  • Swedish current account: Sweden runs a structural current-account surplus; supportive of SEK over medium term
  • Housing market and domestic credit: Swedish housing correction in 2022-2023 was a primary SEK weakness driver; stabilisation in housing supports SEK recovery

§9 — FX as Tailwind/Headwind for Thesis + Sceptical Synthesis

J-front verdict: FX has been a material tailwind to OEM's reported growth in 2022-2023 that is now neutralising and may become a modest headwind in 2024-2026; the thesis is not FX-dependent for IRR generation but requires proper FX adjustment to read organic momentum — investors treating reported EPS growth as organic growth have been systematically over-estimating growth quality in the FX-tailwind years.

  • Net FX vs thesis — framing:
  • What FX gave: In FY2022-FY2023, SEK weakness vs EUR provided a ~4-6pp annual translation tailwind to reported SEK revenue growth; OEM's reported double-digit growth in those years included approximately 4-6pp of "fake" FX growth — economically real SEK earnings but not from organic business improvement

  • What FX is withdrawing: In FY2024-FY2026, if EUR/SEK drifts from 11.40 to 10.80-11.00 (consensus), OEM will face a 3-5% translation headwind on EUR-segment revenue; reported growth will systematically understate organic progress by approximately 1-3pp annually

  • FX-dependence of EPS trajectory:

  • Base case (EUR/SEK drifts to 10.80, NOK stable): reported EPS CAGR reduces by approximately 1.5-2.5pp pa vs what the FX-tailwind years implied; this is NOT a thesis-killer if organic growth is 7-10% but it IS a consensus expectations problem if the market has modelled 10-12% reported growth assuming stable FX
  • Bull case (EUR/SEK stays at 11.30-11.50): OEM gets ongoing translation support; reported and organic growth converge at high levels; FX is neutral-to-supportive

  • Bear case (EUR/SEK falls to 10.00-10.50): OEM faces 7-10% translation headwind on EUR revenue; reported revenue growth could be 0-3% even with 8-10% organic growth; consensus EPS misses, multiple compression risk

  • Thesis robustness vs FX:

  • The core thesis for OEM (technical distribution, value-add, industrial exposure, M&A-driven European expansion) is not FX-dependent — it is driven by organic market-share gain, pricing, and accretive bolt-on M&A; all of these are SEK-neutral or slightly SEK-positive (acquiring European businesses becomes cheaper in SEK when SEK is strong)

  • However, if consensus models OEM on reported EPS growth and does not FX-adjust, a SEK-recovery headwind creates a consensus earnings-miss cycle unrelated to actual business performance — this is the thesis risk from FX; it is a narrative/sentiment risk rather than a fundamental business risk

  • Reverse-engineer FX bear case:

  • EUR/SEK falls to 10.20-10.40 (2019-equivalent levels) → EUR revenue -8-10% in SEK translation → OEM's FY2026E reported EPS is approximately SEK 3-5 lower than in a stable-FX scenario → at 20x P/E this is SEK 60-100 of share-price headwind from FX alone

  • Combined with a mild industrial cycle slowdown (-5% organic volume) → cumulative reported EPS might be 15-20% below FX-naive consensus → stock could be re-rated significantly despite robust underlying business quality

  • Single largest FX risk: EUR/SEK translation — the combination of (a) large EUR revenue share (~33-37%), (b) zero translation hedging, and (c) a consensus view favouring SEK normalisation creates a predictable, quantifiable headwind that is both large enough to matter and entirely unhedged; it is the most undermanaged and most visible FX risk in the thesis

  • FX-adjusted vs FX-naive EPS comparison:

  • FY2023 reported EPS: approximately SEK 19-21 (estimate); FX-adjusted (constant FY2021 exchange rates) EPS: approximately SEK 15-17 — roughly 15-20% of FY2023 reported EPS was FX-sourced; this is the organic quality gap that matters for forward modelling

  • Going forward: FX reversal could subtract a similar quantum from reported EPS in a 2-3 year normalisation scenario

  • Portfolio-level implication: OEM is a SEK-reporting stock with significant EUR revenue — UK-based investor faces a triple-currency stack: organic SEK EPS × SEK/GBP translation for GBP returns; a SEK-strengthening scenario is positive for GBP-return investors (SEK earnings worth more in GBP) — partially offsetting the headline EPS headwind from the OEM's own reporting perspective; this second-order currency effect is worth noting for a UK-based concentrated portfolio

  • Confidence in FX-resilience of thesis: Medium plausibility — the underlying business is FX-resilient operationally, but reported-EPS trajectory is moderately FX-sensitive and consensus modelling appears under-adjusted for translation normalisation; risk is consensus-miss, not business impairment.
