Section 3 – What the company does 21 findings

Section summary
  1. Humana operates a defensive, publicly funded care model in the Nordics, holding market-leading positions in personal assistance and specialized care.

Core Segment Operations

1.
Dominant Swedish engine: Sweden remains the primary market, contributing 64% of group net revenue through specialized individual care and personal assistance operations
2.
Diversified Norwegian business: Norway generates 23% of net revenue, delivering specialized child care and personal assistance services under decentralized municipal agreements
3.
Restructured Finnish segment: Finland generates 13% of group net revenue, focusing exclusively on specialized disability and child care after divesting its low-margin elderly care portfolio
4.
Complete Danish divestment: Operations in Denmark have been completely eliminated from the group after management signed a divestment agreement in mid-2024
5.
High-complexity individual care: The Individual and Family segment in Sweden represents 37% of revenue, providing highly specialized institutional placements for youth and adults
6.
Predictable personal assistance: Personal Assistance in Sweden accounts for 26% of net revenue, offering crucial daily support under government-reimbursed frameworks
7.
Niche Swedish elderly care: Elderly care represents a small but highly profitable 10% of Swedish revenue, operating modern facilities running close to full occupancy

Revenue Models and Moats

8.
Contractual revenue streams: Revenue is highly recurring and predictable, with over 95% of sales derived from long-term, index-regulated municipal contracts
9.
Inflation indexation mechanisms: Most municipal agreements incorporate price indexation clauses designed to offset structural wage inflation and administrative cost increases
10.
Extremely low concentration: Customer concentration risks are virtually non-existent, with services delivered directly to approximately 8,000 individual welfare recipients
11.
Deep relational moat: Competitive advantages are anchored in high switching costs, as care relationships are deeply personal and difficult to disrupt
12.
Formidable permit barriers: Strict regulatory vetting by Nordic inspectors creates immense barriers to entry, preventing smaller unvetted competitors from easily entering the market
13.
Welfare suitability criteria: Swedish regulators conduct exhaustive suitability audits of private owners and managers to completely eliminate fraudulent or substandard operators

Market Structure and Dynamics

14.
Demographic demand driver: Structural demand is expanding rapidly, with the Scandinavian population aged 80 plus projected to grow by 50% between 2020 and 2030
15.
Expanding private provision: Private operators are taking an increasing share of care provision, particularly as public municipalities struggle with severe capacity constraints
16.
Massive capacity deficit: Sweden alone is estimated to require up to 460 new nursing homes by 2032, presenting a huge long-term pipeline for private developers
17.
Public outsourcing trends: Private care providers account for 28% of the Swedish welfare market, 21% of Norway, and 59% of Finland's addressable care market
18.
Fragmented competitive landscape: Humana operates alongside listed peers Ambea and Attendo, holding the leading position in Swedish personal assistance
19.
Durable market position: Humana's brand equity was validated after being named Sweden's most sustainable care provider for the fourth consecutive year in 2026
20.
Other side of the story: Left-wing political shifts could trigger renewed debate over profit restrictions for private welfare providers, potentially capping operating margins or banning private municipal contracts

Section 7 – Key risks and the other side 11 findings

Section summary
  1. While regulatory permit risks and wage inflation remain active threats, the severe downside bear case is highly overblown by the market.

Regulatory and Operational Vulnerabilities

1.
Formidable regulatory risks: The company requires extensive permits from Nordic health authorities, exposing Humana to catastrophic risk if any license is revoked
2.
Catastrophic permit revocation: The Swedish Health and Social Care Inspectorate previously revoked Humana's personal assistance license, triggering a heavy 30% customer outflow
3.
Delayed indexation pressure: Pricing indexation under municipal contracts may fail to immediately offset steep wage increases negotiated under Nordic collective bargaining agreements
4.
Severe staffing shortages: Chronic labor shortages across the healthcare sector could force Humana to hire expensive temporary agency staff, severely damaging margins
5.
Strained municipal budgets: Tight fiscal budgets at the municipal level could lead to cautious placement decisions, particularly within child and youth care
6.
Accrued goodwill risk: A massive SEK 4,470m in goodwill creates significant impairment risks if the Swedish or Finnish segments fail to hit targets

Failure Modes and Early Warning Signals

7.
Primary failure mode: The absolute worst-case failure mode would be a renewed, successful permit revocation by the Swedish Health and Social Care Inspectorate
8.
Dangerous false friend: A temporary reduction in corporate overhead costs could mask underlying structural understaffing and severe long-term customer volume declines
9.
Critical warning signal: Investors must closely monitor monthly net customer inflows within Swedish Personal Assistance as the definitive early-warning indicator
10.
Other side of the story: The bear case is highly overblown because the administrative court's 2023 ruling established an exceptionally high legal threshold for permit cancellations, protecting Humana from future arbitrary regulatory actions
