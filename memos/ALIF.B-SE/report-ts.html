Section 3 – What the company does 12 findings

Section summary
  1. Operating as a decentralized European distributor, AddLife holds a highly profitable niche position insulated from macroeconomic business cycles.

Segment Distribution and Operational Focus

1.
Resilient segment breakdown: AddLife operates through two main business segments, Labtech and Medtech, which accounted for net sales of SEK 985.00 million and SEK 1,661.00 million respectively in Q1 2026. This distribution reflects a gradual shift towards hospital-focused medical technologies over laboratory research products
2.
Labtech product offering: The Labtech segment provides advanced diagnostic instruments, assays, reagents, and clinical consumables to university labs, public health diagnostics, and biopharma research departments. Consumables sales in this segment are highly stable, acting as a recurring baseline of revenue
3.
Medtech clinical niche: The Medtech segment focuses on specialized medical devices, orthopedic implants, and homecare assistive technology, which are heavily linked to elective surgery volumes. These clinical solutions require specialized technical support and training for the hospital staff using them
4.
Contractual revenue model: The revenue stream is highly predictable, with approximately 80.00% of sales tied to long-term public procurement contracts and recurring diagnostic test reagents. This high percentage of recurring revenue significantly insulates the company's baseline cash flow from general economic cycles
5.
Strategic product mix: Management is continuously pruning lower-margin transactional equipment and instrument lines to focus strictly on high-margin proprietary consumables and advanced technical niches. This active portfolio optimization has been a key driver behind the recent group EBITA margin expansion

Decentralized Economics and Competitive Positioning

6.
Highly fragmented market: The European medtech market is extremely fragmented with over 33,000 active companies, providing an extensive and sustainable pipeline for localized M&A. This fragmentation presents a multi-year consolidation runway for nimble distributors
7.
Customer and supplier profile: The company serves public hospital laboratories, research centers, and care facilities, with no material customer concentration because accounts are highly localized across Europe. This highly diversified customer base minimizes commercial credit risks
8.
Strong switching costs: A durable competitive advantage is driven by high customer switching costs, as hospital diagnostic systems and specialized surgical equipment require extensive staff training and proprietary consumables. This locks in long-term revenues, as switching to competitors is both clinically disruptive and economically expensive
9.
Decentralized management economics: AddLife leverages an agile, decentralized organizational structure with 85 autonomous entrepreneur-led operating subsidiaries, which minimizes post-merger corporate overhead. Local managers retain full ownership of their P&L while benefiting from the consolidated group's financial backing and shared back-office tools
10.
High value-add services: The company differentiates itself through an extensive local service, calibration, and support offering, which strengthens supplier relationships and secures customer loyalty. This technical expertise makes AddLife the preferred distributor for global OEMs seeking to expand in Europe
11.
Other side of the story: Large medical manufacturers represent a material disintermediation risk if they bypass distributors to go direct, as seen when the UK endoscopy supplier partner chose to go direct. This structural shift resulted in a SEK 140.00 million revenue headwind during the 2025 financial year
