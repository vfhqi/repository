KEY FINDINGS - Structurally exposed: AddLife reports in SEK but generates the majority of revenue in EUR and Scandinavian currencies (NOK, DKK, SEK-outside-Sweden); USD and GBP are secondary but non-trivial — the company is a multi-currency translator with limited transaction risk in the classical sense

  • Translation-dominant: The primary FX risk is translation (converting foreign-subsidiary P&L into SEK), not transaction (cash-flow mismatches at individual entity level); subsidiaries typically sell in their local currency and source partly locally, reducing entity-level transaction exposure
  • EUR is the swing factor: EUR/SEK is the single most consequential rate — EUR-denominated revenue is estimated at 35-45% of group and most EUR subsidiaries carry EUR costs, making EUR/SEK the dominant P&L lever
  • Hedging policy is minimal and largely transaction-focused: AddLife states it hedges material transaction exposures (forward contracts) but explicitly does NOT hedge translation exposure; no disclosed hedge ratio, no disclosed hedge horizon, no hedging programme transparency comparable to a manufacturing peer — this sits in the lower tier of hedging disclosure quality
  • FX was a tailwind 2021-2023, turning more neutral/headwind in 2024-2025: SEK weakness versus EUR/USD through 2021-2023 flattered reported SEK revenue and EBITDA; SEK partial recovery in 2024 has compressed this tailwind; consensus suggests EUR/SEK and USD/SEK will be range-bound to modestly SEK-strengthening over N12-24M, creating a mild headwind to reported numbers
  • Sensitivity: ±10% EUR/SEK move ≈ ±3-4% group EBITDA impact (translation), ±10% USD/SEK ≈ ±1% group EBITDA — numbers are manageable individually but combined-shock scenarios are more meaningful
  • No peer comparison possible given absent cohort data; sector-general principles applied to rank AddLife's hedging discipline — it scores weakly on duration/transparency, acceptably on natural-hedge structuring
  • Thesis implication: FX is a moderate, not dominant, risk to the EPS trajectory; the bigger FX risk is a sustained SEK-strengthening scenario (reverting toward pre-2020 levels vs EUR) which would create a multi-year structural translation headwind that consensus may not fully model

§1 — Revenue by Currency

J-front verdict: AddLife's revenue is materially non-SEK (estimated 60-70% of revenue in EUR + Scandinavian non-SEK currencies + USD), making it a structural SEK-reporting/foreign-earning translator — concentrated in EUR, not broadly diversified

Revenue currency analysis

  • Reporting vs functional mismatch: AddLife reports in SEK but operates through ~150+ subsidiaries across Europe, each with their own functional currency; revenue is generated locally in each market and translated into SEK at average rates
  • Geographic revenue split (disclosed, FY2024): AddLife reports at two business-area levels — Labtech and Medtech — and provides geographic disclosure primarily as Nordic vs Rest of Europe; precise currency-level revenue is not disclosed on a currency-by-currency basis in the AR face; the table below is reconstructed from geographic revenue data + country-level presence
  • Nordic revenue: Sweden , Norway , Denmark , Finland — collectively ~50-55% of group revenue based on FY2024 geographic breakdowns; within this, Finland is EUR-functional; Sweden is SEK (reporting currency, no translation effect); Norway and Denmark introduce NOK/DKK
  • Rest of Europe: Germany, Italy, UK, Netherlands, Belgium, Austria, Switzerland, Poland, Czech Republic, etc. — collectively ~40-45% of group revenue; predominantly EUR-functional, with GBP and CHF (Switzerland) as secondary non-EUR European currencies
  • USD exposure: Primarily indirect — AddLife purchases some products from US vendors (medical devices, lab instruments) denominated in USD; direct USD revenue is small (no material US sales operations); USD matters primarily on the cost side

§2 — Cost by Currency

J-front verdict: Cost-currency mix is partially but imperfectly matched to revenue — structural natural hedging at the subsidiary level, with a residual USD transaction mismatch from US-sourced products that creates margin sensitivity

Cost currency analysis

  • Cost structure overview: AddLife is a distributor/value-added reseller of medical devices and laboratory instruments; gross margin is ~37-40% (FY2024); COGS represents ~60-63% of revenue; opex (SG&A + R&D) is ~25-28% of revenue; capex is modest (~1.5-2% of revenue) given the asset-light distribution model

  • COGS currency mix:

  • The majority of COGS is product procurement from OEM suppliers — a mix of European (EUR-denominated), US (USD-denominated), Japanese (JPY-denominated), and Nordic-domestic suppliers
  • Estimated EUR-denominated COGS: ~40-50% of total COGS (European OEM suppliers, local European distributors)
  • Estimated USD-denominated COGS: ~15-25% of total COGS (US medical device manufacturers — Abcam, Bio-Techne, Thermo Fisher, Roper products etc. — priced in USD or USD-linked EUR contracts)
  • Estimated SEK-denominated COGS: ~15-20% (Swedish domestic subsidiary procurement)

  • Other (NOK, DKK, GBP-sourced): ~10-15% — Scandinavian and UK suppliers

  • Opex currency mix:

  • Personnel costs dominate opex; employees are located in the same geographies as the revenue — so opex currency mix broadly mirrors revenue currency mix
  • EUR opex: ~35-40% (Continental Europe staff + offices)
  • SEK opex: ~28-32% (Swedish headquarter + domestic operations)
  • NOK/DKK opex: ~15-18%
  • GBP opex: ~3-5%

  • Central group costs (Stockholm HQ) are predominantly SEK

  • USD structural mismatch: The primary cost-currency mismatch is the USD-denominated procurement of US-origin lab/med products sold into non-USD markets (primarily EUR, NOK, SEK markets); this is a transaction exposure — the subsidiaries buy in USD and sell in local currency; when USD/EUR strengthens, cost base rises while pricing must be renegotiated with customers (typically annual price-list cycles with 3-6 month lag)

Sceptical bullet — cost-currency: The "natural hedge" narrative at the group level breaks down at the entity level — individual subsidiaries (e.g. a Norwegian Labtech subsidiary selling in NOK but sourcing instruments in USD) carry concentrated transaction exposure that the consolidated natural-hedge ratio obscures; in a simultaneous USD-strengthening + NOK/EUR-weakening scenario (not rare — risk-off episodes often produce both), the subsidiary-level margin squeeze would be acute and the 3-12 month pricing lag would mean the hit flows through with delay, making two consecutive half-years of margin compression visible even as the FX rate itself recovers

§3 — Translation Exposure

Translation exposure analysis

  • Mechanism: AddLife consolidates ~150+ foreign subsidiaries; each subsidiary's P&L is translated at average annual SEK rate; balance sheets are translated at closing rates; translation differences go to OCI (not P&L), but the revenue/EBITDA P&L lines are directly exposed to average-rate changes

  • FY2023 reported revenue growth: ~+10-12%; FX contribution ~+2-3pp; organic excl. FX and M&A closer to +5-7%

  • FY2024 reported revenue growth: ~+3-5%; FX was a mild drag of -1-2pp; underlying organic broadly in line with or slightly ahead of reported

  • Balance-sheet translation: AddLife carries significant goodwill and intangibles on its balance sheet (~SEK 12-15bn, representing ~60-65% of total assets FY2024) from acquisitions made in foreign currencies; when SEK strengthens, the SEK-translated value of these assets falls, flowing through OCI and reducing equity; this is balance-sheet translation exposure, not P&L, but it can affect leverage ratios and debt covenants indirectly — important for a net-debt-levered acquirer

  • OCI translation reserve: AddLife's cumulative translation reserve in equity is a barometer of accumulated SEK weakness periods; the reserve was positive (gains) through 2021-2023 as SEK weakened; a sustained SEK strengthening unwinds this over time — not P&L, but equity-reducing

Sceptical bullet — translation: The FY2021-2023 translation tailwind may have embedded a false growth signal in analyst models — three consecutive years of ~2-5% p.a. FX boost to reported revenue creates a CAGR that overstates underlying business momentum; if SEK retraces toward its pre-2020 historical range vs EUR (approximately EUR/SEK 10.0-10.5 vs current ~11.4), the mechanical reversal over 2-3 years would create a sustained ~3-4% p.a. translation headwind that would compress reported revenue and EBITDA even if underlying organic growth is healthy — a consensus model anchored on the last 3-year reported CAGR without FX-stripping is structurally overestimating medium-term reported growth

§4 — Transaction Exposure

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is concentrated in USD-denominated product procurement, is structurally unhedged at the group level beyond forward contracts on individual subsidiary flows, and represents a margin risk of ~1.5-3% gross margin compression in a USD shock scenario

Transaction exposure analysis

  • Primary transaction exposure points:
    1. Subsidiary USD procurement: Norwegian, Swedish, Danish, German subsidiaries buying US-origin lab/medical instruments invoiced in USD, selling in local currency (NOK/SEK/DKK/EUR) — this is the dominant transaction exposure
    2. Intercompany flows: AddLife's parent-entity charges management fees and group services to subsidiaries; while denominated in SEK for Swedish subsidiaries, cross-border intercompany flows for international subsidiaries introduce minor exposures
    3. Acquisition earn-outs: Some deferred acquisition consideration is denominated in the selling currency (EUR, GBP), creating transactional P&L exposure on settlement

  • USD transaction exposure magnitude: Estimated USD procurement is ~10-15% of group COGS (~SEK 1.5-2.5bn per annum at FY2024 scale); a 10% USD/local-currency move against AddLife creates an unhedged gross profit hit of ~SEK 150-250m (±0.5-1.0% of group revenue, ±1.5-3% of gross profit) before pricing adjustments

  • Magnitude vs translation exposure: Transaction exposure from USD procurement is smaller in absolute SEK terms than translation exposure from EUR/SEK movement (EUR translation affects ~35-40% of revenue vs USD transaction affecting ~10-15% of COGS) — translation is the dominant risk; but transaction exposure is more "durable" in the sense that it directly compresses real cash margins, not just reported SEK numbers

  • Pricing pass-through capability:

  • AddLife serves public healthcare and research institutions; contracts are typically 1-3 year framework agreements with annual price adjustment mechanisms
  • In the Nordic markets, healthcare tender processes allow price escalation mechanisms but with lag — a sudden USD cost spike takes ~6-12 months to be reflected in contract renewals
  • In German and other Continental European markets, hospital procurement is similarly rigid; price adjustments require renegotiation cycles

  • Net assessment: Pricing pass-through exists but is lagged by 3-12 months; in a sharp/sustained USD appreciation, AddLife would absorb ~1-2 half-years of margin compression before pricing catches up

  • JPY transaction exposure: Certain Labtech instruments from Japanese vendors are JPY-priced (or USD-priced with JPY pass-through); the JPY has been highly volatile (moved ~40% vs SEK from 2021-2023); individual exposure is sub-5% of COGS but creates episodic margin noise in Labtech margins — has been a marginal tailwind as JPY weakened (lower yen-denominated procurement costs in SEK terms) but is now risk-of-reversal

  • Earn-out and deferred consideration: AddLife is an active acquirer; earn-out payments denominated in EUR and GBP create periodic transaction exposures on settlement that flow through the P&L as financial items; these are not systematically hedged and create minor lumpy FX gains/losses in the interest/financial line — not material to EBITDA but can distort EPS in any given year by SEK 20-50m

  • Natural offsets at entity level: Most entities have revenues and operating costs in the same currency — the Norwegian subsidiary earns NOK and pays staff in NOK — so entity-level transaction exposure is actually low for opex; the transaction exposure is specifically in the COGS supply chain (USD procurement) at entities that sell in non-USD currencies

Sceptical bullet — transaction: The "entity-level natural hedge" argument for low transaction exposure ignores that the USD procurement risk sits precisely where AddLife has least flexibility: COGS from branded US OEM suppliers is non-substitutable in the short run (3-5 year distribution agreements, regulatory approvals for alternatives, customer switching costs are high); in a USD +20-30% shock, AddLife cannot rapidly switch to EUR-sourced equivalents, so the pricing-pass-through is the only safety valve — and that valve has a lag and a ceiling (healthcare customers will eventually resist price increases, especially in public-sector tender markets where budgets are fixed annually)

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Hedging policy is minimal, transaction-focused, with no disclosed hedge ratio or horizon; translation is explicitly unhedged; discipline is below average for a European mid-cap with ~70% non-reporting-currency revenue — AddLife is not a "boring hedger"

Hedging policy analysis

  • Disclosed policy (AR2024 financial risk management note): AddLife discloses that foreign exchange risk is managed at group level and that "the Group uses forward exchange contracts to hedge material transaction exposures in foreign currencies"; translation exposure is explicitly stated as NOT hedged, in line with the company's policy that translation exposure is a long-term structural characteristic of a multinational business

  • Hedge mandate — qualitative assessment:

  • Transaction hedging: present but with limited specifics; "material" is undefined; no disclosed hedge ratio, no disclosed horizon, no disclosed instrument mix beyond "forward exchange contracts"
  • Translation hedging: none, by policy — standard for a buy-and-build European mid-cap

  • Policy transparency score: Low — among the least transparent hedging disclosures in the European medtech/labtech distribution space

  • Hedge ratio: Not disclosed — this is a significant transparency gap; the AR states forward contracts are used for "material" transaction exposures but no percentage of exposure hedged is given; analyst estimate from derivative-instrument disclosures in the balance sheet is that hedge book is modest — disclosed fair-value of derivative instruments in the AR notes is typically SEK 20-80m, consistent with hedging a fraction of total USD procurement exposure (at full-cover, a SEK 2bn USD procurement book would generate far larger mark-to-market swings)

  • Hedge horizon: Not disclosed — the AR does not state whether hedges are 3M, 6M, 12M rolling; given the small derivative book apparent from balance sheet disclosures, the most likely horizon is 3-6 months (short-duration)

  • Instruments: Disclosed as forward exchange contracts; no mention of options, collars, or cross-currency swaps; the instrument set is limited to plain-vanilla forwards — standard but not sophisticated

  • Translation hedging (absent): AddLife follows the standard European mid-cap approach of not hedging translation exposure on the grounds that (a) it is costly and complex given the number of subsidiary currencies, (b) it reverses over time, and (c) it would require taking on economic risk to offset accounting risk; this is defensible intellectually but leaves the P&L materially exposed to EUR/SEK multi-year trend moves

  • Hedge effectiveness L3Y:

  • FY2022-2024 AR notes show derivative fair-value positions consistent with a forward-contract book; no explicit hedge-effectiveness ratios are disclosed (IFRS 9 hedge accounting requires effectiveness testing but AddLife appears to apply cash-flow hedge accounting only to a subset of exposures)
  • The magnitude of realised gains/losses on derivatives in the income statement is small (typically SEK 10-50m per year in financial items) — this is consistent with a modest hedge book relative to total FX exposure, not a comprehensive programme

  • Conclusion: Hedging is better characterised as "partial opportunistic forward-cover on identified large procurement transactions" rather than a systematic rolling hedge programme

  • Hedge cost: Not explicitly broken out; forward points on SEK/EUR and SEK/USD have varied from neutral to mildly positive carry for SEK (when SEK interest rates were higher); not a material cost driver

  • Applying sector-general hedging principles (per D-RSR-33):

  • Duration: Short (estimated 3-6M) — weak; vulnerable to sustained FX shocks beyond the hedge horizon
  • Counter-cyclical vs pro-cyclical: Cannot assess without disclosed hedge-ratio timeseries; assumed neutral/slightly pro-cyclical (typical of ad-hoc hedgers — they hedge after shocks, not before)
  • Instrument mix: Forwards only — adequate but not sophisticated; no collar protection; no options upside preservation
  • Natural vs financial hedging: Natural hedging at entity opex level is reasonable; financial hedging programme is minimal
  • Transparency ("boring hedger" test): Fails — no quarterly hedge position, no disclosed ratio, no disclosed horizon, qualitative-only policy language; this is not a boring hedger in the positive sense

  • Disclosed policy vs realised: The small derivative book on the balance sheet is consistent with the policy of hedging "material" exposures only, but the word "material" is doing a lot of work; total FX transaction exposure likely far exceeds the small derivative book

  • Acquisition-related FX risk — partially managed: When AddLife acquires a foreign business for EUR or GBP consideration, it sometimes has a short acquisition-funding FX risk between deal signing and completion; this appears to be managed case-by-case (no systematic acquisition FX hedge programme disclosed)

Sceptical bullet — hedging: The phrase "material transaction exposures" in the hedging policy is the critical weasel-word; if management defines "material" as transactions above SEK 50m, a large proportion of the distributed USD-procurement cost stream (consisting of many smaller-value purchase orders) may fall below the materiality threshold and be entirely unhedged; the actual hedge coverage could be far below 50% of total transaction exposure, and there is no public disclosure that would allow verification — Richard is effectively buying an unquantified hedge programme with no accountability mechanism

§6 — Quantified Sensitivity Analysis

J-front verdict: EUR/SEK is the dominant single-currency sensitivity (~3-4% EBITDA per 10% move); USD/SEK is the secondary mismatch risk (~1-2% EBITDA per 10% move via transaction channel); combined EUR+USD stress is the tail risk; hedge break is at approximately ±15-20% move given the short hedge horizon

Sensitivity analysis

  • Methodology: Translation impact calculated as: (% revenue in currency X) × (FX move %) × (EBITDA margin / revenue, scaled for partial cost offset); transaction impact calculated separately for USD mismatch as: (USD cost % of total costs) × (FX move %) × (1 - pricing pass-through %) × (impact on EBITDA)

  • Base assumptions used in tables:

  • Group revenue FY2024: ~SEK 8.0-8.5bn (consensus estimate)
  • EBITDA margin: ~12-14% of revenue (adjusted, excl. amortisation of acquired intangibles)
  • EPS base: ~SEK 4.0-5.0 per share (adjusted, FY2024)
  • EUR revenue share: 38% (translation); EUR cost share: 42% (slight cost-heavy — net EUR position is slightly negative, i.e. EUR weakness is marginally negative on EBITDA margin)
  • USD revenue share: ~1%; USD cost share: ~12% ; net USD exposure: -11% of costs = structural short USD
  • NOK revenue: 11%; NOK cost: 8%; net NOK position: +3% of costs
  • Pricing pass-through : 50% within 12M (conservative); 75% within 24M (base)
  • Hedge cover: estimated 20-30% of USD transaction exposure (consistent with small derivative book)

  • Transaction (primarily USD/non-USD mismatch): ~25-30% of total FX sensitivity; does NOT automatically reverse; represents real margin compression until pricing adjusts; more durable and economically damaging per unit of move

  • Hedge break point: At an estimated 3-6M hedge horizon and ~20-30% hedge cover of transaction exposure:

  • Any FX move that sustains beyond 6 months is effectively unhedged from month 7 onward
  • A USD shock of +15% or greater exhausts the hedge book's protection for new procurement contracts within one quarterly procurement cycle

  • This is a duration shortfall problem — not a quantum (ratio) problem alone; even with 70% hedge ratio, a 6-month horizon leaves the company fully exposed to any FX move that sustains into the second half of the year
