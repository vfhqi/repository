A — Prefilled syringe CDMO contract delay [PRIMARY — HIGH plausibility]

What happened: Rovi's prefilled syringe manufacturing agreement with an unnamed "global pharmaceutical company" (announced Apr 2024; 100m units/year capacity at the San Sebastián de los Reyes facility) has been delayed. Routine manufacturing operations have not commenced as planned; they remain subject to regulatory authorisation. Q1 guidance revision explicitly cites "a delay in the initially expected commencement of routine manufacturing operations, which remain subject to regulatory authorisation, as well as increased uncertainty regarding anticipated demand."

Consensus deviation: This contract was a visible, publicly announced cornerstone of the 2026 growth story. The market had been pricing HSD/LDD revenue growth (8–12%+) for 2026 in part on the back of this production ramp. The delay eliminates that revenue pillar and introduces demand uncertainty on top — moving guidance to LSD/MSD (2–4%) is a cut of roughly 6–8pp on the forecast growth rate. Critically, the phrase "increased uncertainty regarding anticipated demand" goes beyond a simple timing slip — it suggests the counterparty's own demand picture has deteriorated, not just the regulatory schedule.

Plausibility: HIGH. The guidance announcement directly names this as the primary driver. Magnitude and timing align exactly with the 6-May spike.

Suggested follow-on: Identify the counterparty (circumstantial candidates: Moderna, a GLP-1 innovator, or a large biologics fill-and-finish customer — the 100m unit syringe capacity points toward a high-volume injectable); monitor CNMV and company disclosures for regulatory authorisation news; model revenue sensitivity — if the contract commences H2 2026 vs H1 2027, what does the 2026 vs 2027 revenue bridge look like? §4 — Synthesis

Most likely cause(s), ranked:

1. Prefilled syringe CDMO contract delay + demand uncertainty (primary): The single development that changes the investment case. This was not a discretionary R&D pivot or a managed transition — it is an external regulatory and demand uncertainty imposed on a contract the market had already priced. Eliminates the 2026 growth inflection thesis and pushes the recovery horizon into 2027 at earliest.

2. Q1 EBITDA collapse (compounding): The magnitude of EBITDA and net profit deterioration is worse than would be explained by the contract delay alone. It signals that the underlying cost base is not well-controlled during the expansion phase. This is the less-recoverable element — guidance cuts can be revised back up if contracts restart, but if the cost inflation is structural, the earnings power of the business at steady-state is lower than previously modelled.

3. Heparin deterioration (secondary): Adds to the negative skew but not a new thesis-breaker — this has been in plain sight for 18 months.

Counter-hypothesis: The delay is a timing slip, not a contract cancellation. The unnamed counterparty has not walked away — it is awaiting regulatory authorisation on a new manufacturing line, which is a normal (if frustrating) feature of pharma supply chain buildouts. If authorisation arrives H2 2026 or the contract kicks in H1 2027, the CDMO growth story restores. The €3.4bn PE interest for ROIS reported on ≈15-May suggests external value remains above the current stock price implied value — a potential floor or M&A kicker. The counter-hypothesis fails if: (a) the counterparty reduces or cancels demand beyond the timing delay, or (b) the cost base continues inflating without volume to absorb it.

What is still unexplained: The 24-Apr –4.5% decline lacks a confirmed catalyst. Also unknown: who the "global pharmaceutical company" counterparty is, and what specifically is gating the regulatory authorisation (is this a site inspection backlog, a process deviation, or a product-specific regulatory question — the answer determines the timeline).

⚡ Outlier flag: The Citeline "€3.4bn windfall" ROIS CDMO sale headline (≈15-May-26) is notable. Market cap post-drop is approximately €1.8bn; if the ROIS division alone is worth €3.4bn to a PE buyer, the implied rest-of-business (pharma + heparin + specialty) is being valued at effectively zero or negative by the market. Either the PE valuation is stale/optimistic, or the selloff has created a significant dislocation. Worth monitoring closely.
