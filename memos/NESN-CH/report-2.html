Change Force 1: New CEO Strategic Reset

Nestlé is undergoing a profound structural and strategic transformation following the abrupt departure of Mark Schneider in August 2024 and the subsequent appointments of Laurent Freixe and later Philipp Navratil. This leadership transition marks a decisive pivot away from the M&A-heavy, premiumization-focused approach of the Schneider era. Under Navratil, who took the helm in September 2025, the company is accelerating its "back to basics" strategy, prioritizing real internal growth , aggressive cost discipline, and targeted reinvestment behind core billionaire brands.

Reversing the M&A and Premiumization Strategy

During his eight-year tenure, Mark Schneider actively shaped Nestlé's portfolio by pursuing external acquisitions and driving premiumization, particularly in health science and coffee. However, this strategy ultimately left the company vulnerable to consumer downtrading amid a global cost-of-living crisis and post-pandemic inflation. The new leadership has explicitly shifted the organization's primary focus back to organic volume growth, known internally as RIG-led growth.

Rather than chasing expensive external acquisitions, Nestlé is now concentrating resources on expanding its existing high-potential growth platforms, which have been scaled to represent 30% of total group sales. The company's strategic framework seeks to capture both premium and affordable nutrition segments, acknowledging the increasingly polarized consumption environment. This represents a return to traditional, consumer-centric brand building, emphasizing market share retention and core product superiority over rapid portfolio turnover.

'Fuel for Growth' and Cost Discipline

To fund this volume recovery without structurally compressing margins, Nestlé has significantly upsized its cost-reduction initiatives under Navratil. The company originally targeted CHF 2.5 billion in savings but has since expanded the "Fuel for Growth" program to target CHF 3.0 billion by the end of 2027.

A major component of this efficiency drive is a planned global headcount reduction of approximately 16,000 roles over two years. This includes the elimination of 12,000 white-collar positions across various functions and geographies, designed to streamline the operating model, accelerate decision-making, and increase the utilization of automated shared services. The financial benefits of these cuts are already materializing, with the company achieving 20% of its targeted white-collar operational savings ahead of schedule in 2025.

These cost savings are being directly recycled into consumer-facing initiatives. Nestlé has steadily increased its advertising and marketing (A&P) spend, which reached 8.6% of sales in 2025, aiming for roughly 9% to ensure optimal brand visibility and innovation support.

Market Credibility of the Pivot

The market's reception to this strategic reset has been cautiously optimistic, recognizing the necessity of the changes while bracing for near-term transition friction.

Analysts at Rothschild & Co Redburn maintain a Neutral rating, noting that while Nestlé's diverse portfolio is well positioned to deliver 4% or more organic sales growth over time, 2025 represents a transition year burdened by softer consumer dynamics and necessary marketing reinvestments.

Quilter Cheviot views the reduction of 16,000 jobs as a clear signal that Navratil is willing to take drastic, necessary action to arrest Nestlé's recent market share slide, viewing the recent organic sales growth beat as a short-term positive that should reassure investors about the medium-term trajectory.

Management's credibility has been further bolstered by early results, as the company successfully reduced its value share loss gap by 60% and brought its billionaire brands back to being share-neutral for the first time in nearly a decade.

Force Assessment: Direction, Magnitude, Timing, and Probability

Direction: Positive for long-term volume stability and free cash flow generation, though it carries near-term margin friction due to heavy restructuring costs (estimated at roughly twice the annual savings) and increased marketing expenditures.

Magnitude: High. A structural reduction of 16,000 employees (6% of the global workforce) and a pivot away from a decade-long M&A strategy represents a fundamental rewiring of the world's largest food company.

Timing: Short to Medium-term. The structural reorganizations and job cuts are actively underway, with the full CHF 3.0 billion financial benefit and structural optimization expected to be realized by the end of 2027.

Probability: Very High. The execution is already in motion, leadership is fully aligned behind the new mandate, and preliminary cost-saving targets for 2025 were exceeded by over CHF 350 million.

Change Force 2: Pricing vs. Volume Dynamic

Nestlé's organic growth algorithm is undergoing a fundamental transition away from price-driven expansion towards volume-led recovery. After years of offsetting severe macroeconomic headwinds through aggressive pricing, the company is now navigating a highly price-sensitive consumer landscape.

The End of the Post-COVID Pricing Cycle

Through 2022 and 2023, the company implemented approximately 25% cumulative pricing to combat historically steep input cost inflation. By 2024, the pricing contribution decelerated significantly to 1.5%, signaling a normalization of the broader inflationary environment. While 2025 saw pricing temporarily re-accelerate to 2.8%, this was a targeted response to address specific commodity spikes in coffee and cocoa rather than a broad-based structural trend.

The era of relying on broad list price hikes to drive top-line growth has definitively ended. Elasticity has forcefully reasserted itself, with consumers exhibiting entrenched deal-seeking behaviors, migrating toward value packs, and showing steep resistance to further incremental price increases. In categories like confectionery, management noted that short-term volume elasticities were highly pronounced following recent price actions. Consequently, the company has had to selectively roll back pricing in areas where products moved out of reach for the average consumer.

Rothschild & Co Redburn • 19 Mar

The Strategic Imperative of Volume Recovery

With the pricing lever largely exhausted, generating Real Internal Growth —Nestlé's metric for volume and product mix—has become the absolute highest priority for management. The new leadership has explicitly stated that achieving the company's medium-term target of 4% or more organic growth must be fundamentally led by RIG.

Progress is already materializing, as group-level RIG improved from 0.8% for the full year 2025 to 1.2% in the first quarter of 2026. To sustain this momentum, Nestlé is recycling savings from its efficiency programs directly into consumer-facing investments. Furthermore, the company has restructured executive incentives, introducing a strict RIG "gatekeeper" metric into the annual bonus framework to guarantee that management remains hyper-focused on volume recovery.

Consumer Downtrading and Private Label Risks

The current macroeconomic environment remains highly polarized, forcing lower-income consumers to make difficult budgetary choices. In major developed markets like the US and Europe, households have actively traded down to private label alternatives, shifted their shopping to discount and convenience channels, or reduced overall consumption.

Private label market share has expanded aggressively, particularly in the US, where it captured over 21% dollar share in 2025 as shoppers normalized store-brand purchases. To defend its market share against this threat, Nestlé is heavily utilizing sophisticated price-pack architecture. By adjusting pack sizes and grammage, the company aims to ensure its brands remain accessible at critical entry price points without structurally destroying operating margins.

Change Force 3: Portfolio Simplification

Nestlé is aggressively sharpening its portfolio around four key businesses: Coffee, Petcare, Nutrition, and Food & Snacks. Under the new leadership, this simplification aims to divest non-core or underperforming assets to unlock value and fund targeted reinvestments in structural growth categories. The strategy definitively unwinds several major acquisitions from the prior decade while reducing exposure to capital-intensive and highly scrutinized product lines.

Strategic Divestitures and Carve-Outs

The most significant structural shift involves the impending exit from the bottled water category. Nestlé Waters & Premium Beverages has been reorganized into a standalone unit and is expected to be fully deconsolidated from the group by 2027. Management has commenced formal engagement processes to explore partnership opportunities or outright sales for its international premium brands, including Sanpellegrino and Perrier. Former management experts note that despite premium positioning, the water segment faces increasing regulatory scrutiny in Europe regarding natural labeling claims and broader social perception risks. Furthermore, there are growing operational constraints regarding clean water availability, driving the strategic necessity to exit the water business entirely.

In the health and wellness sector, the company is integrating its Nutrition and Nestlé Health Science units into a single global powerhouse to eliminate structural duplication and drive commercial synergies. As part of this consolidation, Nestlé is launching a formal sale process for its mainstream vitamins, minerals, and supplements business. This includes the divestment of mass-market brands such as Nature's Bounty, Osteo Bi-Flex, Puritan's Pride, and various US private label operations. Moving forward, the VMS strategy will strictly pivot to premium, science-backed brands like Pure Encapsulations and Solgar, which offer higher margins and distinct competitive advantages.

In the Food & Snacks division, portfolio rationalization includes advanced negotiations to sell the remaining ice cream operations to Froneri, an existing joint venture with private equity firm PAI Partners.

Analysts at Rothschild & Co Redburn anticipate the ice cream transaction will close in the second quarter of 2026 and expect the disposal to be broadly neutral to earnings per share.

Rothschild & Co Redburn • 4 Mar

Even within its core coffee segment, management reached an agreement in early 2026 to sell the Blue Bottle Coffee brand to Centurium Capital, shedding a slower-growing asset to streamline its global operations.

Capital Reallocation to Growth Powerhouses

Proceeds and cost savings from these divestitures are fueling a massive capital reallocation toward high-growth platforms, which the company aims to expand from 10% to 30% of total group sales. Nestlé has committed CHF 600 million in additional investment for 2026 specifically to support these priority growth platforms, ensuring they have the marketing and innovation fuel required to capture market share.

Petcare is a primary beneficiary of this capital shift, with heavy investments directed toward expanding manufacturing capacity for premium wet pet food and specialized therapeutics. For example, Nestlé recently invested CHF 370 million to open a state-of-the-art wet pet food factory in Brazil, designed to double regional production capacity while running entirely on renewable energy.

Similarly, capital is flowing heavily into the high-margin cold coffee and out-of-home segments. Nestlé is leveraging its Global Coffee Alliance with Starbucks to launch targeted innovations like Starbucks Coffee Craft concentrates, aiming to capture the rapidly expanding Gen Z consumer base that strongly prefers customizable, iced beverages.

Force Assessment: Direction, Magnitude, Timing, and Probability

Direction: Highly positive for operating margins and return on invested capital, as the company trades low-growth, asset-heavy businesses for scaled investments in structurally advantaged categories.

Magnitude: High, given the multi-billion-franc scale of the water, mainstream VMS, and ice cream assets being carved out of the portfolio.

Timing: Near to medium-term. The VMS, Blue Bottle, and ice cream sales are actively underway, while the Waters deconsolidation is slated for completion by 2027.

Probability: Very High. The strategic reviews are already resulting in signed agreements, and the executive board has formally committed to the specific deconsolidation timelines.

Change Force 4: Competitive Dynamics

Nestlé operates in a highly polarized consumer environment where the middle market is being aggressively squeezed by both premium niche challengers and rapidly expanding value options. Defending market share requires continuous innovation and a willingness to accept short-term margin friction to fund protective marketing.

Private Label Squeeze in the US and Europe

Following a period of severe post-pandemic inflation, consumer tolerance for price increases has hit a definitive ceiling across developed markets. Shoppers are actively trading down, driving a structural expansion of private label products across multiple retail tiers. Store brands offer highly functional value, typically pricing at a roughly 30% discount compared to established fast-moving consumer goods. While certain deeply emotional categories like infant nutrition remain somewhat insulated due to strict safety trusts, standard packaged foods and mainstream beverages are highly vulnerable. Retailers themselves have evolved, heavily investing in the quality, packaging, and marketing of their own brands to capture higher margins.

Analysts at Rothschild & Co Redburn observe that divergent pricing strategies over recent quarters significantly widened the price gap between established manufacturers like Nestlé and their private label competitors. This public-private pricing gap has directly contributed to higher consumer elasticity and subsequent volume share losses for legacy brands.

Rothschild & Co Redburn • 25 Mar

Fierce Pet Food Competition: Purina vs. Royal Canin and Hill's

Petcare remains one of Nestlé's most vital growth engines, with the overall global category projected to expand by 4% to 5% annually through 2027. Purina commands the number one market position in North America and ranks second globally. However, the segment is an intense battleground, pitting Purina directly against Mars' Royal Canin and Colgate-Palmolive's Hill's Science Diet. To maintain its competitive edge against these specialized rivals, Purina leverages deep scientific credibility with veterinarians while frequently employing a deliberate underpricing strategy.

Despite the long-term tailwinds of pet humanization, economic headwinds have introduced psychological price ceilings for super-premium pet foods in Europe. Value-conscious pet owners are increasingly extending the intervals between veterinary visits and trading down to budget-friendly kibble. Consequently, private label penetration is actively breaching the pet food sector. In Europe, private label pet food now captures an estimated 15% to 20% average market share, spiking as high as 30% in highly competitive markets like Germany.

Strategic Management of the L'Oréal Stake

Beyond operational dynamics, Nestlé's financial architecture is heavily influenced by its 20.2% equity stake in the French cosmetics giant L'Oréal. Holding over 107.6 million shares, this strategic investment was valued at approximately CHF 36.7 billion at the close of 2025. Investors frequently question whether the company should monetize this massive asset to fund aggressive share buybacks or rapidly deleverage the balance sheet.

Change Force 5: GLP-1 Drugs and Obesity Treatment Impact

The rapid and widespread adoption of GLP-1 weight-loss medications, such as Ozempic and Wegovy, represents a generational disruption in consumer food and beverage habits. While initially perceived as a major systemic threat to the packaged food industry, Nestlé's highly diversified portfolio and proactive strategic responses are effectively neutralizing the downside risks. Rather than viewing this pharmacological shift purely as a headwind, management has successfully positioned the company to capture new, high-margin revenue streams from this rapidly expanding consumer base.

Shifts in Food Category Demand

Clinical studies and consumer behavior tracking demonstrate that GLP-1 agonists structurally reduce patient calorie intake by an estimated 15% to 20%. These medications do not merely suppress appetite; they fundamentally rewire the psychological reward systems associated with comfort eating and indulgent snacking.

Analysts at Rothschild & Co Redburn note that grocery spending declines sharply in the six months following GLP-1 initiation, with the steepest reductions clustered in high-calorie, high-sugar, and high-fat categories. According to their research, spending on sweet bakery items dropped by approximately 9%, while cookies and crackers declined by 7% and 3%, respectively, during this initial treatment window.

Rothschild & Co Redburn • 25 Mar

Despite these striking category contractions, Nestlé's exposure to these highly vulnerable segments is structurally limited. Corporate executives have emphasized that only 15% of global revenue, and roughly 20% of North American revenue, is derived from center-of-the-plate or traditional snacking categories. Furthermore, consumers on GLP-1 therapies engage in active "satiety substitution," intentionally gravitating toward nutrient-dense whole foods and elevated protein sources.

Crucially, there is currently no data or market evidence indicating that human use of GLP-1 medications negatively impacts pet food consumption. This ensures that Nestlé's PetCare division, one of its most critical growth engines, remains completely insulated from the caloric reduction trends pressuring human food volumes.

Offensive Food Launches: Vital Pursuit

Rather than passively absorbing volume losses, Nestlé was the first major consumer packaged goods company to aggressively design food explicitly for the GLP-1 demographic. In May 2024, the company introduced Vital Pursuit in the US, a dedicated line of frozen meals engineered as a companion for weight-loss medication users.

These products are meticulously portion-controlled and fortified with at least 20 grams of protein, essential vitamins, and dietary fiber. This formulation directly combats the malnutrition and muscle-loss risks associated with severe appetite suppression. The brand is actively expanding beyond frozen meals, with a new ready-to-drink beverage containing 30 grams of protein and no added sugar targeted for release in 2025.

Specialized Nutrition and Nestlé Health Science

The most significant financial upside for Nestlé regarding the GLP-1 trend lies within its Health Science division. Internal tracking reveals that at least 75% of GLP-1 users experience at least one side effect, such as gastrointestinal discomfort, skin elasticity loss, or lean muscle degradation.

To capture this lucrative companion market, the company launched a comprehensive digital platform in June 2024, aggregating specialized products from its established Garden of Life, Nature's Bounty, and Boost brands. These targeted offerings provide clinical-grade solutions for muscle preservation, gut health, and micronutrient replenishment. Furthermore, Nestlé is developing transitional products, such as a proprietary pre-meal drink designed to naturally stimulate GLP-1 responses for patients who are stepping off pharmaceutical therapies but wish to avoid subsequent weight rebound.

Change Force 6: Emerging Market Growth

Nestlé's emerging market footprint remains a critical structural growth engine for the group, counterbalancing the increasingly subdued consumer environment in developed regions. As the company navigates the post-inflationary landscape, the trajectory of its EM operations is characterized by a strong underlying volume recovery, normalizing pricing actions, and severe translational currency headwinds.

Volume Recovery and Regional Divergence

The most encouraging aspect of Nestlé's emerging market profile is the definitive return of volume-led growth outside of China. For the first quarter of 2026, emerging markets excluding China delivered a robust organic growth rate of 6.8%. Crucially, this expansion was highly balanced, consisting of 2.9% Real Internal Growth and 3.9% pricing. Management has successfully stimulated demand through targeted investments and affordable product innovations. For example, the Maggi brand in India achieved strong double-digit organic and RIG growth by catering to rural and younger consumers with accessible price points and new flavor profiles.

However, this broad-based momentum is currently being masked by severe operational challenges in Greater China. In early 2026, organic growth in Greater China contracted by 10.6%, driven almost entirely by a 10.4% plunge in RIG. This sharp decline stems from a necessary, ongoing correction of trade inventory levels and the lingering reputational and operational impacts of an industry-wide infant formula recall.

Analysts at Quilter Cheviot highlight that despite the notable weakness in China, emerging market sales growth is structurally outpacing developed economies.

Analysts at Nuvama Institutional Equities observe that in critical EM hubs like India, general trade remains highly resilient due to deeper rural penetration, while the rapid expansion of quick-commerce is actively driving modern channel volumes.

Pricing Normalization

Following a prolonged period of aggressive price hikes to offset severe commodity inflation, Nestlé's pricing lever in emerging markets is steadily normalizing. Total emerging market pricing landed at 3.4% in the first quarter of 2026. This represents a clear deceleration from 2025 levels as the company begins to annualize the steep, targeted pricing actions previously taken on coffee and cocoa products. While management continues to monitor consumer elasticity closely, the stable transition toward positive RIG indicates that EM consumers are largely adapting to the new price floors for essential food and beverage categories.

Severe Currency and FX Headwinds

While underlying operational momentum in emerging markets is strengthening, reported financial metrics are being heavily heavily penalized by structural foreign exchange headwinds. The persistent strength of the Swiss Franc against a basket of emerging market currencies resulted in a massive 9.3% negative FX impact on total group sales in Q1 2026. The translation drag is particularly acute in Latin America, where foreign exchange movements drove a 10.5% negative impact on reported sales, effectively erasing the optical benefits of a 9.1% organic growth rate in the region.

Change Force 7: Cost Savings and Efficiency Programmes

Nestlé's broader strategic transformation is heavily dependent on extracting structural costs to fund vital growth investments and protect operating margins amid input inflation. To facilitate this, the company has launched a massive internal restructuring effort that aims to fundamentally alter how the organization procures materials, manages headcount, and leverages data.

The 'Fuel for Growth' Upsize

The flagship efficiency initiative is the "Fuel for Growth" program, which management originally targeted at CHF 2.5 billion but recently upsized to CHF 3.0 billion by the end of 2027. The company has accelerated the delivery of these savings significantly, achieving CHF 1.1 billion in 2025, which beat the initial internal target of CHF 700 million by over CHF 350 million. Looking ahead, management expects the in-year financial savings to scale to CHF 2.0 billion for 2026. These cost reductions sit on top of the ongoing, baseline Nestlé Continuous Excellence initiatives, which routinely yield over CHF 1 billion in annual efficiencies.

Procurement Consolidation and AI Integration

Approximately three-quarters of the total Fuel for Growth savings are projected to come from procurement optimization. With an annual global procurement spend of approximately CHF 60 billion, the company is deploying artificial intelligence to systematically review supplier contracts and consolidate vendor overlap. Management recently audited 3,000 of its largest suppliers, representing over 80% of total spend, to identify historical invoice inconsistencies and secure immediate one-time financial recoveries.

A former Nestlé procurement manager notes that the organization already possesses highly mature global procurement hubs, making these accelerated targets feasible by effectively leveraging existing infrastructure. This expert further emphasizes that utilizing advanced analytics and automation will be critical to pushing these centralized procurement functions to the next level of efficiency.

Operational Transformation and Headcount Reductions

The remaining quarter of the target savings will be generated through sweeping operational and commercial efficiencies. A central pillar of this structural overhaul is a planned global workforce reduction of approximately 16,000 roles over a two-year period. This sweeping cut includes eliminating 12,000 white-collar professional roles across multiple geographies to simplify spans and layers, a move expected to generate CHF 1.0 billion in standalone annual savings by 2027. An additional 4,000 positions are slated to be eliminated through ongoing productivity initiatives across the manufacturing and supply chain footprint.

To execute this leaner operating model without disrupting commercial momentum, Nestlé is aggressively scaling its shared services model to standardize historically fragmented end-to-end processes, such as procure-to-pay and hire-to-retire. Management noted that in-market finance teams currently spend roughly 40% of their time on raw data compilation, a metric the company intends to reduce to under 5% through immediate automation investments.

Margin Trajectory and Reinvestment Dynamics

While the absolute scale of these savings is vast, the net incremental margin expansion will be partially muted by the strategic necessity of reinvestment. The company ultimately aims to reach an underlying trading operating profit margin of 17.0% or higher in the medium term, recovering from the 16.1% margin recorded in 2025. However, a significant portion of the cost savings will be directly recycled into consumer-facing activities, specifically stepping up advertising and marketing expenses to a sustained target of 9% of sales. Furthermore, the structural reorganization carries heavy transition friction, with management modeling one-off restructuring charges at approximately two times the annual savings, creating a roughly CHF 2.0 billion cash drag.

Analysts at Rothschild & Co Redburn point out that achieving near-term profit expansion remains challenging due to the confluence of worsening currency headwinds, US tariffs, and steep input cost inflation for key commodities like cocoa and green coffee.

Rothschild & Co Redburn • 4 Feb

Analysts at Maybank Investment Banking Group highlight that Nestlé's ability to leverage its global procurement scale for superior raw material pricing provides a crucial defensive moat, aiding the company in keeping a lid on cost of goods sold relative to smaller competitors.

Analysts at Sunidhi Securities note that the dual mandate of cutting multi-billion-franc costs while simultaneously boosting global marketing spend to defend brand equity represents a defining and difficult balancing act for the new leadership team.

Force Assessment: Direction, Magnitude, Timing, and Probability

Direction: Positive for long-term structural profitability, though net operating margin accretion will be partially offset by the strategic imperative to rebuild the company's marketing muscle and combat sticky commodity inflation.

Magnitude: High, as the CHF 3.0 billion target necessitates a massive structural reorganization and a highly disruptive 6% reduction in the global workforce.

Timing: Short to Medium-term, with accelerated financial benefits already materializing (CHF 1.1 billion achieved in 2025) and full run-rate realization formally scheduled for the end of 2027.

Probability: High, given that the execution is heavily centralized around scalable procurement algorithms and shared service standardizations, areas where the company possesses a historically strong implementation track record.

Change Force 8: ESG and Sustainability

Nestlé's environmental, social, and governance framework is anchored by its comprehensive Net Zero Roadmap, alongside material commitments to packaging circularity and water stewardship. While these initiatives are critical for defending long-term brand equity and maintaining a societal license to operate, they introduce meaningful near-term structural costs across the supply chain.

Net-Zero Carbon Commitments and Regenerative Agriculture

To address its massive agricultural footprint, the company has committed to achieving net-zero greenhouse gas emissions by 2050, with an interim target of a 50% reduction by 2030. A central pillar of this decarbonization effort is a commitment to source 50% of its key ingredients through regenerative agriculture by 2030.

Plastic Packaging and Circularity

On the packaging front, the company has achieved a milestone where 95.6% of its global packaging is explicitly designed for recycling. Furthermore, Nestlé aims to reach complete plastic neutrality by ensuring that none of its packaging ends up as litter or in landfills. The shift away from virgin plastics involves significant transition costs, primarily driven by the constrained supply and high unit price of recycled PET (rPET).

A leading sustainability executive notes that the lack of market maturity and financing in the recycling industry means rPET prices are unlikely to drop in the near term, keeping sustainable packaging costs elevated for the next five years.

Analysts at HC Wainwright highlight that this packaging transition is largely mandatory rather than voluntary, as over 60 countries have imposed strict bans and levies to curtail single-use plastics, forcing consumer conglomerates to secure recycled content regardless of the margin impact.

Water Sustainability and Watershed Impact
