§6 — Counter-Arguments to Bull Case

J-front verdict: The offshore execution bull thesis is the most vulnerable to bear rebuttal; every major bull thesis point has a credible adversarial inversion, with the leverage + timeline + CfD triple-risk being the strongest compound bear scenario [#J]

  • Bull thesis #1: Management is executing a transformational offshore wind build that will re-rate PEP-PL from a Polish utility to a major European renewables developer [#A]
  • Bear rebuttal #1a: Offshore wind development track record in Poland is zero; PEP-PL has never built or operated an offshore wind farm; Baltica 2/3 would be the first Polish offshore project at scale
  • Bear rebuttal #1b: European offshore precedent is brutal — Orsted, Vattenfall, BP's Lightsource, RWE all experienced material cost overruns or cancellations 2022-24; PEP-PL faces identical supply chain, inflation, and financing risks

  • Evidence supporting rebuttal: Turbine prices (Siemens Gamesa, Vestas) rose 20-40% in 2022-23; foundation steel costs increased; installation vessel scarcity persists; PEP-PL has no privileged supply chain position

  • Bull thesis #2: Polish CfD auction regime provides revenue certainty post-FID [#A]

  • Bear rebuttal #2a: Polish CfD auctions are administered by URE and subject to political interference; the 2015 "wind law" (10H rule) demonstrated that Polish governments will impose retroactive restrictions on renewables when politically convenient
  • Bear rebuttal #2b: Strike prices set today may be economically insufficient if construction costs continue to inflate; CfD contracts with fixed strike prices can become uneconomic traps (as seen with early UK CfD rounds where developers sought to renegotiate)

  • Bear rebuttal #2c: Auction participation is not guaranteed; if PSE (Polish grid operator) cannot guarantee timely grid connection, CfD awards may be conditional on interconnection milestones that slip

  • Bull thesis #3: Equinor JV partnership de-risks balance sheet and brings operational expertise [#A]

  • Bear rebuttal #3a: Equinor has publicly signalled strategic caution on offshore wind economics; at its 2024 Capital Markets Day, Equinor reduced offshore wind capacity targets and flagged return requirements — creating FID delay risk
  • Bear rebuttal #3b: JV structures mean Equinor holds leverage in FID discussions; PEP-PL's smaller balance sheet means it cannot unilaterally fund the project if Equinor pauses

  • Bear rebuttal #3c: Partnership governance complexity adds decision latency that could push Baltica FID to 2027-28+, materially deferring the bull case earnings inflection

  • Bull thesis #4: Polish energy transition creates structural demand tailwinds for domestic renewable developer [#A]

  • Bear rebuttal #4a: Poland's energy transition pace is set by government, not market; Poland has the most coal-dependent electricity system in the EU (65-70% coal share as of 2023) and EU carbon policy enforcement is the primary driver of transition speed
  • Bear rebuttal #4b: European offshore wind policy is at a political inflection point; EU and national support frameworks face permit reform, grid investment gaps, and subsidy review — transition pace could slow

  • Bear rebuttal #4c: Competing developers (PGE, Tauron with EDPR partnership) also have offshore permits — PEP-PL is not a monopolist in Polish offshore

  • Bull thesis #5: Current valuation offers a discount to NAV of development assets [#A]

  • Bear rebuttal #5a: NAV of development assets is model-dependent; discount rates, construction costs, and CfD strike prices are all management assumptions — bears argue NAV should be discounted further for execution risk not fully priced

  • Bear rebuttal #5b: Optionality value of a development pipeline is worth zero if FID is never taken; bears argue the market is pricing FID as near-certain when it remains conditional on Equinor alignment, financing close, and Polish permitting

  • Bull thesis #6: Onshore wind + gas + distribution provides stable earnings base while offshore ramps [#A]

  • Bear rebuttal #6a: Polish wholesale power prices have been volatile and declining; merchant gas generation EBITDA declined materially in 2023-24 as European gas spreads compressed — the "stable base" has been unstable

  • Bear rebuttal #6b: Onshore wind capacity is modest (sub-400MW); EBITDA contribution is insufficient to cover offshore development costs — the stable base is not actually subsidising the build

  • Bull rebuttal (sceptical): The weakest bear rebuttal is #4 (energy transition tailwinds); EU carbon prices are structurally above the level that makes coal competitive, meaning Polish energy transition is not fully discretionary — the direction of travel is locked in even if the pace varies; bulls are likely right that PEP-PL benefits from transition regardless of political cycle
