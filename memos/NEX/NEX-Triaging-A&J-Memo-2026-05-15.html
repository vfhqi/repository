Section B — Investment case: Executive summary

Section-level BLUF: Nexans clears the Triaging qualitative test on its change forces — they are large and structural — but it is the cohort name where the gap between the strength of the change forces and the attractiveness of the case as an investment is widest, because Nexans carries two binary company-specific overhangs neither peer has and has re-rated hardest of the three on the softest print of the three.
  • ❌ Nexans carries two binary company-specific overhangs that neither NKT nor Prysmian has: and they are the defining feature of the case. A mid-cycle, board-initiated CEO transition (Guérin out, Hueber in, October 2025) and a frozen ~EUR 1.43bn flagship project (the Great Sea Interconnector, halted after a Turkish Navy confrontation, excluded entirely from 2026 guidance). The cross-cohort research is explicit that Nexans is the most binary of the three: if the transformation execution lands the re-rating is large, if it stumbles the thesis is exposed
  • ❌ The entry-point problem is the most acute in the cohort. Nexans has re-rated ~+39% since the research base, the hardest move of the three, on the softest Q1 print of the three (+1.3% standard-sales growth, versus genuine consensus beats at NKT and Prysmian). The research base's entire Nexans thesis was "the cheapest name, a wide discount to peers, a re-rating opportunity if execution lands", the stock has now re-rated, blown through both the old and the new consensus targets, before the execution has been demonstrated
  • The change forces are nonetheless genuinely large and structural. A ~EUR 7.7–8.1bn Transmission backlog securing ~90% of 2024–2028 revenue, a near-complete pure-play transformation that has shed the low-margin legacy businesses, the cohort's steepest two-year margin-improvement trajectory (+240bps in two years), the cohort's highest-margin single segment (PWR-Grid at 16.4%), and a fortress 0.36x balance sheet.   • Nexans is the cohort's "show-me" case, and it is now a show-me case at a show-me price. At the research base it was a cheap show-me case, the discount compensated for the binary risk. After a ~+39% re-rating the discount has narrowed sharply (EV/EBITDA from ~7.6x to ~10x), so the buyer is now being asked to underwrite two binary overhangs and an unproven new CEO without the cushion that the cheapness used to provide
  • Recommendation: PROGRESS to Early-Stage Assessment: but this is the closest progress/park call of the three, and the memo flags it as such. But the cross-cohort Key Drivers report's own prior was "conditional proceed, consider parking until end of Q2 2026 pending early Hueber evidence," and the ~+39% re-rating since then makes the entry-point question sharper, not softer. Nexans progresses, but as the cohort's highest-risk name, into an Early-Stage Assessment that must resolve two binary questions and a valuation question before any capital conversation. RA1 — Change forces? — summary (6 bullets minimum)A
  • Verdict: strong, dual-sourced change forces, comparable in strength to the cohort peers, predominantly external with a real internal transformation leg: RA1 itself is [A]-strength, the gap that pulls E1 to [C] is RA2, not RA1
  • The external change force is the same policy-mandated supercycle the cohort shares: the EU Grids Package (the source research cites a EUR ~1.2tn grid-investment commitment 2024–2040), offshore-wind awards projected to reach ~30 GW a year by 2027, and data-centre electricity demand doubling by 2030, Nexans sits at the intersection of every driver, the same structural demand picture as NKT and Prysmian
  • The internal change force is a near-complete pure-play transformation: Nexans has shed the non-electrification businesses (AmerCable, Lynxeo, and Autoelectric, the last closing mid-2026) in a multi-year portfolio rotation, roughly EUR 1.1bn-plus of disposal proceeds, ~EUR 2.2bn of legacy revenue removed, leaving a focused electrification company; this is a genuine, value-relevant internal change, comparable in kind to NKT's pure-play transformation
  • The change is visible in delivered numbers: the cohort's steepest two-year margin trajectory (+240bps, 9.5% to 11.9%), FCF nearly doubling in FY2025 (EUR 177m to EUR 344m), net debt collapsing from EUR 681m to EUR 266m, the change forces have been converting
  • The pricing leg of the change force is real: Nexans is part of the same supply-constrained HVDC oligopoly, with 800-plus-day factory lead times handing incumbents pricing power; Transmission grew +29.8% organically in FY2025 and PWR-Grid expanded its margin +226bps to 16.4%

RA1 Core Questions:

E1 RA1 CQ1 — Strong external change forces / tailwinds? (6 bullets minimum)

  • Verdict: yes: the external tailwind is the same policy-mandated grid-electrification supercycle the whole cohort shares, and Nexans has no less exposure to it than its peers: this Core Question is as strong for Nexans as for NKT or Prysmian
  • The demand is policy-mandated and quantified: the EU Grids Package's EUR ~1.2tn 2024–2040 commitment, the EIB's record EUR 11bn to energy grids in 2025 (roughly triple the 2023 level), ENTSO-E's estimate of EUR ~3.4bn a year of interconnector investment 2025–2040, binding regulatory demand, not discretionary capex
  • The demand is supply-constrained, which is the strongest demand signature: factory lead times of 800-plus days, scarce cable-laying-vessel capacity, a skilled-labour shortage, the [C] research is explicit that "demand is not the bottleneck, supply is," which hands incumbents pricing power
  • The offshore-wind and interconnector vectors are large and growing: Morgan Stanley projects offshore-wind formal awards rising from ~15 GW in 2025 toward ~30 GW in 2027; each GW of offshore wind needs EUR 250–400m of specialised cable
  • A data-centre demand layer is additive: data-centre electricity consumption rising from ~1.5% to ~3% of the global total by 2030, and Hueber has explicitly flagged the US data-centre market as a growth vector, a meaningful emphasis shift
  • The energy-security framing gives the tailwind political durability: post-Ukraine and post-Iran, grid investment is reframed as national-security infrastructure with cross-party support
  • Counter-consideration: the one genuine external-demand soft patch is PWR-Connect's exposure to European residential and industrial construction, which is subdued, the same kind of cyclical-segment soft patch Prysmian carries in Electrification I&C; it does not weaken the aggregate external tailwind, which is strong, but it is the composition caveat

E1 RA1 CQ2 — Strong internal change forces? (6 bullets minimum)

  • Verdict: yes, but with a sharper double edge than either peer, the internal change is a near-complete transformation and a destabilising leadership transition at the same time: the internal change force is real, but it is the most two-sided in the cohort
  • The portfolio transformation is the positive internal change, and it is nearly complete: the disposal of AmerCable, Lynxeo and Autoelectric, the reclassification of Industry & Solutions as discontinued operations, Nexans is now a focused electrification pure-play, the culmination of the seven-year Guérin transformation
  • The capacity build-out is a second positive internal change: the Nexans Electra cable-laying vessel (commissioning Q2 2026, with the unusual capability to lay four cables simultaneously), the NCCCR copper-recycling facility at Lens, and the completed Halden and Charleston HV plant expansions
  • But the CEO transition is the destabilising internal change, and it is board-initiated: Christopher Guérin, the architect of the entire transformation, was replaced by Julien Hueber in October 2025, board-initiated, with the board's stated rationale being to "create new momentum," which the [C] research reads as corporate boilerplate for "the board wanted a change." Boards do not usually replace successful CEOs at the peak of a transformation without a reason
  • The new ExCo structure adds organisational change on top of the leadership change: a 13-member ExCo (large by European standards), a regional-business-unit reorganisation, a genuine operational reshuffling, mid-cycle, that could either sharpen execution or dilute decision-making speed
  • Counter-consideration: and this is a reason E1 is [C]: NKT's internal change is largely done (the pure-play transformation, banked); Prysmian's is large but in-flight under stable leadership; Nexans' internal change is a near-complete transformation handed to unproven new leadership in a mid-cycle reshuffle, the internal change force is real, but it is the most execution-uncertain version of it in the cohort, and it is one of the two binary overhangs that define the case
RA2 — Robust base? — summary (6 bullets minimum)C
  • Verdict: this is the weakest Required Attribute in the entire three-memo cohort set: Nexans' base is operationally improving but carries two binary overhangs, the hardest re-rating of the three, and the softest print of the three: RA2 is what pulls E1 to [C]
  • Operationally the base has genuinely strong elements: a fortress balance sheet (0.36x leverage, the lowest in the cohort), the cohort's steepest margin-improvement trajectory, the highest-margin single segment in the cohort (PWR-Grid at 16.4%), and a ~EUR 7.7–8.1bn backlog securing ~90% of 2024–2028 revenue
  • But the base carries overhang one: the frozen Great Sea Interconnector: a ~EUR 1.43bn flagship subsea project (Greece–Cyprus), halted after Nexans' survey vessel was confronted by seven Turkish Navy warships, with ~EUR 70m of payments frozen, tenders for related works cancelled, and the project excluded entirely from 2026 guidance, a binary, geopolitical, not-resolvable-by-execution overhang that neither NKT nor Prysmian has

  • And the mispricing has not just narrowed: it has substantially inverted: the research base's whole Nexans thesis was "the cheapest name, a wide discount to peers (~7.6x versus Prysmian ~9–11x and NKT ~8–10x), a re-rating opportunity if execution lands"; the stock has since re-rated ~+39%, blown through both the old EUR 141 and the new ~EUR 159 consensus targets, and the discount has narrowed to ~10x, the re-rating has happened, before the execution that was supposed to justify it
  • Counter-consideration / what stops RA2 failing outright: Nexans is still the cheapest name in the cohort even after the re-rating, the balance sheet genuinely is a fortress, and the backlog genuinely does secure ~90% of near-term revenue, so the base is exposed, not broken; that is the difference between a [C] on E1 and a park recommendation, and it is a fine line that Section E addresses directly

E1 RA2 CQ1 — Absence of external headwinds? (6 bullets minimum)
