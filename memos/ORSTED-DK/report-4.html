  • Peers such as RWE and Iberdrola also saw revenue normalisation post-2022 power price spike, but retained positive EPS throughout

  • Mix: Revenue mix has shifted unfavourably — construction/project revenue is large and lumpy, masking operational run-rate; partnership gains (farm-downs) are non-recurring [#A]

  • Ørsted's business model generates revenue from: (1) operational wind farms on CfD/PPA, (2) construction progress revenue, (3) farm-down gains on partial asset sales

  • Farm-down revenue in FY22-23 was substantial but non-recurring; the pipeline of future farm-down proceeds is less certain given project cancellations

  • However, the contracted strike prices on legacy US projects that were cancelled were below current build cost — this is the mechanism of the impairments

  • Volume: Operational generation volumes broadly in line with capacity factor expectations (offshore wind load factors ~45-50%) — no evidence of systemic underperformance on operational assets [#I]

  • Construction delays on Sunrise Wind and Revolution Wind impacted volume growth timing; near-term volume ramp is slower than 2022 projections

  • Market share: In offshore wind, Ørsted remains a global top-3 developer by installed capacity, but its pipeline shrinkage vs competitors (Vattenfall, RWE, EDPR) means share of future capacity development is declining [#A]

  • Ørsted withdrew from several UK, US, and Nordic auction rounds in 2023-24 citing unacceptable economics — a deliberate share retreat to protect returns

  • M&A contribution: Farm-down transactions have been a significant revenue contributor; these are financial engineering within the business model rather than traditional M&A, but have the same effect of boosting reported revenue/earnings without organic growth [#A]

  • The reduction in farm-down activity (fewer projects to sell-down) is a forward revenue headwind

  • FX impact: Ørsted reports in DKK; USD exposure significant (US projects); the abandonment of US projects has actually reduced USD revenue exposure, simplifying FX dynamics but forfeiting USD revenue upside [#I]

  • Forward guide: Management's FY25-26 EBITDA guidance (reintroduced at FY24 results) targets a recovery trajectory — the revenue component relies on operational fleet growth and CfD revenues, not speculative trading or farm-downs [#A]

  • Sell-side revisions: Direction of FY25-26 revenue estimates was flat-to-up through H2 FY24 as the worst-case US project outcomes were already recognised — a negative revision cycle appears to have bottomed but not reversed sharply [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish revenue read: construction revenue recognition accounting creates a period of apparent revenue growth that reverses sharply when projects complete; farm-down gains are episodic; if AR6/future auction pipeline delivers fewer projects than planned, the organic volume growth story stalls at ~5-6% capacity CAGR — insufficient to drive material EPS recovery without margin expansion [#J]

  • EBITDA margin: FY22 EBITDA (excl. new partnerships) approximately DKK 19.8bn on revenue ~DKK 102.6bn = ~19% — already compressed vs FY21 by trading noise; FY23 EBITDA approximately DKK 12.8bn on DKK ~77.7bn revenue = ~16.5% — the core deterioration [#A]
  • FY24 EBITDA partially recovered — management guided toward DKK 23-26bn in FY25 (medium-term target), implying margins must recover to ~30-35% on a normalised revenue base

  • Iberdrola EBITDA margin through FY23-24 held at ~25-28% — structurally more stable due to regulated networks income base

  • EBIT margin: Deeply negative in FY23 on reported basis due to DKK ~28.4bn impairment; adjusted EBIT margin was positive but compressed; the gap between reported and adjusted EBIT is the most significant disclosure-risk dimension for Ørsted [#A]

  • Direction: Contracting from FY21 to FY23 trough; tentative recovery from FY24 — the direction is improving but from an abnormally depressed base [#A]

  • The magnitude of contraction (peak-to-trough approximately 20-25 percentage points on normalised EBITDA margin) exceeds anything in the comparable peer set

  • Decomposition — input costs: Steel, cable, and installation vessel costs rose 30-50% from 2020 to 2023 peak — this is the primary gross margin driver and affects the development economics of new projects, not directly the P&L of operational assets [#A]

  • Operational fleet costs (O&M) also inflated but more modestly; managed through long-term service agreements with Siemens Gamesa/Vestas

  • Decomposition — price contribution: CfD-linked revenues for operational fleet are indexed — limited ability to reprice upward; conversely, downside price protection exists [#A]

  • New project strike prices (post-AR6) are higher in nominal terms — if delivered, they will support forward margin recovery

  • Decomposition — mix contribution: The US project impairments removed high-optionality, high-expected-return assets from the development portfolio — a structural mix deterioration even though it removed earnings volatility risk [#J]

  • The residual portfolio is more Europe-concentrated, which is lower-risk but also lower-return than US offshore wind was projected to be at 2020 planning assumptions

  • Operating leverage: Ørsted has a semi-fixed cost structure (large project development organisation, corporate overhead) — revenue decline without proportional cost reduction is the mechanism of margin compression [#A]

  • FY23 saw headcount restructuring (announced December 2023: ~800 roles cut, ~15% of workforce) — management attempting to right-size the cost base to the revised project pipeline

  • Cost programmes: The restructuring programme (2024) targeted DKK 1bn+ in annualised savings — material relative to the ~DKK 12.8bn FY23 EBITDA base (~8% uplift if fully realised) [#A]

  • Early signs of cost delivery in H1 FY24 results; full-year run-rate savings expected to crystallise in FY25

  • Productivity: Operational asset productivity (load factors, availability rates) has remained strong — the margin deterioration is entirely a development portfolio and impairment story, not an operational underperformance story [#A]

  • This distinction matters: operational assets are performing well; the value destruction is in the project development book

  • Sell-side outlook: Consensus margin expectations (per publicly available estimates) converge on FY25-26 EBITDA margin recovery toward 28-32% — contingent on no further impairments and successful delivery of the revised project pipeline [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The margin recovery case rests on three simultaneous conditions: (1) no further project impairments, (2) cost restructuring savings being sustained rather than reinvested in pipeline rebuild, and (3) new project economics (AR6 and equivalent) delivering as modelled — any one failure reverts the trajectory; history of the last 24 months shows all three failed simultaneously in FY23 [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: The adjusted EPS recovery trajectory is entirely contingent on no further project impairments — yet the development portfolio still contains projects at risk from similar cost inflation mechanisms (Hornsea 3 in UK, US residual projects); the adjusted EPS metric excludes the losses that matter most for long-term value destruction; a bear would argue the "clean" recovery story collapses on the next project re-assessment [#J]

§7 — Sector & Value Chain Context

Net signal: The offshore wind sector's fundamental difficulties are real and sector-wide, but Ørsted's deterioration was amplified by company-specific factors — concentration in US offshore, pre-inflation fixed-price contracts, and management's overconfidence in US policy certainty distinguish Ørsted from peers who navigated the same environment with less damage [#J]

  • Sector revenue: European utility/renewable sector revenue broadly stabilising in FY24 after FY22 price-spike-driven extraordinary highs — all major peers have absorbed the normalisation; Ørsted's additional revenue pressure is from project portfolio reduction [#A]

  • Sector margin: Offshore wind development margins industry-wide compressed 15-25% from 2020 to 2023/24 trough, per publicly available project economics analysis (BNEF, Wood Mackenzie references) — this is a structural shift, not a cyclical dip [#A]

  • The margin compression is driven by turbine cost increases (Siemens Gamesa/Vestas), installation vessel scarcity, and cable infrastructure bottlenecks — all supply-chain structural issues with multi-year resolution timelines

  • Sector revisions: Industry-wide, the offshore wind buildout has been revised down for 2025-2030 by approximately 20-30% from peak 2021 projections (IEA, BNEF public estimates) — a significant downgrade to the structural growth story [#A]

  • Supplier signals: Siemens Gamesa (key turbine supplier) reported multi-billion restructuring charges and delivery delays in FY22-24 — a direct upstream pressure on Ørsted's project delivery and cost certainty [#A]

  • Vestas has also highlighted margin compression and reduced order intake for offshore models — the supplier ecosystem is under stress, creating ongoing risk for project developers

  • This is a sector-wide headwind but Ørsted has higher exposure than diversified peers

  • Customer signals: Grid operators and utilities purchasing offshore wind power (through CfD/PPA) are not signalling demand reduction — electricity demand in Europe is expected to grow structurally (electrification, data centres) — the demand-side is intact [#A]

  • End-market growth: Long-term electricity demand forecasts in Europe (IEA, National Grid, RTE) show structural growth of 2-4% per annum through 2030-2035 — a genuine structural tailwind for all generators [#A]

  • This end-market tailwind does not, however, solve the capital cost and margin problem for new project development

  • Competitive intensity: The offshore wind development market is experiencing some rationalisation — several developers have withdrawn from specific auctions (Vattenfall UK Round 4, Ørsted US exits) — which could reduce competition and support future project returns [#A]

  • Counter-intuitively, market exits may improve economics for those who remain, though this is speculative at this stage

  • Cyclical vs structural: The key analytical question is whether offshore wind cost inflation is cyclical (reversing as supply chain normalises) or structural (permanent reset to higher capex intensity) — evidence is mixed [#J]

  • Cyclical: installation vessel capacity is being added (DEME, Seaway7 orders); steel prices have partially normalised from 2022 peak; Siemens Gamesa restructuring may restore delivery reliability

  • Structural: grid connection costs and consenting timescales have not normalised; the UK/EU planning environment has become more complex, not less

  • Policy environment: European policy support for offshore wind remains strong (EU Net Zero Industry Act, UK offshore wind targets, Danish energy island projects) — Ørsted's home market policy environment is genuinely supportive [#A]

  • US policy under the current administration is less certain — but Ørsted's US exposure has been substantially reduced following the project cancellations

  • Sceptical bullet: The cyclical recovery narrative for offshore wind rests on supply chain normalisation that may not arrive as quickly as consensus assumes — Siemens Gamesa's restructuring is ongoing, installation vessel availability is tight through 2026-27, and the consenting/grid bottlenecks are entirely structural; if the cost curve only partially normalises, Ørsted's development-heavy business model remains impaired regardless of market share dynamics [#J]

§8 — Quality of Earnings & Accounting

Net signal: Ørsted's earnings quality is compromised by a large and persistent non-GAAP gap, farm-down gains masking operational earnings, and a history of asset impairments that call into question the rigour of project-level IRR assumptions at inception [#J]

  • Non-GAAP gap: The gap between reported EPS (DKK -34.9 in FY23) and management-adjusted EPS (approximately DKK +10-12) is DKK ~47/share — one of the largest non-GAAP adjustments in absolute terms for any European utility over L5Y [#A]

  • The impairments are real economic losses (value of cancelled projects, stranded development costs) — presenting adjusted EPS that excludes them fundamentally misrepresents the investment cycle returns

  • Cash conversion: Operating cash flow was positive (approximately DKK 8-12bn in FY23) against negative reported net income — confirming the impairments are non-cash and the operational business generates real cash [#A]

  • OCF/adjusted EBITDA ratio approximately 60-70% in FY23 (peer median closer to 70-80%) — below peer median, suggesting some working capital drag or construction-timing cash outflows ❌

  • Farm-down gains: Ørsted recognises gains on partial asset sales (farm-downs) in EBITDA — these are episodic, non-recurring, and can be large in any given year [#A]

  • Stripping farm-down gains from EBITDA would reduce the reported figure by DKK 2-5bn in peak farm-down years — the "EBITDA excl. new partnerships" metric management publishes is the correct comparison, but its disclosure is not always prominently positioned

  • One-offs: Beyond impairments, Ørsted has recognised: (1) project development cost write-offs, (2) contract termination penalties (estimated DKK 5-6bn for Ocean Wind 1&2 cancellations), (3) restructuring charges (~DKK 1bn) — all excluded from adjusted metrics [#A]

  • Peers (RWE, Iberdrola) have also taken charges in renewable portfolios but at significantly smaller absolute levels relative to market cap

  • NWC days: Construction-in-progress creates large capital tied up in the balance sheet; NWC in the traditional sense (receivables/payables) is less material for Ørsted than for industrial peers — the relevant working capital concept is project capex timing [#A]

  • Receivables days for the operational generation business are short (CfD payments are relatively prompt) — not a red flag in isolation

  • Adjustments peer norms: RWE and Iberdrola typically adjust for impairments and restructuring — non-GAAP presentation is sector-standard; the concern with Ørsted is the magnitude of adjustments relative to reported, not the practice itself [#A]

  • Inventory / project pipeline accounting: Development costs are capitalised until project FID or abandonment — the pipeline of pre-FID development costs on the balance sheet carries risk of further impairment if projects are cancelled [#A]

  • Post-FY23 cleanup, the remaining pre-FID balance appears lower, but disclosure granularity is limited from [C] sources

  • Sceptical bullet: The non-cash nature of impairments is often cited as a "doesn't matter for operations" argument — but it does matter: it reflects past capital misallocation decisions (committing to projects at economics that proved unviable) that will repeat if project selection discipline has not fundamentally improved; the adjusted EPS metric structurally hides the cost of being wrong on project economics, and the track record of being wrong is recent [#J]

§9 — Sceptical Synthesis (Cross-Cutting)

Verdict: The bear case for Ørsted is not that the company fails — it is that recovery is slow, guidance is structurally untrusted, and the valuation requires a return to peak economics that the sector may not support for 3-5 years — generating insufficient IRR for a concentrated 18M-3Y investor [#J]

  • Reverse-engineered bear: The strongest bear case is a value trap: Ørsted is cheap on EV/EBITDA vs history, but cheap for structural reasons — if offshore wind project economics do not normalise, the EBITDA recovery embedded in the FY26 targets (DKK 23-26bn) is unachievable [#J]
  • EV/EBITDA at current prices (approximately DKK 250-270 range mid-2025) of ~8-10x FY25E EBITDA looks optically cheap vs history; but if EBITDA peaks at DKK 16-18bn rather than the DKK 26bn CMD target, the stock is not cheap at all

  • The mechanism: continued supply chain inflation or project cancellations crystallise additional impairments → EBITDA guidance revised down again → analyst consensus downgrades → further de-rating

  • Single largest risk: Hornsea 3 (2.4GW, UK) is Ørsted's largest construction-stage project and represents the single biggest near-term impairment risk — cost overruns on this project could repeat the FY23 dynamic [#J]

  • Hornsea 3 FID was taken at economics predating the full magnitude of cost inflation; revised project economics disclosed in FY24 suggest it remains viable but at reduced IRR — the margin of safety is thin

  • Early warning signals forming: Three patterns visible in the last 12M earnings data suggest caution [#A]

  • OCF/EBITDA ratio below peer median (§8) — some cash generation quality concern
  • Pipeline shrinkage (project exits and auction absences) limiting future growth capacity — not yet in P&L, but a leading indicator

  • Management guidance conservatism post-FY23 may be obscuring a continued lack of project pipeline confidence rather than genuine trajectory improvement

  • Peer-relative downside: If sector headwinds intensify (rising interest rates, supply chain re-inflation, US policy reversal on IRA incentives for European developers), Ørsted's mono-line offshore wind exposure means it absorbs 2-3x the P&L impact vs diversified peers like Iberdrola or Enel who have regulated networks as earnings floors [#J]

  • Breaking points — mechanism 1: Any single large project impairment (Hornsea 3, Greater Changhua 2b-3a Taiwan, or pipeline rebuild projects) triggers an immediate EBITDA guidance revision → consensus downgrades → share price re-rating downward [#J]

  • Breaking points — mechanism 2: If the Danish state (50.1% majority owner) decides to provide capital support or strategic direction that dilutes minority shareholders, the return profile for external investors deteriorates regardless of operational recovery [#A]

  • This is a low-probability but plausible tail risk given the state's active involvement in energy policy

  • Breaking points — mechanism 3: Rising European interest rates (ECB policy path uncertainty for 2026-27) would compress the NPV of long-duration offshore wind assets, increasing discount rates and potentially triggering further balance sheet write-downs even on performing assets [#J]

  • Positive thesis vulnerabilities: The recovery thesis requires (1) no further impairments, (2) cost restructuring sustained, (3) project pipeline rebuilding at improved economics, (4) interest rate environment supportive — four simultaneous conditions that have not all been true at the same time in the last 3 years [#J]

  • Valuation pricing of thesis: The market appears to be pricing approximately a 50% probability of the recovery scenario and a 50% probability of continued impairment — the stock has not re-rated to reflect a clean recovery, suggesting the market remains unconvinced [#A]

  • Contrarian case (upside): If supply chain costs normalise faster than expected, Hornsea 3 delivers on time/cost, and UK AR6 bidding confirms a viable new project pipeline — all three of which have early green shoots — the stock has significant re-rating potential from the current trough valuation [#A]

  • This contrarian case is plausible but not yet evidenced — it requires monitoring over the next 2-4 quarters of results

  • Confidence in thesis: LOW — the recovery thesis is plausible and the valuation trough may be real, but execution risk on the single largest outstanding project (Hornsea 3) and the structural uncertainty around new project economics mean the 18M-3Y EPS trajectory required for a 25%+ IRR remains too uncertain for a high-conviction position at this stage [#J]
