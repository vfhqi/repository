EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (BLUF)

Helios Towers operates a leading independent digital infrastructure platform across nine high-growth emerging markets, executing a robust, inflation-protected colocation model that mechanically converts structural data demand into expanding returns and accelerating shareholder distributions. The company has successfully

transitioned from aggressive footprint expansion toward sustained cash compounding, delivering a rare combination of double-digit organic growth, structurally expanding margins, and rapidly inflecting free cash flow generation within frontier markets. The core business acquires, builds, and operates passive telecommunications infrastructure, providing mission- critical tower site space to mobile network operators while customers maintain active antennae. This infrastructure sharing model reduces operator costs by approximately 30% versus traditional self-build alternatives , creating a powerful value proposition that mathematically justifies the tower company's existence and secures customer compliance with contracted rate increases. Revenues are secured through 10-15 year master lease agreements with minimal cancellation rights and automatic renewal provisions, with the company holding $5.3 billion in contracted future revenue without assuming any new business and an average remaining contract term of 6.6 years as of 2025. Contracts contain embedded protections against local macroeconomic volatility through annual CPI escalators that automatically adjust lease rates and quarterly power price escalators that facilitate direct pass-through of fuel cost fluctuations to customers. This contractual architecture isolates margins from the severe inflation and energy volatility that typically characterizes emerging market operations. 4 Despite operating in emerging markets, 71% of adjusted EBITDA is generated in hard currency, with four of nine markets utilizing US Dollars or Euro-pegged CFA Francs, while remaining markets use contract structures directly linked to hard-currency pricing mechanisms to preserve dollar-denominated returns. This hard-currency exposure dramatically mitigates foreign exchange risk and disconnects operating reality from perceived emerging-market sovereign risk, though severe sovereign debt crises forcing abandonment of currency pegs remains the single largest structural vulnerability. The fundamental economic engine relies on colocation, with a single-tenant build-to-suit tower generating an initial 12% cash-on-cash return on invested capital, while adding a second tenant pushes ROIC to 25% and a third yields 34%. Because site operating and maintenance costs remain largely fixed, incremental tenancy revenues flow almost entirely to profitability, allowing the company to translate 9.8% revenue growth into 12% EBITDA growth in FY25. This extreme operating leverage guarantees structural margin expansion as portfolio tenancy ratios mature over the long term. - Earnings Calls Ten consecutive years of revenue expansion culminated in FY25 revenues of $854.1 million, representing an 8%

margin reached 55% in FY25, expanding by 200 basis points year-on-year through high operating leverage. Return on invested capital climbed to 14.5% in Q1 2026, up from 10% in 2022, comfortably exceeding the company's 11% weighted average cost of capital. Incremental ROIC on growth capital expenditure consistently exceeds 30% , demonstrating exceptional unit economics that drive rapid payback periods.

Free cash flow more than tripled year-on-year to $66.4 million in FY25, with recurring free cash flow growing 40% to $208 million as the business transitioned from heavy investment into a cash-harvesting phase. Net debt to EBITDA finished FY25 at 3.4x, notably lower than European peers at 10.0x, reflecting a conscious deleveraging

The company recently launched an inaugural $75 million share buyback program and a $25 million ordinary dividend policy commencing in FY26, with cumulative distributions through 2030 targeted to exceed $400 million.

African tower markets feature deep structural growth, with the region requiring roughly one million additional towers to match developed-market infrastructure density, while Ericsson forecasts sub-Saharan African mobile data traffic will expand by 5.2x to 5.4x between 2024 and 2030. Tower ownership continues to transfer structurally from mobile network operators to independent tower companies, with independent towercos managing 52% of sub-Saharan towers by 2025 as operators deliberately divest passive infrastructure to release capital for 4G and 5G spectrum acquisitions. This permanent outsourcing trend creates a multi-decade structural tailwind independent of cyclical economic conditions.

Helios operates as the third-largest independent towerco across Africa and the Middle East, managing nearly 15,000 sites while maintaining leading independent market share across seven of its nine operating geographies. The company controls an estimated 68% of marketable independent towers across its active markets, providing massive negotiating leverage when interacting with the three or four mobile operators competing in each country. The massive baseline deficit in African tower infrastructure ensures competitors rarely need to

wars across primary independent African operators. Helios occupies the most profitable, structurally protected infrastructure bottleneck, with tower companies capturing the highest structural margins across the entire telecommunications ecosystem through adjusted EBITDA margins of 56% as of Q1 2026, vastly exceeding the 10-20% margins typical for upstream equipment manufacturers. The fundamental economic engine relies on adding secondary tenants to existing

margins, driving structural expansion of returns on invested capital as portfolio tenancy matures. Despite extracting high margins, Helios provides passive infrastructure at approximately 30% discount to mobile network operators' internal total cost of ownership, creating unique cost arbitrage that mathematically justifies the tower company's existence. 22 Revenue concentration is material but diversified, with Airtel Africa representing 27.9% of total revenue while the top four customers account for 72.3%, though no single customer exceeds 28% of Group revenue. Importantly, 99% of total revenue derives from multinational mobile network operators, with approximately 70% stemming from investment-grade counterparties including Vodacom, Orange, and Omantel , significantly mitigating frontier- market default risks. Customers face severe switching costs, with relocating active radio equipment involving immense physical complexity creating near-insurmountable barriers to exit, estimated at €50,000 per point-of- service in European markets with equally punitive dynamics applicable to African relocations. Diesel fuel constitutes the single most expensive operating input, powering generators in regions where average grid power availability remains limited to approximately 17 hours per day, forcing heavy continuous reliance on upstream fossil fuel supply chains to maintain network uptime. However, embedded quarterly power

escalators effectively shield EBITDA margins from volatile input cost spikes, allowing the operator to directly pass escalating fuel expenses onto downstream mobile networks. Management intentionally maintains up to three months of diesel and critical equipment stock across active markets, with this massive buffer inventory successfully insulating network operations from widespread disruption during the COVID-19 pandemic and ongoing geopolitical conflicts.

Helios enjoys immense structural dominance within its specific footprint, managing approximately 68% of all marketable independent towers across its active nine-market footprint, with the company controlling roughly two- thirds of regional sites in key anchor markets like Tanzania and the Democratic Republic of Congo. Mobile operators are permanently pivoting away from owning passive infrastructure to focus entirely on active network deployment, with independent towercos now controlling approximately 52% of sub-Saharan towers, while operators lack the appetite to reassume the extreme operational complexity of running remote African power grids. Low-earth orbit satellite networks currently function as backhaul partners rather than direct terrestrial competitors, providing essential connectivity to ultra-rural towers while inherent capacity limitations prevent satellite networks from effectively servicing dense, high-traffic urban African centers.

Helios combines an exceptional Lean Six Sigma operating culture with a mechanically compounding colocation model, driving peer-leading 14.5% ROIC and robust recurring free cash flow, with the strategic pivot aligning perfectly with portfolio maturity by extracting >30% incremental returns on invested capital via colocation rather than dilutive acquisitions. Over 63% of the global workforce is Lean Six Sigma certified, driving systematic continuous operational improvement that has delivered ten consecutive years of adjusted EBITDA growth at a 24% compound annual rate persisting through severe macro shocks including COVID-19, oil price spikes, and frontier-market currency devaluations. The operator consistently achieves 99.99% power uptime across deeply power-constrained markets, drastically outperforming regional telecommunications base rates.

African mobile markets face an urgent requirement to bridge the digital divide, with Ericsson forecasting sub- Saharan African mobile data traffic expanding by 5.2x to 5.4x between 2024 and 2030, while populations across the continent are forecast to double by 2050, radically expanding the total addressable market. Building and operating remote African tower grids requires immense upfront capital averaging $100,000 to $150,000 per unit plus months of buffer diesel inventory to guarantee 99.99% network uptime, severely limiting the emergence of new independent challengers. Low-earth orbit satellite networks act as complementary infrastructure rather than existential threats, effectively addressing remote rural coverage gaps where traditional tower economics fail while direct-to-device satellite capacity cannot match the density required to support surging data consumption in urban African centres.

The company faces two primary structural vulnerabilities requiring ongoing monitoring. First, severe sovereign debt crises could force governments to abandon hard-currency pegs in anchor markets, destroying the contractual protections that currently insulate towerco returns despite embedded escalators. Second, while current satellite technology remains complementary, a radical breakthrough in satellite capacity could eventually cannibalize suburban data traffic if direct-to-device technology compounds faster than consensus assumes. Early

warning signals include deterioration in days sales outstanding or rising receivables, indicating blue-chip mobile network operators are struggling to secure hard currency to pay lease invoices. The business model strongly aligns with current investor appetite for predictable, inflation-protected cash compounding, though Helios trades at a 35% discount to US and European peers despite offering superior 11% annual EBITDA growth prospects, with the company recently pivoting to shareholder distributions targeting over $400 million in dividends and buybacks through 2030. The combination of severe physical switching costs, embedded inflation protections, and exponentially growing African mobile data consumption creates an exceptionally durable compounding engine with minimal near-term disruption risk and multi-decade structural growth runways.
