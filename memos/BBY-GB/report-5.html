BBY-GB — Most Recent Earnings Delivery

[C] Research Memo | Triaging Stage | Query 5

Period covered: FY2024 (full year ended 31 December 2024) Results announced: 12 March 2025 Authored: 26-May-26 | Model: Claude Sonnet 4.6

§1 — Executive Summary

  • J-front verdict: FY2024 delivery modestly strengthens the investability thesis on underlying metrics, with a significant non-underlying distortion from legacy liabilities. [#J]
  • Period covered: Full year ended 31 December 2024; group results announced 12 March 2025. [#I]
  • Revenue outcome: Revenue £10,015m, up 4% (6% at CER); beat consensus by approximately 2.2% (Yahoo Finance/Simply Wall St). [#A]
  • Underlying PFO: £252m from earnings-based businesses, up 7%; underlying PBT up 11% to £289m — in-line to modest beat on operating lines. [#A]
  • EPS divergence: Underlying EPS 43.6p, up 17% — strong beat. Reported (total) EPS 34.2p vs prior year 35.3p; Yahoo Finance flagged EPS missed estimates by approximately 17% due to £83m Building Safety Act (BSA) charge classified as non-underlying. [#A]
  • ❌ Non-underlying drag: £49m after-tax non-underlying charge (2023: £11m) — BSA provision and US Civils jury verdict combined to produce a 4x step-up in legacy costs. [#A]
  • Market reaction: Shares rose approximately 9% on results day to ~765p (City A.M.); buyback uplift to £125m for 2025 was the primary driver. [#I]
  • Single most important finding: The £18.4bn order book (+12%) underpins 2025 and 2026 growth visibility; however, the US Civils margin deterioration (1.4% → 1.1%) and the BSA provision represent active risks that must resolve before thesis conviction can escalate. [#J]
  • Sceptical anchor: Reported EPS declined year-on-year (34.2p vs 35.3p); a shareholder buying on headline underlying numbers faces rising legacy charge risk that could structurally compress reported earnings. [#J]

§2 — Earnings Delivery and Beat/Miss Analysis

  • J-front verdict: Underlying metrics delivered a clean beat on operating lines; the statutory picture was a miss, driven entirely by two lumpy non-recurring charges. [#J]
  • Revenue beat: Group revenue £10,015m vs consensus ~£9,800m — approximately 2.2% beat; Gammon (+14% revenue) and Support Services (+20% revenue) led. [#A]
  • Guidance context: January 2025 Trading Update guided for "further growth"; management did not provide a specific revenue range, so directional in-line. [#I]
  • EBIT drivers: UK Construction PFO £81m vs £69m (prior year) — UK was the beat driver; offset by US Construction PFO £40m vs £51m — US was the material drag. [#A]
  • ❌ US Construction miss: Underlying PFO down 22% year-on-year to £40m; small number of fixed-price civils projects with schedule overruns inflated costs; peers Morgan Sindall (MGNS) delivered margin expansion in same period (adjusted margin 3.6% vs 3.4% prior). [#A]
  • Support Services beat: PFO up 16% to £93m at 7.7% margin — strong absolute and directional outperformance. [#A]
  • Underlying EPS beat: 43.6p vs 37.3p (prior year) — 17% increase; buyback-enhanced through share count reduction (521m vs 558m shares). [#A]
  • Reported EPS miss: 34.2p vs 35.3p (prior year); Yahoo Finance flagged ~17% miss vs analyst estimates — attributable to non-underlying BSA and US Civils charges. [#A]
  • One-off quality: Non-underlying charge £49m after-tax (2023: £11m); £83m BSA provision + £52m US Civils jury verdict (Group's 40% share of $112m) — both arguably non-recurrent but require monitoring. [#A]
  • BSA trajectory: First year BSA has been classified non-underlying due to scale; risk is further provisions in 2025-2026 as legal landscape continues to evolve. [#J]
  • Sceptical anchor: The pattern of growing non-underlying charges (£11m → £49m after-tax) warrants attention; if BSA provisions continue to scale, the split between "underlying" and "reported" risks becoming a recurring presentation issue. [#J]

§3 — Revenue and Organic Growth

  • J-front verdict: Revenue growth solid at 4% reported (6% CER) with genuine organic volume increases in two out of three construction divisions; no red flags on recognition quality. [#J]
  • Reported group revenue: £10,015m (2023: £9,595m); +4.4% reported; +6% constant exchange rate (CER). [#I]
  • FX impact: Negative currency translation on US and Hong Kong operations reduced reported growth by approximately 2 percentage points. [#I]
  • UK Construction: Revenue £3,011m vs £3,027m (prior year) — broadly flat (-0.5%); volume held despite project selectivity on new bids. [#A]
  • US Construction: Revenue £3,638m vs £3,697m (prior year) — flat (-1.6% reported, c.+2% CER); buildings growth offset by civils drag. [#A]
  • Gammon (HK): Revenue £1,550m vs £1,357m — +14% reported; standout growth, though margin held flat at 2.5%. [#A]
  • Support Services: Revenue £1,210m vs £1,006m — +20%; power transmission and distribution the dominant driver. [#A]
  • Order book: £18.4bn at year-end vs £16.5bn (2023) — +12%; power transmission doubled its order book in the year; US buildings order book +26% (24% CER). [#I]
  • ⚡ Power transmission trajectory: Order book growth of over 100% in a single year for UK energy is a structural signal; SSEN ASTI framework (c.£10bn) and Skye 132kV project (£690m) provide pipeline beyond the reported order book. [#J]
  • Revenue recognition: No specific pull-forward or deferral signals identified in management commentary; UK Construction's flat volume despite a growing market suggests deliberate selectivity not demand shortfall. [#A]
  • Peer reference: Morgan Sindall (MGNS) delivered group revenue of £4,546m (+10%); Kier Group (KIE) delivered £4bn revenue (+16.6% for year to June 2024) — both growing faster in revenue terms, though off smaller bases. [#A]
  • Sceptical anchor: Revenue growth at 4% is below Morgan Sindall (+10%) and Kier (+17%) on a peer comparison; BBY's deliberate volume-cap strategy (higher bid margins, selective contracting) is the stated rationale but limits near-term revenue upside. [#J]

§4 — Margin Profile and Quality

  • J-front verdict: UK Construction margin improvement is the standout structural signal; US Construction margin deterioration is the key watch item. [#J]
  • Group blended EBIT margin: Underlying PFO/revenue (earnings-based) = 2.7% (2023: 2.6%) — 10bps improvement; modest but directionally consistent. [#A]
  • UK Construction margin: 2.7% (2023: 2.3%) — +40bps year-on-year improvement; seventh consecutive year of margin improvement; driven by higher-quality, target-cost and cost-plus contracts. [#A]
  • ⚡ UK margin trajectory: UK Construction margin has expanded from sub-2% in 2020 to 2.7% in 2024; this is a multi-year structural improvement, not a one-year event; reflects a deliberate contract mix shift away from fixed-price work. [#J]
  • ❌ US Construction margin: 1.1% (2023: 1.4%) — -30bps deterioration; fixed-price civils projects with schedule overruns; buildings remained robust but was insufficient to offset civils drag. [#A]
  • Gammon margin: 2.5% (2023: 2.7%) — slight compression despite 14% revenue growth; input cost and labour dynamics in HK region. [#A]
  • Support Services margin: 7.7% (2023: 8.0%) — slight compression despite 20% revenue growth; management commentary: "close to the top of its targeted range". [#A]
  • Infrastructure Investments: Pre-disposals operating loss of £8m (2023: profit £5m); gains on disposals £43m (2023: £26m) — profitability dependent on disposal timing. [#I]
  • Cost dynamics: Management cited discipline in labour procurement through early subcontract issuance in US buildings; UK power transmission hired 500+ new starters; voluntary attrition fell from 12% to 10% in UK. [#I]
  • Structural vs transient assessment: UK Construction improvement is structural (contract mix, governance frameworks); US margin dip appears transient (delayed civils projects; management guided improvement in 2025); Support Services compression is volume-driven (mix of lower-margin power work scaling up). [#J]
  • Peer comparison: Morgan Sindall adjusted operating margin 3.6% (+20bps); Kier adjusted operating margin 3.8% — both peers above BBY's blended construction margin; BBY's PPP/Investments portfolio differentiates the group-level return profile. [#A]
  • Sceptical anchor: At 1.1%, US Construction operates with minimal margin for error on fixed-price civils; a further adverse ruling or project delay could push the division into loss. [#J]

§5 — Cash Flow and Capital Allocation

  • J-front verdict: Cash position is a genuine competitive strength; average net cash of £766m and year-end £943m demonstrate that negative working capital structural advantage is intact. [#J]
  • Average net cash (recourse): £766m (2023: £700m) — 9.4% increase year-on-year; guided £800m for 2025. [#I]
  • Year-end net cash (recourse): £943m (2023: £842m); excludes non-recourse project-level borrowings and lease liabilities. [#I]
  • Cash generation quality: Construction businesses generate negative working capital (advance payments exceed work-in-progress billings); this is structural, not a function of favourable timing. [#A]
  • Working capital: Favourable working capital £1,228m (2023: £1,232m); negative working capital as % of revenue 14.9% (2023: 15.4%) — slight compression but structurally robust. [#I]
  • Cash conversion: Underlying PFO from earnings-based businesses £252m; group-level average cash £766m implies very strong cash-to-earnings ratio; specific operating cash flow not isolated in headline numbers. [#A]
  • Capital expenditure guidance: £35–40m for 2025 (management guidance); low capex intensity consistent with asset-light construction model. [#I]
  • Shareholder returns: £161m returned in 2024 (dividends + buybacks); 2025 total expected cash return c.£188m (+17%). [#I]
  • Dividend: Recommended final dividend 8.7p per share; full year 12.5p (2023: 11.5p) — 9% increase; progressive policy intact. [#I]
  • Buyback: £125m buyback confirmed for 2025; cumulative return since 2021 capital allocation framework introduction over £940m. [#I]
  • PPP recycling: Infrastructure Investments gains on disposals £43m (2023: £26m); Directors' valuation £1.3bn (+3%); two new projects added; 2025 disposal gains guided £20–30m — slightly lower than 2024. [#I]
  • ⚡ PPP portfolio value: £1.3bn Directors' valuation against a market cap of approximately £2.5bn (at ~500p) implies the investments portfolio represents c.52% of market cap — a substantial embedded asset often underappreciated by the market. [#J]
  • Sceptical anchor: BSA cash outflows expected "over a number of years" — management has not quantified the timing; this represents an unknown call on the cash pile. [#J]

§6 — Management Guidance and Forward Outlook

  • J-front verdict: Guidance is clear, directional and conservative by historic pattern; specific quantification is limited but the directional signals are unambiguously positive. [#J]
  • PFO guidance 2025: "Increase in PFO from earnings-based businesses in 2025"; further growth in 2026 also signalled. [#I]
  • Margin guidance: Growth in Construction Services "weighted towards further margin improvement rather than higher volumes" — explicit statement that margin expansion is the primary mechanism. [#A]
  • US Construction recovery: US Construction PFO expected to improve in 2025 as delayed civils projects approach completion — management statement; timeline unquantified. [#I]
  • Support Services growth: Driven by expansion in power transmission and distribution — "not reliant on Government funding or the ongoing comprehensive spending review" — important resilience qualifier. [#I]
  • Net cash guidance: Average net cash 2025 guided at approximately £800m vs £766m in 2024 (+4.4%). [#I]
  • CapEx guidance: £35–40m for 2025 — broadly flat. [#I]
  • Investments disposals guidance: Gain on disposals £20–30m for 2025 vs £43m in 2024 — guided reduction in disposal gains. [#A]
  • Order book guidance: Not explicitly quantified in FY2024 results; but subsequent reporting (March 2026 results) revealed order book reached £22.7bn (+23% from £18.4bn) — confirms guidance materialised. [#I]
  • Guidance track record: BBY has a consistent track record of conservative guidance; management has delivered PFO growth in each of the last several years against stated targets; no guidance cuts in recent memory. [#J]
  • Labour government risk: Initial concern that Labour infrastructure cutbacks would impact volumes; management resolved this by citing energy transition, defence, and transport commitments as non-discretionary; this concern appears to have abated. [#A]
  • Tariff risk (US): Management flagged awareness of US tariff environment in 2025 commentary; early subcontracting and supply chain insurance cited as structural mitigants. [#I]
  • Sceptical anchor: "Further growth in 2026" is a single-line statement with no quantification; the lack of numerical guidance ranges limits independent verification of management's medium-term confidence level. [#J]

§7 — Strategic Execution

  • J-front verdict: Strategic execution is on track across all four growth markets; the power transmission acceleration and US buildings order book growth are the two most material strategic developments in the period. [#J]
  • Four chosen markets: UK energy (power transmission/distribution), UK defence, UK transport, and US buildings — all showed positive progress. [#I]
  • Power transmission — standout: Order book for UK power transmission more than doubled in 2024; SSEN ASTI framework (~£10bn) and two SCAPE Civil Engineering frameworks extended two years; Skye 132kV reinforcement project (£690m) added to pipeline. [#A]
  • US buildings expansion: Order book +26% (24% CER) to £7.1bn; organic growth strategy winning new geographies (education, aviation, hospitality); data centre demand cited as a rising opportunity. [#A]
  • UK defence — new win: Selected by Rolls-Royce as construction partner for MoD and AUKUS expansion — new strategic relationship in FY2024. [#I]
  • Hinkley Point C: BBY involvement in nuclear construction (Hinkley C, Net Zero Teesside) positions the group for next generation nuclear work. [#I]
  • Safety record: Record LTIR of 0.09 (2023: 0.11); three-day LTIR 0.07 (2023: 0.08); observations raised 470,000+ (2023: 400,000) — continuous improvement on safety metrics. [#I]
  • Employee engagement: 84% (2023: 81%), 11 percentage points above the industry average — seventh successive year of improvement. [#I]
  • PPP portfolio: Directors' valuation £1.3bn (+3%); two new projects added; disposal gains £43m — portfolio managed actively within capital allocation framework. [#I]
  • BSA — strategic risk: £83m provision relates to pre-2022 residential work; BBY has largely exited residential construction but legacy exposure is material; ongoing legal developments create future provision risk. [#J]
  • ❌ US Civils — operational miss: Fixed-price civils projects delayed; root cause not fully disclosed; management guided improvement in 2025 but the inability to exit problem contracts is a structural risk of fixed-price exposure. [#J]
  • Multi-year plan status: Group is on track against its broader ambition; 2024 marks the fifth or sixth year of consecutive underlying PFO growth since the 2016 turnaround under Leo Quinn. [#I]
  • Sceptical anchor: BBY's strategic roadmap is heavily reliant on the UK government's multi-year infrastructure commitment materialising on schedule; the spending review remains ongoing and could still introduce phasing delays. [#J]

  • Consensus EPS revisions: Post-results consensus analyst price target rose from approximately £6.39 to £6.57 (+2.8%); small positive revision reflecting underlying beat offset by non-underlying drag. [#A]
  • Bull narrative: Order book at £18.4bn providing 2-year earnings visibility; power transmission market share expanding; buyback programme compressing share count materially; underlying EPS growth strong (+17%). [#J]
  • Bear narrative: Non-underlying charges growing (£11m → £49m); US Construction structurally challenged in fixed-price civils; reported EPS declined year-on-year; BSA provision could escalate. [#J]
  • Analyst price target range: Investec at 600p (bear); Berenberg at 760p (bull); consensus approximately 657p — mid-point approximately 680p. [#A]

§9 — Sector Context and Sceptical Synthesis

  • J-front verdict: BBY delivery is ahead of the sector on quality metrics (cash, order book, margin trajectory) but behind peers on revenue growth; this reflects strategy not underperformance. [#J]
  • Morgan Sindall comparison (MGNS): MGNS revenue +10% vs BBY +4%; MGNS adjusted margin 3.6% vs BBY construction margin 2.7%; MGNS delivered in-line vs consensus in FY2024 — MGNS superior on revenue growth but lacks BBY's US/PPP diversification. [#A]
  • ❌ Kier Group comparison (KIE): Kier FY2024 (year to June 2024) revenue +16.6% to £4bn; adjusted operating margin 3.8% — structurally higher than BBY on UK construction margin alone; though Kier is purely domestic and much smaller (£4bn vs £10bn). [#A]
  • Galliford Try comparison (GFRD): Revenue +27.2% to £1.8bn in FY2024; pre-tax profit up 205.9% to £30.9m; rapid small-cap growth off a low base — not directly comparable in scale or complexity. [#A]
  • Sector-wide tailwinds (real): UK Labour government committed to infrastructure acceleration; energy transition driving power transmission volumes across all listed contractors; SSEN, National Grid and similar capex programmes benefit multiple peers simultaneously. [#A]
  • Company-specific vs sector-wide: Power transmission boom benefits BBY disproportionately given its scale and frameworks; US buildings and data centre exposure are company-specific growth engines absent from domestic-only peers. [#J]
  • Macro risks: US tariff environment flagged by management; UK comprehensive spending review ongoing; both represent exogenous risks but management has provided structural mitigants (early US subcontracting, non-government-dependent Support Services). [#A]
  • Input cost environment: Materials and labour costs stabilising relative to 2022-2023; voluntary attrition improvement (12% → 10%) suggests labour market normalisation in BBY's favour; no red flags in management commentary on cost escalation. [#A]
  • Construction sector cycle context: UK public infrastructure spending rising after years of austerity; private data centre and energy investment accelerating; the macro backdrop in 2024 was genuinely supportive for the sector. [#A]
  • Order book as quality signal: £18.4bn order book at 1.8x annual revenue provides c.22 months of forward visibility — peer-leading in scale; MGNS order book approximately £9.9bn (2.2x revenue); both are well-positioned on visibility. [#A]
  • Non-underlying charge risk (sector-specific): BSA is a UK-construction sector issue affecting multiple contractors; BBY's £83m provision is material in absolute terms; Kier and others face similar exposure — this is sector risk not isolated to BBY. [#A]

Final Verdict — ESA Progression Assessment

  • Does FY2024 delivery support progression to ESA stage? Conditionally yes. [#J]
  • For: Underlying EPS growth +17%; UK Construction margin trajectory is a genuine multi-year structural improvement signal; order book +12% with quality characteristics (target-cost weighting); PPP portfolio generating £43m disposal gains; cash pile building; buyback programme compounding per-share value. [#J]
  • Against: Reported EPS declined; US Construction margin deteriorated materially; BSA provision signals latent legacy risk; non-underlying charges quadrupled year-on-year; revenue growth lags domestic peers. [#J]
  • Net assessment: The underlying business is performing well and is competitively positioned. The key ESA questions are: (1) whether BSA exposure is fully provisioned or will recur; (2) whether US Civils margin recovers as guided; (3) whether the power transmission market share gain is durable and defensible. These are change-thesis questions appropriate to ESA. [#J]
