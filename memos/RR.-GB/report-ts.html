Section 2 – The 80/20 (bottom line up front) 9 findings

Section summary
  1. Rolls-Royce is delivering a historic earnings turnaround driven by pricing power and widebody engine recovery.
1.
Primary business: Rolls-Royce designs large commercial turbofans and generates predictable service revenues through pay-by-the-hour maintenance agreements
2.
Investment thesis: Restructured contract terms on legacy passenger agreements combined with a sharp cyclical recovery in commercial travel have unlocked unprecedented free cash flow
3.
Swing factor: The most critical driver of forward EPS remains widebody engine flying hours, which dictate pay-by-the-hour services billing
4.
Multiyear profit: Underlying operating profit reached £3.46bn in FY25, rising five times higher than the levels achieved in FY22
5.
Cash conversion: Free cash flow expanded to £3.27bn in FY25 from just £505m in FY22, proving excellent cash generation capacity

Destructive risk: Severe supply chain constraints or geopolitical disruption in flight corridors could constrain engine delivery volumes and increase shop visit costs

8.

Section 3 – What the company does 16 findings

Section summary
  1. The commercial engine business is protected by high entry barriers and generates highly predictable service cash flows.

Divisional business segments

1.
Widebody civil: Civil Aerospace designs large gas turbines and services commercial aircraft, making up over half of underlying revenues
2.
National defense: The Defence division provides military aero-engines and supports nuclear propulsion reactors for the UK Royal Navy's submarine fleet
3.
Onsite power: Power Systems manufactures diesel and gas propulsion and power generation units for data centres and governmental marine clients
4.
Core nuclear: The Small Modular Reactor division operates in adjacent low-carbon nuclear power generation, securing strategic European early works contracts

Aftermarket service economics

5.
Captive services: Aftermarket services generate more than half of group revenue, locking airlines into high-margin long-term agreements
6.
Contractual pricing: Approximately 90% of the active commercial fleet is contracted under long-term service agreements based on pay-by-the-hour billing
7.
Onerous renegotiations: Strategic renegotiations of legacy unprofitable agreements have systematically shifted maintenance cost risks back to airline operators
8.
Delivery deficit: Commercial engines are typically sold at a cash loss, making aftermarket the primary profit center for the Civil division
9.
Predictable hours: Aftermarket cash generation is highly predictable as airline utilization patterns dictate long-term cash inflows on a monthly basis

Industry value chain and competitive moat

10.
High barriers: Massive capital, strict safety certification, and extreme engineering complexity preserve a tight global engine duopoly in widebodies
11.
Capture advantage: Powering four out of five new widebody aircraft establishes a major captive service pipeline for the next two decades
12.
Switching friction: Airline customers face prohibitive switching costs as replacing engine platforms requires complete aircraft re-engineering or fleet replacement
13.
Industry consolidated: The commercial aero-engine space remains highly consolidated, allowing dominant players to protect pricing and drive aftermarket value
14.
First-mover SMR: Contractual wins in Great Britain and Czechia position Rolls-Royce SMR as a clear first-mover in the European nuclear market
15.
Other side of the story: Technical challenges and unexpected durability issues on newer platforms can lead to severe aircraft on ground grounding penalties and compensation that erode service profitability

Section summary
  1. Restructuring initiatives have unlocked intense operating leverage, driving historic high operating margins and return on capital.

Multiyear financial trajectory

1.
Revenue growth: Statutory revenue expanded from £18,909m in FY24 to £21,207m in FY25, supported by robust demand across core segments

Margin expansion: The group statutory operating margin reached 17.3% in FY25, tripling from the margin level recorded in FY22

EPS surge: Statutory basic EPS more than doubled from 30.05p in FY24 to 69.41p in FY25, showing impressive operating leverage
4.
Free cash inflection: Free cash flow grew to £3.27bn in FY25 from £2.43bn in FY24, showing a rapidly expanding liquidity base

Balance sheet strength and capital return

6.
Net cash position: Net cash reached £1.9bn at the end of FY25, comparing highly favorably to a net cash position of just £475m in FY24
7.
Capital efficiency: Return on capital surged to 18.9% in FY25, up from 13.8% in FY24, representing superior industry-wide capital allocation
8.
Debt reduction: The company repaid a €750m bond in February 2026, continuing to de-risk its capital structure
9.
Buyback scale: A multi-year share buyback program of £7bn to £9bn was announced for 2026-2028, with £2.5bn allocated for FY26
10.
Credit ratings: Solid operational progress led Fitch and Moody's to upgrade credit ratings to A- and A3 respectively

Performance quality and execution

11.
Operating cost discipline: Total cash costs as a proportion of gross profit fell to 0.36x in FY25, demonstrating rigorous cost discipline across segments
12.

13.

14.
Operating confirmation: In April 2026, the company confirmed FY26 guidance, targeting £4.0bn to £4.2bn in underlying operating profit
15.
Other side of the story: Persistent supply chain bottlenecks in commercial aerospace continue to limit new engine deliveries, imposing an estimated £150m to £200m cash drag in FY26

Section 5 – The possible investment case 17 findings

Section summary
  1. The investment case is highly compelling on structural turnaround self-help and excellent visibility of aftermarket services.

Turnaround and pricing optimization

1.
Contract resets: Legacy unprofitable long-term service agreements are being aggressively repriced to reflect current high maintenance costs
2.
Price plausibility: This driver is highly plausible as the most significant onerous agreements have already been successfully renegotiated with airlines
3.
Pricing failure: High wage inflation could escalate shop visit costs beyond contractual indexation caps, squeezing Civil Aerospace operating margins
4.
Durability enhancements: A massive £1.0bn investment is being deployed to extend time on wing across the Trent engine families
5.
Durability plausibility: Plausibility is high as certified High Pressure Turbine blade enhancements have already successfully doubled durability in operation
6.
Durability failure: Unanticipated technical failure modes or material wear in operational service could force unexpected engine groundings, eroding service margins

Cyclical widebody aviation recovery

7.
Flight activity: Under long-term service contracts, engine flying hours directly translate into highly profitable services billing
8.
Activity plausibility: Plausibility is high as Q1 FY26 Engine Flying Hours reached 115% of 2019 levels, tracking well against guidance of 115-120%
9.
Activity failure: A major global recession or renewed travel restrictions would suppress international flying hours, directly reducing cash inflows
10.
Fleet youth: Average engine fleet age is below 10 years, ensuring high airline utilization and a prolonged service revenue runway

Portfolio diversification drivers

11.
Data centre growth: Power Systems is capturing rapid structural growth in mission-critical back-up generators for hyperscale AI data centres
12.
Power plausibility: Plausibility is high, evidenced by a massive 50% year-on-year increase in gas and diesel generator orders in Q1 FY26
13.
Power failure: Severe global delays in data centre grid connections or construction could slow generator delivery schedules and revenue recognition
14.
Bull scenario: To achieve the full bull case, widebody engine flight hours and pricing resets must sustain a simultaneous positive trajectory
15.
Independent segments: Fortunately, these drivers are independent, meaning robust demand in Power Systems can insulate group earnings from localized aerospace shocks
16.
Other side of the story: Elevated capital expenditure demands for long-term options like SMR nuclear and narrowbody re-entry could limit cash available for buybacks if commercial trends soften

Market expectations: The current valuation suggests the market is pricing in immediate turnaround goals while partially discounting long-term value from nuclear SMR projects
6.
Other side of the story: If engine flying hours contraction occurs in FY26, the valuation premium could rapidly deflate to historical multiples around pre-pandemic levels of 13x EV/EBITDA

Section 7 – Key risks and the other side 9 findings

Section summary
  1. The risk landscape is consistently improving although supply chain constraints and engine durability demand constant diligence.

Operational and durability risks

1.
Material shortages: Prolonged supply chain delays in titanium casting and precision parts could continue to constrain engine delivery volumes and delay shop visits
2.
Durability risks: Any premature wear or structural cracking on newly deployed turbine blades could trigger massive warranty cash outflows and fleet grounding penalties
3.
Customer friction: High-profile disputes with airlines over engine durability highlights a potential risk of near-term reputational damage
4.
Operational warning: The monthly count of aircraft-on-ground for the global fleet remains the most sensitive indicator of engine operational health

Geopolitical and financial risks

5.
Traffic exposure: A severe escalation of conflict in the Middle East represents a key risk that could negatively impact long-haul air traffic flying hours
6.
Contractual protection: The company is insulated as long-term service agreements contain minimum flying hour clauses, providing stable cash flow protection
7.
False friend: Substantial cash contract catch-up revenue can look favorable but may temporarily mask a structural decline in core cash collection
8.
Other side of the story: The worst-case bear scenario of a complete turnaround collapse is highly unlikely given that systemic technical issues across the Trent fleet have been resolved through extensive blade redesigns
