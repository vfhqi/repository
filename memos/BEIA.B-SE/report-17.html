2. KEY FINDINGS - Structurally multi-currency: Beijer Alma reports in SEK but derives the majority of revenue outside Sweden — EUR, USD, GBP, and a tail of Nordic/EM currencies collectively represent the dominant P&L exposure, making SEK strength the primary translation headwind risk for this reporting-currency investor

  • Translation exposure dominates transaction exposure: The group structure — distinct operating subsidiaries in their own functional currencies — means most FX impact flows through the translation line rather than the transaction line; management largely accepts this and does not hedge it

  • Lesjöfors vs Habia Cable asymmetry matters: The two operating segments carry materially different currency profiles — Lesjöfors (springs/pressings) operates across Europe and Asia with broad currency scatter; Habia Cable (specialty cable) is more EUR/USD-centric with Russian-market exposure now structurally zeroed post-2022 (binary event already realised)

  • Hedging policy is limited and transaction-focused: Beijer Alma hedges transactional exposures at subsidiary level on a rolling short-horizon basis (typically ≤12M); group-level translation is unhedged by policy — this is explicitly stated in annual report risk disclosures

  • EUR/SEK is the dominant sensitivity axis: EUR is the largest single foreign currency in revenue; a 10% SEK appreciation vs EUR reduces group EBITDA by an estimated SEK 150-200M (approximately 5-7% of group EBITDA at 2024 run-rate), based on triangulated geographic revenue mix

  • USD matters but is secondary: USD exposure primarily via Habia Cable's international sales and Lesjöfors' North American operations; estimated 10% USD/SEK move → SEK 60-90M EBITDA impact (2-3% of group EBITDA)

  • Macro FX outlook N12-24M is modestly adverse for SEK-reporting companies: Consensus EUR/SEK in the 11.0-11.5 range through 2026 implies limited further SEK weakness from current levels; rate-differential thesis (Riksbank cutting faster than ECB) has substantially played out — balance of risks tilts toward mild SEK recovery, which is a mild headwind for translated earnings

  • L3Y FX has been a net tailwind: SEK weakness 2022-2024 inflated reported revenue and EBITDA in SEK terms; this tailwind is fading and partially reversing — investors pricing 2025-26 on L3Y reported growth should strip out 2-4% annualised FX flattery

  • Hedging discipline rated below "boring hedger" standard: Disclosure is thin relative to peers; no explicit hedge ratio disclosed at group level, no instrument-by-instrument breakdown, no realised effectiveness ratio in public filings — this opacity is the primary governance flag on FX for this stock

  • FX resilience of thesis: medium plausibility — thesis survives a neutral-to-modest-adverse FX environment but is meaningfully dented in a sustained SEK recovery scenario (EUR/SEK 10.5 or below), which would mechanically compress reported EBITDA by 8-12% vs consensus without organic offset

§4 — Transaction Exposure (Economic Impact)

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is real but secondary to translation; concentrated in Habia copper/USD and cross-border intra-group flows; partially but incompletely mitigated by pricing formulas

Transaction exposure points identified:

  • Habia Cable — copper procurement: Copper purchased at LME USD prices; Habia's sales are a mix of EUR and USD-priced contracts; the mismatch between USD input and EUR-priced output creates a genuine transactional cross-currency exposure — estimated net transaction exposure from copper alone at ~USD 40-70M equivalent per annum, after natural offsets from USD-priced sales

  • Lesjöfors — intra-group and cross-border supply: Some Lesjöfors entities source materials or semi-finished goods from sister entities in different currency zones; these intra-group transactions create minor transactional exposures at entity level that are eliminated on consolidation but managed at subsidiary level

  • USD-invoiced international Lesjöfors sales: Lesjöfors sells some export volumes in USD (particularly to non-European markets and US subsidiaries manage local pricing); genuine transaction exposure where USD revenues are earned into SEK or EUR cost bases

  • Transaction vs translation magnitude: At current scale, transaction exposure (primarily Habia copper) is estimated SEK 50-150M EBITDA sensitivity for a 10% adverse move — smaller than the pure translation sensitivity of SEK 150-250M for the same 10% EUR/SEK move; translation dominates the total FX sensitivity picture

  • Pricing pass-through — Habia copper: Habia uses LME-linked pricing formulas for copper in most medium-term customer contracts; this means copper price (and its USD denomination) is substantially passed through to customers on a lagged basis; the lag is typically 1-3 months; the residual transaction risk is the lag exposure and any fixed-price short-term contracts that cannot adjust

  • Pricing pass-through — Lesjöfors: Steel/wire rod cost pass-through is less systematised than copper (no LME-linked formula); Lesjöfors has demonstrated meaningful pricing power in the 2021-2023 inflationary cycle, passing through steel cost increases with lags of 2-6 months; in a pure currency-driven cost move (not commodity price move), the pass-through mechanism is weaker because customers resist currency as a justification for price increases

  • Pricing power limits in transaction stress: The critical distinction is commodity pass-through (Habia copper — accepted by customers because it's LME-observable) vs pure currency pass-through (Lesjöfors SEK/EUR moves — less accepted); if SEK appreciates while steel prices are flat, Lesjöfors cannot invoke commodity pass-through to offset the margin pressure on its SEK cost base vs EUR revenue

  • Cash-flow currency mismatch at remittance level: Subsidiary dividends upstream to the Swedish holding entity are typically remitted in local currency and converted to SEK; in a SEK-appreciation environment, the SEK value of these dividend flows falls even if local-currency performance is unchanged; this is a translation effect at the cash level, not a structural transaction risk

  • Sceptical bullet — transaction exposure: The apparent comfort from Habia's LME-linked pricing formulas conceals a timing trap: in a rapid USD spike scenario (e.g. +15% USD/EUR in 90 days, as occurred in early 2022), Habia's copper costs spike immediately while the LME-linked customer price adjustment lags 60-90 days; during this window, Habia's working capital absorbs the mismatch and cash conversion deteriorates sharply; if Habia carries a meaningful order backlog at fixed prices, the loss crystallises on that backlog before the formula adjusts; Lesjöfors' pass-through in a currency-only shock (no commodity move) has even less formula protection and could see a full quarter of margin compression before pricing reflects the FX reality

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Hedging policy is limited, transaction-focused at subsidiary level, explicitly non-hedging at translation level; disclosure quality is below the "boring hedger" standard — inadequate for precise programme assessment

Hedging mandate (from annual report disclosures):
- Beijer Alma's stated policy: subsidiaries hedge "commercially significant" foreign currency transaction exposures; group does not hedge translation exposure
- No group-level hedging mandate with explicit ratio or duration target is disclosed in the public annual report
- The hedging programme is decentralised — each operating subsidiary (Lesjöfors entities, Habia) manages its own transactional hedging within group policy guidelines

Hedge horizon:
- Annual report indicates subsidiaries use forward contracts and in some cases options to hedge transactional exposures; horizon referenced is typically ≤12 months, consistent with annual cycle
- No multi-year hedging ladder disclosed; no evidence of 18M+ horizon hedging in public disclosures

Hedge ratio:
- No explicit hedge ratio disclosed at group or subsidiary level in public filings — this is a critical disclosure gap
- Inferred from disclosure language: "material" transactional exposures are hedged, implying partial coverage rather than comprehensive; best estimate based on language calibration is 50-80% of identified transactional exposures, but this is not confirmable from public data

Hedge instruments:
- Forward exchange contracts: confirmed in annual report derivative notes (IFRS 7 disclosures)
- Currency options: referenced but not quantified
- Cross-currency swaps: no evidence in notes — consistent with no translation hedging
- Instrument concentration: no counterparty concentration flag raised in the annual report; given Beijer Alma's size, likely uses 2-4 relationship bank counterparties for derivatives

Translation hedging:
- Explicitly absent by policy — management accepts translation risk as inherent to the multi-national industrial structure; this is conventional and appropriate for the business model

Transaction hedging:
- Active at subsidiary level; forward contracts the primary instrument; Habia's copper LME-linked pricing formula is the most structurally robust hedge (operating-side hedge) supplemented by financial forwards for the residual

Hedge effectiveness L3Y:
- IFRS 9 hedge effectiveness ratios not explicitly published in the annual report; the notes disclose the notional value of derivative instruments outstanding and the fair value gain/loss, but effectiveness percentages are not stated
- Inferred effectiveness: the alignment of notional instruments to identified exposures and the absence of any large unexpected FX losses in the notes suggests broadly effective hedging of the targeted (transactional) exposures in normal conditions
- FY2022 was the real stress test — USD +20% vs SEK; the derivative notes for FY2022 showed modest fair-value gains on USD forward contracts, consistent with effective hedging of the hedged portion but no coverage of translation exposure

Hedge cost:
- Not explicitly disclosed; at current interest rate differentials (SEK vs EUR, SEK vs USD), forward points on EUR/SEK are approximately neutral to modestly adverse for a SEK-based hedger; USD/SEK hedging carries a modest positive roll (USD rates above SEK rates at the margin of the curve), meaning hedging USD receivables via forwards is marginally costly relative to unhedged

Recent hedge realisation:
- FY2022 and FY2023 derivative notes show net gains on derivative instruments (benefiting from USD strength and EUR strength vs SEK) — consistent with transaction hedging partially offsetting FX moves on specific cash flows, while allowing full translation uplift to flow through (since translation was unhedged)

Disclosure quality assessment — below "boring hedger" standard:
- The "boring hedger" standard requires: explicit hedge ratio, named horizon, realised effectiveness ratio, instrument breakdown, quarterly position summaries; Beijer Alma provides: yes instruments (forwards + options), yes outstanding notional, yes fair-value gain/loss — but NO explicit ratio, NO explicit horizon, NO effectiveness percentage
- This places Beijer Alma at approximately "partial transparency" — better than pure obfuscation but well below best-practice disclosure

Sceptical bullet — hedging discipline: The combination of decentralised execution, absence of disclosed ratios and horizons, and limited group-level oversight creates execution risk that does not appear in the headline "we hedge our material transactional exposures" language; in a stress scenario where subsidiary financial controllers are managing their own hedge books independently, there is no assurance that the group's aggregate position is coordinated — two subsidiaries could theoretically take offsetting hedges that collectively cancel out and cost premium without net group protection; additionally, the short (≤12M) horizon means a sustained multi-year FX headwind (e.g. 3-year SEK appreciation cycle) is entirely unhedged at the transactional level after the initial hedge roll expires

Hedge break point analysis:
- The ≤12M forward-contract horizon means any sustained FX move beyond 12 months is entirely unhedged at the transactional level
- At ±15% EUR/SEK sustained for >12M: hedge rolls expire into unfavourable rates; new hedges lock in the unfavourable level; the economic loss becomes permanent, not temporary — this is the critical non-linearity
- At ±25% EUR/SEK sustained: pricing power erodes for Lesjöfors (customers resist currency-driven price increases); EBITDA margin compression accelerates; EPS impact is non-linear because revenue base is now compressed AND margin is compressed simultaneously

Second-order pricing pass-through:
- Habia Cable: copper pass-through via LME formula — operates with 1-3M lag; effective for commodity price moves; less effective for pure currency moves where copper price is flat but USD/SEK has moved; in a USD-spike-with-flat-copper scenario, Habia's formula may not trigger, leaving currency mismatch un-passed-through
- Lesjöfors: steel pass-through relies on commercial negotiation, not formula; works with 2-6M lag in inflationary cycles; in a pure currency stress (no commodity inflation), customers resist; pass-through rate in currency-only scenario estimated at 30-50% of the theoretical full pass-through — the remaining 50-70% compresses Lesjöfors margins until the next contract cycle

Benchmarking management's disclosed sensitivity vs L3Y empirical record:
- Management does not publish an explicit sensitivity table in the annual report (this is a disclosure gap — the memo does not have a management sensitivity figure to benchmark against)
