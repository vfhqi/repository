1. Company Description

Nexans is a French pure-play electrification company — the transformation from diversified conglomerate to focused electrification leader is nearly complete.

  • Nexans SA, Euronext Paris (NEX.PA). Market cap ~€5.2bn. HQ Paris. ~25,678 employees, 41 countries, 45 manufacturing plants. #I
  • Three segments post-divestitures: PWR-Transmission (HV/HVDC submarine and underground cable systems, EPCI model — 27% of standard sales), PWR-Grid (medium/low-voltage distribution, 22%), PWR-Connect (building wires, accessories, 38%). Remaining 13% is internal copper metallurgy. #I
  • FY2025: standard sales €6.1bn (organic growth +8.3%), adj. EBITDA €728m (11.9% margin), FCF €344m (+94% YoY), net debt €266m (0.36x leverage). S&P upgraded corporate debt to BB+. #I
  • [AS] Nexans operates a unique degree of vertical integration extending upstream into raw copper metallurgy — copper rod mills in Canada, France, Chile, Peru. Unlike most competitors who rely on external commodity markets, this structural independence insulates downstream factories from raw material bottlenecks.
  • [AS] The SHIFT transformation program has been the engine of margin expansion — systematically evaluating every product line, facility, and customer relationship. Product catalogue reduced from 350,000 to 250,000 items; customer base pruned from 20,000 to 4,000 most profitable. Now being upgraded to SHIFT AI using machine learning for margin leakage detection and real-time pricing.
  • PWR-Transmission: standard sales €1.657bn (+29.8% organic), adj. EBITDA margin 12.3%. Adjusted backlog €7.7bn securing ~90% of revenue through end-2028. Facilities: Halden (Norway) and Charleston (South Carolina), both recently expanded. #I
  • [AS] PWR-Grid: standard sales €1.319bn, adj. EBITDA margin 16.4% (+226bps YoY) — the strongest margin segment in the group. Seven-year framework agreement with Enedis (€600m+) provides recurring visibility. Strategy: drive 40% of grid revenues from "advanced offers" (smart accessories, AI-driven Adaptix.Grid platform) by 2028.
  • [AS] PWR-Connect: standard sales €2.341bn, adj. EBITDA €289m (12.3% margin). Serves residential, commercial, industrial, e-mobility. Strategy: premiumise toward fire-safety cables, ergonomic installation (MOBIWAY system), 30% advanced solutions by 2028. Americas and Middle East thriving; European residential soft.
  • [AS] Former CFO at Prysmian: "Nexans' complete withdrawal from special application markets to focus strictly on transmission and distribution represents a formidable competitive shift — a much more potent and focused rival today than prior to the restructuring."

7. Value Chain — Demand

Rating: GREEN — Structural demand supercycle with multiple reinforcing drivers. Supply is the bottleneck, not demand.

  • I [AS] Global electricity demand projected to surge 20% by 2030 (EVs, digitisation, building electrification). Global grid investments lagging by ~$800bn annually. Existing networks over 40 years old on average. Mandated infrastructure replacement, not discretionary capex.

  • I [AS] Offshore wind: Morgan Stanley projects 15.3GW formal awards in 2025, rising to 25GW in 2026 and 30GW in 2027. 156GW global offshore wind installations 2025-2030 (51GW Europe). Each GW of offshore wind capacity requires €250-400m in specialised cable value. Floating wind (~14% of market by 2032) opens new geographies.

  • I EU Grids Package: €1.2 trillion committed 2024-2040. EIB record €11bn to energy grids in 2025 (3x 2023 level). ENTSO-E estimates €3.4bn/year in interconnector investment 2025-2040. France doubling interconnection to 18GW by 2030.

  • I [AS] Distribution grid: 80 million km of new medium-voltage cables needed globally over the next decade. European grid operators need >€6bn/year on cross-border interconnection alone.

  • I [AS] Data centres: global DC electricity consumption rising from 1.5% to ~3% of global total by 2030. [AS: Morgan Stanley: 44% of DC executives told it would take >4 years to connect new facilities — forcing tech companies to fund localised grid upgrades]

  • I [AS] Fire-safety cable market: growing at 2x the rate of standard PVC cable market. Regulatory-driven shift (LFH requirements). Premium pricing, direct NEX Connect beneficiary.

  • J The demand picture has one critical caveat: demand is not the bottleneck — supply is. Factory lead times 800+ days, vessel availability scarce, skilled labour in short supply. This gives pricing power to incumbents but creates execution risk.

  • I [AS] Energy security framing post-Ukraine, post-Iran: grid investment reframed as national security, not just climate policy. Cross-party political support hardens spending commitments.

  • I [AS] Western oligopoly in HV submarine cables: Prysmian (~40% market share), Nexans (~21%), NKT (~15%). Collectively control the vast majority of premium European and North American contracts.

  • I [AS] Competitive moats in Transmission: (1) Vessel fleet — Prysmian has 6 CLVs, NEX has 2 (Electra adds third), NKT has 1 (NKT Victoria). (2) Factory qualification — 3-5 years to build and qualify. (3) Track record — utilities require proven execution history. (4) Financial strength. (5) Technical capability — 525kV HVDC at 3,000m depth.

  • I [AS] NEX technological leadership: first to qualify 525kV mass-impregnated HVDC subsea cable at 3,000m depth. Tyrrhenian Link — world record 2,150m installation depth. First supplier of dynamic cables for floating offshore wind. Stella Nova Centre (Hanover, 2025) — world-first superconducting LV DC cables for data centres.

  • J [AS] Prysmian is the 800-pound gorilla and scale is self-reinforcing. NEX risks permanent #3, winning contracts the other two don't want. This would cap margin expansion.

  • J [AS] NKT is the closest structural threat to NEX's ambitions. Larger HV backlog, higher margins, 30% EPS CAGR projected. But NEX has no FCF crisis and benefits from broader business mix.

  • I [AS] Asian competitors: LS Cable, Taihan (South Korea), Sumitomo Electric (new subsea HVDC factory in Scotland, mid-2026). 11. Value Chain — Supply Side

Rating: YELLOW — Supply constraints create pricing power but also execution risk. NEX's vertical integration is a structural advantage.

  • I [AS] NEX operates one of the most resilient supply chains in the cable industry — vertical integration into copper metallurgy (Montreal, Lens, Chile, Peru). Unlike most competitors relying on external commodity markets.

  • I Copper pass-through mechanism: standard sales strip out metal price effects on margins. But commodity spikes inflate working capital requirements — NEX uses AI-driven inventory optimisation.

  • I [AS] Copper scarcity: 10-20% of global copper demand could go unmet by 2030. NEX deliberately phasing out external copper rod sales, redirecting all metallurgical output to own factories. NCCCR recycling facility (80,000 tonnes/year by 2027) provides partial insulation.

  • I [AS] XLPE insulation: sourced from concentrated group of global chemical conglomerates. Ultra-pure quality critical for HV subsea cables — massive clean-room storage silos at Halden and Charleston.

  • I [AS] Vessel availability: only a handful of specialised CLVs globally. Prysmian has 6, NEX has 2 (Electra adding third), NKT has 1. Owning vs chartering: cost certainty and scheduling control.

  • I Cable factory lead times: 800+ days for complex submarine orders. New factories not emerging before 2027. Incumbents have near-monopolistic pricing power for 2-3 years.

  • I [AS] Labour: submarine cable manufacturing and installation requires highly specialised workers. Industry-wide skills gap. Labour cost inflation in Europe is a margin risk.

  • J [AS] Jefferies: "NEX possesses the most structurally robust upstream supply chain among all tier-one European cable manufacturers." Vertical integration eliminates spot-market purchasing risks that paralyse smaller manufacturers.
