§1 — General

Polenergia S.A. is a Warsaw-listed integrated Polish energy group combining renewable generation, energy distribution, and wholesale trading. The business was built through acquisition and organic development by the Kulczyk family and is now positioned as Poland's leading private offshore wind developer.

Description: Polenergia operates across four reportable segments — Renewables (onshore wind + solar), Offshore Wind (development pipeline), Energy Trading (wholesale), and Distribution (regulated grid assets in north-central Poland) [#I]
- Sub: Renewables is the current cash-generating core — approximately 600 MW onshore wind operational
- Sub: Offshore is the strategic engine — 3.6 GW pipeline in the Baltic; principal projects Baltyk I (1.5 GW), Baltyk II (600 MW), Baltyk III (720 MW), FEW Baltic 2 (800 MW)

Organisation: Corporate structure includes Polenergia S.A. (holding) with subsidiaries per segment; offshore assets held in SPVs — Polenergia has 50/50 JV with Equinor on Baltyk I/II/III (signed 2021), a landmark partnership providing Equinor's offshore execution capability and capital co-commitment [#I]
- Sub: JV structure with Equinor (NOK multi-bn balance sheet) de-risks offshore execution meaningfully relative to solo development
- Sub: Distribution assets (PGD — Polenergia Dystrybucja) are a regulated RAB-based business; minor contributor to group EBIT relative to renewables + trading

Listing: PEP-PL listed on Warsaw Stock Exchange ; majority-controlled by Mansa Investments Sp. z o.o. (Kulczyk vehicle); free float approximately 30-40%; no ADR/GDR listing; index membership WIG and WIG-ESG [#I]

§3 — Industry Context

Net signal: Polish renewables sector is structurally attractive (coal exit, EU mandates, Baltic wind quality) but market structure is dominated by state-owned entities; private developers like Polenergia operate in a politically sensitive environment with regulatory risk to CfD auction design and grid access [#J]

Market structure: Polish electricity generation is dominated by state-owned PSE, PGE, Tauron, Energa (owned by Orlen), and Enea — together commanding ~70-75% of installed capacity; private renewables developers hold the remainder [#A]
- Sub: Offshore specifically is a duopoly in private hands — Polenergia/Equinor and Orlen/Baltic Power are the two credible private players; state player PGE also holds offshore rights
- Sub: Top-5 concentration by installed capacity (including state players) is ~85%+ — highly concentrated

Industry rank: Polenergia is #1 private onshore wind operator in Poland by installed capacity (~600 MW); among top-2 private offshore developers by pipeline GW; but is a distant #3-5 by total generation capacity behind state giants [#A]
- Sub: "Largest private" is meaningful because the state players operate under different mandates (energy security, employment) — private operators compete on economics, not strategic obligation

Competitive intensity: Onshore wind new builds are intensely competed — Polish CfD auctions are price-competitive with 20-40 developers bidding per round; Polenergia does not enjoy pricing power in auction submissions [#A] ❌
- Sub: Offshore is less competed at project level (permits already allocated) but supply chain (turbines, installation vessels) is globally constrained — cost pressure risk is real
- Sub: Solar PV is highly competitive with Chinese module imports driving cost deflation — Polenergia's solar exposure is modest

New entrant: Vanilla new entrants to onshore wind face auction competition and grid connection queues; meaningful barrier for offshore (multi-year permitting); solar has low barriers and many small entrants [#A]
- Sub: The real new entrant risk for offshore is a state player (PGE, Orlen) prioritising offshore over economics — they have balance sheet advantage and political support

Disruption: Grid constraint is the near-term disruption force — Polish transmission grid was not designed for variable renewables; curtailment risk for new onshore/solar assets is rising; offshore will require substantial grid investment (PSE programme) [#A]
- Sub: Battery storage is an emerging adjacency — Polenergia is early-stage on storage; if it doesn't build storage capacity, third parties could capture balancing value
- Sub: Hydrogen is a medium-term adjacency for offshore wind monetisation; no near-term contribution

Regulatory shift: Polish RES Act (Ustawa OZE) and offshore wind act (Ustawa offshore) define the regulatory framework; CfD auction mechanism provides revenue certainty post-award but award quantities and pricing caps are government-determined [#A]
- Sub: Phase 1 offshore CfD allocations (non-competitive, regulatory negotiation) benefited early movers including Polenergia's Baltyk projects — a significant regulatory advantage now locked in
- Sub: Phase 2 offshore (competitive auction) creates pricing risk for later-stage capacity; Polenergia's Phase 1 contracted capacity is the "safe" book

Political shift: Poland's political transition (Tusk government from 2024) is broadly more EU-aligned and renewables-supportive than the prior PiS administration [#A]
- Sub: PiS-era 10H rule (minimum 10x turbine height distance from dwellings) severely constrained new onshore wind; Tusk government amended this to general principle distance rule — opens onshore pipeline nationally
- Sub: Risk: Polish state entities (Orlen, PGE) may receive preferential treatment in future auction rounds or grid allocation — private developers exposed to state-player competition that doesn't optimise on ROCE

Macro shift: European energy price normalisation post-2022 spike is headwind for merchant/uncontracted generation and trading margins; long-run electricity price in Poland is structurally supported by coal exit and renewables intermittency [#A]
- Sub: PLN/EUR exchange rate exposure is indirect — offshore turbine supply contracts likely EUR-denominated while CfD revenues are PLN — creates construction cost/revenue currency mismatch risk
- Sub: Interest rate environment (ECB/NBP tightening cycle 2022-24) raised project finance costs — IRR compression on projects financed at higher rates vs initial developer projections

Thematic trend: Energy security (post-Ukraine war) elevated political support for domestic renewables across CEE — Poland specifically accelerated offshore permitting and auction timelines [#A]
- Sub: Corporate PPA market in Poland is nascent but growing — large industrials (Amazon, Microsoft, Google data centres, Polish manufacturing) are actively seeking long-duration renewable PPAs; Polenergia positioned to benefit

Sceptical bullet: Bearish cross-cutting read — the structural advantage of early offshore permitting could be eroded if the Polish government adjusts Phase 2 CfD pricing caps downward (as has happened in UK CfD rounds), forcing merchant exposure or project cancellation; state-player competition (Orlen offshore) benefits from implicit government subsidy through refinancing access and preferential grid treatment; and if Polish grid investment lags offshore commissioning, curtailment losses could materially reduce actual vs contracted generation revenues [#J]
