  • Primary bear: The business is fundamentally two companies — a declining RAN franchise and a re-rating AI/optical story — packaged together.   • Secondary bears: R&D capitalisation inflates reported EBIT; Nokia Technologies patent revenue is a regulatory risk event waiting to happen; Infinera integration absorbs management bandwidth; serial restructuring track record suggests structural not cyclical cost issues; and hyperscaler capex is in "vertical ascent" with FCF turning negative for the first time in 35 years — a cycle peak risk. §1 — Bear Case Overview: What Do Bears Argue?

  • Verdict: Bears argue Nokia is a structurally weakening RAN business receiving a temporary AI-optical multiple that will compress when the cycle turns or execution disappoints. [#J]

  • Dual-business problem: Bears note Nokia is two very different businesses bundled together — Mobile Networks (declining margins, lost AT&T, facing Huawei price pressure) and Optical/IP Networks (AI-driven re-rating, Infinera-enhanced). The blend obscures deterioration in the core. [#A]

  • Mobile Networks net sales fell ~21% YoY in 2024; Q2 revenues fell ~€700m between 2020 and 2025 to ~€1.7bn per quarter [#I]

  • Bears argue the optical premium will not survive if Mobile Networks continues to shrink and drag blended margins [#A]

  • Valuation multiple compression risk: Nokia trades at 26.2x EV/EBITDA (May 2026) vs its own 10-year median of 8.4x — 212% premium. If optical/AI growth disappoints or the capex cycle peaks, the multiple re-rates to structural levels. [#A]

  • At 10x EV/EBITDA (10-year median) with flat EBITDA, the implied share price decline is ~60-70% from current levels [#A]

  • BofA's bull case itself embeds a 30x EBIT multiple on the optical segment — signalling how much premium is already required to justify the current price [#I]

  • Serial restructuring pattern: Nokia has executed three major restructurings in 10 years (2014-15 Alcatel-Lucent integration, 2020 Lundmark reorganisation, 2023 11,000-14,000 headcount reduction). Bears ask: each restructuring creates the conditions for the next one. [#A]

  • The 2023 restructuring targets €800m-€1.2bn gross cost reduction by end-2026 vs 2023 baseline [#I]

  • ❌ Third major restructuring in a decade is not consistent with a business that has solved its structural cost problem [#J]

  • Sceptical note on §1: The bear case requires multiple things to go wrong simultaneously — optical cycle peak, RAN share loss continuation, AND multiple compression. Individual probability is moderate; joint probability is lower but not negligible. [#J]

§2 — Structural Business Model Concerns

  • Verdict: Nokia's core RAN business is in structural decline in the US and facing headwinds in Europe; the optical pivot is real but exposes Nokia to a different cyclicality (hyperscaler capex) it did not previously face. [#J]

  • RAN share loss narrative: Nokia's global RAN market share fell from 19.5% in 2023 to 17.6% in 2024 — a 190bp share decline in a single year. ❌ Ericsson gained from 24.3% to 25.7% over the same period. [#I]

  • Nokia has lost both Verizon and AT&T as US mobile customers this decade — the two largest US carrier capex spenders [#I]
  • Bears note: losing Verizon was painful; losing AT&T (~$14bn five-year contract to Ericsson) signals Nokia is not competitive at the top of the US market [#A]

  • Mobile Networks operating margin target: double-digit at ~€10bn net sales by 2026; currently running at approximately 5% [#I]

  • RAN market contraction compounds share loss: Total RAN spend fell from $45bn in 2022 to $35bn in 2024 — a 22% market decline. Nokia is losing share in a shrinking market. [#A]

  • Bears argue Nokia's RAN turnaround thesis (regain share, improve margin) is fighting a structural headwind on two fronts [#J]

  • ❌ Nokia must both grow share AND survive market contraction to hit 2026 margin targets — a compound execution challenge [#J]

  • Huawei price pressure in non-Western markets: Huawei holds ~30% global RAN share and continues to compete aggressively in Africa, Latin America, and Southeast Asia via low-cost equipment and preferential financing. ZTE compounds this. [#A]

  • Nokia's "clean supply chain" premium works in Western markets; bears note it does not travel into price-sensitive emerging markets where Nokia needs volume to justify its R&D cost base [#J]

  • Optical cyclicality substitution: Nokia's pivot to optical/AI data centre is celebrated by bulls, but bears note it replaces one cyclicality (carrier RAN capex) with another (hyperscaler capex). [#A]

  • Hyperscaler capex grew 60% in 2025 while revenues grew 16.5%; in 2026 capex may grow 80% while revenues grow 15.5% — hyperscaler FCF is turning negative for the first time in 35 years. [#I]

  • Bears argue Nokia has traded a known, well-modelled cycle for an unprecedented one with less visibility [#J]

  • Portfolio Businesses restructuring — DAC/Enterprise Campus Edge: Nokia announced at its November 2025 CMD that Enterprise Campus Edge (DAC, private 5G for SMEs/factories) is being moved to a Portfolio Businesses segment for strategic review with divestiture expected ~2026. [#I]

  • Bears read this as Nokia admitting its private wireless growth narrative was flawed. Enterprise private 5G was a key premium growth story in 2021-2023 analyst decks; its demotion is a credibility blow. [#A]

  • ❌ A business unit promoted as a growth driver for 3+ years being designated for sale is a strategic reversal, not an evolution [#J]

  • Sceptical note on §2: Nokia's structural arguments are strongest in RAN; the optical business is genuinely different and less challenged. Bears need to be careful not to conflate the two businesses in a way that overstates total structural risk. [#J]

§3 — Accounting Quality + Earnings Quality Concerns

  • Verdict: Nokia's accounting quality concerns are moderate rather than extreme, but the gap between reported "comparable" earnings and cash earnings is meaningful and deserves scrutiny. [#J]

  • R&D capitalisation inflates EBIT: Nokia capitalises a material portion of development costs under IAS 38, adding to intangibles on the balance sheet rather than expensing through the P&L. [#A]

  • Nokia's total R&D expenses were $4.88bn in 2024 (reported, including both expensed and capitalised portions) [#I]
  • The gap between reported comparable EBIT (which adds back amortisation of acquired intangibles) and cash earnings is material — bears should stress-test cash conversion. [#A]

  • When capitalised R&D is amortised, it flows back through the P&L in later periods — creating an earnings smoothing effect that flatters near-term reported margins [#J]

  • "Comparable" vs IFRS EBIT divergence: Nokia consistently reports "comparable operating profit" which strips out restructuring charges, amortisation of acquired intangibles, and share-based compensation. [#A]

  • In a company that is serially restructuring (three restructurings in 10 years), treating restructuring charges as non-recurring is questionable. ❌ Bears argue restructuring is a recurring cash cost for Nokia, not a one-time item. [#J]

  • 2023 restructuring: €800m-€1.2bn gross cost target; previous 2020 restructuring also had multi-hundred-million costs; the pattern suggests these are ongoing operating costs [#I]

  • Infinera goodwill and intangibles: The $2.3bn Infinera acquisition (completed Feb 2025) will have generated substantial goodwill and acquired intangible amortisation. [#A]

  • Nokia's comparable EBIT excludes amortisation of acquired intangibles, which optically improves reported earnings relative to GAAP/IFRS [#I]

  • Bears note the "comparable" earnings basis obscures the full economic cost of the Infinera acquisition in the near term. [#A]

  • Free cash flow conversion variability: Nokia guided FCF conversion at 50-80% of comparable operating profit for 2025 — a wide 30pp range. [#I]

  • Low FCF conversion (50% scenario) relative to reported profit is a quality flag. Bears ask what is consuming cash that comparable earnings does not show. [#J]

  • Working capital absorption from Infinera integration, restructuring cash costs, and capex for optical product development all consume cash not visible in "comparable" P&L [#A]

  • Nokia Technologies margin opacity: The licensing segment is reported at near-100% gross margin with minimal disclosure of the litigation costs, renewal negotiation costs, and amortisation of IP development embedded in reaching those revenues. [#A]

  • The segment's apparent margin purity is partly an accounting artefact — the true economics of maintaining a global patent enforcement programme are embedded in SG&A, not disclosed separately. [#A]

  • Sceptical note on §3: Nokia's accounting is not egregious by European industrial standards; the concerns are more about the gap between "comparable" and IFRS measures creating a flattering presentation, than about outright manipulation. The risk is narrative, not forensic. [#J]

§9 — Sceptical Synthesis

  • Verdict: The Nokia bear case is not predicated on imminent collapse but on multiple compression from a starting point that prices perfection on the most uncertain parts of the business. At 26x EV/EBITDA vs a 10-year median of 8.4x, the burden of proof lies with the bulls, not the bears. [#J]

  • The core bear thesis in one sentence: Nokia is a cyclical, imperfectly managed hardware company trading at a software/platform multiple because of a temporary AI-optical tailwind that has not yet proven durable, in a business (hyperscaler capex) that is itself at unprecedented levels relative to underlying revenue generation. [#J]

  • Three compounding risks that bears would emphasise: [#A]

  • (1) Multiple compression risk — 26x EV/EBITDA in a business with a 10-year median of 8.4x leaves almost no safety margin for disappointment
  • (2) Execution complexity — four concurrent transformation workstreams (Infinera integration, restructuring, DAC divestiture, Mobile Networks recovery) under a management team with a documented delivery gap

  • (3) Cycle synchronisation dependency — Nokia's earnings recovery requires both carrier capex (5G-A) AND hyperscaler optical capex to cooperate simultaneously through 2026-2028

  • The bull-bear asymmetry problem: At 26x EV/EBITDA, upside scenarios are already significantly priced in. Bear scenarios (even moderate ones) imply disproportionate downside. This is an asymmetric R/R in the wrong direction for a new position. [#J]

  • What would change this view: If Nokia delivers Q2-Q3 2025 optical growth >20% YoY AND Mobile Networks shows margin progression toward 8%+ AND Infinera integration costs come in at the low end of guidance, the bull case earns credibility. [#A]

  • Until then, the sceptical prior is: Nokia is receiving optical credit it has not yet earned and RAN credit it has demonstrably lost [#J]

  • ESA-stage conclusion: The bear case is sufficiently substantive to merit serious weight at ESA stage. This is not a "nuance-and-move-on" risk — it is a central challenge to the investment thesis. The key question for DD: does Nokia's optical franchise have sufficient design-win depth and technological moat to sustain the multiple re-rating over a 3-5 year horizon in the face of RAN deterioration?
