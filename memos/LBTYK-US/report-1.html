KEY FINDINGS [#J]

  • Portfolio-in-transition, not an operating business: LG is best understood as a HoldCo liquidating its way to a simpler structure — Sunrise spun off Nov-24, Ziggo Group spin-off targeted H2-27, VMO2 stake optionally saleable to Telefónica. The "Liberty Global" that an investor buys today looks structurally different from the entity that will exist in 18 months.
  • HoldCo discount is structural, not temporary: Management SOTP claim of $25+ vs market price ~$12 (May-26) implies a ~50% discount; analyst average target of $15.89 suggests the market assigns ~40% discount even on sell-side numbers — the discount reflects genuine complexity, leverage, and execution risk, not simply a mis-pricing.
  • ARPU trajectory is under pressure at both major OpCos: VodafoneZiggo fixed ARPU +1.0% YoY in Q4-25 despite DOCSIS 3.1 gigabit upgrades; Telenet fixed ARPU -0.7% YoY in Q4-25 partly driven by the Belgian football rights non-renewal. Neither is demonstrating structural ARPU compounding; both are defensive pricing plays. ❌
  • Subscriber losses are still a live problem in the Netherlands: VodafoneZiggo lost ~115k broadband customers in 2024 and broadband net losses continued (albeit slowing) through 2025; fibre overtook cable as NL's dominant technology in Q1-25. Structural headwinds are real and mounting. ❌
  • Belgium capex cycle is brutal and prolonged: Telenet/Wyre is guiding 38% capex intensity in 2025 for FTTH rollout; negative FCF of €150-180m in 2025; completion of full FTTH Flanders footprint not until 2038. ❌ This is a 13-year runway of capex pressure, not a 2-3 year investment peak.
  • VMO2 is the valuation wildcard: LG's 50% stake in VMO2 (UK's second-largest fixed operator) is not consolidated; it generates $18bn of the $21.6bn "Liberty Telecom" aggregate revenue. Telefónica is actively exploring a buyout. Exit at a sensible multiple could close 30-40% of the HoldCo discount in one transaction.
  • Ziggo Group consolidation is a credible catalyst: Acquiring Vodafone's 50% stake in VodafoneZiggo for €1bn + 10% Ziggo Group equity, then listing Ziggo Group on Euronext Amsterdam in H2-27, is a clear, time-bounded event with management track record (see Sunrise spin-off, which executed cleanly in Nov-24).
  • Q1-26 commercial inflection is real but nascent: VodafoneZiggo improved broadband net adds for four consecutive quarters; Telenet posted best broadband quarter in 10+ years in Q1-26. Too early to declare a trend but momentum is directionally right.
  • Leverage at OpCo level is structurally high: VodafoneZiggo managed to 4.5-5.0x Covenant EBITDA target; Telenet also levered post-buyout; HoldCo corporate cash of $2.2bn (EOY-25) provides buffer but no consolidation flexibility. ❌
  • Animal CEO test: Fries passes with caveats. Track record on M&A and spin-offs is strong; the simplification thesis is credible. But 25+ years as CEO raises succession/complacency risk, and SOTP promise-to-delivery gap has been persistent for a decade.

§1 — Business Overview & Structure

Liberty Global (LG) is a Bermuda-domiciled, London/Amsterdam/Denver-headquartered HoldCo whose raison d'être has shifted from cable empire-building to asset monetisation and structural simplification. Understanding the entity requires mapping what it actually controls today versus what it reports.

  • Structure: LG is a three-layer system. [#I] HoldCo (LBTYK/LBTYA/LBTYB listed entities) → Liberty Telecom OpCo layer → individual country businesses. The HoldCo does not itself operate networks.
  • Liberty Telecom aggregate scale: [#I] $21.6bn aggregate revenue including ~$18bn from non-consolidated JVs (VMO2, VodafoneZiggo) and ~$3.7bn from consolidated operations (Telenet, Virgin Media Ireland, UPC Slovakia pre-sale).
  • Approximately 80 million connections across European operations.
  • Consolidated revenue at LG HoldCo level: ~$4.9bn in FY-25, a fraction of the economic exposure.
  • Consolidated EBITDA FY-25: [#I] ~$1.0bn on $4.9bn consolidated revenue (Adj EBITDA margin ~20%); misleadingly low because VMO2 and VodafoneZiggo are equity-accounted, not consolidated.

Operating Markets — What LG Actually Controls

  • Telenet (Belgium) — 100% owned, fully consolidated: [#I] LG acquired remaining public float and completed squeeze-out in October 2023; €2.9bn revenue (est FY-25 annualised from quarterly data); cable/FTTH network covering Flanders and Brussels; fixed+mobile via BASE brand.
  • Wyre JV (Telenet 66.8%, Fluvius 33.2%): the NetCo responsible for FTTH infrastructure build in Flanders, targeting 78% footprint coverage by 2038.

  • FMC convergence play: BASE mobile brand used as FMC vehicle; BASE net adds positive in Q4-25, broadband net adds +12,400 in Q4-25 (best in three years). [#A]

  • VodafoneZiggo (Netherlands) — 50% JV with Vodafone, equity-accounted: [#I] €4.1bn revenue (est FY-25); cable operator (Ziggo) + Vodafone mobile network; ~7m broadband-capable homes on DOCSIS 3.1, upgrading to 2Gbps.

  • LG acquiring Vodafone's 50% for €1bn cash + 10% Ziggo Group equity; expected to close July-26. Upon close, VodafoneZiggo becomes fully consolidated. [#I]

  • Ziggo Group spin-off: LG intends to list 100% of Ziggo Group (VodafoneZiggo + Telenet) on Euronext Amsterdam in H2-27 and distribute its 90% stake to shareholders. [#I]

  • Virgin Media O2 (UK) — 50% JV with Telefónica, equity-accounted: [#I] LG's largest exposure by revenue (~$18bn group contribution); cable (Virgin Media) + O2 mobile; not consolidated at LG HoldCo. Telefónica is reportedly in preliminary discussions to acquire LG's 50% stake. VMO2 2026 guidance: Adj EBITDA -3-5% YoY; Adj FCF ~£200m. [#I]

  • nexfibre: LG/Telefónica/InfraVia JV building full-fibre challenger to Openreach; acquired Substantial Group (Netomnia) for £2bn in Feb-26; combined nexfibre+Netomnia target footprint ~8m premises by end-27, rising to ~20m when combined with VMO2 cable network. [#I]

  • Virgin Media Ireland — consolidated: [#I] €115.8m revenue Q1-25 (-5.9% YoY reported; -2.9% rebased); cable operator, FTTH rollout underway targeting 80% fibre coverage by year-end-26; smallest consolidated OpCo. EBITDA $37.2m Q1-25 (-7% YoY). [#I]

  • UPC Slovakia — exiting: [#I] Included in ~$400m non-core disposals announced Dec-25; no longer a strategic asset. Minor revenue contributor.

  • Sunrise (Switzerland) — spun off November 2024: [#I] Completed Nov-8-24; LG shareholders received 1 Sunrise ADS per 5 LBTYK/LBTYA shares; Sunrise trades independently (SNRE on Nasdaq, SUNN on SIX). No longer in LG perimeter; this removes the highest-quality LG OpCo from investor exposure. [#A] The Sunrise exit was LG's best-performing asset — its departure structurally lowers the quality floor of what remains.

  • Growth portfolio — $3.4bn fair market value: [#I] Venture/minority stakes portfolio; top-5 assets represent >70% of FMV. Includes AtlasEdge (data centres), Formula E (media), and other non-core investments. Not a telecom business; value is real but realisation timeline uncertain.

Revenue Breakdown (Illustrative, FY-25 basis)

  • Total "economic" revenue (LG's proportionate share): [#I] VMO2 (~$9bn at 50%) + VodafoneZiggo (~€2bn at 50%) + Telenet (~€2.9bn at 100%) + Ireland (~€0.5bn) = ~$14-15bn proportionate basis; consolidated P&L reports only $4.9bn.

  • Segment mix (consolidated basis): [#I] Telenet is the primary consolidated revenue driver; broadband, mobile (BASE), video/TV, B2B.

  • Sceptical bullet: LG's 80-million-connections claim and $21.6bn aggregate revenue headline are designed to make the HoldCo look larger than what investors actually own economically. Post-Sunrise, post-Ziggo-spin, LG's residual entity could be thin — a Growth portfolio plus a small rump corporate structure. The investment case rests heavily on the realisation of embedded values, not on organic OpCo performance. [#J]

§2 — Business Model & Economics

  • Verdict: [#J] LG's core cable economics are sound in principle — high fixed costs, low incremental cost per subscriber — but are being disrupted by FTTH migration capex, fixed-to-mobile substitution in video, and fibre overbuilders. The converged bundle is more defensive necessity than value-creation flywheel at current ARPU levels.

  • Revenue mix — fixed-dominant: [#I] Across all OpCos, fixed broadband is the primary revenue driver; mobile (BASE in Belgium, O2 in UK via VMO2) is secondary. Video (TV) is in structural decline across all markets as cord-cutting accelerates.

  • VodafoneZiggo: fixed broadband ~45% of revenue; mobile ~35%; B2B ~15%; video declining.

  • Telenet: fixed broadband + B2B dominant; BASE mobile growing via FMC bundles.

  • Bundle economics (convergence): [#A] LG's strategy in Belgium centres on BASE FMC (fixed+mobile convergence). Telenet broadband net adds +12,400 in Q4-25 and best performance in 3 years cited as evidence of bundle pull-through. However:

  • Telenet fixed ARPU -0.7% YoY in Q4-25 despite FMC push. ❌ Bundle stickiness not yet translating to ARPU uplift.

  • VodafoneZiggo fixed ARPU +1.0% YoY in Q4-25 — partially offset by front-book price recalibration and right-pricing of recontracting customers; underlying pricing power limited by fibre competition. [#A]

  • Cable/HVM network — owned infrastructure: [#I] Telenet owns its HFC/cable network in Flanders; Wyre JV owns the emerging FTTH layer. VodafoneZiggo owns cable network covering ~7m Dutch homes. VMO2 owns UK cable network (HFC), upgrading to FTTH via nexfibre JV.

  • Key distinction: Wyre is a separate NetCo with Fluvius as co-owner; Telenet (ServCo) will lease from Wyre. This structure mirrors the UK model and creates a separation of infrastructure from retail.

  • Capex intensity — structurally elevated: [#I] Telenet 2025 capex intensity guided at ~38% of revenue for FTTH via Wyre; this compares to EU telco median capex/revenue of ~18-22% (Proximus: ~24%; Swisscom: ~15%). ❌

  • VodafoneZiggo also investing in strategic plan (network resilience, gigabit upgrade); modest capex as DOCSIS platform is relatively cheap to upgrade vs full FTTH build.

  • VMO2: capex commitment to nexfibre FTTH rollout on ~4.6m overlapping homes; separate from cable network maintenance capex.

  • ARPU trajectory L3Y — mixed picture: [#A]

  • VodafoneZiggo fixed ARPU: +4.2% YoY in Q1-24 → +1.0% YoY in Q4-25. Decelerating. Demand-pulse from post-COVID pricing; structural moderation as competition intensifies. [#A]
  • Telenet fixed ARPU: positive through FY-24; turned negative (-0.7% YoY) in Q4-25 following Belgian football rights non-renewal and mix-shift to lower-value FMC front book. ❌ [#A]

  • VMO2 UK: not consolidated; limited direct visibility from LG filings but VMO2 2026 EBITDA guidance -3-5% suggests ongoing revenue pressure. [#I]

  • Margin profile: [#I] Telenet Adj EBITDA margin: Q4-25 Adj EBITDA €316.2m on €723.8m revenue = 43.7%; historically mid-40s. VodafoneZiggo: Q4-25 Adj EBITDA €425.2m on €1,020.2m = 41.7%.

  • Both compare favourably to EU telco median EBITDA margins of 30-35% (Proximus: ~33%; Swisscom: ~37%; Deutsche Telekom: ~36%). ⚡ Cable EBITDA margins structurally superior to telco, reflecting lower opex per customer on shared HFC infrastructure.

  • However, Telenet EBITDA -8.8% YoY in Q4-25 signals margin erosion under football-rights repricing and capex step-up effects on opex. ❌

  • B2B: [#I] Telenet growing B2B through corporate ICT and connectivity services; VMO2 B2B benefits from the scale of the Daisy Technology acquisition (SME connectivity). B2B provides some diversification from consumer pressure but remains <20% of OpCo revenues.

  • Sceptical bullet: The cable business model advantage (high EBITDA margin) is being eroded structurally. The FTTH NetCo separation creates an intermediate ServCo layer (Telenet) that must pay rent to Wyre — adding a new cost line that didn't previously exist. Margin contraction risk is underappreciated in bull-case models. [#J]

§3 — Competitive Dynamics

  • Verdict: [#J] LG faces a two-front competitive war in each market — fibre overbuilders attacking from the infrastructure side, mobile substitution threatening video from the demand side. The competitive moat (cable's last-mile advantage) is eroding faster in the Netherlands than in Belgium. Regulatory coordination on fibre sharing in Belgium may partially mitigate the Belgian threat.

Netherlands (VodafoneZiggo)

  • KPN FTTH leadership: [#I] KPN passed >4m homes with FTTH by early-25 and is targeting national fibre coverage by 2026. This is the primary structural threat to VodafoneZiggo's cable monopoly in each local market.
  • Fibre overtook cable as NL's dominant broadband technology in Q1-25 — a historic inflection point. ❌ [#I]

  • VodafoneZiggo broadband base declined ~115k in 2024; Q4-25 broadband net losses improved sequentially to -11,900 but still negative. [#A]

  • Alt-net overbuild (Delta, Open Dutch Fiber): [#I] 2.5m+ Dutch homes passed by alt-net competitors (Delta, Open Dutch Fiber); Delta Fiber sold 200k-home network to KPN (pending regulatory approval); ACM concerned about duopoly risk post-deal.

  • ~526k addresses already sit in three-network overbuild areas; pressure most acute in Zeeland, Friesland, South Holland. [#I]

  • VodafoneZiggo's response — DOCSIS 3.1 / 2Gbps: [#I] Upgrading all ~7m cable homes to 2Gbps capability by end-25 via DOCSIS 3.1 upgrade (relatively cheap vs full FTTH build). Positions as "fastest widely available broadband" on speeds, but lacks the quality-of-service perception advantage of point-to-point fibre among tech-savvy Dutch consumers. [#A]

  • Mobile (VodafoneZiggo): [#I] Genuine converged operator — Vodafone mobile network + Ziggo cable; FMC bundling meaningful. However, mobile market is competitive: KPN, Odido (T-Mobile) are strong. Odido has own fibre rollout ambitions. [#A]

Belgium (Telenet)

  • Proximus fibre (Fiberklaar JV): [#I] Proximus accelerating FTTH via Fiberklaar in Flanders; targeting 1.5m Flanders connections and 0.6m Wallonia via Unifiber by 2028. Proximus is the primary fixed threat to Telenet's cable base.

  • FTTH-sharing agreement (Telenet/Proximus): [#I] Proposed cooperation for medium-density Flanders fibre sharing between Fiberklaar and Wyre; Belgian Competition Authority opened market test October-25; if approved, reduces duplication and lowers individual capex burden materially. Positive for LG/Telenet if it proceeds. [#A]

  • Belgian BCA also investigating Orange/Proximus Wallonia FTTH MoU (July-25). [#I]

  • Digi Belgium: [#I] New mobile/fibre entrant (Digi Communications + Citymesh); launched late-24; plans to pass ~2m homes in five years. Low-price disruptor — history of margin disruption in European markets (Romania, Spain, France precedent). ❌ Early stage but meaningful risk vector for Telenet's B2C pricing.

  • BASE FMC as competitive response: [#A] BASE (Telenet's mobile brand) used to bundle fixed+mobile; broadband net adds turning positive in Flanders South supports thesis that FMC reduces churn. However, ARPU drag from FMC migration (customers trading down from premium TV bundles to slim FMC packages) is visible in Q4-25 numbers.

  • Telenet mobile — genuine operator: [#I] Telenet operates its own mobile network (not MVNO); 3 million+ mobile subscribers; LG's mobile in Belgium is a real network-based mobile play, unlike some "convergence" stories that rely on MVNO arrangements.

UK (VMO2 — indirect)

  • BT/Openreach dominance: [#I] Openreach passes ~16m homes with FTTH vs VMO2's ~5.5m cable footprint; Openreach's long-tail rural footprint less relevant to VMO2's urban/suburban cable overlap but urban FTTH rollout increasing competitive intensity.
  • nexfibre as LG's fibre response: [#I] nexfibre (LG/Telefónica/InfraVia) targets 8m premises by end-27 via organic build + Substantial Group (Netomnia) acquisition; combined with VMO2 cable, could reach ~20m premises. This is LG's bet that building a wholesale FTTH NetCo creates long-term value independent of VMO2's ServCo operations. [#A]
  • CMA review: [#I] CMA scrutinising nexfibre/Substantial Group acquisition; CityFibre raised competition concerns. Regulatory risk to the UK consolidation strategy. [#I]

Competitive Moat Assessment

  • Switching costs: [#A] Cable bundle (broadband + mobile + TV) creates moderately high switching friction — multi-service contracts, installation complexity, equipment return. However, Dutch consumer switching appetite is high relative to UK/Belgian peers.
  • Brand: [#I] Ziggo (NL) and BASE/Telenet (BE) are strong regional brands; Virgin Media (UK, Ireland) is strong consumer brand. Brand provides some defensive quality.

  • Infrastructure moat diminishing: [#J] The cable network's primary competitive advantage — being the only broadband infrastructure to the home — is being eliminated market-by-market as FTTH rollout progresses. LG's moat is a melting ice cube. ❌ The question is how fast it melts.

  • Sceptical bullet: The Proximus/Telenet FTTH-sharing agreement, if approved, reduces LG's capex burden but also reduces the competitive differentiation of Wyre's network. A shared infrastructure removes the option of competing on network quality — Telenet's ServCo becomes a pure retail competitor with no network edge. [#J]

§4 — Financial Profile

  • Verdict: [#J] LG's consolidated financials badly misrepresent the group's economic footprint — VMO2 and VodafoneZiggo are excluded from the P&L. On a proportionate basis, LG has sound EBITDA generation but is highly leveraged at OpCo level, deeply FCF-negative during the capex cycle, and reliant on asset sales and JV dividends to fund HoldCo operations and buybacks.

Net Debt / Leverage

  • HoldCo corporate net debt: [#I] LG HoldCo had $2.2bn cash at EOY-25 (after $400m+ non-core disposals in 2025 and $700m+ buyback in 2024); no significant debt at HoldCo level.
  • OpCo leverage: [#I] VodafoneZiggo 4.5-5.0x; Telenet estimated 3.5-4.5x; VMO2 estimated 4-5x. EU telco median net debt/EBITDA ~2.8x (Deutsche Telekom: 2.5x; Proximus: ~2.0x; Swisscom: ~1.5x). All three LG OpCos are materially above peer median. ❌ [#A]
  • Ring-fenced debt structure: [#I] Each OpCo carries its own debt; LG HoldCo has no guarantees on OpCo debt. This means HoldCo doesn't bear OpCo bankruptcy risk but also cannot easily upstream capital without JV distributions.

Capex and FCF

  • Consolidated capex FY-25: ~$1.3bn, largely Telenet (Wyre FTTH acceleration). [#I]
  • Capex cycle position: [#A] Belgium (Telenet/Wyre) is at peak investment — 2025 represents the steepest part of a multi-year FTTH rollout with full completion not until 2038 for full footprint. Capex guided to step down in 2026. Netherlands (VodafoneZiggo) benefits from lower-cost DOCSIS upgrade path. UK (VMO2) — nexfibre capex sits outside VMO2 P&L.
  • Consolidated Adj FCF Q1-26: -$319m — heavily negative due to Telenet Wyre capex. [#I] Full-year 2026 trajectory depends on Telenet FCF recovery and Wyre capex pacing.

Dividend / Buyback Policy

  • No dividend: [#I] LG does not pay a dividend; all capital returned via buybacks. This concentrates risk/return in share price appreciation and buyback execution.
  • Buyback track record: [#I] ~$700m buyback in 2024 (~10% of shares); ~10% buyback authorisation for 2025. Funded by non-core asset disposals and JV dividend upstreaming.
  • FCF cover: [#A] Buybacks are not funded by operating FCF (which is negative on a consolidated basis including capex); they are funded by asset recycling. Buyback sustainability is contingent on continued disposal pipeline. This is a capital allocation model dependent on asset monetisation, not organic cash generation. ❌

HoldCo Discount / SOTP

  • Management SOTP claim: $25+ per share vs market price ~$12 (May-26) = ~50% discount. [#I]
  • Analyst average target: $15.89 (range $9.90-$35.00); Pivotal Research raised to $18 (May-4-26). [#I]
  • Components of management SOTP (illustrative): VMO2 (50% stake) + VodafoneZiggo (100% post-close) + Telenet (100%) + Growth portfolio ($3.4bn FMV) + Corporate cash ($1.9bn) - HoldCo costs capitalised.

  • Why the discount persists: [#J] (1) Complexity penalty — investors price complexity with a discount; (2) Execution risk on Ziggo spin-off; (3) VMO2 exit uncertainty; (4) OpCo leverage limiting dividend capacity; (5) Fries-era LG has promised SOTP value for a decade without closing the gap; market scepticism is earned. ❌

  • Sceptical bullet: The $700m buyback in 2024 at ~$10 average price was good capital allocation if SOTP is real. But management's SOTP has been "above market price" continuously since at least 2018; the discount is not a timing issue but a structural reflection of complexity, leverage, and execution risk. Investors pricing LG at a discount are not wrong. [#J]

§5 — Management & Capital Allocation

  • Verdict: [#J] Mike Fries is one of the more capable HoldCo CEOs in European telecoms — operationally experienced, financially sophisticated, shareholder-aligned via significant equity ownership. The simplification thesis he is now executing is the right one, but it should have been executed five years earlier. The Sunrise spin-off proves he can deliver. The Ziggo Group plan is testable.

Mike Fries — CEO and Chairman

  • Tenure: [#I] CEO since 2005 (21+ years); simultaneously Chairman — combined role that reduces governance independence. Longest-serving major European telecom CEO outside family-controlled entities.
  • Skin in game: [#I] Fries holds significant equity and options; his compensation is heavily equity-linked. No specific quantum found in web search but publicly documented as large. Alignment with shareholders is genuine.
  • Track record: [#A]
  • Executed complex M&A across European cable (Ziggo, Unitymedia, Telenet) through 2010s.
  • Navigated VMO2 JV creation with Telefónica (2021) — clean execution on a complex transaction.
  • Sunrise spin-off (Nov-24) — completed on schedule, shareholders received shares directly. ✓
  • Telenet full buyout and delisting (Oct-23) — clean execution at strategic price. ✓
  • Non-core disposals: $400m+ in 2025 (UPC Slovakia + other); on-track with $500-750m full-year target. ✓

Animal CEO Test

  • Does Fries pass? [#J] Conditionally yes. He is proactive, financially sophisticated, willing to make bold structural decisions (spin-offs, JV buyouts), and has the operational experience to manage complex multi-market cable businesses. The Ziggo Group simplification — acquiring Vodafone's stake then spinning the whole Benelux business to LG shareholders — is the kind of value-unlock move that a passive or CYA CEO would not attempt.
  • Caveats: [#J] (1) 21-year tenure is long enough to raise complacency/succession risk; (2) "Animal" instinct may have led to over-complex HoldCo construction in the first place; (3) The growth portfolio (Formula E, data centres, etc.) represents capital allocation that looks more like a family office than a focused telecom HoldCo.

Capital Allocation Record

  • M&A: [#A] LG's M&A in the growth era (2010s) created value but also created excessive complexity. The pivot to simplification (2022-present) is strategically correct.
  • Disposals: [#I] Sunrise (Nov-24 spin-off), UPC Slovakia ($400m non-core package 2025), VMO2 partial asset monetisation via nexfibre, non-core growth portfolio rotations ($500-750m targeted 2025). The disposal/rotation machine is running.
  • Share buybacks: [#I] $700m in 2024 at depressed prices — good value if SOTP is directionally correct. 2025 programme authorised at 10% of shares.
  • Dividend policy: [#I] No dividend; buybacks only. Rational given capex intensity and OpCo leverage.

Massive Corporate Change — 4-Point Credibility Test

  1. Is there a clear before/after state? [#J] Yes — before: sprawling multi-geography HoldCo with minority stakes in unlisted and listed entities; after: two listed entities (Liberty Global + Ziggo Group) each with clean balance sheets and focused investor bases. Credible articulation.
  2. Is management executing on visible, time-bound milestones? [#J] Mostly yes — Sunrise spin-off delivered Nov-24 ✓; VodafoneZiggo buyout close July-26 (on track); Ziggo Group listing H2-27 (ambitious but achievable). Slovakia sold Dec-25 ✓.
  3. Is the end-state independently valuable? [#J] Ziggo Group (Belgium + Netherlands, €5-6bn+ EBITDA, listed on Euronext) is a real, investable entity. Residual Liberty Global (VMO2 stake + Growth portfolio + HoldCo cash) is less clean — depends on VMO2 exit.
  4. Is there a credible value bridge from current price to end-state? [#J] At $12 stock price vs $25+ management SOTP, the value bridge requires: Ziggo Group listed at reasonable EV/EBITDA multiple, VMO2 stake sold or distributed, Growth portfolio partially monetised. All three must execute within 3-5 years for IRR to be compelling. Execution risk is real.
  • Sceptical bullet: Fries built the complexity now being dismantled. The simplification thesis amounts to "we created too much complexity and are now unwinding it." While the unwinding may generate shareholder value, the capital allocation that created the complexity (repeated JV structures, minority stakes, growth portfolio diversification) also destroyed value for a decade. Crediting management for fixing self-created problems deserves a discount. [#J]

§6 — Growth Outlook & Optionality

  • Verdict: [#J] Organic growth outlook is modest to negative at OpCo level in 2026 (EBITDA declining at VodafoneZiggo and VMO2); the investment thesis is primarily structural/event-driven (Ziggo Group listing, VMO2 exit, HoldCo discount closure) rather than organic growth compounding. A 25%+ IRR requires multiple catalysts firing within 3-5 years.

Organic Growth Drivers

  • ARPU recovery potential: [#A] VodafoneZiggo fixed ARPU at +1% YoY is below EU cable peer ARPU trajectory (Liberty-owned cable in Belgium showing similar pressure). Price indexation provides a floor; real ARPU growth requires churn reduction and upselling into gigabit/2Gbps tiers. [#A]
  • Telenet 2026 guidance benefits from April annual price indexation but also headwinds from football rights gap and FMC mix-shift.
