EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (BLUF) 53 findings

Section summary
  1. Volex has evolved from a commodity cable manufacturer into a highly diversified, vertically integrated system provider, successfully mitigating cyclicality across five attractive end markets

Volex has successfully transformed from a commodity cable manufacturer into a highly diversified, vertically integrated system provider under Executive Chairman Nat Rothschild's leadership since November 2015, shifting from low-margin power cords generating just 1% to 2% operating margins toward highly complex, high-margin custom interconnect systems. Operating margins expanded dramatically from 2.8% in FY2017 to 10.2% in FY2026 , while group revenues scaled from less than $400 million in FY2015 to $1.24 billion in FY2026. The group achieved an outstanding return on capital employed of 21.0% in FY2026, improving from 19.7% in FY2025 and consistently exceeding the group's through-cycle 20% target. Management established ambitious FY2030 targets of $2.0 billion revenue and 12% operating margins , positioning Volex as a high-quality operator delivering exceptional capital efficiency in structurally attractive end markets.

Business Model Transformation and Divisional Diversification

Volex operates across five attractive end markets through a decentralized manufacturing structure spanning 23 advanced facilities across 25 countries. Revenue is diversified across Complex Industrial Technology (31% of sales, $382.9M), Off-Highway (22%, $279.3M), Consumer Electricals (20%, $242.4M), EV & Electrification (15%, $181.8M), and Medical (13%, $156.2M). More than 70% of products are complex high-spec systems requiring advanced engineering rather than basic assembly, spanning printed circuit board assembly, custom wire harnesses, and complete mechanical box builds. The Complex Industrial Technology division accelerated

dramatically, with high-speed data center cable sales doubling in FY2026 to support global AI infrastructure expansion. However, a single EV customer accounts for 11.40% of group revenue, with Tesla representing the majority of sales within the EV and Electrification division , creating concentrated customer risk despite overall portfolio diversification.

Geographic revenue is balanced across North America (52.0%, $645.5M), Europe (35.3%, $439.1M), and Asia (12.7%) , with Tijuana capacity doubled to support North American nearshoring, and a new 15,000 sqm facility in San Luis Potosi tripling regional capacity. Volex targets early design-in partnerships to secure multi-year revenue visibility of 5-8 years, with high switching costs created by rigorous regulatory approvals for certified medical and industrial assemblies.

Financial Performance and Superior Peer Benchmarking

Volex increased global revenues from $320M in FY2017 to $1,242.6M in FY2026, representing a 19.2% revenue CAGR since FY2022, with organic sales growth reaching 14.2% in FY2026 driven primarily by data center demand. Underlying operating margins expanded from 2.8% in FY2017 to 10.2% in FY2026, with margin expansion of 40 basis points in FY2026 driven by complex high-speed solutions, while underlying operating profit rose 19.9% year- on-year to $127.3M. Prudent leverage levels improved to 0.8x in FY2026 from 1.0x in FY2025, remaining well below the board-approved target range of 1.5-2.0x. Underlying free cash flow reached $42.3M in FY2026, while capital intensity typically trends within 3% to 4% of group sales, with capital expenditure decreasing to $34.7M in FY2026 from $45.3M in FY2025. However, cash conversion declined to 63.9% in FY2026 compared to 67.2% in FY2025, with operating cash conversion before capital expenditure standing at 91%. Volex's ROCE of 21.0% is significantly higher than capital goods peer TT Electronics' ROCE of 6.20% , while Volex outpaced the sector average by 6.4% per annum on organic growth over five years. Volex spent approximately $400 million on 12 strategic acquisitions since FY2019, delivering an impressive cumulative cash payback of 20.3% by FY2024 and generating a 15.3% EBITA return on investment. Management demonstrated valuation discipline by withdrawing from bidding for TT Electronics in December 2024, refusing to overpay for the underperforming asset.

Value Chain Positioning and Margin Capture Strategy

Volex captures enhanced profitability by bypassing commoditized assembly stages and using strategic vertical integration to control high-value component and system integration stages, positioning itself to deliver complete, tested systems directly to OEMs and capture double-digit EBIT margins. In-house cable extrusion capabilities across multiple facilities globally allow Volex to reduce exposure to raw material price fluctuations and capture internal margins, supporting $180 million of end-product business in fiscal year 2026. Manufacturing custom pins and connectors in-house optimizes unit costs and controls lead times, with vertical integration of EV connectors successfully insulating product margins during OEM price-reduction campaigns, while custom appliance harness assemblies generate 5x to 10x the dollar value of basic power cords.

Volex's high-speed copper data center products operate at leading-edge specifications of up to 1.6 terabits-per- second, representing the single most profitable product category within the portfolio, with sole-qualified positioning on hyperscale programs allowing pricing premiums for rapid delivery. expansion from the FY2026 baseline, driven by moving up the complexity curve and further vertical integration.

Customer Relationships and Switching Cost Advantages

Historical top-three customer concentration has halved from 50.00% in FY2014 to 25.00% in FY2020 under disciplined diversification initiatives, with acquisitions systematically introducing thousands of smaller, high- margin accounts to the platform. Volex operates as a licensed partner of Tesla for the North American Charging Standard system and is primarily exposed to technology giants Amazon and Nvidia in the high-growth data center space, competing against giant competitors like Amphenol. The Off-Highway segment serves four of the five largest global agricultural equipment OEMs, the largest bus builder in Turkey, and the largest European lift truck manufacturer through the Murat Ticaret acquisition.

Volex typically does not sign five-year customer contracts, with contracts generally capped at one to two years max, yet customer relationships are long-lived, often recurring for five to eight years due to design-in lifecycle locks. Switching costs are highly defensive because Volex's products represent performance-critical components in highly complex supply chains, with customers locked into proprietary technical architectures through co- development, while sector certifications such as ISO 13485 for medical applications make it extremely challenging for customers to substitute suppliers. Volatility in copper and PVC prices is fully protected via indexation pass-through clauses in most contracts, with pass-through effects most pronounced in the Consumer Electricals segment, while incremental costs from trade tariffs are proactively passed through to customers due to Volex's sole-source position.

Supply Chain Resilience and Operational Flexibility

Volatility in copper and PVC prices is protected via pass-through clauses in the majority of contracts, with copper pass-through mechanisms most pronounced in the Consumer Electricals segment and contractual price adjustments typically featuring a short time lag of one to two months. Turkish operations face persistent labor cost inflation driven by government macroeconomic reforms, with labor costs typically increasing twice per year, which reduced the margins of Murat Ticaret relative to initial acquisition projections. However, domestic Turkish contracts feature inflation clauses allowing direct labor cost recovery, with headwinds mitigated by reducing headcount, accelerating automation, closing two expensive Turkish factories, and transferring production to a newly established, highly cost-competitive central Turkish facility.

Based on trade policies, about 12% of total group revenue is exposed to tariff changes, with only 2% of group sales representing direct Chinese exports into the United States and approximately 4% stemming from non- USMCA compliant Mexican imports. Incremental costs arising from tariffs are passed through in their entirety

to customers due to Volex's position as a critical sole-source manufacturer, while the company's 23 global sites allow production lines to be relocated dynamically, successfully transferring EV cable production from China to Indonesia and Mexico.

Competitive Positioning and Industry Structure

The global connector market is highly concentrated, dominated by a few large international manufacturers, with market share of the top ten firms expanding from 38% to 61% over recent decades, led by TE Connectivity (15.6% to 17.8% market share), Amphenol (8.5% to 8.8%), and Molex and Aptiv (5.7% and 5.8% respectively). Amphenol and TE Connectivity dominate the high-precision connector and terminal layers with elevated EBITDA margins of 20% to 30% through standardized products, but Volex largely avoids head-to-head component battles by focusing on custom system-level assemblies. BizLink operates as Volex's most direct competitor in data center rack and electric vehicle connectivity, enhancing high-precision mechanical capabilities by acquiring Interplex Datacom for $392 million and expanding white-goods footprint through Mexico-based PAS Appliance Systems in June 2026.

The global cable and connector market is projected to reach $140.5 billion by fiscal year 2026, representing a 6.1% CAGR since fiscal year 2020, with the global cable assembly market estimated to reach $348.66 billion by fiscal year 2035 (6.03% CAGR), while active electrical cables are expected to lead growth with a 45% CAGR and direct attach copper cables forecast to grow at 25%. Global data center power demand is projected to grow at a 17% CAGR through 2030, reaching 1,114 terawatt-hours, with data center annual power load additions projected to grow at a 31% CAGR, reaching 65 gigawatts by fiscal year 2030.

Disruption Risks and Long-Term Secular Growth Drivers

Barriers to entry are substantial, including scale economies that are extremely difficult for smaller competitors to duplicate, high-speed copper assemblies requiring continuous engineering iterations up to 1.6T speeds, and strict certifications such as ISO 13485 creating prohibitive switching barriers for medical accounts. The potential shift from high-speed copper to optical fiber poses a long-term technology risk, though roadmaps confirm copper and optics will coexist to serve different reach and power needs, with high-speed copper retaining significant stability, cost-effectiveness, and power efficiency advantages over short distances and remaining the dominant scale-up interconnect choice for years.

Surging AI rack power densities drive unprecedented demand for high-current copper interconnects, with transitioning to 120kW+ supernode architectures multiplying copper interconnect content per rack and requiring specialized rack-level busbars and custom power whips. Tesla's NACS has emerged as the universal electric vehicle charging standard in North America, and as a licensed NACS partner, Volex is uniquely positioned to win new western OEM programs. Global AI data center electricity demand is projected to grow at an exponential 15% CAGR, requiring over 70 GW of new installed capacity globally, with North American utility companies substantially raising capital expenditure plans to support electrification, while next-generation speed upgrades

trigger recurring, margin-accretive product replacement cycles.

Volex aligns perfectly with the active secular capex supercycle in electrification and AI infrastructure, with software gains unable to bypass physical infrastructure, anchoring scarcity value in copper interconnects, while shifting supply chains and nearshoring mechanically channel corporate investments to flexible integrators.
