§3 Revenue + Organic Growth Analysis

Verdict: Domestic revenue is structurally stable-to-slightly-declining on a nominal basis but with a positive mix shift towards higher-value fibre/convergent products; Group-level revenue growth is structuredly negative driven by Proximus Global deterioration — two very different businesses under one reporting entity [#J]

  • Group revenue FY2025: €6.25bn, -2.0% YoY (FY2024: ~€6.38bn); the decline reflects both Global segment erosion and the Be-Mobile disposal [#A]
  • Domestic revenue FY2025: €4,821m, -0.1% YoY reported; on a like-for-like basis (ex-Be-Mobile divestiture) the trend is flat to marginally positive [#A]
  • Convergent revenue domestic: +2.8% YoY in Q4 2025; growing share of domestic revenue mix driven by fibre migration and bundle pricing power [#A]
  • Broadband ARPU: €50.3 in Q3 2025 (latest granular data), +5.2% YoY; driven by January 2025 inflation indexation + fibre upsell premium (fibre speed tiers priced higher than copper); EU telco average broadband ARPU trends are flat-to-+2% — Proximus's +5.2% is above peer median [#J]
  • Mobile postpaid ARPC: ~€59, +2.2% YoY; Q4 2025 saw net +33,000 mobile postpaid adds in an intensely competitive environment — defensive performance [#A]
  • ARPU trajectory cohort question (CQ1): L3Y ARPU growth for both broadband and mobile has been consistent (inflation indexation + product upsell); this is structural not a demand pulse — Belgian CPI indexation is contractually embedded in tariff structures for many subscribers [#J]
  • Convergent ARPC: ~€90.5 (+0.5% YoY) — modest growth versus the +5.2% broadband ARPU; the convergent bundle effectively locks in revenue but ARPC is diluted as entry-tier bundles are included; the relevant uplift vs standalone customers is the churn benefit, not just the ARPC differential [#A]
  • B2B/Enterprise revenue: ICT and managed services growing; Proximus ICT (Belgian IT services arm) is cited as a key domestic growth driver alongside fibre — specific revenue split not publicly disclosed in quarterly reports [#I]
  • Be-Mobile disposal impact: Be-Mobile (fleet management) divested during 2025; impacts the like-for-like comparisons for domestic revenue — management adjusted for this in guidance; investors need to use the ex-disposal figure [#A]
  • Proximus Global revenue: Declining; TeleSign (digital identity / CPaaS), BICS (international carrier), and Route Mobile all facing competitive headwinds; CPaaS market is structurally commoditising; BICS under pressure from OTT substitution; the combined entity is a revenue headwind, not a growth engine [#J]
  • Q4 revenue -6.6% YoY: Telecompaper-reported figure includes full Global segment impact and Be-Mobile; the headline number is the worst single-quarter comparison but reflects non-recurring disposal effects rather than pure organic deterioration [#A]
  • Revenue quality: Domestic revenue is high recurring (subscription-based, multi-year contracts for both residential and B2B); Global revenue is more transactional and exposed to carrier volume pressure — the domestic/global split materially affects quality assessment [#J]
  • ARPU vs volume balance: FMC growth (+4% YoY customers) is volume-driven; ARPU simultaneously growing +5.2% broadband — both legs working — positive for domestic revenue durability [#A]
  • Sceptical read: The 1% Domestic Services revenue CAGR target for 2026-2028 is the same promise Proximus has effectively made for three prior years; population growth is limited, mobile SIM penetration is mature, and alt-net fibre overbuilders (Telenet/Wyre in Flanders) could price compete — the CAGR target assumes competition remains cooperative [#J]

§4 Margin Profile + Quality

Verdict: Domestic EBITDA margin is expanding modestly and with high quality — cost discipline plus fibre mix improving unit economics; Group margin is compressed by the Global drag; at the group level Proximus sits at the lower end of EU incumbent telco margins [#J]

  • Group EBITDA margin FY2025: €1,883m / €6,250m = ~30.1%; EU telco sector median is ~30-35%; Proximus is at the floor of the peer range [#A]
  • Domestic EBITDA margin: Domestic EBITDA +1.9% on flat domestic revenue = margin expansion of ~2ppt YoY; cost discipline and fibre-driven opex reduction (fewer truck rolls vs copper network) are the drivers [#J]
  • Proximus Global EBITDA margin: ~€445m on the Global segment revenue base; margin compression relative to prior year; the global segment's margin dilutes the group average meaningfully [#A]
  • Cost programme: €180m cost reduction target by 2028; 1,200 job cuts by 2030 (~15% workforce); €25m from contractor reduction by 2028; these are substantial commitments relative to a ~€1.9bn EBITDA base (~9% of EBITDA if fully delivered) [#A]
  • Peer margin comparison: KPN EBITDA margin ~40%; Swisscom ~35%; Deutsche Telekom group ~38%; Orange Belgium (pure Belgian mobile) lower; Proximus's 30.1% group margin is partly explained by the Global drag — domestic-only implied margin would be higher [#A]
  • Fibre network economics: Fibre capex intensity is front-loaded; once rolled out, opex per line falls materially (fewer faults, lower energy, no copper maintenance); Proximus is in the transition zone — capex still elevated, opex savings beginning to flow through [#I]
  • Operating leverage: The CMD 2030 target of ~€400m organic FCF vs current €130m implies ~€270m incremental FCF; if EBITDA stays stable and capex falls below €1bn post-rollout, the math works — this is the core bull case [#J]
  • Margin quality: Domestic EBITDA quality is high — subscription-based, CPI-indexed, Belgian market structural duopoly/triopoly dynamics; Global EBITDA quality is low — volume-driven, carrier-grade, exposed to OTT substitution [#J]
  • Indirect cost base: ~1,200 FTE reduction over 4 years translates to ~€75-100m annual savings at Belgian telco labour cost levels — partially offset by AI/automation investment; net benefit likely €50-70m vs the €180m stated target [#A]
  • D&A and amortisation: High amortisation of fibre assets will begin as more homes are connected; EBITDA metric excludes D&A but EBIT and earnings are materially affected — EPS compression is an additional headwind as fibre depreciation builds [#I]
  • AI efficiency narrative: Management explicitly cited AI/automation as the mechanism for the 1,200 job cuts; while plausible directionally, the €180m cost target by 2028 requires execution across multiple operational domains simultaneously — execution risk is non-trivial [#J]
  • Sceptical read: ❌ At 30.1% group EBITDA margin, Proximus is at the EU sector floor; the path to peer-median margins (~33-35%) requires either Global stabilisation or disposal — absent that, margin convergence with peers is structurally blocked [#J]

§5 Cash Flow + Capital Allocation

Verdict: FCF conversion is the core structural weakness — organic FCF of €130m on €1,883m EBITDA (6.9% conversion) is substantially below EU telco median (~15-20%); elevated fibre capex is the primary cause, but the dividend cut reveals management's own assessment that leverage and FCF are uncomfortably stretched [#J]

  • Reported FCF FY2025: €480m; organic FCF (ex-asset sales) €130m; the delta of €350m is non-recurring asset disposal proceeds — investors correctly exclude this from run-rate assessment [#A]
  • FCF conversion ratio: €130m organic FCF / €1,883m EBITDA = 6.9% conversion; KPN runs ~20%+, Swisscom ~22%, Deutsche Telekom ~15%; Proximus is at bottom-decile EU incumbent FCF conversion during peak capex — this is expected to be temporary but the duration matters [#J]
  • Capex: FY2025 €1,249m; guidance FY2026 €1.2-1.25bn; post-rollout ambition <€1bn by 2029-2030; capex intensity (~20% of revenue) is 5-7ppt above EU incumbent median during off-peak years [#A]
  • Asset disposal programme: Proximus targets >€500m asset disposal proceeds by 2027 (confirmed at CMD); Be-Mobile was one such disposal in 2025; proceeds have been the bridge financing the organic FCF gap [#A]
  • Dividend reset rationale: Management framed the €0.60→€0.30 cut as aligning dividend to FCF; the three-year ladder (€0.30/€0.40/€0.50) is tied to FCF improvement as capex normalises; at €130m organic FCF vs ~€180m dividend payout at €0.60/share, the old policy was clearly unsustainable [#J]
  • Net debt trajectory: ND/EBITDA ~2.8x (S&P definition) at year-end 2025; guided stable at ~2.8x for 2026; management's comfort zone 2.5-3.0x — sitting at upper bound; EU telco median ~2.5-2.8x (Proximus at top of EU range) [#A]
  • FCF bridge to 2030: CMD target ~€400m organic FCF by 2030; requires capex below €1bn + EBITDA stable/growing + Global contribution (however small); the capex step-down is the largest single variable [#J]
  • Dividend yield at current prices: At €0.30/share and ~€7 share price (post-cut), implied yield ~4.3%; Belgian state's dividend income drops by ~50% — the political economy of a 53%-state-owned company cutting its dividend is unusual and may constrain future policy flexibility [#I]
  • Capital allocation priority stated at CMD: Debt reduction > fibre investment > dividend growth; buybacks not mentioned as a near-term tool — this is a balance-sheet-repair allocation framework, not a return-maximisation one [#J]
  • Sceptical read: The €350m asset disposal proceeds are finite; once Be-Mobile and any other identifiable disposals are completed, the bridge disappears and organic FCF of €130m must support both the dividend (€0.30/share = ~€90m payout) and any remaining debt reduction — the margin of safety is thin if Global continues to underperform [#J]

§9 Industry + Sector Context + Sceptical Synthesis

Verdict: Belgian telco market dynamics are not uniquely hostile by EU standards — fixed-mobile convergence penetration is below German/Dutch peers, FTTH is mid-rollout creating a structural improvement trajectory, and the domestic incumbent position is defensible; the bear case is entirely in Proximus Global and management execution credibility, which are company-specific not sector-specific risks [#J]

  • CQ1 — ARPU trajectory structural or demand-pulse: Structural; Belgian CPI indexation is contractually embedded; fibre upsell is a genuine product upgrade driving willingness to pay; L3Y broadband ARPU growth of ~4-5% annually is durable assuming competition does not trigger a price war — which the Telenet/Fiberklaar cooperation pact reduces the probability of [#J]
  • CQ2 — Alt-net overbuild intensity: Telenet/Wyre was the primary overbuild threat in Flanders; the binding cooperation agreement signed 30-Apr-26 (under BCA review) restructures this from competitive overbuild to cooperative access — materially reduces the overbuild risk if regulatorily approved; Orange Belgium in Wallonia is cooperating not competing on infrastructure [#J]
  • CQ3 — FMC churn reduction: FMC customer growth +4.0% YoY with stable ARPC ~€90.5; management confirms FMC customers churn at meaningfully lower rates than standalone; the mechanism is defensive (lock-in) not transformational; FMC is a necessary condition for maintaining revenue share in Belgian residential, not a premium growth driver [#J]
  • CQ4 — Balance sheet posture: ND/EBITDA ~2.8x at upper bound of comfort range; EU telco median is ~2.5-2.8x (Proximus at the top); dividend cut is the management's own acknowledgement that leverage is constraining; FCF below dividend payout in organic terms until capex normalises (2028+) — balance sheet is a genuine constraint on strategic flexibility [#J]
  • CQ5 — Fibre rollout positioning: 42% coverage end-2025, on-track for 2028 targets; peak capex year likely past; trajectory is from elevated-capex to normalising — the fibre option value is real and will materialise if the rollout completes on time and competitive dynamics permit pricing power [#J]
  • Peer comparison — Belgium vs EU: KPN (Netherlands) is the closest structural peer — similar incumbent with FMC strategy and FTTH ambition; KPN has achieved higher EBITDA margins (~40%) and better FCF conversion; Swisscom (Switzerland) operates in a less competitive but smaller market with higher ARPUs; Proximus is 2-3 years behind KPN on FCF normalisation after fibre rollout [#A]
  • EU telco sector context: Rate environment (cost of debt) matters for telcos with 2.5-3x leverage; ECB rate path in 2026 is a tailwind vs 2022-2024; Proximus's fixed/floating debt mix relevant but not disclosed in public summaries [#I]
  • Football rights loss — unmodelled risk: ❌ Belgian Pro League rights loss from Proximus TV is a domestic content differentiator removed; the impact on FMC bundle churn is not quantified in public guidance; sports content is typically a 3-5% ARPU driver for convergent bundles; Proximus has not disclosed compensating content strategy [#J]
