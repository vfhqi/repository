3. THEME §1 — Revenue Drivers + Visibility

J-front verdict: Revenue-side uncertainty is HIGH — JDG provides no consolidated order-book, organic split is estimated not disclosed, and end-market diversification across >20 subsidiaries makes top-down probing essential

Q1.1 — Order-entry cadence by subsidiary cluster

  • Question: "Across your subsidiary clusters — vacuum, materials, life-science instruments — what is the current book-to-bill trend, and how does it compare to 12 months ago?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely describe a mixed picture — some strength in academic/government-funded segments, softness in industrial/capex-sensitive segments — without giving explicit ratios
  • Good answer: Specific directional commentary per cluster; willingness to distinguish between government-funded grant cycles vs industrial capital-spending cycles; acknowledgement of lead-time compression or extension
  • Bad answer: "Our businesses are all performing well" — generic reassurance with no segmentation; deflection to "our model is resilient across cycles"
  • Follow-up probe: "Is there a single subsidiary whose book-to-bill is the leading indicator you watch most closely — and what is it telling you today?"
  • Diagnostic note: Fluent answer with segment detail supports the bull thesis (diversified, visible, cycle-resistant). Vague answer supports the bear/false-friend thesis that JDG's organic engine is weaker than the acquisition-driven headline suggests; cross-refs Q19 ACH failure-mode #1 (organic stagnation masked by M&A)

Q1.2 — Organic revenue growth decomposition

  • Question: "Of the organic revenue growth reported in the last full year, how much was price/mix vs volume — and which end-markets drove each?"
  • Expected answer: JDG will cite pricing contribution in high-single-digit range (consistent with instrument pricing power) plus modest volume growth — but exact decomposition has never been disclosed
  • Good answer: Clear willingness to distinguish price from volume; evidence of pricing power maintained without volume sacrifice; visibility into which academic or industrial sub-markets drove volume
  • Bad answer: Refusal to decompose or conflation of price and volume; "we don't track it that way at group level" — which, while operationally true given subsidiary autonomy, is itself a diagnostic signal about management information systems
  • Follow-up probe: "If volume was flat or negative in any segment, which segment — and is that structural or cyclical?"
  • Diagnostic note: Price/volume split discriminates between durable pricing power (bull) and volume erosion covered by pricing (false-friend); critical for bear-case scenario #2 in Q19 ACH — pricing exhaustion into weakening volume

Q1.3 — Academic/government grant-cycle dependency

  • Question: "What proportion of group revenue is directly dependent on academic or government grant funding — and what is your visibility into grant cycles for the next 18 months?"
  • Expected answer: Management will estimate 50-65% of revenue is ultimately traceable to academic/government sources but will note it is diffuse across many sovereigns and grant agencies, reducing concentration risk
  • Good answer: Specific estimate with confidence interval; articulation of geographic diversification across UK/EU/US/Asia grant cycles; evidence that no single grant programme represents >5% of group revenue
  • Bad answer: "We don't have that visibility at group level" — plausible given subsidiary structure but concerning if combined with no bottom-up estimation methodology
  • Follow-up probe: "Have you observed any pull-forward in grant-funded orders ahead of potential budget cuts in the US or UK — or the reverse, a pause?"
  • Diagnostic note: Grant-cycle dependency is a latent cyclicality risk that consensus may under-weight given JDG's resilient historical revenue trend; discriminates against the bull thesis of "demand is structural and non-cyclical"

Q1.4 — Customer concentration at subsidiary level

  • Question: "At the individual subsidiary level, what is the typical top-customer concentration — and are there any subsidiaries where a single customer represents more than 20% of that sub's revenue?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely indicate that at subsidiary level some concentration exists (instrument businesses can have lumpy capital-equipment sales) but group-level effect is diversified away
  • Good answer: Transparent answer with rough distribution; acknowledgement of any subsidiaries with elevated concentration and evidence of customer-replacement plan or product-breadth mitigant
  • Bad answer: Deflection to group-level diversification without engaging the subsidiary-level question; refusal to estimate
  • Follow-up probe: "Have any subsidiaries lost a major customer in the last 2 years — and what was the revenue impact and recovery period?"
  • Diagnostic note: Customer concentration at sub level is a hidden single-point-of-failure risk; relevant to Q19 ACH failure mode #4 (subsidiary impairment following customer loss)

Sceptical close: A fluent answer on order-entry trends from David Cicurel should be read cautiously — he is sophisticated, controls the information flow from subsidiaries, and can describe the portfolio at the level of detail he chooses; fluency here is not comfort, it is a signal that this level of question is already rehearsed

4. THEME §2 — Margin Trajectory + Levers

J-front verdict: Margin trajectory is the #2 diagnostic uncertainty — recent acquisitions appear sub-scale on EBIT margin at entry; the question is whether JDG's operational improvement cadence is intact or structurally slowing

Q2.1 — Acquired-company margin improvement timeline

  • Question: "For acquisitions completed in the last 3 years, what is the average EBIT margin at acquisition vs current — and what is the typical time to reach group-average margin?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite a 2-4 year normalisation period with margin uplift from operational improvement, pricing alignment, and overhead rationalisation; likely claim 3-5pp margin improvement on average
  • Good answer: Specific cohort data with at least directional averages; differentiation between acquisitions that have met, exceeded, or lagged the improvement target; honest acknowledgement of any underperformers
  • Bad answer: "Our businesses improve over time, we don't disclose sub-level margins" — while technically defensible, this is a red flag if management cannot give any directional indication
  • Follow-up probe: "Is there any acquisition in the last 3 years where margin improvement has been slower than expected — and what is the diagnosis?"
  • Diagnostic note: Margin improvement velocity is the most-testable claim in the JDG bull thesis; slowing improvement discriminates strongly for bear case; cross-refs Q19 ACH failure mode #3 (acquisition quality deterioration)

Q2.2 — Group gross margin dilution from sub-scale entries

  • Question: "Recent acquisitions appear to have entered at below-group gross margins. Is this a deliberate trade-off for growth — and at what gross margin threshold would you decline to proceed?"
  • Expected answer: Management will acknowledge that entry margins vary and that improvement potential matters more than entry margin; will likely cite a floor (perhaps 40%+ gross margin as minimum threshold) but may be reluctant to specify
  • Good answer: Clear articulation of margin floor with examples of deals declined on margin grounds; evidence that gross-margin improvement has been modelled and achieved in prior cohorts
  • Bad answer: "We look at the whole picture, not just margins" — sounds reasonable but is a red flag if there is no quantitative floor in the acquisition criteria
  • Follow-up probe: "Have you ever acquired a business that you subsequently determined was incorrectly priced for margin improvement potential — and what happened?"
  • Diagnostic note: Gross-margin floor discipline discriminates between durable compounder (bull) and EPS dilution risk from sub-scale accumulation (false-friend)

Q2.3 — Overhead leverage at group level

  • Question: "Group overheads have historically been kept minimal — as revenue scales toward £200m+, what is the incremental overhead investment required, and how does this affect reported group margins?"
  • Expected answer: Management will defend the lean head-office model as structural, not cyclical — likely cite flat or modestly growing head-office costs as subsidiaries scale; may note that some central services (HR, finance, compliance) require investment above certain scale thresholds
  • Good answer: Specific overhead-to-revenue ratio target; honest identification of any overhead categories that grow semi-fixed vs variable; evidence of deliberate structural decision rather than opportunistic cost suppression
  • Bad answer: "Head office stays small, that's our model" — true historically but insufficient for a DD-stage investor; failure to engage with the scale-threshold question is itself diagnostic
  • Follow-up probe: "If JDG were to cross £300m revenue organically, is the current management team and head-office infrastructure sufficient — or does that require new hires?"
  • Diagnostic note: Overhead scalability is a latent margin risk as the group scales; relevant to bull thesis claim that operating leverage compounds indefinitely

Q2.4 — FX pass-through and pricing-cost spread

  • Question: "Given that a meaningful share of your cost base is in sterling while revenues are globally diversified — what is your net FX exposure and how do you manage pricing to protect margins?"
  • Expected answer: Management will describe a natural hedge from global sales vs UK manufacturing costs; may cite that pricing reviews incorporate FX movements with a lag; expect claim of 6-12 month pass-through capability
  • Good answer: Specific net FX exposure estimate; evidence of pricing discipline maintaining spread through GBP appreciation/depreciation cycles; subsidiary-level differentiation where relevant
  • Bad answer: "FX moves around, we manage it" — absence of specific pass-through methodology or acknowledgement of margin exposure in GBP-appreciation scenarios
  • Follow-up probe: "In the last GBP strengthening episode, which subsidiaries experienced the most margin pressure — and what was the recovery period?"
  • Diagnostic note: FX pass-through credibility is a near-term margin watchpoint given sterling volatility; discriminates against a bear scenario of unexpected margin compression

Sceptical close: Management answers on margin trajectory are likely to be competent and well-practised — JDG has fielded these questions from sell-side for several years; the marginal information is in the specific subsidiary underperformers, not the aggregate claims

5. THEME §3 — Capital Allocation + Balance Sheet

J-front verdict: Capital allocation is the LOAD-BEARING pillar for JDG — the acquisition engine is the compounding mechanism, and any deterioration in RONIC discipline or pipeline access is the highest-impact bear catalyst

Q3.1 — Acquisition pipeline depth and structural change

  • Question: "Is the pipeline of actionable acquisition targets in your sweet spot (£1-10m EBIT, owner-managed, scientific instruments) structurally deeper, shallower, or similar to five years ago — and why?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely claim the pipeline remains deep — the UK/European scientific instruments sector is fragmented with hundreds of owner-managed businesses; will note that competition from PE has increased in certain size tranches
  • Good answer: Specific pipeline commentary with deal-flow metrics (number of opportunities screened per year, conversion rate); honest acknowledgement of where competition has intensified and where JDG still has proprietary access
  • Bad answer: "The pipeline is full" — generic reassurance; failure to engage with structural trends in deal competition or owner-seller motivations
  • Follow-up probe: "What proportion of current pipeline was sourced through inbound vs outbound — and has that ratio shifted?"
  • Diagnostic note: Pipeline depth/quality is the highest-plausibility long-term failure mode (Q19 ACH #1); structural pipeline thinning at JDG's sweet spot is the single scenario that most threatens the 3-year EPS trajectory

Q3.2 — Valuation discipline under competitive pressure

  • Question: "Have you walked away from any deal in the last 18 months primarily on price — and if so, what multiple were sellers asking vs your walk-away point?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely confirm they have walked away from deals; JDG has historically been disciplined at 4-6x EBIT entry; will be reluctant to specify exact walk-away multiples
  • Good answer: Explicit confirmation of walk-aways with approximate price rationale; articulation of a clear EBIT multiple ceiling with evidence it has been maintained; no sign of "multiple creep" under pressure
  • Bad answer: Inability or unwillingness to confirm walk-aways; vague language about "evaluating all opportunities on their merits"; any indication that the multiple ceiling has risen without explicit justification
  • Follow-up probe: "As larger private equity firms target businesses that look like your acquisition candidates, has your average entry multiple increased over the last 5 years?"
  • Diagnostic note: RONIC discipline is the load-bearing RA3 attribute — multiple creep from 5x to 7x+ EBIT entry mechanically destroys the compounding proposition; most diagnostic capital-allocation question in the memo

Q3.3 — Post-acquisition integration and working capital

  • Question: "On working capital — do subsidiaries run their own balance sheets independently, or does head office impose a working-capital efficiency standard post-acquisition?"
  • Expected answer: Management will describe deliberate autonomy with light-touch financial disciplines imposed — perhaps cash-upstreaming targets but no active working-capital rationalisation programme
  • Good answer: Evidence of consistent cash conversion across subsidiaries post-acquisition; any working-capital improvement levers identified and achieved; clear description of inter-subsidiary treasury structure
  • Bad answer: "Subsidiaries manage their own working capital" with no evidence of any group-level discipline or improvement programme — raises risk that acquired businesses carry structural inefficiency that is never optimised
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the group's average debtor days and how has that trended over the last 3 years as the mix shifts toward more project-based revenues?"
  • Diagnostic note: Working capital quality is a latent cash-conversion risk; deteriorating debtor days would signal either customer quality issues or project-revenue growth outpacing collections capability

Q3.4 — Dividend policy and leverage philosophy

  • Question: "JDG has maintained a progressive dividend policy — at what point would you suspend or cut the dividend to fund an exceptionally large acquisition, and what size deal would trigger that conversation?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely defend the progressive dividend as a commitment to shareholders but note flexibility if the right large acquisition appeared; will likely cite a deal size above £30-50m as the threshold for a balance-sheet conversation
  • Good answer: Explicit acknowledgement of the tension between dividend commitment and acquisition optionality; clear leverage ceiling (likely net debt/EBITDA 1.5-2.0x); articulation of how equity issuance or temporary leverage would be sequenced
  • Bad answer: "We would never compromise the dividend" — suggests inflexibility that could cause capital allocation suboptimality if a large deal appears
  • Follow-up probe: "Is there an upper limit on the size of acquisition JDG would consider, or is the constraint purely the balance sheet capacity at the time?"
  • Diagnostic note: Dividend-vs-M&A tension is under-probed by sell-side; resolution discriminates between a management team that is genuinely acquisition-first vs one that has allowed the dividend commitment to constrain strategic optionality

Sceptical close: Capital allocation answers from David Cicurel are likely to be among the strongest in the meeting — he has executed this model for 20+ years; the marginal information is in the edges — walk-away discipline under competition, pipeline conversion rates, and whether the model can scale to larger deals without margin dilution

6. THEME §4 — Strategy + Competitive Position

J-front verdict: Strategic-position uncertainty is MODERATE — JDG's model is clear and internally consistent; the open question is whether it remains defensible as scientific instruments consolidation accelerates and as larger platforms compete for the same targets

Q4.1 — Moat trajectory at individual subsidiary level

  • Question: "For a typical JDG subsidiary, how does the competitive moat evolve post-acquisition — does it strengthen, stay stable, or gradually erode without active investment?"
  • Expected answer: Management will describe moats as largely durable — instruments with switching costs, proprietary applications, and niche customer relationships that are self-reinforcing; will likely acknowledge that some product lines require periodic R&D refresh to maintain differentiation
  • Good answer: Specific examples of moat reinforcement post-acquisition — new customer relationships opened, adjacent products developed, IP renewed; honest acknowledgement of any subsidiaries where the moat has been harder to maintain
  • Bad answer: "Our businesses have strong competitive positions" without specifics — generic moat claim without evidence of mechanism; failure to engage with the "what sustains it" question
  • Follow-up probe: "Are there any subsidiaries where you would describe the competitive position as weaker today than at acquisition — and what is the plan?"
  • Diagnostic note: Moat durability is the bull thesis's most important unverifiable claim; evidence of any subsidiary-level moat erosion is the strongest available bear signal

Q4.2 — Adjacency expansion and scope creep risk

  • Question: "As JDG scales, is there pressure to move into adjacent instrument categories that are less niche — and how do you maintain acquisition discipline against the temptation of larger deals in less-specialised segments?"
  • Expected answer: Management will assert a strict focus on the scientific instruments niche and the owner-managed culture filter; will likely acknowledge that deal size has gradually increased but will argue quality criteria have not changed
  • Good answer: Evidence of deals declined because they were too large or too removed from the niche; articulation of the specific cultural/operational criteria that rule out adjacent diversification
  • Bad answer: Any indication that JDG has expanded or is considering expanding into industrial automation, medical devices, or other adjacent categories without explicit acknowledgement of the higher competition and lower RONIC those segments carry
  • Follow-up probe: "Has the board ever debated making an acquisition above £20m enterprise value — and what was the conclusion?"
  • Diagnostic note: Scope creep into larger, more competitive deal categories is the strategic false-friend risk — the model looks the same but the RONIC is structurally lower; cross-refs Q19 ACH failure mode #2

Q4.3 — Competitive response to consolidation by larger platforms

  • Question: "Larger instrument groups — Spectris, Halma — and PE platforms are increasingly active in scientific instruments M&A. Are you losing deals to these buyers that you would have won 5 years ago — and how often?"
  • Expected answer: Management will acknowledge competitive pressure at the upper end of deal size but argue that JDG's cultural proposition (autonomy, founder respect, no cost-cutting) differentiates it from PE and larger strategics in the sub-£10m EBIT segment
  • Good answer: Specific evidence that JDG wins deals on non-price grounds — seller-preference stories, post-close outcomes shared with prospective sellers; acknowledgement that competition has increased but win rate in JDG's sweet spot has not materially deteriorated
  • Bad answer: Dismissal of competitive pressure with vague assertions about cultural differentiation — insufficient at DD stage; any admission that win rate has declined is a significant bear signal
  • Follow-up probe: "If a target founder has three offers on the table — JDG, a PE fund, and Halma — what is the single most important reason they choose JDG?"
  • Diagnostic note: Competitive positioning in deal-sourcing is the most fragile part of the JDG model; deterioration here is irreversible in the short term

Sceptical close: Strategic answers are the easiest to make sound good — Cicurel's model is intellectually elegant and well-articulated; the diagnostic value is in concrete evidence (win rates, declined deals, specific subsidiary examples) not in model articulation

7. THEME §5 — Management Quality + Operator Track Record

J-front verdict: Management quality is HIGH by historical evidence but SUCCESSION RISK is the single under-probed structural concern — David Cicurel is the model; the question is what the model looks like without him

Q5.1 — Succession planning (load-bearing)

  • Question: "David, what is the succession plan for the CEO role — is there an identified internal candidate, and what is the timeline for any transition?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely describe an ongoing governance process with the board, note that Brad Ormsby has deepened the management bench, and likely deflect from a specific named successor or timeline
  • Good answer: Evidence of a structured succession programme with identified candidates at exec and sub-CEO level; acknowledgement that the board has discussed and has a plan; willingness to describe the characteristics of the ideal successor
  • Bad answer: "We have a strong board and we'll cross that bridge when we come to it" — any indication that succession is not actively planned is a structural governance red flag at the DD stage
  • Follow-up probe: "Has JDG ever brought in a senior operational or M&A executive to develop as a potential successor — and if not, is that a deliberate choice?"
  • Diagnostic note: Succession is arguably the highest single-point-of-failure risk for JDG given Cicurel's centrality to deal-sourcing relationships; discriminates bull vs false-friend; cross-refs Q19 ACH failure mode #5 (key-man event)

Q5.2 — Incentive structure and compensation drivers (Q20-MMO mandatory)

  • Question: "How is your own compensation structured — what proportion is fixed vs variable, and what metrics drive the variable element? Does your equity holding change the incentive materially?"
  • Expected answer: Management will note that Cicurel's equity ownership (substantial, as reported) aligns his incentives directly with shareholders; variable compensation metrics likely include group revenue growth and EPS; may note that the equity holding dwarfs any annual bonus in economic significance
  • Good answer: Transparent description of compensation structure; explicit alignment between equity ownership and long-term value creation; evidence that short-term compensation metrics do not incentivise aggressive near-term acquisition activity at the expense of RONIC
  • Bad answer: Any indication that short-term bonus is tied to deal volume, revenue growth without RONIC gate, or metrics that would incentivise earnings management; reluctance to discuss compensation structure at all
  • Follow-up probe: "If JDG's share price were to underperform for 2-3 years, does your personal economic interest create pressure to do something — or does the equity ownership make you patient?"
  • Diagnostic note: Compensation structure predicts behaviour — the power-of-incentives probe is mandatory here per Q20-MMO gate; equity-heavy alignment is the bull signal; short-term volume-incentive is the bear signal

Q5.3 — Communication clarity and sandbagging discipline

  • Question: "You have historically given minimal forward guidance. If you had to identify one metric where your public communication has been less transparent than it could have been, what would it be?"
  • Expected answer: Cicurel will likely deflect or note that JDG's policy is deliberately minimal guidance to avoid short-termism — this answer is itself diagnostic
  • Good answer: Willingness to engage with the self-critical framing; honest identification of an area where more disclosure would be investor-useful; shows meta-awareness of the communication policy's costs
  • Bad answer: Complete deflection with "we communicate what we're required to" — signals a management team that is defensively opaque rather than deliberately focused on long-term investor clarity
  • Follow-up probe: "Is there a metric you monitor internally that you believe investors would find most useful — but that you choose not to disclose for competitive reasons?"
  • Diagnostic note: Communication clarity is a second-order management quality signal — the answer reveals how management thinks about investor relations vs competitive protection; both have legitimate rationales

Q5.4 — Crisis playbook

  • Question: "During the COVID period, what was the single biggest operational decision you made differently from how you would have managed pre-COVID — and what did that experience change in how you run the group?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely describe the decision to give subsidiaries maximum autonomy during COVID while providing group-level financial support where needed; may cite supply-chain adaptation or customer payment flexibility
  • Good answer: Specific operational decision with counterfactual reasoning; honest acknowledgement of what went wrong and what was learned; evidence of institutional learning embedded in current operating model
  • Bad answer: "Our businesses are resilient, they managed well" — generic response that avoids the "what did you learn" element; absence of specific decisions suggests either perfect execution (unlikely) or reluctance to surface weaknesses
  • Follow-up probe: "Were there any subsidiaries that required group-level financial support during COVID — and did any emerge structurally weaker?"
  • Diagnostic note: Crisis playbook quality is a latent resilience signal; the post-COVID learning question surfaces how adaptive the operator genuinely is

Sceptical close: Management quality questions are the most susceptible to rehearsed answers — Cicurel is an experienced executive who understands what investors want to hear; the diagnostic value is in responses to questions he has NOT been asked before (§8 unconventional questions do this work)

8. THEME §6 — Risk + Downside (Pre-Mortem Cross-Ref)

J-front verdict: Downside questions probe three ACH failure modes: organic revenue stagnation masked by M&A, RONIC deterioration from multiple creep, and key-man succession; all three are live and under-tested

Q6.1 — Bear-case probe: organic stagnation test

  • Question: "If I strip out all acquisition contribution and look purely at organic growth across the portfolio over the last 3 years — what has that number been, and what does it tell you?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely acknowledge organic growth has been variable — possibly 3-6% in good years, flatter in weaker years — and will argue that the acquisition contribution is the intended design of the model, not a mask for organic weakness
  • Good answer: Willingness to engage with the stripped-organic question; honest range provided; explicit articulation of which subsidiaries are organic growth contributors vs stable earners vs managed-decline
  • Bad answer: "We don't separate it that way" or deflection to the total return of the model — organic growth ex-acquisitions is precisely the question management least wants to answer cleanly if organic is soft
  • Follow-up probe: "Are there any subsidiaries that are in managed decline on a standalone organic basis — where the plan is harvest rather than growth?"
  • Diagnostic note: This is the highest-diagnostic question on the revenue side — the answer discriminates directly between bull (organic growth plus acquisition) and false-friend (acquisition covers organic stagnation); cross-refs Q19 ACH failure mode #1

Q6.2 — Failure mode test: integration failure / impairment

  • Question: "In JDG's history, have you ever had to write down or impair a subsidiary acquisition — and if so, what were the root causes and what process changes resulted?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely acknowledge one or two historical cases with honest accounting; will describe the learning (usually: don't acquire businesses with customer concentration or technology obsolescence risk without a specific plan)
  • Good answer: Transparent acknowledgement with specific root-cause analysis; evidence of process improvement (tighter due diligence, different earnout structure, earlier intervention trigger); framing that makes the learning institutional rather than ad hoc
  • Bad answer: "We haven't had write-downs" — if accurate, this is fine; if combined with any observable softness in subsidiary performance, it is a red flag
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the earliest warning signal you look for that suggests an acquisition may underperform — and which of your current subsidiaries, if any, is showing early-warning signals?"
  • Diagnostic note: Impairment risk is a tail event with high impact; historical evidence of impairments plus credible learning discriminates between mature compounder (bull) and acquisition machine with hidden losses (bear)

Q6.3 — Pricing fragility under academic budget pressure

  • Question: "If UK/US/EU government science budgets face cuts of 10-20% over the next 3 years, which of your instrument categories are most exposed — and what is your quantified estimate of the revenue impact?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely argue that government budget cuts have a delayed, diffuse effect on instrument demand because most purchasing comes from individual PI grant budgets rather than centralised procurement; will likely estimate direct exposure below 20% of revenue
  • Good answer: Specific scenario analysis by end-market; differentiation between capital equipment (most exposed to budget cuts) vs consumables/service (more recurring, less exposed); evidence of historical resilience through prior austerity cycles
  • Bad answer: "Science funding is always prioritised" — this is demonstrably untrue in multiple European contexts and the claim would itself be a red flag
  • Follow-up probe: "Have any of your subsidiaries begun to see quote-to-close rates deteriorating in government-funded segments in the last 6 months?"
  • Diagnostic note: Government budget sensitivity is a macro risk that has materially re-rated in 2025 given US/UK fiscal pressures; most critical near-term risk question; cross-refs Q19 ACH failure mode #3

Q6.4 — False-friend probe: EPS reported vs economic EPS

  • Question: "Amortisation of acquired intangibles runs through reported PBT. How would you characterise the relationship between reported EPS and the underlying cash earnings power of the group — and do you think the market values JDG on the right earnings line?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely prefer adjusted EPS or EBIT pre-amortisation as the more representative metric; will argue that intangible amortisation is an accounting artefact from acquisition accounting rather than economic cost
  • Good answer: Clear articulation of adjusted vs reported earnings with honest view on which is more representative; acknowledgement of where the accounting treatment creates noise; willingness to guide on a normalised earnings basis
  • Bad answer: "Reported numbers speak for themselves" — avoidance of the question signals either that reported EPS is deliberately preferred because the adjustment is flattering, or that management is not investor-communication-focused at this level
  • Follow-up probe: "If you were building a DCF of JDG from scratch, which earnings line would you use as the starting point — and what normalised capex would you assume?"
  • Diagnostic note: EPS quality is a false-friend discriminator — a group trading on reported earnings multiples that embed high amortisation charges may be cheaper or more expensive than it appears; critical for valuation thesis validation

Sceptical close: Which Q19 ACH-matrix diagnostic items are NOT covered in this memo? Flagged explicitly: (1) technology obsolescence at the instrument level (addressed partially in Q4.1 but not quantified); (2) talent retention at subsidiary management level post-acquisition (not covered — a gap); (3) insurance and product liability risk for life-science instrument subsidiaries (not covered — a gap for Q22 follow-up)

9. CROSS-CUTTING META + NON-OBVIOUS QUESTIONS

J-front verdict: The non-obvious questions below are designed to surface information management would NOT volunteer — the answers (and non-answers) to these are the highest marginal-information-per-minute questions in the meeting

Non-obvious angle — Q8.1: "If you had to describe JDG to a bright person who had never heard of it in a single sentence that emphasised what COULD GO WRONG rather than what has gone well — what would that sentence be?"
- Why non-obvious: forces management into reverse-stress-test framing; a CEO who can articulate their own downside clearly is a more credible bull signal than one who can only describe the opportunity
- Good answer: Specific, self-aware, not the standard "acquisitions could disappoint" boilerplate
- Bad answer: deflection back to the bull case; inability to articulate the downside clearly is itself diagnostic

WH Smith probe — Q8.2: ⚡ "What was the most surprising thing a target founder told you during a deal process in the last 2 years — something that changed how you think about acquisitions?"
- Why unconventional: the consensus interview never asks about management's own surprises; this surfaces cultural intelligence and learning agility — the WH Smith archetype (unconventional question, unconventional answer)
- Expected answer: Management will likely have a genuine story — experienced operators accumulate these; the nature of the story reveals what they consider surprising vs expected
- Diagnostic note: the content of the surprise is less important than whether the answer is specific and genuine vs vague and generic

Cross-roads question — Q8.3: ⚡ "The JDG model has worked exceptionally well for 20 years. What is the single assumption embedded in the model that, if it turned out to be wrong, would require the most fundamental rethink?"
- Why load-bearing: this is a Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 3 (multiple truths coexist) question — it acknowledges the bull case while explicitly inviting the bear-case nuance; management cannot dodge into either-or framing
