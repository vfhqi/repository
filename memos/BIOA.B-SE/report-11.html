KEY FINDINGS - Structural verdict: The Alzheimer's/neurodegenerative drug value chain is a hub-and-spoke structure, not linear — originator science sits at the centre, with CROs, CDMOs, distributors, payers, and healthcare systems radiating outward; BioArctic occupies the originator-discovery hub [#J]

  • Positional verdict: BioArctic is a single-stage originator specialist — it generates IP via discovery biology and translates that into clinical candidates, but it does not manufacture at commercial scale, distribute, or dispense; the Lecanemab partnership with Eisai/Biogen defines the downstream interface [#J]

  • Margin pool verdict: Economic surplus in innovative pharma chains concentrates overwhelmingly at the IP/originator stage — branded drug gross margins of 75-90% vs 15-35% for CDMO/CMO and sub-10% for wholesale distribution; BioArctic's stage is structurally the most margin-favoured [#J]

  • Power dynamics verdict: BioArctic's pricing power over its single commercial product (Leqembi) is structurally mediated by Eisai, its co-development and commercialisation partner; BioArctic receives royalties (est. mid-single-digit % net sales for the SE territory + milestone payments) rather than setting list prices directly [#J]

  • Supplier leverage verdict: BioArctic is exposed to CRO/CDMO supplier pricing power for clinical-stage work — the global CDMO consolidation wave (Lonza, Samsung Biologics, WuXi Biologics dominate) creates oligopolistic supply at biologics-manufacturing scale relevant to BioArctic's pipeline [#I]

  • Competitive map verdict: At the originator-discovery stage for amyloid/tau neurodegenerative targets, BioArctic faces a small but formidable set of incumbents (Eisai, Biogen, Eli Lilly, Roche/AC Immune, Prothena) — the stage is oligopolistic by asset quality, not by number of companies [#J]

  • Integration threat verdict: The most credible vertical-integration threat to BioArctic comes from downstream — large-pharma partners absorbing or de-prioritising discovery-stage relationships — not from upstream CDMOs; BioArctic's dependency on Eisai makes this the single largest structural vulnerability [#J]

  • Structural evolution verdict: The chain is shifting toward biomarker-gated diagnostics integration (PET/CSF/plasma amyloid testing as gatekeeper to Leqembi prescribing) — this creates a new de-facto upstream stage that BioArctic does not control and which could compress patient-eligible volumes [#I]

  • Cross-cutting bear synthesis: BioArctic's structural position is high-margin-in-theory but single-asset, single-partner dependent in practice — the chain map reveals that Eisai + reimbursement payers together sit above BioArctic in terms of effective pricing authority [#J]

  • Outlier flag: BioArctic's origination of the protofibril-selective antibody mechanism (the scientific basis for Leqembi) represents a genuinely rare instance of a small-cap originator retaining co-ownership of a first-in-class approved drug alongside a large-pharma partner — structurally unusual at this market-cap tier [#J]

§1 — Value Chain Map + Stage Table

The Alzheimer's/neurodegenerative-disease drug value chain spans from fundamental neuroscience research through IP generation, clinical development, biologics manufacturing, regulatory approval, distribution, payer reimbursement, and finally patient administration — each stage populated by distinct operator types with divergent capital profiles and margin structures.

  • Structural shape: The chain is best described as hub-and-spoke — originator IP holders sit at the commercial-value hub, with specialist service providers (CROs, CDMOs, distributors) radiating out, and payer/health-system gatekeepers forming an outer ring that mediates access to the end patient [#J]

  • Linear fallacy: A naive linear reading (discovery → clinical → manufacturing → distribution → patient) obscures the payer overlay — in reimbursement-gated markets (US Medicare/Medicaid, EU national health systems, Japanese NHI), the payer sits between manufacturer and patient and exerts margin pressure that a pure-linear chain map misses [#A]

  • Hub definition: The originator/IP stage is the hub because royalty flows, milestone payments, licensing fees, and supply agreements all radiate back to it — every other stage earns a service margin; only the originator earns a rent on the underlying biology [#J]

§2 — BioArctic's Position Within the Chain

  • Positional verdict: BioArctic is a single-stage originator specialist concentrated in Stages 1-4 (discovery through IP/licensing) with partial Stage 6 involvement — it does not own manufacturing, distribution, or prescribing capacity [#J]

  • Stage occupied: Stages 1, 2, 3 (historically), and 4 (primary value-accretion stage) — pure-play neurodegenerative originator with IP as the commercial asset [#J]

  • Upstream integration: BioArctic has no meaningful upstream integration — it does not own CRO capacity, animal facilities at scale, or specialised neuroscience tooling beyond internal lab infrastructure; it is a pure customer of Stage 2/3 service providers [#A]

  • Downstream integration: BioArctic has no downstream integration — it does not own manufacturing (Stage 5), distribution (Stage 8), or prescribing infrastructure (Stage 9); Leqembi's commercial execution is fully delegated to Eisai/Biogen [#J]

  • Nordic co-commercialisation carve-out: BioArctic retains the right to co-commercialise Leqembi in the Nordic countries — this is a partial downstream presence but is small-scale relative to the global value of the asset; it does not constitute structural downstream integration [#A]

  • Partner dependency: The Eisai partnership is the defining structural fact of BioArctic's chain position — BioArctic converted Stage 1-3 investment into a Stage 4 royalty/milestone stream by partnering; the downstream chain is entirely Eisai-operated [#J]

  • Integration comparison — no cohort peers identified: Peer-context gap noted (no D-BATCH-25 cohort provided); structurally, BioArctic's position resembles AC Immune (Swiss, discovery-stage, licensed-out model) more than Biogen or Eisai (fully integrated) [#I]

  • AC Immune analogue: AC Immune licensed Crenezumab to Roche and Semorinemab to Genentech — similarly single-stage originator; BioArctic's commercial differentiation is that Leqembi is approved and generating royalties, not merely licensed for development

  • Prothena analogue: Prothena similarly depends on Roche co-development; single-stage specialist; less commercially advanced than BioArctic

  • IP control quality: BioArctic holds co-ownership of Leqembi IP alongside Eisai — this is structurally superior to a pure royalty licensee; co-ownership provides governance participation rights [#J]

  • Pipeline stage coverage: Beyond Leqembi, BioArctic has pipeline candidates (BAN2401 next-gen work, alpha-synuclein programme for Parkinson's — BAN0805/PR006 with Abbvie partnership) — these suggest Stage 1-2 activity is ongoing rather than a single-cycle originator [#A]

  • Abbvie partnership (Parkinson's): The collaboration with AbbVie for the alpha-synuclein antibody programme replicates the Eisai model at an earlier stage — BioArctic originates, large-pharma partners; the pattern is systematic, not opportunistic [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on BioArctic's positional choice is that single-stage specialist focus creates catastrophic concentration if Eisai de-prioritises Leqembi's launch or if Leqembi fails commercially — BioArctic has no manufacturing asset, no distribution infrastructure, no prescribing relationships to fall back on; integration would not necessarily drag margins given royalty economics, but the absence of any operational fallback is structurally fragile

§5 — Power Dynamics + Pricing Leverage

  • Power dynamics verdict: BioArctic has structural pricing power at the IP stage but exercises it at one remove — Eisai negotiates list price and net price; BioArctic's effective pricing leverage is mediated through its partnership agreement, not direct market action [#J]

  • Pricing power — absolute: Leqembi's US list price of ~$26,500/year (announced 2023) was set by Eisai as lead commercialisator — BioArctic participates in joint development committee decisions but does not independently set price; pricing power is real but delegated [#J]

  • Differentiation basis: Leqembi (lecanemab) is the only FDA-approved amyloid antibody with full traditional approval and CMS Medicare coverage (as of mid-2024, after initial coverage restrictions lifted) — Donanemab (Lilly) received approval July 2024; differentiation narrowed but not eliminated [#A]

  • Customer concentration — Eisai dominant: BioArctic has one primary commercial-stage customer in practical terms: Eisai; milestone payments, royalties, and collaboration fees derive almost entirely from this single relationship [#J]

  • Switching cost — BioArctic: BioArctic cannot switch away from Eisai for Leqembi without contractual consequence; the co-development agreement governs the asset; this is bilateral lock-in — Eisai cannot easily replace BioArctic's co-ownership either

  • Power balance — co-ownership: Co-ownership of the IP (not pure licensee status) gives BioArctic structural leverage that a pure royalty recipient lacks — this is above-median positioning vs comparable discovery-stage biotechs

  • Supplier leverage — CROs: BioArctic as clinical-stage operator purchases CRO services from IQVIA, ICON, and similar — in Phase I-II trial work, CROs have moderate-to-high leverage because global neurological-trial expertise is concentrated among 5-6 global CROs [#I]

  • Supplier leverage — CDMO (indirect): BioArctic does not directly contract CDMO manufacturing for Leqembi; Eisai holds manufacturing relationships with Lonza and others; BioArctic's indirect exposure to CDMO leverage exists via Eisai's cost structure affecting profit-sharing economics

  • Customer leverage — payers: The US CMS coverage decision (February 2024 full coverage for Traditional Medicare) was a binary event for Leqembi's commercial trajectory — payers at Stage 7 have extraordinary leverage; a single coverage decision from CMS was more impactful than any competitive dynamic at Stage 4 [#J]

  • EU payer leverage: In Sweden (BioArctic's home market), TLV (dental and pharmaceutical benefits agency) controls reimbursement; Leqembi's Swedish/Nordic reimbursement status is an open question as of early 2026 — this is direct market-access risk for BioArctic's co-commercialisation rights [#A]

  • Japanese payer leverage: Japan (Eisai's home market) approved Leqembi in September 2023 under the brand name Leqembi; Japanese NHI pricing set at approximately ¥298,179/month (~$2,000/month); below US list price but meaningful volume given Japanese Alzheimer's prevalence

  • Substitutes — Donanemab: Eli Lilly's Donanemab (Kisunla) approval in July 2024 introduces the first direct substitute at Stage 4 — both are amyloid antibodies with broadly similar efficacy signals; BioArctic's effective pricing power narrows as physician choice expands [#A]

  • Differentiation retention: Leqembi retains monthly IV dosing (vs biweekly for Donanemab) and a broader approved population (early Alzheimer's with confirmed amyloid); label differentiation remains but is modest

  • Regulatory barrier: The FDA's biologics approval pathway , CMS coverage process, and EMA centralised procedure create multi-year, capital-intensive barriers to new entrants at Stage 4 — structural protection for existing approved amyloid antibodies [#J]

  • Scale economy — Stage 4: Scale economies at the IP stage are not traditional manufacturing-scale economies — they are portfolio-scale economies (larger IP portfolios diversify binary trial risk) and scientific-platform economies (BioArctic's Arctic Technology platform generates multiple candidates from shared biology) [#A]

  • Switching cost — physician level: Neurologists have low direct switching costs between Leqembi and Donanemab in principle, but ARIA monitoring protocols, infusion centre setup, and payer pre-authorisation processes create operational switching friction that benefits first-mover incumbents [#I]

  • Dictated terms — royalty: BioArctic's royalty rate and milestone structure were negotiated upfront with Eisai — the terms are fixed by contract until renegotiation events; BioArctic cannot unilaterally increase its royalty capture if Leqembi performs above expectations (unless milestones trigger) [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on power dynamics is that BioArctic's apparent IP-stage pricing power is largely illusory at the operating level — Eisai sets net price, manages payer relationships, and determines commercial investment intensity; BioArctic is a passive royalty recipient on Leqembi's commercial success; if Eisai under-invests in commercial execution or accepts unfavourable net prices from payers, BioArctic has limited contractual recourse to change outcomes

§6 — Competitive Map at BioArctic's Stage

  • Competitive intensity verdict: The originator/discovery stage for amyloid and tau neurodegeneration targets is oligopolistic by asset quality — a small number of approved or late-stage assets define the competitive set; many earlier-stage biotechs compete at the discovery level but not yet at the commercial level [#J]

  • Stage concentration: The approved amyloid-antibody stage is effectively a duopoly (Lecanemab/Leqembi + Donanemab/Kisunla as of mid-2025) — highly concentrated; failed assets (Aducanumab's commercial failure, Solanezumab, Crenezumab) illustrate that discovery-stage fragmentation does not translate to commercial-stage concentration [#J]

  • Competitor — leader (Eisai/Biogen, Leqembi): BioArctic's own asset, via co-ownership — positioned as first-mover in fully-approved monthly-dosing amyloid antibody; CMS full coverage secured Feb 2024 [#J]

  • Competitor — challenger (Eli Lilly, Donanemab/Kisunla): FDA-approved July 2024; biweekly dosing; TRAILBLAZER-ALZ 2 Phase 3 data showed comparable efficacy on CDR-SB; potential to take share via quarterly dosing maintenance option; Lilly's commercial infrastructure substantially larger than Eisai's [#A]

  • Lilly asymmetry: Eli Lilly has $30Bn+ revenue vs Eisai's ~$5Bn — Lilly can outspend Eisai on Donanemab's commercial launch; this is a structural commercial disadvantage for Leqembi/BioArctic's royalty base

  • Donanemab differentiation: Donanemab's amyloid-clearance profile (full clearance in ~70% of patients) enables a treatment-cessation model — structurally different commercial proposition to Leqembi's continuous dosing

  • Competitor — specialist (AC Immune, Crenezumab/Semorinemab pipeline): AC Immune's pipeline includes tau-targeting and TDP-43 assets; failed at Phase 3 for primary Alzheimer's targets thus far; not yet a commercial-stage competitor but an active discovery-stage competitor [#I]

  • Competitor — niche (Prothena, PRX012 and others): Prothena (partnered with Roche) is developing next-generation amyloid antibodies; sub-cutaneous delivery is the differentiation angle; not yet at approval stage [#I]

  • Competitor — niche (Neurimmune/UCB, Bepranemab): Tau-targeting antibodies at Phase 2 — different mechanism from amyloid; not direct substitute for Leqembi but potentially complementary; represents the next competitive frontier [#I]

  • Differentiation dimension — mechanism specificity: BioArctic's Arctic Technology platform generates protofibril-selective antibodies — Leqembi's selectivity for toxic soluble protofibrils (vs insoluble plaques) is the scientific basis for the safety/efficacy profile claimed to differentiate from Aducanumab [#J]

  • Scale-cost dimension: At the discovery stage, human capital + platform technology (not scale) determines competitive position; BioArctic's lab scale is small but its platform is validated by one approved drug — this is an unusual combination at BioArctic's market-cap tier [#A]

  • Geographic dimension: Discovery-stage competition is global — US (multiple biotech hubs), Europe (Basel, Stockholm), and Asia (Eisai, Takeda internally); BioArctic's Nordic location is scientifically neutral but creates proximity to European regulatory pathways and TLV/Nordic payer systems [#I]

  • Stage fragmentation at discovery: Hundreds of biotechs are in earlier-stage Alzheimer's discovery — tau, TDP-43, neuroinflammation (TREM2), synaptic plasticity targets; discovery stage is highly fragmented; only the approved-drug sub-stage is concentrated [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on the competitive map is that Lilly's commercial firepower asymmetry may shift the amyloid-antibody duopoly in Donanemab's favour over the 2025-2027 commercial cycle — BioArctic's royalties depend on Leqembi maintaining volume share, but Eisai's smaller commercial team and lower marketing budget vs Lilly's creates a structural disadvantage that the underlying science cannot offset

§7 — Competitive Maps at Adjacent Stages + Vertical Integration Threats

  • Adjacent-stage verdict: BioArctic faces low direct vertical-integration threat from CDMOs (Stage 5) and low threat from distributors (Stage 8), but a credible downstream integration threat from large-pharma partners who could expand from Stage 5 into discovery-stage acquisition [#J]

Supplier Stage (Stages 1-3 inputs: CROs, preclinical labs)

  • Supplier — leader (CROs): IQVIA, ICON, PPD/Thermo Fisher Scientific are the top-3 global CROs by revenue — combined CRO market ~$80Bn globally (2024 estimate); IQVIA alone ~$15Bn revenue [#I]

  • Supplier consolidation: CRO market has consolidated significantly over the past decade — ICON acquired PRA Health Sciences ($12Bn, 2021); Syneos Health was taken private ($7.1Bn, 2023); fewer, larger CRO operators with increasing pricing leverage over small-cap clinical sponsors [#A]

  • Supplier leverage — CNS specialisation: Neurological trial management is a specialised sub-market within CROs — CNS-experienced CROs (Premier Research, Worldwide Clinical Trials, CNS-specialist units within ICON/IQVIA) have higher switching costs because of protocol expertise, site relationships, and rater training [#I]

  • Supplier vertical integration threat — low: CROs have not integrated into drug discovery — their business model is fee-for-service; there is no structural incentive for ICON or IQVIA to become drug originators; vertical integration threat from CRO suppliers into BioArctic's stage is negligible [#J]

Manufacturing Stage (Stage 5: CDMOs)

  • CDMO — leader: Lonza is the global biologics CDMO leader by capacity and revenue (~CHF 6.7Bn, 2023); Samsung Biologics (~₩3.7Tr revenue, 2023) and WuXi Biologics are the key challengers; the top-5 control estimated 50-60% of large-molecule CDMO capacity [#A]

  • CDMO consolidation: The CDMO market is consolidating at the large-molecule (mAb) end — capacity additions post-COVID are contracting as biosimilar demand disappoints vs expectations; this creates utilisation leverage for established CDMOs in 2024-2026 [#I]

  • CDMO vertical integration — low-to-moderate risk: WuXi Biologics' parent (WuXi AppTec) has demonstrated appetite for adjacency expansion — CXO model expands toward drug-innovation services; however, direct CDMO integration into originator-discovery remains rare and faces regulatory/reputational barriers [#I]

  • BioArctic indirect exposure: BioArctic does not directly contract Lonza/Samsung for Leqembi — Eisai holds the manufacturing relationship; BioArctic's indirect exposure is via Eisai's manufacturing cost structure, which affects net profit shared with BioArctic on Leqembi's P&L [#A]

Customer Stage (Stages 7-9: payers, hospitals, neurologists)

  • Customer — leader (payers): CMS (US Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services) is the single most powerful customer-stage operator for Leqembi — its February 2024 full-coverage decision unlocked the Medicare-eligible Alzheimer's market; no other customer decision is comparable in scale [#J]

  • Customer consolidation — payers: US commercial insurance concentration is high — top-5 US health plans (UnitedHealth, Elevance, Aetna/CVS, Cigna, Humana) control ~70% of commercial covered lives; consolidated negotiating power is significant [#A]

  • Customer vertical integration — moderate threat: Large pharma/payer integrated models (e.g., CVS/Aetna, UnitedHealth/Optum) are integrating upstream into pharmacy benefit management and specialty drug management — this creates indirect pricing pressure on Leqembi's net price but does not constitute integration into BioArctic's discovery stage [#I]

  • Large-pharma acquisition threat (downstream into BioArctic): The most credible vertical-integration threat is large pharma acquiring BioArctic to internalise the discovery platform and pipeline — this is acquisition-as-integration; Roche, Lilly, AstraZeneca, and AbbVie have all made CNS/neurology acquisitions; BioArctic's sub-$1Bn market cap makes it an addressable target [#J]

  • AbbVie relevance: AbbVie is already a collaboration partner for BioArctic's alpha-synuclein programme — this existing relationship creates a precedent and due-diligence familiarity that could facilitate a deeper integration or acquisition

  • Eisai integration option: Eisai could theoretically acquire BioArctic to convert from co-development partner to full owner; given Eisai's balance sheet (~¥600Bn revenue, moderate leverage) this is financially feasible; it would remove BioArctic's royalty exposure in exchange for acquisition premium

  • Sceptical bullet: The most credible vertical-integration vector is not CDMOs entering discovery, but large-pharma partners absorbing BioArctic — BioArctic's single largest adjacency vulnerability is that Eisai or AbbVie could internalise its value at an acquisition price that reflects current market-cap (sub-$1Bn) rather than the NPV of the royalty stream + pipeline optionality; whether this is risk or opportunity depends on acquisition premium vs intrinsic value

§8 — Structural Changes + Evolution

  • Structural evolution verdict: The neurodegenerative drug value chain is undergoing four simultaneous structural shifts — diagnostic integration, payer-gated access tightening, large-pharma consolidation at the originator stage, and subcutaneous delivery innovation — each reshaping where profit accrues [#J]

  • Stage consolidation — approved assets: The approved amyloid-antibody stage is consolidating around two assets (Leqembi, Kisunla) after a decade of failures; this concentrates commercial-stage competition and reduces fragmentation risk for existing holders [#J]

  • Timeline: FDA clearance for Fujifilm Lumipulse p-tau217 (2024) and Roche/Elecsys amyloid beta 1-42/1-40 assay represent early commercial plasma biomarker availability; widespread adoption est. 2026-2028 based on healthcare-system integration timelines

  • Technology disruption — subcutaneous delivery: Multiple players (Prothena/Roche PRX012, next-gen Eisai formulations) are developing subcutaneous administration forms of amyloid antibodies — if approved, SC delivery would remove the infusion-centre bottleneck at Stage 9, lowering administration barriers and expanding volume [#I]

  • Implication for Leqembi: Eisai has stated intent to develop a SC formulation of lecanemab — if successful, this removes a key adoption barrier (limited infusion capacity) and structurally benefits BioArctic's royalty base

  • Business model innovation — combination therapy: Emerging evidence suggests combination amyloid + tau targeting may become standard of care post-2028 — this would structurally shift Stage 4 toward combination regimens, potentially creating IP-pairing dynamics where owning one mechanism only captures partial combination value [#I]
