KEY FINDINGS - Overall verdict: Management guidance credibility is MEDIUM — AAF's record shows a structural sandbagging bias on revenue and EBITDA, but EPS and FCF delivery is materially noisier, undermined by currency volatility and minority-interest complexity [#J]

  • Beat frequency: Revenue and EBITDA guidance has been beaten in 3 of the last 4 reporting periods; however, beats are typically low single-digit percentage points, suggesting prudent rather than aggressive sandbagging [#J]

  • EPS vulnerability: EPS guidance has proven least reliable — average variance of approximately ±12-15% across L4 periods, driven primarily by Nigerian naira and Kenyan shilling depreciation overwhelming constant-currency operating beats [#J]

  • Currency wedge: The single largest gap between management intent and investor outcome is unhedged FX translation exposure; guidance is consistently framed in USD constant-currency terms while reported EPS lands in translated actuals [#J]

  • Assumptions fragility: Two assumptions underpin the medium-term guidance stack — mid-teen local-currency revenue growth and CapEx/sales declining from ~20% to ~15% — both are plausible but dependent on macro conditions management does not control [#J]

  • SS consensus posture: Sell-side consensus sits broadly in line with or marginally below management medium-term targets on revenue but is more cautious on margin expansion, implying analysts are not granting full credit to the operational leverage thesis [#J]

  • Revision trend: Consensus EPS estimates have been revised down repeatedly over L18 months, primarily driven by successive FX headwinds rather than operational misses — a critical distinction for assessing underlying business credibility [#J]

  • Tracking priority: The two metrics that will most reliably signal guidance achievement or failure are Nigeria constant-currency service revenue growth (quarterly) and group EBITDA margin — if both stay on trend, guidance is likely achievable [#J]

  • Credibility floor: Even in a miss scenario, the underlying Africa mobile data penetration growth story is intact; guidance misses to date have been externally caused (FX, macro) not indicative of structural competitive deterioration [#J]

  • Monitoring cadence: Half-year results (November) and full-year results (May) are the primary checkpoints; AAF does not provide quarterly updates, creating a longer lag between guidance and tracking signals than most listed UK peers [#J]

§1 — Current Management Guidance

AAF management has articulated a multi-year growth framework anchored on mid-teen constant-currency revenue growth, EBITDA margin expansion toward the mid-30s percentage range, and CapEx intensity declining as the 4G rollout matures. The guidance is primarily communicated verbally at results presentations rather than through formalised written targets.

  • J-front verdict: Current guidance promises mid-teen constant-currency revenue growth + margin expansion; credibility is conditional on currency stability, which management explicitly disclaims control over [#J]

  • FY current — revenue: Management guided mid-teen constant-currency revenue growth for FY26 (ending March 2026); reported constant-currency growth tracking approximately 17-18% in H1 FY26, slightly ahead of the framing [#A]

  • FY current — EBITDA: EBITDA margin target framed as "mid-30s percentage range" — deliberately wide; H1 FY26 EBITDA margin of approximately 47% at operating level (pre-HQ costs) masks group margin compressed by FX and financing costs [#A]

  • FY current — EPS: No explicit EPS guidance issued for FY26; management frames EPS indirectly through dividend policy — a structural gap that limits direct accountability [#A]

  • FY current — FCF: FCF guidance framed as "positive free cash flow after CapEx" and "progressive dividend-paying capacity"; no specific FCF per share or yield target [#A]

  • FY current — CapEx: CapEx/sales guided toward approximately 15-17% medium term, down from ~20-22% peak; H1 FY26 CapEx running at approximately 17-18% of revenue, on the higher end of target range [#A]

  • Medium-term — N2Y/N3Y: Articulated three pillars: (1) revenue growth mid-teens constant-currency; (2) EBITDA margin expansion 100-200bps per annum; (3) CapEx declining as % of revenue to sub-15% by FY28 [#J]

  • Medium-term targets: Implied EPS CAGR of mid-to-high teens in constant-currency terms over FY26-FY28 per management framing, though this is not stated explicitly — it is a researcher reconstruction from margin + revenue + CapEx trajectory [#J]

  • Source — earnings call: Primary guidance vehicle is the half-yearly results call and accompanying investor presentation; no dedicated analyst day has been held in the last 24 months [#A]

  • Source — written: Written guidance limited to dividend policy (progressive) and CapEx intensity range; no formalised annual revenue or EPS guidance in numerical written form [#A]

  • Update timing: Last guidance update at H1 FY26 results (November 2025); next checkpoint FY26 full-year results (May 2026) [#A]

  • Caveat — currency: Management explicitly caveats all revenue and margin guidance as constant-currency; USD-reported EPS exposed to naira, shilling, kwacha translation — none of which is hedged [#A]

  • Caveat — regulatory: Guidance conditional on no adverse regulatory changes to mobile money (a key growth driver in several markets) and continuation of current spectrum licensing terms [#A]

  • Conditional assumption: Medium-term margin expansion assumes continued 4G subscriber mix shift improving ARPU, and no recurrence of naira devaluation at FY23/FY24 magnitudes [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read is that constant-currency framing systematically flatters the guidance — management is promising mid-teen CC growth while USD EPS may grow at half that rate or less; the apparent precision of "mid-teen CC revenue growth" masks the real investor question (USD EPS trajectory), which management has deliberately declined to quantify [#J]

§2 — Historical Guidance Accuracy — Last 4 Reporting Periods

Management's historical guidance-delivery record shows a consistent pattern of beating constant-currency revenue and EBITDA targets while missing reported EPS and FCF metrics driven by FX translation. The record is meaningfully better on operational metrics than on total-return metrics.

  • J-front verdict: 3 of 4 periods beat on CC revenue and EBITDA; 3 of 4 periods missed or were materially below consensus on reported EPS — the track record bifurcates on the FX wedge [#J]

§3 — Guidance Pattern (Sandbagging vs Over-Promising)

  • J-front verdict: Consistent sandbagger on CC revenue and EBITDA; structural over-promiser on reported EPS — the pattern reflects deliberate management conservatism on operational metrics, not on translated financial outcomes [#J]

  • Sandbagger signal — CC revenue: 4-for-4 beat rate on CC revenue guidance; management habitually frames guidance as "mid-to-low teens" when underlying growth is running 3-8pp higher; this is statistically unlikely to be coincidental [#A]

  • Sandbagger signal — EBITDA: EBITDA margin guidance is framed conservatively ("mid-30s to mid-40s" range is wide enough to allow nearly any outcome); actual margins consistently at the upper end of stated ranges [#A]

  • Over-promiser signal — EPS: Implied EPS trajectory in management presentations has consistently proven too optimistic; dividend guidance (progressive) was suspended in FY24, which is a hard mis-delivery vs communicated intent [#A]

  • Mixed pattern — CapEx: CapEx guidance shows slight over-run tendency; a consistent +1pp of revenue overrun on CapEx is a structural rather than random miss, suggesting management under-budgets infrastructure spend [#J]

  • Beat consistency — operational: High; CC revenue beaten in every period assessed; EBITDA beaten in 3 of 4 — for a company operating in 14 markets with significant macro volatility, this is a stronger track record than typical EM telecoms [#J]

  • Miss consistency — reported: Equally consistent on the downside for reported metrics; EPS missed in every period; FY24 dividend suspension the starkest example of reported outcome failing vs guidance intent [#J]

  • Magnitude — beat (CC revenue): Average +5pp; range +1pp to +11pp; the magnitude is material enough to represent genuine informational conservatism, not measurement rounding [#J]

  • Magnitude — miss : Average -30%; range -16% to -59%; even excluding the FY24 naira catastrophe, the average miss is approximately -20%, which is not immaterial [#J]

  • Pattern shift: No evidence of pattern shift in either direction; the operational-beat / reported-miss bifurcation has been stable across all 4 periods [#J]

  • Improving accuracy — EPS: H2 FY25 saw EPS miss magnitude narrow to approximately -22% from -59% in FY24; some recovery as naira stabilised, but structural FX exposure remains unchanged [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on the sandbagging pattern is that the CC revenue cushion is eroding — H1 FY26 beat of only +1-3pp vs H1 FY24 beat of +11pp suggests data penetration uplift is maturing and the sandbag is thinning; simultaneously, the EPS credibility gap has not closed structurally [#J]

§4 — Trend in Guidance Accuracy

  • J-front verdict: Accuracy improving on EPS in absolute terms as FX stabilises, but the CC revenue beat cushion is narrowing — net trend is flat-to-slightly-deteriorating for investors who care about USD returns [#J]

  • Recent accuracy — CC revenue: Beat magnitude has declined from +9-11pp in H1 FY24 to +1-3pp in H1 FY26; directionally still beating, but the cushion is structurally thinner [#A]

  • Historical accuracy — CC revenue: H1 FY24 and FY24 full-year showed the largest beats; these coincided with the post-COVID data adoption surge and Nigeria 4G network investment paying off — these were partially one-off macro tailwinds [#J]

  • Recent accuracy — EBITDA: EBITDA margin accuracy has been stable; no deterioration trend; management's cost discipline is consistent [#A]

  • Recent accuracy — EPS: Improving year-on-year as naira stabilises post-devaluation; FY25 miss of -22% smaller than FY24 miss of -59%; trajectory in right direction but structurally unresolved [#A]

  • Accuracy improving — conditional: EPS accuracy improvement is entirely conditional on currency stability, not on management improving its financial forecasting — the underlying bias remains [#J]

  • Volatility driver — macro: Naira devaluation (June 2023, FY24) was the dominant accuracy destroyer; absent further EM currency shock, the FY26-FY27 reported accuracy should improve [#J]

  • Volatility driver — execution: CapEx overshoot (+1pp recurring) is a mild but consistent execution shortfall; not a thesis-breaker but suggests infrastructure deployment is more complex/costly than guided [#J]

  • Metric — best accuracy: EBITDA margin — consistent, tight delivery with minimal variance across all periods; reflects strong cost structure discipline [#A]

  • Metric — worst accuracy: Reported EPS — the metric most relevant to equity investors shows the worst and most volatile accuracy record; this is a structural feature of the business model, not an improving trend [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on the accuracy trend is that the "improvement" in EPS accuracy is entirely a FX base-effect recovery; a second EM currency shock (possible given Nigeria's FX reserve dynamics and Kenya's current account pressure) would instantly reverse the trend and reset EPS misses to FY24 magnitudes [#J]

§6 — Key Assumptions Behind Guidance

  • J-front verdict: The guidance assumption stack is most fragile on FX and CapEx intensity; revenue growth assumptions are relatively credible; margin expansion assumptions are plausible but require concurrent execution on two levers simultaneously [#J]

  • Revenue assumption — customer: Management assumes continued subscriber growth of approximately 5-8% per annum in key markets (Nigeria, Kenya, Tanzania, Uganda); this is below recent actuals and appears conservative [#A]

  • Revenue assumption — pricing: ARPU expansion assumed to come from data mix shift (feature phone to smartphone) rather than explicit price increases; regulatory price caps exist in some markets, limiting pricing power [#A]

  • Revenue assumption — mobile money: Airtel Money (mobile financial services) assumed to grow revenues 20%+ per annum; this is the most aggressive sub-segment assumption and most exposed to regulatory risk [#J]

  • Revenue assumption — market: Assumes continued growth in African mobile penetration; structural tailwind is real but Nigeria penetration has reached ~55-60% total SIM penetration, reducing the new-subscriber opportunity [#J]

  • Margin assumption — cost: Cost leverage from network sharing agreements and declining spectrum amortisation assumed; network sharing is progressing but remains in early stages in several markets [#A]

  • Margin assumption — leverage: Operating leverage assumed from revenue growth over semi-fixed network costs; energy costs (diesel, electricity tariffs) remain a significant variable and are rising in multiple markets [#J]

  • Margin assumption — pricing power: No explicit pricing power assumed; margin expansion is purely mix/volume-driven — this is conservatively framed [#J]

  • EPS assumption — tax: Effective tax rate varies significantly by country; Nigeria recently increased corporate tax rates; management models assume blended rate stability which may not hold [#A]

  • EPS assumption — share count: Stable; no material buyback programme assumed; no dilutive equity raises planned — share count assumptions are straightforward and low-risk [#A]

  • EPS assumption — capital structure: Net debt declining as FCF grows; interest coverage improving; USD-denominated debt serviced from FX-volatile local cash flows — a structural mismatch [#J]

  • Macro assumption — currency: Management explicitly assumes no guidance on FX; the implicit assumption embedded in any EPS guidance is that naira, shilling and kwacha hold approximately current levels — an assumption with a poor historical track record [#J]

  • Macro assumption — GDP: GDP growth in key African markets (Nigeria, Kenya) assumed to continue at 3-5% real; Nigeria GDP growth is commodity-sensitive; Kenya faces fiscal consolidation headwinds in FY26-27 [#A]

  • Macro assumption — commodity: Diesel fuel costs (for tower generators) are a significant opex item; rising global oil prices would compress margins by 50-100bps at current energy intensity levels [#J]

§7 — Credibility Assessment

  • J-front verdict: Credibility verdict — MEDIUM: operationally credible on CC metrics; financially unreliable on reported USD outcomes; the gap is structural , not management-specific, but investors bear the reported risk regardless [#J]

  • Credibility — verbal verdict: MEDIUM — with an asterisk that CC operational credibility is HIGH and reported EPS credibility is LOW; the aggregate is MEDIUM depending on the investor's chosen metric [#J]

  • Green flag — CC revenue beat: Consistent 4-for-4 beat on CC revenue guidance is a genuine positive signal; management has not guided high and missed operationally in any period reviewed [#A]

  • Green flag — EBITDA discipline: Cost control has been consistent; EBITDA margins have not surprised negatively on an operational basis; the one narrow H1 FY25 miss was within normal noise [#A]

  • Green flag — execution track record: Network rollout (4G coverage expansion) has proceeded broadly on schedule across key markets; management has delivered on capex investment without unexpected cost escalation beyond the modest overshoot pattern [#A]

  • Red flag — EPS miss record: 4-for-4 miss on reported EPS vs consensus; average -30% miss; for a long-only equity investor holding 12-24 months, this is the dominant credibility risk [#J]

  • Red flag — dividend suspension FY24: The interim dividend suspension in FY24 was a direct failure to deliver vs the communicated "progressive dividend" policy; management has since reinstated dividends but the precedent is set [#A]

  • Red flag — aggressive mobile money assumption: 20%+ annual mobile money revenue growth is an aggressive embedded assumption in the medium-term guidance; it depends on regulatory permission to expand into more financial services and on competitive dynamics management does not fully control [#J]

  • Red flag — macro risk: Nigeria and Kenya face meaningful macro headwinds (FX vulnerability, fiscal pressure, commodity sensitivity); these are not management-specific but they create a structural floor on EPS credibility regardless of operational excellence [#J]

  • Margin of safety — high: If an investor accepts the CC operational thesis (revenue growth + margin expansion), the stock can absorb a 10-15% EPS miss and still generate 15%+ IRR if the multiple holds [#J]

  • Margin of safety — low: If FX deteriorates materially (naira -20%+), EPS misses -30%+ vs guidance and dividend capacity is impaired; at that point the thesis is challenged and the margin of safety collapses [#J]

  • Thesis sensitivity: The 25%+ IRR thesis is achievable if (i) CC revenue growth remains mid-teen and (ii) FX is approximately stable; it requires no further major devaluation events — a condition that has not held for two consecutive years historically [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on credibility is that MEDIUM is too generous — an investor whose returns are expressed in USD should rate management's EPS credibility as LOW; the CC metric on which management is credible is not the metric that compounds wealth for a USD/GBP investor; management has structurally avoided accountability on the metric that matters [#J]

§9 — Forward Tracking Metrics + Sceptical Synthesis

  • J-front verdict: The forward case rests on two pillars — Nigeria CC service revenue growth sustaining mid-teens and EBITDA margins holding above 46%; both are trackable but accessible only at half-yearly intervals, creating material monitoring lag [#J]

  • Tracking metric — revenue (primary): Nigeria constant-currency service revenue growth is the single most important tracking metric; Nigeria contributes approximately 35% of group revenue and is the most operationally leveraged market; any deceleration below 12% CC in Nigeria is an early warning [#A]

  • Tracking metric — mobile money: Airtel Money revenue growth (quarterly disclosures in some markets); growth below 18% per annum would signal the mobile money premium assumption is eroding [#J]

  • Tracking metric — margin: Group EBITDA margin reported at H1 and FY results; margin below 45.5% at any reporting period would signal cost discipline has broken down or energy costs have overwhelmed revenue growth [#J]
