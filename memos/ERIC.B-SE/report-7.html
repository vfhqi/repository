§2 — Key Driver #1: North America Capex Normalisation

  • Verdict: North America is the single most important driver of Ericsson's share price over the next 24 months — it accounts for ~41% of group revenue and determines whether FY2026–27 looks like a trough or an extended decline. #J
  • Observation: Q1 2026 saw a mid-single-digit NA revenue decline following a particularly strong Q1 2025 comparative; management explicitly guided that Q1 trajectory is "probably similar to what should be expected for the year." #I
  • Observation: Verizon guided FY2026 capex of $16.0–16.5bn, representing a staggering ~$4bn reduction vs combined 2025 levels; the C-band 5G rollout covering 300 million Americans is effectively complete, removing the major build impulse. #I
  • Observation: AT&T and T-Mobile have their own capex cycles; T-Mobile's mid-band 5G build is more advanced, while AT&T's FirstNet obligations and fibre convergence spending provide some floor to equipment demand. #I
  • Analysis: The relevant question is not whether NA is declining (confirmed) but whether 2026 represents a trough with 2027 recovery or whether densification/5G Advanced/6G prep sustains capex depression through 2028. #A
  • Analysis: Historical RAN cycle pattern: the 2014–2016 NA downcycle lasted approximately 2–3 years before 4G densification spending restarted; the current 5G primary build cycle ended in 2022–2023 and the normalisation period has already lasted 2+ years. #A
  • Observation: Ericsson's North America revenue peaked at approximately $12–13bn equivalent in 2022 at the height of C-band deployments; current run-rate implies meaningful step-down, creating a more favourable base effect from 2H 2026 onwards. #I
  • Analysis: The 5G Advanced upgrade cycle (Release 18/19 features) and eventual 6G infrastructure could re-stimulate NA capex from 2027–2028; Ericsson's technology leadership in AI-native RAN positions it well for this refresh if it materialises. #A
  • Verdict: Ericsson does not need NA capex to grow in absolute terms to recover its share price — it needs NA to stop declining, which could occur as soon as H2 2026 if the comparison base normalises. #J
  • Observation: T-Mobile has been gaining subscriber share aggressively, creating incentives for AT&T and Verizon to invest in network experience differentiation; this competitive dynamic is an underappreciated floor on NA capex. #I
  • Analysis: Ericsson's NA market share is roughly stable at around 40–45% of the non-Huawei, non-ZTE NA RAN market; Nokia is the primary competitor domestically; Samsung is a smaller but growing challenger. #A
  • ❌ Management's comfort with "Q1 trajectory repeating through 2026" effectively pre-guides a full-year NA revenue decline; investors who model even flat NA for H2 2026 are likely too optimistic. #J
  • Observation: Latin America partially offset NA weakness in Q1 2026; the Americas segment total declined approximately 2%, better than the NA component in isolation would imply. #I
  • Observation: FWA (Fixed Wireless Access) infrastructure spending by US carriers is a growing sub-category that partially offsets traditional mobile RAN softness — Ericsson participates in this via both RAN and core network equipment. #I

Plausibility verdict: Medium — NA cycle troughs in 2026 and recovers modestly in 2027; key assumption is that 5G Advanced refresh begins 2027 and operators do not enter a multi-year capex freeze given competitive dynamics.

§3 — Key Driver #2: Networks Gross Margin Recovery and Restructuring Delivery

  • Verdict: Networks segment margin recovery is the most tangible and near-term value driver — structural improvement from 38–42% (2019–2022) to the current 49–51% range is real and management-guided to sustain. #J
  • Observation: Networks segment gross margin was 50.4% in Q1 2026; the company guided Q2 2026 Networks adjusted gross margin of 49–51%, confirming the corridor is intact. #I
  • Observation: The rolling 4-quarter adjusted gross margin for Networks reached 49.9% at end-FY2025, and the overall group adjusted gross margin was 48.1% in Q1 2026 — both peer-leading versus Nokia's typical 38–40% group gross margin. #I
  • Analysis: The margin improvement has been driven by: (1) product mix shift away from low-margin hardware toward software licensing; (2) pruning of sub-scale geographies and unprofitable contracts; (3) manufacturing footprint rationalisation; (4) headcount reduction (Ericsson cut ~11,000 roles in 2023). #A
  • Observation: FY2026 restructuring charges are guided at an elevated level, with "a fairly large part" front-loaded in Q1 2026 per CFO Lars Sandström — the front-loading is cash-costly near term but should yield recurring savings from H2 2026. #I
  • Observation: Operating expenses (ex-restructuring) dropped SEK 2bn YoY in Q1 2026, driven partly by FX and partly by the divestment of iconectiv and genuine headcount savings — the underlying cost trajectory is improving. #I
  • Analysis: The 15–18% adjusted EBITA margin target for FY2026 requires a meaningful H2 step-up from the 11.3% achieved in Q1; historical seasonality favours H2 (Q4 typically largest revenue quarter), but the H2-weighting creates execution risk. #A
  • Observation: Networks EBITA margin (rolling 4Q) was 20.3% at end-FY2025 — this segment is already delivering toward the long-term target, implying the drag on group EBITA is coming from Cloud Software & Services and Enterprise, not Networks. #I
  • Verdict: The structural gross margin expansion story is credible and data-supported; the residual risk is whether volumes decline fast enough to create operating deleverage that offsets the mix/cost tailwinds. #J
  • Analysis: Peer context: Nokia's Networks gross margin is structurally lower (~38–42%) partly due to different product mix and partly due to less aggressive contract discipline; Ericsson's demonstrated ability to hold 50%+ Networks gross margin through a revenue downcycle is a genuine competitive quality signal. #A
  • Observation: The company's stated focus on "best networks" product leadership — maintaining #1 position in ABI Research RAN Automation rankings and Frost & Sullivan 5G Infrastructure rankings for six consecutive years — provides pricing power that underpins margin sustainability. #I
  • ⚡ RARE: Ericsson has sustained Networks gross margin above 49% for 5+ consecutive quarters through a meaningful NA capex downcycle — this is unusual for capital equipment vendors and suggests the mix-shift to software has created genuine margin stickiness. #J
  • Analysis: The restructuring-to-savings payback timeline matters: if FY2026 charges deliver run-rate savings visible from FY2027, then H2 2026 earnings progression sets up favourably for a 2027 re-rating. #A

Plausibility verdict: High — margin sustainability is already demonstrated through multiple quarters; the key assumption is that revenue volumes do not decline sufficiently to create material operating deleverage below ~SEK 45bn quarterly revenue.

§4 — Key Driver #3: Cloud Software & Services Organic Growth Inflection

  • Verdict: Cloud Software & Services is the highest-quality segment in the portfolio and its inflection to double-digit organic growth in Q4 2025 is the most strategically significant development in Ericsson's recent results. #J
  • Observation: CSS achieved 12% organic growth in Q4 2025, contributing to nine consecutive quarters of YoY adjusted EBITA margin expansion at group level through end-2025. #I
  • Observation: CSS revenue encompasses 5G Core, OSS/BSS, managed services, and cloud-native network functions — all of which benefit from operators' transition to standalone 5G architectures. #I
  • Analysis: The transition from 5G Non-Standalone to 5G Standalone is a software-rich event — it requires 5G Core upgrades, network slicing capabilities, and new OSS/BSS tooling, all of which flow through CSS rather than Networks hardware. #A
  • Observation: As of Q3 2025, Dell'Oro reported the mobile core network market was growing +14% YoY with 72 5G SA core networks deployed globally; Ericsson is a leading 5G Core vendor. #I
  • Observation: Airtel signed a multi-billion dollar 5G Core partnership with Ericsson in February 2025, specifically to drive 5G SA evolution; this contract structure (multi-year, software-heavy) is representative of the revenue quality shift occurring in CSS. #I
  • Analysis: Managed services (a subset of CSS) provide recurring revenue with multi-year contract structures; as operators rationalise their own engineering workforce, outsourcing to vendors like Ericsson of network management creates long-duration revenue streams that are different in character from hardware deployments. #A
  • Verdict: CSS moving from margin drag to margin contributor is the key P&L inflection that makes the 15–18% group EBITA target achievable; without CSS improvement, Networks alone cannot deliver the target at current volume levels. #J
  • Observation: The Vonage acquisition (completed 2022, $6.2bn) is now substantially written down; the enterprise network API/programmability elements remain and are being integrated into the CSS strategy. #I
  • Analysis: Network API monetisation — exposing carrier network capabilities (location, QoS, edge compute) via programmable APIs to enterprise developers — is a nascent but potentially significant revenue stream; early GSMA Open Gateway contracts are small but the addressable market is large. #A
  • Observation: Cloud RAN (software-defined baseband running on commodity hardware) commercial deployments with Tier-1 operators are targeted for 2025–2026; this represents potential share of the value chain shifting from hardware to software, benefiting Ericsson's CSS segment. #I
  • ❌ CSS margins are still below Networks margins at group level, and the path from the current CSS margin trajectory to CSS becoming a margin-accretive rather than margin-dilutive segment requires sustained double-digit organic growth — execution across multiple product lines simultaneously. #J
  • Analysis: Competitive dynamics in CSS are more complex than in RAN hardware; cloud-native network vendors (Mavenir, Rakuten Symphony), hyperscalers (AWS, Microsoft Azure for Operators), and Nokia all compete for portions of the CSS opportunity. #A

Plausibility verdict: Medium-high — 5G SA transition creates genuine software spend and Ericsson has demonstrably won major contracts; key assumption is that CSS organic growth sustains at 8–12% through 2026–2027 without pricing pressure from cloud-native alternatives.

§5 — Key Driver #4: FX Translation (SEK Strength vs USD/EUR)

  • Verdict: FX is the most underappreciated near-term share price driver — it directly masks or reveals the organic growth story and the SEK 7.8bn headwind in Q1 2026 means reported numbers are a systematically poor proxy for business performance. #J
  • Observation: Q1 2026 reported revenue declined 10% YoY to SEK 49.3bn despite 6% organic growth; the SEK 7.8bn currency headwind was the primary driver of the reported miss vs analyst estimates of SEK 51bn+. #I
  • Observation: Ericsson bills approximately 70–75% of revenues in non-SEK currencies (primarily USD, EUR), with the cost base more SEK-anchored (Swedish employees, Swedish-listed cost structure) — a stronger SEK is inherently margin-negative in reported terms. #I
  • Analysis: The SEK/USD rate is driven by Swedish Riksbank policy, US Fed policy divergence, and global risk sentiment; Ericsson has no control over this variable and hedging covers only near-term exposures, not structural translation effects. #A
  • Observation: The strong SEK environment in 2025–2026 is partly attributable to the Riksbank's rate cuts being shallower than market expectations and relative safe-haven dynamics in Northern Europe given geopolitical uncertainty. #I
  • Verdict: FX normalisation — not further SEK depreciation, merely stabilisation — would immediately improve reported metrics; this creates an asymmetric setup where any SEK weakening is a free earnings upgrade from current depressed reported levels. #J
  • Analysis: Analysts following Ericsson in USD (on NASDAQ) versus SEK (on Nasdaq Stockholm) have materially different reported revenue trajectories from the same underlying business — this creates persistent investor confusion and potentially suppresses US-based buying interest. #A
  • Observation: Management guidance is provided in SEK terms, and the company does not provide USD-equivalent targets; investors need to apply their own FX assumptions to translate organic guidance into reported outcome. #I
  • ❌ FX is a genuine risk factor as well as potential tailwind — if the SEK weakens from here due to Swedish economic deterioration, organic growth would be amplified in reported terms, but if SEK strengthens further, even solid organic delivery will appear weak in reported numbers. #J
  • Analysis: Historical base: Ericsson's reported revenue was approximately SEK 245bn in FY2022 and approximately SEK 193bn in FY2025 — part of this decline reflects underlying market softness and part reflects adverse FX; separating the two is critical for investment analysis. #A

Plausibility verdict: Medium — FX stabilisation/reversal materialises within 24 months given current US dollar trajectory; key assumption is that SEK/USD does not diverge further from current levels, which is a macro assumption with genuine uncertainty.

§6 — Key Driver #5: IPR/Patent Licensing Renewal Stability

  • Verdict: IPR licensing is not a growth driver but a material earnings floor — SEK 14–15bn p.a. of near-pure-margin revenue underpins the group P&L in ways that are not well understood by investors who focus solely on network equipment revenue. #J
  • Observation: FY2025 IPR licensing revenue was SEK 14.5bn, up from SEK 14.0bn in FY2024; approximately 82% is reported within the Networks segment, with the remainder in CSS. #I
  • Observation: Quarterly IPR revenues are lumpy due to retroactive settlements and new license agreements; Q2 2025 was elevated at SEK 4.9bn (benefiting from a settlement) while Q3–Q4 2025 were lower at SEK 3.1bn and SEK 3.3bn respectively. #I
  • Analysis: The 5G patent landscape is structurally favourable for Ericsson: LexisNexis analysis published January 2026 ranks Ericsson as one of the top holders of 5G standard-essential patents in a market estimated at $15bn annually; Ericsson's declared SEP portfolio positions it for licence renewals as 5G devices proliferate. #A
  • Observation: Ericsson's IPR programme has expanded following the Lenovo deal resolution, with revenues demonstrating a multi-year upward trend from approximately SEK 10–11bn p.a. in 2020–2021 to SEK 14–15bn currently. #I
  • Analysis: Key renewal risk: major smartphone manufacturers (Apple, Samsung, other Android OEMs) negotiate multi-year agreements; any lapse in renewals or unfavourable judicial decisions on FRAND rates could depress IPR revenue in a specific year, though the underlying SEP portfolio strength limits this risk. #A
  • Verdict: IPR revenue at SEK 14–15bn represents approximately 7–8% of group revenue but carries minimal cost, making it disproportionately important to EBITA; loss of this revenue floor would require approximately 3–4 percentage points of additional EBITA margin from operations to compensate. #J
  • Observation: The global 5G device market is still in early adoption phases — as 5G penetrates the installed base of approximately 5bn+ global smartphone users, the addressable per-unit royalty base grows, supporting IPR revenue growth into the late 2020s. #I
  • Analysis: Huawei is both a competitor in RAN and a significant cross-licensee in the patent domain; any geopolitical resolution affecting Huawei's market access could alter the cross-licensing dynamics, though Ericsson's net position is likely positive from Huawei exclusion scenarios. #A
  • ⚡ RARE: Ericsson's IPR revenue has grown from ~SEK 10bn (2020) to ~SEK 14.5bn (2025) at approximately 7–8% CAGR while the company was simultaneously losing revenue in its network equipment business — this decoupling demonstrates genuine patent portfolio compounding. #J

Plausibility verdict: High — IPR revenue is contractually backed and structurally growing; key assumption is absence of adverse court rulings on SEP/FRAND rates and no major licensee default.

§7 — Key Driver #6: India 5G Phase 2 and FWA Expansion

  • Verdict: India is a meaningful offset to NA weakness in the near term, but the primary pan-India 5G coverage build is largely complete — the next growth phase is FWA monetisation and 5G SA upgrade, which is smaller in magnitude than the initial build. #J
  • Observation: Jio and Airtel have substantially completed their pan-India 5G coverage rollouts as of 2024–2025, with Ericsson explicitly citing "normalisation of investments in India" as a revenue headwind in 2025. #I
  • Observation: India 5G penetration reached approximately 42% of Jio and Airtel subscribers by mid-2025, with Jio at approximately 210 million and Airtel at approximately 153 million 5G subscribers — rapid subscriber growth is driving demand for network densification. #I
  • Observation: Ericsson won a multi-billion dollar 5G equipment contract from Bharti Airtel in October 2024, and Airtel signed a multi-billion renewal with Ericsson (alongside Nokia and Samsung) totalling $1.2bn for 4G/5G equipment in December 2024 — these are multi-year contracts. #I
  • Analysis: The FWA opportunity in India is material: Jio became the world's largest FWA provider with 9.5 million connections as of Q3 2025, with combined Jio + Airtel FWA reaching 12 million by September 2025; continued FWA growth requires capacity additions to the RAN. #A
  • Observation: Ericsson Mobility Report (November 2025) forecasts robust demand for FWA in India; the intersection of low wireline penetration and high 5G coverage creates strong structural demand for wireless home broadband. #I
  • Analysis: The 5G SA (Standalone) transition in India — Airtel has explicitly partnered with Ericsson for 5G Core to enable SA — creates a software upgrade cycle that flows through CSS rather than Networks hardware; this shifts the India revenue mix toward higher-margin software spend over time. #A
  • Verdict: India in 2026–2027 is a steady-state rather than step-change contributor; the initial build impulse is over, but recurring maintenance, densification, FWA capacity adds, and the SA upgrade together provide durable mid-single-digit growth in the India segment. #J
  • Observation: SE Asia, Oceania, and India collectively grew sales in Q1 2026, providing meaningful geographic diversification away from the NA weakness; this regional balance reduces concentration risk though absolute revenue from these regions is smaller than NA. #I
  • ❌ The India contribution risk is downside from here if Jio and Airtel enter a period of network spending consolidation post-rollout; Indian operator capex cycles tend to be lumpy and a digestion period of 12–18 months is plausible before the next upgrade cycle. #J
  • Analysis: Enterprise private networks in India — ports, manufacturing, mining — represent an emerging opportunity aligned with India's industrial development trajectory; Ericsson's private network portfolio and Cradlepoint enterprise capabilities position it for early market participation. #A

Plausibility verdict: Medium — India contributes positive growth but not step-change revenue; key assumption is that Jio/Airtel densification and FWA spending absorbs the post-rollout normalisation within 12–18 months.

§8 — Key Driver #7: AI-Native RAN Leadership and Defence/Mission-Critical Optionality

  • Verdict: AI-native networks and defence/mission-critical are genuine long-term growth vectors, but neither is a 24-month earnings driver; they are embedded optionality that justifies a higher terminal multiple without driving near-term free cash flow. #J
  • Observation: Ericsson was ranked #1 in ABI Research's 5G RAN Automation Platform competitive assessment (February 2026), ahead of seven competitors including Nokia, Samsung, and Mavenir; CSPs including AT&T, Swisscom, Telstra, and Vodafone have chosen Ericsson's Intelligent Automation Platform. #I
  • Observation: Ericsson CEO Börje Ekholm explicitly targeted defence and AI-fuelled growth as strategic priorities at start-2026, with plans to increase R&D investment in defence during 2026 specifically. #I
  • Analysis: The defence/mission-critical market is growing at approximately 10.8% CAGR (2025–2026) toward a $34bn total addressable market by 2030 per research estimates; Ericsson's 3GPP-based mission-critical portfolio (public safety, government, defence) competes with legacy TETRA providers and Motorola Solutions. #A
  • Observation: AI inference in the RAN — embedding AI models directly in base stations for interference management, beam prediction, and energy optimisation — is being commercialised in 2026; Ericsson's April 2026 blog series on "embedding AI in RAN" signals active deployment rather than research-stage work. #I
  • Analysis: Autonomous network management (Level 4/5 autonomy) could reduce operator total cost of ownership by 20–30% vs manually managed networks; operators paying for this capability are willing to share savings through higher software licence fees — this is the monetisation logic underpinning AI-native premium pricing. #A
  • Observation: Ericsson and Intel announced collaboration to accelerate commercial 6G network paths (2026 announcement); 6G is a 2030+ revenue event, but early standard participation secures patent positioning that protects future IPR revenue. #I
  • Verdict: AI-native RAN leadership translates to share price through two mechanisms: (1) near-term premium pricing defence vs Nokia/Samsung in RAN tenders; (2) longer-term new addressable market in network software and managed automation services. #J
