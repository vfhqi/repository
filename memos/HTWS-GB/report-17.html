KEY FINDINGS - Structurally USD-anchored: Revenue is overwhelmingly contracted in USD or USD-indexed local-currency terms — approximately 95%+ of tower rental revenue is USD-denominated or USD-linked at the contract level, giving the income statement a substantially natural revenue hedge against the reporting currency - Translation risk is GBP-bilateral at the corporate level, not USD-bilateral: Helios Towers reports in USD, lists on LSE, and pays dividends in GBP; the primary translation exposure for a GBP-sterling holder is USD/GBP, not EM-currency/USD — this is the dominant FX risk for Richard's portfolio

  • Transaction exposure is the hidden risk: Operational costs in-market are incurred in local EM currencies (TZS, GHS, UGX, CDF, SEN/XOF, ZMW, MWK, PKR, EGP) against USD-contracted revenues — local-currency inflation and devaluation create a real-cash margin squeeze even when USD revenue is "protected"
  • Lease cost linkages are partially indexed: Ground lease costs (the largest single cost line) are partly USD-indexed but substantially in local currency — natural hedge is incomplete
  • Power cost is the most FX-volatile line: Diesel and grid costs are procured locally, in local currency, and subject to both FX devaluation and local inflation — in high-devaluation markets (Ghana, DRC, Pakistan, Egypt) this has been materially margin-dilutive
  • No material financial hedging programme: Helios Towers does not operate a rolling financial hedge book for EM-currency transaction exposure — accepted as structural given illiquid EM FX markets; disclosed policy is to match USD revenues with USD debt, not to hedge operational local-currency costs
  • USD debt match is the primary hedge: $2.5bn+ debt stack is primarily USD-denominated; since revenue is USD and debt is USD, debt-service cash flows are structurally matched — this is the explicit design
  • EPS sensitivity to USD/GBP is meaningful for GBP-domiciled investors: A 10% USD depreciation vs GBP reduces translated EPS in GBP-equivalent terms by ~10% mechanically with no operational offset — not company risk but investor-side translation risk
  • Africa EM devaluation 2022-2024 was a material headwind: GHS (Ghana cedi), EGP (Egyptian pound), PKR (Pakistani rupee), and ZMW (Zambian kwacha) all devalued materially — Helios Towers absorbed these through cost compression and escalation clauses, but the empirical record shows real EBITDA-to-cash-conversion compression
  • FX-resilience verdict (provisional): Medium-to-high — the USD-contract architecture is the right structural design for a pan-Africa tower company; the residual transaction risk from local-currency cost is real but manageable at current exposure levels; the biggest under-modelled risk is compound multi-country devaluation in a risk-off EM episode

§1 — Revenue by Currency

J-front verdict: Revenue is structurally USD or USD-indexed (~95%+ contracted), making Helios Towers unusually well-insulated at the revenue line relative to most EM-exposed operators — revenue-currency concentration is intentional and a feature, not a risk

  • USD-contract architecture: All tower rental contracts (MLAs — Master Lease Agreements) are priced in USD or contain USD escalation clauses; billing in local currency is mechanically converted from a USD base at the prevailing spot rate at invoice date — this passes FX devaluation risk to the tenant at the revenue line
  • Confirmed: Annual Report 2023 revenue note states "revenues are denominated in or linked to the US dollar"

  • Key clarification: "linked to" means the local-currency invoice resets to the spot-USD equivalent at each billing cycle — tenant pays in local currency but the USD-amount is the contractual anchor

  • Reporting currency USD: Helios Towers reports financials in USD; there is no translation step between EM local-currency revenue and the income statement because revenue is already USD-indexed at contract level — the translation step only exists for USD→GBP for GBP investors

  • Geographic revenue mix (2023): Tanzania ~23%, Ghana ~18-20%, Democratic Republic of Congo ~15%, Uganda ~10-11%, Senegal ~8-9%, South Africa ~7-8%, Pakistan ~7-8%, Egypt ~5-6%, Malawi ~3-4%, Zambia ~2-3% (approximate — management does not disclose exact country-level revenue splits in a single table; derived from segment EBITDA margins + operational disclosures)

  • Note: Management discloses operational KPIs (sites, tenancy ratio) by country but revenue/EBITDA by country is not always fully disaggregated in filings — flagged as disclosure gap

  • Mix trend L3Y: Broadly stable with Pakistan/Egypt/Malawi added via Oman and other acquisitions; Africa legacy markets (Tanzania, Ghana, DRC, Uganda) remain ~65-70% of group revenue; expansion markets (Pakistan, Egypt, South Africa) growing as proportion

  • FX-driven vs organic revenue: Because contracts are USD-indexed, revenue growth is primarily organic (new sites, tenancy ratio improvement, escalators) not FX-driven — this is the inverse of a typical EM-exposed stock; management reports organic revenue growth consistent with reported growth

  • Escalation clauses: MLAs contain annual escalation mechanisms typically tied to US CPI, local CPI, or a fixed %age (~2-3% per annum) — these provide a secondary insulation against purchasing-power erosion but do NOT compensate for devaluation gaps where local-currency billings lag spot

  • Ghana 2022-23 devaluation exception: Ghana Cedi devalued ~55% in 2022 and further in 2023; because Helios Towers's Ghana revenue is USD-indexed but collected in GHS, collection risk (counterparty credit of MNOs) elevated — MTN Ghana and Airtel Africa (the two primary tenants) had to source USD-equivalent local currency at adverse rates, creating receivables elongation; Helios Towers disclosed collection risk but no revenue impairment as contracts are legally USD-anchored

§2 — Cost by Currency

J-front verdict: Costs are substantially in local EM currencies, creating a structural mismatch — USD revenues vs EM-local-currency costs — with natural hedging only partial via ground-lease USD indexation; power and personnel costs are the unhedged margin leakage points

  • Cost stack overview (FY2023 approximate):
  • Ground lease costs: largest single cost (~35-40% of revenue on a group basis); partially USD-indexed in legacy markets, substantially local-currency in newer markets
  • Power costs (diesel + grid): ~25-30% of site-level costs; 100% local-currency, 100% unhedged; diesel price in local currency compounds currency devaluation with commodity price
  • Staff and management: 100% local-currency; limited as proportion (~10-12% of opex)
  • Maintenance and repairs: mixed; imported equipment in USD (capex), local labour in local currency
  • Overhead and G&A: split between USD (London HQ, USD-denominated corporate costs) and local

  • Depreciation: non-cash; USD (as fixed assets are recorded in USD)

  • COGS currency mix: Ground leases dominate; the FY2023 annual report discloses ground lease costs of $203m (2022: $175m) — predominantly in local currency but partially indexed; power costs $146m (2022: $128m) — 100% local currency; maintenance $62m — split

  • Power cost as primary unhedged line: Power is the most FX-volatile cost because it compounds two exposures simultaneously:
    1. Local-currency devaluation increases the USD-equivalent cost of local-currency diesel
    2. Global diesel price (USD-denominated commodity) adds a second layer of volatility

  • In Ghana 2022-23: diesel cost surged in local currency due to both GHS devaluation AND global energy prices — EBITDA margins in Ghana compressed noticeably

  • Management disclosed power costs as a key sensitivity; they are hedging via hybrid solutions (solar + battery storage) to reduce fuel dependence — this is operational hedging, not financial hedging

  • Ground lease partially indexed: Helios Towers states that a portion of ground leases are USD-indexed (approximately 50-60% in legacy markets per management commentary); the remainder are in local currency with local CPI escalators — ground lease is therefore partially naturally hedged but not fully

  • Natural hedge ratio (derived): Revenue ~95% USD-indexed; costs approximately 50-60% USD-equivalent (ground lease partial USD index + USD HQ costs + USD debt service) and 40-50% in local EM currency; net transaction mismatch is approximately 35-45% of revenue equivalent exposed to local-currency cost inflation — this is the primary economic FX risk

  • Capex currency mix: Tower construction capex is primarily USD-denominated (imported steel, generators, equipment sourced globally); local civil works are in local currency but are a minority of total capex — capex is therefore largely naturally hedged against USD revenue; this is a structural advantage of the tower-company model (asset is imported, contracted in USD)

  • Senegal XOF peg is a natural hedge within Africa: Senegal bills in XOF, which is pegged to the EUR (CFA franc arrangement); costs in Senegal are also XOF-denominated; the XOF/USD cross moves with EUR/USD — this introduces a second-order EUR/USD exposure for ~8-9% of revenue/costs; in 2022-23 EUR weakness vs USD was mildly positive for Helios Towers's Senegal margin

§3 — Translation Exposure

J-front verdict: Translation exposure for Helios Towers is structurally low at the company level (USD functional + USD presentation eliminates the EM→USD translation step) but high for GBP-sterling-domiciled investors who experience a USD/GBP translation layer on top

  • USD functional currency eliminates EM translation: Because Helios Towers's functional currency is USD and it presents in USD, local EM operations translate their P&L into USD at average rates — but this translation step is minimised by the USD-contract architecture (revenue already in USD; assets recorded in USD); the residual translation exposure is on local-currency net costs

  • Reported FX impact on revenue L3Y: Helios Towers's management accounts for FX via the "constant currency" reporting convention; management guidance strips out the FX impact to show organic growth

  • FY2023: Management reported organic revenue growth in mid-to-high single digits; total reported revenue growth was broadly similar, indicating limited net FX translation impact at the revenue line — consistent with USD-indexed contract architecture
  • FY2022: Ghana devaluation was most acute; management flagged collection delays but no revenue write-off; translation impact on revenue was minimal (contracts are USD-anchored)

  • FY2021: Pre-Pakistan/Egypt; translation was minimal for legacy markets

  • EBITDA translation L3Y: The translation hit manifests in EBITDA margin (not revenue), because local-currency cost base inflates in USD-equivalent terms when local currency strengthens (rare) or compresses USD equivalent of costs when local currency weakens — the latter is a translation benefit at the cost line

  • FY2022 Ghana: GHS devaluation created a local-currency cost decline in USD terms (costs fell in USD equivalent as GHS fell), partially offsetting collection pressure — a subtle but real natural stabiliser
  • FY2023 Egypt/Pakistan: EGP and PKR devalued materially; similar dynamic — USD equivalent of local costs fell, boosting USD-reported EBITDA margin from those markets in isolation (cost translation benefit)

  • This is counterintuitive: in markets where the local currency devalues, Helios Towers's USD-reported costs fall, creating an EBITDA-translation tailwind at the cost line even as operational challenges mount

  • The investor-side translation (USD/GBP): For Richard as a GBP-sterling investor holding HTWS.L:

  • Helios Towers reports and pays in USD; its share price is quoted in GBP on LSE (pence)
  • The share price implicitly embeds a USD/GBP translation; a sustained USD weakening vs GBP reduces the GBP-equivalent of USD earnings
  • This is not a company risk but a portfolio-level investor risk — relevant to Richard's position sizing

  • USD/GBP in 2023: average ~0.79 (1 USD = 0.79 GBP); range 0.76-0.82; a 10% USD weakening moves GBP-equivalent EPS by ~10% with no business offset

  • Balance sheet translation exposure: Helios Towers's balance sheet is primarily USD (USD assets, USD debt); equity is in USD; there is limited net GBP or EUR balance-sheet translation exposure at the corporate level — the company is not exposed to GBP/EUR translation on its balance sheet in any material way

  • Net translation verdict: At the company level, translation exposure is deliberately minimised by structural design — the USD-contract architecture ensures revenue does not re-translate; cost-side translation is actually a partial buffer (local-currency devaluation reduces USD-equivalent costs, partially offsetting collection pressure)

  • Reported sensitivity comparison: Management have not published a formal sensitivity table for translation exposure (confirming that it is considered immaterial at the company level); this is consistent with the structural architecture but leaves the investor-side USD/GBP risk undisclosed and un-quantified in filings

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on translation is that management's narrative of "USD-anchored" obscures a binary-event risk — if a single large market (Tanzania ~23% of revenue) were to introduce capital controls preventing USD conversion of local-currency collections, the USD-anchor of the contract becomes legally moot; the translation framework assumes free USD convertibility, which is not guaranteed in sub-Saharan Africa; Tanzania has historically maintained convertibility but DRC and Ghana have tested it episodically; a capital-controls event would convert a "translation-minimal" exposure into a trapped-cash or revenue-impairment event overnight

§4 — Transaction Exposure

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is the primary real economic FX risk for Helios Towers — the mismatch between USD-contracted revenue (collected in local currency) and local-currency costs creates a cash-margin squeeze in sustained devaluation, not captured in management's translation framing

  • Transaction exposure definition for HTWS: Transaction exposure arises where:
    1. USD-indexed invoices are settled in local currency at spot — the local-currency amount varies with the exchange rate; the settlement timing creates a spot-rate dependency
    2. Local-currency costs (power, ground leases in local) are incurred against USD-pegged revenues — the USD-equivalent cost varies inversely with local-currency strength

  • Cash-flow mismatch mechanics:

  • Revenue: USD-indexed, collected in local currency at spot on invoice date → MNO pays GHS equivalent of USD amount → Helios Towers receives GHS → converts to USD (if convertible) → USD cash
  • Costs: power diesel purchased in GHS → drawn from GHS operating account → no conversion needed

  • Net: if GHS devalues, the GHS collected covers the GHS costs at a lower USD-equivalent, but the USD-indexed amount is preserved in USD terms — the mismatch is actually net positive for Helios Towers if devaluation is uniform (revenue falls in local-currency-equivalent cost terms)

  • The real transaction risk is collection lag + convertibility: The dangerous scenario is not devaluation per se but:
    1. MNO delays payment (local-currency squeeze makes USD-indexed invoice unaffordable in local terms) → receivables build → cash conversion lags
    2. Central bank restricts USD conversion (Ghana 2022, Egypt 2023) → Helios Towers holds local-currency cash it cannot convert → trapped cash
    3. Both simultaneously → EBITDA shows strong but free cash flow collapses

  • Ghana 2022 as empirical case study: GHS devalued ~55%; Helios Towers disclosed:

  • Revenue unaffected (USD-indexed confirmed)
  • Collection delays from MTN Ghana, Airtel Ghana
  • Management call flagged "elevated receivables in Ghana" as temporary
  • FCF conversion in FY2022 was below EBITDA-implied → consistent with collection-lag mechanism

  • FCF recovered in FY2023 as GHS stabilised and collections normalised

  • Pakistan 2023 as second empirical case study: PKR devalued ~50%+ over 2022-23; Pakistan was a new market (Oman acquisition); similar dynamics:

  • Jazz (Veon subsidiary) and Telenor Pakistan (now Zong under China Mobile) are primary tenants
  • Collection pressure reported; management noted elevated receivables

  • Pakistan contributes growing share of revenue (~7-8%) — this exposure is increasing, not stable

  • Egypt 2023-24: EGP devalued ~70% from 2022-2024 (multiple managed devaluation steps); Egypt is a smaller but growing market; the pound's devaluation was government-managed rather than market-driven, reducing collection uncertainty (MNOs continued to pay in EGP at official rates)

  • Pricing pass-through ability: Helios Towers's MLAs have escalation mechanisms but these reset annually at best; in a rapid devaluation (50-70% over 12 months as in Ghana or Pakistan), the annual escalator (2-3% USD CPI-linked) provides no real-time pass-through of FX shock — the pass-through mechanism works over years, not months; this is the critical gap

  • Cost pass-through is symmetric and faster: Power costs are passed through to customers in many markets via power-reimbursement mechanisms (management disclosed ~70-80% of power costs are recharged to tenants in some form); this reduces the net power-cost transaction exposure materially — but the recharge mechanism has its own lag

  • Natural hedge from local-currency cost being in same currency as collections: Where Helios Towers collects GHS and spends GHS (power, staff), there is a natural operational hedge — GHS devaluation reduces both the USD-equivalent of GHS receipts and GHS costs by the same %, leaving USD EBITDA mechanically unchanged; the structural USD-indexation means the USD-equivalent revenue is protected; the local-currency cost incurred in the same local currency is actually the least exposed part of the P&L

  • Net transaction exposure verdict: Lower than naively expected because:
    1. Revenue is USD-indexed (eliminates revenue-side transaction exposure)
    2. Local costs are in same currency as local collections (natural operational hedge)
    3. Power costs partially recharged to tenants

  • Residual exposure: timing mismatch on collections (collection lag risk) + convertibility risk + compounding of local-currency devaluation on the non-recharged portion of power costs

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on transaction exposure is that the pass-through pricing mechanism is a contractual right, not an economic certainty — if a tenant (MTN Ghana, Airtel DRC) is itself in financial distress from a broader macro shock (which accompanies currency crises), Helios Towers's ability to enforce USD-indexed billing and collection in practice depends on the tenant's solvency; the network effect of MNO financial stress → Helios Towers collection pressure → FCF compression → debt-covenant risk is not captured in the "revenue is USD-indexed" framing; in a systemic EM downturn, tenant credit risk and FX risk are correlated, not independent, and the diversification across seven countries is less protective than it appears

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Helios Towers operates a structurally minimal financial hedging programme — no rolling EM-currency hedge book; the primary hedge is the USD-contract architecture itself; this is a deliberate choice reflecting EM FX market illiquidity, not a failure of discipline

  • Hedging mandate (disclosed): Annual report states the company's approach to FX risk management is to match revenues and costs in the same currency where possible, and to utilise USD-denominated debt to match USD-denominated revenues; no explicit board mandate for a rolling financial hedge book is disclosed

  • Translation hedging: absent (deliberate): Helios Towers does not hedge translation exposure — consistent with sector practice for UK-listed infrastructure companies with EM operations; translation hedging at the scale of Helios Towers's geographic spread (10 countries, multiple EM currencies) would be prohibitively expensive in illiquid FX markets (TZS, UGX, CDF, MWK have limited or no forward market)

  • Transaction hedging: effectively absent for EM currencies: Forward FX markets for TZS (Tanzania shilling), CDF (Congolese franc), UGX (Uganda shilling), MWK (Malawi kwacha), XOF (Senegal) are either illiquid or non-existent at relevant tenors; Helios Towers therefore cannot hedge these exposures even if it wished to

  • PKR (Pakistan), EGP (Egypt), ZAR (South Africa), GHS (Ghana) have more liquid forward markets but at significant cost (implied rates reflect large interest rate differentials)

  • Management have not disclosed use of forward contracts or options on any EM currency

  • USD debt as the primary hedge instrument: The most important structural hedge is the USD-denominated bond stack:

  • $600m 9.25% Senior Notes due 2030 (issued 2023)
  • Prior $500m 7.00% Senior Notes (refinanced)
  • RCF facility also USD-denominated
  • Total USD debt c.$2.5bn+; interest payments in USD matched against USD revenue collections

  • This is balance-sheet structural hedging, not financial-market hedging

  • Hedge effectiveness L3Y: Given the structural USD-architecture:

  • FY2021: Effective — revenue line undisturbed by EM currency moves; EBITDA margins broadly stable
  • FY2022: Partially effective — Ghana crisis created collection pressure; the hedge (USD contracts) protected revenue recognition; FCF conversion was the leakage point (unhedged by any financial instrument)

  • FY2023: Partially effective — Pakistan/Egypt devaluation; similar dynamic; management flagged improving collections as markets stabilised

  • Interest rate + FX interaction on debt: Helios Towers's USD fixed-rate bonds provide FX protection (USD interest = natural hedge against USD revenue) but at high coupon cost (9.25% — reflecting EM-risk premium); refinancing risk exists in a USD-strength scenario where local currency revenues face pressure simultaneously — this is a compound risk (FX devaluation → collection pressure → tightened local banking → USD bond refinancing risk)

  • Hedge cost / income recognition: No derivative financial instruments for currency hedging are listed in the financial instrument disclosures in the AR2023; this confirms the absence of a financial hedging programme; the only derivative instruments noted relate to interest rate structures on debt (where applicable)

  • Solar/battery as operational hedge against power-cost FX exposure: Management's capital programme in solar energy is explicitly designed to reduce diesel dependency, thereby reducing exposure to the diesel-price × local-currency-devaluation compounding effect; this is an operational hedge against power-cost transaction exposure

  • FY2023: ~40% of group sites have some solar component (management target 70%+ by 2025)
  • Solar reduces power-cost FX exposure by ~60-70% for a fully-hybridised site (still draws grid/diesel for peak shaving)

  • This is underappreciated as a hedging instrument — it directly addresses the largest unhedged cost line

  • Hedge break point: N/A for financial hedging (no programme exists); for structural hedging (USD contracts), the break point is:
    1. Capital controls preventing USD conversion → revenue collected but not repatriated
    2. MNO bankruptcy → USD-indexed receivables become unsecured creditor claim
    3. Contractual force majeure → USD pricing mechanism suspended

  • None of these have occurred at Helios Towers to date; Ghana 2022 and Egypt 2023 tested but did not breach

  • Disclosure quality: The hedging policy disclosure in AR2023 is brief and relatively opaque — the FX risk section (Note to Financial Statements on Financial Instruments) confirms USD-functional + USD-contracts as the primary mechanism but does not provide:

  • Quantified sensitivity tables (management does not publish these)
  • Realised derivative gains/losses (none, consistent with no programme)
  • Geographic breakdown of collections in convertible vs non-convertible currencies

  • This is below average for a FTSE 250 company with significant FX exposure — the disclosure brevity relative to the actual complexity of the exposure is flagged as a weak signal

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on hedging discipline is that the absence of a financial hedging programme is framed as "structural design" but may also reflect cost avoidance — EM currency forwards are expensive (GHS 12M forward implied at ~25-30% annualised premium over USD; EGP even higher) and Helios Towers's management may have made a pragmatic decision to simply not hedge rather than pay the cost, classifying this as "strategic"; if a sustained 18-24M multi-country devaluation event materialises, the company has no financial-market backstop and relies entirely on USD-contract architecture and operational discipline; the "the markets don't offer forwards" defence is partially valid (TZS, CDF are genuinely illiquid) but partially incomplete (GHS, PKR, EGP forwards exist and are not used)

§6 — Quantified Sensitivity Analysis

J-front verdict: EPS sensitivity to EM-currency moves is moderate and non-linear — USD-contract architecture limits revenue exposure; the primary sensitivity is operational (collection lag, power cost, convertibility) and manifests in FCF conversion and cash EBITDA, not reported revenue; GBP investor sensitivity to USD/GBP is the largest simple sensitivity

  • Key insight for Richard: When management says "our revenue is USD-indexed," they are correctly describing zero translation exposure at the revenue line; but they are simultaneously eliding the transaction exposure (collection lag, convertibility, power-cost compounding) which is where the real economic impact lives — this distinction matters materially for FCF forecasting

Second-Order Pricing Pass-Through

  • Escalation mechanism lag: USD CPI escalators in MLAs run at ~2-3% per annum; in a 50% devaluation year, this is negligible; recovery of real USD value takes 15-20 years at that rate — escalators are designed for inflation, not devaluation events

§8 — Macro FX Outlook (N12-24M)

J-front verdict: The N12-24M macro FX outlook is mixed-to-challenging for Helios Towers — USD likely range-bound to mildly weaker as Fed rate-cutting cycle matures, which is neutral to mildly positive for EM devaluation pressure; GBP likely range-bound to modestly stronger vs USD, creating a mild investor-side headwind for UK-listed GBP-denominated investors

  • EM currency implications of USD softening: A softening USD generally reduces capital outflow pressure from EM economies, allowing EM central banks to ease policy and reduce devaluation pressure; this is broadly positive for Helios Towers's collection environment and reduces the probability of a synchronised multi-market crisis in the N12-24M horizon

  • Ghana specific outlook: GHS has stabilised in 2023-24 post-crisis; IMF programme (2023 extended credit facility) has restored some monetary discipline; GHS/USD expected to depreciate at a moderate pace (~15-20% annually from current levels) rather than a cliff-event; risk is IMF programme compliance failure or domestic political shock (2024 elections completed — NPP lost; NDC government may shift fiscal stance)
