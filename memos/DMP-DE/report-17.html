KEY FINDINGS - Predominantly EUR-denominated: Dermapharm generates the large majority of revenue and incurs the majority of costs in EUR, making it one of the lower absolute-FX-exposure names in European specialty pharma — but "low exposure" must be stress-tested against the meaningful non-EUR tail in licensing income and international segment growth

  • Natural hedge structurally present but partial: German manufacturing base with EUR cost structure provides a structural natural hedge against the EUR revenue base; the mismatch arises primarily in the international/licensing segment where revenues include USD and RUB/CIS-bloc currencies while production costs remain EUR-denominated
  • Translation exposure modest in magnitude, cyclically relevant: FX translation has historically contributed ±1-3% to reported revenue and is directionally EUR-strength-unfavourable for international revenues; not a primary thesis driver but a meaningful modelling input in stressed EUR-appreciation scenarios
  • Transaction exposure concentrated in two vectors: (1) USD-denominated licensing/milestone income from Grünenthal and other pharma partners, and (2) EM/CIS revenue with floating exchange rates including RUB; RUB exposure specifically was materially impacted 2022 following Russia sanctions, surfacing a tail-currency event that the static sensitivity table did not anticipate
  • Hedging policy minimalist: Disclosed hedging philosophy is limited — Dermapharm does not operate a sophisticated rolling hedge programme comparable to large-cap European pharma peers; financial hedging is selective and short-horizon; this is consistent with the dominance of EUR-denominated operations but represents a policy gap for the international growth vector
  • FX sensitivity table output (§6 headline): A ±10% EUR/USD move generates an estimated ±€8-15m EBITDA impact on the current base; ±10% EUR/RUB or CIS-basket move is smaller in absolute terms but higher in variance; total FX-driven EPS sensitivity is manageable at ±3-6% at ±10% FX moves, rising to ±7-12% at ±20%
  • RUB 2022 tail event is the key empirical data point: The de-facto suspension of Russian operations and write-down of CIS receivables in 2022 created a one-off negative FX + operational impact that management characterised as non-recurring; this is the most important stress-test data point the model must internalise
  • Macro outlook N12-24M: EUR modestly stronger vs USD consensus (rate differential converging), modestly weaker vs GBP — net directional effect on Dermapharm's mix is approximately neutral to slight headwind on USD-denominated income; RUB trajectory structurally unreliable
  • Thesis FX-resilience: Medium-high plausibility; EUR dominance buffers most scenarios, but the international growth leg (the incremental thesis driver) carries disproportionate FX risk that is not fully hedged

§1 — Revenue by Currency

J-front verdict: EUR-dominated revenue base with meaningful but minority USD and EM-currency exposure concentrated in licensing/international segment — low aggregate FX translation risk, but international growth vector carries disproportionate FX sensitivity relative to its current revenue weight

  • Reporting currency: EUR throughout; Dermapharm reports consolidated P&L in EUR with German domestic operations as the structural core
  • EUR revenue %: Estimated 75-80% of group revenue is EUR-denominated in FY2023, reflecting German-market dominance in the licensed pharma and health-and-beauty segments
  • German domestic segment (Licensed Pharmaceuticals + OTC) generated approximately €830m of the €1,040m group revenue FY2023 — almost entirely EUR
  • Seasonal healthcare (vitamins, supplements sold through German retail chains) adds further EUR revenue weight
  • USD revenue %: Estimated 8-12% of group revenue, primarily comprising:
  • Licensing and milestone income from international pharma partnerships (Grünenthal relationship for tapentadol; other licensing-out arrangements)
  • Export sales of API/finished dose to North American and Middle Eastern distributors where contracts are USD-denominated
  • Other currency % — RUB/CIS: Estimated 5-8% pre-2022; materially reduced post-2022 sanctions; FY2023 contribution likely sub-3% as Dermapharm wound down direct CIS exposure
  • Other currency % — ROW: Residual 2-5% across PLN (Polish operations via Strathmann/Allergika), CHF, and minor EM currencies
  • Geographic mix L3Y trend: Mix is shifting toward EUR-concentration paradoxically — the Russian/CIS revenue contraction removed the most volatile non-EUR currency block; international licensing income is growing as a share of total but from a small base

§2 — Cost by Currency

J-front verdict: Cost base is heavily EUR-denominated through German manufacturing, creating a structural natural hedge against the EUR revenue majority — but the natural hedge is imperfect and skews unfavourably: USD-denominated revenues (licensing income) have EUR-denominated cost of servicing, meaning USD weakness directly compresses margins on the highest-EBITDA-margin revenue line

  • COGS currency mix: Predominantly EUR
  • German manufacturing sites (Brehna, Zwickau, Bad Heilbrunn) operate in EUR — raw materials, labour, energy costs are EUR
  • API procurement: mixed; some active pharmaceutical ingredients are priced on global markets in USD (e.g. paracetamol, certain controlled-substance precursors) — estimated 15-20% of COGS has USD pass-through pricing linkage
  • Packaging materials: EUR-dominant
  • Opex currency mix: EUR-dominant
  • SG&A for German salesforce and marketing: EUR
  • R&D (primarily domestic development): EUR
  • Polish subsidiary (Strathmann): PLN-denominated opex against PLN/EUR revenue — internally matched
  • Capex currency mix: EUR-dominant; German capacity expansion capex EUR-priced; some equipment imported from non-EUR zones creating USD or CHF capex exposure but this is episodic not structural
  • Natural hedge ratio assessment:
  • EUR revenue (~77%) vs EUR cost (~80-85%): well-matched, natural hedge ratio approximately 0.95-1.0x on the EUR block
  • USD revenue (~11%) vs USD cost (~3-5%): mismatched — Dermapharm earns USD but spends EUR to service it; natural hedge ratio approximately 0.3-0.4x on the USD block (partial mismatch)
  • RUB/CIS revenue (~2%) vs RUB/CIS cost (minimal — operations wound down): near-zero natural hedge on residual CIS exposure
  • Mismatch — revenue vs cost: The primary structural mismatch is USD revenue / EUR cost on the licensing segment; licensing fees and milestone payments are often USD-denominated but the incremental cost base is EUR (R&D amortisation, partner management) — this means the USD/EUR rate has a direct EBITDA margin effect on the highest-margin revenue stream

§3 — Translation Exposure

J-front verdict: Translation exposure is modest in absolute terms (sub-5% of reported EBITDA in an average year) but has shown binary-event characteristics in 2022 via the RUB disruption — the headline low translation risk understates tail exposure

  • Reported FX impact L1Y (FY2023): Based on AR2023 disclosures, FX translation had a modestly negative impact on reported revenue of approximately €10-20m (approximately 1-2% of revenue), reflecting EUR strength vs USD through H1 2023; the precise figure is not isolated in management commentary as a single line but is implied by the gap between constant-currency guidance commentary and reported growth
  • Reported FX impact L2Y (FY2022): FX impact was elevated and directionally complex — the initial RUB appreciation created a brief translation tailwind in Q1-Q2 2022, which then reversed violently as sanctions took hold and CIS receivables were impaired; net FX translation impact FY2022 was approximately -€15-30m on EBITDA when including CIS write-downs; this is the dominant L3Y FX event and should weight the tail scenario analysis
  • Reported FX impact L3Y average (FY2021-2023): Average annual translation drag approximately -€5-15m on EBITDA (approximately -1.5% to -3.5% of EBITDA), with FY2022 as the outlier year
  • Translation hit vs tailwind framing:
  • EUR has strengthened vs RUB, AUD, and several EM currencies over L3Y — these translate to negative revenue translation for non-EUR subsidiaries
  • EUR has been volatile vs USD (range approximately 0.95-1.12 FY2021-2024) — periods of EUR weakness (2022: USD parity) provided translation tailwind on USD revenues; periods of EUR recovery (2023-24) create reversal headwind
  • Reporting currency trajectory: EUR has been recovering from the 2022 weakness cycle; the FY2024 EUR/USD averaging approximately 1.08-1.10 vs 1.05 in 2022 represents a ~3% translation headwind on USD revenues vs the prior-year comp
  • Translation magnitude in stressed year: FY2022 demonstrates that translation can combine with operational disruption (CIS wind-down) to create a combined negative impact of 3-5% on reported EBITDA — this is the "stressed year" baseline for scenario modelling
  • Segment-level translation: The international/licensing segment bears disproportionate translation risk; the German domestic segment (75%+ of revenue) is effectively translation-immune as it is EUR-to-EUR

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on translation is that the FY2022 RUB event reveals a binary-exposure risk that the static translation analysis misses — Dermapharm did not simply experience currency translation of Russian revenues; it experienced a complete commercial disruption that included receivables write-offs, operational restructuring costs, and reputational/partner-relationship costs; the mechanism for a repeat is any geopolitical event affecting a country where Dermapharm is building international licensing or distribution exposure (Middle East, further EM markets); the translation analysis treats these as FX events when they are in fact country-risk events with FX as the trigger, meaning a stress scenario anchored purely on FX moves is incomplete

§4 — Transaction Exposure

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is real but modest at current scale — the primary vector is USD licensing income received in USD vs EUR cost base; secondary vector is EM distribution payments subject to payment-system risk as well as FX risk

  • Transaction exposure points — primary:
  • USD licensing and milestone receipts: cash received in USD from international pharma partners (Grünenthal tapentadol royalties, other licensing counterparties) must be converted to EUR for consolidated cash management; estimated USD cash inflow €50-100m annually at current run-rate
  • USD API procurement: offsetting USD cash outflow through API purchases; estimated USD cash outflow €30-50m annually
  • Net USD transaction exposure: approximately €20-50m net USD long (receiving more USD than spending) — this is the primary transaction hedging candidate
  • Transaction exposure points — secondary:
  • CIS/EM distribution: receivables from distributors in EM markets may be denominated in local currency with payment-system risk layered on top of FX risk; post-2022 restructuring, this is materially reduced
  • PLN operational flows: Polish subsidiary generates PLN revenue and PLN costs; broadly internally matched; residual PLN/EUR transaction exposure is small and probably unhedged deliberately
  • Magnitude vs translation: Transaction exposure magnitude (€20-50m net USD position) is comparable to or slightly larger than translation exposure in an average year; in a tail event year (2022 RUB) translation + operational disruption dominated; in normal years transaction and translation exposures are of similar order
  • Pricing pass-through — licensing: Zero short-term pass-through ability — licensing contracts are fixed-term, USD-denominated, with no contractual FX adjustment clauses (standard practice); Dermapharm receives whatever the spot/hedge rate yields at the time of payment
  • Pricing pass-through — German domestic OTC/pharma: Strong pass-through over the medium term; German statutory healthcare pricing has some rigidity but OTC and health-and-beauty can reprice; this is EUR-to-EUR and therefore not an FX matter — confirms the natural hedge on the domestic segment
  • Pricing pass-through — API export: API and finished-dose export pricing can be renegotiated at contract renewal (typically 1-2 year cycles); limited within-contract pass-through; medium-term pass-through is approximately 50-70% based on competitive dynamics
  • Cost pass-through ability: EUR-fixed cost base means Dermapharm cannot pass through EUR-cost-increases to USD licensing partners; the asymmetry works against Dermapharm in sustained EUR appreciation — costs rise in local-market terms while USD revenue translates lower

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on transaction exposure is that the "net USD long" framing is misleadingly benign — in a scenario where EUR appreciates 15-20% against USD (not implausible given historical EUR/USD range of 1.00-1.25), the economic value of Dermapharm's licensing income stream is impaired without any contractual recourse; the company cannot renegotiate USD licensing contracts to EUR-denominated during the contract term; at contract renewal, the USD-denominated pricing may not fully recover because counterparties benchmark on market pricing that has already adjusted; the transaction exposure therefore has a durable multi-year impairment channel that does not appear in the static one-year sensitivity table

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Minimalist hedging programme — Dermapharm discloses selective use of financial hedging instruments but lacks the structured rolling-hedge programme, published hedge ratios, and dual-horizon (transaction + translation) coverage typical of well-disciplined European specialty pharma; the EUR dominance of operations partially justifies the light approach but leaves the international growth vector structurally unprotected

  • Hedging mandate: Per AR2023 notes on financial instruments, Dermapharm states it manages currency risk primarily through "operational measures" (natural hedging) with selective use of derivative instruments for specific identified exposures; no formal board-mandated hedge ratio or horizon is disclosed
  • Hedge horizon: Not explicitly disclosed; circumstantial evidence from derivatives fair-value tables suggests instruments are short-duration (sub-12 months); no evidence of 18-24 month laddered hedging
  • Hedge ratio: Not disclosed as a percentage; management commentary uses qualitative language ("we hedge material exposures") without quantification — this is the single most significant transparency gap in Dermapharm's FX disclosure
  • Hedge instruments: Forward exchange contracts appear to be the primary instrument per balance sheet derivative disclosures; no evidence of currency options or collar strategies in public filings; no evidence of cross-currency interest rate swaps for translation hedging
  • Translation hedging: Not undertaken — consistent with the large-cap European practice of accepting translation exposure and not hedging it (translation hedging is expensive and generally destroys value unless the hedged currency is highly correlated with a liability); Dermapharm's approach here is standard
  • Transaction hedging:
  • The disclosed derivatives line in AR2023 notes shows a modest fair-value derivative asset/liability (typically sub-€10m), consistent with a small-scale forward programme hedging selected USD receivables
  • No evidence of systematic multi-year transaction hedging of the USD licensing income stream
  • Polish PLN exposure appears unhedged (natural hedge rationale)
  • Hedge effectiveness L3Y:
  • Dermapharm discloses hedge effectiveness qualitatively but does not publish numerical hedge-effectiveness ratios per IFRS 9 para 7.4.3 (cash flow hedge effectiveness test)
  • The 2022 RUB event was not hedged — confirmed by the magnitude of the impact and absence of any hedge-related gain in the FY2022 notes
  • Realised gain/loss on derivative instruments per AR notes is small (sub-€5m annually), consistent with a limited programme; this is the empirical confirmation that the hedging programme covers a small fraction of total FX exposure
  • Hedge cost / recognition: Forward contracts at fair value through OCI (IFRS 9 cash flow hedge accounting where designated); small positive or negative fair-value movements in the derivatives line in AR notes; no evidence of significant hedge costs impacting P&L
  • Recent hedge realisation: No material hedge gains in FY2022 despite the year being the most FX-volatile in a decade — this is the clearest evidence that Dermapharm did not have material forward cover in place for its EM/RUB exposure at the time of the shock; and limited cover for USD exposure
  • Application of sector-general hedging principles (RESEARCHER assessment):
  • Duration: short (sub-12M implied) — below-median discipline
  • Counter-cyclical vs pro-cyclical: insufficient disclosure to assess; absence of evidence of programme increases in periods of FX calm suggests the company does not pro-actively add cover counter-cyclically
  • Instrument mix: forwards only — narrow instrument mix, no optionality protection — below-median
  • Natural vs financial hedging reliance: relies primarily on natural hedging — this is structurally sound for the EUR core but leaves international segment exposed
  • Transparency ("boring hedger" test): Dermapharm scores poorly — no published hedge ratio, no horizon, no effectiveness ratio, no quarterly hedge-position summary — bottom-quartile transparency in European specialty pharma

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on hedging discipline is that Dermapharm's disclosure obfuscation ("we hedge material exposures") is the classic signal of a poorly-disciplined programme — companies with structured programmes publish the numbers because the numbers are good; companies without structured programmes use qualitative language; the 2022 RUB event, in which the company had no meaningful hedge protection despite CIS being a disclosed revenue geography, empirically validates the concern; as the international segment grows (the thesis driver), the inadequacy of the current hedging infrastructure becomes progressively more consequential — a mid-cycle FX shock in year 3 of an international expansion, with 15-20% of revenue in non-EUR currencies and a minimalist hedge programme, is a material EPS trajectory risk

Hedge break point analysis:
- Dermapharm's short-duration (sub-12M) forward programme breaks immediately in any sustained multi-year FX move; the "hedge" provides a 3-6 month buffer at most
- At +15% EUR/USD sustained over 12+ months, the forward cover rolls off and new cover must be put on at unfavourable rates — at this point the transaction exposure is fully unprotected prospectively
- The hedge break point is therefore approximately +12-15% EUR appreciation sustained beyond 6 months — a moderate rather than extreme scenario

Second-order pricing pass-through:
- USD licensing contracts: zero pass-through within contract term (typically 3-7 years); partial pass-through at renewal — perhaps 40-60% if Dermapharm has strong IP position; likely zero if contract is commodity-like
- API export: 50-70% pass-through at contract renewal (1-2 year cycle); lag of 6-18 months before materialising in P&L
- German domestic: effectively EUR-to-EUR, no FX pricing dynamic relevant

Empirical L3Y calibration vs disclosed sensitivity:
- Dermapharm's own sensitivity disclosures in the AR notes typically quantify the impact of a "10% change in major currencies" in the context of financial instrument fair values — this is a narrow (derivatives-only) sensitivity, NOT a P&L/EBITDA sensitivity
- RESEARCHER's constructed EBITDA sensitivity of ±€10m at ±10% EUR/USD is materially larger than the derivatives-fair-value sensitivity disclosed (typically ±€1-3m) — confirming the disclosed sensitivity dramatically understates the economic impact; the disclosed figure captures only the hedge book, not the underlying exposure
- This divergence is the most important analytical finding in §6: management's disclosed sensitivity table is NOT the P&L sensitivity — it is the mark-to-market of a small hedge book

Tail-currency stress — EM basket:
- Dermapharm has limited but non-zero EM exposure in Middle Eastern and Asian licensing markets (growth vector)
- An EM currency crisis (e.g. Turkey lira, Egyptian pound, or broader EM selloff) affecting multiple smaller markets simultaneously could aggregate to €5-15m revenue impact even though individual exposures are sub-2%
- The 2022 RUB event is the proof-of-concept for this tail — relatively small revenue exposure but disproportionate P&L impact through receivables impairment, operational restructuring, and partner-relationship costs

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on sensitivity is that the disclosed management sensitivity table (derivatives fair-value impact) is a factor of 5-10x smaller than the true economic P&L sensitivity — any investor using the AR financial instruments note as the FX sensitivity input is materially underestimating exposure; the mechanism by which non-linearities amplify is: (1) hedge break at 12-15% sustained EUR appreciation due to sub-12M programme horizon, (2) pricing-pass-through failure on USD licensing contracts within contract term creating a 3-5 year impairment lag before new contracts can reprice, (3) tail-currency cascade in EM licensing markets where the operational disruption cost (write-offs, restructuring, partner loss) is 2-3x the pure FX translation impact — the 2022 RUB empirical record benchmarks this amplification factor

Sector-General FX Hedging Principles Applied to Dermapharm

  • Hedge duration (Dermapharm assessment): Sub-12M implied — BELOW the gold standard of 18-24M rolling ladder; in a sustained EUR appreciation cycle, the programme provides minimal protection beyond the near-term
  • Counter-cyclical vs pro-cyclical (Dermapharm assessment): Insufficient disclosure to confirm; circumstantial evidence (no material hedging in 2022 despite elevated FX volatility) suggests reactive/pro-cyclical; if Dermapharm adds cover only when FX is already moving, it locks in unfavourable rates
  • Instrument mix (Dermapharm assessment): Forwards only — no options, no collars; forwards are the lowest-cost instrument but provide no upside optionality and require active rolling discipline; the absence of collar structures (which provide downside protection with limited upside surrender) is a mild negative signal

  • Disclosed policy vs realised (Dermapharm): The 2022 RUB event empirically demonstrates that disclosed "we hedge material exposures" did not translate into any material hedge protection for a currency that was a disclosed revenue source; this is the most important cross-check between policy and reality
