Brief company descriptions

OEM International is a leading European technology trading group that operates as a value-adding link between industrial component manufacturers and over 30,000 end customers.

The company acts as an exclusive local representative for its suppliers, explicitly refraining from marketing competing products in order to build long-term trust and loyalty.

It offers an extensive and highly technical portfolio of over 60,000 products sourced from more than 400 specialized manufacturers globally.

Beyond standard distribution, OEM creates distinct customer value through deep application expertise, highly efficient logistics flows, and customized component modifications.

The company's geographic footprint spans 37 operating business units across 16 countries, primarily covering Northern Europe, East Central Europe, the British Isles, and China.

The Sweden region represents the group's historical core and largest operating segment, consistently generating approximately 58% to 59% of total consolidated net sales over the past three years.

Sweden also commands the highest profitability within the group, boasting an EBITA margin of approximately 16% to 17% in recent years due to significant scale advantages and a mature market presence.

The Finland, the Baltic states, and China segment accounts for roughly 18% to 20% of group revenue and was initially established to follow Nordic customers relocating their production facilities abroad.

This secondary region has historically exhibited the lowest profitability profile, with EBITA margins hovering around 11% to 12% amid subdued regional demand and a heavier weighting toward standard automation components.

The Denmark, Norway, the British Isles, and East Central Europe segment constitutes the remaining 23% of sales, operating across a highly diverse set of localized niches and industrial customer bases.

DNB Carnegie highlights that across all regions, the "mation" product grouping dominates the revenue mix, accounting for nearly 58% of total sales and serving as the primary growth engine over the last decade.

DNB Carnegie further notes that the remainder of the product portfolio is distributed across "Other" products at 23%, specialized "Components" at 12%, and high-growth "Installation components" at 7%.

DNB Carnegie estimates that highly profitable proprietary and private-label products, such as the Hide-a-lite brand, currently make up approximately 10% to 15% of total sales and offer a plausible pathway for future margin expansion.

The business model is highly resilient and insulated from concentration risk, as no single customer accounts for more than 4% of sales and no single supplier exceeds 3% of total revenue.

Over the past three years, OEM has consistently achieved record revenue levels, surpassing SEK 5.3 billion in 2025 despite a broadly sluggish European industrial macroeconomic backdrop.

DNB Carnegie points out that OEM has historically outgrown underlying industrial production by approximately 1.5 percentage points annually, driven by structural tailwinds like nearshoring and industrial automation.

Redeye emphasizes that the company's return on invested capital has expanded significantly, reaching approximately 28% to 29% in recent years due to high cash conversion and disciplined capital allocation.

Looking plausibly over the next three years, OEM's corporate trajectory is expected to pivot following the early 2025 acquisition of a majority voting stake by private equity firm EQT.

DNB Carnegie expects EQT's ownership to act as a major catalyst for accelerating OEM's M&A engine, potentially pushing the company into new geographic markets or product-owning niches over the next 36 months.

Consequently, the company's traditional three-pillar growth strategy of organic market share gains, geographic expansion, and bolt-on acquisitions will likely see a disproportionate surge in acquisition volume and deal size going forward.

Degree of Change Rating: OrangeThe rating is "Orange" because while the underlying business model, core revenue sources, and geographic footprint remain highly stable and deeply entrenched, the recent change in majority ownership to EQT introduces a massive shift in plausible future M&A intensity and overarching growth ambition over the next three years. The core operations are fundamentally sound, but the pace of structural expansion and capital deployment is poised to change dramatically. A higher "Red" rating would be warranted if EQT signaled an intention to fundamentally alter the decentralized operational structure or drastically lever the balance sheet, while a lower "Yellow" rating would apply if the new owners maintained the historical status quo of moderate, bolt-on dealmaking.

Key components summary

The company is undergoing a major leadership transition, as Jörgen Zahlin, who has been with the company since 1985 and served as CEO since 2000, will step down during 2026.

A new CFO, Daniel Warnholtz, has been appointed and is scheduled to assume the role on August 1, 2026.

The current CFO, Rikard Tingvall, will transition to a new role as Head of Controlling & Accounting once the new CFO assumes his position.

The overarching corporate structure shifted dramatically in March 2025 when private equity firm EQT assumed a majority voting ownership position.

Following EQT's entry, a new Board of Directors was established with a clear mandate to accelerate growth and strengthen the company's long-term strategy.

The new strategic direction focuses heavily on accelerating the pace of acquisitions, transforming the company into a more aggressive M&A compounder.

As part of this shift, OEM broadened its acquisition mandate beyond traditional technical trading firms to also encompass product-owning companies within selected niches.

Acquisition momentum has already accelerated markedly, with two deals completed in 2025 and four additional transactions executed across February and March 2026.

Recent transactions over the past 12 to 18 months include the acquisitions of Vierpool BV, Multicomponent Norden, CRE8 Systems, Ronnie Bergdahls Försäljnings, Weingrill, and Motion29.

FULCRUM - M&A

OEM International is currently transitioning into a phase of aggressively accelerated growth, explicitly positioning corporate acquisitions as the central pillar of its newly updated corporate strategy under EQT's ownership.

To structurally support this heightened M&A ambition and ensure rapid deal execution, the company recently established a dedicated acquisition team tasked with systematically identifying targets alongside regional business area managers.

Management has historically prioritized the acquisition of pure technical trading companies, but the M&A mandate has now been decisively broadened to encompass product-owning companies within selected industrial niches.

Recent deal momentum forcefully confirms this strategic acceleration, with two acquisitions completed in 2025 and four additional transactions swiftly executed in the first quarter of 2026.

The company traditionally structures its acquisitions with a base cash consideration and a contingent earn-out tied directly to the target's future financial performance over a strict three-year period.

DNB Carnegie notes that the official corporate target historically aimed for SEK 250 million in annual acquired sales, but this relatively subdued figure is no longer viewed as representative of the new board's ambitions.

DNB Carnegie aggressively projects that the newly mobilized M&A engine could sustainably add between 10% and 15% to group EBITA annually across the entirety of the next business cycle.

DNB Carnegie highlights that OEM operated with a highly measured acquisition pace over the past decade, adding merely 3% to annual sales growth on average.

DNB Carnegie points out that during this historical period, the average acquired entity was relatively small, contributing approximately SEK 39 million in annualized sales.

DNB Carnegie emphasizes that OEM has historically maintained strict valuation discipline, executing deals at highly attractive multiples averaging between 5.7x and 6.5x EV/EBITA.

DNB Carnegie fully expects the company to sustain an average acquisition multiple of 6.5x EV/EBITA going forward, translating to an impressive day-one return on capital of approximately 15%.

DNB Carnegie suggests that expanding the mandate to include product-owning and manufacturing companies might slightly raise average multiples paid, but overall returns will remain compelling due to the higher inherent gross margins of these assets.

DNB Carnegie believes the total addressable market for European M&A remains massive, explicitly identifying a deep runway of over 24,000 high-quality SME targets in the UK, Germany, and Italy alone.

DNB Carnegie expects OEM to increasingly expand its geographic footprint well beyond the Nordics, taking advantage of a fragmented European landscape where competition for smaller deals remains structurally lower.

DNB Carnegie notes that OEM currently operates with a deeply conservative balance sheet, sitting on a net cash position that provides immense, unencumbered dry powder for an immediate dealmaking spree.

DNB Carnegie estimates that simply levering up the balance sheet to the company's internal ceiling of a 100% debt-to-equity ratio would swiftly unlock approximately SEK 2.3 billion in deployable capital.

DNB Carnegie warns of a highly significant cultural disruption risk, as EQT's overarching pressure to rapidly deploy capital could fatally clash with OEM's historically patient, family-business-like decision-making processes.

DNB Carnegie also highlights deep execution risks, noting that a relentless focus on sourcing and integrating M&A targets could inadvertently distract senior leadership from nurturing the core organic business.

Redeye models a gradual but highly significant ramp-up in group deal volume, ultimately projecting the execution of four successful acquisitions per year from 2028 through 2033.

Redeye simultaneously forecasts the average annual sales per acquired entity to step up to approximately SEK 60 million, further accelerating the compounding top-line contribution.

Redeye calculates that historical deal-making discipline generated a median acquired growth of roughly 2% per year over the last decade, primarily serving to consolidate localized niches.

Redeye explicitly cautions that the number of public and private serial acquirers has increased considerably in recent years, introducing a material risk that heightened competition could drastically drive up the multiples required to secure quality targets.

Redeye emphasizes that OEM has historically funded its limited acquisitions using a mere 11% of generated cash flows, opting instead to routinely distribute massive dividends to legacy shareholders.

Redeye firmly expects EQT to drastically reduce the traditional dividend payout ratio, aggressively redirecting free cash flow toward high-return reinvestments in acquired growth.

KEY - Profit margins

OEM International has demonstrated remarkable profitability resilience over the past three years, consistently delivering EBITA margins that rank among the highest for European technical trading companies.

The group reached a historical profitability peak during the post-pandemic recovery, posting an exceptional EBITA margin of 16.1% in 2022 and 15.8% in 2023.

During 2024, the EBITA margin experienced a slight contraction to 15.3% as the company navigated weaker industrial demand in the second half of the year while continuing to invest heavily in its personnel and organizational structure.

For the full year 2025, the consolidated EBITA margin compressed further to 15.0%, representing a slight year-over-year decline despite the company achieving record top-line sales.

DNB Carnegie notes that for 2025, the company's reported EBIT margin settled at 14.3%, while the EBITDA margin hovered at approximately 16.3%.

Management attributed the 2025 margin squeeze primarily to a weaker gross margin caused by adverse currency fluctuations, alongside a structurally higher cost base stemming from recently completed acquisitions.

The margin pressure was particularly acute during the second quarter of 2025, when the group's EBITA margin temporarily dropped to 13.9%.

DNB Carnegie points out that the sharp Q2 2025 margin dip was primarily a temporary transactional FX effect, as the strengthening Swedish krona negatively impacted gross margins on goods purchased in EUR and USD.

Profitability rebounded strongly in the third quarter of 2025, with the EBITA margin surging back to 16.6% on the back of an improved gross margin and operational cost control.

DNB Carnegie highlights that the robust Q3 2025 margin recovery definitively confirmed that the prior quarter's weakness was FX-driven and temporary rather than a structural deterioration.

Regionally, the Swedish operations remain the bedrock of group profitability, posting an industry-leading EBITA margin of 16.3% in 2025 despite localized currency headwinds.

The Finland, the Baltic states, and China segment saw its EBITA margin improve materially to 12.2% in 2025, up from 11.5% in the prior year, driven by strong top-line leverage and tight cost discipline.

The Rest of Europe segment (Denmark, Norway, British Isles, East Central Europe) reported a solid EBITA margin of 12.9% for 2025, although this was down from 14.3% in the prior year due to a weaker gross margin and acquisition-related costs.

Redeye notes that the Finland, Baltics, and China segment had historically started catching up to Sweden's high profitability, peaking at roughly 15% in 2021 before sluggish regional construction demand dragged the trailing margin down to 11%.

Redeye expects an eventual economic recovery in the Nordic construction sector and adjacent end-markets to prompt a substantial margin rebound for the Finland, Baltics, and China segment in the coming years.

DNB Carnegie asserts there are no structural reasons preventing the Finland, Baltics, and China segment from eventually reaching a similar margin profile to the highly profitable Swedish operations over time.

The group's official overarching financial target is to maintain a minimum EBITA margin of 12% across an entire business cycle.

OEM has consistently and significantly exceeded this 12% threshold over the last five years, demonstrating massive operational resilience even during periods of severe supply chain disruption and industrial sluggishness.

DNB Carnegie expects future margin appreciation to be primarily gross-profit driven, assuming the company successfully manages to continuously improve its sales mix over the next three years.

DNB Carnegie highlights that increasing the sales contribution from higher-margin modified products, value-added services, and proprietary private-label goods will be the central engine for this structural gross margin expansion.

DNB Carnegie also anticipates additional margin potential to be unlocked through operating leverage, given the company's strong historical track record of increasing sales per full-time employee and reducing staff costs relative to total revenues.

Redeye models a steady, long-term expansion of the group's adjusted EBITA margin, projecting it to grow from approximately 15% in 2024 to roughly 17% by the end of their forecast period in 2033.

DNB Carnegie estimates that the core EBITA margin will strengthen to 16.2% by 2027, underpinning an adjusted organic EBITA compound annual growth rate of roughly 7% over the medium term.

Looking ahead, the newly accelerated M&A strategy under EQT's ownership is expected to significantly boost absolute profit growth, even if the integration of lower-margin product-owning companies temporarily dilutes consolidated group margin percentages.

DNB Carnegie suggests that combining the company's underlying organic margin expansion with a rapid ramp-up in balance-sheet-funded M&A could add a staggering 10% to 15% to total group EBITA growth annually over the next business cycle.

KEY - Cash conversion

OEM International possesses a fundamentally strong cash generation profile characterized by an asset-light technology trading model.

Over the past decade spanning 2015 to 2024, the company delivered a highly resilient average cash conversion rate of approximately 61 percent.

Redeye corroborates this long-term trend, noting that median adjusted EBITA to free cash flow to the firm conversion stood at roughly 61 percent between 2013 and 2023.

The group's bottom-line cash realization is even stronger, with median net income to free cash flow to equity conversion reaching roughly 84 percent over the same historical period.

This structural cash generation is heavily supported by a very low capital expenditure requirement, with capex-to-sales averaging just 1.2 percent over the last ten years.

If routine lease payments are included in the calculation, the historical capex-to-sales ratio only rises slightly to a highly manageable 1.7 percent.

Prior to the pandemic, the company operated with a relatively stable working capital intensity, maintaining an average net working capital to sales ratio of approximately 22 percent between 2015 and 2020.

Cash conversion suffered materially during 2021 and 2022 as severe global supply chain disruptions forced the company to build up significant precautionary inventory buffers.

This strategic decision to safeguard customer deliveries resulted in the group's working capital to sales ratio peaking at an elevated 26 percent by 2024.

As global supply chains began to normalize, the company experienced considerable working capital releases throughout 2023, temporarily boosting cash conversion well above normal historical levels.

The statutory operating cash flow for the full year 2023 surged to SEK 791 million, a massive increase from the depressed SEK 263 million recorded in the prior year.

In 2024, the group generated a highly robust operating cash flow of SEK 692 million, reflecting a more normalized operational environment.

The most recent financial year of 2025 demonstrated continued cash flow strength, with operating cash flow expanding to SEK 763 million prior to major M&A outflows.

Redeye notes that while cash conversion naturally displays some annual volatility, this metric is expected to stabilize over the next three years as extreme pandemic anomalies completely fade.

DNB Carnegie explicitly forecasts that there are no structural barriers preventing the company from gradually reducing its elevated inventory levels over the near term.

Consequently, the net working capital to sales ratio is projected to trend downwards from recent peaks, gradually normalizing to approximately 24 percent by 2027.

This anticipated release of trapped working capital is expected to structurally enhance the group's cash generation over the next 36 months.

DNB Carnegie models an impressive acceleration in the cash conversion rate, projecting an average free cash flow to EBITA conversion of approximately 74 percent across the 2025 to 2027 forecast period.

Redeye similarly projects a healthy cash generation profile over the medium term, forecasting adjusted EBITA to FCFF conversion to range between 67 percent and 73 percent from 2025 through 2033.

Redeye further estimates that net income to FCFE conversion will remain robust, hovering between 89 percent and 101 percent over the coming years.

Over the forecast horizon, the group's absolute FCFF margins are expected to structurally improve to between 10 percent and 13 percent, compared to a historical median of just 6 percent.

DNB Carnegie emphasizes that OEM's cash conversion is particularly high-quality because the group owns 100 percent of its subsidiaries, completely avoiding the minority dividend leakage seen in some rival serial acquirers.

Historically, the company has funded its limited acquisition agenda using a mere fraction of its generated cash flow, historically opting to routinely distribute massive dividends to legacy shareholders instead.

Redeye expects the new majority owner EQT to drastically alter this cash allocation framework over the next three years by redirecting a significantly larger portion of free cash flow away from dividends and toward M&A.

Degree of Change Rating: YellowThe degree of change regarding free cash flow conversion prior to M&A is rated "Yellow". The underlying mechanics of OEM International's cash generation remain highly stable, underpinned by structurally low capital expenditure requirements and a highly profitable distribution model. The primary change occurring over the next three years is a normalization of working capital following severe pandemic-era supply chain disruptions, which pushed the net working capital to sales ratio to an elevated 26%. As inventory buffers are gradually released, this metric is expected to improve toward 24%, boosting cash conversion from its historical 61% average toward a projected 74%. While this represents a meaningful improvement in liquidity generation, it is a cyclical normalization rather than a fundamental shift in the company's operating model. A higher "Orange" or "Red" rating would be appropriate if the core business was becoming structurally more capital intensive or if cash conversion was deteriorating significantly.

KEY - Leverage

OEM International has historically operated with an incredibly conservative balance sheet, maintaining a median total net debt to EBITDA ratio of approximately 0.2x between 2013 and 2024.

This deep financial conservatism provided the company with rock-solid catastrophe insurance during economic downturns but severely constrained its pace of acquisition-driven growth.

At the close of the 2025 financial year, the group reported cash and bank balances of SEK 812 million.

When combining this cash with committed undrawn credit facilities, the company boasts total available liquidity of SEK 1,413 million, providing massive immediate firepower for deals.

Consequently, the company finished 2025 in a significant net cash position, with a reported net debt to EBITDA ratio of negative 0.8x.

The group's overarching financial targets stipulate that its debt-to-equity ratio must remain below 100 percent across a business cycle.

For the full year 2025, OEM vastly outperformed this ceiling, reporting a nominal debt-to-equity ratio of just 4 percent.

DNB Carnegie calculates that the company could theoretically take on approximately SEK 3.6 billion in additional debt before breaching its primary bank covenant, which requires an equity-to-assets ratio above 35 percent.

DNB Carnegie also estimates that merely levering up to the internal 100 percent debt-to-equity target would swiftly unlock approximately SEK 2.3 billion in completely untapped capital for immediate M&A deployment.

Redeye shares a similar view on the company's latent balance sheet capacity, noting that pushing net leverage to roughly 2.0x EBITDA would easily unleash around SEK 2 billion in dry powder.

Redeye views this massive underutilization of the balance sheet as a primary catalyst for EQT's recent acquisition of a majority voting stake in the company.

Following EQT's assumption of ownership in early 2025, the new board immediately signaled a clear intention to combine the group's strong net cash position with a significantly accelerated pace of corporate acquisitions.

The company explicitly states that its unleveraged properties and existing cash buffers provide an ideal foundation for financing this incoming wave of acquisition-driven growth.

DNB Carnegie argues that the most value-accretive pathway for an M&A compounder like OEM is to systematically combine internally generated cash flows with supplementary debt to fund higher-yielding acquisitions.

DNB Carnegie points out that leveraging acquired earnings by approximately 2.5x EV/EBITA allows compounders to structurally accelerate growth without triggering dangerous absolute leverage ceilings.

DNB Carnegie models a bull-case scenario where OEM successfully allocates all free cash flow and supplementary leverage into acquisitions, pushing net debt to EBITDA to 2.0x by 2030 and generating 30 percent annual EBITA growth.

DNB Carnegie's base-case scenario for accelerated M&A assumes the company will gradually deploy its balance sheet to reach a highly manageable net debt to EBITDA ratio of 0.6x by 2032.

Redeye strongly advocates for the company to rapidly increase its net debt to EBITDA ratio toward 2.0x by acquiring high-quality trading and product-owning companies at conservative valuation multiples.

If the company stubbornly refused to execute new M&A over the next two years, its high cash generation would force its net debt to EBITDA ratio down to an incredibly inefficient negative 1.5x by 2027.

OEM maintains minimal exposure to interest rate shocks, as it carries no liabilities with fixed interest rates, protecting its cash conversion from sudden macroeconomic rate spikes.

The group's interest-bearing liabilities are structurally negligible, totaling just SEK 110 million at the end of 2025, of which the vast majority consists of mandatory IFRS 16 leasing liabilities.

While high interest rates generally suppress cash conversion and restrict M&A capacity for highly levered compounders, OEM's net cash position completely insulates it from this structural headwind.

DNB Carnegie suggests that OEM's total lack of leverage currently represents a distinct competitive advantage over more indebted Swedish serial acquirers, allowing it to aggressively buy assets while peers are forced to pause for deleveraging.

Change in leadership

OEM International is facing a monumental leadership transition at the highest level, as long-time Managing Director and Chief Executive Officer Jörgen Zahlin is scheduled to step down during 2026.

Jörgen Zahlin has been employed by the group since 1985 and has served as its CEO since 2000, establishing a deep legacy of operational stability and consistent value creation over more than two decades.

Redeye highlights that because Mr. Zahlin has consistently championed the same strategic vision since taking the helm, his impending departure introduces elevated uncertainty regarding the company's future operational rhythm.

DNB Carnegie cautions that if the board recruits an external successor to replace the CEO, the new appointee could face initial challenges in gaining organizational alignment within a company accustomed to extreme leadership continuity.

In a move designed to support this impending executive transition and accelerate the company's growth agenda, Henrik Forsberg Schoultz was appointed as Deputy CEO, effective April 2026.

The incoming Deputy CEO brings extensive experience in sustainable value creation from acquisition-driven organizations, having previously held senior leadership roles at CellMark AB, Nordstjernan, and Neudi & Co.
