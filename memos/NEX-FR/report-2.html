1. BRIEF COMPANY DESCRIPTION

Nexans SA is a French-listed cable manufacturer headquartered in Paris. Market cap ~€5.2bn. The company has undergone a fundamental transformation over the past five years — from a diversified industrial cables conglomerate to a focused electrification pure-play.

The business operates across three segments. PWR-Transmission: high-voltage submarine and underground cable systems (EPCI model — engineering, procurement, construction, installation). PWR-Grid: medium-voltage distribution cables and systems. PWR-Connect: low-voltage building wiring, data cables, and accessories.

Nexans operates ~45 manufacturing plants globally. It owns a fleet of cable-laying vessels — currently two operational (Nexans Aurora, Nexans Skagerrak) with a third (Nexans Electra) due Q2 2026. The company employs approximately 23,000 people across 38+ countries.

The company's competitive position rests on its vertically integrated EPCI model in submarine cables — design, manufacture, transport, and install. Only Prysmian, Nexans, and NKT possess this end-to-end capability in the Western world. This creates a structural oligopoly in the highest-margin, highest-growth segment of the cable industry.

Revenue split (FY2025 standard sales €6.1bn): PWR-Transmission is the margin engine. PWR-Grid is the growth engine. PWR-Connect provides the recurring revenue base. The non-electrification businesses (Lynxeo, Amercable, Autoelectric) are being divested — Autoelectric is the final piece, expected to close mid-2026.

3. CHANGE IN LEADERSHIP

Rating: ORANGE — High uncertainty. Positive trajectory could continue; disruption risk is real.

The CEO change is the most significant internal change force. Christopher Guérin led Nexans from 2018 to October 2025. Under his tenure, Nexans was transformed from an underperforming conglomerate into a focused electrification player. Revenue mix shifted. Margins expanded. The stock re-rated materially. He was the architect.

The Board "resolved to part ways" with Guérin. That language matters. This was not a planned succession. It was a Board decision to change direction — or at least speed, emphasis, or style. The press release stated the Board "wishes to create a new momentum to further optimize performance while executing the roadmap."

Julien Hueber is the replacement. He is an insider — joined Nexans in 2002, Executive Committee member since 2018. He ran PWR-Grid & Connect Europe, a €2.6bn, 23-plant operation. He has deep operational experience: supply chain, purchasing, Asia-Pacific leadership (China, South Korea), and industrial cables management.

His initial messaging signals continuity-plus-acceleration: "from execution to expansion." He restructured the Executive Committee in January 2026 — 13 members, organised around regional business units rather than global product lines. This signals decentralisation, faster decision-making, and closer customer proximity.

The bear case on leadership: Guérin was a strategic visionary who repositioned the company. Hueber is an operations man. The strategy is set. But can an operations-oriented CEO navigate the high-stakes submarine cable market where project execution, vessel management, and multi-billion-euro contract negotiation are paramount? Operations capability might actually be exactly what Nexans needs in the current phase — less transformation, more execution.

The bull case: the Board saw that transformation was done. Now Nexans needs operational rigour, cost discipline, and flawless project delivery. Hueber's profile matches that need. His Asia-Pacific experience is relevant as competition from Asian cable makers intensifies.

The new Executive Committee structure — 13 members, regional focus — is a meaningful organisational change. It breaks the prior functional/product-line silos. It should improve market responsiveness. But reorganisations consume management attention, and the risk of execution distraction during the transition is non-trivial.

Key questions: Why was Guérin really dismissed? Was there a strategic disagreement with the Board? Were there execution problems we cannot see? The narrative is "new phase, new leader" but involuntary CEO departures always carry information that the market cannot fully price.

Additional leadership changes to monitor: the CFO, CTO, and heads of PWR-Transmission are critical roles. Stability in these positions would provide reassurance. Any further turnover in the senior team would be a yellow flag.

The Chairman, Jean Mouton, provides continuity. The Board composition has not changed materially, suggesting this was a CEO-specific decision rather than a wholesale governance overhaul.

4. CHANGE IN STRATEGY/STRUCTURE

Rating: GREEN — Strategy is clear, coherent, and well-advanced in execution.

Nexans' strategic transformation is one of the clearest "company reinvention" stories in European industrials. The shift from diversified cable conglomerate to pure electrification player is nearly complete.

The strategy has three pillars. First: portfolio rotation. Sell everything that is not electrification. Amercable (sold Jan 2025, $280m EV). Lynxeo (sold Q3 2025, ~€525m EV). Autoelectric (sale to Motherson, €207m EV, closing mid-2026). Once Autoelectric completes, Nexans is a pure-play. Total divestiture proceeds exceed €1bn. The divested businesses employed ~16,000 people and generated ~€1.5bn in revenue — this is not trimming at the edges. This is radical surgery.

Second pillar: invest in high-growth electrification segments. PWR-Transmission gets the lion's share of capex. The Nexans Electra vessel (~€200m+) is the single biggest investment. Manufacturing capacity expansion in HV cable plants. The NCCCR copper recycling facility (€90m) secures raw material supply. Total capex budget: €1.2bn for 2025-2028.

Third pillar: bolt-on M&A in electrification. Electro Cables Canada (acquired end 2025) is the template — small, complementary, value-accretive. The company has earmarked further acquisition firepower from the €1.4bn cumulative FCF target.

The structural change under Hueber — regional business units replacing product-line organisation — is a second-order strategic shift. It optimises for execution rather than innovation. It should improve local market capture in PWR-Grid and PWR-Connect. It risks losing some of the global coordination advantages in PWR-Transmission, but submarine cable projects are inherently managed as global programmes.

The 2025-2028 CMD targets provide a clear strategic financial framework. EBITDA target: €1,150m (±€75m) by 2028 — implying ~60% growth from the 2024 base. Organic growth: +3-5% CAGR. ROCE >20%. FCF conversion >45%. These targets require continued margin expansion and topline growth. They are ambitious but not unreasonable given the demand backdrop.

A structural risk: Nexans is betting heavily on submarine cables and grid electrification. If the European energy transition slows (regulatory pushback, fiscal austerity, political change), the investment thesis weakens. The portfolio concentration that creates upside leverage also creates downside vulnerability.

The comparison with peers is instructive. Prysmian has diversified into telecoms (Encore Wire acquisition) while maintaining cable leadership. NKT is a purer HV cable play but smaller. Nexans sits between — more focused than Prysmian, larger than NKT, but with a higher proportion of lower-margin distribution cables.

5. CHANGE IN PRIORITIES/AMBITION

Rating: GREEN — Ambition level has materially stepped up.

The November 2024 Capital Markets Day marked a clear escalation in Nexans' financial ambition. Prior targets were more conservative. The new 2028 framework represents a step-change.

Previous ambition (pre-CMD): steady margin expansion, organic growth, gradual portfolio optimisation. New ambition: €1,150m EBITDA by 2028 (from €665m in 2023 and €804m in 2024). That is a near-doubling in four years.

The €350m incremental EBITDA target from Electrification businesses alone signals that the company expects massive operating leverage as submarine cable projects scale, grid investment accelerates, and portfolio rotation removes margin-dilutive businesses.

ROCE target >20% is notable. This implies capital discipline alongside growth investment. The €1.2bn capex envelope is substantial but bounded. No blank-cheque investment programme.

The FCF target of €1.4bn cumulative (2025-2028) is aggressive but supported by the backlog. Submarine cable projects typically carry favourable working capital dynamics — advance payments from utilities. The FY2025 result (€344m FCF, 47.3% conversion) demonstrates this already.

Dividend policy: progressive, 20%+ payout of recurring net income. The buyback programme (128,000 shares) is small — more employee share plan management than aggressive capital return. This suggests the Board prefers to retain capital for growth and M&A. At this stage of the cycle, that is the right priority.

The 2026 guidance (€730-810m EBITDA) represents a step-down from 2025's €728m on a like-for-like basis, adjusted for divestitures. The company expects H1 2026 softer than H2, driven by project phasing. This creates a sentiment risk: the market dislikes flat-to-down guidance years, even when the multi-year trajectory is strongly positive. Whether this is genuine operational caution or new-CEO conservatism to set a beatable bar is the key interpretive question.

The shift in organisational priorities under Hueber — from transformation to operational excellence, from global product lines to regional business units — reflects a practical recalibration. The big strategic bets have been placed. Now the priority is execution and extraction.

M&A ambition has subtly shifted. Under Guérin, M&A was primarily divestitures (cleaning the portfolio). Under Hueber, M&A is expected to pivot to bolt-on acquisitions in electrification. The Electro Cables Canada acquisition is the template. Expect more of these — small, complementary, filling geographic or product gaps.

7. VALUE CHAIN — DEMAND

Rating: GREEN — Structural demand supercycle with multiple reinforcing drivers.

The demand environment for Nexans is the most favourable in the company's history. Multiple demand drivers are converging simultaneously. This is not a single-variable story.

Driver 1: European Grid Renovation. Over 40% of European distribution grids are over 40 years old. The EU Grids Package mandates €1.2 trillion in grid investment 2024-2040. Annual investment needs: €65-100bn. The EIB committed a record €11bn to energy grids in 2025, tripling from 2023. This is not speculative demand — it is mandated, funded, and politically supported across the EU. PWR-Grid is the direct beneficiary.

Driver 2: HVDC Interconnectors. EU target: 15% interconnection by 2030. Cross-border power exchange requires massive HVDC submarine cable systems. ENTSO-E estimates €3.4bn/year in interconnector investment 2025-2040. France alone plans to double interconnection to 18GW by 2030. The RTE framework agreement (>€1bn, March 2025) is a tangible proof point. PWR-Transmission benefits directly.

Driver 3: Offshore Wind Export Cables. North Sea countries target 120GW offshore wind by 2030, 300GW by 2050. Every turbine needs export cable to shore. Array cables connect turbines. Export cables connect substations. Nexans has 14% revenue exposure to offshore wind — higher than peers. The supply bottleneck (800+ day lead times) gives pricing power to incumbents.

Driver 4: Data Centre Electrification. Data centre electricity demand grew 17% in 2025. AI-focused data centres are growing even faster. Electricity consumption from data centres is set to double by 2030, and AI-focused ones to triple. Every data centre needs medium- and low-voltage cable infrastructure. This benefits PWR-Grid and PWR-Connect. Nexans has a dedicated data centre solutions offering with low-carbon cables.

Driver 5: Electrification of Transport, Heating, Industry. Heat pump installations require upgraded domestic wiring. EV charging infrastructure needs cable. Industrial electrification replaces gas. Each driver creates incremental demand for PWR-Connect and PWR-Grid products.

Driver 6: Energy Security / Geopolitical. Post-Ukraine, post-Iran escalation — European energy security is a top political priority. Grid investment is now framed as critical infrastructure, not just climate policy. This hardens the political commitment to spending. Bipartisan support reduces regulatory/policy risk.

The demand picture has one critical caveat: demand is not the bottleneck. Supply is. Cable factory capacity, vessel availability, and skilled labour are the constraints. This means pricing power for incumbents but also execution risk — can Nexans deliver on its backlog without cost overruns, delays, or quality issues?

Demand pulse caution (per Richard's framework): the risk is not that demand disappears but that it is lumpy and project-driven. Submarine cable awards are chunky. A single delayed project can create a revenue gap. The 90% backlog coverage through 2028 mitigates this substantially but does not eliminate it.

8. VALUE CHAIN — COMPETITION

Rating: YELLOW — Oligopoly is favourable but Prysmian's lead is widening.

The competitive landscape in submarine/HV cables is structurally favourable. Three Western players dominate: Prysmian (Italy), Nexans (France), NKT (Denmark). Asian competitors (Sumitomo Electric, LS Cable, Zhongtian Technology) are present but lack the full EPCI capability and vessel fleet required for the most complex projects.

Prysmian is the 800-pound gorilla. FY2025 EBITDA €2.4bn. The Encore Wire acquisition added US distribution. The €5bn Amprion contract in Q1 2025 was a statement of dominance. Prysmian's Transmission margins (20.9% in Q4'25) are industry-leading. Its scale advantages in procurement, R&D, and vessel fleet are widening. Prysmian's market share in submarine cables is ~40% by value.

NKT is the focused HV specialist. FY2025 EBITDA €390m. HV backlog €10.2bn (larger than Nexans in absolute terms). NKT is expanding capacity — new factory in Karlskrona (Sweden). The company's "Charging Forward" strategy targets 2030 with aggressive growth ambitions. NKT's pure-play HV focus gives it higher margins than Nexans' blended portfolio. NKT announced booking commitments of >€3.5bn on top of its backlog — forward visibility is exceptional.

Nexans sits at ~21% submarine cable market share. The Nexans Electra vessel (Q2 2026) adds installation capacity. Manufacturing investment is ongoing. But the gap with Prysmian is widening in absolute terms while NKT is catching up from below.

Asian competitors are a medium-term risk. LS Cable (South Korea) and Sumitomo Electric (Japan) are investing in HV capability. Chinese players (Zhongtian, Hengtong) are competitive on price but face barriers in European markets (permitting, qualification, political sensitivity to Chinese infrastructure involvement). The Asian threat is real for lower-complexity projects but limited for the most technically demanding HVDC submarine installations.

Grid and Connect competition is more fragmented. Dozens of regional players compete on price in MV and LV cables. Nexans' advantages here are brand, qualification lists (utility-approved suppliers), geographic footprint, and operational efficiency. Margins are lower but stable. Competition does not pose an existential threat but limits pricing power.

Competitive moats in Transmission: (1) Vessel fleet — only three Western players have dedicated CLVs. (2) Factory qualification — submarine cable factories take 3-5 years to build and qualify. (3) Track record — utilities require proven execution history before awarding multi-billion-euro contracts. (4) Financial strength — submarine projects require large advance payment facilities and balance sheet capacity. (5) Technical capability — HVDC technology at 525kV+ is cutting-edge.

The bear view on competition: Prysmian's scale is becoming self-reinforcing. Larger revenue → more R&D → better technology → larger contracts → higher margins → more investment. Nexans risks becoming the permanent #3, winning the contracts Prysmian and NKT don't want. This would cap margin expansion potential.

9. VALUE CHAIN — DISRUPTION

Rating: GREEN — Low disruption risk. Cable technology evolves slowly. Incumbents benefit.

Cable is one of the least disruptable industries in the world. The physical laws governing electricity transmission (resistivity, capacitance, insulation) do not change. Copper and aluminium conductors have dominated for over a century. The product is, at its core, metal wrapped in plastic.

That said, there are incremental technology shifts:

HVDC voltage escalation: Industry is moving from 320kV to 525kV systems. Higher voltage = more power per cable = fewer cables needed per project. This is a mixed blessing — higher ASP per cable but potentially lower volume. Nexans has qualified 525kV capability. All three oligopoly players are at parity.

Superconductivity: Nexans is researching superconducting cable for ultra-high-capacity transmission. Currently a laboratory/pilot technology. If it matures, it could transform grid architecture. But the timeline is 10+ years. No near-term disruption risk.

Wireless power transmission: Theoretically possible over very short distances. Not relevant for utility-scale power transmission. No disruption risk.

Alternative materials: Carbon nanotube conductors and graphene-enhanced cables are in R&D. No commercial-scale production exists. Copper and aluminium are safe for the foreseeable future.

Digital cables / smart grids: Embedded sensors, monitoring, and digital twins are emerging. Nexans is investing here — "amplified performance" through digital solutions. This is additive to existing cable products, not disruptive. It could support higher ASPs and service revenue over time.

Recycled materials: Nexans is leading here. The NCCCR facility (€90m investment, 2027 commissioning) will process 80,000 tonnes of recycled copper annually. Target: 30% recycled content by 2030. This is both a cost advantage and an ESG differentiator. Customers increasingly require low-carbon products.

Hydrogen / non-electric energy transmission: If hydrogen infrastructure scaled rapidly, it could reduce electricity grid investment. However, hydrogen is currently more expensive and less efficient than direct electrification for most applications. Electricity transmission investment is not threatened by hydrogen in the medium term.

The disruption environment is benign. Cable is a picks-and-shovels business for the energy transition. The product is essential, the technology is mature, and incumbents have enormous advantages.

10. VALUE CHAIN — POLITICAL/REGULATORY

Rating: GREEN — Regulatory tailwinds are the strongest they have ever been.

The political and regulatory environment is overwhelmingly favourable for Nexans. This is the single most important external change force.

EU Grids Package (2024-2025): The European Commission's grids action plan commits to €1.2 trillion in grid investment 2024-2040. This is legislative, not aspirational. It includes regulatory reforms to accelerate permitting, standardise procurement, and guarantee utility returns on investment. The European Court of Auditors reviewed progress in early 2025 and recommended acceleration. The legislative framework is hardening, not softening.

National Grid Investment Plans: France (RTE doubling interconnection to 18GW), Germany (Amprion grid expansion), UK (40GW offshore wind by 2030), Netherlands, Belgium, Denmark — all have committed to massive grid investment programmes. These are multi-party commitments, not single-government whims. The political consensus on grid modernisation is as broad and deep as any in European energy policy.

EIB Financing: The European Investment Bank committed €11bn to energy grids in 2025 — triple the 2023 level. This provides concessional financing for grid operators, reducing the cost of capital for cable-intensive projects. More EIB capital = more projects = more cable demand.

Permitting Reform: The EU is streamlining environmental review and permitting for grid infrastructure. Cable projects face fewer regulatory hurdles than wind farms or nuclear plants. Underground and submarine cables have relatively low environmental impact. Permitting is a constraint but a smaller one than for other energy infrastructure.

Energy Security Framing: Post-Ukraine, post-Iran — grid investment is increasingly framed as national security. This hardcodes spending commitments into defence/security budgets, making them less vulnerable to fiscal austerity cycles. Cross-party support is strong.

Climate Regulation (Scope 3): Large corporates and utilities face Scope 3 emissions targets. Low-carbon cables (Nexans offers products with 50% CO₂ reduction) become a procurement criterion, not just a nice-to-have. Nexans' investment in recycled materials positions it well here.

Regulatory risks: (1) Tariff/trade policy — EU could impose tariffs on Asian cable imports, which would benefit Nexans. Alternatively, free trade could expose it to cheaper competition. (2) Utility regulation — if grid operators face tighter return regulation, they could delay investments. (3) Public opposition — subsea cables face less NIMBY resistance than onshore infrastructure but are not immune. (4) Fiscal austerity — if European governments face fiscal pressure, grid investment could be delayed. However, the EU Green Deal has embedded grid spending in structural policy, making it resistant to short-term budget cycles.

The political risk that should concern Nexans: a pan-European political shift away from climate/energy transition policy. This is currently a tail risk. Centre-right and centre-left parties across the EU support grid investment. Far-right parties (which are gaining support in several countries) tend to oppose renewable energy but support energy security and grid modernisation. The political base for cable investment is broader than the political base for wind farms.

11. VALUE CHAIN — SUPPLY SIDE

Rating: YELLOW — Supply constraints are real and create both opportunity and risk.

The supply side of the cable industry is under severe strain. This is both a positive (pricing power) and a negative (execution risk) for Nexans.

Cable Factory Capacity: Submarine cable factories take 3-5 years to build, cost €500m+, and require specialised workers. Existing factories are operating at or near full capacity. Lead times have extended to 800+ days for complex submarine cable orders. New capacity is not emerging before 2027 at the earliest. This structural bottleneck gives pricing power to incumbents — Nexans, Prysmian, NKT — and limits competitive entry.

Cable-Laying Vessel Availability: Only a handful of specialised vessels globally can install HVDC submarine cables. Each project ties up a vessel for months. Nexans' Electra (arriving Q2 2026) is a direct response to this constraint. Owning rather than chartering vessels provides cost certainty and scheduling control. Prysmian owns the Leonardo da Vinci. NKT uses a mix of owned and chartered vessels.

Copper Supply: Global copper consumption is projected to rise from 30mt (2020) to 40mt (2030). Mining supply growth is not keeping pace. Copper prices have been volatile. Nexans manages copper exposure through a pass-through mechanism (standard sales exclude copper price impact). The NCCCR recycling facility (80,000 tonnes/year by 2027) provides partial insulation. But copper scarcity could slow the overall electrification buildout, indirectly reducing cable demand in certain segments.

Aluminium: Increasingly used as a copper substitute in distribution cables. Nexans is expanding its recycled aluminium content (10% recycled aluminium cables launched 2025). Aluminium supply is less constrained than copper.

Labour: Submarine cable manufacturing and installation require highly specialised workers. Vessel crews, factory technicians, and project engineers are in short supply. Training pipelines are slow. Labour availability is a real constraint on industry growth. Nexans operates training programmes but the industry as a whole faces a skills gap.

Polymer and Insulation Materials: XLPE (cross-linked polyethylene) is the dominant insulation material for HV cables. Supply is adequate currently but could tighten as cable production scales. No major supply risk flagged.

Supply chain geopolitics: European policymakers are increasingly sensitive to supply chain security for critical infrastructure components. "Made in Europe" preferences for grid equipment could benefit Nexans (French-headquartered, European manufacturing base). This could formalise into local content requirements for grid projects.

12. REVENUE/DEMAND CYCLE

Rating: GREEN — Early-to-mid cycle in a structural multi-decade upcycle.

The cable industry has historically been cyclical, driven by construction activity, utility capex cycles, and commodity prices. The current demand environment represents a structural break from the normal cycle.

Cycle position: We are in the early-to-mid phase of a multi-decade grid investment supercycle. European grid infrastructure was underinvested for 20+ years. The energy transition, decarbonisation targets, and energy security imperatives have created a structural inflection. Annual grid investment is roughly doubling from historical levels. This is not a cyclical uptick — it is a regime change.

Backlog as cycle indicator: Nexans' €8.1bn transmission backlog (Q1 2025) provides ~4 years of forward revenue visibility at current run rates. NKT's €10.2bn backlog suggests even longer visibility.
