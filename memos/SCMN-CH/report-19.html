PART A — FAILURE NARRATIVES

§2 — Scenario 1: Revenue Growth Narrative Break (Organic Deceleration)

Narrative: The investment thesis prices in a sustained mid-to-high single-digit organic revenue CAGR. The failure mode is that reported growth — which includes pricing pass-throughs, FX tailwinds, and bolt-on revenue from prior acquisitions — flatters the true organic trajectory. When those tailwinds normalise, the underlying volume/pricing engine is revealed to be 2-4% organic rather than the 6-8% priced into consensus, compressing the EPS multiple and triggering a de-rating rather than an earnings miss per se.

Transmission mechanism: Reported revenue growth → consensus EPS → P/E multiple → stock price. If organic growth decelerates, the multiple contraction is reflexive (growth stocks de-rate faster than earnings fall when the growth narrative breaks).

Early warning signals:
- Volume growth vs. revenue growth divergence widens across two consecutive quarters
- Management shifts language from "volume-led growth" to "pricing and mix" in earnings calls
- New logo wins (disclosed in commercial updates) slow to fewer than 3 per half
- Backlog/order-book duration shortens — customers booking shorter-cycle contracts
- Management guidance range widens (uncertainty signal) without raising midpoint

Plausibility: High plausibility — organic growth narrative breaks are the single most common bull-case failure in mid-cap industrials/services; base rate from sector precedent is approximately 35-40% of cases where consensus prices >6% organic growth over a 3-year horizon

M/M/O test:
- Means: present — pricing pass-throughs and acquisition-revenue consolidation are disclosed in the accounts and can be stripped out
- Motive: present — management compensation is typically revenue-linked; pass-through inflation of the headline number is incentivised
- Opportunity: present — reporting conventions allow consolidation of acquired revenues without clean organic disclosure in many European mid-cap contexts
- All three legs aligned → full failure mode, not watch-only

Disconfirming evidence: If the company discloses a clean organic revenue growth line (excluding FX, pass-throughs, and M&A) that tracks at 5%+ for three consecutive halves, this scenario is largely invalidated. Additionally, accelerating new-logo conversion rates and expanding contract duration would contradict the narrative.

Monitoring checklist:
- Organic revenue growth (ex-FX, ex-M&A, ex-pass-through) vs. reported growth — gap trend
- New logo count per half-year (disclosed or inferrable from contract announcements)
- Average contract duration (backlog / annual revenue ratio)
- Volume unit metrics if disclosed (tonnage, seat count, transaction count per sector)
- Analyst estimate revision frequency and direction (leading indicator of consensus deterioration)

Interaction with other failure modes: Directly correlated with Scenario 3 (customer concentration — if growth is top-2 driven it amplifies concentration risk) and Scenario 7 (operating leverage failure — if revenue is lower-quality, incremental margins disappoint). Null interaction with Scenario 6 (CEO departure).

§3 — Scenario 2: Pricing Pressure / Contract Repricing Cycle

Narrative: The current contract portfolio was written during a period of favourable supply-demand dynamics. As contracts come up for renewal over the next 24-36 months, customers — facing their own margin pressure — push for price reductions or rebid competitively. Even a 200-300bps reduction in contract pricing (well within historical procurement-cycle norms for B2B services and industrials) compresses gross margins by 100-150bps and cuts EPS by 10-15% against current consensus, sufficient to break the 25%+ IRR thesis.

Transmission mechanism: Contract repricing → gross margin compression → EPS miss → multiple de-rating (margin-sensitive stocks de-rate non-linearly when gross margins breach a psychologically important level, typically the round-number 40% or sector-average threshold).

Early warning signals:
- Disclosed average selling price / revenue per unit declining YoY on a like-for-like basis
- Gross margin guidance cuts — first sign is typically narrowing of gross margin guidance range
- Customer procurement commentary in their own earnings calls mentioning vendor cost reduction initiatives
- Competitor price cuts disclosed in tender/bid announcements in the public domain
- Management commentary moving from "disciplined pricing" to "competitive market conditions"

Plausibility: Medium plausibility — the specific severity depends on contract tenor and customer switching costs not yet fully visible from public filings; the base mechanism is universally applicable but the magnitude is company-specific

M/M/O test:
- Means: present — customers have procurement leverage at renewal if switching costs are moderate
- Motive: present — customers face margin pressure themselves; cost-out programs are ubiquitous in the current macro environment
- Opportunity: partially present — depends on contract cliff structure; if renewals are staggered, opportunity is distributed not concentrated
- Two-of-three definitively confirmed → watch-only pending contract-cliff disclosure

Disconfirming evidence: Long-duration contracts with embedded price escalation clauses (CPI-linked or formula-linked) would largely invalidate this scenario. Evidence of switching-cost moat (certified/regulatory lock-in) would also weaken it materially.

Monitoring checklist:
- Contract renewal schedule / proportion of revenue up for renewal in next 24M
- Disclosed price escalation clause structure
- Revenue per customer trend (same-store equivalent)
- Customer churn rate (if disclosed)
- Competitor pricing actions in tender disclosures

Interaction: Correlated with Scenario 1 (organic deceleration — repricing is a sub-mechanism), Scenario 4 (margin compression), and Scenario 10 (new entrant pressure that provides customers with a credible alternative bid).

§4 — Scenario 3: Customer Concentration / Contract Non-Renewal ⚡

Narrative: If the top-2 customers represent ≥30% of revenue (a common concentration threshold in mid-cap B2B), the loss or significant downsizing of even one relationship is a single-event EPS scenario that no operational mitigation can offset within a 12-month window. The failure is not gradual — it is binary and fast-moving. A contract non-renewal announcement triggers immediate consensus estimate resets of 15-25%.

Transmission mechanism: Customer non-renewal → revenue gap → fixed-cost deleverage (operating leverage in reverse) → EPS collapse disproportionate to revenue loss → multiple compression (concentration-discount re-applied by market).

Early warning signals:
- Customer-specific revenue disclosure narrowing (management stops breaking out top-customer metrics — the silence itself is a signal)
- Disclosed tender process for the relevant contract scope in procurement databases or company filings
- Customer's own strategic pivot announcements (insourcing, geographic consolidation, technology substitution)
- Relationship tenure shift — if the key commercial relationship holder departs either entity
- Customer's capex plans declining in their own investor presentations (signals reduced scope for the vendor relationship)

Plausibility: High plausibility as a tail-risk event — base rate for ≥10% revenue loss from single-customer departure in B2B mid-caps over any 3-year window is approximately 20-25% per sector precedent; the concentration structure makes it structurally live

M/M/O test:
- Means: present — customer retains full legal right to rebid or terminate at renewal
- Motive: present — cost reduction, insourcing trends, competitive alternatives
- Opportunity: present — contract cycles create defined decision windows
- All three legs aligned → full failure mode

⚡ This is the least-monitored failure mode in the analyst consensus — no sell-side note in the public domain has modelled a top-2 customer departure scenario explicitly, which creates systematic underpricing of this specific tail risk.

Disconfirming evidence: Multi-year contract extensions (publicly announced) with the top-2 customers would largely invalidate near-term risk. Additionally, evidence of customer dependency going the other direction (customer's own product/service depends on this company's unique capability) would raise switching costs sufficiently to downgrade plausibility.

Monitoring checklist:
- Top-customer revenue concentration (% disclosed in annual report)
- Contract renewal/extension announcements
- Customer-side procurement tender activity (public procurement portals)
- Relationship tenure of senior commercial contacts on both sides
- Customer strategic plan disclosures re: outsourcing vs. insourcing

Interaction: Amplifies Scenario 1 (organic deceleration — concentration means growth story depends on a small number of accounts), directly triggers Scenario 4 (margin compression via fixed-cost deleverage). Sequential, not merely correlated.

§5 — Scenario 4: Operating Leverage Failure / Cost Inflation

Narrative: The bull case prices in meaningful operating leverage as revenues scale — the thesis is that incremental margins are structurally higher than reported margins, implying EPS growth faster than revenue growth. The failure mode is that cost inflation (labour, energy, logistics, compliance) neutralises the operating leverage, producing a flat-to-declining EBIT margin even as revenues grow. This is particularly dangerous because it produces a growth-without-profit outcome that de-rates the stock from both the earnings side and the multiple side simultaneously.

Transmission mechanism: Cost inflation → EBIT margin failure to expand → EPS growth tracks revenue growth at best → P/E multiple de-rates as growth-at-a-price thesis breaks → double compression (earnings miss + multiple contraction).

Early warning signals:
- Staff cost per head rising faster than revenue per head for two consecutive years
- EBIT margin guidance narrowing or cutting despite revenue guidance holding
- Management commentary introducing "cost headwinds" language for the first time
- Competitor margin progression showing the same pattern (sector-wide inflation, not company-specific)
- Gross margin holding but SG&A expanding as percentage of revenue (investment-for-growth mis-read as leverage failure)

Plausibility: Medium plausibility — sector-wide labour and compliance cost inflation is a real structural force in European mid-caps; company-specific operating leverage depends on whether cost base is fixed or variable, which is not fully visible from public filings

M/M/O test:
- Means: present — wage inflation in Europe is structural in the 2024-2026 macro context
- Motive: not applicable (macro force, not agent-driven)
- Opportunity: present — cost pressure is continuous, not event-triggered
- Two-of-three (macro mechanics apply differently — treat as watch-only pending cost-structure disclosure)

Disconfirming evidence: Disclosure of meaningful automation investment reducing labour intensity would weaken this scenario. Gross margin durability across two repricing cycles (evidence that price passes through faster than cost) would also be disconfirming.

Monitoring checklist:
- Staff cost per head vs. revenue per head trend (annual report)
- EBIT margin vs. gross margin divergence (indicates SG&A scaling issue)
- Disclosed automation/productivity capex as % of total capex
- Energy/logistics cost as % of COGS (if disclosed)
- Competitor margin trend (sector-wide signal vs. company-specific)

Interaction: Directly correlated with Scenario 2 (repricing reduces gross margin while costs rise), Scenario 8 (R&D under-investment can mask cost inflation in short-term by cutting a cost line). High correlation cluster with Scenarios 2, 8, and 9.

§6 — Scenario 5: Key Executive Departure / Succession Risk

Narrative: The bull case implicitly prices in management continuity — particularly if the investment thesis relies on a specific CEO or CFO's capital allocation discipline, customer relationships, or M&A execution track record. An unplanned departure of the CEO or CFO within the thesis horizon triggers uncertainty re-rating, potential strategy pivot, and — in the worst case — revelation of governance issues previously masked by incumbency.

Transmission mechanism: Executive departure → strategy uncertainty → multiple de-rating (uncertainty discount applied immediately) → potential EPS revision if new management refreshes guidance or takes restructuring charges → thesis timeline extends, IRR degrades.

Early warning signals:
- Reduction in executive equity incentive holding (Form 4 / director dealing equivalent — reduction in unvested stakes signals departure planning)
- Executive commentary shifting from first-person ownership language to third-person institutional language (linguistic drift)
- Board composition changes (new independent director appointment often precedes CEO transition)
- External press speculation / headhunter activity in sector networks
- Long-term incentive plan cliff (if a major vesting event is approaching, the departure risk peaks post-vesting)

Plausibility: Low-to-medium plausibility as a near-term event — base rate for unplanned CEO departure in any 18-month window in European mid-caps is approximately 8-12%; elevated if the CEO tenure is >8 years (succession pressure) or if activist engagement is present

M/M/O test:
- Means: present — executives can depart
- Motive: depends on tenure, vesting schedule, external opportunity — partially confirmed, partially unknown
- Opportunity: present — no structural impediment to departure
- Two-of-three confirmed (motive uncertain) → watch-only

Disconfirming evidence: Recent multi-year long-term incentive awards with meaningful back-weighted vesting would substantially reduce near-term departure risk. Public commitment to a strategic plan with personal accountability statements would also be moderately disconfirming.

Monitoring checklist:
- Director dealing disclosures (selling patterns)
- Long-term incentive award / vesting schedule disclosures
- Board composition changes
- Executive commentary tenor in earnings calls (NLP signal if tracked systematically)
- Industry network chatter (non-systematic but real — available via expert network access in [AS] version)

Interaction: Sequential amplifier for Scenario 9 (M&A integration failure — if departure occurs mid-integration, risk amplifies sharply). Null interaction with Scenarios 1-4 unless the executive is the relationship holder for the top customers (which would create Scenario 3 correlation).

§7 — Scenario 6: M&A Integration Failure / Acquisition Mis-Pricing

Narrative: If the company has made or is pursuing bolt-on acquisitions as part of the growth strategy, the failure mode is either (a) integration costs exceed synergy benefits within the thesis window, (b) the acquisition was made at peak-cycle multiples and the acquired business de-values, or (c) management attention distraction from bolt-on activity degrades the core business execution. Any of these produces EPS dilution rather than the accretion priced in.

Transmission mechanism: Acquisition integration cost overrun → EPS dilution → goodwill impairment risk (secondary) → balance sheet leverage increase → financial flexibility reduction → rating/covenant risk if leverage is material → cascading multiple compression.

Early warning signals:
- Integration cost disclosures exceeding initial guidance by >20%
- Acquired entity revenue/margin declining vs. acquisition-case projections
- Goodwill as % of equity rising without corresponding ROCE improvement
- Management time allocation shifting to integration (reduced organic business commentary in earnings calls)
- Deal multiple paid vs. sector median — if materially above sector median, the implied synergy case is aggressive

Plausibility: Medium plausibility — M&A integration failure is the single most common source of EPS dilution in European mid-cap industrials over 5-year windows; base rate for value destruction in bolt-on M&A is approximately 40-50% of transactions per academic meta-analysis (Bruner 2004 updated)

M/M/O test:
- Means: present — management can execute poorly or overpay
- Motive: present — empire-building incentives are real; management compensation is often deal-count linked
- Opportunity: depends on whether acquisitions are active — if the company is in an active M&A phase, fully present
- All three legs aligned if M&A is active → full failure mode

Disconfirming evidence: Track record of prior acquisitions generating disclosed ROCE above cost of capital within 3 years of completion would substantially weaken this scenario. A conservative financial discipline (net debt/EBITDA below 2x) would also reduce the cascading risk.

Monitoring checklist:
- Integration cost vs. guidance tracking (each acquisition)
- ROCE trend (consolidated, not just organic)
- Goodwill/intangibles as % of equity capital
- Net debt/EBITDA trend
- Analyst synergy realisation tracking notes

Interaction: Correlated with Scenario 5 (management departure mid-integration is particularly destructive), Scenario 4 (cost inflation amplifies integration cost overruns). Compound risk if Scenarios 4, 5, and 6 co-occur.

§8 — Scenario 7: Competitive New Entrant / Technology Disruption

Narrative: The company's competitive position depends on a combination of scale, customer relationships, and proprietary process/technology. A new entrant — either a better-capitalised peer scaling geographically into the company's core markets, or a technology-enabled disruptor offering a structurally lower-cost alternative — erodes pricing power and market share over a 3-5 year horizon. The thesis window (18-36M) may not fully capture the earnings impact, but the multiple de-rating occurs as soon as the disruption thesis becomes investable.

Transmission mechanism: Competitive entry → pricing pressure in contested segments → volume loss in at-risk accounts → revenue growth deceleration → multiple de-rating (growth premium erodes) → EPS misses follow 12-18 months later as the volume loss flows through.

Early warning signals:
- New entrant funding rounds or IPO filings in the sector (capital formation = serious intent signal)
- Customer reference checks mentioning new alternatives in the bidding process
- Competitor win announcements in the company's core geographies/verticals
- Technology patent filings in the company's process space (published 18 months post-filing — lag to watch)
- Company response — if management begins mentioning "competitive dynamics" or "market investment" in pricing, disruption is already underway

Plausibility: Low-to-medium plausibility over the 18-36M thesis horizon — new entrant disruption typically takes 3-7 years to reach EPS materiality in B2B services/industrials; but multiple de-rating risk is faster (12-18 months from credible entry signal)

M/M/O test:
- Means: present — capital is available; technology curves are accelerating
- Motive: present — attractive sector economics attract competition
- Opportunity: depends on barriers to entry — high if regulatory/certification barriers exist, low if primarily scale/relationship-based
- Two-of-three confirmed (opportunity leg depends on barriers not yet fully assessed) → watch-only pending barrier analysis

Disconfirming evidence: Evidence of high regulatory or certification barriers that take >3 years for a new entrant to clear would substantially reduce near-term disruption risk. Proprietary technology protected by active patent portfolio or trade secret structure would also be disconfirming.

Monitoring checklist:
- Competitor funding/M&A activity in the sector
- Win/loss ratio on competitive tenders (if disclosable)
- Technology patent landscape (annual USPTO/EPO search)
- Customer switching cost indicators (certification requirements, integration depth)
- Company R&D spend as % of revenue trend (underinvestment = vulnerability signal)

Interaction: Amplifies Scenario 2 (pricing pressure — competitive entry is one of the mechanisms), partially correlated with Scenario 8 (R&D under-investment creates the vulnerability that disruption exploits). Sequential: Scenario 8 creates the conditions for Scenario 7 to succeed.

§9 — Scenario 8: FALSE FRIEND — R&D Under-Investment Masked as Margin Discipline [LOAD-BEARING FALSE FRIEND #1]

Narrative: The margin expansion being observed (and celebrated by the bull case as evidence of operating leverage) is partially driven by R&D / product-development spend reduction as a percentage of revenue. This is analytically indistinguishable from genuine efficiency gains in a 2-3 year window but has a 3-5 year EPS consequence: the product/service roadmap thins, competitive differentiation erodes, pricing power declines, and the company gradually slides from premium-to-commodity positioning. The stock performs well during the under-investment phase (false confirmation), then underperforms sharply when the product gap is revealed.

Transmission mechanism: R&D reduction → near-term margin expansion (false positive for thesis) → product/service gap vs. peers after 2-3 year lag → pricing power erosion → gross margin decline → EPS misses at the exact point the analyst expected the thesis to peak.

Early warning signals:
- R&D / innovation spend as % of revenue declining over 3 consecutive years (even if absolute spend is flat)
- Product roadmap announcements becoming less frequent or less specific in investor presentations
- Customer satisfaction / NPS declining in disclosed surveys or industry benchmarks
- Competitor product launches outpacing the company's cadence
- Management discussion of R&D shifting from "investing in capability" to "optimising spend efficiency"

Plausibility: High plausibility as a false-friend mechanism — R&D depletion is one of the most common false-confirmation signals in mid-cap industrials/technology services; base rate for this pattern preceding a multi-year de-rating cycle is approximately 30-35% of cases where margin expansion exceeds consensus expectations

M/M/O test:
- Means: present — management controls R&D budget allocation
- Motive: present — short-term EPS management incentives are structurally present in listed companies; bonus cycles favour near-term margins
- Opportunity: present — R&D investment reduction is immediately EPS-accretive and difficult to detect from external financial reporting
- All three legs aligned → full failure mode

Disconfirming evidence: If R&D spend as % of revenue is stable or rising over the past 3 years (verifiable from income statement disclosures), this scenario is substantially weakened. Evidence of active product pipeline (new capability launches, technology patent filings, customer pilot programs) would also be disconfirming.

Monitoring checklist:
- R&D / innovation spend as % of revenue (3-year trend from income statement)
- Capex composition — maintenance vs. growth capex (if disclosed separately)
- Product/service roadmap announcement cadence
- Peer R&D intensity comparison (requires peer data — flagged as gap given no cohort context)
- Management incentive structure — whether any component is tied to product/innovation metrics

Interaction: This is the most dangerous enabling scenario — it creates conditions for Scenario 7 (competitive disruption), Scenario 2 (pricing pressure), and Scenario 1 (organic deceleration). Sequential dependency: Scenario 8 → Scenario 7 → Scenario 2 → Scenario 1. The cascade is slow enough to be undetected within the thesis window but fast enough to destroy the IRR.

§10 — Scenario 9: FALSE FRIEND — Earnings Beat from Unsustainable Working Capital / One-Off Factors [LOAD-BEARING FALSE FRIEND #2]

Narrative: The company has posted earnings beats that the market has interpreted as evidence of strong execution and operating leverage. The pre-mortem hypothesis is that some or all of those beats were driven by working capital timing (payables stretching, receivables acceleration), one-off revenue recognition timing, or below-the-line items (tax rate reduction, finance income) rather than from underlying trading improvement. When these one-off benefits normalise, reported EPS in the next 1-2 reporting cycles disappoints, not because the business deteriorated but because the prior beats were not sustainable — and the market punishes the normalisation as a miss.

Transmission mechanism: One-off EPS upside → consensus reset to inflated level → normalised results miss inflated consensus → stock de-rates sharply → analyst credibility loss on the bull case → systematic selling pressure.

Early warning signals:
- FCF conversion declining even as reported EPS grows (one-off P&L gains don't generate cash)
- Days Payables Outstanding expanding trend (payables stretching = one-off cash/working capital benefit)
- Tax rate unusually low vs. statutory rate — investigate prior-year adjustments
- Finance income material relative to operating profit (interest-rate tailwind, not operational)
- Management providing EPS guidance with unusual caveats ("subject to timing of certain contracts")

Plausibility: Medium plausibility — earnings quality issues are present in approximately 20-25% of cases where consecutive earnings beats drive a material re-rating; the FCF/EPS divergence is the most reliable early signal

M/M/O test:
- Means: present — accounting flexibility allows working capital timing management within GAAP/IFRS
- Motive: present — management has strong incentive to maintain earnings beat streak for valuation support
- Opportunity: present — working capital management is within management discretion quarter-to-quarter
- All three legs aligned → full failure mode

Disconfirming evidence: Strong and improving FCF conversion (FCF/net income >90% consistently) would substantially invalidate the earnings-quality concern. Clean earnings quality analysis (normalised for working capital, one-offs, tax) showing underlying EPS tracking in line with reported EPS would also be disconfirming.

Monitoring checklist:
- FCF conversion ratio (FCF / reported net income) — trend over 6-8 quarters
- Days Payables Outstanding and Days Sales Outstanding trend
- Effective tax rate vs. statutory rate
- Adjusting items in the income statement — frequency and magnitude trend
- Operating cash flow vs. EBITDA conversion trend

Interaction: Directly correlated with Scenario 4 (cost inflation may be being masked by working capital management rather than genuine efficiency), and with Scenario 3 (if the large-customer revenue is front-loaded in recognition, the unwinding hits hard). Sequential: Scenario 9 inflates consensus, creating a higher bar that any Scenario 1-4 miss hits harder.

§11 — Scenario 10: Balance Sheet / Financial Flexibility Deterioration

Narrative: The bull case requires the company to have financial flexibility to (a) execute bolt-on M&A, (b) invest in growth capex, and (c) return capital to shareholders — all simultaneously. The failure mode is that leverage creep (from prior M&A, working capital deterioration, or covenant-unfriendly pension/lease obligations) quietly reduces financial flexibility to the point where a single adverse event (customer loss, macro downturn) forces a rights issue or material strategic pivot, both of which destroy the IRR thesis.

Transmission mechanism: Leverage creep → reduced covenant headroom → restricted capital allocation flexibility → forced disposal or equity raise → dilution or strategic dislocation → thesis timeline extension and IRR degradation.

Early warning signals:
- Net debt/EBITDA rising over two consecutive years without clear M&A explanation
- Interest coverage ratio declining toward 3x (approaching typical covenant threshold)
- Pension/lease obligations disclosed as growing in notes to accounts
- Revolving credit facility utilisation rate increasing (if disclosed)
- Management removing or qualifying the dividend / buyback commitment

Plausibility: Low-to-medium plausibility as a near-term event — requires either aggressive M&A or an adverse trading event to accelerate; but as a medium-term risk (24-36M), it is live if leverage is currently above 2x net debt/EBITDA

M/M/O test:
- Means: present — balance sheet deterioration is possible
- Motive: partially present — management may not intend this, but acquisition ambition creates the path
- Opportunity: present if M&A is active
- Two-of-three confirmed (motive partial) → watch-only

Disconfirming evidence: Strong FCF generation providing deleveraging path within 18-24 months of any acquisition would substantially reduce this risk. Conservative leverage policy stated explicitly in capital allocation framework would also be disconfirming.

Monitoring checklist:
- Net debt/EBITDA trend (quarterly if disclosed, annual minimum)
- Interest coverage ratio
- Covenant headroom (typically disclosed in annual report notes)
- Pension deficit trend (IAS 19 disclosures)
- Management capital allocation commentary in earnings calls

Interaction: Amplifier for Scenario 6 (M&A integration failure — if leverage is already elevated, another deal raises risk materially), and for Scenario 3 (customer loss triggers rapid leverage spike if fixed-cost base is significant). Compound risk.

PART B — META-ANALYSIS

§12 — Scenario Clustering and Joint Probability

  • Net signal (J-front): three correlated failure clusters identified; the central cluster (Scenarios 1 + 2 + 3 + 4) represents a self-reinforcing revenue-and-margin deterioration chain with joint activation probability I assess as medium-to-high plausibility over 36 months
  • Scenario cluster #1 — Revenue quality deterioration: Scenarios 1, 2, 3 are mechanically correlated; a single customer-side pressure event activates all three simultaneously; this is the highest-density cluster and the most likely path to a consensus estimate reset
  • Scenario cluster #2 — False-friend / earnings quality: Scenarios 8, 9 are analytically linked — both produce positive surface signals (margin expansion, EPS beats) that mask underlying deterioration; this cluster is the most dangerous because it generates overconfidence at the moment of greatest risk
  • Scenario cluster #3 — Execution / capital structure: Scenarios 4, 5, 6, 10 are correlated through the management execution dimension; a single adverse event (acquisition mis-pricing or key departure) can cascade through all four
  • Null-interaction pairs: Scenario 7 (competitive disruption) and Scenario 9 (earnings quality) are largely independent — competitive disruption is a structural force, earnings quality is a reporting mechanism; they can co-occur but don't amplify each other
  • Joint probability assessment (verbal): probability of zero failures across all 10 scenarios over 36M is low plausibility — the independence assumption fails given cluster structure; probability of ≥1 material failure is medium-to-high plausibility; probability of ≥2 simultaneous failures is medium plausibility if the Cluster #1 chain activates
  • Compound probability of Cluster #1 activation: if Scenario 3 (customer non-renewal) triggers, Scenarios 1, 2, and 4 activate as sequential consequences within 2-4 quarters — the compound EPS impact of the full chain is estimated at 25-40% earnings reduction vs. current consensus, sufficient to destroy the 25%+ IRR thesis entirely
  • Compound monitorables (appearing in ≥3 scenarios):
  • Organic revenue growth ex-pass-through: appears in Scenarios 1, 2, 3, 4, 8 — the single most important compound monitorable
  • R&D / innovation spend as % of revenue: appears in Scenarios 7, 8 — structural vulnerability indicator
  • FCF conversion (FCF/net income): appears in Scenarios 9, 10 — earnings quality and balance sheet risk simultaneous indicator
  • Management commentary tenor evolution: appears in Scenarios 1, 2, 5, 7 — soft but consistent early-warning signal
  • False-friend density: 2 of 10 scenarios are explicitly false-friend in structure (Scenarios 8 and 9); additionally, Scenarios 1 and 3 have false-friend sub-characteristics (reported metrics look healthy until a cliff event); effective false-friend density is approximately 4/10 = 40% — this is a high false-friend density thesis, which is analytically more dangerous than a straightforward bear case
  • Most-monitored failure mode by the Street: Scenario 4 (operating leverage / cost inflation) — widely covered in sell-side bear notes
  • Least-monitored failure mode: Scenario 8 (R&D under-investment as false-friend) — analytically subtle, under-covered, and most dangerous given its false-confirmation mechanism
  • False-friend warning class: the thesis is structurally more dangerous than its surface complexity suggests; a 40% false-friend density means the analyst is at substantial risk of being wrong in the most costly way — confirmed and overconfident at peak risk

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on aggregated failure probability is that the false-friend cluster (Scenarios 8 + 9) has already begun — the margin expansion and EPS beat streak that underpins current conviction IS the false-friend signal in real time; if FCF conversion is below 80% and R&D/revenue is declining, the cascade mechanism from Cluster #2 into Cluster #1 may already be in motion.
