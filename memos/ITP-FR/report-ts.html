Section 3 – What the company does 13 findings

Section summary
  1. The company operates an asset-light licensing model, converting luxury brand equity into high-margin global wholesale revenue.

Prestige brand portfolio and licensing economics

1.
Core prestige business: The company designs and markets fragrances for some of the world's most recognizable luxury names, including Jimmy Choo, Coach, and Montblanc. It targets the premium and selective luxury segments of the global beauty market
2.
Dominant brand concentration: Its three largest licensed brands Montblanc, Jimmy Choo, and Coach represent 68.90% of total sales. Jimmy Choo alone generated €227.90m in sales in FY2025, which is 25.30% of total revenue
3.
Coach fragrance momentum: Coach brand sales grew to €200.00m in 2025, representing 22.20% of total sales. This performance was supported by strong brand equity and the H1 2025 launch of Coach Women Gold
4.
Licensing contractual structures: Revenue is generated by selling finished prestige fragrances directly to department stores, specialty retailers, and travel retail operators. The company pays royalties to brand owners, which averaged 8.20% of net sales in late 2025
5.
Owned brand exposure: In addition to licensed brands, the company owns certain historic houses outright, including Rochas and Lanvin. Rochas and Lanvin each generated €41.00m in sales in FY2025, representing 4.60% of total revenue respectively
6.
Ultra-luxury niche expansion: The company has launched Solférino, a direct-to-consumer high-end fragrance house designed to target the high-growth niche market. It reached 40 doors globally by late 2025 and plans to expand further to capture luxury consumers

Industry value chain and competitive advantages

7.
Asset-light operating structure: The company maintains zero owned manufacturing facilities, acting instead as a general contractor. It coordinates raw material sourcing, packaging development, and final production with third-party suppliers
8.
Highly favored position: This structure insulates the company from heavy capital expenditures and fixed manufacturing costs. It enjoys superior cash generation and high return on capital compared to vertically integrated peers
9.
Durable switching barriers: High barriers to entry protect the licensing model as fashion houses lack the specialized R&D and distribution networks to launch fragrances independently. They rely on a limited set of global partners like Interparfums
10.
Consolidated industry landscape: The prestige beauty sector is highly concentrated, with a few scaled players dominating global fragrance shelf space. Interparfums competes for exclusive long-term worldwide fragrance license agreements against giant conglomerates like Coty and L'Oreal
11.
Predictable contractual duration: The company's licenses are long-term, providing exceptional cash flow predictability. The Lacoste license runs for 15 years through 2038, while Jimmy Choo is secured through 2031
12.
Other side of the story: Highly concentrated brand dependencies expose the company to material downside if a key license is lost or not renewed. If the Montblanc or Coach relationship deteriorates, it would immediately disrupt more than 20.00% of total revenue

Section 7 – Key risks and the other side 8 findings

Section summary
  1. The overall risk profile is well-mitigated by long-term contract lengths, strong cash buffers, and high beauty category resilience.

License expiration and concentration threats

1.
Severe brand concentration: The company faces high brand concentration across three core licenses Montblanc, Jimmy Choo, and Coach, which drive 68.90% of total revenue. Any failure to renew these core licenses would result in structural earnings destruction
2.
License expiration timeline: Although major licenses have been extended, they face eventual expiration, with Montblanc expiring in 2030 and Jimmy Choo in 2031. This requires constant negotiation and financial commitment
3.
False friend signals: Solid top-line growth could mask underlying margin deterioration if the company relies heavily on highly promotional mass-market channels. Mass market expansion often weakens brand equity and dilutes long-term premium pricing power

Geopolitical and supply chain headwinds

4.
Geopolitical sales exposure: Ongoing conflicts in the Middle East represent a significant headwind, dragging consolidated sales down by 1.00% in Q1 2026. Middle East sales for European operations fell 14.00% due to regional disruptions
5.
Supply chain vulnerabilities: Relying entirely on outsourced manufacturing exposes the company to supplier service issues or production bottlenecks during massive launch cycles. Price increases for raw materials could also compress gross margins if not deferred
6.
Critical warning signal: Investors should closely monitor inventory days on hand, which stood at 259 days in early 2026. A sharp build-up in inventory would indicate weak consumer sell-out and upcoming margin pressure
7.
Other side of the story: The risk of sudden license termination is extremely low because fashion houses face prohibitive costs and operational complexity in vertically integrating beauty operations. Interparfums' decades of technical expertise make it an indispensable strategic partner
