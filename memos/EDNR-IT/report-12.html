§2 — Switching Costs + Pricing Leverage

  • Overall verdict: Switching costs are genuinely high across multiple dimensions — qualification cycles, embedded software, and cross-functional integration — giving Edison real but not unconstrained pricing leverage [#J]

  • Switching cost — qualification: Customer qualification processes for Edison's engineering and technology services engagements are typically 12-24 months — consistent with European industrial services peers where qualification windows of 12-18 months are standard for critical infrastructure customers [#A]

  • Sub: A 12-24 month qualification cycle means a customer who begins evaluating alternatives today cannot operationally switch for at least 12 months — this is a structural moat layer [#J]

  • Switching cost — integration: Edison's software and technology components appear to be embedded within customer operational systems, creating integration-layer switching costs — ripping out and replacing requires system reconfiguration, data migration, and retraining [#J]

  • Sub: The depth of integration switching costs depends on the share of "platform" vs "project" revenue in Edison's mix — platform revenue carries deeper switching costs than one-off project delivery [#A]

  • Switching cost — training: Customer engineering and operational teams trained on Edison's systems require retraining to adopt alternatives — moderate switching cost layer, less decisive than qualification and integration but additive [#A]

  • Switching cost — product: Edison's specialist product segments appear to have proprietary technical specifications that are not directly compatible with alternatives — customers would face specification-change costs as well as switching costs [#J]

  • Sub: Proprietary specifications are a two-edged sword — they lock in existing customers but can limit addressable market expansion [#A]

  • Pricing pass-through: Based on earnings call commentary and sell-side broker analysis, Edison has demonstrated partial pricing pass-through over the 2021-23 inflationary period — able to recover a portion of input cost increases in customer pricing [#A]

  • Sub: "Partial" pass-through (estimated 50-70% recovery based on gross margin trajectory) is below the strongest-positioned European industrial peers who achieved 80-90% recovery — Edison is mid-range on this dimension [#A]

  • Customer pushback: CEO and CFO commentary acknowledges that certain large customers "engage constructively on pricing" — language that signals active negotiation rather than unconstrained pricing power [#J]

  • Sub: "Constructive engagement" is management euphemism for customer pushback; the actual concession depth is not disclosed [#A]

  • Renewal pricing: No explicit disclosure of renewal-price uplift rates; the lack of disclosed pricing metrics (e.g. revenue per unit, average contract value trend) limits precision here — a gap for DD [#J]

  • Competitive bidding: No explicit evidence of competitive RFP pressure in public disclosures, but the absence of disclosure does not mean RFP pressure is absent — in a tighter procurement environment, large European customers are increasingly benchmarking incumbent suppliers [#J]

  • Sub: European utilities and infrastructure operators have become more procurement-disciplined post-2022 — even where they renew incumbents, they are seeking more competitive pricing [#A]

  • Volume loss signal: No publicly disclosed volume losses; revenue trajectory is consistent with stable-to-growing volumes in core segments, which is indirect evidence against meaningful pricing concession + volume loss dynamics [#A]

  • Concession signal: The slight gross margin softening visible in Edison's financials (approx. 50-100bps over the observable period) is consistent with either input cost absorption or modest pricing concessions — likely a combination [#J]

  • Sub: Distinguishing input-cost absorption from customer-driven concessions requires segment-level gross margin data not publicly available [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on switching costs and pricing leverage is that switching costs are high but not absolute; for sufficiently large customers (which constitute 35-45% of revenue by the §1 estimate), the ROI of switching becomes positive if Edison's pricing exceeds alternatives by more than the switching cost — and large customers have procurement teams whose job is to make that calculation; the risk is not imminent defection but slow, grinding price erosion at each renewal [#J]
