3. THEME §1 — REVENUE DRIVERS + VISIBILITY

J-front verdict: Revenue-side uncertainty is HIGH on liver/kidney timing and MODERATE on lung; most diagnostic lever is the consumables attachment rate, which management controls the narrative on but which the model is load-bearing on

Q1 — Consumables attachment rate, US Lung

  • Question: "What is the current consumables-per-installed-device ratio in your US Lung installed base, and how has that trended over the past four quarters?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely give a directional positive ("attachment improving") but deflect on specific ratio
  • Good answer signature: Specific ratio cited (e.g. "~3 procedures per device per quarter, up from ~2.2 a year ago") + explanation of which centre archetypes drive the highest utilisation
  • Bad answer signature: "We don't track it that way" or "volumes are growing" without ratio granularity — suggests model is not being managed at unit-economics level
  • Follow-up probe: "What's the utilisation rate at your top-quartile centres vs median, and what drives the gap?"
  • Diagnostic note: High-specificity answer supports bull case (management has operational grip); vague answer supports false-friend (growth headline masks unit-economics weakness); this is the single highest-variance question for the razor/razor-blade P&L model

Q2 — Liver: procedural adoption slope

  • Question: "For XVIVO LIVER, what does a typical centre's ramp from first case to steady-state procedural volume look like — months and procedure count?"
  • Expected answer: 6-18 month ramp citing training and protocol standardisation as gating factors
  • Good answer signature: Specific ramp curve with named reference centres + identification of which adoption blockers management has already solved vs unsolved
  • Bad answer signature: "Early days, centres are enthusiastic" — narrative without ramp data; suggests liver is still a sales story, not an operational story
  • Follow-up probe: "Which of your first 10 XVIVO LIVER centres have now reached what you'd call steady-state volume, and what's that volume?"
  • Diagnostic note: Slow ramp (>18M to steady state) supports the bear case on near-term liver revenue contribution; fast ramp (<9M) with specifics supports bull; answer also discriminates vs OrganOx NMP adoption curves

Q3 — US vs EU revenue concentration risk

  • Question: "What share of 2025 revenues do you expect from the US, and what single-centre or single-country concentration would worry you internally?"
  • Expected answer: US growing toward ~40-50% of revenues; management likely comfortable with concentration
  • Good answer signature: Explicit concentration thresholds named + commentary on which markets are structurally independent of US reimbursement
  • Bad answer signature: US concentration rising without management acknowledging the binary risk of CMS coverage policy changes
  • Follow-up probe: "If US Medicare reimbursement policy for perfusion-assisted transplant changed materially, what % of your 2026 revenue would be at risk within 12 months?"
  • Diagnostic note: Reimbursement-dependency question is underasked in consensus coverage; high US concentration without reimbursement hedge is a false-friend flag

Q4 — Forward order book / backlog visibility

  • Backlog visibility: "Do you have a contractual order book for OCS devices, and if so, what is the current book-to-bill?"
  • Expected answer: Device backlog limited; consumables are effectively recurring but non-contracted
  • Good answer signature: Acknowledgement of lack of hard backlog + explanation of leading indicators management uses (centre pipeline, training queue, LOI/MOU count) as proxy visibility
  • Bad answer signature: Overstating revenue visibility on a business that is structurally non-contracted — would flag a credibility concern
  • Follow-up probe: "How many centres are in your training pipeline today that have NOT yet done a first case?"
  • Diagnostic note: A long training pipeline with low first-case conversion is an adoption bottleneck signal; a short training pipeline with high conversion is a capacity-constrained demand signal

Q5 — Kidney: timeline and revenue contribution

  • Question: "What is the gating factor for OCS Kidney achieving CE-marked commercialisation, and what revenue run-rate do you model at full EU commercial launch?"
  • Expected answer: CE mark 2026-2027; revenue contribution modest initially
  • Good answer signature: Named regulatory milestones + a realistic revenue contribution range without hockey-stick framing
  • Bad answer signature: Optimistic timeline without named milestones or deflection to "it's too early to model" — the latter is avoidance, not humility
  • Follow-up probe: "Is the gating factor clinical data, regulatory review, or commercial readiness — and which is on the critical path today?"
  • Diagnostic note: Timeline slippage on Kidney would not materially harm 2025 numbers but would compress the 3Y revenue CAGR case; tests management's disciplined honesty on pipeline

Sceptical bullet: Fluent answers on revenue drivers in specialty medtech frequently reflect rehearsal for investor roadshows, not operational mastery; the diagnostic test is whether management can give a SPECIFIC number they didn't pre-prepare — probe with the follow-ups, not the headline questions

4. THEME §2 — MARGIN TRAJECTORY + LEVERS

J-front verdict: Gross margin trajectory is the highest-uncertainty financial variable for XVIVO; device vs consumable mix and manufacturing scale are the key levers, but neither has been disclosed with sufficient granularity to close the model**

Q6 — Gross margin by product line

  • Question: "Can you give us gross margin by product line — OCS device vs OCS consumable — and how those margins have trended over the past two years?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely decline to give full segmentation but may indicate consumables carry higher margin than devices
  • Good answer signature: Directional split provided (even approximate — "consumables at least 15-20pp above device margin") + explanation of what drives the spread (COGS structure, manufacturing leverage)
  • Bad answer signature: "We don't break that out" with no directional guidance — opaque gross-margin structure in a consumable-heavy business model raises quality concerns
  • Follow-up probe: "As liver procedures grow as a share of the mix, is the per-procedure gross profit higher, lower, or similar to lung?"
  • Diagnostic note: Liver/lung gross-margin comparison tests whether revenue mix-shift is margin-accretive or dilutive — this is the single most load-bearing unknown in the 3Y margin model

Q7 — Manufacturing scale and fixed-cost absorption

  • Question: "At what revenue level does your current manufacturing footprint reach full utilisation, and what does the next capacity increment cost?"
  • Expected answer: Current capacity headroom is meaningful; next increment likely mid-scale (not a step-change CapEx)
  • GM driver: High-specificity answer here determines whether gross margin can expand meaningfully or whether capex steps interrupt the margin ramp
  • Good answer signature: Named utilisation % ("we're at roughly 60% of rated capacity") + next increment sized in SEK terms
  • Bad answer signature: "We have plenty of capacity" without specifics — non-answer on a question that is directly financial-model-relevant
  • Follow-up probe: "Is your manufacturing footprint in Gothenburg the long-term home of production scale-up, or is there a geographic diversification rationale you're considering?"
  • Diagnostic note: Geographic manufacturing concentration (all in Gothenburg) is an unpriced operational risk; management's awareness of it signals risk culture

Q8 — Operating leverage: when does it show up?

  • Question: "At what revenue level do you expect operating margin to inflect positively — and what are the two or three cost lines that drive that inflection?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite a revenue threshold (likely 800-1,000 MSEK range as indicative) + point to R&D and SG&A as the scale-sensitive lines
  • Op leverage: The answer tests whether management has a specific operating leverage model or is running on narrative
  • Good answer signature: Named revenue threshold + quantification of the operating leverage ("each 10% revenue growth at this stage drops X% to operating profit") + identification of the R&D-as-% lever
  • Bad answer signature: "We're investing for growth" without any inflection horizon — standard growth-company deflection; signals no near-term margin discipline
  • Follow-up probe: "How much of your current R&D spend is committed to programmes that will generate revenue in the next 24 months vs programmes that are 5+ years from commercialisation?"
  • Diagnostic note: R&D efficiency decomposition tests whether the P&L is investable on a 24M horizon or is a longer-duration bet; directly relevant to the 25% IRR hurdle

Q9 — FX pass-through

  • FX pass-through: "How much of your cost base is USD-denominated, and do your OCS pricing contracts allow you to pass through USD COGS inflation to customers?"
  • Expected answer: Material USD COGS exposure (components); pricing is largely EUR/SEK contract-based with limited pass-through clauses
  • Good answer signature: Quantified FX sensitivity (e.g. "1 SEK/USD move = X MSEK on gross margin") + evidence of pricing renegotiation success in recent years
  • Bad answer signature: "FX is not a major factor" — implausible for a company with global manufacturing supply chain and Swedish-krona reporting
  • Diagnostic note: FX sensitivity is under-modelled by consensus in Swedish medtech; a bad answer here is a credibility flag, not just a modelling gap

Sceptical bullet: Management fluency on margins may reflect awareness that margins are the principal investor concern — the most diagnostic follow-up is always asking for a NUMBER, not a direction

5. THEME §3 — CAPITAL ALLOCATION + BALANCE SHEET

J-front verdict: Capital allocation discipline is UNVERIFIED at DD depth; XVIVO's cash generation remains modest relative to investment needs and the M&A/partnership philosophy has not been stress-tested publicly**

Q10 — Returns hurdle and M&A philosophy

  • Question: "What is your internal returns hurdle for capital deployment — organic CapEx, M&A, or partnerships — and how did you apply it in your most recent capital decision?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite an IRR threshold or WACC-plus-spread; most likely in the 15-20% IRR range for organic, higher for M&A
  • RONIC discipline: The most revealing element is whether management can name a specific recent decision where the hurdle was applied AND a decision they walked away from
  • Good answer signature: Named hurdle + named decision where discipline was exercised ("we looked at X acquisition but walked away because returns didn't clear our threshold")
  • Bad answer signature: Generic "we're disciplined allocators" without a single named example — standard defensive response; reveals no actual hurdle discipline
  • Follow-up probe: "Has XVIVO ever paid for a capability it subsequently decided was non-core, and what was the lesson?"
  • Diagnostic note: Historical capital-allocation errors that management acknowledges openly are a strong quality signal — absence of any acknowledged error is a weak credibility signal

Q11 — Partnership model vs own-commercialisation

  • Question: "Is the long-term commercialisation model for XVIVO fully self-owned, or do you see partnership/distribution structures for certain geographies or organ types?"
  • Expected answer: Management will assert direct commercialisation preference but acknowledge distribution partnerships in specific markets (Asia, LatAm)
  • Investment runway: Fully self-owned commercialisation requires ongoing SG&A investment; distribution dilutes margin but reduces capital intensity
  • Good answer signature: Clear framework for when XVIVO goes direct vs uses distribution + named markets in each category
  • Bad answer signature: "We prefer direct" without any geographic or organ-specific qualification — a non-answer on a question that has major P&L implications
  • Follow-up probe: "In Japan specifically, what is the regulatory and commercial pathway, and is a partnership the most capital-efficient route?"
  • Diagnostic note: Japan/Asia pathway is underexplored in consensus; clarity here could expand the TAM narrative materially

Q12 — Balance sheet adequacy and dilution risk

  • Question: "Given your current cash burn trajectory, at what point would you consider returning to equity markets, and what conditions would trigger that decision?"
  • Expected answer: Management will assert current cash is sufficient; will resist discussing dilution scenarios
  • Leverage target: Willingness to name a cash-floor threshold and dilution trigger is a strong signal of financial discipline
  • Good answer signature: Named minimum cash buffer ("we operate with at least X months of operating expense in cash") + clear articulation of conditions (e.g. "a transformational M&A opportunity" vs "operating needs")
  • Bad answer signature: "We don't need to raise capital" without any conditions — leaves the investor unable to model the probability of dilutive scenarios
  • Diagnostic note: Concentration investors with 25%+ IRR hurdles are particularly sensitive to dilution risk at higher multiples; this question directly tests the downside scenario valuation

Sceptical bullet: Swedish management teams are often structurally reluctant to discuss dilution risk in investor meetings — the non-answer is itself signal; a CFO who can answer this crisply is a quality marker

6. THEME §4 — STRATEGY + COMPETITIVE POSITION

J-front verdict: Competitive-position questions are the most rehearsed in management's toolkit; the diagnostic value lies in probing specific competitive DECISIONS, not general moat narratives**

Q13 — OrganOx competitive response

  • Question: "OrganOx's MERIT trial results have strengthened NMP as a standard of care narrative for liver — how does XVIVO LIVER differentiate, and where will you NOT compete with OrganOx?"
  • Expected answer: Management will emphasise multi-organ capability, system integration (one platform across organ types), and clinical flexibility as differentiators
  • Moat trajectory: The answer tests whether management has a specific competitive-response playbook or is relying on first-mover advantages that OrganOx has contested
  • Good answer signature: Identification of ONE specific clinical scenario where XVIVO LIVER materially outperforms NMP + frank acknowledgement of where OrganOx has an entrenched position
  • Bad answer signature: "We're complementary, not competitive" — a non-answer that may be diplomatically correct but strategically evasive
  • Follow-up probe: "In a centre that already has OrganOx installed, what is your win rate on converting to XVIVO LIVER, and what's the primary objection you overcome?"
  • Diagnostic note: Win rate at OrganOx-installed centres is the single most diagnostic competitive metric for the liver business; management's willingness to give a number is a credibility test

Q14 — TransMedics US lung competitive position

  • Question: "TransMedics has built an OCS Lung position in the US with its Organ Care program bundling logistics — does XVIVO intend to offer a comparable bundled service, or stay equipment-only?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely emphasise clinical differentiation and resist the bundled-logistics model as capital-intensive
  • Competitive response: TMDX's logistics integration is a moat-extension play that XVIVO has not replicated — the question tests whether management has a strategic answer or is in denial
  • Good answer signature: Honest acknowledgement of the TMDX logistics moat + a specific articulation of why XVIVO's approach is differentiated enough to win without logistics
  • Bad answer signature: "Our clinical outcomes speak for themselves" without addressing the bundle question — suggests management is not engaging with the competitive threat at strategic depth
  • Diagnostic note: ⚠️ TMDX is the closest listed comparable (peer context flag) but operates in a partly different commercial model; do not assume full transferability of TMDX dynamics to XVIVO's European-origin base

Q15 — Regulatory positioning: FDA IDE/PMA pipeline

  • Regulatory positioning: "What is the current FDA regulatory pathway status for XVIVO LIVER in the US, and what is the minimum clinical evidence package required for PMA submission?"
  • Expected answer: US liver regulatory pathway is pre-IDE or early IDE stage; PMA submission likely 3-5 years out
  • Good answer signature: Named trial design + patient numbers required + expected IDE filing timeline
  • Bad answer signature: "We're working with FDA" without a named milestone or timeline — signals regulatory uncertainty that the market may not be pricing
  • Follow-up probe: "Has the FDA expressed any preference for your trial design compared to the OrganOx PROTECT-OLT design?"
  • Diagnostic note: FDA design precedent from OrganOx's experience is a publicly-available anchor; management's awareness of it tests regulatory preparedness depth

Sceptical bullet: Strategic fluency does not equal strategic correctness; the most rehearsed answers (TAM, differentiation, pipeline) are also the most likely to contain motivated reasoning — probe the SPECIFIC competitive DECISION, not the narrative

7. THEME §5 — MANAGEMENT QUALITY + OPERATOR TRACK RECORD

J-front verdict: Management quality is HIGH on clinical credibility but UNVERIFIED on operational-scale and capital-allocation track record; incentive structure is the under-interrogated dimension**

Q16 — LTI structure and incentive alignment

  • Power of incentives: "Can you walk us through the CEO and CFO LTI vesting conditions — specifically, are the performance metrics revenue-based, margin-based, or share-price-based, and what are the cliff and vest dates?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely cite a mix of revenue growth and TSR conditions; margin conditions are less common in Swedish medtech LTI structures
  • Incentive probe: Revenue-only LTI with no margin component creates a structural incentive to grow revenue at any gross-margin cost — precisely the false-friend risk for this business model
  • Good answer signature: LTI conditions that include at least one margin or return metric + meaningful CEO/CFO equity ownership (>1% of shares outstanding)
  • Bad answer signature: Revenue-only LTI + low personal equity ownership + short vesting window (<3 years) — the full false-friend incentive alignment
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the CEO's and CFO's personal equity ownership as a percentage of shares outstanding today, inclusive of unvested awards?"
  • Diagnostic note: Equity ownership level is publicly available in proxy filings but the vesting-condition split is often only disclosed at a high level; getting specifics in this meeting is a data advantage

Q17 — Sandbagging discipline

  • Sandbagging discipline: "Looking back at the guidance you gave 12 months ago — revenue, gross margin, key milestones — where did you beat, where did you miss, and what drove each?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely acknowledge delays on specific milestones while citing revenue growth as on-track
  • Operator track record: The pattern of beats and misses is more diagnostic than the direction — systematic sandbagging (always beating) is a quality signal; systematic optimism (always missing milestones) is a red flag
  • Good answer signature: Frank attribution of misses to named causes + evidence that the same cause has been remediated ("we missed the Q3 centre certification milestone because of X; we've since changed the training process to Y")
  • Bad answer signature: "We've delivered on our commitments" without engaging with the specific miss question — evasion that confirms optimism bias
  • Follow-up probe: "On the XVIVO LIVER clinical timeline — what is the range of outcomes, and what's the single most likely cause of a 12-month delay?"
  • Diagnostic note: Management's ability to name a specific realistic failure mode is one of the strongest quality signals available — operators who've stress-tested their own plan can name the failure mode

Q18 — Culture archetype: how does management handle the "no" from a customer?

  • Culture archetype: "When a major transplant centre tells you they won't adopt OCS for the next 12 months, what does your team do differently in month 2 vs month 1 of that 'no'?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely describe a consultative re-engagement process — clinical data sharing, peer-to-peer surgeon outreach
  • Animal-CEO probe: This question tests whether the sales-and-adoption culture is consultative (appropriate for high-complexity clinical adoption) vs transactional (a mismatch)
  • Good answer signature: Specific example of a "no" that converted + the exact intervention that changed the decision
  • Bad answer signature: Generic "we focus on education and data" — rehearsed; tells nothing about the actual culture
  • Diagnostic note: The adoption-culture question is the single best proxy for the commercial-execution quality; clinical-sale culture is the moat, not the technology alone

Sceptical bullet: Management quality is the hardest dimension to assess in a single meeting; calibrate heavily on SPECIFIC examples and numbers rather than descriptive responses

8. THEME §6 — RISK + DOWNSIDE (PRE-MORTEM CROSS-REF)

J-front verdict: The three highest-plausibility failure modes are (1) liver adoption slower than priced, (2) US reimbursement policy change, and (3) competitive bundling by TMDX/OrganOx that commoditises OCS as hardware**

Q19 — Bear-case probe: what does the 3Y downside scenario look like in management's own framing?

  • Bear-case probe: "If you had to construct the bear case for XVIVO in 2027 — what's the scenario, and how would it unfold? What would be the first observable signal that bear case was playing out?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely cite regulatory delay and macro hospital-budget pressure as the bear case
  • Failure mode test: The quality signal is whether management's bear case matches the analyst's bear case; divergence is a flag
  • Good answer signature: A bear case that includes competitive response (not just internal/regulatory risk) + a named early-warning indicator ("if our Q2 consumable reorder rate drops below X, that's the first signal")
  • Bad answer signature: A bear case that only includes macro or regulatory risk without naming competitive or adoption risk — motivated self-censorship
  • Follow-up probe: "What single competitor action, if you observed it tomorrow, would most worry you and force a strategic response?"
  • Diagnostic note: Management's competitive awareness test — the answer discriminates between operators who are actively stress-testing their position and those who are narrating their strengths

Q20 — Reimbursement fragility

  • Pricing fragility: "US DRG/bundled payment dynamics — are OCS perfusion costs separately reimbursed, included in transplant bundle, or at transplant centre's cost? And how has that reimbursement status changed in the last 24 months?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely describe a mixed landscape — some centres absorbing cost in bundle, others with add-on billing; trend toward improved coverage
  • Regulatory exposure: Reimbursement fragility is the highest-impact single-event risk; management's granularity on the current landscape is diagnostic of how well they understand their own business model's vulnerability
  • Good answer signature: Specific breakdown of what % of US procedures have favourable separate reimbursement vs are bundled-absorbed + named CMS or MAC policy reference
  • Bad answer signature: "Reimbursement is improving" without specifics — standard deflection; this is a HIGHLY specific question that requires a specific answer
  • Follow-up probe: "If CMS changed DRG policy to bundle OCS as standard supply in transplant reimbursement, what would happen to your average selling price?"
  • Diagnostic note: This is a low-probability, high-impact scenario that consensus does not explicitly price; management's answer reveals both knowledge and risk culture

9. §8 — CROSS-CUTTING META + NON-OBVIOUS QUESTIONS

J-front verdict: Questions management WOULDN'T volunteer centre on competitive-loss data, incentive structure tensions, and the unit-economics of a centre that churned — these are the load-bearing diagnostic questions that no roadshow prep includes**

  • Non-obvious angle: "Have you ever lost a centre that was previously a regular XVIVO user — not a trial, but an established user — and if so, what was the reason?"

  • This question is deliberately uncomfortable; management will be surprised by it; the answer is maximally diagnostic — a centre that churned reveals product, service, or competitive vulnerabilities that no bull-case narrative addresses

  • WH Smith probe: "If you woke up tomorrow as the CEO of TransMedics, what would you do to most effectively harm XVIVO's position in the next 18 months?"

  • Forces management to think from the competitor's perspective; a high-quality operator will give a specific, credible answer; an evasive or generic answer signals either denial or strategic shallowness

  • Cross-roads question: "Is XVIVO a diagnostics/data company that happens to use hardware as its delivery mechanism, or is it a hardware company with a recurring consumable model — and does it matter which?"

  • Tests strategic clarity on the business model's long-term identity; data/digital adjacencies (monitoring, outcomes analytics, AI-assisted viability assessment) represent a potential moat extension or distraction; management's answer reveals whether they've done the strategic work

  • Edge-detection probe: "What is the single metric, if it moved against you, that would most concern you — and what is its current trend?"

  • The answer reveals what management's internal early-warning dashboard actually looks like; compare to the analyst's own model of the most-sensitive variable

  • Incentive probe: "When did you last personally buy XVIVO shares in the open market, and at what price?"

  • Open-market purchases by CEO/CFO are among the highest-quality insider-commitment signals; absence of recent purchases at current price levels is soft but relevant signal

  • Multiple-truths probe (Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 3): "I accept the bull case — strong clinical evidence, growing installed base, regulatory moat. AND YET, gross margins have not expanded at the rate the original model implied, and adoption is slower than 3 years ago guidance suggested. Which of those two 'and yets' concerns you most, and what's the honest explanation?"

  • The "and yet" frame forces management out of either-or framing; a bull-and-bear co-existing answer is the most information-rich response available; a management team that can hold both truths simultaneously is a quality signal

  • Diagnostic crystalliser: "If this investor meeting were the last one before a 3-year lockup — what one thing would you most want us to understand that we probably don't?"

  • Open-ended but time-constrained; surfaces the information management WANTS to transmit but hasn't been asked; often reveals the slide that's not in the deck

  • Stewing trigger: "What has surprised you most — negatively — about the pace of market adoption in the last 12 months, and what did you change as a result?"

  • Forces disclosure of a negative surprise; management's framing of what counts as "negative" and whether they changed anything is the diagnostic signal, not the surprise itself

  • Multiple-truths probe — CFO-specific: "Your revenue is growing double-digits and your cash burn is still material — walk me through the cash-conversion inflection. At what revenue level does XVIVO become sustainably free-cash-flow positive, and what's the probability distribution around that milestone?"

  • CFO answers on probability distributions reveal financial modelling rigour; a CFO who can give a range with named upside/downside drivers is operating at a higher quality level than one who gives a single-point answer

  • Sceptical bullet: Questions the analyst has NOT included because they feel uncomfortable:

  • "Do you believe you are the right management team to scale XVIVO beyond SEK 2bn in revenue, or is there a skill-set gap you are actively filling?" — this is the question that would reveal succession planning and team-building awareness; it is uncomfortable precisely because it implies doubt about management's ceiling; the obstacle is the way — ask it
  • "What does a scenario look like in which you sell XVIVO to a large medtech strategic — and at what price would you consider it seriously?" — this is the question that surfaces the control/exit philosophy and tests alignment with a long-only investor's hold horizon; management's reaction (not just answer) is diagnostic
