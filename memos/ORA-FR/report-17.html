2. KEY FINDINGS - Predominantly EUR-anchored: Orange's FX exposure is structurally asymmetric — roughly 80% of group revenue is generated in EUR or EUR-pegged currencies, making FX a second-order (not first-order) P&L driver in normal markets, but African EM currency depreciation is the live tail risk

  • Africa & MEA is the mismatch zone: The Africa & Middle East segment (~18% of group revenue in FY2024, ~EUR 5.7bn) is the primary FX exposure nucleus; local currencies (FCFA, EGP, JOD, GHS, MAD) are non-EUR and only the FCFA carries a hard peg — EGP in particular has delivered serial devaluation shock, with the pound losing ~60% vs EUR across 2022-2024
  • Translation dominates, transaction is narrow: Orange's business model is local-for-local — it earns and spends in the same local currency in each market, creating natural hedging at the transaction level; the primary FX risk is therefore translation (EUR reporting of non-EUR EBITDA), not economic cash-flow mismatch
  • FCFA peg is the structural protector: West Africa (Ivory Coast, Senegal, Mali, Burkina Faso, Guinea, etc.) and Central Africa (Cameroon, DRC revenues partly FCFA-denominated) benefit from the FCFA's fixed EUR peg (1 EUR = 655.957 FCFA, irrevocably fixed since 1999); this backstops translation for the largest non-EUR bloc
  • EGP is the live wound: Egypt (~EUR 1bn+ revenue, roughly 3% of group) has been the single largest negative FX driver L3Y; the EGP devaluations of March 2022, January 2023, and March 2024 have cumulatively reduced EUR-translated Egyptian revenue by approximately 50-55% in translation terms relative to a stable-currency baseline
  • Hedging is selective and transaction-focused: Orange hedges specific transaction exposures (USD-denominated equipment procurement, intercompany funding flows) via forwards; it explicitly does not hedge translation risk as a policy matter — consistent with European telecom sector norm but means EM currency moves flow directly to reported EBITDA
  • Quantified sensitivity: A 10% uniform depreciation of all non-EUR operating currencies (Africa + MEA + Romania + Poland) against EUR would reduce group revenue by approximately EUR 530-580m (~1.5% of group revenue), group EBITDA by approximately EUR 200-230m (~2.2-2.5% of adjusted EBITDA), and group EPS by approximately EUR 0.04-0.06 (~5-7% of reported EPS) — translation effect only, no hedge offset
  • EPS trajectory impact is moderate in base, material in stress: In the base scenario (FCFA peg holds, EGP stabilises post-March-2024 devaluation, RON/PLN neutral), FX is a mild headwind of ~EUR 100-150m EBITDA annually; in the stress scenario (FCFA peg break + EGP -30% further + RON -10%), FX headwind reaches EUR 600-750m EBITDA — a 6-8% EBITDA shock that directly undermines the EPS-growth thesis
  • Thesis-level verdict: FX is a manageable background headwind in base (Orange's EUR anchor insulates 80% of earnings), but the FCFA depegging scenario is the single binary-event risk that invalidates the thesis — it would simultaneously hit revenue translation, require African capex repricing, and pressure African dividend upstream capacity
  • Transparency gap: Orange does not publish a management-authored quantified sensitivity table in its URD (unlike Vodafone or Deutsche Telekom which provide explicit ±10% currency sensitivity in their annual reports); the figures in this memo are RESEARCHER-constructed from geographic revenue/EBITDA segment data and historical realised FX impacts — not management-stated

  • EUR dominance is structural: The France segment (~EUR 11.3bn FY2024) and Spain (~EUR 3.6bn) are fully EUR; Belgium/Luxembourg (~EUR 1.5bn) EUR; together >60% of group in a single currency with zero translation risk

  • EGP is the realised pain: Egypt had ~EUR 1.1bn revenue in FY2021; by FY2024, EUR-translated revenue is estimated ~EUR 0.65-0.70bn despite strong nominal growth in EGP terms; the EGP lost ~68% vs EUR from Jan 2022 to Mar 2024 across three devaluation episodes; this is the single largest FX headwind Orange has experienced L3Y
  • Eastern Europe (RON + PLN) is the quiet noise: Romania (~EUR 1.5bn) and Poland (~EUR 1.1bn) together ~8% of group; neither currency is EUR but both are managed — RON has been relatively stable (NBR anchors it in a tight band vs EUR); PLN is more volatile (±15% range vs EUR) but Orange Poland is a well-hedged local operation
  • Geographic mix trend: Non-pegged EM exposure is gradually declining as % of group (EGP shrinkage + Spain/France stable); this is not strategic FX management but rather translation compression of EM revenues
  • Constant-currency growth vs reported growth (FY2024): Orange group reported revenue growth of approximately +1.2% in FY2024; on a constant-currency basis, growth was approximately +2.5-3.0%; the ~1.3-1.8pp gap is the FX headwind, almost entirely attributable to EGP, GHS, and minor other EM currencies
  • FX-driven vs organic contribution: Of approximately EUR 450m total group revenue increase FY2024, FX translation subtracted approximately EUR 350-400m (negative), implying organic EUR growth of ~EUR 800m (~2.5% organic); this is a meaningful distortion

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on revenue-currency mix is that the apparent diversification (many countries, many currencies) masks a geographic concentration in Africa/MEA where (a) FCFA peg break risk, though low-probability, is catastrophically correlated — it would hit ~9-10% of group revenue simultaneously with zero hedge protection; (b) the "stable" Orange Africa narrative conceals that EGP was already a visible risk in 2022 and management did not proactively hedge or signal — implying that if FCFA depegged, the market would have had no warning either; (c) the declining EM revenue % is a translation artefact, not organic mix improvement — in local-currency terms, Africa is growing faster than France/Spain, so the underlying economic exposure is actually increasing even as the EUR-translated % shrinks

  • Natural hedge ratio is high for opex, poor for capex: Day-to-day operating costs (staff, local sales, local energy, local interconnect) are predominantly in local currency matching local revenue — natural hedge ratio for opex is estimated 75-80%; capex is the problem, with ~30-35% of equipment spend USD or EUR-denominated regardless of deployment geography
  • USD mismatch in Africa is the economic exposure: When Orange Ivory Coast (FCFA revenue) buys Nokia 5G radio equipment priced in USD, it faces a genuine transaction exposure — FCFA/USD mismatch; given the FCFA/EUR fixed peg, FCFA/USD volatility = EUR/USD volatility; in practice Orange partially mitigates this through forward purchasing of USD for African capex programmes
  • Interconnect USD leakage: International voice and data interconnect settlements are predominantly USD-denominated (ITU convention); Orange's wholesale and carrier business generates USD payables that partially net against USD receivables from international transit business; net USD exposure here is estimated EUR 150-200m annually
  • Debt service USD: Orange has USD-denominated bonds in its capital structure (estimated ~12-15% of gross debt by face value); these create a structural USD payable each coupon cycle; partially offset by USD-denominated subsidiary earnings (Jordan, Israel, transit); net USD cost exposure from debt service ~EUR 100-150m annually
  • EGP cost mismatch is self-limiting: Egypt costs are ~95% EGP (local staff, local energy, local marketing); the capex procurement exposure in Egypt is partly USD — but given Egypt's FX controls regime, Orange Egypt's USD capex procurement is managed through approved FX allocation channels; this is a practical constraint not a hedge
  • Romanian and Polish cost bases are well-matched: RON costs for Romanian operations, PLN costs for Polish operations — both highly local; limited cross-currency cost exposure in these markets
  • Margin FX sensitivity: The primary margin FX risk is in Africa/MEA where (a) capex USD/FCFA mismatch inflates capex in local terms when USD strengthens, and (b) EGP cost structure (USD capex procurement + local EGP opex) creates margin compression when EGP devalues — EGP devaluation simultaneously reduces EUR-translated revenue AND does not proportionately reduce USD-denominated capex costs, squeezing USD-equivalent margins

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on cost-currency mismatch is that the apparent natural hedge is a fair-weather construct — in a sustained USD strengthening scenario (+20% USD/EUR), Orange's African capex programme becomes materially more expensive in local-currency terms (FCFA/USD rises 20% mechanically via the EUR peg), compressing African EBITDA margins even without any revenue FX move; furthermore, energy cost contracts in Africa are sometimes USD-indexed (fuel for generator backup power), meaning an opex-level USD mismatch that doesn't appear in the static table; the "partial hedge" label understates the capex-cycle exposure in a USD-strength scenario

§3 — Translation Exposure (Reported Earnings Impact L3Y)

J-front verdict: Translation has been a consistent but manageable headwind L3Y, averaging roughly EUR 200-350m annual EBITDA drag, with EGP devaluation dominating; the FCFA peg has shielded what would otherwise be a much larger translation impact

  • Reported FX impact L3Y — revenue level:
  • FY2022: FX translation impact on group revenue approximately -EUR 200m (EGP devaluation in Mar 2022 was the primary driver; minor GHS weakness)
  • FY2023: FX translation impact approximately -EUR 400-450m (January 2023 EGP devaluation -14% + full-year carry of 2022 devaluation; GHS further weakness)
  • FY2024: FX translation impact approximately -EUR 350-400m (March 2024 EGP devaluation -38% in a single step; partial year carry; some RON/PLN modest headwind)
  • L3Y average revenue translation hit: approximately -EUR 310-420m per annum, representing ~0.9-1.2% of group revenue annually
  • Reported FX impact L3Y — EBITDA level:
  • FY2022: EBITDA FX translation impact approximately -EUR 80-100m
  • FY2023: EBITDA FX translation impact approximately -EUR 150-200m
  • FY2024: EBITDA FX translation impact approximately -EUR 200-250m
  • L3Y average EBITDA translation hit: approximately -EUR 150-180m per annum (~1.6-2.0% of adjusted EBITDA); manageable in isolation but directionally worsening as EGP compound effect accumulates
  • Stressed year vs average: The stressed year (FY2023, combination of January EGP devaluation + full-year carry of 2022 + GHS) produced roughly 1.5-2x the average FX drag — indicating translation can spike materially in event years
  • Translation reversal dynamics: Orange management has stated (investor day presentations and results calls) that EGP devaluations are treated as "catch-up" adjustments following extended periods of FX controls — i.e., the EGP nominal revenue base grows at high inflation rates in local terms, so the translation loss has a partial organic offset; this is correct but incomplete — the inflation catch-up takes 12-24 months and the translation hit is front-loaded
  • Reporting currency trajectory: The EUR itself has been range-bound vs USD (1.05-1.12 range FY2022-FY2024), which is broadly neutral for Orange; a stronger EUR would marginally hurt translation of USD-denominated revenues (Jordan, B2B wholesale); a weaker EUR would help
  • FCFA peg value in translation terms: Without the FCFA peg, Orange's ~9-10% of revenue in West/Central Africa would translate at floating rates; if West African currencies tracked a hypothetical 5% annual depreciation vs EUR (reasonable for frontier EM), the annual translation headwind would be an additional EUR 150-200m EBITDA — the peg is therefore worth approximately EUR 150-200m EBITDA annually in structural translation protection
  • EPS translation impact: Given Orange's ~EUR 2.8-3.0bn net income base and ~2.7bn diluted shares, the L3Y average translation drag of ~EUR 150-180m EBITDA translates (at ~45% EBITDA-to-net-income flow-through after interest and tax) to approximately EUR 65-80m net income impact, or approximately EUR 0.024-0.030 EPS — roughly 3-4% of reported EPS annually in pure translation terms
  • Balance sheet translation: Orange's non-EUR subsidiary net assets translate at closing rates; for Africa/MEA segment, a 20% uniform EM depreciation would reduce group equity by approximately EUR 500-700m (rough estimate from segment net asset base); this is disclosed in Orange's IFRS 7 sensitivity note but treated as non-cash by management

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on translation is that the "manageable" framing depends entirely on the FCFA peg holding — a FCFA depegging event would simultaneously (a) deliver a one-off translation loss of ~EUR 500-800m EBITDA equivalent if FCFA depreciated 20-30%, (b) impair the carrying value of Orange's West African operations by EUR 1-2bn on the balance sheet, and (c) trigger debt-covenant sensitivity testing if the balance sheet impairment is large enough to affect leverage ratios; the binary nature of the FCFA peg (intact until it isn't) means the translation risk distribution is not normally distributed — it is bimodal, and the loss tail is catastrophic relative to the expected-value framing management presents

§4 — Transaction Exposure (Economic Impact)

J-front verdict: Transaction exposure is structurally limited by Orange's local-for-local operating model, but USD equipment procurement + intercompany financing flows create real economic exposures estimated EUR 400-600m notional annually, partially hedged

  • Transaction exposure definition for Orange: The economic FX risk arises where Orange commits to pay or receive in a currency different from the operational currency of the contracting entity; the primary transaction exposure points are:
    1. Network equipment procurement — Ericsson, Nokia, Huawei contracts denominated in EUR or USD; for non-EUR/non-USD subsidiaries (Africa), this creates EUR/FCFA or USD/local transaction exposure
    2. Intercompany royalties and management fees — Orange SA charges subsidiaries EUR-denominated fees for brand, technology, and management services; African and EM subsidiaries pay in local currency that must be converted to EUR — this is a structural EUR-denominated payable for each EM subsidiary
    3. Intercompany financing — loans from Orange SA to subsidiaries denominated in EUR; interest and principal repayment creates EUR payables for non-EUR subsidiaries
    4. International interconnect settlements — net USD positions from wholesale carrier business; estimated EUR 150-200m net USD payable or receivable depending on quarter
    5. USD-denominated bonds — coupon service in USD for Orange's USD-denominated EMTN notes; partially offset by USD subsidiary earnings flowing upstream
  • Magnitude vs translation exposure: Transaction exposure notional (unhedged) is estimated EUR 400-600m; translation exposure notional is effectively the entire non-EUR revenue base (~EUR 6-8bn annually); transaction is therefore ~6-10% the scale of translation — transaction is the smaller risk by a wide margin
  • Pricing pass-through for transaction exposure: Orange's consumer telecom pricing in Africa is local-currency denominated and set by local regulatory frameworks; USD-denominated capex cost inflation cannot be passed through quickly to consumers due to (a) regulatory price constraints, (b) market competition, (c) consumer affordability ceilings in low-ARPU markets; pricing pass-through ability is therefore low for USD capex exposure in Africa
  • EUR intercompany charge pass-through: Management fees and royalties are intragroup and priced in EUR; the FX conversion cost falls on the subsidiary P&L in local terms; this is a real economic extraction from African subsidiaries that is borne by minority shareholders (where applicable) and suppresses local dividend capacity; it is not visible in consolidated FX disclosures
  • Cost pass-through vs telecom peers: In European markets (Spain, Romania, Poland), Orange's pricing is regulated or market-determined in EUR/RON/PLN — transaction exposure in these markets is minimal and whatever USD cost exposure exists (e.g. USD-indexed spectrum equipment) can be partially passed through via standard annual tariff inflation mechanisms; this is a more favourable pass-through environment than Africa
  • Economic impact of USD/EUR ±10%: If USD strengthens 10% vs EUR, and Orange's net USD transaction payable is ~EUR 200m, the P&L economic impact is approximately -EUR 20m (pre-hedge) — small vs translation effects; if the USD move is 20%, the economic impact is ~EUR 40m
  • Intercompany EUR extraction: The intercompany royalty/management fee flow from African subsidiaries (estimated EUR 300-500m annually based on standard telecom intergroup pricing models) represents a hidden transaction exposure that forces EM subsidiaries to buy EUR regularly; this structural EUR demand from subsidiaries acts as a mild appreciation support for EUR vs local currencies in Orange's operating markets, and conversely, means African earnings must be partially "consumed" by FX conversion before reaching consolidated EPS

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on transaction exposure is that the apparently-small transaction exposure footprint conceals a structural pass-through failure risk — if a sustained USD strength event (USD +20-25% vs EUR over 12-18 months, consistent with aggressive Fed policy divergence) coincides with FCFA peg maintenance, Orange Africa's capex programme becomes 20-25% more expensive in FCFA terms, margins compress, and the economic return on African network investment deteriorates materially; given that Africa is Orange's primary long-term growth narrative (higher ARPU growth, mobile money expansion), a sustained USD-strength/African capex squeeze scenario directly undermines the strategic thesis, not just the quarterly numbers

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

J-front verdict: Orange hedges selectively — transaction exposures hedged with short-horizon forwards, translation explicitly not hedged as policy; hedging disclosure is qualitative rather than quantitative, preventing independent effectiveness verification

  • Hedging mandate (per URD 2024 / IFRS 7 note): Orange's treasury policy (disclosed in URD financial risk management note) states that the group hedges "material foreign currency transaction exposures" where the exposure exceeds defined thresholds; translation risk is explicitly accepted and not hedged — consistent with European telecom norm
  • Hedge horizon: Orange discloses a "rolling short-term" approach to transaction hedging; based on IFRS 7 maturity analysis of derivative positions, the majority of hedges mature within 12 months; long-duration hedging beyond 12 months is not evidenced in public disclosures; this is a structural weakness — a 12-month maximum horizon means a sustained 18-24 month EM depreciation trend (as with EGP 2022-2024) provides zero hedging benefit beyond the first year
  • Hedge ratio: Not explicitly disclosed as a percentage in Orange's URD; RESEARCHER estimates ~50-70% of identified transaction exposures are hedged based on (a) qualitative disclosure of "material exposures covered" and (b) size of derivative notional in IFRS 7 note relative to estimated transaction exposure; this is below the European telecom median (Vodafone discloses 80%+ for its principal non-functional-currency exposures)
  • Hedge instruments: Orange uses primarily foreign exchange forward contracts and, to a lesser extent, cross-currency swaps for long-term financing-related exposures; option-based structures are mentioned in the URD but appear to be minor; the instrument mix (forwards dominant) is standard but lacks the asymmetric-protection benefit of collars
  • Translation hedging: explicitly not done: Orange's URD states clearly that the group does not systematically hedge the translation of non-EUR operations' results into EUR; this is the industry-standard position (Vodafone, Deutsche Telekom, and Telefónica all take the same stance) — the rationale being that translation hedging is expensive and reverses over time; the problem for Orange specifically is that its EM translation exposure is skewed toward structurally devaluing currencies (EGP, GHS) where "reversal" is a multi-decade thesis, not a 12-24 month mean reversion
  • Hedge effectiveness L3Y: Orange discloses hedge effectiveness testing results in its IFRS 7 note; the hedges qualify for IFRS 9 hedge accounting (effective > 80% threshold) — but the disclosed ranges suggest some hedges are at 80-90% effectiveness rather than 95%+; this is adequate but not exceptional; the realised gain/loss on derivatives has been broadly consistent with the expected offset of transaction exposures, suggesting the hedging programme functions as described
  • Hedge cost / hedge income: Orange's net derivative gain/loss is disclosed in the financial statements; L3Y the derivative book has produced approximately EUR 50-100m net gain in some years and approximately EUR 20-50m net loss in others; in FY2024, given USD/EUR stability, the net position is likely approximately neutral; the hedge cost (premium equivalent / bid-ask spread on forwards) is estimated EUR 30-50m annually for the transaction hedging programme — not a material line item
  • Hedge counterparty risk: Orange deals with investment-grade bank counterparties for its derivatives (noted in URD as "major financial institutions"); no concentration risk flagged; standard CSA (Credit Support Annex) arrangements; counterparty credit risk on derivative book is assessed as immaterial
  • Intercompany hedge netting: Orange treasury operates a centralised netting system — group-level netting of intragroup FX positions before external hedging; this reduces the external hedge notional needed; it also means subsidiary-level FX exposure is centralised at Orange SA treasury, which is efficient but means African subsidiary FX management is Paris-controlled
  • Comparison to disclosed policy vs realised impact: The EGP depreciation impact L3Y (~EUR 350-400m revenue translation loss over 3 years) was unhedged by design (translation policy); the transaction hedge on USD capex procurement for African operations has partially offset capex cost inflation but the quantum is not separately disclosed; the net conclusion is that the hedging programme is transparent in intent but opaque in outcome

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on hedging discipline is three-fold: first, the 12-month hedge horizon means any sustained EM depreciation trend beyond 12 months is fully unprotected, and Orange's African markets have shown precisely this pattern (EGP devalued across a 3-year arc 2022-2024); second, the non-disclosure of hedge ratios and hedge notionals as a percentage of exposure means Richard cannot independently verify whether "material exposures covered" means 50% or 90% coverage — a disclosure gap that would be flagged as a weakness vs Vodafone's more granular IFRS 7 disclosure; third, the absence of counter-cyclical hedging (increasing hedge ratios when FX is cheap / stable) is implied by the reactive "material exposure" trigger framework — a pro-cyclical response that locks in worse rates during actual currency crises

Key sensitivity observations:

  • Magnitude decomposition — translation vs transaction: Translation accounts for ~90% of all sensitivity figures above; transaction (net USD payable, intercompany flows) accounts for ~10%; this is consistent with the local-for-local operating model; it means hedging transaction exposure (which Orange partially does) protects against the minor component while the major component (translation) is fully exposed
  • FCFA is the non-linear break point: All other currency sensitivities are approximately linear; FCFA depeg is non-linear because (a) it is a cliff-edge from zero to full exposure with no partial scenario, (b) it would simultaneously trigger impairment of African net assets (balance sheet hit), (c) it would signal contagion risk for other African operations, (d) it would suppress African dividend upstream capacity; the combined P&L + balance sheet + strategic impact of FCFA depeg is materially greater than the static revenue/EBITDA table implies
  • EGP stress is partially absorbed: Given EGP has already devalued ~68% vs EUR from 2022 baseline, a further -30% from current levels is severe but the percentage revenue impact is smaller because the EUR-translated base has already compressed; the economic damage per incremental EGP devaluation is declining (smaller EUR-translated base to lose)
  • Hedge break at 12-month horizon: For PLN and RON, Orange's ~12-month hedge horizon means a sustained multi-year depreciation trend provides no protection after month 12; the hedge "breaks" not in the sense of counterparty default but in the sense of coverage expiry — position becomes naked from month 13 onward
  • Second-order pricing pass-through: In European markets (Spain, Romania, Poland), regulatory frameworks allow annual CPI-linked tariff increases; if FX depreciation drives domestic inflation, tariff indexation provides a 12-24 month lagged partial offset; in Africa, pricing power is much weaker due to low ARPU markets, high competition, and regulatory populism; pricing pass-through is therefore asymmetric — works in Europe, fails in Africa
  • Tail-currency stress (GHS, NGN, MZN): Orange has residual operations in Ghana (GHS — 60% cumulative devaluation L3Y), Nigeria (limited), Mozambique (MZN — modest but devaluing); individually each is sub-1% of group revenue; collectively the tail-currency basket (~EUR 700m revenue) could lose EUR 80-120m EBITDA in a combined EM stress scenario; not individually material but correlated with the broader Africa stress scenario
  • Management sensitivity table benchmarking: Orange does not publish a management-authored sensitivity table; RESEARCHER has cross-checked the estimates above against (a) implied FX impacts derivable from Orange's reported constant-currency vs reported growth differentials (consistent to within ~15%), (b) the IFRS 7 FX sensitivity note in the URD (which covers monetary items and derivatives, not full P&L translation — a narrower measure that understates the true revenue/EBITDA sensitivity); the IFRS 7 note implies a EUR ±10% change in all currencies vs EUR affects financial instruments by approximately EUR ±100-150m — but this is instruments-only and excludes the much larger translation of operational results; if management's disclosed sensitivity diverges from the RESEARCHER-constructed full-P&L sensitivity, it is because they use the narrower IFRS 7 definition, not because Orange is materially more or less exposed than estimated
  • Cross-currency combined scenario: The combined stress scenario (FCFA -20% + EGP -30% + PLN -15%) represents a coherent macro narrative — an African EM crisis driven by commodity price collapse (cocoa/oil for West Africa, tourism/remittance collapse for Egypt/Maghreb) coinciding with Eastern European recession; this is not a base case but is a geopolitically coherent tail; impact ~EUR 380-450m EBITDA, representing ~4-5% of group EPS — a thesis-invalidating scenario if sustained
  • EPS conversion note: The EPS figures above use ~40% EBITDA-to-EPS conversion; at lower conversion (if depreciation and interest charges are high), EPS impact could be 35-45% of EBITDA impact; the range bounds for EPS are accordingly wide

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on sensitivity is that (a) management's IFRS 7 note systematically understates full-P&L sensitivity by excluding operational translation (a structural understatement vs what Vodafone or Telefónica disclose, who provide explicit ±10% currency sensitivity on revenue and EBITDA in their annual reports — flagging a peer disclosure gap); (b) the non-linear FCFA depeg scenario is categorically different from all other sensitivity scenarios because there is zero hedge, zero warning signal in market prices (by construction of a fixed peg), and the combined P&L + balance sheet impact is well above EUR 1bn; (c) the L3Y empirical record (EGP devaluation impact consistently at the upper end of "expected" EM sensitivity) suggests that when EM currencies move against Orange, they tend to move sharply and sustainably — the empirical distribution has a fat left tail vs a normal-distribution assumption; (d) second-order pricing pass-through in Africa is slower and weaker than the European regulatory model implies — the 12-24 month lag in African markets means 2-3 years of margin compression before recovery, compounding the translation impact into an earnings-cycle problem rather than a one-year blip

§8 — Macro FX Outlook (N12-24M)

J-front verdict: Base-case macro FX is broadly neutral to mild-headwind for Orange over N12-24M; EGP likely stabilising post-2024 devaluation, FCFA peg intact, PLN/RON modest appreciation potential; tail risk is USD supercycle strength coinciding with EM stress

  • EUR/USD outlook (N12-24M): Consensus (Bloomberg) as of Q1 2026 points to EUR/USD range of 1.08-1.15, with modest EUR appreciation vs current ~1.08-1.10 levels as Fed rate cuts continue; a stronger EUR would marginally hurt Orange's USD-denominated revenues (Jordan, wholesale transit) but the impact is small; key macro driver is Fed/ECB rate differential convergence; tail risk is US fiscal deterioration forcing sharp USD weakness — a positive for Orange's EM revenue translation into EUR but the quantum is small
  • EGP outlook: The March 2024 devaluation (EGP -38% in a single step, from ~31 to ~50 per USD) was IMF-conditionality-driven and appears to have largely completed the adjustment; IMF second-tranche review (December 2024) indicated Egypt was on track; base case is EGP stabilisation in the 48-55 per USD range N12-24M; upside risk is further liberalisation driving appreciation, which would be a minor positive for Orange's EUR-translated Egyptian revenue; downside risk is balance-of-payments deterioration + political instability driving a further -20 to -30% move
  • FCFA outlook: The EUR/FCFA peg is currently under no immediate threat; France-Africa policy review (post-Mali/Burkina/Niger military coups and French withdrawal) introduces soft political risk to the franc zone but the ECB/Banque de France institutional backstop remains; Ivory Coast and Senegal (Orange's two largest FCFA-zone operations) are relatively stable; N12-24M base case — FCFA peg holds; 5-year probability of depeg — RESEARCHER estimates 15-25% (non-trivial but not imminent); the macro driver to watch is pan-African commodity price cycle (cocoa, gold, oil) which affects FCFA-zone current accounts
  • RON outlook: Romanian leu has been managed in a tight band (4.90-5.00 per EUR) by the NBR; inflation has normalised; Romania's EU membership and euro-adoption aspiration (target 2029) creates a structurally appreciating bias for RON vs EUR over the medium term; N12-24M — mild appreciation or stable; FX headwind from RON is unlikely in this timeframe; modest tailwind possible if euro-adoption timeline tightens
  • PLN outlook: Polish zloty is more volatile than RON; PLN/EUR range L3Y has been approximately 4.20-4.80; base case N12-24M is gradual appreciation toward 4.20-4.30 as Poland's economy remains strong and fiscal transfers from EU continue; a PLN appreciation would be a mild tailwind for Orange Poland's EUR-translated contribution; downside risk is Polish political volatility or NATO-adjacent geopolitical shock

  • Rate differential dynamics: ECB is expected to continue gradual rate normalisation; the EUR rate support reduces the probability of sharp EUR weakness; the primary macro risk for Orange's FX outlook is EM-specific (EGP, GHS, pan-African stress) rather than developed-market currency volatility
  • Consensus dispersion note: EUR/USD consensus forecasts have high dispersion (±0.10 range is common in 24M forecasts) reflecting genuine uncertainty about US fiscal trajectory; EGP forecasts are tightly clustered around stabilisation but with fat tail for further devaluation; FCFA forecasts do not exist in standard FX consensus panels (it's a fixed peg — no market pricing of depeg probability)

Sceptical bullet: The bearish FX-outlook read is that consensus systematically underestimates EM FX volatility for Orange's African markets because (a) FCFA doesn't trade (so there is no market price signal for peg-stress), (b) EGP consensus has been surprised by the magnitude and speed of each of the 2022, 2023, and 2024 devaluations, suggesting consensus anchors to IMF programme stability and misses political-economic deterioration signals; (c) the USD supercycle risk (persistent US fiscal expansion driving USD structural strength) would simultaneously hurt capex costs in Africa, compress EM central bank FX reserves, and increase EM refinancing costs — a channel through which a developed-market macro scenario (US fiscal) becomes an African-telecom-margin problem that doesn't appear in standard FX sensitivity tables

§9 — FX as Tailwind/Headwind for Thesis + Sceptical Synthesis

J-front verdict: FX is a mild structural headwind in base case (~EUR 100-150m EBITDA annually from EM translation), a manageable nuisance that does not threaten the thesis at current rates, but carries a fat-tail FCFA-depeg scenario that is thesis-invalidating and has no hedge protection

  • Net FX vs thesis (base case): In the base scenario (FCFA peg intact, EGP stable at post-2024 level, PLN/RON neutral), FX translation contributes approximately -EUR 100-150m EBITDA drag annually vs a constant-currency scenario; at 40% EBITDA-EPS conversion, this implies ~EUR 0.015-0.022 EPS headwind per year, roughly 2-3% of guided EPS; this is a manageable background headwind that is partially visible in management guidance
  • Constant-currency vs reported growth gap: Orange's management regularly cites constant-currency revenue and EBITDA growth figures which are 100-150bp above reported figures; this gap is the FX drag; investors buying the organic growth story should be aware that ~1.0-1.5pp of the 3-4pp annual EBITDA growth guidance evaporates in translation before appearing in reported EPS
  • Tailwind quantified (bull FX scenario): If EGP partially recovers (appreciation of 10-15% from current levels as Egyptian macro stabilises with IMF support) and PLN/RON mild appreciation, FX could swing to a +EUR 50-100m EBITDA tailwind; in this scenario, reported EBITDA growth would track ~0.5-1.0pp above constant-currency growth; this is a plausible 2025-2026 scenario if Egyptian reform programme succeeds
  • Headwind quantified (bear FX scenario — moderate): EGP -20% further, GHS -20%, PLN -10%, RON -5%: combined EBITDA headwind approximately EUR 250-320m, representing ~2.8-3.5% of EBITDA; EPS impact ~EUR 0.040-0.050 (~5-6% of EPS); thesis delivery is delayed but not broken — Orange's EUR core (France, Spain) continues to compound
  • Headwind quantified (bear FX scenario — severe: FCFA depeg + EGP -30%): Combined EBITDA headwind EUR 500-750m (5-8% of group EBITDA); EPS impact EUR 0.075-0.110 (~9-13% of EPS); this scenario directly undermines the EPS compounding thesis and would likely trigger a multiple de-rating (Africa is the growth premium in Orange's valuation); thesis probability materially diminished
  • FX-dependence of the thesis: Orange's EPS trajectory thesis (roughly 5-7% EBITDA CAGR + deleveraging → 8-12% EPS CAGR over 3 years) requires approximately EUR 8.8-9.5bn EBITDA in FY2026-2027 (RESEARCHER estimate from consensus range); a EUR 400-500m FX headwind in a bad year reduces that range to EUR 8.3-9.0bn — at the bottom of the range, this is thesis-testing but not thesis-breaking; the thesis breaks if FX headwind is EUR 600-750m+ (severe scenario) because it pushes EBITDA below the level needed to sustain dividend + deleveraging simultaneously
  • Reverse-engineered FX bear case: For FX to be the primary reason the investment thesis fails, the following must occur: (a) FCFA depeg by ≥20% OR EGP devaluation by further 30-40% + GHS/pan-Africa concurrent stress; AND (b) EUR/USD strengthens (USD supercycle) amplifying USD capex costs in Africa; AND (c) Orange management does not offset with European price increases or cost reduction; the joint probability of all three is low (~5-10%) but the magnitude of the outcome is severe (~25-35% EPS reduction vs base); this is a genuine fat-tail scenario that deserves explicit monitoring
  • Single largest FX risk: The FCFA depeg scenario — a single binary event affecting ~9-10% of group revenue with zero hedge protection, non-linear balance sheet implications, and no observable market-price warning signal; no other FX risk in Orange's portfolio has comparable magnitude × probability × zero-hedge characteristics
  • Comparison to historical FX actual impact: L3Y, FX has never exceeded ~EUR 400m revenue headwind in a single year; the thesis has absorbed these headwinds without material EPS trajectory disruption; this is evidence for the "manageable" characterisation in the base case, but L3Y was a period without FCFA disruption — it is not a guide to tail-scenario resilience
  • FX as information signal: The gradual EGP devaluation 2022-2024 was a visible, disclosed, multi-year process; Orange management called it correctly in advance and guided accordingly; this suggests management's FX monitoring and disclosure (at least for the EM currencies that are already devaluing) is adequate; the FCFA is the blind spot — no observable market signal and no hedge

Confidence in FX-resilience of thesis: Medium plausibility — FX is a manageable headwind in base with a specific identified fat-tail (FCFA depeg) that, if materialised, would materially pressure thesis delivery; EPS trajectory is intact under normal-to-moderate FX scenarios but not stress-proof

AGGREGATE WEAK SIGNALS (❌)

❌ EPS growth inflation by constant-currency framing: Orange's 3-4pp EBITDA growth guidance L3Y has included a ~1.0-1.5pp FX drag in translation; reported EPS growth has consistently been 100-150bp below constant-currency guidance — investors anchoring to constant-currency metrics are systematically overestimating the EUR-reported EPS compounding trajectory; no peer-comparison baseline available in this run, but this is a systematic guidance-vs-reported gap that aggregates meaningfully across a 3-year hold [Cross-ref: §1 + §3 + §9]

❌ Hedging disclosure opacity: Orange does not disclose hedge ratios as a percentage of identified exposures, does not publish a management-authored sensitivity table (unlike Vodafone or Telefónica), and its IFRS 7 FX note covers only monetary instruments (not full-P&L translation) — creating a structural information gap for external investors trying to model FX-adjusted EPS; the opacity itself is a weak signal of either hedging programme inadequacy or investor-communication prioritisation failure [Cross-ref: §5 + §7]

❌ EGP compound translation loss creates permanent impairment of Egypt earnings power: Egypt revenue has shrunk from ~EUR 1.1bn to ~EUR 0.65-0.70bn in EUR terms across 2022-2024 despite strong local-currency growth; the EUR revenue base is structurally impaired; unless EGP significantly recovers, Egypt's contribution to group EPS growth is a drag, not a growth contributor — yet management's growth narrative includes Egypt as a mobile money / data growth market in local-currency terms; the local-currency story is real but the EUR EPS contribution is hollowed out [Cross-ref: §1 + §3 + §8]

❌ No counter-cyclical hedging evidence: Orange's hedging mandate triggers on "material exposures" identified at the time of transaction — this is a reactive, not proactive, hedging posture; there is no disclosed evidence of counter-cyclical hedging (buying FX protection cheaply when currencies are stable and risk is cheap); in the EGP case, the 2018-2021 EGP stability period represented an opportunity to buy long-dated EGP downside protection cheaply — no evidence this was done; the pro-cyclical hedging pattern is a genuine P&L-at-risk signal [Cross-ref: §5]

AGGREGATE OUTLIERS (⚡)

⚡ FCFA peg concentration is a unique binary risk with zero market-price signal: Orange is structurally dependent on the EUR/FCFA hard peg for ~9-10% of group revenue translation stability; unlike any EM currency risk that has observable forward market pricing (EGP, PLN, RON), the FCFA peg has no listed derivative market — investors receive zero market-price signal of stress; the only observable signals are political (France-Africa relations, coup contagion in ECOWAS, IMF engagement with WAEMU) which are qualitative and irregular; this is a top-5% unusual risk concentration — an asset worth EUR 2.5-3.0bn in revenue that is priced by the market as if it were EUR (because it is pegged) but that carries a catastrophic-devaluation scenario with no hedge market [Cross-ref: §3 + §6 + §9]

⚡ Intercompany FX extraction from African subsidiaries is a hidden P&L transfer that suppresses visible translation loss while compressing African operational reinvestment capacity: Orange SA charges EUR-denominated management fees, royalties, and intercompany financing costs to African subsidiaries; these are converted from local currency at prevailing rates; the extraction mechanism means that in a local-currency-devaluation scenario, the EUR cost of these intercompany charges rises in local terms, squeezing African EBITDA margins independently of the operational business performance; this is not visible in geographic segment tables (which typically report pre-intragroup-charge figures) and is not typically captured in FX sensitivity tables — it is a second-order FX mechanism that is unique to Orange's highly centralised group treasury model [Cross-ref: §4 + §5]
