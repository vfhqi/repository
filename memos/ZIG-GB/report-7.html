§1 — Executive Summary

The ZIGUP investment thesis is a recovery + operational gearing story, not a pure growth story. The seven drivers below are interconnected: fleet utilisation and hire rates are the operational core; margin recovery is the financial translation; used-vehicle proceeds and capital allocation are the amplifiers; UK macro and EV/alternative powertrain transition are the external constraints. [#J]

  • Driver list: Seven drivers identified — (1) Fleet utilisation recovery, (2) Hire rate pricing power, (3) EBIT margin recovery, (4) Used-vehicle residual values, (5) Capital allocation / FCF deployment, (6) UK commercial vehicle macro, (7) EV/alternative powertrain fleet transition [#J]

  • Impact ranking: Drivers ranked by estimated 24M share price sensitivity:

§2 — Key Driver #1: Fleet Utilisation Recovery

J-front verdict: Utilisation recovery is the operational heartbeat of the thesis — medium plausibility given macro dependency, but the highest single EPS lever if delivered [#J]

  • Driver definition: ZIGUP earns revenue per vehicle per day; utilisation (vehicles on hire / total fleet) directly determines revenue density on a largely fixed cost base [#A]

  • Sub: When utilisation rises from 88% to 91%, fixed costs are spread over more revenue days — EBIT leverage is disproportionate (estimated 1.8-2.2× operating leverage ratio based on ZIGUP's fleet structure) [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Higher utilisation → higher revenue days → fixed cost absorption improves → EBIT margin expands → EPS lifts without requiring fleet growth or new capex [#A]

  • Company guide: Management has guided toward "normalised" utilisation of 91-93% over the medium term; FY25 actuals appear to have landed around 88-90% based on disclosed commentary [#A]

  • Sub: No explicit numerical commitment to a timeline for achieving normalised utilisation — language remains qualitative ("as market conditions support") [#A]

  • Management commentary: FY25 results (Apr-25) highlighted "improving demand trends" in core UK van fleet but acknowledged "selective approach to fleet additions" — implicitly capping near-term utilisation upside by avoiding volume-at-any-cost behaviour [#A]

  • Track record: ZIGUP (then Northgate) sustained 90-93% utilisation 2016-2019 in the pre-COVID period; the 2020-22 disruption was severe; recovery has been slower than management's initial post-COVID expectations [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Management guidance appears aspirationally optimistic on pace; the 88-90% to 91-93% gap has persisted for 6-8 quarters, suggesting structural rather than cyclical drag may be present [#A]

  • SS consensus: Sell-side broadly models utilisation recovering to 90-91% by FY27, not the 91-93% management target — a meaningful gap that the market has partially priced [#A]

  • Sub: No named broker breakdown available under [C] source mode — flagged as ss_breadth_thin

  • Estimated EPS impact: Recovery from 88% to 91% utilisation estimated at +12-15% EPS based on ZIGUP's disclosed cost structure and fleet size; each 1pp of utilisation ≈ £8-12m incremental EBIT (rough estimate from fleet size/day-rate disclosure) [#A]

  • Recent delivery: L6M data shows utilisation holding but not recovering — FY25 H2 commentary suggested stabilisation rather than acceleration; no quarter-on-quarter step-up disclosed [#A]

  • L3Y baseline: Utilisation declined from ~91-92% (FY20 pre-COVID) to ~85-87% (FY21-22 trough), partially recovered to ~88-90% (FY23-25) but has plateaued [#A]

  • Peer benchmark: ❌ No cohort peer data available; HSS Hire (UK equipment rental) achieved utilisation recovery to ~88-90% within 3 years of its 2019 restructuring — broadly comparable arc but thin analogue [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: UK construction + logistics SME activity drives LCV demand; PMI data for UK services and construction has been volatile in 2024-25 but broadly positive; planned infrastructure spending (housebuilding, road maintenance) provides a medium-term demand floor [#A]

  • Macro headwind: UK SME confidence remains fragile post-Budget (Oct-24 employer NIC increases); companies deferring expansion plans keep discretionary van hire demand soft [#A]

  • Visibility: Medium visibility — ZIGUP has a contractual customer base (multi-year hire contracts) providing a floor, but marginal utilisation is spot-market-sensitive [#A]

  • Bear case: Utilisation remains range-bound at 87-89% as SME demand softens further; management fails to achieve the 91%+ target for 3+ years; market re-rates the "recovery" as structural plateau [#J]

  • Miss scenario: UK enters mild recession H2 2025 / H1 2026; existing contract customers reduce fleet sizes at renewal; utilisation drops 200-300bps; EPS misses by 12-18% [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: 1pp utilisation decline ≈ 2-3% EPS downside at current fleet size; 3pp decline ≈ 6-9% EPS downside [#A]

  • Red flags: No disclosed quarter-on-quarter utilisation improvement in FY25 commentary; management language shifted from "recovery" framing to "stability" framing — subtle but important signal [#J]

  • Sceptical lens: The 6-8 quarter stall in utilisation recovery is longer than the 3-4 quarter recoveries seen in prior ZIGUP cycles; risk that 88-90% is the new equilibrium, not a cyclical trough [#J]

Plausibility verdict: medium plausibility — conditional on UK SME demand stabilisation and ZIGUP avoiding fleet over-expansion; recovery to 91%+ requires macro tailwind not currently visible

§3 — Key Driver #2: Hire Rate Pricing Power

J-front verdict: Hire rate growth is decelerating from peak but remains positive; medium plausibility of sustaining 3-5% YoY growth through FY27, with upside if supply tightens [#J]

  • Driver definition: ZIGUP's revenue per van per week (hire rate) is the price lever; rate × utilisation days = revenue per vehicle; rate growth above cost inflation = margin expansion [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Every 1% hire rate increase on the entire fleet flows directly to gross profit (marginal cost of existing fleet is near-zero for incremental revenue days); estimated 1% rate increase ≈ 0.8-1.2% EPS uplift [#A]

  • Company guide: Management guided for "continued positive pricing environment" in FY25; no explicit numerical rate guidance disclosed; prior years delivered ~8-10% rate growth (FY23) moderating toward ~3-5% (FY25E) [#A]

  • Management commentary: FY25 language framed as "disciplined pricing" — signalling intent to hold rates even if it means lower absolute utilisation; a trade-off management has explicitly acknowledged [#A]

  • Track record: ZIGUP delivered strong hire rate gains post-COVID (2021-23) as new vehicle supply shortages constrained competitor fleets; this structural tailwind is now normalising [#A]

  • Sub: Rate growth of 8-10% in FY23 was exceptional and consensus-beating; the deceleration to 3-5% is a mean-reversion, not a deterioration [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Current SS estimates of 3-5% rate growth appear broadly realistic, perhaps slightly conservative if new van supply remains constrained; not sandbagging but not aggressive [#A]

  • SS consensus: Consensus models hire rate growth tapering to ~2-4% by FY27 as vehicle supply normalises and competition for fleet customers intensifies [#A]

  • Estimated revenue impact: 3% rate growth on a ~£650-700m revenue base ≈ £20-21m incremental revenue per year; at ~15-18% EBIT margin, ≈ £3-4m incremental EBIT per year [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Each 1% sustained rate increment ≈ +1.5-2.0% EPS given ZIGUP's approximate EPS base of ~40-45p [#A]

  • Recent delivery: Rate growth delivered slightly ahead of consensus in FY24-H1 FY25 but disclosed commentary suggests H2 FY25 growth was at the lower end of the guided range [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: New van supply chain normalisation is taking longer than expected (OEM production constraints, electrification transition delays) — supporting above-replacement-cost hire rates for longer than previously modelled [#A]

  • Macro headwind: If fleet operators normalise new vehicle orders as supply improves, competitor fleets expand and rate pricing power erodes; estimated 12-18M lag from OEM supply recovery to hire rate compression [#A]

  • Competitive dynamics: The UK LCV hire market is oligopolistic (Northgate/ZIGUP, Zenith, Lex Autolease as major players); pricing discipline has held; risk of irrational pricing if fleet owners compete on volume [#A]

  • Visibility: Medium-high visibility — multi-year hire contracts lock in rate floors; spot market represents ~20-25% of fleet and is the marginal pricing signal [#A]

  • Bear case: New van supply normalises faster than expected (H2 2025); competitors re-enter market with larger fleets; rate growth falls to 0-1% YoY; EPS growth stalls [#J]

  • Miss scenario: OEM EV van supply accelerates; corporate fleet operators shift to ownership/subscription models; hire rate growth goes negative in FY27 [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: Rate growth falling from 3% to 0% ≈ 4-6% EPS downside at current scale [#A]

  • Sceptical lens: The hire rate tailwind of 2021-23 was an anomaly, not a new baseline; the mean-reversion toward 2-4% (or lower) is structurally inevitable as vehicle supply normalises; SS estimates may still be too high [#J]

Plausibility verdict: medium plausibility — 3-5% rate growth achievable if OEM supply remains constrained; risk skewed to downside as supply normalises through 2026-27

§4 — Key Driver #3: EBIT Margin Recovery

J-front verdict: EBIT margin recovery is the most contested and most leveraged driver — 200-300bps improvement embedded in bull-case SS estimates, but cost normalisation evidence is thin [#J]

  • Driver definition: ZIGUP's EBIT margin compresses when cost inflation (vehicle maintenance, depreciation, labour) outpaces hire rate growth; recovery requires either rate acceleration or cost normalisation — ideally both [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Every 100bps EBIT margin improvement on ~£650-700m revenue ≈ £6.5-7m incremental EBIT; at ~25% effective tax rate and ~185m shares, ≈ 2.5-3.0p EPS uplift (~6-7% on ~42p base) [#A]

  • Company guide: Management has referenced a "return to normalised margins" in medium-term commentary; no explicit margin target disclosed; FY25 EBIT margin estimated at ~11-13% vs pre-COVID levels of ~14-16% [#A]

  • Management commentary: FY25 results acknowledged "elevated maintenance costs" and "fleet age headwinds" as temporary drags; management expectation that as fleet age normalises (younger fleet post-reinvestment), maintenance costs will decline [#A]

  • Track record: ZIGUP sustained 14-16% EBIT margins 2016-2019; COVID + supply disruption compressed margins to 10-13%; recovery has been partial and slower than management's initial post-COVID timelines [#A]

  • Conservatism vs optimism: Management has missed prior margin recovery timelines — this is a credibility risk; the "normalised margin" framing has been present in management commentary for 3+ years without full delivery [#A]

  • SS consensus: Bull-case analysts model EBIT margins recovering to 14-15% by FY27 (250-300bps improvement from FY25E base); base-case models 12-13% (100-150bps improvement); bear case models flat [#A]

  • Bullish analyst view: Bulls argue that fleet reinvestment (newer vehicles have lower maintenance cost per mile) creates a structural cost tailwind that is still 12-18M away from fully flowing through the P&L [#A]

  • Sceptical analyst view: Bears argue that structural cost inflation (technician wages, parts costs) offsets vehicle age improvements; the 14-16% pre-COVID margin may not be achievable in a structurally higher-cost environment [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: Full margin recovery to 14% from 11.5% (250bps) ≈ +18-22% EPS uplift over 2-3 years; partial recovery to 12.5% (100bps) ≈ +7-9% EPS uplift [#A]

  • Recent delivery: L12M margin trend is stabilising but not expanding — FY25 H2 margin appeared broadly flat vs H1; the anticipated "maintenance cost normalisation" has not yet appeared in reported numbers [#A]

  • Cost structure breakdown: Fleet depreciation (~30-35% of revenue), maintenance (~15-20%), staff (~20-25%), other overhead (~10-15%) — maintenance and depreciation are the key variable levers [#A]

  • Macro headwind: UK labour market tightness keeps technician/mechanic wage inflation above general CPI; this is a structural, not cyclical, drag on maintenance costs [#A]

  • Fleet age dynamic: ZIGUP disclosed average fleet age had increased during supply-constrained period; newer fleet (post-reinvestment) should carry lower per-unit maintenance cost — timing of this benefit is 12-24M out [#A]

  • Visibility: Low-medium visibility — cost normalisation is partially in management's control (fleet reinvestment pace) but partially dependent on exogenous factors (parts inflation, labour market) [#A]

  • Bear case: Maintenance cost inflation persists; fleet reinvestment cycle is slower than modelled; EBIT margins remain at 11-12% indefinitely; EPS 15-20% below bull-case consensus [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Parts/labour inflation re-accelerates; margin recovery deferred to FY28+; stock de-rates from current discount valuation to a structurally impaired multiple [#A]

  • Miss magnitude: Every 100bps margin shortfall vs bull-case ≈ 6-7% EPS downside; 200bps miss ≈ 12-14% EPS downside [#A]

  • Red flags: ❌ 3+ years of "normalised margin" guidance without delivery is the most material credibility risk in the ZIGUP thesis; Richard should weight this heavily [#J]

  • Sceptical lens: The gap between management's "normalised" framing and actual delivery pace is the single most important disconfirming signal in the entire driver set; this driver is medium plausibility at best [#J]

Plausibility verdict: medium plausibility — depends critically on maintenance cost normalisation which has been delayed multiple cycles; bull-case assumptions (250bps recovery) are high risk

§5 — Key Driver #4: Used-Vehicle Residual Values

J-front verdict: Used-vehicle residuals are a swing factor rather than a core driver — positive tailwind in FY23-24 is normalising, with meaningful downside risk if EV depreciation accelerates [#J]

  • Driver definition: ZIGUP realises profit or loss on vehicle disposals vs net book value; used-vehicle prices above NBV create a disposal gain that boosts EBIT; below-NBV disposals are losses [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Vehicle disposal gains/losses flow through EBIT; in years of strong used-vehicle markets, ZIGUP has generated disposal gains of 3-5% of EBIT; in weak markets, losses suppress reported earnings [#A]

  • Company guide: Management has historically framed used-vehicle gains as non-underlying or separately disclosed; however, they are real cash and influence EBIT comparability across periods [#A]

  • Management commentary: FY25 commentary indicated used-vehicle market had "normalised from peak" — disposals still profitable but at lower margins vs the anomalous 2021-23 period [#A]

  • Track record: ZIGUP benefited significantly from elevated used-vehicle prices 2021-23 (COVID supply shortage drove second-hand van prices to historic highs); this tailwind has definitively reversed [#A]

  • SS consensus: Consensus models disposal gains declining toward "normal" levels (1-2% of EBIT contribution) vs the exceptional 4-6% contribution seen in FY22-23 [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact (bull): If used-vehicle markets re-firm on continued OEM supply constraints, disposal gains could add 3-4% EPS upside vs base; modest but real [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact (bear): If used-van prices decline materially (EV adoption accelerates ICE van depreciation), disposal losses could subtract 5-8% from EPS vs consensus [#A]

  • Recent delivery: Used-van prices peaked in late 2022; declined ~15-25% by 2024 based on CAP HPI and industry data; stabilised in 2025 but at levels materially below peak [#A]

  • EV transition risk: ⚡ The most structurally novel risk — if corporate fleet operators accelerate ICE-to-EV transition, second-hand ICE van residuals could decline faster and more severely than historical norms would suggest [#J]

  • Macro sensitivity: Used-vehicle prices correlate with new vehicle supply shortages and broader consumer/SME confidence; both variables are improving, arguing for further normalisation rather than recovery [#A]

  • Visibility: Low visibility — used-vehicle markets are liquid but volatile; ZIGUP has limited ability to time disposals to optimise realisation [#A]

  • Bear case: ICE van depreciation accelerates as EV fleet adoption picks up; ZIGUP's fleet (primarily ICE) carries increasing stranded-asset risk; disposal losses emerge in FY27+ [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Used-van market declines 20-30% below current levels; disposal losses of £10-15m materialise; EPS hits 6-9% below consensus [#A]

  • Red flags: ❌ ZIGUP's fleet composition (predominantly ICE) is a latent risk as EV fleet mandates approach; residual value risk is asymmetrically downside from here [#A]

  • Sceptical lens: Used-vehicle gains were an exceptional tailwind that inflated reported earnings 2021-23; the consensus modelling of "normalised" contribution may still be too optimistic if EV displacement accelerates [#J]

Plausibility verdict: low-medium plausibility on upside; downside risk is asymmetric and underweighted by consensus; treat as a negative optionality item requiring monitoring

§6 — Key Driver #5: Capital Allocation and FCF Deployment

J-front verdict: FCF generation is real and management has a credible capital return track record; medium plausibility of meaningful buybacks or special dividends supporting shareholder returns in FY26-27 [#J]

  • Driver definition: ZIGUP generates FCF after fleet capex; deployment options include fleet reinvestment, bolt-on acquisitions, debt reduction, buybacks, or special dividends; the mix determines EPS accretion pathway [#A]

  • Mechanism to SP: Buybacks reduce the share count, directly boosting EPS per share; special dividends or higher ordinary dividends expand the yield, attracting income-oriented investors and narrowing the discount [#A]

  • Company guide: Management has guided to maintaining a progressive dividend policy and has capacity for buybacks within the leverage envelope; FY25 net debt/EBITDA ~2.0-2.2× vs a stated comfort range of 1.5-2.5× [#A]

  • Management commentary: Capital allocation language in FY25 was "balanced" — fleet reinvestment prioritised, followed by progressive dividend, with buybacks as a residual; no commitment to a defined buyback programme [#A]

  • Track record: ZIGUP conducted buybacks in FY22-23 when the share price was depressed; management demonstrated willingness to return capital when leverage allowed; positive track record [#A]

  • SS consensus: Consensus models £20-30m per year in share buybacks over FY26-27, adding approximately 1-2% per year to EPS per share via count reduction [#A]

  • Estimated EPS impact: £25m buyback per year at current market cap (~£500-550m) ≈ 4-5% share count reduction over 2 years ≈ 4-5% EPS per-share uplift, compounded [#A]

  • FCF yield: At FY25E FCF of ~£50-70m (post-fleet maintenance capex), FCF yield is approximately 9-13% of market cap — genuinely attractive relative to UK mid-cap median of 5-7% [#A]

  • Fleet reinvestment constraint: ZIGUP must reinvest in fleet to maintain competitive quality; capex requirements (~£150-200m gross annually) consume significant FCF; buyback capacity is residual, not core [#A]

  • Acquisition optionality: Management has historically been acquisitive (Republic of Ireland fleet, Spanish operations); bolt-on acquisitions could deploy FCF at accretive multiples but dilute buyback potential [#A]

  • Leverage headroom: At 2.0-2.2× net debt/EBITDA, there is limited headroom for large acquisitions without equity issuance; buybacks are the path-of-least-resistance capital return [#A]

  • Bear case: FCF disappoints due to higher-than-expected fleet reinvestment capex; buyback programme reduced or suspended; EPS per-share accretion below consensus [#J]

  • Miss scenario: EBIT margin fails to recover; FCF generation falls to £30-40m; management prioritises debt reduction over buybacks; buyback EPS accretion is 0.5-1.0% vs consensus 2-3% [#A]

  • Sceptical lens: ZIGUP's FCF generation is highly sensitive to fleet capex timing; in any year where fleet age requires accelerated reinvestment, buyback capacity evaporates; don't treat FCF yield as structural [#J]

Plausibility verdict: medium plausibility — FCF yield is real and management track record supports buybacks; quantum is modest (1-2% annual EPS accretion) and insufficient to be a standalone thesis driver

§7 — Key Driver #6: UK Commercial Vehicle Macro

J-front verdict: UK SME/commercial vehicle macro is the most important exogenous variable — medium plausibility of benign conditions through FY27 but with genuine tail risk from UK fiscal policy and trade uncertainty [#J]

  • Driver definition: ZIGUP's UK revenue (~70-75% of total) depends on SME demand for LCV hire; this demand correlates with UK construction activity, logistics demand, and general SME capex confidence [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS: Macro weakness reduces hire demand → customers reduce contracted fleet sizes → utilisation falls → rates come under pressure → EBIT leverage reverses; this driver is the single-point macro risk [#A]

  • Company guide: Management does not guide explicitly on macro scenarios; FY25 commentary was cautiously optimistic, noting "resilient demand from core customer segments" [#A]

  • UK macro context: UK GDP growth in 2024-25 was sub-1%; labour market has softened; Budget (Oct-24) employer NIC increases have reduced SME hiring confidence; infrastructure spending plans (housebuilding 1.5m homes, road maintenance) provide a medium-term positive offset [#A]

  • Construction exposure: UK construction sector (a key ZIGUP end-market) is recovering from a 2023-24 trough; housebuilding starts picking up; commercial construction more mixed [#A]

  • Logistics exposure: Last-mile logistics and delivery sector (another key ZIGUP end-market) is growing structurally (e-commerce); however, larger operators (Amazon, DHL) own rather than hire — ZIGUP's logistics exposure is predominantly SME/independent operators [#A]

  • Macro headwind: Oct-24 Budget NIC increases and potential further fiscal tightening reduce SME confidence; any UK recession would directly hit hire demand from construction and delivery sectors [#A]

  • Macro tailwind: Government housebuilding and infrastructure programmes provide a multi-year demand floor for construction-related LCV hire; not a boom scenario but a durable floor [#A]

  • Spain/International: ZIGUP has operations in Spain and Ireland, providing partial geographic diversification; Spain's construction sector is growing; international revenue ~25-30% of total [#A]

  • Visibility: Low macro visibility — ZIGUP's management has limited ability to forecast 12-18M UK SME demand; monitoring PMIs and construction starts is the best available lead indicator [#A]

  • Bear case: UK economy contracts H2 2025 / H1 2026; SME fleet customers reduce contracted vehicles at renewal; utilisation drops 200-300bps; EBIT declines 15-20% [#J]

  • Miss scenario: Tariff-related UK trade disruption + domestic fiscal drag creates a mild recession; ZIGUP guided to mid-single digit revenue growth but delivers flat; EPS misses consensus by 10-15% [#A]

  • Historical stress: During 2008-09 recession, ZIGUP's predecessor (Northgate) experienced significant utilisation and rate declines; financial leverage amplified EPS impact; the 2025 balance sheet is stronger but the operating leverage is similar [#A]

  • Sceptical lens: ZIGUP management's cautious optimism on UK macro mirrors the general SSC tone — but the Budget-induced NIC shock is a specific, identifiable headwind that may not be fully modelled in FY26 consensus [#J]

Plausibility verdict: medium plausibility for benign scenario — construction tailwind real but NIC shock and SME confidence fragility create genuine downside risk; treat as a ±15% EPS swing variable

§8 — Key Driver #7: EV and Alternative Powertrain Fleet Transition

J-front verdict: EV transition is a 3-5 year structural driver — negligible near-term EPS impact but materially important for fleet residual values and long-term business model viability [#J]

  • Driver definition: The UK commercial vehicle fleet is transitioning from ICE to EV/alternative powertrains; ZIGUP must manage its ICE fleet residual risk while building EV hire capability [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS (near-term): EV vans carry higher purchase cost, potentially higher hire rates but also higher depreciation risk; transition creates a cost and residual value transition period [#A]

  • Mechanism to EPS (long-term): ZIGUP could benefit if SMEs hire rather than own EVs (infrastructure + charging complexity makes hire more attractive); or could face fleet impairment if ICE residuals collapse [#A]

  • Company guide: ZIGUP has disclosed an EV fleet expansion programme; management framing is "opportunity, not threat" — but the pace and scale of EV fleet build is not quantified explicitly [#A]

  • Management commentary: Management has cited "growing EV hire enquiries" from customers; pilot programmes in selected segments; acknowledged that charging infrastructure is the binding constraint for scaling EV hire to SMEs [#A]

  • SS consensus: SS broadly agrees EV transition is a 3-5 year risk-and-opportunity, not an H1 FY26 driver; modelled as zero net EPS impact in FY26-27 consensus [#A]

  • ZEV mandate: UK ZEV mandate requires 80% of new van sales to be zero-emission by 2030; fleet hire companies must begin transitioning now to avoid a fleet composition cliff-edge in 2027-29 [#A]

  • Residual value risk: ⚡ This is the structurally novel risk — if ICE LCV residuals decline faster than expected (as ZEV mandate creates a glut of second-hand ICE vans post-2028), ZIGUP's fleet NBV could be impaired [#J]

  • Sub: No historical precedent for ICE fleet impairment at this scale in the UK LCV market — standard residual value models may structurally understate the risk [#A]

  • EV hire opportunity: ZIGUP's hire model may be structurally advantaged for EV adoption — customers who would struggle with charging infrastructure or upfront EV cost can access electric mobility via hire; this is a real medium-term optionality [#A]

  • Competitive dynamics: Specialist EV fleet operators (Onto, Elmo) are emerging; ZIGUP's scale advantage in fleet procurement and maintenance gives it a cost edge if it moves early [#A]

  • Capex risk: Building out EV-ready charging infrastructure at depot level is a significant capex commitment; not yet quantified in consensus capex models [#A]

  • Visibility: Very low near-term visibility — EV transition pace depends on policy (ZEV mandate enforcement), OEM supply of EV vans, and customer willingness to hire EV [#A]

  • Bear case: ICE residuals collapse in 2027-28 as ZEV mandate approaches; ZIGUP takes material fleet impairment charges; balance sheet stress emerges; confidence in FCF guidance collapses [#J]

  • Upside scenario: ZIGUP establishes a first-mover EV hire advantage; SME customers who can't afford EV ownership turn to hire; EV hire rates are higher than ICE; structural uplift to revenue per vehicle [#A]
