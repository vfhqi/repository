KEY FINDINGS [J] FX is a structural top-line headwind for HTRO but a largely neutralised margin headwind: the "localisation strategy" (produce-where-you-sell) creates a high degree of natural P&L hedging, meaning the −5% FX drag on 2025 group revenue translated to negligible EBITA dilution. This is the central FX truth for the thesis.

[J] USD is the single most consequential currency: North America generated SEK 2,591m (34% of 2025 revenue) of which the vast majority is USD-denominated. The USD weakened meaningfully vs SEK in 2024–2025, and the Q1 2026 USD/SEK rate is running ~10% weaker year-on-year — making FX the largest near-term reported-revenue variable facing the business.

[J] The IFRS 7 sensitivity table from the 2025 annual report (Note 3) is explicit and load-bearing: a +10% SEK vs USD reduces group operating profit by SEK 8m (translation) against USD operating profit of USD 9m. Separately, a +10% SEK vs GBP reduces group operating profit by SEK 21m — GBP is the larger EBITA translation sensitivity in 2025 despite smaller absolute revenue weight, driven by high Harsh Environment margins in the UK.

[J] The company does not use financial derivatives to hedge FX in any material disclosed way. Mitigation is structural (natural hedging through local production) and operational (contract currency clauses, internal flow netting). This is a deliberate policy choice — not an oversight — but it leaves residual translation exposure fully unhedged on the balance sheet (net asset exposure: EUR 67m, USD 35m, GBP 70m).

[A] The BEAD-driven USD uplift scenario (H2 2026 and beyond) is a positive FX thesis catalyst only if USD/SEK stabilises or reverses. At current USD/SEK (~9.8–10.0), the BEAD volume benefit would be partially offset by translation headwind vs the 2022–2023 peak USD/SEK of ~11.0. The net effect is real volume gain but below-potential reported SEK revenue growth.

[A] Currency composition is shifting structurally: Data Center (high USD/EUR mix, 19% of 2025 revenue, fastest-growing segment at +45% reported) is expanding its share, while Fiber Solutions (historically North America heavy) contracts. The USD-EUR blend of the overall book is therefore drifting upward, increasing sensitivity to USD-EUR corridor moves.

[J] FX is not a thesis-killer but is a material reported-earnings modifier of ±5–8% of adjusted EBITA in a ±10% USD or GBP move scenario. At the current USD/SEK trajectory (weakening USD), FX is a quantifiable drag on the path to EBITA recovery in 2026–2027 and must be modelled explicitly as a headwind of SEK ~100–200m revenue at current spot vs consensus SEK assumptions.

[J] Cost currency mix closely mirrors revenue currency mix at the subsidiary level — this is the mechanism of the natural hedge. Local manufacturing entities incur wages, raw materials and overheads in their functional currency, creating a high but imperfect match between revenue and cost currency within each region.

§4 — Transaction Exposure

[J] Transaction exposure is modest in scale relative to translation exposure, and is actively managed through internal flow optimisation and contract currency clauses — but a structural residual exposure exists in European subsidiaries that source inputs in USD, creating a natural mismatch that cannot be fully eliminated through operational means.

[A] The 2025 annual report (Note 3) defines the transaction exposure precisely: "Transaction exposure within the Group is mainly concentrated in European subsidiaries that make external purchases in EUR and USD where neither of these currencies are the company's accounting currency." This is a reference primarily to Swedish and Norwegian entities invoicing in SEK but purchasing fibre optics inputs, specialty components or cable-making materials in EUR or USD.

[A] Three mechanisms are used to reduce transaction exposure per the annual report: (1) managing the currency of internal invoicing flows — i.e., where Hexatronic Group AB acts as internal treasury, it can invoice subsidiaries in their functional currency to avoid entity-level mismatch; (2) contract currency clauses — passing FX risk to customers via contracts denominated in the customer's currency, effectively making the sale and cost match; (3) matching of external currency flows — netting purchases in a foreign currency against sales in the same currency where possible.

[A] Pricing pass-through: HTRO's fibre cable and duct products have limited spot-pricing flexibility in competitive markets. However, large infrastructure contracts (BEAD-related, long-term telecom operator supply agreements) often include raw material and FX escalation clauses. This is consistent with industry practice at Prysmian and Nexans where long-term project contracts carry commodity/currency adjustment mechanisms. For shorter-cycle Distribution channel sales (which dominate European Fiber Solutions), pass-through is harder and lag effects are real — typically 3–6 months for price increases to flow through distributor stock.

[A] The EUR operating profit of €−7m in 2025 (vs €9m profit in 2024) is a signal of transaction exposure impact: the eurozone entities swung to a loss year despite steady EUR-billed revenues, which likely reflects input cost inflation and possible EUR/USD mismatch on purchased components. This warrants further investigation (see §9).

[I] HTRO does not disclose the quantum of transaction exposure in absolute currency-flow terms. There is no forward contract or options position disclosed in the annual report — no level 2 financial instruments in the fair value hierarchy table (Note 3.3), confirming zero financial derivative hedges.

[A] The "six-currency cash pool with real-time netting" (Note 3) is the primary transaction management tool — it allows the treasury to net USD inflows against USD outflows group-wide on a daily basis, reducing the need for external hedging. This is efficient but provides zero protection against the structural mismatch where EUR-denominated sales face USD-denominated input costs.

❌ The absence of any disclosed derivative hedging programme (forwards, options, cross-currency swaps) is unusual for a company with ~SEK 7.5bn revenue and ~40% non-SEK revenue. Comparable peers of similar size (Belden, ~USD 2.5bn revenue) disclose net investment hedges, fair value hedges, and cash flow hedge programmes. HTRO's approach is structurally lean — appropriate only if the natural hedge quality is genuinely high, which is the assertion management makes but does not quantify in investor-facing materials.

Sceptical bullet: HTRO's reliance on "internal flow optimisation" rather than external FX derivatives may reflect a treasury philosophy that views hedging costs as value-destroying — which is defensible — but it also creates earnings volatility when SEK strengthens sharply (as in 2024–2026). The current regime of sustained USD/SEK weakening is precisely the scenario where a systematic short-USD cash flow hedge programme would have delivered material earnings protection.

§5 — Hedging Policy Assessment

[J] HTRO's hedging policy is explicitly structural-only: no financial derivatives for FX risk (confirmed in Note 3 of the 2025 annual report). The policy relies entirely on natural hedging via localised production and operational cash flow matching. This is a legitimate choice but it is undisclosed in scope and untested across full FX cycle ranges.

[A] Policy framework as disclosed: the Group Treasury Policy (referenced in Note 3 but not published) is the responsibility of the President/CEO and CFO. The Board establishes the policy; Group Management implements it; subsidiary Presidents have operational compliance responsibility. Risk management is handled centrally by Hexatronic Group AB.

[A] The policy explicitly states: "Hexatronic does not generally use derivative instruments to adjust underlying interest exposure" (regarding interest rate risk). The word "generally" introduces ambiguity — it is possible that limited derivative use occurs in specific subsidiaries, but no derivatives appear in the fair value hierarchy (Note 3.3: "The Group did not have any assets measured at fair value on 31 December 2025 or 31 December 2024"), confirming zero financial derivative instruments on balance sheet.

[A] The cash pool architecture (six currencies, real-time netting) is an active operational hedge. By netting USD inflows and outflows daily across all subsidiaries, the Group reduces the open USD position that would otherwise need to be converted. This is equivalent to a partial internal forward — but without the certainty of a contractual hedge.

[A] Translation hedging: none. The balance sheet net assets in USD ($35m), GBP (£70m), EUR (€67m) are fully unhedged for translation. The USD and GBP debt (USD 88m, GBP 26m) partially offsets the USD and GBP net asset positions, providing an accidental or deliberate balance sheet hedge. A +10% SEK strengthening would reduce translated net assets by approximately SEK 330m (OCI impact) — this would reduce the equity ratio, although from a comfortable base of 46% as of December 2025.

[A] Hedging effectiveness (implicit, via operating profit by currency track record): USD operating profit fell from USD 18m in 2024 to USD 9m in 2025 — a 50% decline. This occurred during a period when HTRO's North American FTTH revenues contracted sharply (Fiber Solutions North America −19%). So the EBITA decline reflects both volume compression and FX translation. GBP operating profit, by contrast, rose from £8m to £16m in 2025 — reflecting genuine earnings growth in UK Harsh Environment, partially offset by GBP/SEK translation.

[A] No hedge accounting is applied (consistent with zero derivatives). Forward contracts or hedging relationships under IFRS 9 are absent. This means any future decision to initiate a hedging programme would require documentation, effectiveness testing and potentially OCI volatility — a non-trivial implementation step.

[I] Instrument mix: 100% structural/natural (no forwards, swaps, or options disclosed). Horizon: indefinite (structural, not time-bounded). Ratio: varies by segment and entity; estimated 85–92% USD natural hedge (North America entities), ~70–80% EUR natural hedge (European entities), ~80–90% GBP natural hedge (UK entities).

[I] Peer context: Prysmian uses project-specific FX hedges for large contracts (forward purchases of USD/EUR for multi-year EPC jobs). Nexans hedges its "copper core" exposure but not all FX risk. Belden (US-listed, mixed-currency revenues) uses cash flow hedges and net investment hedges. Corning hedges specific JPY and CNY exposures. HTRO's policy — naturalise everything, hedge nothing — sits at the structural extreme of the peer spectrum.

[A] Cost of not hedging: in 2025, the approximately −5% revenue FX drag on SEK 7,519m revenue represents an unrealised cost of approximately SEK 375m. If this could have been hedged at cost of 1–2% of notional (typical forward premium for USD/SEK), the cost would have been approximately SEK 50–100m. The actual FX revenue loss of ~SEK 375m would have been largely insulated. This is not to say HTRO should hedge — the natural hedge may have protected most of the EBITA — but it illustrates the opportunity cost of the current policy in a sustained SEK-strengthening environment.

❌ The hedging policy framework is not publicly disclosed in any investor-facing document. Investors must infer the policy from brief notes in the annual report. No treasury policy summary, no hedging objectives, no hedge ratio targets, and no management of hedging effectiveness metrics is publicly available. This is below best practice for a company of HTRO's international revenue complexity and represents a governance gap relative to large-cap peers.

Sceptical bullet: The implicit assertion underlying the "no hedging required" policy is that operating margins are sufficiently high and natural hedges sufficiently strong that FX volatility can be absorbed. At 17.9% Data Center EBITA margins, this holds. At the 0.5% Fiber Solutions EBITA margin recorded in 2025, a 5% FX headwind on European/North American Fiber revenues could produce a loss with no hedge buffer — which is precisely what happened in EUR-currency operations (€−7m operating loss in 2025). The policy is margin-dependent and becomes inappropriate at trough margins.

This reverses the conventional framing where HTRO is viewed primarily as a USD/BEAD story.

Sceptical bullet: All sensitivities above are static — they assume operating profit in local currency stays constant as FX moves. In reality, a sharp SEK strengthening typically coincides with global risk-off environments that also suppress end-market demand (infrastructure capex), creating a correlated shock: lower volumes AND adverse FX simultaneously. The true stressed EBITA impact of a "SEK strength + weak North American FTTH demand" scenario could be 2–3x the standalone FX sensitivity.

[A] Six sector FX hedging principles applied to HTRO:

  1. Duration > Ratio: Hedging duration (i.e., how far forward you commit) matters more than the hedge ratio for earnings predictability. HTRO hedges nothing forward — zero duration — meaning all FX exposure is spot-rate dependent. Prysmian extends hedges 12–36 months on project contracts, providing multi-year earnings visibility. This is a structural advantage for Prysmian on large EPC jobs.

  2. Counter-cyclical > Pro-cyclical: Ideal hedgers buy protection when FX is stable and sell when stressed. HTRO's structural hedge (localisation) is inherently pro-cyclical: if USD weakens sharply (as in 2024–2026), US-manufactured goods become cheaper in global markets, potentially opening export opportunities — but this benefit does not accrue to HTRO's SEK-reporting shareholders who still see a translated USD profit shrinkage.

  3. Instrument mix: No instruments at HTRO (structural only). Prysmian uses forwards + swaps; Nexans uses forwards + copper derivatives (dual-exposure tool); Belden uses forwards + net investment hedges. The lack of any derivative programme at HTRO is the most notable differentiator.

  4. Disclosed ≠ Realised: Even well-hedged peers such as Prysmian show residual FX impacts because project hedge programmes are imperfect and translation is never hedged. HTRO's "translation headwind, limited EBITA impact" narrative is a disclosed claim — realised performance must be tracked against it quarter by quarter.

  5. Natural > Financial: For HTRO, the natural hedge quality is genuinely high (local manufacturing in USD, GBP, EUR regions). This is the correct primary tool. The question is whether the residual 10–15% mismatch from input procurement currencies is material enough to warrant financial hedging — management has judged it is not.

  6. Boring hedger wins: Companies with systematic, low-drama hedging programmes (consistent 12-month rolling cash flow hedges, well-documented) consistently outperform on earnings predictability. HTRO is not a "boring hedger" — its FX line is structurally volatile on a reported-SEK basis.

[A] HTRO's disclosure quality is materially below peer average. The IFRS 7 sensitivity table is present and informative, but there is no currency revenue bridge (unlike Prysmian and Nexans), no disclosed natural hedge ratio, no forward FX guidance for the coming year, and no management commentary on the economics of the hedging decision. The absence of these disclosures is itself an investment risk — it prevents investors from accurately modelling FX sensitivity.

[A] Prysmian and Nexans both publish explicit currency revenue bridges in results presentations (e.g., "organic growth +5%, FX −3%, scope +2%, total +4%"). HTRO provides a single-line FX percentage impact on revenue in its results summaries — this is minimal disclosure by cable sector standards.

[I] Belden (USD 2.5bn revenue, multi-currency) reported FX as approximately −3% revenue impact in 2024, broadly comparable to HTRO's −5% in 2025. Belden's active cash flow hedge programme did not eliminate the impact but moderated it relative to unhedged scenario. ❌ HTRO's FX disclosure quality is the weakest in its peer group for a company of equivalent revenue complexity. Sceptical bullet: Peer comparison is imperfect because HTRO's business model (mid-cap, acquisition-driven, locally manufacturing) is genuinely different from Prysmian's project-intensive model. The "local mfg = zero need for financial hedges" thesis is coherent for a pure domestic business; for a company that reports in SEK but generates 88% of revenue outside Sweden, the case for some level of financial hedging — even if only translation hedging for key balance sheet exposures — is stronger than management currently acknowledges.

This implies approximately zero incremental FX headwind from USD vs the current run rate, but no reversal of the 2024–2025 losses.

[A] EUR/SEK outlook: the ECB cutting cycle and EUR softness in 2024 has partially reversed in Q1 2026 as European fiscal spending (defence, infrastructure) firmed. [A] GBP/SEK outlook: GBP has held up relatively better than USD/EUR against SEK, supported by UK sticky inflation and BoE caution. GBP/SEK approximately 12.8–13.2 is the consensus range. A modest GBP firming vs current rates (~12.8) would be mildly positive for HTRO's translated Harsh Environment UK profits.

[I] Macro tail risks relevant to HTRO FX:

§9 — FX as Tailwind/Headwind for Thesis

[J] FX is a net headwind to the thesis over the next 12 months at current rates, primarily through USD/SEK (BEAD uplift partially translated away) and GBP/SEK (UK Harsh Environment growth partially eroded). FX is not a thesis-killer but it is a material modifier: a 1–2% reduction in 2026 adj. EBITA margin attributable to FX is the central case, with bull/bear FX corridors creating an approximately ±SEK 100–200m adj. EBITA swing range.

[A] Thesis FX decomposition for 2026E:

The HTRO investment thesis rests on three pillars: (1) BEAD-driven North American FTTH recovery (H2 2026 onwards), (2) Data Center continued organic growth at 30%+ rates, (3) Harsh Environment UK/offshore structural demand. FX impacts all three:

  1. BEAD / North American Fiber Solutions: USD is the revenue currency. At current USD/SEK ~9.8 vs 2022/2023 peak ~11.0, the FX-neutral recovery scenario must generate approximately 12% more USD volume to produce the same SEK revenue as the 2022/2023 cycle peak. BEAD programs are large enough to achieve this — but only if USD/SEK does not decline further.

  2. Data Center: Primarily USD (North America, SEK 501m) and EUR (Europe, SEK 903m) revenue. The USD Data Center growth trajectory (+43% in 2025, likely +25–35% in 2026) is sufficiently strong that even a 5–10% USD/SEK headwind leaves adj. EBITA contribution growing. EUR Data Center is currently in an aggregate loss year — strengthening USD and recovering EUR margins are both needed for this segment to reach its full potential.

  3. Harsh Environment / UK: GBP-denominated; profitability rising sharply (£16m operating profit in 2025 vs £8m in 2024). A GBP recovery vs SEK (toward 13.0–13.5) would amplify the already-improving EBITA trajectory. A further GBP weakness would dampen it. GBP is the FX asymmetry with most upside in the thesis.

[A] Quantifying the FX-dependence of the thesis: using the IFRS 7 sensitivity data, the 2026 EPS outcome is approximately:
- FX-neutral scenario (currencies flat vs 2025 averages): adj. EBITA approximately SEK 700–800m (consensus), EPS approximately SEK 3.0–3.5
- USD +10% scenario (USD/SEK recovers to ~10.8): adj. EBITA approximately SEK 708–808m (+8m FX tailwind), immaterial
- USD −10% scenario (USD/SEK falls to ~8.8): adj. EBITA approximately SEK 692–792m (−8m FX headwind), approximately −1% to −2%
- GBP −10% scenario (GBP/SEK to ~11.5): adj. EBITA approximately SEK 679–779m (−21m), approximately −2.6% to −3.0%
- ALL-IN −10% adverse (all currencies weaken vs SEK): adj. EBITA approximately SEK 595–752m (−37 to −44m), approximately −5.5% to −6.5% reduction

[A] FX bear case reverse-engineering: for FX to become a thesis-killer in 2026, USD/SEK would need to fall to approximately 8.0 or below (a further ~18% USD decline) — combined with EUR softness and GBP weakness. In this extreme scenario, North American revenues would be approximately SEK 500m lower than at current spot, wiping out most of the BEAD uplift. Probability assessment: this scenario requires a severe US recession + continued Riksbank hawkishness + global risk-on (SEK-buying) — low probability but not negligible given the late-cycle macro environment.

[A] The key FX asymmetry in the thesis: if the Iran conflict drives a risk-off surge into USD (a USD/SEK recovery toward 10.5–11.0), HTRO's North American revenue would receive a windfall SEK uplift of approximately SEK 100–200m on unchanged volumes — an unmodelled earnings beat risk that consensus does not price. This is the FX upside case for the thesis.

[I] Translation vs transaction decomposition of FX impact: approximately 80–85% of HTRO's FX earnings sensitivity is translation (subsidiaries' functional-currency profits translated into SEK) and 15–20% is transaction (specific cross-currency purchases, primarily European entities sourcing in USD). This weighting means the primary FX lever is the SEK/G10 exchange rate complex, not specific bilateral transactions.

[A] The shift in segment mix toward Data Center (higher USD/EUR revenue concentration, higher margins) and Harsh Environment (GBP-heavy, improving margins) is slowly improving the quality of HTRO's FX position. Data Center's 19% EBITA margin means USD/EUR revenue from this segment has a higher earnings multiplier than Fiber Solutions' sub-7% margin. As Data Center grows from 19% to perhaps 25–30% of revenue by 2027/2028, USD exposure becomes proportionally more valuable on a per-SEK basis.

[A] One underappreciated FX mechanism: HTRO's acquisition strategy has been largely USD/GBP/EUR (KNET, Impact Data Solutions, USNet, Communication Zone all acquired in non-SEK currencies). As these entities are marked-to-market on the balance sheet in SEK, a weaker USD directly reduces the SEK value of acquired goodwill — creating a potential impairment risk if USD falls significantly (a SEK 88m goodwill write-down risk scenario at USD/SEK ~8.0). While not an immediate concern, it adds to the downside optionality of the FX bear case.

Confidence in FX-resilience of thesis: medium plausibility — natural hedge is structurally sound, BEAD volume uplift is large enough to absorb moderate USD headwinds, but sustained SEK strength at current levels creates a material drag on reported EBITA recovery trajectory through 2026–2027.
