membership penetration. These markets demonstrate a distinct "penetration premium" compared to the rest of
the continent, a structural advantage that is expected to persist. A key driver of this high penetration is
affordability, as Nordic ﬁtness club memberships are the most affordable in Europe when measured against
median income levels and overall leisure spending.

Favorable generation dynamics also fuel industry expansion, with younger demographics proving to be highly
ﬁtness-oriented and likely to maintain these habits as they age. Additionally, the sector serves as a critical
countermeasure to the global health challenge of physical inactivity, elevating the societal importance of
accessible ﬁtness facilities. From a macroeconomic perspective, the ﬁtness industry has historically
demonstrated strong resilience through economic cycles, buoyed by loyal member bases and entrenched lifestyle
habits.

upstream component, encompassing cleaners, customer support agents, and specialized staff like

1. Industry Overview

SATS operates in the Nordic fitness market (Norway, Sweden, Denmark, Finland). The Nordic market was valued at approximately USD 3.1bn in 2024. This is a separate market from the European mainstream low-cost gym market that Basic-Fit and The Gym Group operate in.

Nordic market characteristics differ materially from continental Europe:
- Higher average income per capita → higher ARPM sustainable
- Greater outdoor/active culture → higher baseline fitness consciousness
- Lower population density (ex-urban Norway, Finland) → fewer viable club locations
- Stronger group class tradition — Les Mills, spinning, yoga are mainstream expectations in mid-market Nordic gyms
- Higher construction costs and labour costs, particularly in Norway

Penetration: The Nordic region has higher fitness penetration than continental Europe generally. Norway's gym penetration is estimated at 20-25% of the adult population — among the highest in Europe. This limits the volume growth potential vs underpenetrated southern European markets.

2. Value Chain & Economics

The SATS value chain is more complex than the basic low-cost gym model because of the group exercise component:

Member acquisition: Digital-first but supported by in-club sales teams at higher-traffic locations. Members self-select into tiered plans. SATS has a strong retention-focused model — group class regulars churn at significantly lower rates than equipment-only users.

Club operations: More staff-intensive than low-cost operators. Group class instructors are a significant cost line — either employed directly or contracted. Energy costs are elevated (pool facilities in some clubs, larger facility footprints). Cleaning and maintenance intensity is higher than a basic gym floor.

Group exercise infrastructure: SATS holds Les Mills licensing agreements. This provides a branded class programme that members recognise and value, but comes at a per-member royalty cost. The move to more classes (8% increase in 2024) requires instructor hiring or freelance contracting.

Equipment suppliers: Same dynamics as European low-cost market but lower purchasing scale advantage. SATS's 272-club network is ~20% the size of Basic-Fit's, limiting volume negotiating power.

Technology: SATS has invested in a proprietary app. In-app booking for group classes is a genuine differentiator — it captures member behaviour data and creates stickiness beyond the gym floor. SATS Online (digital/streaming) is a separate revenue stream, small but growing.

Where margins accrue: Club-level margin is lower than low-cost operators due to staff and instructor costs. The premium ARPM (~€56/month vs €24/month for Basic-Fit) partially compensates. Corporate overhead per member is higher because the network is smaller.

3. Competitive Landscape

Nordic market tiers:

Tier 1 (mid-market, group exercise included):
- SATS ASA: dominant. No comparable listed peer in Nordics.
- ACTIC: smaller, Sweden-focused, sold to private operators multiple times. Less financially robust.

Tier 2 (low-cost, equipment only):
- Evo Fitness: Norway and Sweden. Growing. Pricing at NOK 299-399/month. No group classes. Direct competition for price-sensitive SATS members.
- Fitness World / Clever fit (Denmark): largest Danish low-cost operator, ~180 clubs. Competes directly in SATS's Danish market.

Tier 3 (premium):
- Local premium clubs, hotel gyms: not a material competitive threat to SATS's volume base.

Competition intensity in the Nordics: Moderate. SATS holds dominant share in the mid-market. The budget segment (Evo, Fitness World in Denmark) is growing but has not structurally disrupted SATS's member base yet.

International competition: Non-existent in SATS's core markets. Basic-Fit has no presence in Norway or Sweden. The Nordic market is geographically and culturally distinct enough to deter easy entry by European low-cost chains.

4. Barriers to Entry & Exit

Entry barriers in the Nordic market:

  • Cultural/language: Gym operator success in Norway and Sweden requires local language capability, local regulatory knowledge, and culturally-attuned marketing.
  • Real estate: Norwegian and Swedish real estate is expensive. Large gym spaces in urban centres (SATS's core location type) are scarce and costly. New entrants face significant real estate competition.
  • Group exercise programming: Running a credible group exercise studio requires certified instructors, Les Mills licensing, and scheduling infrastructure. This takes time and capital to build.
  • Penetration level: Norway's already-high gym penetration (20-25%) means the incremental market available to a new entrant is limited. Market share must be taken, not created.

What locks in competitors once entered:

Long leases (10-15 years minimum), sunk cost in instructor relationships and member base, Les Mills licensing contracts. Exit is costly — closures require lease negotiation, redundancy payments, and equipment disposal.

Structural exits: ACTIC has been sold multiple times — a signal that mid-market Nordic fitness is difficult for smaller operators. The market tends towards SATS dominance by default.

Revenue drivers:
1. Member retention / churn reduction (primary lever — group class regulars churn significantly less)
2. ARPM growth (tiered pricing, premium tier migration)
3. Net member additions (limited in high-penetration markets)
4. Digital revenue growth (SATS Online, small but incremental)

Cost structure: Semi-fixed. Instructor costs are the largest variable line — more classes require more instructors. Staff costs run at 40-45% of revenue (estimated). Rent is fixed. Energy is variable.

Capital intensity: Lower than European low-cost chains in absolute terms (fewer new openings). CapEx is oriented toward refurbishment and technology rather than greenfield expansion. Operating cash flow of NOK 509m in 2024 funds capex, dividend, and debt service comfortably.

NWC dynamics: Similar to low-cost operators — members pay in advance, creating negative working capital and good cash flow. Corporate memberships are typically invoiced monthly but prepaid annually.

Cash conversion: Strong. NOK 509m operating cash flow in 2024. Dividend of NOK 0.67/share semi-annually = significant return of capital relative to market cap.

Operating leverage: Now becoming visible. FY2024 EBITDA grew at 20% on 7% revenue growth — the fixed cost base is absorbing membership price increases and class expansion with high incremental margins.

What breaks the model:
- Price-sensitive members downgrading to Evo/budget alternatives in a meaningful wave
- Instructor costs rising structurally (Norway minimum wages are already high; any further increase compounds)
- Real estate cost step-change on lease renewals — Norwegian and Swedish property markets are expensive
- Failure to grow in Sweden and Denmark at rates that justify the cost base there

8. Key Success Factors

Retention, not acquisition, is the dominant value driver. A member who attends 4+ group classes/week is structurally unlikely to leave. SATS's strategy of increasing class offerings and workout cadence per member is the right strategic response to its market position.

ARPM growth requires genuine product investment. Unlike low-cost operators that can tier without adding product, SATS must deliver genuine value (more classes, better equipment, digital content) to justify ARPM increases above the NOK 399/month budget alternative.

Scale in each country matters. SATS's Norwegian density (117 clubs) creates genuine network benefits — members can access any Norwegian club. In Sweden and Denmark, the estate is smaller and network benefits are weaker.

Corporate partnerships are structurally advantageous. Norwegian employers routinely subsidise gym membership. This creates a captive B2B channel with low churn. Replicating this in Sweden, Denmark, and Finland is a material opportunity.

Sustainable margin level: EBITDA margin (pre-IFRS 16) of 15-20% appears achievable as the estate matures and operating leverage continues. Expanding to 20%+ requires either sustained ARPM growth or further cost discipline (or both).

9. Industry Evolution

Nordic-specific:
- The Nordic market is relatively mature in penetration terms. Volume growth (new members) is limited compared to continental Europe. Value growth (ARPM, retention, ARPU expansion) is the primary lever.
- Budget fitness is growing in the Nordics but has not disrupted SATS's core member base. The quality and loyalty of SATS's group-class member base is a meaningful structural buffer.
- Digital fitness (SATS Online, Les Mills on-demand) is an incremental supplement, not a substitute. Nordic gym culture is social and community-driven — digital-only does not replicate this.
- Sustainability and ESG considerations are prominent in Nordic fitness. SATS has net-zero targets; Norwegian corporate culture expects environmental credentials.

Consolidation: SATS is the natural acquirer of smaller Nordic operators. ACTIC has changed hands multiple times — SATS could acquire to deepen Swedish density.

Where pricing power is moving: To SATS in the mid-market. Budget operators cannot add group exercise; premium operators are too expensive. SATS occupies the value-for-money mid-market position that is hardest to displace.

International expansion: SATS has deliberately stayed in the Nordics. Any expansion into Germany, Benelux, or UK would require capital and management bandwidth it has not historically deployed.

10. Sceptical View

Bull case: SATS is the dominant Nordic fitness platform with no comparable rival.
Bear case: Dominance in a small, high-penetration market. Norway, Sweden, Denmark, Finland together have ~25 million people. At 20-25% penetration, the addressable volume is effectively close to saturation.

Bull case: Group exercise creates high retention and pricing power.
Bear case: Les Mills licensing is a recurring cost; instructor wages in Norway are among the highest in Europe. The premium ARPM is partially consumed by premium operating costs. If Evo adds one group class or if members perceive the value gap narrowing, migration risk increases.

Bull case: 20% EBITDA growth in 2024 signals improving operating leverage.
Bear case: 2024 benefited from post-pandemic recovery, strategic price increases, and equipment lifespan extension driving lower depreciation. These are partly one-off effects. Sustaining 15-20% EBITDA growth in a maturing high-penetration market with structurally high labour costs is demanding.

Bull case: Corporate membership base is sticky and defensible.
Bear case: If Norwegian or Swedish employers tighten benefits in an economic slowdown, corporate memberships do not renew or are renegotiated at lower subsidy levels. The corporate book is a concentration risk if it represents 20%+ of revenues.

Bull case: Semi-annual dividend and 61% payout signals cash generation confidence.
Bear case: 61% payout constrains capital available for growth or M&A. If SATS faces a competitive challenge from a well-capitalised budget entrant, it may need to cut the dividend to fund defensive investment.

Structural competitive position: YELLOW. SATS holds genuine market leadership in a defensible Nordic niche. The business model is improving in quality and cash generation. The constraints are structural: small addressable market, high labour cost base, limited international optionality, and a budget-gym threat that is real if not yet severe.
