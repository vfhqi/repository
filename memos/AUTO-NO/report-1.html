KEY FINDINGS

Store Holdings operates at a critical inflection point where exceptional underlying business fundamentals clash violently with severe near-term cyclical headwinds, creating a complex investment landscape that defies simple categorization. The company commands a 97% market share in cubic automated storage technology and delivers industry-leading 70%+ gross margins through a highly scalable, asset-light partner distribution model that generates structural profitability vastly superior to legacy capital goods peers. However, this financial dominance masks a dangerous vulnerability: less than 10% of revenue comes from recurring streams, leaving the company catastrophically exposed to brutal project-based cyclicality that forced revenue to contract 10.4% in 2025 following a protracted warehouse automation trough.

The company's pristine balance sheet with net debt at just 0.7x EBITDA and robust free cash flow generation provides crucial strategic flexibility, yet management's aggressive transition toward the Store-as-a-Service subscription model is deliberately cannibalizing near-term statutory revenues in exchange for long-term recurring quality that may take years to materialize. While Store benefits from powerful secular tailwinds including chronic labor shortages, wage inflation, and 80% of global warehouses remaining entirely manual, the immediate macro environment remains treacherous as elevated interest rates continue to freeze customer capital expenditure decisions and extend already-lengthy project conversion timelines.

The land-and-expand revenue engine delivering 60% of quarterly sales from existing customers demonstrates exceptional customer loyalty and prohibitive switching costs once systems are embedded, but emerging agile nomous Mobile Robot competitors from China threaten to permanently disrupt this locked-in installed base by offering vastly superior deployment flexibility without requiring massive permanent grid infrastructure. Management's recent executive overhaul, workforce restructuring, and $10 million cost reduction program signal decisive action to right-size operations during the downturn, though persistent forecasting failures and guidance misses raise serious questions about pipeline visibility and the leadership team's ability to accurately predict conversion dynamics in this volatile environment.

The company's heavy reliance on external integration partners like Swisslog, Dematic, and Kardex creates structural tension, as these independent entities increasingly manufacture competing proprietary shuttle systems and possess conflicting financial incentives that could systematically stifle Store's penetration into large-scale enterprise deployments. Perhaps most critically, Store's historically deficient end-to-end warehouse execution software restricts viability in massive high-throughput e-commerce facilities, forcing dependency on third-party integrator platforms that frequently fail to optimize the underlying hardware and leaving the company vulnerable to software-first competitors who embed intelligence directly into their offerings.

The late 2025 order inflection with Q1 2026 beating consensus by 14% on revenue suggests the cyclical trough may have finally passed, yet bears correctly highlight that this apparent momentum was artificially inflated by aggressive project pull-forwards that cannibalized future quarters and perpetuate dangerous quarterly lumpiness. With massive untapped white space in an immature $225 billion addressable market and automation penetration languishing near 15% globally, the multi-decade structural growth runway remains intact, but the pathway from current depressed volumes to sustainably recapturing hyper-growth economics will require successfully navigating ongoing geopolitical volatility, commodity inflation risks, intensifying Asian price competition, and the existential threat of flexible robotics rendering rigid cubic storage architecturally obsolete.

SECTION 1 — GENERAL: 1. Description

Core Business and Technology: Store Holdings is a Norway-based robotics and software technology company that pioneered the cubic automated storage and retrieval system. The system utilizes a compact, vertical aluminum grid where products are stored in bins and retrieved by autonomous robots moving across the top. This highly standardized, modular design allows customers to store up to four times more products in the exact same warehouse footprint compared to traditional shelving.

Value Proposition: The company's core purpose is to automate and optimize order fulfillment, enabling goods to arrive faster with minimal environmental impact. The technology delivers exceptional energy efficiency, with ten Store robots consuming the same amount of power as a single standard vacuum cleaner. Furthermore, the system boasts a 99.7% worldwide uptime and 99.9% picking accuracy, driving a typical customer return on investment within one to three years.

Software-Led Operations: Store operates a system-led approach where advanced software functions as the operational brain coordinating robot movements, bin management, and order fulfillment. The recently launched CubeVerse platform integrates AI-driven analytics and cloud software to optimize warehouse traffic and enhance throughput without requiring additional physical hardware. This software integration creates a high barrier to entry and serves as the foundation for the company's long-term customer value proposition.

Asset-Light Business Model: Store employs an asset-light, outsourced business model by relying on a global network of 23 distribution partners, including Swisslog, Dematic, and Kardex. These partners handle the direct sales, system integration, installation, and ongoing maintenance, allowing Store to focus purely on core technology development and manufacturing. This channel strategy enables Store to maintain exceptional profitability, consistently delivering gross margins above 70% and adjusted EBITDA margins near 45%.

Go-to-Market Evolution: Recently, the company has evolved from a strictly partner-led model to a more integrated go-to-market approach emphasizing direct customer intimacy. Store established an enterprise account management framework to deepen relationships with large, multi-site customers like DSV and CVS. This land-and-expand strategy has been highly successful, generating roughly 60% of recent quarterly revenue directly from existing customers expanding their initial installations.

Product Innovation Strategy: The company operates on a structured bi-annual product launch cycle to accelerate innovation and expand its total addressable market. Strategic product priorities include optimizing the core cube, enabling new use cases, and introducing adjacent workflows. Recent hardware additions include the Multi-Temperature Solution for frozen grocery applications, FlexBins for mixed-size storage, and Case for automated case induction.

Recurring Revenue Transition: A key financial priority is transitioning toward higher-quality recurring revenue streams to mitigate inherent project-based cyclicality. To achieve this, Store launched Store-as-a-Service, a flexible, operational expenditure-based subscription model designed to lower the initial capital barrier for customers. The company is also aggressively monetizing its software suite through standardized packages to increase software's share of the overall revenue mix.

Commercial Organization: Operationally, Store recently regionalized its commercial organization to enable faster local decision-making and greater agility closer to the end customer. This regional structure is supported by a global commercial framework that ensures best practices and standardization are repeated efficiently at scale. Management also deliberately reallocated internal resources away from sustaining mature product lines toward high-potential growth segments like the United States market and high-throughput applications.

Product and Supply Chain Structure: Internally, the company transformed its product division into a fully accountable Product Business Unit guided by data-driven insights and customer return on investment. On the supply side, Store maintains a diversified, outsourced manufacturing base with dual assembly plants located in Poland and Thailand. This decentralized supply chain strategy provides crucial resilience against input cost volatility and short-term fluctuations in global aluminum prices.

Financial and Strategic Objectives: Store's overarching financial objective is to capture white space in an immature market where roughly 80% of global warehouses remain entirely manual. The company targets revenue growth that structurally outpaces the broader automated storage market while sustaining its high profit margins and strong cash conversion rates. Management remains highly disciplined in capital allocation, prioritizing organic reinvestment while maintaining flexibility for strategic acquisitions and shareholder returns.

Peer Rating (A-): Store earns an A- rating compared to industrial capital goods peers for its disruptive cubic storage technology and highly scalable, asset-light model. The company offers a rare combination of high growth potential, industry-leading margins, and exceptional cash generation that traditional legacy intralogistics peers fundamentally struggle to match.

Bearish View (Disruptive Competition): The warehouse automation market is facing intense disruption from agile Asian robotics players and nomous Mobile Robot vendors. These alternative systems offer a highly flexible, infrastructure-light alternative that can achieve greater throughput rates than cubic storage without requiring a massive, rigid grid investment.

Bearish View (Partner Disintermediation): Store's heavy reliance on external integration partners intrinsically distances the company from the end user. Because integrators are financially motivated to maximize their own revenue, they frequently push their own proprietary shuttle solutions over Store's technology, potentially stifling Store's growth in large-scale enterprise deployments.

Bearish View (Software Deficiencies): Historically, Store has struggled to provide a fully comprehensive, end-to-end warehouse execution software suite comparable to its legacy competitors. This persistent software gap restricts its viability in massive, high-throughput e-commerce operations, forcing customers to rely on third-party integrator software that frequently fails to optimize the underlying hardware.

Bearish View (Severe Cyclical Exposure): The company currently derives less than 10% of its total revenue from recurring software and service streams. This overwhelmingly project-based revenue profile leaves the company acutely vulnerable to severe cyclical downturns and sudden customer capital expenditure freezes.

Historical Revenue Trajectory: Store exhibited hyper-growth characteristics through the pandemic and immediate post-pandemic period, delivering an outstanding 79.9% top-line expansion in 2021 followed by 78.1% in 2022.

Historical Revenue Trajectory (cont.): This aggressive expansion was fueled by significant e-commerce capacity additions and historically low interest rates that stimulated warehouse automation capital expenditures across Europe and North America.

Historical Revenue Trajectory (cont.): Following this boom, the company entered a severe cyclical correction, with total revenue contracting by 6.9% in 2024 and a further 10.4% in 2025 as the warehouse capital expenditure cycle hit a protracted trough.

Gross Margin Resilience: Despite the severe revenue cyclicality, Store has successfully defended its industry-leading gross margin profile, which rebounded to an impressive 73.2% in 2024 and stabilized near 72.3% in 2025.

Gross Margin Resilience (cont.): The company's highly standardized, modular product architecture and outsourced manufacturing strategy provide substantial pricing power and structural insulation against modest volume declines.

Gross Margin Resilience (cont.): Management deliberately shifted sourcing to alternative facilities, such as its Thailand plant, to counter localized supply chain inflation and maintain this structurally high profitability.

Operating Leverage and Adjusted EBITDA: Store operates with tremendous fixed-cost operating leverage, historically converting robust gross margins into adjusted EBITDA margins approaching the 50% threshold during peak volume years.

Operating Leverage and Adjusted EBITDA (cont.): However, as revenue contracted sequentially through 2024 and 2025, the company experienced reverse operating leverage, causing adjusted EBITDA margins to compress from 47.8% in 2023 down to roughly 41.9% by 2025. This margin compression was exacerbated by management's strategic decision to sustain high research and development and sales staffing investments despite the cyclical revenue downturn.

Statutory Profitability Volatility: The company's reported net income and unadjusted EBIT have historically exhibited extreme volatility due to massive one-off adjustments, including substantial litigation expenses and non-cash option costs. For example, reported EBIT plummeted to negative USD 38 million in 2021 before recovering to USD 223 million in 2024 as these exceptional corporate items and legal settlements naturally rolled off the income statement.

Statutory Profitability Volatility (cont.): Consequently, adjusted metrics are fundamentally required to accurately assess Store's true underlying operational cash-generation capacity.

Net Working Capital Dynamics: Store's net working capital requirements have historically been highly efficient, occasionally dipping into negative territory, such as the negative USD 28 million recorded during the 2021 supply chain squeeze. More recently, working capital intensity has deteriorated significantly, with NWC to sales spiking from 4.2% in 2023 to 27.1% in 2025. This rapid buildup was driven by an active decision to elevate internal inventory buffers to manage supply chain unpredictability alongside a slowdown in customer installation schedules.

Capital Expenditure Profile: The company's asset-light business model is visually evident in its incredibly low tangible capital expenditure requirements, which peaked at merely USD 16 million in 2024 despite generating over USD 600 million in global sales. The vast majority of Store's recurring investment is allocated toward capitalized development costs to fund software engineering and next-generation robotic platforms.

Capital Expenditure Profile (cont.): This structural lack of heavy manufacturing maintenance capex empowers the company with a massive structural cash flow advantage over legacy intralogistics peers.

Return on Invested Capital : Store generated exceptional after-tax adjusted ROIC figures during the e-commerce boom, peaking at 17.5% in 2023 as high-margin project deliveries naturally flowed through the P&L. However, ROIC degraded sharply to 11.4% in 2025, penalized by the dual headwinds of declining absolute operating profits and an inflated capital base tied to excessive working capital accumulation.

Return on Invested Capital (cont.): A sustained recovery in ROIC is strictly dependent on the broader warehouse automation capex cycle returning to structural growth.

Deleveraging and Balance Sheet Strength: Following a highly leveraged private equity ownership phase, Store has aggressively repaired its balance sheet, driving net financial debt from USD 657 million in 2020 down to roughly USD 167 million by 2025. This organic cash generation successfully collapsed the company's net debt to adjusted EBITDA ratio to a highly conservative 0.7x in 2025.

Deleveraging and Balance Sheet Strength (cont.): This pristine balance sheet provides management with deep strategic flexibility to navigate the current cyclical trough without requiring dilutive external capital injections.

Capital Allocation Strategy: Historically, Store has aggressively prioritized organic reinvestment and internal deleveraging over direct shareholder remuneration, paying zero common dividends over the last eight years. Share buybacks have also been practically non-existent outside of specialized corporate transactions, emphasizing a pure-play growth equity capital allocation philosophy.

Capital Allocation Strategy (cont.): Looking forward, analysts increasingly view the company's robust free cash flow and under-levered balance sheet as an inevitable catalyst for the initiation of material share repurchases.

Financial Management Rating (A-): Store earns an A- rating for its overall financial profile. The company operates an inherently superior, high-margin, asset-light financial model that generates massive structural cash flow. While current ROIC and top-line growth have undoubtedly suffered due to severe end-market cyclicality, management's ability to defend 70%+ gross margins during a demand depression highlights exceptional pricing power and disciplined operational execution.

Bearish View (Commodity Margin Squeeze): Skeptical analysts explicitly warn that Store's industry-leading 70%+ gross margins are unsustainable and inherently vulnerable to surging global aluminum prices.

Bearish View (Commodity Margin Squeeze) (cont.): Because aluminum profiles constitute roughly one-third of the system's cost of goods sold, sudden commodity inflation functions as a direct tax on the company's profitability, particularly if intense market competition prevents management from raising end-customer prices.

Bearish View (Cyclical Revenue Base): Short sellers frequently highlight that Store generates less than 10% of its revenue from high-quality, recurring software or service streams. This overwhelming reliance on massive, one-off capital hardware installations forces the company to entirely replenish its order book every single year, leaving financial forecasts dangerously exposed to sudden macroeconomic shocks and customer budget freezes.

Bearish View (Permanent Multiple Contraction): Bears argue that Store's previous hyper-growth valuation multiples were a zero-interest-rate phenomenon that will permanently compress toward legacy industrial averages.

Bearish View (Permanent Multiple Contraction) (cont.): With revenue organically contracting over consecutive years, the market will inherently refuse to value the company as a disruptive technology asset, instead pricing it as a cyclical capital goods manufacturer with limited forward visibility.

Revenue Scale and Segment Focus: Store generated USD 541 million to USD 601 million in recent annual revenue, making it a highly focused, pure-play warehouse automation technology provider compared to diversified legacy peers. Gross Margin Dominance: Store operates with industry-leading gross margins consistently exceeding 70%, driven by its highly standardized, modular product architecture and software-led value proposition. Jefferies notes that Store's gross margins are substantially insulated because it focuses on the highest value-add components (R&D and proprietary technology) rather than low-margin third-party hardware integration. System integrators like Swisslog, Dematic, and Honeywell Intelligrated typically report significantly lower gross margins because they operate as general contractors, purchasing and integrating third-party equipment which inherently dilutes their overall profitability.

Unit Costs and Supply Chain Structure: Store's unit costs are highly dependent on global aluminum prices and raw materials, as aluminum profiles form the core of its storage grids. To optimize manufacturing unit costs and protect its margins against regional inflation, Store maintains a diversified, outsourced production footprint with dual assembly plants in Poland and Thailand. Legacy peers like KION face higher structural manufacturing costs and are heavily investing in internal capacity, such as a EUR 100 million strategic investment to build a new SCS manufacturing facility in Jinan, China.

EBITDA and Operating Profitability: Store's operating profitability dramatically outpaces the broader intralogistics industry, consistently delivering adjusted EBITDA margins between 42% and 48%. DNB Carnegie explicitly highlights that Store's 50% historical EBITDA margin profile makes it an anomaly compared to traditional capital goods peers, supported by a highly scalable partner-based go-to-market model. In comparison, Kardex reported a 2025 EBIT margin of 11.9%, Jungheinrich achieved an adjusted EBIT margin of 8.1%, and KION's SCS segment delivered a 6.0% adjusted EBIT margin.

Fixed Cost Operating Leverage: Store benefits from tremendous fixed-cost operating leverage because its primary expenses are concentrated in personnel, research and development, and corporate IT. Morgan Stanley notes that as Store transitions to winning larger, high-throughput contracts, the company may need to structurally elevate its fixed R&D investments, which could moderately pressure its robust historical margin profile during demand troughs. Conversely, Jungheinrich is currently executing a massive restructuring program aimed at achieving EUR 100 million in annual cost savings to mitigate its heavy fixed-cost manufacturing burdens and improve organizational efficiency.

Capital Intensity and Asset Lightness: Store operates an extremely asset-light model, relying on a global network of integration partners to handle capital-intensive installation and service tasks. The company targets a capital expenditure-to-sales ratio of approximately 7%, with the vast majority allocated toward capitalized software development rather than physical plants. Kardex, while relatively asset-light compared to forklift OEMs, recently saw its free cash flow decline due to elevated tangible capex required for infrastructure investments and increased net working capital for security stock.

Return on Invested Capital : The structural differences in capital intensity directly translate to return profiles, where asset-light players generate vastly superior capital efficiency. Kardex reported a highly impressive ROIC of 35.0% in 2025, easily exceeding its long-term target of 25%. Traditional forklift and heavy-metal automation providers generate substantially lower returns on capital, with Jungheinrich reporting a Group ROCE of just 8.3% in 2025.

R&D Investments and Innovation: Store allocates approximately 10% of its total revenue toward research and development, which is aggressively capitalized to fund its proprietary software pipeline and next-generation robotic platforms. KION maintains R&D spending at approximately 3% of group revenue, reflecting a mature product portfolio and a heavier reliance on standard mechanical engineering. Jungheinrich similarly reported R&D expenditures of EUR 205 million in 2025, representing a comparatively lower percentage of its massive EUR 5.5 billion revenue base.

Recurring Revenue and Services Mix: A key structural vulnerability for Store versus its peers is its overwhelming reliance on new capital projects, with less than 10% of revenues coming from recurring software or services. KION's SCS segment benefits from a much more resilient mix, generating 41% of its historical revenue from high-margin customer services and aftermarket support. Kardex also enjoys a highly profitable service division, with Life Cycle Services representing approximately 32% of its revenue mix.

Integrator Challenges (Swisslog & Dematic): The financial difficulty of the system integrator model is evident in KUKA's Swisslog division, which historically struggled to generate meaningful profitability and posted low-single-digit EBIT margins. Dematic (KION SCS) similarly suffered severe margin compression in 2022 and 2023 due to fixed-price legacy contracts being devastated by supply chain inflation, though management has successfully driven margins back to 6.0% through improved execution excellence.

Industry Rating (A-): Store earns an A- rating for its comparative financial profile, as its combination of 70%+ gross margins, asset-light scalability, and high cash conversion is fundamentally superior to the heavy-metal capital intensity of Kion and Jungheinrich.

Bearish View (Commodity Squeeze): Norne Securities explicitly points out that Store is highly sensitive to raw material costs, estimating that a 10% increase in global aluminum prices directly translates into a 0.5 percentage point contraction in both gross and EBITDA margins.

Bearish View (Operating De-leverage): Short sellers argue that Store's recent decision to heavily increase fixed sales, marketing, and R&D personnel expenses leaves the company dangerously exposed to reverse operating leverage if the warehouse automation capex trough persists longer than expected.

Bearish View (Integrator Dependence): Because Store relies on partners like Swisslog, Dematic, and Kardex to sell its systems, it is forced to share project economics; these integrators frequently operate on thin margins and may increasingly push their own proprietary hardware to capture a larger share of the total contract value.

Bearish View (Inferior Cyclical Resilience): Unlike Honeywell Intelligrated or KION, which generate massive, predictable cash flows from decades-old installed bases requiring continuous aftermarket service, Store's near-total reliance on greenfield and brownfield capital hardware sales makes its financial predictability inherently inferior during macroeconomic downturns.
