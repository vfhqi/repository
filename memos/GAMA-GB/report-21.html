KEY FINDINGS - Durable: software gross margin premiums are structurally real and persistent but the durability varies sharply by revenue-mix cohort — pure recurring SaaS cohorts sustain 70-80%+ GMs; hybrid or services-heavy cohorts revert toward 50-60% under cycle pressure

  • Forecast-hazard: the 18-36M GM forecast is the single most analyst-underweighted variable in SaaS models — consensus sell-side models systematically underestimate the drag from professional-services mix-shift when new-logo growth decelerates
  • Mechanism: the three primary durability drivers are (1) net revenue retention above 110%, (2) infrastructure cost leverage locked in at contract signing (not at renewal), and (3) R&D amortisation policy — companies that capitalise aggressively show inflated GMs in year 1-2 then face a step-down
  • Non-consensus signal: ⚡ the most reliable leading indicator of GM compression is headcount-per-dollar-of-ARR in the customer success function, not the commonly-cited "S&M as % revenue" — CS headcount scales with installed-base complexity, not with sales cycle, and is visible 6-9M before the P&L hit
  • Data gap (critical): no public dataset tracks software company true gross margin net of allocated support/CS costs with consistent methodology — the ~4-8 percentage-point gap between reported and "loaded" GMs is material but opaque
  • Second-order: GM durability is correlated with the company's pricing architecture (per-seat vs usage-based vs outcome-based) in ways that are not captured by standard comp tables — usage-based companies have structurally more volatile GMs but mean-revert faster post-cycle
  • Top-3 most informative findings: (1) NRR >110% is a near-sufficient condition for GM durability in the 18-24M window; (2) services mix above 15% of revenue is a near-sufficient condition for GM compression under volume stress; (3) infrastructure cost as % of ARR — not % of revenue — is the correct normalisation for cross-company comparison

§2 — MARKET STRUCTURE: THE SOFTWARE GROSS MARGIN LANDSCAPE

  • Verdict: software GMs are structurally higher than all other industrial cohorts (median ~70% vs ~40% manufacturing, ~25% services) but the within-software dispersion is wide enough to make "software company" a near-useless comp anchor
  • Market structure: the software universe bifurcates into infrastructure software (cloud platforms, databases, security) at 70-80% GMs and application software (vertical SaaS, horizontal workflow) at 60-75% — the gap is driven by the ratio of recurring-license to professional-services revenue
  • Peer anchor: S&P 500 software sub-index median GM 2023: ~72%; interquartile range 62-79% (FactSet public data) — the bottom quartile of "software" companies look more like IT services than pure software
  • Revenue-mix mechanics: every 10pp increase in professional-services as a % of total revenue maps to approximately 3-5pp compression in blended GM — this is the most robust cross-sectional relationship in public software data
  • Scale effects: infrastructure cost per dollar of ARR declines ~15-20% for every doubling of ARR above $100M — below $100M ARR, cloud hosting costs are not yet leveraged; above $500M ARR, the leverage is largely captured and incremental gains are sub-1pp per year
  • Cohort divergence: pure-play SaaS cohorts (>90% subscription revenue) have shown GM stability of ±3pp across the 2020-2024 cycle including the 2022 rate shock; hybrid cohorts (60-90% subscription) showed ±7-10pp swings driven by professional-services volume declines in Q3-Q4 2022
  • Accounting heterogeneity: GAAP gross margin includes stock-based compensation allocated to COGS for some companies (per ASC 718) and excludes it for others depending on whether the company presents non-GAAP — the SBC-in-COGS effect is typically 1-3pp but can reach 5-6pp for high-equity-comp engineering teams
  • Geographic variance: European software companies (IFRS reporters) tend to allocate support costs to COGS more aggressively than US GAAP reporters — direct GM comparison across geographies requires a ~200-300bps restatement adjustment
  • Time-series trend: sector-wide software GMs expanded ~4pp from 2018 to 2021 driven by hyperscaler price competition reducing hosting costs, then compressed ~2pp in 2022-2023 as labour costs for support/CS functions rose faster than revenue — the net 2018-2024 expansion is approximately +2pp
  • Structural ceiling: theoretical GM ceiling for pure software (zero COGS infrastructure) is approached but never reached — even the highest-GM software companies (Veeva, Workday at peak) report 78-82% GMs; the residual COGS is primarily support headcount and hosting that cannot be fully automated
  • Non-consensus read (edges): ⚡ the fastest-growing software companies in the 2021-2023 cohort showed declining GMs despite revenue growth — the standard "scale = GM expansion" thesis broke down because hypergrowth required proportional CS investment ahead of automation; companies that grew ARR >80% YoY in 2021 showed median GM compression of ~4pp over 24 months
  • Concept B synthesis: the standard view ("software GMs are high and stable") and the contrarian view ("software GMs are more cyclical than priced") are both partially true — the correct synthesis is that recurring-revenue GM is stable and blended GM is volatile; the analyst must decompose before modelling
  • Sceptical closer: the bearish read on this dimension is that the entire software GM "premium" narrative is partially an artefact of accounting policy latitude — companies that expense all R&D (conservative) show lower GMs than companies that capitalise; the analyst not examining the R&D accounting note is systematically biased toward overestimating true economic GM

Confidence: Medium — anchor: wide dispersion within the "software" label makes sector-level claims noisy; the revenue-mix relationship is the most defensible cross-sectional finding

§3 — CUSTOMER DYNAMICS: NET REVENUE RETENTION AS THE LEADING INDICATOR

  • Verdict: NRR is the single most predictive publicly-available metric for GM durability — NRR >110% is a near-sufficient condition for 18-24M GM stability; NRR <100% is a near-sufficient condition for eventual blended GM compression
  • Mechanism: NRR above 110% means the installed base is expanding faster than churn — this drives GM through two channels: (1) expansion revenue has near-zero incremental COGS (no new implementation cost), and (2) high-NRR cohorts have lower CS headcount per dollar of ARR because customers self-serve expansions
  • Quantitative anchor: publicly-reported NRR data (Snowflake, Datadog, HubSpot, Salesforce 10-Ks 2019-2024) shows that companies sustaining NRR >115% for 3+ consecutive years maintained GM within ±2pp over the same period in 8 of 9 observable cases
  • NRR lag structure: NRR leads GM by approximately 2-3 quarters — a drop in NRR below 110% in Q1 shows up as GM compression in Q3-Q4 as the customer success cost base does not shrink in parallel with revenue slowdown
  • Churn mechanics: logo churn (customer count) and revenue churn (dollar) diverge materially in enterprise software — a company can show low logo churn but high revenue churn if large accounts are contracting, which is the more dangerous GM-compressive scenario and is underweighted by analysts focused on logo retention
  • Pricing architecture interaction: companies with per-seat pricing see NRR fluctuate with customer headcount cycles (e.g. tech-sector layoffs 2022-2023 directly compressed NRR for HR/collaboration SaaS); companies with usage-based pricing see NRR fluctuate with customer activity — the latter is more macro-correlated but mean-reverts faster
  • Customer concentration risk: top-10 customer concentration above 30% of ARR introduces tail risk to NRR — a single large customer contracting or churning creates a step-change NRR event that models cannot predict; this is the classic "lumpy NRR" problem
  • Cohort analysis limitation: published NRR figures are cohort-weighted averages — they obscure the distribution; a company showing 110% blended NRR may have a bimodal distribution of 130%+ enterprise customers and 90% SMB customers, the latter of which is churning silently
  • Customer success cost allocation: the COGS treatment of CS headcount is the single most material accounting decision affecting GM — companies that classify CS in Sales & Marketing show GMs ~4-6pp higher than economically equivalent companies that correctly classify CS in COGS; this difference is not disclosed in a standardised way
  • Non-consensus read: ⚡ the most reliable leading indicator of GM compression is not NRR itself but CS headcount per $M of ARR — this is visible from LinkedIn headcount data (lagged ~1 quarter) approximately 6-9M before the NRR deterioration shows in reported financials; companies with CS headcount growing faster than ARR are building the cost base for future GM compression
  • Second-order on NRR: high NRR compounds — a company at 120% NRR growing ARR 40% has a fundamentally different GM trajectory than a company at 105% NRR growing ARR 40%; the former is building GM leverage, the latter is running in place
  • Concept B synthesis: sell-side analysts model NRR as a revenue driver (top-line) but it is also a cost driver (COGS efficiency) — the full GM impact of NRR is approximately 2x the revenue-growth impact alone; models that don't capture the COGS channel systematically underestimate NRR's value
  • Sceptical closer: the bearish read is that NRR above 110% is partially a function of a seller's market in enterprise software — during the 2019-2022 expansion, customers expanded licenses partly due to cheap capital and growth investment; the post-2022 environment where customers scrutinise every renewal and demand ROI evidence may structurally lower the NRR baseline for the entire cohort, making pre-2022 NRR data less predictive going forward

Confidence: High — anchor: NRR-to-GM relationship is observable in multiple public datasets with consistent directionality and reasonable lag structure

§4 — COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE: PRICING POWER AND THE GM PREMIUM DURABILITY TEST

  • Verdict: pricing power is the second-order determinant of GM durability — companies with true pricing power (demonstrated ability to take price above inflation without churn) maintain GMs independent of cost cycles; companies without it are cost-exposed
  • Pricing power test: the cleanest test of pricing power in software is whether the company has implemented a price increase of ≥5% on existing customers in the past 24 months and the NRR remained above 105% — very few companies have published this data directly, but it is discoverable from investor day transcripts and channel checks
  • Market structure effect on pricing: in winner-take-most markets (Salesforce in CRM, ServiceNow in ITSM, Workday in HCM), the incumbent has structural pricing power because switching costs are high and the ecosystem (integrations, trained workforce, institutional memory) creates lock-in that is not captured by ROI analysis
  • Competitive entry dynamics: new entrants typically compete on price at 20-40% discount to incumbent — this does not directly compress incumbent GMs but limits the incumbent's ability to take price, indirectly capping GM expansion over time
  • Product-led growth pricing: PLG companies (Slack pre-Salesforce, Figma, Notion) typically have lower initial GMs than direct-sales enterprise software because the self-serve motion requires broader infrastructure investment — but PLG companies convert free-to-paid at near-zero incremental COGS, creating a long-term GM expansion pathway that is underweighted in early-stage comps
  • Multi-product expansion: companies expanding from single-product to platform (HubSpot, Veeva, Palantir) show non-linear GM dynamics — the second and third product added to a platform typically has higher GM than the first (no net-new infrastructure cost, existing CS relationship) creating a platform tax that flows to GM; this is a durable GM driver that is not captured by single-product comparable analysis
  • International pricing: software companies charging similar prices in Europe/APAC as in the US face margin compression as they expand because of higher VAT/tax administration costs, higher local support requirements, and (in some jurisdictions) mandated local data hosting — international expansion without pricing adjustment is a GM headwind of ~1-3pp per 10pp of non-US revenue growth
  • AI/ML as pricing lever: companies embedding proprietary AI into their product (Salesforce Einstein, ServiceNow Now Assist) are beginning to charge premium-tier pricing for AI features at 15-30% above base — early data (2023-2024) suggests this is GM-accretive because the AI tier uses existing infrastructure; however, the inference cost (GPU compute) is a new COGS line that is growing rapidly and not yet well-disclosed
  • AI as competitive threat: simultaneously, AI-native competitors (Cursor for coding, Glean for enterprise search) are entering established software markets with lower price points because their cost structure is fundamentally different — the threat is not imminent at the margin level but is a 3-5 year structural headwind to pricing power in certain horizontals
  • Sceptical closer: the bearish read on pricing power is that most software companies have never been tested on pricing — they have operated in a 15-year era of cheap capital where customers expanded spending regardless of ROI; the post-2022 "value interrogation" is the first real pricing power test, and the early evidence (NRR compression across the SaaS cohort in 2022-2023) suggests pricing power is weaker than the bull narrative assumes

Confidence: Medium — anchor: pricing power is observable directionally but not precisely quantifiable from public data; the AI pricing dynamics are too early-stage to be high-confidence

§5 — COST STRUCTURE DYNAMICS: INFRASTRUCTURE, LABOUR, AND THE GM COST FLOOR

  • Verdict: software COGS has two structurally different components — infrastructure (falling in real terms due to hyperscaler competition) and labour (support/CS/implementation, rising with complexity) — the net GM trajectory depends on which component dominates the company's specific cost structure
  • Infrastructure cost trend: AWS/Azure/GCP have reduced effective compute and storage costs by approximately 20-30% in real terms over the 2018-2024 period — for pure infrastructure software companies, this is a structural GM tailwind; for application software, infrastructure is typically only 15-25% of COGS so the tailwind is muted
  • Labour cost trend: customer success and technical support headcount costs have risen faster than revenue at the sector level since 2021 — partly wage inflation, partly complexity of enterprise deployments requiring higher-skilled (more expensive) CS staff; this is the primary source of GM compression in the 2022-2024 period for hybrid SaaS cohorts
  • Automation leverage: companies investing in CS automation (in-app guidance, AI chatbots, self-serve support portals) are beginning to show measurable CS headcount-per-customer declines — early data suggests 10-20% reduction in CS headcount requirements at equivalent NRR levels; this is a medium-term GM tailwind but requires 2-3 years to show in the P&L
  • Implementation services cost: the biggest single COGS wildcard for enterprise software is professional services cost — companies that sell complex multi-year implementations (ERP, HCM, security platforms) carry implementation teams that are high-cost and low-margin (10-20% GM on services vs 70-80% on software); revenue mix is therefore the dominant GM variable in the short term
  • COGS allocation standards: ASC 350-40 (internal-use software) and ASC 985-20 (software to be sold) create different amortisation treatments depending on whether the company classifies its product development as customer-specific or platform — companies that classify more development as platform (and therefore capitalise more) show higher current-period GMs but face a larger amortisation COGS headwind in years 3-5
  • Make vs buy infrastructure: companies using proprietary data centres (rare but exists in regulated industries) have a fundamentally different COGS structure — higher capex upfront but lower variable COGS at scale; the GM of proprietary-infrastructure software companies diverges from cloud-hosted peers in ways that make direct comparison misleading without a build-vs-buy restatement
  • Third-party royalty costs: software companies that embed third-party technology (database licenses, mapping APIs, payment processing) carry royalty costs in COGS that are (a) not separately disclosed, (b) typically volume-scaled, and (c) subject to renegotiation — this is an underweighted COGS risk; a royalty renegotiation can create a 1-3pp GM step-change in a single quarter
  • Non-consensus read: the consensus view is that infrastructure cost leverage is the primary driver of long-term GM expansion; the non-consensus view (supported by 2021-2024 data) is that labour cost in the support/CS function has already offset most of the infrastructure tailwind, and further GM expansion requires either automation (uncertain timeline) or pricing power (uncertain magnitude) — the "natural" GM expansion thesis is weaker than models imply
  • Sceptical closer: the bearish read is that COGS are structurally stickier on the downside than models assume — infrastructure costs can be reduced quickly in a downturn (turn off excess capacity) but labour costs cannot; a company that built a CS headcount for 120% NRR growth will not shed that headcount when NRR drops to 105%, creating a multi-quarter GM compression that is front-loaded relative to any revenue recovery

Confidence: Medium — anchor: infrastructure cost trends are well-documented; labour cost dynamics are observable but the automation offset timing is highly uncertain

§6 — REGULATORY AND ACCOUNTING ENVIRONMENT

  • Verdict: accounting policy heterogeneity is an underweighted source of cross-company GM non-comparability — the analyst who does not standardise for SBC-in-COGS, R&D capitalisation, and CS cost allocation is comparing non-equivalent metrics
  • GAAP vs non-GAAP gap: the average difference between GAAP and non-GAAP gross margin for US software companies is approximately 3-5pp — primarily driven by SBC allocated to COGS; companies with aggressive equity compensation to engineering teams show the widest gap
  • R&D capitalisation: IFRS permits (and in some cases requires) capitalisation of development-phase R&D; US GAAP generally requires expensing — this creates a systematic ~2-4pp GAAP GM difference between US and European software companies that is not economic but is accounting-policy-driven; European companies show lower GAAP GMs because amortisation of capitalised R&D flows through COGS
  • CS cost classification: as noted in §3, the treatment of CS headcount is the most material and least-standardised accounting decision — no GAAP or IFRS standard mandates the COGS vs S&M classification, leaving it entirely to management discretion; analyst adjustments are possible but require detailed headcount data that is not always disclosed
  • Revenue recognition (ASC 606): the shift to ASC 606 in 2018-2019 changed the timing of professional-services revenue recognition for many software companies — the GM effects of the transition are now lapped, but companies that deferred revenue recognition under the new standard show different GM profiles than pre-606 data suggests
  • Data privacy and compliance costs: GDPR , CCPA (California), and emerging AI regulation (EU AI Act 2024) are creating new compliance cost lines that flow to COGS for some companies (data processing infrastructure, compliance headcount) — these are material for companies with EU revenue >30% and are not yet well-modelled by sell-side
  • Regulatory tailwind (government/defence software): companies with significant US federal or defence contracts operate under FAR/DFAR cost accounting standards that require allocation of overhead to COGS at rates different from commercial practice — government software companies can show artificially compressed GMs under GAAP that do not reflect their economic margins
  • Sceptical closer: the bearish read is that the entire accounting-standardisation exercise is an asymmetric risk — the companies with the highest reported GMs are the most likely to have aggressive accounting policies, meaning that "top-quartile GM" is correlated with "most likely to face a downward restatement or reclassification"; the analyst who takes reported GMs at face value is systematically exposed to the highest-GM names

Confidence: High — anchor: accounting policy differences are well-documented in academic literature and in SEC comment letter patterns; the CS cost classification issue is the most material but least-disclosed

§7 — HISTORICAL PRECEDENT AND CROSS-SECTOR ANALOGUES

  • Verdict: the software GM durability debate has precedent in two analogous industries — pharmaceutical gross margins (IP-driven, structurally high, subject to cliff-risk from generic entry) and financial services net interest margins (spread businesses, structurally cyclical, mean-reverting) — the software case has elements of both
  • Pharma analogue: pharmaceutical companies sustain 70-85% GMs on patented products, collapsing to 30-50% at generic entry — the analogue to software is that platform lock-in (equivalent to patent protection) sustains high GMs until a competitive disruption equivalent to generic entry occurs; the key difference is that software "generic entry" is gradual (competitive erosion) not cliff-event, making the GM compression slower but also harder to time
  • Financial services analogue: net interest margin compression in banking is the closest analogue to software GM compression under cost pressure — both are spread businesses where the cost base (deposits/cost of funds for banks; infrastructure/labour for software) is partially variable and partially fixed; the mean-reversion of NIMs after rate cycles is analogous to the expected mean-reversion of software GMs after labour cost cycles
  • Historical software cycles: the 2000-2003 dot-com bust provides the clearest historical test of software GM durability — companies that survived (Oracle, SAP, Microsoft) showed GM compression of ~3-8pp at trough but recovered within 18-24 months; companies that did not survive are selection-bias-excluded from the analysis; the survivor bias in this historical read is material
  • 2008-2009 financial crisis: enterprise software (pre-SaaS dominance) showed GM compression of ~5-10pp in 2009, primarily from professional-services revenue decline; pure-license businesses were more resilient; the SaaS cohort of 2008-2009 was too small to draw conclusions but the directional evidence (Salesforce 2009 GM: ~73% vs ~76% prior year) supports the recurring-revenue resilience thesis
  • 2022-2023 rate shock: the most recent and most relevant precedent — the shift from 0% to 5% rates in 2022-2023 created a "value interrogation" of software spend; NRR compressed broadly (~3-5pp across the public SaaS cohort); GM compression followed with a 2-3 quarter lag (~2-4pp for hybrid cohorts, ~0-1pp for pure-SaaS); the recovery in 2024 has been faster than sell-side expected, consistent with the structural resilience of recurring-revenue GMs
  • Cross-sector (enterprise vs SMB software): SMB-focused software (Toast, Mindbody, Lightspeed) shows structurally lower GMs (40-60%) because the SMB customer requires proportionally more support, has higher churn, and cannot self-serve — the enterprise/SMB split is a more predictive GM anchor than the "software/non-software" split
  • Cross-sector (vertical vs horizontal): vertical SaaS (Veeva, nCino, Toast) has historically shown lower GMs than horizontal SaaS (Salesforce, ServiceNow) because vertical markets are smaller and require more customisation — but vertical SaaS companies have lower competitive risk and more durable pricing power, creating a GM quality/level trade-off that is not captured by simple GM level comparisons
  • Sceptical closer: the bearish historical read is that every prior software cycle had a structural recovery driver (internet adoption, mobile, cloud migration) that sustained demand and allowed pricing; it is not clear that an equivalent structural demand driver exists in the 2024-2028 period — AI could be that driver but the timing and magnitude are genuinely uncertain; a cycle without a structural demand driver would test software GM durability in a way that historical precedent does not fully cover

Confidence: Medium — anchor: historical precedent is informative but every cycle has different structural features; the pharma/finserv analogues are useful frameworks but not predictive

Interpretation bullets:

  • High-confidence finding: NRR as a leading GM indicator is the most investable insight from this landscape — it is observable, has a known lag, and is directionally consistent across cohorts
  • Medium-confidence finding: the infrastructure cost leverage story is real but the labour cost offset is larger than models assume — the net GM expansion trajectory is flatter than consensus implies
  • Low-confidence area: the impact of AI-native competition on incumbent software GMs over the 18-36M window is genuinely unknown — the directional threat is real but the magnitude and timing are indeterminate

  • Cycle dependency: the 2022-2023 cycle showed GM resilience in pure-SaaS and compression in hybrid — but the next cycle trigger is unknown; the analyst should model a ±4pp GM sensitivity as the base uncertainty range for hybrid cohorts
  • Aggregate assessment: the KQ is resolvable at the structural level (what determines GM durability) but not at the forecast precision level (exactly what GM will be in month 18-36) — the honest answer is a range with named drivers, not a point estimate

§N+2 — DATA GAPS

  • Most material gaps verdict: the three most material gaps are (1) CS cost allocation methodology by company, (2) NRR distribution (not just average), and (3) AI inference cost disclosure — resolving these would significantly improve 18-36M GM forecast precision

  • Critical data gap: no public dataset provides software company gross margin net of all customer-facing headcount costs (support + CS + implementation) on a consistent basis — the 4-8pp "loaded vs reported" GM gap is material and currently unquantifiable without primary research

  • Resolvable via: management interviews asking directly how CS headcount is classified in COGS; LinkedIn headcount data cross-referenced against COGS disclosures

  • Critical data gap: NRR distribution (enterprise vs SMB, cohort by vintage) is not disclosed — the blended NRR figure masks bimodal distributions that are the primary tail risk to GM forecasts

  • Resolvable via: investor day transcripts where management occasionally discloses cohort-level NRR; expert calls with former VP Customer Success who would know the distribution

  • Material gap: AI inference cost as a % of COGS is a new and rapidly growing cost line that is not separately disclosed by any major software company — the current disclosure lumps it into "hosting and infrastructure" or "cost of revenues" without granularity

  • Resolvable via: wait-and-monitor — this will likely be disclosed separately as it grows; note that Snowflake and Datadog have begun breaking out AI-related costs in supplemental disclosures

  • Material gap: the relationship between pricing architecture (per-seat vs usage-based vs outcome-based) and GM volatility has not been systematically studied using post-2020 data — the pre-2020 literature on usage-based pricing GM dynamics is based on a different macro environment

  • Resolvable via: cross-sectional analysis of the public SaaS cohort stratified by pricing architecture — achievable with public 10-K data but requires analyst time

  • Minor gap: the geographic adjustment required to compare GAAP GMs between US and European software companies is known directionally but not precisely quantified — estimates range from 100-400bps depending on R&D intensity

  • Resolvable via: restatement exercise using disclosed R&D capitalisation balances and amortisation schedules

  • Unresolvable from desk: the pricing power test (has the company taken price on existing customers without churn?) requires primary research — channel checks with customers or former account managers; not discoverable from public filings

  • Expert call would resolve: a former VP Sales or VP Customer Success at the target company would know the price increase history and customer reaction

  • Sceptical closer: the data gaps listed above are genuine but may also reflect an analyst not looking in the right places — GAAP gross profit disclosures are supplemented by non-GAAP bridges, 10-K MD&A discussions of cost drivers, and earnings call Q&A where analysts push on CS cost classification; the analyst who has read only the income statement and missed the MD&A cost section has a self-inflicted data gap

§N+3 — SECOND + THIRD-ORDER IMPLICATIONS

  • Edge implications verdict: if the KQ resolves toward the non-consensus answer (GM durability is weaker than the bull thesis assumes), the valuation implication is a 15-25% reduction in fair value for software companies trading above 10x revenue, not a marginal adjustment

  • Second-order: if NRR is the primary GM driver, then the most important question for the APM's model is not "what is the current NRR?" but "what is the NRR trend?" — a company at 115% NRR declining from 125% is in a fundamentally different position than a company at 115% NRR stable or rising

  • The second-order implication is that the rate of change of NRR is a more important model input than the level, and it is underweighted in most DCF models that take NRR as a static assumption

  • Second-order: if CS cost classification is arbitrary (§6 finding), then companies with the highest reported GMs are the most likely to have future margin pressure from accounting reclassification or investor scrutiny — the high-GM outlier in a software comp table may be the most dangerous position, not the safest

  • This is the inverse of the standard investment heuristic ("buy the highest-margin business") applied to the accounting heterogeneity problem

  • Third-order: if AI-native competitors do materialise as a pricing threat in 3-5 years (§4 finding), then current software moat valuations are pricing a durability that is longer than the actual window — the terminal value assumption in a 10-year DCF for a software platform may be systematically overvalued if the competitive disruption cycle is shorter than the model assumes

  • Cross-pillar: GM durability is linked to the management quality pillar — the CEO/CFO who has made accurate multi-year GM commitments and delivered has demonstrated both forecasting ability and cost discipline; the CEO who has consistently missed GM targets has revealed either a forecasting failure or a cost management failure, either of which is a red flag for the case

  • Cross-thematic: the software GM debate is converging with the AI capex debate — companies that are large AI consumers (using LLMs in their product) face a new variable cost line (inference) that will grow non-linearly with product adoption; the GM durability of AI-enabled software is a genuinely new question without historical precedent

  • M/M/O application #1 — Hyperscaler incentive to compress software GM: the major cloud providers (AWS, Azure, GCP) have means (infrastructure pricing power), motive (to move up the stack from infrastructure to application), and opportunity (existing customer relationships + data access) to compress software company GMs by offering competing application-layer products at below-market pricing; this threat is well-known but underweighted in the terminal-value assumptions of most software DCFs

  • The M/M/O test suggests this is not a fringe risk — it is a central structural threat that the investment case must address explicitly

  • M/M/O application #2 — PE/strategic acquirer incentive to reclassify GMs post-acquisition: a private equity acquirer of a software company has means (accounting policy discretion), motive (to show a higher "true" EBITDA to refinancing lenders), and opportunity (post-acquisition audit) to reclassify CS costs from S&M to COGS — this would compress the acquired company's reported GM but is actually a more honest presentation; the implication for public market investors is that the "true" GM of many software companies is lower than reported, and a take-private transaction would reveal this gap

  • M/M/O application #3 — Management incentive to maintain GM optics ahead of a financing event: software management teams with equity comp tied to ARR growth have a structural incentive to underinvest in CS headcount (keeping COGS low) in the 12M before a primary offering or secondary — this is visible in the data as a GM spike followed by a post-offering CS hiring wave and subsequent GM compression; the analyst observing a "GM improvement" ahead of a capital markets event should test whether it is cost discipline or cost deferral

  • Inversion: instead of asking "what would cause GM compression?", invert to "what would have to be true for GM to expand beyond current levels?" — the answer is (1) NRR improving to >120% on a sustained basis, (2) infrastructure costs declining faster than labour costs rise, and (3) pricing power sufficient to take price ahead of cost inflation; all three simultaneously is a high bar; the base case should assume flat to slight compression, not expansion

  • Sceptical closer: the second-order implication the analyst may be flinching from is that software GM durability is a temporal concept — it is durable over 18-24M (the recurring-revenue cohort effect is real and observable), but over 5-10 years, every software market eventually faces either competitive commoditisation or hyperscaler encroachment; the obstacle is the way — the analyst must model the GM durability window explicitly and not treat it as perpetual

AGGREGATE WEAK SIGNALS / DOWNSIDE (❌)

  • ❌ NRR compression is faster than models assume: the 2022-2023 SaaS cohort showed NRR compression velocity of ~3-5pp over 18M — if this rate is the new normal (post-cheap-capital SaaS) rather than a one-time shock, then forward GM assumptions are structurally overstated for the entire sector
  • ❌ AI inference costs are non-linear: the cost of running LLM inference at enterprise scale is growing faster than revenue for most AI-enabled software companies — early disclosures from Salesforce and others suggest inference costs are already ~1-2pp of revenue and growing; if this reaches 3-5% of revenue by 2026-2027, it is a structural GM headwind that is not in current models
  • ❌ CS headcount build is irreversible in the near term: companies that over-hired CS in 2021-2022 have shown difficulty reducing headcount proportionally in the downturn due to institutional inertia, customer contractual SLAs, and talent market dynamics — the labour cost stickiness creates a GM floor that is higher than the infrastructure-cost-leverage models imply
  • ❌ Accounting reclassification risk is asymmetric: as investor and activist scrutiny on software cost classification increases, there is a higher probability of CS costs moving from S&M to COGS (GM compression) than the reverse — the reclassification risk is one-directional

  • ⚡ Hypergrowth GM compression (structural): companies growing ARR >80% YoY in 2021 showed median GM compression of ~4pp over 24M despite revenue growth — this directly contradicts the "scale = GM expansion" thesis and suggests that hypergrowth itself is a GM headwind when it requires proportional CS investment ahead of automation; this is a top-5% outlier finding relative to the consensus landscape view [CONFIDENCE: Medium — based on observable post-IPO data for 2020-2021 cohort SaaS IPOs]

  • ⚡ PE reclassification reveals gap: the pattern of PE-acquired software companies reclassifying CS costs to COGS post-acquisition (visible in leveraged loan marketing documents vs prior GAAP filings) suggests the true GAAP GM of publicly-listed software companies is systematically 4-8pp above economic reality; this is the most important structural data point for understanding why software GM "durability" narratives may be partially accounting-artefact [CONFIDENCE: Low-Medium — directional evidence from public market vs PE-owned comp analysis; not rigorously documented]
