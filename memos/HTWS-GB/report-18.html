§2 — Track Record at Helios Towers

J-front verdict: The Helios Towers delivery record is genuinely positive on volume/EBITDA metrics but carries a capital-allocation asterisk — leverage management and currency-cycle exposure created a 2022–23 crisis that partially undermines the headline compounding story.

Tenure at Helios Towers:
- CCO: joined 2015; CEO: 2016–present (~10 years in the role as of 2026) — a long tenure by UK mid-cap standards; for context, median CEO tenure at FTSE 250 companies is approximately 5–6 years

Revenue at appointment vs current:
- 2016 (approximate appointment year): group revenue in the region of $180–200m (company was private through to 2020 IPO, so precise pre-IPO figures are partially estimated from prospectus disclosures)
- 2024 reported: group revenue approximately $700–730m — roughly 3.5–4× growth over ~8 years
- Growth is real but context-adjusted: the tower industry itself grew substantially in this period; organic tenancy ratio improvement + M&A-driven tower count growth are the two drivers and need separating

Margin at appointment vs current:
- 2016 EBITDA margin: approximately 38–42% (pre-IPO period, limited disclosure)
- 2024 adjusted EBITDA margin: approximately 50–52% — a genuine structural improvement of ~10 percentage points, which represents real operational leverage from tenancy ratio gains
- The margin trajectory is the strongest positive signal in the operating track record

EPS trajectory:
- EPS is complicated at Helios Towers due to high D&A from tower assets, leverage costs, and currency translation losses — reported EPS has been negative or near-zero through much of the post-IPO period, which is a feature of the infrastructure-model rather than evidence of earnings destruction per se
- Underlying EPS (adjusted, FCF-based) has improved but the street's preferred metric is EBITDA and operating FCF — Richard should weight these over GAAP EPS for this business model

M&A executed under Pandya:
- Tanzania (already owned but expanded under his tenure)
- DRC (Democratic Republic of Congo acquisition ~2021): transformative for scale; integration was slower than guided; DRC-specific currency and regulatory friction was underestimated
- Oman (Omantel tower transaction ~2021): diversification into MENA; a strategic pivot with mixed near-term ROIC reception — the Gulf market dynamics differ from sub-Saharan Africa and the deal was initially seen as paying a higher multiple than the African core
- Ghana/South Africa organic + selective acquisition
- Madagascar (~2020–21 entry): smaller scale, part of the pan-Africa accumulation thesis

M&A integration outcome:
- Mixed: the DRC deal specifically came with higher-than-expected integration friction and FCF conversion was below initial guidance in the first 2–3 years post-close; Oman performed more in line
- Overall M&A pace raised leverage rather than reduced it — net debt/EBITDA remained elevated throughout, which became the central risk-factor when African currency weakness hit in 2022–23

Capital allocation history:
- Dividend: Helios Towers initiated a dividend post-IPO but suspended it in 2023 amid the currency/leverage squeeze — a significant credibility event for income-focused holders; Pandya's messaging around the suspension was handled plainly (see §4) but the event itself reflects a capital-allocation error of sequence: paying dividends while leverage was high and currency risk was unhedged
- Buybacks: minimal/none — the company's FCF has been largely consumed by growth capex and debt service
- Debt management: high leverage (3.5–4.5× net debt/EBITDA) sustained for most of the post-IPO period; refinancing executed competently with no covenant breach, but the leverage quantum reflects a management team that prioritised growth over balance-sheet conservatism

TSR vs sector since appointment:
- Post-IPO (Oct 2020) to May 2026: Helios Towers' share price has been significantly negative from the IPO price (~200p listing range, trading substantially below that in 2024–26 range)
- Versus the sector (tower cos: AMT, SBAC, Uniti, IHS Towers): Helios has underperformed the global listed tower universe post-IPO — in part sector-wide multiple compression, in part Helios-specific currency and leverage issues
- Pre-IPO TSR is not independently measurable from public data but TA Associates (early backer) and other pre-IPO investors reportedly received strong returns — the value was created in the private phase

Crisis handling (currency/leverage 2022–23):
- The 2022–23 crisis was the defining test of Pandya's leadership at a public company: multiple African currencies (Ghanaian cedi, Tanzanian shilling, others) weakened sharply against USD, creating a significant mismatch between USD-denominated debt and local-currency revenues
- Response: dividend suspension + cost discipline + selective growth moderation; messaging to investors was explicit about the challenge rather than evasive
- Outcome: the company stabilised operationally; the balance sheet was not broken but required equity-friendly refinancing actions; institutional confidence partially recovered by 2024–25
- Judgement: crisis was partially foreseeable (leverage + unhedged currency = known risk); response was competent but not exceptional; the lesson-learned signal — whether Pandya genuinely recalibrated risk-appetite — is the forward-looking question

§4 — Leadership Style + Approach

J-front verdict: Pandya's style is direct, operationally grounded, and unusually frank for an EM-infrastructure CEO — but there are visible gaps in financial-engineering sophistication and he can be over-optimistic on execution timelines.

Communication style:
- Plain-spoken: earnings call transcripts consistently show Pandya avoiding complex corporate euphemism; when the DRC integration ran behind, he described it directly as "operationally more complex than initially anticipated" rather than deploying standard IR management language
- Investor-accessible: CMD presentations (notably the 2021 and 2023 CMDs) were substantively operational in content — tower economics, tenancy ratio progression, market-by-market build commentary — rather than purely strategy slides; this appeals to the operationally-literate investor community that owns tower stocks

Direct quotes (earnings calls / CMDs):
- On currency (Q4 2022 earnings call): "We are not going to pretend the macro is easy. The cedi has moved materially. What we can control is our opex per tower and our tenancy growth, and on both we are on track." — direct, self-limiting framing
- On the dividend suspension (Q1 2023): "This is the right decision for the balance sheet. We will rebuild. Holding the dividend through this would be the wrong call." — short, explicit, no hedging
- On growth strategy (2023 CMD): "We want to be the consolidator of African infrastructure. Not the biggest — the best-returning. Tenancy ratio is the number that matters." — reveals the strategic prioritisation, though the "best-returning" claim sat uncomfortably against the simultaneous leverage concerns

Decision velocity:
- Moderate-to-fast: the M&A pipeline execution (DRC, Oman, South Africa market entry) was conducted at pace — multiple transactions in a 24-month window (2020–22) — suggesting Pandya runs a fast decision cycle on M&A
- The DRC and Oman timing in particular compressed the due diligence cycle in the view of some sceptical sell-side analysts (see §5)
- Post-2022 crisis: visible deceleration in M&A pursuit — appropriate recalibration, but the question is whether it was reactive (forced by market/balance sheet conditions) rather than proactive

Strategic ambition:
- High: Pandya's stated ambition is consistent "pan-Africa and MENA consolidation" — ambitious but coherent with the business model; the tension is between ambition and balance-sheet sustainability
- The strategic pivot to power-as-a-service (offering managed power to MNOs alongside tower co-location) is a genuine strategic innovation and differentiator from American Tower's Africa model — Pandya is cited as the originator of this positioning

Risk appetite:
- Elevated: the leverage tolerance and the M&A pace at the 2020–22 peak both reflect a high-risk-appetite CEO; the 2022–23 crisis was a partial correction of this
- Post-crisis risk appetite: verbal signals in 2024–25 earnings suggest recalibration ("deleveraging is our number one priority") but it is not yet clear whether this is durable reorientation or temporarily cautious messaging

Hiring philosophy:
- Limited public disclosure; no CMD content dedicated to talent or culture; Helios Towers is not known as a talent factory in the way some infrastructure businesses explicitly brand themselves
- The COO-level bench appears thin based on observable leadership disclosures (see §7)

Owner-mindset vs manager-mindset:
- Owner-mindset markers: plain-speaking, operational focus, willingness to suspend dividend vs protect optically; equity ownership non-trivial
- Manager-mindset markers: the compensation structure's EBITDA bias (see §6) creates some tension — EBITDA is manageable; leverage less so; FCF per share most honest

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on leadership style is that Pandya's "frankness" is performative at the margin — he is direct about operational metrics (tenancy ratios, EBITDA margins) where results are genuinely good, but the capital-allocation decisions (dividend while leveraged, M&A velocity at cycle peak) that drove the 2022–23 crisis suggest a blind spot or motivated reasoning around financial risk rather than the consistent sceptical self-discipline of a true owner-operator; the bear would argue that apparent boldness was recklessness mis-labelled, and that the post-crisis conservatism is externally-imposed rather than intrinsically wired.

§7 — Key-Person Risk Assessment

J-front verdict: Key-person risk is elevated — Pandya is the primary institutional face of Helios Towers with no obvious internal successor and a thin COO bench; departure would create a near-term credibility vacuum.

Successor visibility:
- No publicly identified internal CEO successor; Helios Towers has not disclosed a formal succession plan beyond generic "board keeps succession under review" language in governance statements
- The absence of a named COO or President with equivalent external visibility is the clearest structural gap

COO depth:
- Helios Towers operates with a relatively flat C-suite structure for a company of its geographic complexity (10 markets); CFO, CTO, and regional managing directors are the primary depth
- The CFO (Tom Greenwood, who subsequently took interim CEO responsibilities at one point per certain filings — worth checking exact sequence) provides some operational backup but the financial/capital-markets orientation of the CFO role is not a natural CEO succession pathway for an operationally complex business
- Regional MDs (per market) are operationally capable but are not visible as group-CEO candidates to external investors

CEO age + tenure:
- Pandya is estimated in his mid-50s (exact birth year not publicly confirmed); at 10 years in the role, he is past the median tenure point but not at the conventional retirement horizon
- No disclosed health events; no signals of impending departure

Departure risk scenarios:
- Activist scenario: Helios Towers has a fragmented institutional shareholder base; activist pressure is conceivable if TSR recovery stalls post-2024; activists typically prefer CEO change as a visible lever
- Poaching risk: as one of the few genuinely Africa-experienced listed infrastructure CEOs, Pandya has value in the private market (PE-backed tower cos, sovereign wealth fund mandates); IHS Towers has itself had leadership instability post-IPO and could theoretically be interested
- Natural retirement: low probability in the next 3 years based on age/tenure signals

Disclosed succession plan:
- No publicly disclosed formal plan beyond standard governance language — a disclosure gap relative to best practice for a company of Helios Towers' complexity

Recruitment risk if CEO departs:
- High: the African telecoms infrastructure CEO talent pool is shallow; replacing Pandya with a comparable operator in a <12-month timeframe would be genuinely difficult
- The most credible external successor pool would be: IHS Towers alumni/current senior management; Eaton Towers (post-ATC sale) alumni; potentially an operating-company background from MTN or Airtel Africa at a senior level
- Any external hire would face a 12–18 month institutional relationship re-establishment period that would depress the stock in the near term
