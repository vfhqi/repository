⚠️ LIVE DATA UNAVAILABLE: Methodology notes only.

  • J-front verdict: (historical precedent signal = indeterminate; requires KQ text to select relevant analogues)
  • Base-rate anchoring principle: every investment thesis should be anchored against a base rate drawn from historical precedents — "what fraction of companies in similar structural positions delivered the EPS trajectory this thesis requires?"
  • Cross-sector analogue method: the most useful analogues are often cross-sector rather than within-sector — the within-sector comparison set is too small and too correlated to provide statistically meaningful base rates
  • Survivor bias warning: historical precedent sets drawn from public market data are systematically survivor-biased; the companies that failed or were acquired before achieving the claimed trajectory are underrepresented
  • Analogue selection discipline: a good analogue has three matching characteristics — similar market structure (oligopoly/fragmented/transitional), similar demand driver (secular growth vs cyclical recovery vs share gain), and similar capital structure (asset-light vs asset-heavy)
  • Mean reversion base rate: the most important base rate for a concentrated investor is the mean reversion rate for above-average returns — historically, companies in the top quintile of ROIC revert toward the median over a 5-7Y window at a meaningful rate; the thesis must explain why this company is in the persistent-outperformer minority
  • M/M/O test (third application): in historical precedents where the thesis failed, was there a common pattern of actors with means/motive/opportunity to extract value before the minority shareholder could capture it? If yes, is that pattern present here?

  • Sceptical bullet: the bearish historical read is that the analyst's chosen analogues are cherry-picked to support the thesis; the more disciplined approach is to use a pre-specified analogue selection rule and then look at all companies that match, including the ones that disappointed
