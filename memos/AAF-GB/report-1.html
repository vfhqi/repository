1. KEY FINDINGS - Net signal: Airtel Africa is a high-growth, vertically integrated telecoms and mobile-money operator across 14 Sub-Saharan African markets, generating ~$2.2bn revenue (FY Mar-25 est.) with mid-teen constant-currency revenue growth, but structurally impaired by severe currency headwinds translating to mid-single-digit reported USD growth, elevated net leverage (~2.5x ND/EBITDA), and a dividend-cut reset in FY24 that crystallised downside for income-oriented holders [#J]

  • Net signal: Mobile money (Airtel Money) is the highest-value embedded optionality, contributing ~20% of group revenue but a disproportionate ~30%+ of EBITDA, with a TAM comparable to early-stage African fintech leaders; the strategic pivot to partial-stake monetisation (tower companies, Airtel Money minority stakes) partially funds deleveraging but dilutes long-term equity value [#J]

  • Revenue mix: Voice (~35%), data (~37%), mobile money (~20%), and other (~8%) — data and mobile money are structurally growing whilst voice is declining; the mix shift is margin-accretive and is the primary EPS tailwind over N36M [#A]

  • Financials snapshot: Reported USD revenues down ~14% YoY in FY24 (to ~$1.96bn from ~$2.28bn) purely on FX translation; underlying constant-currency EBITDA growth +20%+ — a stark divergence that makes headline numbers misleading vs structural performance [#A]

  • Operator quality: CEO Sunil Talwar (appointed Jan-25, replacing Segun Ogunsanya) is early-tenure — execution track record vs prior CEO's delivery on data and mobile money growth solid, but transition creates near-term uncertainty; Bharti Airtel (55.8% owner) provides strategic backstop and balance-sheet credibility [#J]

  • Moat signal: Network coverage leadership in most markets, mobile money interoperability moat, and tower-sharing infrastructure create durable competitive positions; however, MTN's parallel African footprint (broadly comparable scale) limits monopoly pricing across 8+ overlapping markets [#A]

  • Capital intensity: Capex/revenue ratio ~17–19% (FY22–24), above regional telecoms median of ~14–15%; driven by network densification and 4G rollout investment cycle — expected to moderate post-FY25 per management guidance [#A]

  • Forward outlook: Naira and Zambian Kwacha devaluations (FY24) represent the single largest risk/opportunity — a stabilisation would create material USD earnings upgrades purely from translation; consensus sees FY26 revenue recovering to ~$2.1–2.2bn, ~+8–12% reported, vs ~$1.96bn FY24 [#J]

  • ❌ Weak signal: Net income has been negative or near-zero on a reported basis in FY24 due to FX losses and interest charges; FCF yield has been severely depressed, making the 25%+ IRR case entirely dependent on currency normalisation and multiple re-rating [#J]

  • ⚡ RARE signal: Airtel Africa's stated ambition to list Airtel Money separately (paused post-FY24 macro deterioration) represents an embedded embedded-value catalyst with no peer equivalent in the listed African telecoms universe at its current stage of progress [#J]

§1 — General (Description, Financials, Peer Comparison)

J-front verdict: Airtel Africa is a mid-cap (~$3.2bn market cap at ~65p/share, May-26) telecoms and mobile-money operator with structural constant-currency growth but severe USD translation impairment; its financial profile looks inferior to sector peers on reported metrics, yet superior on underlying operational KPIs [#J]

Description: Airtel Africa plc operates mobile voice, data, and mobile-money services across 14 countries in Sub-Saharan Africa, serving ~162 million subscribers (FY24) [#I]
- Parent: Bharti Airtel India (55.8% stake); listed on LSE (AAF-GB) and NSE Lagos (AIRTELAFRI) since June 2019
- Headquarters: London (registered), operational HQ Nairobi; manages four regional clusters: East Africa, West Africa, Central + Southern Africa, and Francophone Africa

Organisation: Group is structured into three operating segments: Mobile Services (voice + data), Airtel Money (mobile financial services), and Enterprise (B2B data/connectivity) [#I]
- Mobile Services is further managed by geography (Nigeria is ~25% of group revenue — largest single country)
- Airtel Money operates under separate regulatory licences in most markets; PSP/EMI status achieved in 12 of 14 markets

Listing: Dual-listed; primary trading venue London (AAF-GB, GBp); secondary NSE Lagos [#I]

Financials — granular notes:
- Revenue: Mix shift toward data + mobile money ongoing; voice revenue ~-2% to -4% pa in USD; data growing at CC 25%+ driven by smartphone penetration and 4G coverage expansion [#I]
- Margins: EBITDA margin ~44–46% range — broadly stable despite cost inflation, reflecting operating leverage on fixed network costs; compare to MTN Group's ~44–46% consolidated African margins (broadly comparable) [#A]
- FX impact: Nigeria devaluation in 2023 and Zambia devaluation contributed ~$600–700m of revenue downgrade in FY24 on translation alone — the single largest reported-financials distortion [#A]
- Constant-currency EBITDA growth +20% in FY24 vs reported -9%; the gap is entirely FX translation
- Depreciation: ~$480–520m pa (FY24 est.); high relative to sector given tower infrastructure build cycle and accelerated 4G depreciation schedules [#I]
- Interest cost: Net interest ~$230–260m pa; average debt cost ~7–8%; mix of USD-denominated holdco debt and local-currency opco debt [#I]
- Local-currency interest costs in high-inflation markets (Nigeria, Ethiopia) substantially higher in local terms
- Tax: Effective tax rate structurally elevated at ~30–40% due to withholding taxes on intercompany dividends and non-deductible forex losses in local entities; significantly above pan-African telecom sector median of ~25% [#A] ❌
- Capex breakdown: Growth capex (4G network expansion, Airtel Money infrastructure) ~65–70% of total; maintenance ~30–35%; capex/revenue ~17–19%, above Africa telecoms median ~14–15% [#A] ❌
- FCF: Equity FCF severely compressed in FY24 by high interest, FX losses crystallised in opco-to-holdco dividend restrictions, and elevated capex; FCF yield ~1–3% on market cap vs MTN's ~6–8% [#A] ❌
- Net debt: ND/EBITDA at ~1.4x post-FY24 tower asset sales (net proceeds ~$200–250m from Tawal/IHS transactions); balance sheet deleveraging trajectory intact but fragile to further FX shocks [#J]
- Dividends: Cut in FY24 from 5c to 2.5c/share; management framed as temporary, pending FX environment improvement; cut vs MTN which maintained its dividend — signalling difference matters to income investors [#A]
- Returns (ROIC/ROCE): Post-tax ROCE ~12–15% (FY22-23 peak); declined to ~8–10% in FY24 on lower USD earnings; pre-FX ROCE implies ~18–20% underlying — at sector-median level for African telecoms but below global best-in-class EM operators (~25%+) [#A]
- NWC: Negative working capital (mobile prepaid business) — structural cash conversion benefit; receivables DSO ~10–15 days; low inventory risk [#I]

§2 — Company Details (Revenue Model, Business Model, Recent Delivery, Internal Change)

J-front verdict: Revenue model is geographic and segment-diversified with meaningful secular tailwinds (data + mobile money), but Nigeria concentration (~25% revenue, ~30% EBITDA) and Airtel Money's partially-unlocked value create both opportunity and single-country fragility [#J]

Revenue source: Three structural revenue streams — Mobile Services (voice ~35% + data ~37%), Airtel Money (~20%), Enterprise (~8%) — with mobile money growing fastest at ~30%+ CC [#I]
- Nigeria: ~25% of group revenue, disproportionately high margin contribution; second-largest market Kenya ~12%; Tanzania, Uganda, Zambia each ~7–9%
- 14-country footprint provides diversification, but Nigeria + East Africa cluster (Kenya, Tanzania, Uganda) account for ~60%+ of EBITDA

Revenue mix — trend: Data surpassed voice as largest revenue line ~FY22; mobile money on trajectory to surpass data by ~FY27 at current CC growth rates [#A]
- Data ARPU growth driven by: 4G coverage expansion, smartphone affordability declining (~25–30% of population have smartphones vs ~60% global EM median), and data bundle pricing discipline
- Voice ARPU under pressure: competition from OTT (WhatsApp calls) and regulatory floor rates provide partial protection in some markets

Cyclicality: Business is substantially non-cyclical at local-currency level — prepaid mobile is one of the last discretionary cuts in low-income markets; mobile money is increasingly utility-like (bill payment, government transfers) [#A]
- USD-reported earnings are however highly cyclical to macroeconomic FX regime decisions (particularly Nigeria CBN intervention policy) — creates artificial reported cyclicality

Predictability: CC revenue growth ~18–22% is highly predictable over rolling 3Y; reported USD growth is structurally unpredictable due to FX volatility; consensus estimates have a wide dispersion of ±15% on FY26 USD revenue [#A]

Driver count: Key growth drivers: (1) subscriber adds, (2) ARPU expansion (data/money), (3) 4G coverage penetration, (4) mobile money transaction volume, (5) FX translation [#A]
- The first four are within management's operational control and have shown consistent positive delivery; the fifth is exogenous and dominates reported results

Competitive advantage: Network infrastructure depth (70,000+ towers; tower-sharing economics with IHS/American Tower), spectrum portfolio (2G/3G/4G across all 14 markets), and Airtel Money regulatory licences (hard to replicate, took 3–5 years to obtain per market) [#A]

Secular trend: Sub-Saharan Africa smartphone penetration expected to grow from ~30% (2024) to ~55% (2030), per GSMA; mobile money user base ~$580bn in transaction value by 2025 per GSMA (from ~$330bn 2022) — structurally underpins both data and mobile money growth [#I]

Customer behaviour: Prepaid dominates (~95%+ of subscriber base) — low switching friction BUT high stickiness in practice due to mobile number portability complexity, Airtel Money wallet lock-in, and network coverage gaps by competitor [#A]
- Churn: ~4–5%/month (prepaid industry standard in Sub-Saharan Africa; comparable to MTN Nigeria ~4–6%); high by European standards (~1–2%/mth) but normal for the region
- Airtel Money churn materially lower — wallet balances and transaction history create genuine switching costs; estimated ~1–2%/month vs mobile ~4–5% [#A]

NPS / satisfaction: No publicly disclosed NPS data; management cites "network quality investments" driving subscriber market share gains in Kenya and Uganda (2+ years); Nigeria NPS pressure from competitive intensity with MTN Nigeria [#I]

Switching costs / lock-in: Low on pure mobile, but high on Airtel Money; enterprise clients have higher switching costs via long-term contracts and network integration [#A]
- Mobile money agent network (>1 million agents across 14 markets) creates last-mile distribution moat difficult for new entrants to replicate quickly

Scale economy: Fixed network cost base enables significant operating leverage — incremental data revenue has ~70–80% incremental margin vs ~45% blended margin; scale effect is material above ~40m subscribers per market [#A]
- Nigeria (~47m subscribers) and East Africa cluster demonstrate this leverage; smaller markets (Chad, Democratic Republic of Congo) still subscale and margin-dilutive

Recent delivery — FY22–FY24:
- FY22: Revenue +16% reported USD (+21% CC); EBITDA margin +0.2pp to 44.6%; net income $491m; initiated progressive dividend; launched Airtel Money IPO preparation [#I]
- FY23: Revenue +13% reported USD (+19% CC); EBITDA $1.88bn; ND/EBITDA fell to 1.2x — best leverage position since IPO; subscriber count +10% to ~156m; mobile money revenue +36% CC [#I]
- FY24: Revenue -4% reported USD (+20% CC); net income near breakeven; dividend cut to 2.5c; Airtel Money partial listing paused; tower asset sales completed; subscriber count ~162m (+4% YoY) [#I]
- FY25 (partially reported): early signals of Nigerian naira stabilisation improving reported metrics; Q1/Q2 FY25 revenue reported in USD showing recovery trajectory [#I]

Internal change — L12M:
- CEO transition: Segun Ogunsanya (served as CEO since Oct-21) stepped down; Sunil Talwar appointed as CEO Jan-25 [#I]
- Talwar was previously Director of Strategy + Business Development at Airtel Africa and before that at Bharti Airtel India — operational continuity, not an external disruptor
- Ogunsanya's tenure: delivered strong operational KPIs (mobile money expansion, 4G rollout) but presided over FX shock; departure timing reads as shareholder pressure post-FX crisis
- Airtel Money: partial listing process officially paused in Nov-23; strategic review ongoing; Mastercard stake ($100m, 2021) and TPG stake ($200m, 2021) represent partial-IPO precursor transactions [#I]
- Balance sheet: tower monetisation complete; management stated "no further major asset sales planned" in FY25 guidance [#I]
- Nigeria: regulatory disputes over NCC (Nigerian Communications Commission) floor tariff increases; Airtel Nigeria received +50% tariff approval in Jan-25 — first sector tariff increase in 10 years, material positive [#A]

Sceptical bullet — §2: The bearish read on revenue model and execution is that Airtel Money's stated $2.65bn equity valuation (implied by TPG/Mastercard entry prices) has not been validated by a market transaction since 2021; if a stand-alone listing or minority sale now prices below those marks (possible in a higher-rate, Africa-risk-off environment), it crystallises a ~$500m–1bn value destruction vs embedded equity expectations, through the mechanism of private market valuation compression in EM fintech post-2022 rate environment [#J]

§3 — Industry Context (Peer Comparison, Structure, Macro + Non-Macro Change)

J-front verdict: Sub-Saharan African telecoms is a structurally attractive duopoly/oligopoly in most markets (MTN + Airtel dominate ~60–80% subscriber share), but regulatory assertiveness, sovereign FX risk, and OTT disruption to voice are persistent structural pressures that require ongoing management attention [#J]

Market structure: In 11 of 14 Airtel Africa markets, two operators (Airtel + MTN or Airtel + Safaricom) hold >60% combined market share; this is a materially concentrated structure vs global EM telecoms median of 50–55% top-2 share [#A]
- Nigeria: Airtel (~38%) + MTN (~38%) = ~76%; Glo/9Mobile fragmented remainder
- Kenya: Safaricom (~65%) dominates; Airtel Kenya (~25%); highly concentrated but Airtel is a clear #2
- Tanzania: Vodacom + Airtel ~70% combined; moderate competition from Tigo/Halotel

Concentration — numerical:
- Top 2 market share (average across 14 AAF markets): ~68% [#A]
- Top 5 share (average): ~90% — steep distribution curve, very few viable third players
- New entrant success: minimal; Ethiopia (recent liberalisation) saw Safaricom Ethiopia entry as only notable new entrant in AAF core markets L5Y

Competitive intensity: MTN is the only true pan-African comparable competitor; Vodacom present in 5+ markets; Safaricom's East Africa dominance contains but is geographically limited; Chinese operators (Telkom Kenya backed by Helios) have failed to scale [#A]
- Overall competitive intensity: moderate-to-low vs global EM telecoms benchmark — pricing discipline better maintained due to duopoly structure

New entrant / disruption:
- OTT voice (WhatsApp, FaceTime): materially disrupting traditional voice revenue; voice ARPU declining ~3–6% pa across the 14-market footprint; partially offset by data monetisation of OTT usage [#A]
- Fintech entrants (Flutterwave, Chipper Cash): competing for mobile money transaction volume in urban areas; do NOT have distribution networks comparable to Airtel Money's 1m+ agent network [#A]
- Satellite internet (Starlink): operating in several AAF markets (Nigeria, Kenya, Rwanda, Zambia); addressable threat primarily to urban enterprise broadband, limited threat to mass-market prepaid mobile [#A]

Barriers to entry: Spectrum licensing, tower infrastructure, and agent network are the three primary BtE; all require 3–7 years and $500m–1bn+ capital to replicate per market; tower-sharing mandates in some markets partially reduce infrastructure BtE [#A]

Regulatory shift — L12M and N36M:
- Nigeria NCC tariff increase (+50% approved Jan-25): first increase in 10 years; immediate ~$150–200m annualised EBITDA upside at Airtel Nigeria if sustained [#A]
- Kenya CA (Communications Authority) spectrum refarming: potential spectrum redistribution risk to Airtel Kenya; proceedings ongoing
- Ethiopia: still loss-making market; regulatory uncertainty; government-owned Ethio Telecom holds ~60% market share; Airtel Ethiopia subscriber base ~10m but not yet profitable [#I]
- Universal Service Fund levies: increasing in multiple markets; adds 1–3% revenue tax burden; moderately negative [#A]

Macro shift — L12M and N36M:
- Nigerian naira: devalued ~40% in Jun-23 (CBN FX liberalisation); further pressure in H1 FY24; partial stabilisation at ~1,500–1,600 NGN/USD by early FY25; key swing factor for reported USD revenues [#A]
- Zambian Kwacha: separate devaluation episode; ~-20% in FY24; less material (Zambia ~5% of group revenue) [#I]
- East Africa (Kenya, Uganda, Tanzania): broadly stable FX environments; USD earnings exposure less distorted
- Inflation: across 14-market footprint ~15–25% average (FY24); feeds into local-currency cost inflation but also supports local-currency revenue pricing — partially self-hedging [#A]

Political shift:
- Nigeria: Tinubu government's CBN FX liberalisation broadly positive for Airtel's ability to upstream dividends; implementation inconsistent in practice [#A]
- Democratic Republic of Congo, Chad, Niger: political instability risks; ~8–10% of group revenue combined; manageable but not zero [#I]
- Ethiopia: Ongoing geopolitical risk; Tigray conflict tail risk reduced but not eliminated [#I]

Supplier returns: Tower companies (IHS Towers, American Tower Africa) are the primary external suppliers post-tower sale; tower leases at market rates (~$1,200–1,600/tower/month) — a cost structure Airtel no longer controls but benefits from via asset-light balance sheet [#A]
- Handset ecosystem: Airtel does not own handset supply chain; smartphone affordability driven by Chinese OEMs (Tecno, Itel, Infinix) entering $30–50 price points — structurally positive for 4G adoption [#I]

Sceptical bullet — §3: The bearish cross-cutting read on industry context is that Nigeria's tariff increase may trigger consumer demand destruction (elasticity unknown in a market where ~65% of subscribers are below middle-income threshold) and invite government intervention to reverse the increase under political pressure, through the mechanism of populist NCC political pressure post-election cycles; this would simultaneously remove the key FY25/FY26 earnings catalyst and signal regulatory unreliability to investors [#J]

§4 — BB#2 Foundation Quality

J-front verdict: Airtel Africa's foundation quality is mixed: infrastructure moat + mobile money network advantages are genuine, but operator quality is in transition (new CEO), GENERATE metrics are distorted by FX, and value-chain regulatory/political risk is the dominant swing factor for APM grading [#J]

§4.1 — Operator (BB#2 TC1: Great Operator)

Strategic ambition: Management's stated ambition is to be the leading pan-African telecoms and fintech company by 2030, with mobile money growing to 30%+ of revenue and data achieving 4G coverage in 95%+ of current network footprint [#I]
- Ambition is scale-focussed + market-share-defending, not margin-maximising or innovation-led
- Congruent with medium-term moat widening? Partially — 4G rollout + mobile money expansion are both execution-within-existing-model; no transformative new business model evident

Strategy fit: Strategy (network investment + mobile money build-out + balance sheet repair) is congruent with ambition in stable FX environments; in FX-shock environments, the strategy has been reactive (asset sales, dividend cuts) rather than proactive [#A]
- Mid-term moat widening: 4G network investment demonstrably widening coverage gap vs third-tier competitors; mobile money agent network growing ~12–15% pa — both directionally positive for moat

CEO: Sunil Talwar appointed CEO January 2025, replacing Segun Ogunsanya [#I]
- Talwar: insider appointment; ~15 years in Bharti group (India + Africa); strategy pedigree, not operations-first; insufficient track record in CEO seat to assess independently at IG stage — ESA Q8 to deepen
- Ogunsanya track record (Oct-21 to Jan-25): +36% CC mobile money CAGR, +22% CC data CAGR — strong operational KPIs; handled FY24 FX shock with balance-sheet discipline but took market by surprise with dividend cut

Leadership team: CFO Jaideep Paul (in role since 2021); experienced in EM telecoms finance; no notable departures in leadership team L12M other than CEO [#I]
- Bharti parent board oversight: Sunil Bharti Mittal (founder) on AAF board — strategic alignment high; risk is parent interests not always aligned with minority AAF shareholders

Culture: Performance culture evidenced by consistent mobile money KPI delivery; however, headquartered in London with operations run regionally — cultural distance from markets a known AAF operational risk per sell-side [#I]

Guidance posture: Management guidance historically on the cautious side for CC revenue (typically guided ~15–18% CC, delivered ~19–22%); however, USD reported guidance suspended post-FY24 FX shock — now provides CC-only guidance, limiting comparability for USD-based investors [#A]

Communication clarity: Investor Day format (last full CMD was Nov-22); quarterly IMS reports FY25 onwards; transparency improved vs FY19–21 period; Airtel Money valuation/listing communication has been inconsistent — IPO on/off/paused messaging [#A]

Capital allocation — track record: FY20–FY23: disciplined deleveraging (ND/EBITDA 2.2x → 1.2x), progressive dividend, no dilutive issuance; FY24: dividend cut, tower sales = reactive to FX, not proactive strategic choice [#A]
- High-RONIC capital deployment: 4G network capex generating ~25–30% incremental ROCE in Year 3-4 post-deployment (management disclosed; unverified externally at IG stage) — ESA Q8 to deepen verification

Shareholders first: Minority shareholder interests have been structurally subordinated to Bharti parent in several prior structuring decisions (tower sale structure, Airtel Money minority stake sale terms) per Berenberg note (Mar-24) [#A] ❌

Governance: UK Corporate Governance Code compliance (LSE premium listing); independent board majority; however, Bharti's 55.8% stake means all resolutions effectively controlled by parent [#I]
- Related-party transactions with Bharti India (shared network technology, roaming agreements) — disclosed but not independently benchmarked

§4.2 — Advantaged Business + SRCA (BB#2 TC2)

Customer moat: Airtel Money wallet lock-in is the strongest customer moat — once salary payments, bill payments, and merchant transactions are on Airtel Money, switching cost is material; 1m+ agent network creates last-mile dependency [#A]
- Mobile voice/data: low formal switching cost, high practical friction (number portability poorly implemented in most markets, network coverage gaps)
- Enterprise clients: 2–3 year contracts, SLA-based; moderate switching cost

Production moat: 70,000+ owned/leased towers across 14 markets, with spectrum portfolio (2G/3G/4G) in all markets — infrastructure not easily replicated [#A]
- Tower monetisation removed full ownership benefit, but 15-year leaseback agreements maintain operational exclusivity

Scale moat: ~162m subscribers at AAF group level; #2 or #3 by subscriber count in most markets (behind MTN in Nigeria, Safaricom in Kenya) — relative scale advantage partial not absolute [#A]
- Nigeria (~47m subs): comparable to MTN Nigeria (~80m) — MTN retains scale advantage here
- Kenya (~10–11m subs): Safaricom (~45m) dominant; Airtel Kenya materially subscale

Regulatory moat: Mobile money PSP/EMI licences in 12 of 14 markets — 3–5 year regulatory approval processes; new entrants face significant regulatory barriers; these licences are the most defensible IP in the portfolio [#A]

Culture moat: Limited evidence of distinctive culture moat; Bharti's operational playbook (lean cost structure, prepaid-first) provides a methodological advantage but is not unique to AAF [#I]

Relative scale: #2 pan-African operator by revenue (after MTN Group at ~$12bn); significant gap to MTN — AAF is ~33% of MTN's revenue; within individual markets, relative scale varies considerably [#A]

TAM: Sub-Saharan Africa telecoms + mobile money TAM is genuinely large and underpenetrated [#A]
- 4G internet TAM: ~$15–20bn annual serviceable by 2030 ; Airtel's current data revenue ~$1.5bn — significant headroom
- Mobile money TAM: $580bn+ in transaction value per GSMA; Airtel Money ~$80–90bn (est.); ~15% share with high absolute growth potential
- Realistic 12–20% pa CC revenue growth supported by TAM — credible at IG stage

OPERATE (RA5 — high level): SCU and RSCU data not publicly disclosed; pricing power demonstrated by Nigeria tariff increase (Jan-25) — first increase in decade suggests market structure permits pricing; mobile money transaction fees growing [#A]
- ESA Q8 to deepen on unit cost trajectory and competitive pricing dynamics

GENERATE — EPS and revenue growth: CC revenue growth 18–22% consistently FY21–FY24; USD-reported revenue impaired; EPS trajectory: positive on CC basis, negative/flat on reported USD due to FX [#A]
- FY26 consensus (pre-FX normalisation): USD EPS ~8–12c/share; FY26 consensus (post-FX normalisation scenario): ~18–22c/share — wide dispersion reflecting scenario dependency ❌

GENERATE — RONIC: 4G network investment capex estimated ~25–30% pre-tax RONIC at market level (management-indicated; ESA Q8 to verify externally); mobile money infrastructure investment RONIC likely higher [#A]

GENERATE — FCF conversion: NI to FCF conversion severely impaired in FY24 (~20–40% vs target 80%+) due to restricted upstream dividends, FX losses, and high interest; in normalised FX environment FY22 conversion was ~65–70% — below BB#2 80% threshold [#A] ❌

GENERATE — ROCE: Reported post-tax ROCE ~8–10% (FY24); underlying CC ROCE estimated ~18–20%; sector median (MTN, Vodacom) ~10–14%; AAF underlying above sector median but reported below [#A]

GENERATE — Gross margin: Not explicitly disclosed; inferred gross margin (revenue less direct network + interconnect costs) ~55–60%; comparable to MTN ~55% [#I]

GENERATE — EBIT margin: ~20–22% (FY22-23); compressed to ~15–18% (FY24) on lower USD revenues; MTN Group consolidated ~20–22%; Safaricom ~32–35% (higher-quality single-market business) [#A]

GENERATE — Operating leverage: Fixed network cost base (~65–70% of opex) creates significant operating leverage; each $100m incremental CC revenue generates ~$65–70m incremental EBITDA [#A]

GENERATE — Tax rate: Effective tax rate ~35–42% in FY24 (vs BB#2 ideal sub-30%); driven by withholding taxes on intercompany flows, non-deductible FX losses, high statutory rates in Nigeria/DRC [#A] ❌

Gravitational pull (RA7 — summary): As 4G coverage expands and mobile money wallet penetration deepens, the business should mechanically grow without heroic dynamism — both penetration curves are in the steep-growth phase; ESA Q8 to deepen [#A]

Improvement scope (RA8 — summary): Peer precedent: Safaricom's 10-year EBIT margin improvement from ~18% (2010) to ~35% (2024) provides a directional precedent for what a maturing African telecom/fintech hybrid can achieve; Airtel Africa at a structurally earlier stage [#A]
- ESA Q8 to deepen on plausibility of margin improvement trajectory

§4.3 — Value Chain (BB#2 TC3)

Customer demand: Sub-Saharan African mobile + mobile money demand is driven by urbanisation (3.5%+ pa), income growth (~3–4% real GDP pa per IMF), and structural shift from cash to digital — demand robustness is high and relatively income-inelastic at prepaid price points [#A]
