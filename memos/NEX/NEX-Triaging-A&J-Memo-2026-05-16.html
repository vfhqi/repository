E1 RA1 CQ1 — Strong external change forces / tailwinds?

  • The European grid-upgrade and offshore wind cable supercycle applies to Nexans identically to NKT and PRY: TSO grid investment, offshore wind connection cable demand, and HVDC interconnector awards are structural, multi-decade, and oligopoly-concentrated; Nexans participates in all three
  • Nexans' geographic positioning in the core demand corridors is solid: French operations (La Seyne-sur-Mer submarine cable factory) for Mediterranean and Atlantic wind and interconnectors; Norwegian operations (Halden) for North Sea offshore wind; US operations (Charleston) for the emerging US offshore wind market; UK and Benelux coverage for North Sea projects
  • The US offshore wind market is a genuine additional demand lever for Nexans vs NKT: the Charleston, South Carolina facility positions Nexans to capture US East Coast offshore wind cable contracts (Vineyard Wind, Revolution Wind, South Fork Wind, and successors): a market that NKT cannot currently serve from its European manufacturing base without substantial logistics cost
  • Energy security tailwinds reinforce Nexans' HV order intake: France's domestic grid reinforcement requirements (RTE, Enedis grid investment programmes) and cross-Channel interconnector projects provide a solid domestic French pipeline that Nexans is uniquely well-placed to serve
  • The HV cable supply constraint benefits Nexans alongside NKT and PRY: with industry-wide capacity tight, even Nexans; the weakest of the three; has pricing power and project selectivity that would not exist in a normally-supplied market; the oligopoly's structural advantage extends to the third player
  • Nexans has been winning material HV and submarine cable contracts: recent awards include North Sea interconnectors, offshore wind array cables, and HVDC projects in Europe and the Americas; the order book growth demonstrates that the external demand is translating into Nexans-specific contract wins, not just flowing to NKT and PRY alone

E1 RA1 CQ2 — Strong internal change forces?

  • The "New Nexans" strategic pivot (2018–present) is the primary internal change force: Christopher Guérin disposed of numerous non-cable businesses (Nexans' Belden copper and aluminium assets, Cabling Solutions, various regional businesses), focusing the group on High Voltage, Subsea, and Land HV cable: directionally correct and partially executed
  • Charleston EHV capacity expansion: Nexans has invested in new HV cable manufacturing capacity at its Charleston, South Carolina facility, positioning it for the US offshore wind market; this is the most significant internal capital allocation decision under Guérin and represents a genuine change-from-current in Nexans' US market addressability
  • Halden (Norway) factory upgrades: improvements to the Norwegian submarine cable manufacturing facility increase Nexans' production capacity and technical range for North Sea offshore projects; less transformative than NKT's Karlskrona or PRY's Pikkala but a real incremental capacity addition
  • Margin improvement programme: Nexans' EBITDA margin trajectory has been improving from a lower base (~8–9% in 2020 toward ~10–13% in 2025); the improvement is driven by portfolio rationalisation (exit of low-margin businesses), project selectivity (declining commodity cable work in favour of HV), and operational cost programmes
  • Senior management reinforcement: Guérin has brought in new leadership across CFO, COO, and divisional roles since 2018; the management team is more commercially aggressive and financially disciplined than the pre-Guérin era; the quality improvement is real but is not at the NKT Westerlind or PRY level of track record validation
  • Debt reduction trajectory: Nexans has stated a commitment to deleveraging over 2024–2027; the pathway from ~3.5× to ~2.0× Net debt/EBITDA is contingent on EBITDA growth and cash conversion; execution against this deleverage plan is a key internal change force: but it is also a constraint, as high debt absorbs cash that could otherwise fund capacity expansion or shareholder returns

E1 RA2 CQ1 — Absence of external headwinds?

  • The same permitting delay headwind as NKT and PRY applies to Nexans: European offshore wind permitting delays create order intake timing uncertainty; the impact on Nexans is proportionally larger because its order book is smaller in absolute terms, meaning any single project delay is more visible in the revenue line
  • US offshore wind project cancellations and delays are a more acute risk for Nexans than for NKT or PRY: the US offshore wind market has been subject to project cancellations (Avangrid, BP in 2023–2024) driven by rising costs and supply chain inflation; Nexans' Charleston facility was built for this market; if US offshore wind project execution continues to be disrupted, the Charleston investment thesis is weakened
  • French labour relations represent an ongoing operational headwind: Nexans' French manufacturing base is subject to French labour law and the CGT/FO union dynamic; restructuring actions and efficiency programmes have historically faced union resistance; this is a structural operational friction not present for NKT (Danish) or PRY (in some factories but managed)
  • Currency: Nexans is EUR-reporting with USD revenue from Charleston and GBP/NOK revenues from North Sea projects; similar to PRY, translation effects create quarterly noise; manageable
  • [D] Financial leverage is a systemic headwind: at ~2.5–3.5× Net debt/EBITDA, Nexans' interest costs consume a meaningful proportion of EBITDA; rising interest rates in 2022–2024 have been a direct margin headwind; any slowdown in EBITDA growth increases the leverage ratio, creating a reflexive financial risk that NKT and PRY do not face to the same degree

E1 RA2 CQ2 — Well-invested base?

  • Nexans' manufacturing base is geographically well-positioned but technically less advanced than NKT's or PRY's at the EHV tier: La Seyne-sur-Mer (France) is the primary submarine cable facility; Halden (Norway) for North Sea work; Charleston (US) for US offshore; the factories are capable for standard HV but EHV capability (400kV+, HVDC ultra-high voltage) is more limited than NKT's Karlskrona target or PRY's Pikkala

  • Workforce and skills: Nexans' HV specialist workforce has been growing under the "New Nexans" pivot; however, the EHV technical talent pool is thinner than at NKT or PRY, given the longer history of EHV manufacturing at both competitors; attracting and retaining EHV engineers is a constraint on Nexans' ramp trajectory
  • Capital investment has been constrained by leverage: Nexans cannot invest at the same scale as NKT or PRY while also servicing elevated debt; this creates a compounding disadvantage: less investment → slower capability build → lower-margin projects → slower deleveraging → less investment available; breaking this cycle is the core case thesis
  • The "New Nexans" portfolio rationalisation has freed up some capital: disposals of non-cable businesses have generated proceeds that have partly funded HV capacity investments; however, the transformation is not complete and the capital available for growth capex remains constrained by the debt service obligation
