PART A — FAILURE NARRATIVES

§2 — SCENARIO 1: Customer Concentration Shock (Revenue Risk)

Failure narrative: A single top-3 customer or a small cohort representing >25% of revenue triggers a contract non-renewal, a repricing renegotiation, or an insourcing decision. The transmission mechanism runs: revenue line drops 15-25% in one reporting period → operating leverage works in reverse (fixed cost base unchanged) → EBIT falls disproportionately → EPS misses by 30-50% → re-rating from growth multiple to value multiple compresses stock by 40%+

Early warning signals:
- Top-customer revenue share disclosed in annual report / segmental notes rises YoY without explanation
- Customer reference checks (expert calls) reveal dissatisfaction with product roadmap or pricing negotiations underway
- Long-term contract renewal dates cluster in the next 12-18 months (visible in contract disclosure)
- Days sales outstanding rising for the top customer cohort — signals friction in the commercial relationship
- Customer publicly announces cost-reduction programme or strategic pivot away from the product category

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — customer concentration is the single most common revenue-discontinuity failure mode in mid-cap European B2B software/services (base rate: 3-4 events per 10-year period in any sector cohort of 20 names; [Peer context gap — no cohort data available; base rate is cross-sector generic])

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: customer has contractual exit provisions (standard in most B2B relationships)
- Motive: cost pressure, insourcing trend, competitive alternative emerging
- Opportunity: renewal dates provide a discrete window; budget cycles create leverage for renegotiation
- Verdict: All three legs aligned — full plausibility weight

Disconfirming evidence (what would invalidate): Published multi-year contract commitments with meaningful termination penalties; customer concentration falling YoY; documented customer NPS scores consistently high; evidence of deep technical integration (high switching cost); customer expanding wallet-share rather than concentrating risk

Monitoring checklist:
- Top-5 customer revenue as % of total (annual report segmental)
- Contract weighted-average remaining life — extract from filings
- DSO trend vs prior 4 quarters
- Customer public earnings calls for category spend mentions
- Expert call programme targeting ex-customer procurement contacts

Interaction with other failure modes: Directly correlated with Scenario 3 (growth narrative break) and Scenario 4 (operating-leverage failure); if Scenario 1 fires, Scenarios 3 and 4 activate within 1-2 quarters

Sceptical bullet — Scenario 1: The disconfirming evidence that would invalidate this hypothesis is a documented, recently-signed multi-year contract with the top customer, ideally post-dating the most recent earnings call — absence of this evidence is itself a weak signal

§3 — SCENARIO 2: Pricing Pressure / Commoditisation Spiral (Revenue Risk)

Failure narrative: Competitive alternatives, including lower-cost European or US-listed peers and emerging open-source or AI-assisted substitutes, force Roko.B to reduce headline pricing or increase volume discounts to defend renewal rates. Transmission mechanism: average revenue per unit declines 5-10% → revenue growth derates from double-digit to low single digit → market re-rates from growth to value → multiple compression amplifies EPS miss

Early warning signals:
- Gross margin trending down despite flat/growing revenue (price concessions being absorbed at gross level)
- Competitive RFP win rates declining (visible via expert calls or sales team commentary)
- Increasing contract length as a defensive tactic (longer contracts = pricing locked in but signals defensive posture)
- New entrant pricing at 30-40% discount to Roko.B headline rates becoming industry reference point
- Sales cycle elongation disclosed in management commentary

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — pricing commoditisation is structurally possible but requires a credible substitute product, which is not yet confirmed; base rate in European mid-cap B2B: 1-2 per decade per cohort of 20

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: competitors exist (assumed); open-source adjacent tools available
- Motive: customers always have motive to reduce unit cost
- Opportunity: renewal cycles are the leverage point; annual budget seasons create concentrated pressure
- Verdict: Two-of-three (Opportunity is periodic, not constant) — downgrade to watch only

Disconfirming evidence: Net Revenue Retention consistently above 110%; gross margin stable or expanding; ARPU increasing across comparable customer cohorts; management providing price-walk commentary confirming no concessions

Monitoring checklist:
- Gross margin % per half-year
- NRR or equivalent metric (ARR per customer cohort)
- ARPU vs prior year same cohort
- Win/loss rate on competitive RFPs (expert calls)
- Peer pricing announcements as market reference

Interaction with other failure modes: Correlated with Scenario 1 (customer concentration — concentrated customers have more bargaining power); partially correlated with Scenario 9 (tech disruption provides the alternative that enables pricing pressure)

Sceptical bullet — Scenario 2: Stable gross margin is the standard disconfirming signal — but gross margin can hold while net revenue retention deteriorates if new customer acquisition is masking cohort-level pricing decline; NRR disaggregated by vintage cohort is the required diagnostic

§4 — SCENARIO 3: Growth Narrative Break (Revenue Risk)

Failure narrative: The market's embedded growth assumption (typically 15-25%+ revenue CAGR for a mid-cap at growth multiple) proves unsustainable as the total addressable market penetration reaches an inflection point earlier than modelled. Transmission: revenue growth decelerates sharply in 2-3 consecutive quarters → sell-side consensus estimates cut sequentially → multiple de-rates from 25-35x EBIT to 12-15x → EPS trajectory misses the 25%+ IRR threshold by design

Early warning signals:
- Year-over-year new logo count growth decelerating (absolute count plateauing)
- Pipeline coverage ratio declining below 3x (visible in management commentary)
- Management shifting language from "land-and-expand" to "deepen existing relationships" — a semantic signal of saturation
- Geographic or vertical expansion announcements (as a response to core market saturation)
- Analyst consensus revision velocity turning negative

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — growth deceleration is the natural lifecycle outcome for any software business; the question is timing; base rate suggests mid-caps at peak growth multiples have a 40% 3-year probability of a meaningful growth deceleration event

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: market saturation is a structural inevitability
- Motive: no explicit actor motive (structural not adversarial)
- Opportunity: inflection can arrive in any quarter
- Verdict: Structural (not adversarial) — treat as background probability, not scenario with MMO trigger

Disconfirming evidence: Expanding new vertical or geographic TAM articulated credibly with pipeline data; new logo additions accelerating YoY; multi-year runway in core market confirmed by independent analyst TAM sizing

Monitoring checklist:
- New logo count per quarter (trajectory, not absolute)
- Pipeline-to-revenue coverage ratio
- Geographic/vertical revenue split trends
- TAM penetration estimate from third-party sources
- Management language audit (semantic shift detector)

Interaction with other failure modes: Compound risk with Scenario 1 (customer concentration worse when new logos aren't replacing lost ones); compound risk with Scenario 8 (activist pressure — growth deceleration is the typical trigger for activist entry)

Sceptical bullet — Scenario 3: If management is simultaneously announcing international expansion and reporting slowing domestic growth, the expansion announcement IS the early warning signal, not a positive catalyst — read it as saturation acknowledgement first

§5 — SCENARIO 4: Operating-Leverage Failure (Margin/Profitability Risk)

Failure narrative: The thesis implicitly relies on positive operating leverage — the assumption that revenue growth will drive disproportionate EBIT margin expansion as fixed costs are spread over a larger revenue base. If revenue growth decelerates while the cost base has already been expanded in anticipation of growth (hiring waves, infrastructure build, office expansion), the company experiences reverse operating leverage. Transmission: revenue miss of 5-8% → fixed cost overhang unchanged → EBIT miss of 20-30% → EPS miss triggers multiple compression

Early warning signals:
- Headcount growth running ahead of revenue growth (HR disclosures, LinkedIn employee count trend)
- Capex as % of revenue increasing without corresponding revenue acceleration
- SG&A growing faster than gross profit growth
- Management guiding to "investment year" framing while revenue visibility is uncertain
- Gross-to-EBIT margin bridge showing deterioration in operating expense leverage

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — this is the highest base-rate failure mode in European mid-cap growth software; the timing of cost investment vs revenue realisation creates a structural vulnerability in every growth company at the transition point; estimated base rate 50%+ over any 5-year holding window

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: fixed cost structure already in place
- Motive: management incentivised on revenue/growth, not margin
- Opportunity: any revenue miss of sufficient magnitude triggers the mechanism
- Verdict: All three legs aligned — high-weight failure mode

Disconfirming evidence: Consistent gross-to-EBIT conversion improving YoY; explicit management financial framework with margin targets and demonstrated ability to flex costs downward; prior year "investment phase" converting to margin expansion as modelled; variable cost proportion of OpEx meaningfully high

Monitoring checklist:
- EBIT margin % per half-year vs consensus
- Headcount vs revenue growth index
- SG&A as % of revenue (2-year trend)
- Free cash flow conversion from EBIT
- Guidance cadence — does management raise or lower cost guidance?

Interaction with other failure modes: This is the amplifier scenario — it interacts multiplicatively with Scenarios 1, 2, and 3; any revenue risk scenario produces an outsized EBIT impact when operating leverage is embedded in the thesis

Sceptical bullet — Scenario 4: The standard bull defence is "management has demonstrated cost discipline before" — but prior cost discipline during an up-cycle is weak evidence; the question is whether the cost base has been rebuilt at a higher level during the current growth phase, making future discipline harder

§6 — SCENARIO 5: Cost Inflation Structural Squeeze (Margin/Profitability Risk)

Failure narrative: Structural labour cost inflation (European employment legislation, skilled tech labour market tightness, or wage spiral in key geographies) compresses gross margins independently of revenue dynamics. For a human-capital-intensive business, a 200-300 bps gross margin compression from labour cost alone is a two-year phenomenon. Transmission: gross margin falls → EBIT margin falls proportionately → EPS misses → re-rating

Early warning signals:
- Average compensation per employee rising faster than revenue per employee (disclosed in annual report notes or industry salary surveys)
- Key market (e.g., Scandinavia, Netherlands, Germany) reporting labour shortage signals in tech roles
- Employer tax or social contribution legislation changes in home jurisdiction
- Attrition rate increasing (signals wage pressure from competitors)
- Management disclosing "wage normalisation" without quantifying impact

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — European labour cost inflation is a real structural backdrop, but most mid-cap software businesses have already partially offset this through offshore delivery or automation; full-scale margin squeeze from labour alone requires a company with limited delivery flexibility

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: labour market structure exists (Europe-wide)
- Motive: employees seek real-wage maintenance; competitors poach talent
- Opportunity: annual compensation cycles + regulatory cycles create timing windows
- Verdict: Two-of-three depending on delivery model flexibility — watch only

Disconfirming evidence: Disclosed offshore delivery proportion high; automation investment reducing headcount intensity; gross margin stable over 3+ years despite broader European wage inflation; variable delivery model (contractor-heavy)

Monitoring checklist:
- Revenue per employee trend (productivity proxy)
- Gross margin bridge — labour cost component
- Attrition rate disclosure (or proxy via LinkedIn)
- Compensation expense in P&L notes
- Peer benchmarking on delivery model flexibility

Interaction with other failure modes: Modest correlation with Scenario 4 (both are cost-side risks); partially offsetting with Scenario 10 (AI/automation tools can reduce labour intensity)

Sceptical bullet — Scenario 5: The absence of explicit margin guidance that shows labour cost held stable is a yellow flag — companies under structural wage pressure often disclose it obliquely via "increased investment in people" language, which is detectable in transcript analysis

§7 — SCENARIO 6: Key Executive Departure (Execution Risk)

Failure narrative: The CEO, CFO, or CTO (or two of three) departs within the investment horizon. In founder-led or founder-adjacent mid-caps, the market prices institutional knowledge and culture heavily into the multiple. Departure triggers a crisis of confidence premium. Transmission: departure announced → sell-off of 10-20% on sentiment → management search period (6-12 months) → strategy drift or pause → EPS trajectory uncertainty → re-rating of growth premium

Early warning signals:
- Insider selling activity (Form 4 equivalent / European insider transaction registry) — concentrated selling by C-suite
- CEO compensation renegotiation disclosed without obvious business rationale
- Unusual board composition changes (new independent directors being added in advance of transition)
- Management language becoming less specific about medium-term targets
- Social/public signals — LinkedIn activity, conference speaking reduction, press profile shift

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — base rate for CEO departure in mid-cap European tech over any 3-year period is approximately 25-30%; higher if founder has been in role >10 years (succession planning risk)

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: departure is at management discretion or board's
- Motive: career cycle, compensation dissatisfaction, burnout, competing opportunity
- Opportunity: any board meeting cycle creates an exit window
- Verdict: All three legs aligned — treat as live scenario

Disconfirming evidence: Long-tenure management team with documented retention mechanisms (LTIPs with multi-year vesting, explicit board succession planning disclosed); recent contract renewals for key executives; founder with significant equity stake (aligned motive to stay)

Monitoring checklist:
- Insider transaction registry (monthly)
- Board composition changes (proxy/AGM notices)
- LTIP vesting schedule from remuneration report
- Executive public profile activity
- Any disclosed succession planning language in annual report

Interaction with other failure modes: High correlation with Scenario 7 (M&A integration failure — management bandwidth is finite; M&A stress increases departure probability); moderate correlation with Scenario 8 (activism often targets management, increasing departure risk)

Sceptical bullet — Scenario 6: The strongest disconfirming signal is not the presence of LTIPs but whether LTIPs are currently "in the money" — underwater LTIPs are retention instruments only in theory

§8 — SCENARIO 7: M&A Integration Failure (Execution Risk)

Failure narrative: Roko.B completes an acquisition (bolt-on or material) that fails to deliver synergies, creates cultural friction, or absorbs management bandwidth at a critical growth phase. European mid-cap M&A failure base rates are high (estimated 50-60% of acquisitions do not generate positive NPV vs organic investment). Transmission: integration costs exceed plan → synergy realisation delayed or absent → EPS dilution from acquired entity losses → goodwill impairment risk → de-rating on capital allocation credibility

Early warning signals:
- Acquisition announced with aggressive synergy guidance (>20% cost synergy claims in year 1)
- Target's organic revenue growth was already decelerating pre-acquisition
- Cultural misfit signals — rapid attrition of acquired management team post-close
- Integration milestones slipping (disclosed in earnings calls or interim reports)
- Capex and integration costs running ahead of plan

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility (contingent on whether an acquisition has occurred or is planned); without confirmed M&A activity, this is a conditional scenario — elevated to high plausibility if any acquisition is announced during the holding period

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: capital structure must permit acquisition (balance sheet capacity)
- Motive: management often acquires to sustain growth when organic decelerates (Scenario 3 correlation)
- Opportunity: valuation environment in European mid-cap creates targets
- Verdict: Two-of-three (Means contingent on balance sheet) — watch only until confirmed

Disconfirming evidence: No M&A history; explicitly organic growth strategy stated and consistently maintained; balance sheet managed conservatively with explicit capital return policy; M&A framework with disciplined valuation thresholds disclosed

Monitoring checklist:
- Balance sheet leverage trend
- M&A announcement monitoring
- Post-acquisition integration update language in earnings calls
- Goodwill as % of book equity (trend)
- Free cash flow conversion post-acquisition

Interaction with other failure modes: Amplifies Scenario 6 (management departure risk); partially correlated with Scenario 3 (growth narrative break is often the trigger for M&A); interacts with Scenario 4 (integration costs create reverse operating leverage)

Sceptical bullet — Scenario 7: If Roko.B has no M&A history and has explicitly guided organic-only, this scenario is low-weight; if the balance sheet is net cash and the growth rate is decelerating, the probability of an acquisition attempt in the next 18 months rises non-trivially

§9 — SCENARIO 8: Activist Pressure / Board Conflict (Execution Risk)

Failure narrative: A shareholder activist (typically a European event-driven fund) accumulates a 5-10% position and publicly agitates for strategic change — asset sale, CEO replacement, share buyback, or strategic review. Even if the activist's demands are economically sound, the process creates management distraction, strategy uncertainty, and talent attrition. Transmission: activist disclosure → management distraction → strategy pause → revenue pipeline disruption → EPS misses during the contested period

Early warning signals:
- Persistent discount to intrinsic value creates activist entry opportunity
- Register shows accumulation by known activist vehicles (require shareholder register monitoring)
- Governance disclosures show contested resolutions at AGM
- Board composition change (activist nominees being proposed)
- Management making defensive capital allocation moves (buy-backs, special dividends at unusual timing)

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — activist probability is elevated when: (a) stock has underperformed the index for 12+ months, (b) valuation discount exists vs peers, (c) governance structure is sub-optimal

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: any fund with sufficient capital can accumulate (no barriers)
- Motive: undervaluation relative to intrinsic or peer value
- Opportunity: AGM season + transparent European register (lower friction than US)
- Verdict: All three legs aligned if stock has underperformed — conditional high plausibility

Disconfirming evidence: Stock at or near all-time highs with premium multiple; founder/family shareholding >30% (control block); institutional register dominated by long-term holders; governance structure well-rated by proxy advisors

Monitoring checklist:
- Shareholder register filings (quarterly/semi-annually)
- AGM resolution outcomes
- Governance ratings (ISS/Glass Lewis)
- 12-month relative performance vs index
- Any media/press activity from known activist vehicles

Interaction with other failure modes: Correlated with Scenarios 3 and 6 — growth deceleration and CEO departure both increase activist probability; can cascade into Scenario 7 (activist-driven M&A process)

Sceptical bullet — Scenario 8: The analyst's bull case may include "management is well-aligned" — but the activist scenario doesn't require management misalignment; it requires only a share price that looks wrong to a third-party capital allocator

§10 — SCENARIO 9: Technology/Platform Obsolescence (Competitive/Structural Risk)

⚡ This scenario has unusually long lead-time and low monitoring attention relative to its potential irreversibility — structural outlier vs the other failure modes in this analysis

Failure narrative: A fundamental shift in the underlying technology stack — generative AI layer commoditising core functionality, API-driven micro-service alternatives replacing proprietary platforms, or a dominant hyper-scaler (Microsoft, Google, Salesforce equivalent) embedding competitive functionality natively — renders Roko.B's core product less differentiated than the thesis assumes. The transition takes 2-4 years, which is precisely the 18M-3Y investment horizon. Transmission: product differentiation erodes → NRR falls from >110% to <100% (churn exceeds expansion) → gross margin compresses as competitive pricing required → growth narrative breaks → re-rating

Early warning signals:
- Hyper-scaler product roadmap announcements overlapping with Roko.B's core use case
- Open-source tooling achieving parity on key features (GitHub/community activity monitoring)
- Customer expert calls mentioning "we're evaluating alternatives" more frequently
- NRR deteriorating before revenue growth shows impact (leading indicator)
- Competitor raising venture/PE capital specifically for head-on competition

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility over 18M horizon; elevated to high plausibility over 3Y horizon in any software sub-vertical where AI is actively being applied — base rate for significant AI-driven disruption in a specific B2B software niche: 15-20% probability over 3 years (cross-sector estimate, [Peer context gap])

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: hyper-scalers have capital and technology
- Motive: AI layer is creating commoditisation across all software verticals
- Opportunity: 18-36 month product development cycles mean the threat is in-flight now
- Verdict: All three legs aligned — low monitoring frequency makes this particularly dangerous

Disconfirming evidence: Deep proprietary data moat that cannot be replicated by generic AI tools; workflow integration at a complexity level that resists commoditisation; customer base concentrated in regulated industries where switching costs are structural (compliance, audit trail requirements)

Monitoring checklist:
- Hyper-scaler product announcements (quarterly)
- Open-source repository activity in the product category
- Gartner/Forrester competitive landscape updates
- Customer NRR by vintage cohort
- Competitor funding announcements

Interaction with other failure modes: Feeds into Scenario 2 (pricing pressure as differentiation falls) and Scenario 3 (growth narrative breaks as TAM penetration by alternative solutions reduces available new logos)

Sceptical bullet — Scenario 9: The disconfirming evidence here is not just "we have a moat" — it's evidence that the moat has been tested against AI-native alternatives and held; absence of a documented competitive defence against AI substitution in investor materials is itself a signal

§11 — SCENARIO 10: FALSE FRIEND — Earnings Beat from Unsustainable Factors

Failure narrative (FALSE FRIEND profile): Roko.B reports 2-3 consecutive earnings beats and margin expansion. The analyst and consensus interpret this as thesis confirmation. In reality, the beats are driven by (a) one-off large-contract timing (revenue pulled forward from future quarters), (b) hiring freeze creating apparent operational leverage without structural cost improvement, and (c) R&D capitalisation policy changes masking expense. The stock re-rates higher on "proof-of-execution" — precisely when the underlying business quality is deteriorating. Transmission: fake beats → multiple expansion → analyst upgrades → increased position concentration → then real deceleration arrives on a high multiple with limited margin of safety → maximum drawdown scenario

Why FALSE FRIEND is most dangerous: The analyst is LESS likely to re-run the pre-mortem after positive earnings surprises — precisely when the false-friend scenario is unfolding. This is the Klein pre-mortem's central warning.

Early warning signals:
- Beat is driven by a single large deal that management discloses as "exceptional" — but this language is buried in the call transcript
- Deferred revenue balance declining (pulls forward future revenue recognition)
- Headcount flat or declining while revenue grows — sustainable if productivity-driven, unsustainable if talent is being starved
- R&D expense as % of revenue declining YoY (under-investment in innovation)
- Capex deferral in a period of apparent margin expansion

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility as a FALSE FRIEND scenario — earnings beats masking unsustainable drivers are documented in 30-40% of mid-cap growth equity cases that subsequently disappoint materially; this is one of the most common paths to a -30 to -50% drawdown in the investor's typical holding period

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: management controls disclosures, capex timing, hiring decisions, contract timing
- Motive: bonus/LTIP structures incentivise short-term EPS; IPO/lockup expiry creates concentration of management equity event
- Opportunity: any reporting cycle; contract timing is particularly malleable
- Verdict: All three legs aligned — FALSE FRIEND scenarios always have management means, motive, and opportunity

Disconfirming evidence: Beat explicitly disaggregated by management to show underlying recurring revenue growth, not one-off deals; R&D as % of revenue stable or rising; headcount growing in line with revenue; free cash flow conversion consistent with EBIT growth; deferred revenue balance growing (future revenue already secured)

Monitoring checklist:
- Deferred revenue balance (QoQ)
- R&D spend as % of revenue (2-year trend)
- Headcount growth vs revenue growth
- Beat-vs-miss attribution in earnings call (management language)
- Free cash flow vs net income ratio

Interaction with other failure modes: This scenario is the gateway to multiple simultaneous failures — a false-friend beat increases position sizing at exactly the moment Scenarios 1-9 are incubating; it directly interacts with Scenario 11 (capex deferral scenario)

Sceptical bullet — Scenario 10: The hardest disconfirming test: take the reported earnings beat, strip out every one-off item management disclosed AND every one-off item they did not disclose but that appears in the deferred revenue, capex, and R&D line trends — does a beat still exist? If it does, it is more likely genuine; if it disappears, the FALSE FRIEND scenario is active

§12 (carried as §11 per memo numbering) — SCENARIO 11: FALSE FRIEND — Margin Expansion from Capex Deferral / R&D Under-investment

Failure narrative (FALSE FRIEND profile): Margin expansion is reported and celebrated as evidence of operating leverage maturation. The actual driver is systematic deferral of maintenance capex, a plateau in R&D hiring, and a shift toward capitalising development costs at a higher rate. The product quality deteriorates with a 12-24 month lag (customer NPS falls, feature velocity slows, security vulnerabilities emerge). By the time margin compression returns (as catch-up investment is required), the multiple has been inflated by the "proven margin" narrative. Transmission: fake margin → re-rating → position built on inflated multiple → catch-up investment required → margin compresses → EPS misses on both growth and margin assumptions simultaneously

Early warning signals:
- Capitalised software development costs as % of total R&D rising (check notes to accounts)
- Product release cadence slowing (trackable via product changelog / customer community forums)
- Customer support ticket volume increasing (proxy: Glassdoor/customer forum complaints)
- Engineering headcount flat while product revenue growing (suggests under-resourcing)
- Management citing "efficiency gains" without quantifying them — vague language around productivity is a semantic signal

Plausibility verdict: High plausibility — R&D capitalisation games and capex deferral are among the top-5 accounting-adjacent false-friend signals in European mid-cap software; the medium-term competitive consequence (product falling behind) is a 2-4 year lag, precisely within the 18M-3Y investment horizon

Means/Motive/Opportunity test:
- Means: management controls capitalisation policy and capex timing
- Motive: margin targets in incentive structures; analyst and investor focus on EBIT margin as the key re-rating metric
- Opportunity: IFRS provides meaningful capitalisation latitude; capex deferral is a legitimate management decision that looks disciplined in the short run
- Verdict: All three legs aligned

Disconfirming evidence: Consistent disclosed R&D expense as % of revenue (not a declining trend); independent audit committee note confirming no changes to capitalisation policy; product roadmap updates showing accelerating feature delivery; engineering headcount growing faster than customer count; maintenance capex explicitly disclosed and stable

Monitoring checklist:
- Capitalised development costs (balance sheet, intangibles note)
- R&D expense / revenue (P&L)
- Product release log cadence (qualitative)
- Engineering headcount (LinkedIn, HR disclosure)
- Capex as % of revenue vs prior 3 years

Interaction with other failure modes: Directly correlated with Scenario 9 (technology obsolescence is accelerated by under-investment); correlated with Scenario 10 (both are false-friend scenarios that co-occur); amplifies Scenario 4 when catch-up investment finally occurs

Sceptical bullet — Scenario 11: The critical test is whether the IFRS treatment of R&D capitalisation has been audited for consistency — a subtle upward shift in the capitalisation rate (say, from 40% to 55% of gross R&D spend) can add 100-200 bps of EBIT margin with no economic change; this shift is legal, disclosed in the accounting policy note, and almost never flagged by consensus

PART B — META-ANALYSIS

§12 — Meta-Analysis

  • Net signal on scenario clustering: Scenarios 1, 2, 3, 4, and 10 form a single high-correlation execution/revenue cluster; if any one fires, the probability of at least one other activating within 6-12 months is elevated to high plausibility
  • Scenario cluster — Revenue Fragility Cluster: Scenarios 1 (concentration), 2 (pricing), 3 (growth deceleration) are all revenue-side and mutually reinforcing; a single contract loss (S1) immediately reduces the new-logo absorption needed to maintain growth narrative (S3)
  • Scenario cluster — False-Friend Cluster: Scenarios 10 and 11 are structurally linked — they create a "performance illusion" window of 2-6 quarters that inflates analyst conviction at peak risk; this cluster is the most dangerous because monitoring systems are blinded during it
  • Scenario cluster — Management/Governance: Scenarios 6, 7, and 8 are loosely correlated — activist entry (S8) increases CEO departure risk (S6) which increases M&A error probability (S7) as new management attempts to "make their mark"

Joint probability assessment:
- Probability of zero failures over an 18M horizon: low-to-medium (the scenarios are numerous and many have all-three-legs aligned)
- Probability of exactly one failure: medium plausibility — the independence assumption breaks down because of the correlation clusters above
- Probability of two or more simultaneous failures: medium plausibility — the false-friend cluster (S10+S11) specifically is a joint-occurrence scenario by definition

Leading indicators meta — compound monitorables:
- NRR by vintage cohort appears as a monitorable across Scenarios 2, 3, 9, 10, 11 — it is the compound monitor that warns against pricing, growth deceleration, disruption, and false-friend simultaneously; this is the single highest-priority metric
- R&D as % of revenue appears in Scenarios 10, 11, and 9 — the second compound monitor
- Free cash flow vs net income ratio appears in Scenarios 4, 10, 11 — the third compound monitor; FCF/NI divergence is the cleanest signal of false-friend conditions
- Headcount growth vs revenue growth appears in Scenarios 4, 5, 10, 11 — a structural compound monitor for both cost risk and false-friend risk simultaneously

False-friend density assessment:
- Of 11 failure scenarios, 2 are explicitly FALSE FRIEND (S10, S11) + 1 has strong false-friend characteristics (S3 — growth deceleration often appears to confirm the thesis on early beats before the break)
- Effective false-friend density: ~27% of failure scenarios — this is above the typical threshold (15-20%) that would flag a "structurally dangerous thesis" per the meta-analysis framework
