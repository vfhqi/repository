Section 3 – External Change: Value-Chain Forces (Demand, Competition, Disruption, Regulation, Supply, Cycle, Macro) 33 findings

Section summary
  1. Massive structural grid investments drive unprecedented high-voltage cable demand
  2. supply-chain bottlenecks, commodity volatility, and rising competitor installation capacity threaten execution

Demand

1.
Structural demand pulse: grid capex is set to rise, with ENTSO-E projecting 2031-35 European offshore network investments 41% higher than 2025-30
2.
Rising volume: the European market anticipates approximately 20,000 km of HVDC cable auctions for 2024-2025, reflecting severe structural undersupply
3.
Strengthening customer behaviour: the proliferation of data centres and AI infrastructure is pushing global electricity demand up 140% by 2060
4.
Order acceleration: over 180,000 km of cross-border interconnectors and deep-sea megaprojects are announced across Europe, the Middle East, and Asia
5.
Weakening customer behaviour: the low-voltage construction segment remains subdued across Europe, with high interest rates significantly dampening new building activity

Competition

6.
Consolidating structure: the high-voltage market operates as a tight oligopoly, with NKT, Prysmian, and Nexans dominating the technologically demanding 525 kV HVDC segment
7.
Rising intensity: peers Nexans and Prysmian are deploying new cable-laying vessels like Electra and Monna Lisa, directly threatening NKT Victoria's installation market share
8.
New entrant: Sumitomo Electric is aggressively entering the European market by constructing a new subsea HVDC cable factory in Scotland by mid-2026
9.
Compressing competitor margin: intensified competition in Central Europe's medium-voltage distribution segment drove a 290 bps margin contraction in NKT's Applications division
10.
Favourable geography: NKT's strict European focus inherently shields it from aggressive Chinese supply-chain competition that plagues broader global industrial markets

Disruption

11.
Accelerating technology disruption: the energy transition demands a rapid shift from traditional alternating current (AC) to complex direct current (DC) transmission
12.
Innovative new entrant: the unprecedented commercial application of 525 kV DC land cables introduces massive execution and technological risks over 30-to-50-year lifespans
13.
Substitute emerging: NKT's KFEV 245 dry-type outdoor termination is displacing conventional oil-filled technology, eliminating leakage risks and simplifying grid installation
14.
Strong incumbent response: NKT's newly launched Adaptive Rigid Seajoint provides a modular "one-for-all" solution for submarine cables up to 300 kV

Regulation

15.
Supportive political actor: the EU Green Deal and Net-Zero Industry Act provide a robust long-term policy framework for continuous grid expansion
16.
Adverse macro shift: shifting national agendas recently led France to reduce its offshore wind targets from 18 GW to 15 GW
17.
New regulatory shift: implementation of the EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) introduces significant compliance costs for carbon-intensive imports
18.
Hostile political actor: a change in US administration has cast doubt on offshore wind projects, prompting peers to redirect capacity toward Europe

Supply

19.
Rising supply risk: soaring component demand has severely stretched industry lead times, with cable deliveries now ranging from 4.4 to 7.4 years
20.
Weakening supplier health: grid equipment shortages are acute, with gas turbines and transformers facing lead times exceeding four years
21.
Adverse macro shift: geopolitical tensions in the Red Sea and Ukraine have severely disrupted logistics and availability of key conductor metals
22.
Falling supplier returns: escalating global supply-chain localisation threatens access to critical raw materials, with copper and aluminium facing structural shortages by 2030

Cycle

23.
Peak behaviour: NKT's high-voltage order backlog hit a staggering EUR 13.5bn, locking in production capacity and providing multi-year earnings visibility
24.
Late cycle stage: record high order intake reflects severe current undersupply, but the market is expected to reach equilibrium entering the 2030s
25.
Rising cyclicality: while structural megatrends drive high-voltage demand, the medium- and low-voltage Applications business remains highly exposed to shorter-cycle construction swings
26.
Trough behaviour: Jefferies anticipates a sequential recovery in the currently depressed low-voltage construction market as interest rates gradually normalise

Macro

27.
Adverse macro shift: persistently high interest rates and capital costs have squeezed developer NPVs, causing major utilities to delay wind and solar capex
28.
Rising inflation: volatile copper and aluminium prices, amplified by geopolitical conflicts, create severe margin pressure if costs cannot be fully passed through
29.
Supportive macro context: NKT partially mitigates raw-material inflation through extensive hedging and rigid indexation clauses within long-term framework contracts
30.
Geopolitical risk: escalating supply-chain fragmentation and protectionist trade policies challenge the economic viability of complex cross-border energy infrastructure projects
31.
Other side of the story: aggressive capacity expansions by NKT and its peers could abruptly tilt the current undersupply into a massive supply glut; if high interest rates cancel major offshore projects, stranded capacity would rapidly destroy pricing power across the sector
