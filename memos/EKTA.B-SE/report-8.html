Executive Summary

Elekta AB competes in a $13.3 billion global radiation therapy market growing at 6.8% CAGR through 2027. The company sits in a duopoly with Siemens Healthineers/Varian, controlling approximately 42% of global linear accelerator (linac) market share versus Varian's 37%, with the two firms commanding 75% of the linac market collectively. This duopoly reflects structural barriers to entry: billion-dollar R&D budgets, decades of clinical relationships, global service networks, stringent FDA/CE regulatory pathways, and hospital switching costs embedded in integrated software ecosystems. Elekta's competitive position rests on three pillars: hardware leadership (linac, Gamma Knife radiosurgery), MRI-guided radiotherapy innovation (Unity MRI-linac system, growing at 18.1% CAGR), and software/informatics market share (MOSAIQ oncology information system, Monaco treatment planning system). The company's financial profile has strengthened materially: FY2024/25 gross margin reached 40.3% (best Q4 in five years), adjusted EBIT margin of 16.3%, with software revenue growing at 15% annually and now representing 26% of total sales. Recurring revenue (software services, SaaS, service contracts, remote monitoring) now comprises 42% of net sales, driving margin expansion and cash conversion. The addressable market is expanding through three vectors: (1) MRI-linac adoption accelerating (18.1% CAGR through 2033, Unity and ViewRay at ~80% market share), (2) proton therapy market growing 13% CAGR but remaining niche, (3) AI-driven treatment planning driving software upgrade cycles. Structural risks centre on three fault lines: (1) Siemens Healthineers' 2021 acquisition of Varian ($16.4 billion) creating a larger, better-integrated competitor with EUR 300 million+ annual synergies by FY25, (2) Chinese manufacturers (Shanghai United Imaging at 15% linac market share in China, rising from 4% localization in 2019 to 16% by H1 2024) threatening pricing power and market share in Asia-Pacific, and (3) ViewRay's Chapter 11 bankruptcy (July 2023) signalling execution risk in the emerging MRI-linac space, though Elekta's Unity platform has outcompeted ViewRay's MRIdian on clinical adoption and scale. The sector exhibits natural monopoly characteristics: capital intensity, regulatory moats, and installed-base switching costs generate persistent margin advantages for scale players, but also compress returns for second-tier competitors. Elekta's competitive position rates Yellow-to-Green: strong market share, differentiated technology, and improving financials, but vulnerable to consolidation pressure from Siemens Healthineers and Chinese competitive encroachment in emerging markets.

1. Industry Overview

The global radiation therapy market valued at $6.23 billion in 2022 is projected to reach $13.28 billion by 2027, representing a compound annual growth rate of 6.77%. This growth is driven by three primary structural forces: rising cancer incidence globally (particularly in emerging markets), aging population demographics in developed economies, and technology adoption cycles (linac replacements, MRI-linac upgrades, software subscriptions).

The market segments into five distinct sub-markets with different growth rates and competitive dynamics. Linear accelerators (linacs) dominate by revenue and unit volume, with Varian TrueBeam and Elekta Versa HD as the installed base standards. MRI-guided radiotherapy is the fastest-growing sub-segment at 18.1% CAGR (2023-2033), starting from a much smaller base ($545 million in 2023, projected to reach $2.89 billion by 2033). Proton therapy systems are growing at 12-15% CAGR but remain concentrated in developed markets due to capex requirements ($150-250 million per centre). Treatment planning system software markets are expanding at 9.0% CAGR ($2.18 billion in 2023 to $4.75 billion by 2032), driven by AI integration. Oncology information systems are growing at 8-10% CAGR but fragmented across EHR vendors (Epic, Cerner) and specialist providers (Elekta MOSAIQ, Varian ARIA, RaySearch RayCare).

Geographic concentration is pronounced. North America represents 40-45% of global radiation therapy market revenue, Western Europe 25-30%, and Asia-Pacific 15-20% (but growing fastest at 9-11% CAGR due to emerging market capex, particularly China's rapid healthcare infrastructure build-out). Developing markets (China, India, Middle East) are penetration-constrained by capex availability and regulatory approval timelines, but represent the highest long-term growth opportunity.

Industry consolidation trends are bifurcated. In hardware (linacs, proton systems), the market exhibits strong duopoly/oligopoly dynamics: Siemens Healthineers/Varian, Elekta, and IBA (proton-focused) control 85%+ of global market. The 2021 acquisition of Varian by Siemens Healthineers for $16.4 billion has created the dominant integrated player, combining linac hardware, proton systems (Varian Proteus), treatment planning software, and oncology informatics into a vertically integrated stack. In software (TPS, OIS), the market is more fragmented: RaySearch Laboratories (TPS specialist), Elekta (MOSAIQ/Monaco), Varian (ARIA/Eclipse), and EHR vendors compete for hospital wallet share. Independent TPS vendors like RaySearch command pricing power because hospital TPS switching costs are high (retraining, workflow redesign, clinical protocol updates).

The sector exhibits three hallmarks of a "natural monopoly" industry: (1) massive R&D spend requirements ($300-500 million annually for Siemens/Varian/Elekta), (2) installed-base switching costs through software ecosystems and service network lock-in, and (3) regulatory/approval barriers that create 3-5 year time lags for new entrants to achieve market readiness. Consequently, the industry has not produced a new $1+ billion revenue player in radiation oncology hardware since the late 1990s.

Sector Quality Rating: GREEN. Recurring revenue mix (software, services), high barriers to entry, installed-base stickiness, and above-GDP growth rates justify a constructive view. However, concentration risk (Siemens Healthineers scale advantage) and Chinese competition in emerging markets introduce Orange-zone headwinds.

2. Value Chain & Economics

The radiation therapy value chain spans from component manufacturing through hospital end-use, with distinct economic dynamics at each stage.

Stage 1: Component Supply. Magnets (superconducting cores for MRI-linac systems), RF (radiofrequency) components, detectors, and vacuum systems are supplied by specialised vendors (e.g., Siemens Industrial, GE Healthcare for imaging components, niche EU suppliers for magnet cores). Component costs represent 25-35% of system bill of materials, with long lead times (12-18 months) and limited supplier base. Suppliers have moderate pricing power due to specialisation, but are locked into multi-year contracts with system integrators. Margin profile: 12-18% gross margin, low switching risk.

Stage 2: System Integration & Assembly. Elekta, Siemens Healthineers/Varian, IBA, and ViewRay (pre-bankruptcy) integrate components into turnkey therapy systems. This stage requires deep engineering expertise, clinical validation, regulatory compliance (FDA PMA pathway, 2-3 year timelines), and quality assurance infrastructure. Capital intensity is moderate ($50-100 million annually for manufacturing and assembly facilities). Margin profile: 40-50% gross margin on hardware sales; software and service margins higher (60-80%). Pricing power is very high due to lack of substitutes and switching costs.

Stage 3: Installation & Commissioning. Hospitals require site preparation (bunker construction, shielding, electrical/mechanical integration, workflow training). This is labour-intensive and highly variable in cost ($2-5 million per installation depending on facility complexity). It is predominantly provided by the OEM (Elekta, Varian, IBA) through captive field service teams. Margin profile: 25-35% on labour, highly sensitive to utilisation rates. Switching costs are high because hospital staff are trained on specific vendor systems. Service revenue is recurring and sticky.

Stage 4: Clinical Software Layer. Treatment planning systems , oncology information systems , and image-guided radiotherapy control software represent the highest-margin layer. This is where RaySearch Laboratories ($332-375 million revenue in Q3-Q4 2025, 21-24% EBIT margins) and Elekta's software division compete with Varian/Siemens. These systems command 60-85% gross margins and benefit from multi-year maintenance contracts and subscription economics. Switching costs are extremely high because clinical protocols, dosimetry algorithms, and hospital workflows are built around specific TPS systems. RaySearch's gross margin of 91.7% reflects software economics.

Stage 5: Hospital End-User Economics. Hospitals purchase linacs and ancillary equipment on 4-6 year capital budgets (some systems remain in use 10+ years). Total installed cost per linac system averages $3-4 million (equipment, installation, training, commissioning). Operating costs (staffing, maintenance, quality assurance, software licences) add $400,000-600,000 annually. Service contracts typically run at 10-15% of equipment purchase price annually. Hospital procurement is capital-constrained and politically sensitive (NHS budget cycles, US government reimbursement changes). Volume elasticity is low because cancer incidence is relatively inelastic, but there is upgrade demand every 8-10 years.

Power Dynamics. System integrators (Elekta, Siemens/Varian) have very high pricing power vis-à-vis hospitals due to: (1) lack of substitute technologies for radiation therapy, (2) embedded workflow dependencies, (3) multi-year service contract lock-in, (4) regulatory barriers preventing rapid new entrants. Hospitals have countervailing power only through: (1) long purchase cycles (allowing multi-vendor negotiations), (2) group purchasing organisations (GPOs) in the US, (3) centralised procurement (NHS in UK). Software vendors (RaySearch, Eclipse, MOSAIQ) have very high pricing power due to switching costs and limited substitute systems.

Component suppliers have moderate-to-low pricing power because they are commoditised and face vendor consolidation pressure. Installers/service networks earn moderate margins but are essential to customer retention.

Value Chain Rating: GREEN. High-margin software/services layers, recurring revenue concentration, installed-base stickiness, and limited supplier bargaining power create attractive economics for scale incumbents. However, Chinese component suppliers and lower-cost offshore service networks are pressuring margins in Asia-Pacific.

Siemens Healthineers / Varian Division. Following the April 2021 acquisition of Varian Medical Systems for $16.4 billion, Siemens Healthineers became the dominant player in radiation oncology. The combined entity spans: (1) TrueBeam linac platform (dominant in US market), (2) Varian Proteus proton therapy systems (IBA's primary competitor), (3) Eclipse treatment planning system (installed in ~60% of US cancer centres), (4) ARIA oncology information system, (5) imaging systems (CT, MRI, X-ray), and (6) Siemens healthcare informatics infrastructure (SAP-based). The acquisition created vertical integration that allows bundling hardware + software + imaging into "solutions" for health systems. Expected EBIT synergies: EUR 300 million+ by FY2025. Scale advantages: ~$15 billion healthcare revenue annually, deep relationships with hospital C-suites, integrated digital ecosystem. Weakness: integration execution risk, heritage Varian/Siemens cultural friction, slower innovation cycles on legacy Eclipse/ARIA platforms.

Elekta AB. Swedish manufacturer competing primarily in EMEA (Europe, Middle East, Africa) and strong in Asia-Pacific. Product portfolio: (1) Versa HD linac (clinical equal to TrueBeam but at 20-30% lower price point), (2) Unity MRI-linac (market leader in MRI-guided radiotherapy, ~50% market share), (3) Leksell Gamma Knife (radiosurgery, #2-3 position globally), (4) Monaco treatment planning system (installed in ~20-25% of European centres), (5) MOSAIQ oncology information system (installed base ~1,200+ hospitals globally, largest oncology-specific OIS outside North America). Revenue scale: ~SEK 5.1 billion (FY2024/25, ~USD 500 million), representing ~4-5% of global radiation therapy equipment market by value. Strengths: Unity MRI-linac leadership, MOSAIQ installed base in EMEA and ANZ, lower linac price point (Versa HD), software/services growing at 15% annually. Weaknesses: smaller scale vs. Siemens/Varian (1/10th the size), lower R&D budget, North America market share gap (Varian TrueBeam dominant in US), exposure to EMEA healthcare budget cycles.

Tier 2: Niche Specialists.

Market position: ~60-70% of global proton therapy systems market (vs. Varian, Hitachi, Mevion Medical Systems). Strengths: proton therapy monopoly-like position, turnkey solutions, expanding into emerging markets (Spain contract for 10 systems, India partnerships). Weaknesses: proton therapy remains niche (~5% of radiation therapy market), exposure to capex cycles for large centres, lower software/services revenue mix vs. Elekta/Varian.

RaySearch Laboratories. Swedish software-focused competitor. Revenue: ~SEK 375 million (Q4 2025, ~USD 35-40 million annually). Root cause of failure: (1) prolonged R&D cycle (FDA clearance 2017, but slow clinical adoption), (2) execution delays in installing MRIdian systems due to construction complexity and COVID disruptions, (3) reimbursement risk (CMS delayed RO model, which would have reduced patient visit frequency), (4) balance sheet exhaustion, (5) competition from Elekta Unity, which achieved faster clinical adoption and superior technical specifications. Ceased operations October 2023. Total installed base: ~50-60 MRIdian systems globally. This is a canonical example of a well-funded entrant ($400+ million capital raised) failing to convert technology leadership into commercial success due to execution and market timing risk.

Competitive Intensity: ORANGE. The market is highly concentrated (duopoly in hardware, oligopoly in software) with stable competitive shares, but growth rates are moderate (6-8% CAGR) and new entrants face formidable barriers. Pricing power remains high for incumbents due to switching costs and regulatory barriers. However, Siemens Healthineers' post-Varian scale advantage is growing, and Chinese manufacturers are beginning to exert price pressure in Asia-Pacific. ViewRay's bankruptcy signals execution risk is material for mid-tier competitors.

4. Barriers to Entry & Exit

Barriers to entry in radiation therapy equipment are among the highest in medical devices.

Capital Requirements. Developing a clinically competitive linac system requires $500 million to $1+ billion in R&D and manufacturing capital over 8-10 years. Elekta, Varian, and Siemens each spend $300-500 million annually on R&D across radiation oncology portfolios. New entrants must fund this for a decade before achieving meaningful revenue. Manufacturing facilities, quality systems, and inventory require $200-400 million in CapEx. Service networks (spare parts, field engineers, support) require $100-200 million to establish globally. Total entry cost: $1.5-2+ billion over 15 years. This is prohibitive for most private companies; only large healthcare conglomerates (Siemens, Philips, GE) can absorb this risk.

Regulatory Barriers. Linear accelerators are Class III medical devices in the US (highest regulatory risk tier). FDA approval pathway: Premarket Approval , requiring clinical trials (2-3 years), manufacturing audits, and ongoing post-market surveillance. CE marking in Europe requires notified body review and ongoing compliance. Clinical validation requires peer-reviewed publications and multi-centre trials, adding 3-5 years to market entry. International markets have fragmented approval timelines (China NMPA, Japan PMDA, Australia TGA, etc.). Regulatory compliance cost: $100-200 million for a new linac platform.

Scale Economies in R&D. Incumbent players leverage R&D across multiple product lines (linacs, proton systems, imaging, software) and geographic markets. A single R&D project can be amortised across 3,000-5,000 equipment sales globally. New entrants must hit the same R&D cost with zero installed base, making unit economics uncompetitive. The Varian acquisition by Siemens Healthineers has amplified this: Siemens can now cross-fund innovation across imaging (CT, MRI), oncology informatics, and radiotherapy, while smaller players cannot.

Clinical Relationships & Switching Costs. Hospitals and cancer centres prefer linac systems from vendors with strong clinical track records, published clinical outcomes, and established relationships with key opinion leaders (radiation oncologists, medical physicists). Varian TrueBeam has 10,000+ installations globally and 20+ years of clinical literature. Switching from TrueBeam to an alternative linac requires retraining staff, validating new dosimetry algorithms, and rebuilding clinical protocols. This typically takes 1-2 years and involves $500,000-1,000,000 in staff time. As a result, linac switching rate is <2% annually, providing entrenched installed-base economics.

Software Ecosystem Lock-in. Treatment planning system switching costs are even higher than linac switching. Hospitals invest 2-3 years in validating TPS software for their clinical workflows, building institution-specific protocols, and training physicists. Migrating from Eclipse to RayStation, for example, requires months of validation work and clinical protocol rebuilding. Software vendors benefit from "upgrade stickiness": customers renew annual licences rather than switch. This creates predictable recurring revenue with >95% retention rates.

Technology Complexity & IP. Linear accelerator design involves proprietary RF (radiofrequency) systems, magnet technologies, dosimetry algorithms, and control systems. Multiple patents protect designs (Varian, Elekta, IBA hold 2,000+ patents in radiation therapy). MRI-linac integration is especially complex: combining a 1.5T or 3T superconducting magnet with a linear accelerator requires solving magnetic field interactions, dose calculation algorithms in non-uniform fields, and real-time motion tracking. Elekta Unity and ViewRay MRIdian each took 8-10 years of R&D to achieve FDA clearance. This complexity and IP moat protects incumbents from rapid competitive copying.

Service Network Requirements. Hospitals expect 24/7 service support, field engineers within 4-8 hours, spare parts in stock, and annual preventative maintenance. Building a global service network requires 500-1,000 field engineers, training programs, spare parts depots, and logistics infrastructure. This represents $500 million-1 billion in capital and 10-15 years to establish. Elekta and Varian have 70-year service networks; new entrants cannot replicate this quickly.

Chinese Competition Risk. The one structural exit visible is ViewRay (bankruptcy 2023), but Chinese manufacturers are entering at the lower end. Shanghai United Imaging Medical (United Imaging Healthcare) has captured 15% linac market share in China (up from <5% in 2017) and is expanding internationally. Its uRT linac system is not yet FDA-approved in the US, but is gaining traction in developing markets. Barriers to entry are lower in emerging markets due to: (1) looser regulatory standards, (2) lower price sensitivity, (3) less incumbent market share. Chinese manufacturers may commoditise linacs in Asia-Pacific over the next 5-10 years, pressuring Elekta's APAC margins.

Barriers to Entry Rating: GREEN. Regulatory, capital, R&D, and switching-cost barriers remain extraordinarily high. New linac manufacturers have not emerged in 20 years. Only very large healthcare conglomerates (GE, Philips, Chinese state enterprises) can attempt entry. However, Chinese competition in emerging markets and ViewRay's bankruptcy signal that execution risk and market timing can overcome barriers if a challenger has insufficient capital discipline.

6. Elekta's Position & Model

Elekta competes as the #2 global player in radiation therapy equipment and #1 in MRI-guided radiotherapy systems, with a strong but regional (EMEA-focused) footprint.

Market Share. Elekta holds ~42% of the global linac market by units, versus Varian's 37%, with 75% duopoly share. In MRI-linac systems, Elekta Unity commands ~50% market share with ViewRay's bankruptcy clearing the field; no other commercial MRI-linac competitors currently exist at scale. In gamma knife radiosurgery, Elekta's Leksell Gamma Knife holds #2-3 position globally.

Geographic Footprint. Elekta is particularly strong in EMEA (Europe, Middle East, Africa) where it has ~45-50% linac market share vs. Varian's ~30-35%. In Asia-Pacific (excluding Japan), Elekta holds ~35-40% share, but Chinese manufacturers are gaining (Shanghai United Imaging ~15% China share as of 2024). In North America, Varian TrueBeam dominates with ~55-60% market share; Elekta Versa HD holds ~25-30% share, with the remainder fragmented. This geographic imbalance means Elekta has structural exposure to EMEA healthcare budget cycles (NHS spending, German hospital capex, French regional hospital networks) while missing the higher-margin North American market.

Revenue Model Mix. FY2024/25: Total revenue SEK 5.1 billion. Breakdown by mix: (1) Equipment (linacs, Gamma Knife, imaging systems): ~58-60% of revenue, 40-45% gross margin, (2) Software & Services (MOSAIQ, Monaco, OIS, TPS, SaaS subscriptions): ~26% of revenue, 65-75% gross margin, (3) Service Contracts & Maintenance: ~16% of revenue, 55-65% gross margin. Recurring revenue (software + services) represents 42% of net sales, growing at 15%+ annually. This is a significant structural advantage: as revenue grows, the mix shifts toward higher-margin recurring revenue, providing operating leverage.

Customer Concentration. Elekta's customer base spans hospitals (cancer centres, regional health systems), NHS trusts , French/German national health services, and private cancer networks. No single customer exceeds 2-3% of revenue. Concentration risk is low, but the customer base is heavily public/government-funded (NHS, national health systems represent ~30-40% of EMEA revenue), exposing Elekta to political budget cycles.

Structural Competitive Advantages:

1. Unity MRI-linac Leadership. Elekta Unity is the clinical gold standard for MRI-guided radiotherapy, with FDA approval for motion management (Feb 2023) and faster treatment delivery than competitors. The MRI-linac market is growing at 18.1% CAGR, and Unity holds ~50% market share. This is Elekta's highest-growth product line and highest-margin segment. ViewRay's bankruptcy removed the primary competitor, giving Elekta duopoly-like dynamics in MRI-linac.

  1. MOSAIQ Installed Base. MOSAIQ oncology information system is deployed in ~1,200+ hospitals globally, primarily in EMEA and ANZ (Australia/New Zealand). This creates ecosystem lock-in: hospitals using MOSAIQ are unlikely to switch to ARIA (Varian) or Epic/Cerner because workflow integration and staff training are embedded. MOSAIQ revenue is sticky with high renewal rates (>95% annually) and grows 12-15% annually.

  2. Versa HD Price Advantage. Elekta's Versa HD linac sells at 20-30% lower price point than Varian TrueBeam while offering clinical parity (160-leaf MLC, flattening-filter-free beams, similar dose rate). In price-sensitive markets (EMEA, Asia-Pacific), this gives Elekta competitive advantage in hospital procurement decisions where capital budgets are constrained.

4. Leksell Gamma Knife. Elekta's Gamma Knife radiosurgery systems hold a strong niche position in brain/neurosurgery applications. Revenue is stable, margins are good, and clinical outcomes are well-published. Recent innovation (Leksell Vantage head frame), FDA approval for epilepsy treatment (July 2025) suggest product line is being refreshed.

5. Software Momentum. Double-digit growth in software revenue (15%+ annually) and emerging SaaS adoption (30% of new contracts in 2024, up from 15% in 2022) are providing margin expansion and recurring revenue tailwinds. This is a structural advantage for Elekta because software margins (65-75%) are 1.5-2x hardware margins (40-45%).

Structural Disadvantages:

  1. Scale Gap vs. Siemens/Varian. Elekta's SEK 5.1 billion revenue is 10x smaller than Siemens Healthineers' healthcare revenue and 8-10x smaller than Varian standalone (pre-acquisition). This scale gap means Elekta has lower R&D budgets ($300-400 million annually vs. Siemens' $500+ million), smaller service networks, and weaker bargaining power with component suppliers.

  2. North American Market Underweight. Varian TrueBeam's dominance in North America (55-60% market share) means Elekta has limited footprint in the highest-margin, most stable market. North America represents 40-45% of global radiation therapy market value. Elekta's ~25-30% share in North America leaves material market opportunity uncaptured.

3. EMEA Budget Cycle Exposure. Elekta's 45-50% linac market share in EMEA makes the company structurally exposed to European healthcare spending cycles. UK NHS budgets are constrained post-COVID; German hospital capex is cyclical; French regional health networks are budget-squeezed. Downturns in EMEA translate directly to Elekta revenue pressure, whereas Siemens/Varian have diversified geographic revenue.

  1. Chinese Competition Emerging. Shanghai United Imaging and other Chinese manufacturers are gaining linac market share in China and Southeast Asia. As these competitors expand internationally (FDA approvals are coming), they will pressure pricing in Elekta's higher-margin APAC region.

  2. Integration/Bundling Disadvantage vs. Siemens. Siemens can bundle hardware (TrueBeam) + software (Eclipse, ARIA) + imaging (CT, MRI, Cios) + enterprise informatics into integrated "solutions." Elekta must sell point solutions (linac + separate TPS/OIS choices). This bundling advantage drives customer stickiness and revenue expansion for Siemens.

Competitive Position Rating: YELLOW. Elekta has durable #2 market share, excellent MRI-linac leadership, and improving software margins, but faces scale disadvantage vs. Siemens Healthineers and geographic exposure to EMEA budget cycles. The competitive position is defensible but not expanding.

7. Business Model Mechanics

Elekta's business model is transitioning from a equipment-centric model (linac sales, capex-driven) to a balanced equipment + recurring revenue model, with software/services now driving margin expansion and cash flow predictability.

Revenue Drivers (3-Year Horizon):

1. New Equipment Installations. Linacs and Gamma Knife system sales are driven by hospital capex cycles (typically 4-6 year replacement cycles). ~2,000-2,500 linac units are sold globally annually; Elekta ships ~400-500 units (20% share). Each unit generates SEK 10-12 million revenue, plus installation and training. This segment is cyclical and sensitive to hospital budget availability but provides high absolute margins (40-45% gross margin).

  1. Software Licences & Subscriptions. MOSAIQ, Monaco, and RayCare systems generate recurring annual licence fees. Hospitals pay SEK 200,000-500,000 annually per system depending on number of treatment planning seats and users. SaaS adoption (30% of new contracts in 2024) provides subscription revenue (SEK 100,000-300,000 annually) with higher margins (65-75%) and lower customer acquisition cost. This segment is growing 15%+ annually.

3. Service Contracts. Multi-year service agreements (SLAs) represent ~38% of recurring revenue. Hospitals pay 10-15% of equipment purchase price annually for preventative maintenance, spare parts, and 24/7 support. Service revenue is highly predictable, sticky (>95% renewal rates), and scales with installed base. Service contracts are lower-margin than software (55-65%) but provide cash conversion and customer relationships.

4. Upgrade Cycles. Hardware upgrades (e.g., software modules added to existing linacs, system re-commissioning) and equipment refreshes (moving from Versa to Versa HD, or linac to MRI-linac) are driven by clinical efficacy improvements and technology adoption. These generate SEK 2-4 million per transaction and occur every 5-7 years per installed system.

Cost Structure:

  1. R&D Intensity. Elekta spends ~10-12% of revenue on R&D (SEK 500-600 million annually). This funds new linac platforms (Evo), MRI-linac innovation (Unity next-generation systems), software AI/ML integration, and regulatory compliance. R&D spending is relatively fixed and does not scale linearly with revenue, providing operating leverage.

  2. Manufacturing & COGS. Equipment COGS runs 55-60% of equipment revenue, reflecting component costs (magnets, RF systems, detectors), labour, quality assurance, and logistics. Software COGS is low (5-10%), reflecting licence costs and cloud infrastructure. Blended gross margin across equipment + software is 40-45%, improving as software mix increases.

  3. Sales & Service Network. Elekta maintains a global sales force (~200-250 people) and field service network (~500-700 engineers). This is a fixed-cost layer that represents 18-20% of revenue. As revenue grows and software subscriptions expand, this layer does not scale proportionally, providing margin expansion.

  4. SG&A. Corporate overhead (finance, HR, legal, compliance) represents 8-10% of revenue, relatively fixed.

Capital Intensity & Working Capital:

Equipment sales have extended order-to-revenue cycles (4-6 months from order to installation). Hospitals often order 12-18 months in advance, providing revenue visibility. Inventory turns are slow (linacs are made-to-order, with 8-12 week build times). Accounts receivable average 60-90 days (hospitals are slow payers). This creates working capital drag of ~15-20% of incremental revenue.

However, service contracts and software subscriptions are upfront cash payments (annual renewal), providing cash flow uplift. Elekta's operating cash flow improved by SEK 241 million in FY2024/25 to SEK 1,056 million, suggesting improving cash conversion as software mix increases.

Operating Leverage: As revenue grows 1-3% and software mix increases 2-3 percentage points annually, gross margin expands 50-100 basis points per year (primarily from software mix shift). With fixed R&D and sales costs, incremental EBIT margin expansion is 100-150 basis points per revenue growth percentage point. This is Elekta's highest-conviction operating dynamic: margin expansion through software mix.

Model Sensitivity:

The model breaks if: (1) EMEA healthcare budgets contract (NHS, German hospitals capex freezes), (2) software growth stalls (new TPS/OIS adoption slows), (3) Chinese competition pressures Versa HD pricing by >15-20%, or (4) hospital service contract renewal rates fall below 90%.

Business Model Rating: GREEN. The shift toward software/recurring revenue is a positive structural transformation. However, hardware cyclicality and EMEA exposure create quarterly volatility.

8. Key Success Factors

Winners in radiation therapy equipment compete on five critical dimensions that separate market leaders from also-rans.

Scale & R&D Spend. Top-tier players (Siemens/Varian, Elekta, IBA) spend $300-500 million annually on R&D to maintain clinical leadership and regulatory compliance. This creates a minimum revenue threshold of $2-3 billion to sustain competitive R&D. ViewRay ($79 million revenue) could not sustain its R&D burn; RaySearch ($35 million revenue) survives only because software is lower-capex. New entrants must commit to 10-15 years of R&D burn ($3-5 billion cumulative) before reaching profitability. Elekta's scale is barely sufficient; it risks falling behind Siemens/Varian on long-term innovation if R&D intensity declines.

Service Network Density. Hospitals demand 24/7 field service within 4-8 hours. Service engineers require 2-3 years of training to maintain clinical systems safely. Global service networks cost $500 million-1 billion to build over 15 years. Elekta's service network in EMEA is world-class (70+ years of installation history); in North America, it is weaker. Service network = customer lock-in; without it, equipment is not competitive.

Clinical Relationships & Key Opinion Leader Alignment. Hospital procurement decisions are heavily influenced by radiation oncologists and medical physicists. These are high-trust, low-volume decisions made by specialists who prefer vendors with published clinical outcomes, training programs, and peer support networks (e.g., Leksell Gamma Knife Society). Elekta invests heavily in KOL relationships and clinical conferences. This is difficult to displace: once a medical physics team has 5-10 years of experience with a linac, switching to a competitor requires retraining and clinical validation.

Software/Recurring Revenue Mix. The highest-margin, most defensible revenue comes from software and multi-year service contracts. Elekta's mix (42% recurring revenue, growing 15% annually) is superior to legacy competitors (Varian ARIA has lower adoption; IBA has minimal software). Companies with <25% recurring revenue face margin compression and lower valuation multiples. Elekta's software mix is a key competitive advantage.

R&D Investment as % Revenue. Leaders spend 8-12% of revenue on R&D; laggards spend <5%. This determines long-term innovation velocity. Elekta's 10-12% R&D intensity is appropriate for a $500 million revenue company, but lower in absolute dollars than Siemens/Varian. Over 15-20 years, this R&D gap will compound, putting Elekta at risk of technological obsolescence.

Execution of MRI-Guided Radiotherapy Transition. The MRI-linac market is growing at 18.1% CAGR and represents the highest-growth, highest-margin segment. Elekta Unity is the market leader (~50% share), but execution risk is material: (1) complex installations (building MRI-shielded bunkers), (2) clinical adoption curves (only 200-300 MRI-linac installations globally, vs. 20,000+ linacs), (3) reimbursement (CMS is still defining coverage for MRI-linac). Elekta's ability to scale Unity installations from 10-15 units/year to 40-50 units/year will determine whether MRI-linac becomes 15-20% of revenue by 2030 or remains a 5% niche.

Hospital Procurement Cycles & Political Sensitivity. Hospitals typically budget 4-6 years for linac replacement. Procurement is a Group Purchase Organisation driven in the US, centralised in the UK/NHS, and regional in Europe. Political factors (NHS austerity, German election cycles affecting healthcare budgets, French hospital modernisation programs) create lumpy revenue patterns. Companies that diversify customer concentration and geographies weather cycles better.

Barriers to Switching & Lock-in Economics. Switching costs from Versa HD to TrueBeam (or vice versa) are high (~$1-2 million in retraining, protocol validation, staff time). Switching costs from MOSAIQ to ARIA are even higher (~$2-3 million, 12-18 months). Building a larger installed base creates compound lock-in: more hospitals using your system = more KOLs trained on your platform = higher switching costs for remaining prospects. Elekta's MOSAIQ base (~1,200+ hospitals) is a strategic asset that improves competitive position over time.

Rating Elekta's KSF Execution: YELLOW. Elekta excels at KOL relationships, service networks (in EMEA), software mix, and installed-base lock-in. It is weaker on absolute R&D scale, North American penetration, and hospital procurement cycle management. The MRI-linac opportunity is Elekta's highest-leverage KSF; success here would be transformational.

9. Industry Evolution

The radiation therapy sector is at an inflection point, with three technology/market shifts reshaping competitive dynamics over the next 5-10 years.

MRI-Linac Adoption Acceleration. Real-time MRI guidance during treatment delivery is the most significant clinical innovation since intensity-modulated radiotherapy adoption in the 2000s. MRI-linac systems allow: (1) soft-tissue target visualisation (overcoming conventional linac limitation of bone-only imaging), (2) adaptive radiotherapy (plan re-optimisation session-by-session), (3) reduced treatment margins and toxicity. The MRI-linac market is growing at 18.1% CAGR (vs. 6.8% for overall radiation therapy market). Clinical adoption is accelerating: MRI-linac installations have grown from 20 units globally (2015) to 200-300 units (2025). By 2030, MRI-linac is projected to be 15-20% of radiation therapy market value, vs. 5-8% today. This drives Elekta Unity growth and creates margin expansion opportunity (MRI-linac gross margins are 45-50% vs. 40-45% for standard linacs, due to higher selling price and engineering complexity).

Proton Therapy Niche Growth. Proton therapy is growing 12-15% CAGR but remains <5% of radiation therapy market value due to high capex ($150-250 million per centre) and lower unit volume. However, proton adoption in emerging markets (China, India, Brazil) is accelerating due to health system investments and IBA expansion. Proton does not directly cannibalize linac revenue (they are complementary modalities for different tumour types), but it does shift hospital oncology capex allocation. Elekta has no proton business; growth in proton therapy is a competitive disadvantage because hospital capex for proton reduces budget available for linac upgrades.

AI-Driven Treatment Planning. AI/ML integration in treatment planning systems is reshaping software economics. RaySearch released first machine learning applications in RayStation (2024), automating contouring, dose prediction, and plan optimisation. Elekta is integrating AI into Monaco treatment planning. This drives: (1) faster plan creation (reducing clinical time), (2) improved plan quality (optimisation algorithms improve with more training data), (3) software upgrade cycles (hospitals buy new versions for AI features). Software vendors with large installed bases (Eclipse, ARIA, RayStation) have advantages in AI training data. Elekta's smaller TPS installed base (~20-25% of market) is a competitive disadvantage in the AI era.

Consolidation Trend: Integrated Solutions. The Siemens/Varian merger is establishing a precedent: bundled hardware + software + imaging + enterprise informatics create customer stickiness and revenue expansion. Varian's ARIA OIS is being integrated with Eclipse TPS and TrueBeam linacs into a "TrueBeam ecosystem." Elekta is attempting the same with Versa HD + Monaco + MOSAIQ, but the ecosystem is less integrated. Hospitals increasingly demand turnkey solutions rather than point products, favouring vertically integrated players.

Disintermediation Risk. There is emerging risk that treatment planning software becomes commoditised and disintermediated through: (1) cloud-based planning as-a-service (bypassing OEM software), (2) open-source TPS algorithms (e.g., MatRad, TOPAS proton planning), (3) hospital IT departments building in-house planning tools. This is still nascent (no major hospital has replaced Eclipse with in-house tools), but represents a long-term threat to software vendors' pricing power.

New Entrant Risk: Chinese Manufacturers. Shanghai United Imaging (15% China linac share by 2024) is beginning international expansion. The uRT linac system is FDA-approved for clinical trials in the US (not yet full FDA approval). Chinese manufacturers have lower cost structures and can undercut Elekta Versa HD pricing by 30-40% in emerging markets. This is a 5-10 year structural threat to Elekta's APAC margins.

Pricing Power Trends. Incumbent linac vendors have historically raised prices 2-3% annually (in line with inflation), maintaining gross margins. Chinese competition and hospital budgeting pressure may compress pricing to 0-1% growth by 2028-2030. Software vendors (RaySearch, Eclipse) have maintained pricing power (5-8% annual increases) due to unique capabilities and switching costs.

Margin Trends. Equipment gross margins are compressing (linac gross margins declining 50-100 bps annually due to competition). Software gross margins are expanding (SaaS adoption, AI-driven upgrade cycles). Blended gross margin for integrated players (Siemens/Varian, Elekta) will expand if software mix increases faster than hardware. This is the critical inflection: Elekta wins if software grows 15%+ CAGR and hardware 0-2% CAGR; Elekta loses if hardware declines 5%+ CAGR due to competition.

Industry Evolution Rating: YELLOW. MRI-linac growth and AI-driven software create tailwinds for well-positioned players (Elekta in MRI-linac, RaySearch in AI), but Chinese competition and consolidation pressure create headwinds. The industry is bifurcating: integrated giants (Siemens/Varian) pulling ahead via scale and bundling, mid-tier specialists (Elekta) defending niche strengths (MRI-linac, MOSAIQ), and software-only players (RaySearch) thriving on high margins but constrained unit growth.

10. Sceptical View

For each major bull-case assertion about Elekta, the strongest bear-case counterargument:

Bull Case #1: "Elekta has 42% linac market share (duopoly position with Varian)."

Bear Case: Elekta's 42% share is global, but not defensible in the largest market (North America, 40% of global revenue), where Varian TrueBeam dominates at 55-60% share. In North America, Elekta is a distant #2, not a duopoly player. Siemens Healthineers post-Varian integration is consolidating North American linac share to 65-70%, pushing Elekta toward 20-25% in the core market. Over 10 years, this margin (Varian's 55%+ vs. Elekta's 25%) will compound into an insurmountable scale gap. By 2030-2035, Elekta may be a strong #2 regionally (EMEA, APAC) but a diminishing player in total global market value.

Bull Case #2: "Unity MRI-linac is market leader with 50% share, growing at 18.1% CAGR."

Bear Case: ViewRay's bankruptcy was partly due to MRI-linac market being smaller and slower to adopt than projected. Installations are running 50-60 units globally annually, vs. 2,000-2,500 linacs annually. Even at 18.1% CAGR, MRI-linac will only be 15-20% of radiation therapy market by 2030 ($2-3 billion out of $15-17 billion total). This is a high-growth niche, not a market reshaping opportunity. Furthermore, real-time MRI guidance has clinical benefits, but not all hospital budgets prioritise soft-tissue targeting over cost efficiency. In cost-constrained markets (NHS, EMEA public systems), hospitals will continue buying standard linacs for 5-10 more years. Elekta's MRI-linac upside may be capped at $500-750 million revenue by 2030, not the $1+ billion structural opportunity proponents expect.

Bull Case #3: "Software revenue is growing 15% annually and will drive margin expansion."

Bear Case: Software growth depends on: (1) new hospital builds and linac upgrades (cyclical), (2) SaaS adoption acceleration (uncertain), and (3) continued pricing power (threatened by cloud commoditisation and EHR vendor bundling). Epic and Cerner are building competing OIS modules and will offer them at ~50% discount to MOSAIQ for hospital systems already on Epic/Cerner infrastructure. Elekta's MOSAIQ is limited to 0.79% healthcare market share globally; most hospitals use EHR-bundled OIS. Software growth may decelerate from 15% to 8-10% by 2027 as competition intensifies. Additionally, software margins could compress as hospitals shift to SaaS (lower upfront cost = lower gross margin %). Elekta's software profit growth may be 5-7% CAGR, not 15%.

Bull Case #4: "Elekta's gross margin has reached 40.3%, the highest in 5 years, with more expansion ahead."

Bear Case: Margin expansion is driven by product mix (higher-margin specialty products) and pricing increases. Both are at risk: (1) Chinese linac competition will force Versa HD pricing down 10-15% by 2027, compressing equipment gross margins to 35-38%, (2) software mix growth will plateau at 28-30% of revenue (from current 26%), limiting margin tailwinds, (3) inflation in component costs (superconducting magnets, RF components) may offset pricing increases. Elekta's gross margin ceiling is likely 41-42%; expansion beyond that is unlikely. If hardware is pressured to 35-38% margin and software grows only modestly, blended gross margin could compress to 38-39% by 2030. This would leave no room for margin expansion through operating leverage.

Bull Case #5: "MOSAIQ installed base of 1,200+ hospitals creates ecosystem lock-in and defensible margins."

Bear Case: MOSAIQ is strong in EMEA and ANZ, but weak in North America (most US hospitals are on Epic, Cerner, or Varian ARIA). In a Siemens Healthineers-dominated world (TrueBeam + ARIA integration), MOSAIQ is a niche player. Furthermore, hospital OIS switching is accelerating as health systems consolidate EHR vendors. A hospital acquiring a new facility or merging with another system will rationalize to a single OIS platform; MOSAIQ is rarely the winner in US-based mergers. Installed base growth may slow to 2-3% CAGR (from current 4-5%), limiting software revenue expansion. By 2030, MOSAIQ might have 1,400-1,500 hospitals (vs. 1,200 today), but this is not a growth story.

Bull Case #6: "Elekta's book-to-bill ratio of 1.09 shows strong demand."

Bear Case: Book-to-bill >1.0 indicates orders exceeding current quarter shipments, but this can be misleading if orders are backlog volatility rather than structural demand. COVID-related installation delays created artificial order backlogs in 2021-2023. As installations normalise, book-to-bill may compress to 0.95-1.00, signalling demand is only inline with capacity (not accelerating). Additionally, high book-to-bill constrains free cash flow because Elekta must deliver orders booked in prior quarters, absorbing working capital drag.

Bull Case #7: "Siemens Healthineers' acquisition of Varian is still in integration phase; Elekta has runway to gain market share."

Bear Case: Varian integration is largely complete (2021-2025, with synergies materialising). By 2026-2027, Siemens will have rationalised its medtech portfolio, consolidated sales forces, and achieved most EUR 300 million synergies. Siemens' integrated linac + software + imaging ecosystem will be stronger than any Elekta point-solution strategy. Elekta's window to gain market share is closing (2024-2026), and if the company fails to execute in this window, its relative position will deteriorate through 2030-2035.

Bull Case #8: "Elekta's lower Versa HD price point vs. TrueBeam gives competitive advantage in price-sensitive markets."

Bear Case: Versa HD pricing advantage is 20-30% lower than TrueBeam on a capital basis, but total cost of ownership includes software, service, training, and installation. When hospitals model TCO over 5-10 years, Versa HD's software disadvantage (weaker ecosystem, smaller TPS installed base, less integration with hospital IT) often offsets the capital price advantage. Furthermore, as Chinese manufacturers (Shanghai United Imaging) enter with 40-50% price discounts, Versa HD's 20-30% advantage becomes a commodity position. Pricing advantage erodes as competition intensifies.

Structural Bear Case: Siemens Healthineers Scale Advantage.

The fundamental structural risk is that Siemens' post-Varian position is too dominant for Elekta to compete with over a 10-15 year horizon. Siemens can: (1) cross-subsidise linac development with imaging (CT, MRI, X-ray) R&D budget, (2) bundle hardware + software + imaging into integrated hospital solutions, (3) leverage Siemens' enterprise IT relationships for ARIA OIS adoption, (4) invest more aggressively in AI/ML (larger training datasets from larger installed base). Elekta's ~10x smaller revenue scale means lower R&D budget, weaker software integration, and reduced ability to cross-subsidise innovation. Over 15-20 years, this gap compounds. By 2035-2040, Siemens Healthineers may own 55-60% of radiation therapy market, with Elekta compressed to 20-25%, and smaller regional players filling the remainder.

Chinese Competition Structural Risk.

Shanghai United Imaging and other Chinese manufacturers are following a playbook that has worked in diagnostics (CT, ultrasound): build a competitive product, price 30-40% below incumbents, achieve market penetration in Asia-Pacific, then expand to emerging markets globally. Chinese linac manufacturers have lower cost structures (labour arbitrage, component sourcing from China), less legacy service network overhead, and more aggressive pricing. If Shanghai United Imaging achieves FDA approval and North American market entry by 2028-2030, it will commoditise the linac market in North America and emerging markets. This would compress Elekta's margins by 10-15% across the portfolio.

Overall Structural Risks Rating: ORANGE-RED. Elekta's competitive position is defensible for 5-7 years but faces structural headwinds beyond that. Siemens Healthineers' scale advantage and Chinese competition are materialising risks, not theoretical scenarios.
