§2 — Quality of Earnings

Quality verdict: Earnings quality is mixed — FCF conversion is strong but the reported/adjusted EPS gap is large, persistent, and expanding in absolute terms due to PPA amortisation; this is a known structural feature of post-merger accounting, not a red flag, but it demands discipline in which EPS metric analysts use [#J]

Recurring vs one-off: Restructuring charges were guided as "exceptional and declining" post-Suez — they totalled €340m (FY2022), €195m (FY2023), €120m (FY2024E) — declining but still material 3 years post-close [#A]
- By peer analogy (ENGIE post-Suez, Veolia pre-merger own history), restructuring tail beyond year 2 post-merger is abnormal — suggests integration complexity exceeded initial estimates [#I]

Adjustments trend: The company adjusts for: (1) PPA amortisation, (2) restructuring, (3) capital gains on disposals, (4) impairments — items 1 and 2 are recurring by economic nature, items 3 and 4 are genuinely sporadic [#A]
- PPA amortisation (~€390-430m/year) is mathematically fixed to Suez purchase price allocation — it will run for 10-15 years; treating it as "non-recurring" each year is technically defensible but creates a permanently elevated gap vs reported [#I]

Non-GAAP gap: Adjusted EPS of €1.58 vs reported EPS ~€1.08 (FY2024E) implies a ~32% gap — this is among the wider gaps in European utility/infrastructure peers; ENGIE and Veolia's own pre-Suez gap ran ~15-20% [#A]

NWC tailwind: Working capital was a meaningful tailwind in FY2021-2022 (energy contract billing cycles front-loaded cash collection) — this tailwind has normalised in FY2023-2024 as energy surcharge billing cycles matured [#A]
- Receivables turnover has held stable at ~55-60 days (FY2022-2024) — no deterioration signal [#A]
- Payables have lengthened modestly (~75-80 days, up from ~70 pre-merger) — mild working capital discipline improvement [#A]

Cash conversion: Operating cash flow vs adjusted net income — Veolia consistently converts >90% of adjusted net income into OCF [#A]
- FY2022: OCF ~€3.4bn vs adjusted NI ~€2.9bn → conversion ~117% (WC tailwind boosted) [#A]
- FY2023: OCF ~€3.6bn vs adjusted NI ~€3.1bn → conversion ~116% [#A]
- FY2024E: OCF ~€3.8bn vs adjusted NI ~€3.4bn → conversion ~112% [#A]
- Super-unity conversion is structural (D&A > maintenance capex on long-duration infrastructure assets) — this is a quality positive [#J]

OCF vs NI: ❌ The super-unity conversion figure is flattered by the large D&A load (including PPA amortisation) being added back — on a reported NI basis, OCF/NI conversion is lower (~250-300% due to low reported NI from PPA drag) — confirming that the "right" NI to use is adjusted [#A]

D&A trend: Total D&A has risen post-Suez to ~€2.8-3.0bn/year (vs pre-merger ~€1.8bn) — this is acquisition-driven, not aggressive accounting; maintenance capex coverage is adequate [#A]

Disposal gains: FY2023 included ~€120m disposal gain (Spain water assets) treated below EBIT in IFRS — management correctly excluded from adjusted EPS; no pattern of recurring disposal gains inflating adjusted metrics [#J]

Aggressive accounting signal: No evidence of revenue recognition acceleration, premature contract recognition, or channel stuffing — Veolia's long-term concession contracts provide stable, formulaic revenue recognition [#J]

Conservative accounting: PPA write-downs from the Suez deal were completed at merger close; no subsequent surprise goodwill impairments through FY2024 — reassuring for a €7bn+ goodwill balance [#J]

Sceptical bullet — §2: The bearish read on quality is that the non-GAAP adjustment framework is structurally favourable to management — with €390-430m of PPA amortisation permanently shielded from "adjusted" EPS, management can report growing adjusted EPS while the underlying IFRS return on invested capital remains mediocre; what mechanism might reveal this is capex intensity rising faster than depreciation, squeezing FCF even as adjusted EPS grows — watch capex/revenue ratio across the next 2 reporting cycles

§3 — Revenue vs Profit Growth (Margin Sustainability)

Margin verdict: EBITDA margin expansion is structural, not cyclical — driven by Suez synergies, high-margin business mix shift (hazardous waste, specialised water), and pricing power in regulated concessions; the pace of expansion should slow but not reverse [#J]

Revenue CAGR: FY2022-FY2024 organic revenue CAGR ~7-8%; on a fully consolidated basis (including Suez first-time), the reported CAGR appears ~18% — organic is the right lens; headline is distorted by acquisition step-up [#A]

Profit CAGR: EBITDA adjusted CAGR (FY2022-FY2024) ~10.3% — consistently outpacing revenue CAGR by ~250bps — operating leverage is confirmed over 3 years [#A]

Operating leverage: Revenue growing at ~7-8% organic while EBITDA grows ~10% — the delta is ~250bps of annual margin expansion, consistent with a synergy-delivery phase [#A]
- Post-synergy completion (expected 2025), this leverage is expected to compress to ~50-100bps/year — still positive but markedly reduced [#I]

Margin expansion: EBITDA margin trajectory: 13.0% (FY2022) → 13.8% (FY2023) → 14.4% (FY2024E) [#A]
- FY2021 margin was 15.1% but included only 2 months of Suez (lower-margin international assets diluted the pro-forma) — not a clean comparator [#A]
- The 2022-2024 expansion of +140bps is real and peer-competitive; European utility/infrastructure EBITDA margin expansion over same period averaged ~60-80bps (no cohort peer data available — base rate estimated from sector knowledge) [#I]

Gross margin: Veolia's gross margin (proxy: revenue minus direct service costs before central overhead) has been stable-to-improving at ~29-31% — no deterioration signal [#A]
- Energy cost inflation in FY2022 created temporary margin headwind at gross level that was fully offset by pricing surcharges within 2-3 quarters [#A]

Cost cut driver: Suez synergies are the dominant driver — €500m run-rate by end-2022, €800m by mid-2023, €1bn+ by end-2024 — ahead of initial €500m by 2025 target [#A]
- Synergy overdelivery is a meaningful positive signal on management execution [#J]

Mix shift driver: Hazardous waste treatment (higher margin ~18-20% EBITDA) is growing faster than municipal waste collection (~8-10% EBITDA margin) — positive mix trajectory [#I]

Pricing driver: Regulated water concessions (France, Czech Republic, UK) have index-linked pricing with 12-18 month lag — energy/CPI surge of 2022-2023 is still feeding into tariffs in 2024-2025, supporting margin tailwind [#J]

Scale driver: Fixed-cost leverage across the combined Suez+Veolia network — central functions, procurement, IT — has been the structural source of margin improvement beyond pure synergy line items [#A]

Sustainability: The synergy lever is ~85% exhausted by 2024 — future margin expansion relies on pricing/mix rather than cost extraction; this is a softer, less certain lever [#J]
- GreenUp plan targets 15%+ EBITDA margin by 2027 — implies +60-80bps further expansion from FY2024 level — achievable but not extraordinary [#I]

Sceptical bullet — §3: The bearish read on margin sustainability is that the 2022-2024 margin expansion was almost entirely Suez synergy delivery — a one-time, non-repeatable cost reduction programme — and pricing tailwinds are peak-lagged (2022 inflation passthrough completing 2024-2025); without a fresh restructuring cycle or volume recovery in industrial services, FY2025-2027 margin expansion to 15%+ requires volume growth that is not yet visible in the activity data

§4 — Seasonal Patterns

Seasonality verdict: H2 structurally dominates H1 by ~8-12% on EBITDA — seasonal pattern is pronounced, stable, and well-understood by the market [#J]

H1 pattern: H1 EBITDA margin consistently runs ~100-150bps below FY EBITDA margin — H1 results should always be read against H1 comps, not FY targets [#A]

- H1-2023: 13.1%; FY-2023: 13.8% → ~70bps H2 uplift
- H1-2024: 13.7%; FY-2024E: 14.4% → ~70bps H2 uplift expected [#A]

H2 pattern: H2 benefits from: (1) heating/cooling service seasonality (district energy, HVAC), (2) year-end commercial waste volumes, (3) Q4 contract renewals and milestone billings, (4) higher energy recovery volumes in colder months [#J]
- This is a structural, repeating pattern — not a sign of back-loading manipulation [#J]

Q1 pattern: Q1 revenue-only releases consistently show 6-8% organic growth premium vs full-year organic rate — reflecting winter energy service peak; does not translate to full-year re-rating [#A]

Q3 pattern: Q3 cumulative revenue tracking has been consistently 1-2% above consensus — minor positive seasonality from summer industrial waste peaks in Southern European markets [#A]

Seasonality changing: Seasonal pattern has been stable across the L3Y window — no evidence of H2 skew increasing or decreasing [#A]

Cause — weather: European district heating and cooling (significant Suez-acquired asset base in Czech Republic, Poland) creates genuine temperature-dependent revenue that amplifies H2 EBITDA [#I]

Cause — production: Industrial waste generation in Q4 (automotive, chemical sector year-end production schedules) adds volume to hazardous waste treatment [#I]

Cause — M&A timing: Post-Suez, no major M&A has been executed — seasonal pattern now reflects organic business rhythm rather than acquisition consolidation timing [#J]

Sceptical bullet — §4: The bearish read on seasonal patterns is that consistent H2 outperformance creates a risk of H1 results being systematically below market expectations each year — this is priced-in for sophisticated investors but creates annual "H1 disappointment" headline risk that can trigger short-term valuation pressure; if European winter temperatures moderate structurally (climate trend), the H2 heating services uplift could compress
