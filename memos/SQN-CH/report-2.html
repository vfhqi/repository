§3 — Change in Strategy / Structure

  • [#J] Verdict: Swissquote is executing a deliberate dual-track strategy — deepening Swiss affluent client penetration while building a scalable B2B eForex infrastructure business; both are coherent but the eForex B2B unit remains subscale relative to its potential.

  • [#I] eForex platform — B2B growth vector: Swissquote is a licensed FX prime broker offering white-label trading infrastructure to third parties (banks, brokers, asset managers).

  • eForex revenues grew from ~CHF 20m (2019) to ~CHF 70-80m range (2024 est.) — ~3-4x over five years, making it the fastest-growing segment ex-crypto.
  • Peer anchor: no direct comparator in neobroker cohort — this is a differentiated B2B revenue stream absent from Avanza, Nordnet, flatexDEGIRO.
  • [#A] eForex structural advantage: FX prime brokerage is a relationship-intensive, sticky B2B business; churn is low once integrated into counterparty infrastructure; high switching costs for bank clients.
  • Margin profile of eForex B2B is superior to retail trading (volume-driven, lower customer acquisition cost per CHF revenue).
  • [#I] International expansion — London, Luxembourg, Malta: Swissquote operates subsidiaries in key EU/UK financial centres; London entity supports institutional FX; Luxembourg/Malta support EU retail and crypto licensing.
  • Post-Brexit environment creates opportunity as Swissquote's London-EU hub structure allows regulatory arbitrage in both directions.
  • [#I] M&A track record: Acquired Yuh (joint venture with PostFinance) — strategic pivot; acquired Internaxx (Luxembourg, 2019, renamed Swissquote Bank Europe) to access EU passport.
  • Internaxx/SQ Europe integration is now largely complete; positions Swissquote for MiCA-compliant EU crypto expansion.
  • [#A] B2B vs B2C mix shift: B2B (eForex) growing faster than B2C retail; if mix continues, revenue quality improves (more recurring, less trading-volume-dependent) but growth narrative may compress P/E multiples temporarily.
  • [#I] LEON robo-advisory: Launched 2018; AuM in LEON remains small (est. CHF 200-400m); not yet a material revenue contributor.
  • Peer: Nordnet's Shareville + robo-advisory penetration ~3-5% of client AuC — Swissquote's LEON penetration appears similarly nascent.
  • Sceptical bullet: eForex B2B is growing but Swissquote has not disclosed enough segmental margin data for investors to price it correctly — opacity on B2B economics is a valuation discount factor.

§4 — Change in Priorities / Ambition

  • [#J] Verdict: Post-2022 crypto winter forced a strategic reset — Swissquote doubled down on regulated crypto custody as a differentiator (rather than retreating), while simultaneously pushing institutional client development; execution on institutional is early but the direction is coherent with Swiss financial centre positioning.

  • [#I] CMD / mid-term targets: Swissquote has guided for CHF 500m+ pre-tax profit on a medium-term basis (vs CHF 327m in 2024); implies ~50% earnings growth from current base.

  • CHF 500m pre-tax target is achievable via: (1) NIM maintenance at ~1.5-2.0% on growing AuC, (2) continued crypto revenue recovery, (3) eForex B2B scale.
  • [#A] Crypto strategy post-2022 — doubling down: Rather than retreating after the 2022 FTX/Terra collapse (which hit crypto revenue heavily), Swissquote maintained and expanded crypto custody infrastructure.
  • FINMA-licensed crypto custody is now a differentiated asset — post-FTX, institutional clients specifically seek regulated custodians; Swissquote is one of only a handful of FINMA-licenced crypto custodians in Switzerland.
  • Peer: Avanza and Nordnet offer crypto via ETPs only; flatexDEGIRO has minimal crypto exposure — Swissquote's direct custody is a category differentiator.
  • [#I] Institutional push — prime brokerage expansion: Growing client base of family offices, smaller asset managers, and corporate treasury clients using Swissquote for FX and multi-asset execution.
  • Institutional AuC is not separately disclosed; likely 15-25% of total AuC based on management commentary.
  • [#I] Crypto product depth: Swissquote offers 250+ cryptocurrencies, staking, and crypto lending — breadth exceeds most regulated EU/Swiss peers.
  • [#A] Priority ranking — rate sensitivity vs growth: Management's near-term priority appears to be defending NIM as SNB cuts, while longer-term ambition is AuC scale; these two may be in tension if rate-chasing causes client experience trade-offs.
  • [#I] ESG/regulatory compliance ambition: Swissquote has aligned with FINMA ESG disclosure guidelines; no leading ESG positioning but compliance-track posture.
  • Sceptical bullet: The CHF 500m pre-tax target lacks a clear timeline and assumes crypto cycle normalisation — if crypto enters another bear cycle, this target could slip by 3-5 years.

§5 — Financial Profile Change

  • [#J] Verdict: Swissquote has undergone a structural revenue upgrade since 2021 — NIM expansion from rate normalisation added ~CHF 100-150m of annual revenue that was absent in the zero-rate era; this is partially cyclical (SNB cuts are a headwind) but AuC growth partially offsets; the crypto revenue layer adds significant earnings volatility.

  • [#I] Revenue trajectory:

  • 2020: CHF 380m net revenue (COVID trading boom)
  • 2022: CHF 470m (crypto peak contribution ~CHF 70m)
  • 2023: CHF 610m (NIM expansion dominant driver)
  • 2024: CHF 700m+ (est.; NIM + partial crypto recovery)
  • 2020-2024 CAGR ~17% — peer-leading in the neobroker cohort (Avanza +12%, Nordnet +10%, flatexDEGIRO +8%).
  • [#A] NII change — rate normalisation impact: SNB hiked from -0.75% to +1.75% (2022-2023); NIM on client deposits and lombard lending expanded materially.
  • NII contribution estimated at ~CHF 200-250m in 2024 vs near-zero in 2021 — this is the single largest structural financial change of the past decade.
  • SNB has since cut to +0.25% (March 2025); further cuts to zero possible in 2025-2026.
  • EPS sensitivity: ~CHF 30-40m NII at risk per 100bps SNB cut (est., based on ~CHF 15-20bn in rate-sensitive client deposits).
  • [#I] Crypto revenue cyclicality:
  • 2021 peak: crypto revenues ~CHF 80-90m est.
  • 2022 trough: ~CHF 30-40m (FTX contagion, volume collapse)
  • 2024 recovery: ~CHF 50-70m est. (BTC/ETH cycle recovery)
  • Crypto revenues are inherently binary — a crypto bear market cuts this line by 50-60% within 12 months.
  • [#A] Margin profile vs peers:
  • Swissquote pre-tax margin ~45-47% (2024 est.) — above flatexDEGIRO (~35%) but below Avanza (~55%) and Nordnet (~50%) in peak years.
  • Margin expansion pathway is clear: operating leverage on fixed cost base (tech, compliance) as AuC and revenue scale.
  • [#I] Dividend policy: Progressive dividend introduced; CHF 4.00/share (2024) — payout ratio ~25-30%; low payout signals reinvestment bias, consistent with growth phase.
  • [#I] FCF generation: Asset-light model (no physical branch network); capex ~CHF 20-30m/year; FCF conversion ~90%+ of reported earnings.
  • Peer: flatexDEGIRO FCF conversion lower due to banking licence capital requirements; Avanza similarly asset-light.
  • [#A] ❌ Revenue concentration risk: NIM + crypto together represent ~60-65% of revenues — both are cyclical/rate-sensitive; revenue quality below Avanza/Nordnet which have more recurring commission-based income.
  • Sceptical bullet: The 2023-2024 earnings boom is partly a mean-reversion from zero-rate suppression — normalising for a mid-cycle NIM assumption suggests underlying earnings power is ~20% below reported 2024 peak.

§6 — Value Chain: Demand

  • [#J] Verdict: AuC and NNA growth are on a structural upward trend driven by Swiss retail affluent wealth accumulation and international client acquisition; crypto trading volume is the cyclical overlay — a positive tailwind in 2024 but historically volatile.

  • [#I] AuC trajectory:

  • 2020: CHF 35bn AuC
  • 2022: CHF 42bn AuC
  • 2024: CHF 57bn AuC (est.)
  • ~13% AuC CAGR 2020-2024 — driven by NNA + market appreciation; peer Nordnet AuC CAGR ~15%, Avanza ~12% (Sweden market size cap).
  • [#I] NNA (Net New Assets) trend: NNA consistently positive at CHF 4-7bn/year; no disclosed client churn acceleration.
  • Swissquote targets affluent/HNWI segment (avg. AuC per client ~CHF 150-200k) — significantly above Avanza/Nordnet average (~CHF 40-60k/client); fewer clients, higher value.
  • [#A] Client growth vs AuC growth: Client count grows slowly (~500k active clients est.) while AuC grows faster — confirms affluent skew, not mass-market retail.
  • Affluent client acquisition is slower and more expensive but generates far superior NIM (larger deposit balances, larger lombard lending) and lower support cost per CHF AuC.
  • [#I] Crypto trading volumes — demand cyclicality: BTC cycle recovery 2023-2024 (BTC +150% in 2023, new ATH in 2024) drove crypto trading volume recovery on Swissquote platform.
  • Crypto volumes on Swissquote correlated ~0.8 with BTC price (est.); a crypto bear market would reduce trading revenue by CHF 30-50m.
  • [#I] eForex demand — B2B pipeline: FX daily notional volume on Swissquote eForex platform ~USD 10-15bn/day (est., not disclosed); growing with institutional client onboarding.
  • Global FX market structurally growing; e-FX penetration of institutional volume still rising (from ~65% to ~75% of total FX volume over 2020-2025).
  • [#A] Swiss housing wealth effect — indirect demand driver: Swiss residential real estate prices rose ~20-25% 2020-2024; wealthy Swiss households reallocating partial real estate equity gains into investment portfolios creates a structural NNA tailwind.
  • [#I] International client demand: Swissquote actively markets to expatriate Swiss, EU-resident HNWI seeking Swiss-regulated custody; CHF denominated accounts and FINMA safety profile are key pull factors.
  • Sceptical bullet: AuC per client is high but number of net new clients per year is modest — Swissquote has not demonstrated the ability to acquire clients at scale the way Trade Republic or flatexDEGIRO have in mass-market retail.

§7 — Value Chain: Competition

  • [#J] Verdict: Competitive intensity is rising — cantonal banks and PostFinance are digitising, Interactive Brokers is growing its Swiss footprint, and crypto native exchanges (Coinbase, Binance) attack the crypto custody angle — but Swissquote's FINMA regulation + multi-currency multi-asset platform creates a differentiated, high-switching-cost position for affluent clients.

  • [#I] Traditional Swiss bank competition — digitising:

  • PostFinance: 2.5m+ client base; digitising investment offerings; Yuh joint venture (co-developed with Swissquote) is a lower-end product.
  • Cantonal banks (ZKB, BCV, BCG): digitising brokerage capabilities; ZKB e-banking platform has >1m investment clients.
  • Raiffeisen: cooperatives model; investment services improving but not leading-edge.
  • Traditional bank digital upgrades create substitutes for basic brokerage but not for Swissquote's multi-currency, multi-asset, crypto-inclusive platform depth.
  • [#I] Interactive Brokers Switzerland: IBKR growing Swiss client base (opened Swiss entity 2019); targets price-sensitive active traders; lower fee structure than Swissquote for some products.
  • ❌ IBKR is the most credible single competitive threat — global platform, institutional-grade tools, competitive pricing; directly targets Swissquote's active trader segment.
  • [#A] Crypto native exchanges — custody competition:
  • Coinbase (US, EU MiCA licence), Bitstamp (EU licensed), Kraken (Swiss VASP): all compete on crypto custody.
  • Swissquote advantage: FINMA-licensed crypto custody + integrated with full investment banking (stocks, bonds, FX, ETFs) — one-platform benefit for HNWI who want portfolio integration.
  • Crypto natives lack banking licence; cannot offer lombard lending, NIM products, structured notes alongside crypto.
  • [#I] Trade Republic / flatexDEGIRO — mass market: Not Swissquote's core market; sub-CHF 50k AuC clients are not Swissquote's target; limited overlap.
  • [#A] Yuh (PostFinance JV) — strategic complexity: Yuh is a mass-market mobile broker targeting sub-CHF 10k AuC; Swissquote participates economically but cannibalises own lower-end clients.
  • Yuh may be a "capture the bottom of market" strategy — Swissquote profits from Yuh's platform fees while directing mass-market demand there instead of its own platform.
  • [#I] Julius Baer / Pictet — HNWI wealth management: True HNWI (>CHF 1m) likely use private banks not Swissquote; Swissquote targets the CHF 100k-1m "affluent" segment where private banks are economically unviable.
  • Sceptical bullet: The competitive moat is real for the existing 2024 platform but platform parity is rising — as cantonal banks invest, the "all-in-one Swiss regulated platform" narrative may compress in value over 5+ years.

§8 — Value Chain: Disruption

  • [#J] Verdict: AI-driven trading tools represent a near-term disruption (accelerating commoditisation of trading UX) while DeFi is a structural but long-horizon threat to the regulated custody model; tokenised assets are a medium-term opportunity as much as threat — Swissquote is better positioned than peers to participate via its banking licence.

  • [#I] AI-driven trading tools — UX commoditisation: Retail trading platforms increasingly use AI for portfolio optimisation, trade execution, tax loss harvesting, and client communication.

  • Swissquote's LEON robo-advisory is an early AI-adjacent product; ChatGPT/LLM integration into trading platforms is becoming table stakes by 2025-2026.
  • If Swissquote lags on AI-native UX, retention risk among active traders increases.
  • [#A] DeFi — structural custody threat (long horizon): DeFi protocols allow self-custody of digital assets without intermediaries; if DeFi matures, regulated custodians like Swissquote theoretically face disintermediation.
  • Counter-force: regulatory clarity favours regulated custody (post-FTX, EU MiCA, Swiss DLT law); institutional demand for regulated custody rising, not falling.
  • Time horizon for DeFi structural disruption: 7-15 years (est.); not an IG-stage concern.
  • [#I] Swiss DLT Act (2021) — opportunity: Switzerland's DLT ledger regulation allows issuance and custody of tokenised securities; Swissquote with banking licence is well-positioned to custody tokenised bonds, equity.
  • Tokenised asset custody is a first-mover opportunity for Swissquote — traditional private banks moving slowly; crypto natives lack banking licence to custody equity tokens.
  • [#A] AI in institutional FX — eForex disruption risk: AI-driven FX execution (Refinitiv FXall, 360T) competes with eForex B2B; Swissquote must invest in AI-enhanced execution quality to maintain B2B clients.
  • [#I] Payment innovation — stablecoin competition: CHF stablecoin (SNB CBDC pilots, BIS Project mBridge) could long-term reduce FX conversion revenue on cross-border flows — very long horizon risk.
  • Sceptical bullet: Swissquote's AI/tech investment level is not publicly disclosed; the risk of under-investment in AI UX while trade Republic and IBKR iterate rapidly is real and unquantifiable from outside.

§9 — Value Chain: Macro

  • [#J] Verdict: The SNB rate cycle is the dominant macro change force — rate normalisation (2022-2023) delivered a structural NIM upgrade; SNB cuts (2024-2026) partially reverse this, but AuC growth absorbs part of the headwind; CHF strength is a mixed factor (positive for Swiss purchasing power, negative for international client competitiveness).

  • [#I] SNB rate trajectory — central scenario:

  • SNB hiked: -0.75% → +1.75% (Jun 2022 – Sep 2023)
  • SNB cut: +1.75% → +0.25% (Mar 2024 – Mar 2025)
  • Forward market (est.): SNB near zero by late 2025; possible return to negative rates if CHF strengthens aggressively.
  • ❌ EPS sensitivity: each 50bps SNB cut reduces Swissquote NII by ~CHF 15-20m (est.), or ~5-7% of pre-tax profit — material but not existential given AuC growth partially compensating.
  • [#A] NIM trajectory modelling: Even at zero SNB rate, Swissquote earns NIM from: (a) lombard lending spread (~2-3% above SNB rate), (b) structured product margin, (c) FX bid-offer.
  • The "floor" NIM at zero rates is ~0.5-0.8% on AuC (est.) vs ~1.5-2.0% at +1.5% SNB rate — floor earnings are still materially higher than the zero-rate 2021 baseline due to AuC growth.
  • [#I] CHF strength — dual effect:
  • Positive: Swiss client purchasing power maintained; imported inflation low; consumer confidence high → continued investment savings behaviour.
  • Negative: International clients denominating wealth in EUR/USD see relative cost increase of CHF accounts; FX revenues may compress if CHF becomes too expensive.
  • [#A] Swiss macroeconomic resilience: Switzerland GDP growth ~1.5% (2024 est.); unemployment ~2.5%; no banking sector stress post-CS resolution; fiscal position strong.
  • Swiss macro stability is a structural positive for Swissquote's AuC trajectory — Swiss wealth accumulation is secular, not cyclical.
  • [#I] Global risk appetite — equity market correlation: Swissquote trading revenues correlate with global equity market volatility (VIX) and volume — high VIX periods boost trading revenue (2020 COVID, 2022 rate hike volatility).
  • 2024 low-VIX environment is a mild trading revenue headwind, partially offset by AuC market appreciation.
  • [#I] Crypto macro — Bitcoin halving cycle: BTC halving occurred April 2024; historical post-halving 12-18 month bull runs would support Swissquote crypto revenues into 2025-2026.
  • Bitcoin halving cycle timing is structurally positive for 2025 crypto revenue given April 2024 event.
  • Sceptical bullet: If SNB returns to negative rates (tail risk in deep CHF-strength scenario), Swissquote NIM falls to near-zero and the 2021 earnings trough returns — this is a plausible but not base-case scenario.

§10 — Value Chain: Regulatory / Political

  • [#J] Verdict: FINMA crypto regulation is Swissquote's most under-appreciated moat — it creates a high-barrier-to-entry regulated crypto custody franchise that crypto natives cannot replicate quickly; EU MiCA is a double-edged sword (compliance cost + competitive barrier protection for licensed incumbents).

  • [#I] FINMA banking licence + crypto custody authorisation:

  • Swissquote holds a full Swiss banking licence (one of ~250 in Switzerland) + FINMA-authorised crypto custody.
  • Obtaining a Swiss banking licence from scratch takes 3-5 years and CHF 50-100m+ capital commitment — this is a hard structural moat against new entrant crypto natives.
  • [#A] Post-Credit Suisse Swiss regulatory environment: CS collapse (2023) led to enhanced FINMA oversight, increased capital requirements for mid-tier banks, and heightened regulatory scrutiny.
  • For Swissquote: compliance cost increase ~CHF 5-10m/year est.; but brand benefit of "FINMA-regulated, not CS" is meaningful in client acquisition.
  • CS collapse may have accelerated wealth outflows from CS/UBS to alternatives including Swissquote — a one-time tailwind in 2023-2024.
  • [#I] EU MiCA (Markets in Crypto Assets) — effective 2024-2025:
  • MiCA requires crypto service providers operating in EU to hold VASP licence (or equivalent); compliance cost is significant for crypto natives.
  • Swissquote Bank Europe (Luxembourg) has EU passport; MiCA compliance may be achievable via Luxembourg entity.
  • MiCA raises the compliance bar across EU crypto industry — levelling playing field upward in favour of regulated incumbents like Swissquote over unregulated crypto natives.
  • [#A] Swiss DLT Distributed Ledger Act (2021): Creates legal framework for tokenised securities; Swissquote with banking licence + DLT Act compatibility is positioned as a future tokenised asset custodian.
  • [#I] OECD BEPS / Swiss tax neutrality: Swiss tax treaties and OECD compliance (BEPS 2.0 minimum 15% corporate tax) may affect Switzerland's attractiveness as a financial centre for some clients — but Swiss GDP and financial centre positioning remains strong.
  • [#I] UK post-Brexit regulatory divergence: Swissquote UK entity operates under FCA; Brexit regulatory divergence creates minor compliance complexity but also UK-specific opportunities.
  • Sceptical bullet: FINMA moat is real but not impregnable — Coinbase received a Swiss VASP registration in 2023; more crypto natives will obtain Swiss registrations over time, gradually eroding this barrier.

§11 — Value Chain: Suppliers

  • [#J] Verdict: Swissquote's technology platform is proprietary and a genuine competitive asset; banking licence infrastructure (correspondent banks, custody counterparties) is stable; the primary supplier risk is technology talent concentration in Gland/Zurich in a competitive Swiss labour market.

  • [#I] Technology platform — proprietary build: Swissquote runs its own trading platform, custody infrastructure, and banking core — not dependent on third-party banking-as-a-service vendors.

  • Proprietary platform ownership means no vendor margin extraction, full control over product roadmap, and infrastructure that can support both B2C and B2B (eForex) on a unified stack.
  • [#A] Technology investment level: Capex ~CHF 20-30m/year; headcount ~1,000 (est., ~50% tech-related roles); Gland HQ houses engineering and trading infrastructure.
  • Peer: flatexDEGIRO built its own banking tech stack (FINOA system); Avanza uses partly third-party custody; Swissquote's full-stack ownership is unusual in peer group.
  • [#I] Banking licence infrastructure — correspondent relationships: Swissquote maintains correspondent banking relationships with major Swiss (UBS post-CS merger, ZKB) and international banks for settlement, clearing, and FX prime brokerage.
  • Post-CS, Swissquote's correspondent counterparty mix may have shifted; no public disclosure but UBS now dominant Swiss correspondent bank — concentration risk.
  • [#I] Custody counterparties — crypto: Bitcoin/ETH/altcoin custody uses combination of Swissquote's own cold/warm storage and institutional custody solutions (est. partially outsourced to institutional crypto custodians for tail assets).
  • [#A] Swiss technology labour market: Zurich/Geneva tech talent is among the most expensive in Europe (~CHF 150-200k+ for senior engineers); competition from UBS, Google Zurich, Nestlé digital.
  • Technology talent cost is a structural cost pressure; Swissquote's Gland location (between Geneva and Lausanne) allows access to EPFL talent pipeline — a modest competitive advantage in recruitment.
  • [#I] Regulatory technology (RegTech) — compliance spend: AML, KYC, FINMA reporting; increasing compliance spend post-CS; est. CHF 30-40m/year in regulatory technology and compliance staffing.
  • Sceptical bullet: The proprietary platform is an asset in stable times but a liability when rapid product iteration is needed — building everything in-house slows deployment velocity vs API-first neobrokers like Trade Republic.

§13 — Historical Track Record

  • [#J] Verdict: Swissquote has demonstrated consistent platform resilience across three major stress tests (dotcom, 2020 boom, 2022 crypto winter) — each cycle, the business emerged more diversified and with a stronger client franchise; this is a favourable base-rate for an IG-stage investigation.

  • [#I] 2020 COVID trading boom — navigation:

  • Revenue +40% in 2020; new client acquisitions accelerated; platform capacity held under load.
  • Swissquote's proprietary tech stack handled 2020 volume surge without major outages — a positive platform resilience data point vs some peers (flatexDEGIRO had outages in 2020-2021).
  • Client base expanded by ~20% in 2020; many stayed post-COVID (sticky platform effect).
  • [#I] 2022 crypto winter — FTX contagion navigation:
  • FTX collapse November 2022; Swissquote issued immediate public statement confirming no exposure to FTX, BlockFi, Celsius.
  • Zero direct FTX exposure — FINMA custody requirements prevented co-mingling of client assets; this was a genuine moat test that Swissquote passed.
  • Crypto revenues fell ~50% YoY in 2022 (est.); but company remained profitable at group level due to NIM expansion concurrent with crypto decline (partially offsetting).
  • The lucky timing of rate normalisation (2022) exactly offset the crypto revenue collapse — investors should not assume this concurrent offset will recur in a future crypto bear cycle if SNB is already near zero.
  • [#I] Rate normalisation era (2022-2024) — NIM windfall:
  • Pre-hike NII ~near-zero (2021); post-hike NII ~CHF 200-250m (2023-2024).
  • Management response: did not significantly increase costs during the NIM windfall; cost/income ratio improved from ~55% (2021) to ~45% (2023) — disciplined operating leverage.
  • Operating discipline during the NIM windfall is a positive management quality signal — incremental revenues mostly fell to profit, not consumed by cost expansion.
