EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (BLUF) 1 findings

Strategic Position and Organizational Transformation

Tata Motors announced a voluntary cash tender offer of €14.1 per share to acquire Iveco, expected to close by Q3 2026, creating the fourth-largest global commercial vehicle player with over 540,000 unit sales.

Iveco's Return on Capital Employed is only 14%, far below Tata Motors' commercial vehicle business which operates at a premium ~40% ROCE.

The planned Tata merger targets stabilizing joint ROCE at approximately 20% over time by coordinating R&D and eliminating redundant investments.

Iveco legally carved out and sold its specialized Defence business to Leonardo for €1.7 billion in cash, distributing net proceeds via an extraordinary dividend of €5.82 per share in April 2026.

Olof Persson succeeded Gerrit Marx as Chief Executive Officer in July 2024, bringing Volvo Group restructuring expertise as the architect behind its margin-improving transformation.

Iveco maintains a highly profitable leading position in the European chassis cab light commercial vehicle sub-segment, though heavy-duty trucks have historically operated at breakeven margins due to legacy under-investment.

Financial Performance and Peer Comparisons

Consolidated net revenues fell 7% year-over-year to €13.43bn in FY25, down from €14.42bn in FY24, driven by weak Truck division demand.

Industrial activities FCF turned negative at -€109m in FY25, down from +€240m in FY24, reflecting severe cash absorption pressures.

Bus division production delays at the French Annonay plant caused a €200m negative FCF impact, with unfinished bus products remaining in inventory at year-end.

Volvo and Paccar lead the sector, converting an average 84% and 86% of net profit to FCF, while Daimler and Traton lag at 77% and 73% respectively.

Volvo dominates the European sector with an average ROIC of 41% over the last five years, while Traton generated a lower average ROIC of 14% over the same 2021-2025 period.

Industry Structure and Competitive Dynamics

Europe represents one of the most concentrated heavy truck markets globally, with Volvo, Daimler Truck, Traton, Paccar, and Iveco collectively commanding approximately 98% of European heavy truck registrations.

Traditional truck manufacturers are maintaining commercial discipline on pricing, prioritizing value over volume, though the supply chain crisis enabled OEMs to achieve double-digit pricing growth during the 2022 to 2024 period.

Three years of strong pricing shifted the profit pool from fleet owners to OEMs, stretching customer affordability limits and limiting future margin expansion opportunities.

European truck share (GVW ≥3.5 tons) declined to 10.7% in 2025, down 1.0 percentage points, with European market share falling 1.2 percentage points to 11.9% in LCV and 0.7 percentage points to 8.2% in M&H.

The primary defensive moat in European trucking is the availability of dense aftersales and service networks, as uptime is vital because a broken heavy truck can cost operators between €1,000 and €2,000 per day in downtime.

Electrification is fundamentally altering the industry moat by making procurement highly transparent, while Chinese OEMs are bypassing traditional service moats by partnering with established independent service networks.

Value Chain Positioning and Margin Distribution

Replacement parts distribution captures the most attractive profit pool, yielding highly profitable 20.00% EBIT margins globally, while captive financing arms earn premium 24.00% EBIT margins alongside consistent 2.00% return on assets.

Iveco supplies 45.00% of its engines internally through its FPT Industrial segment, while FPT Industrial distributes 55.00% of its engines externally to third-party commercial vehicle manufacturers.

Industry leader Volvo operates with 96.00% captive engine integration, protecting its proprietary parts loop, while Daimler Truck maintains a highly integrated 78.00% captive engine share across its heavy fleet.

Iveco distributes vehicles via a combined dealer network boasting 1,585 sales points globally, maintaining 3,550 service points to ensure rapid maintenance response for operators.

Customer Relationships and Switching Costs

No single external customer accounts for 10% or more of Iveco Group's consolidated revenues in FY25, reflecting minimal customer concentration.

Iveco Bus signed three major framework agreements with Île-de-France Mobilités to supply up to 4,000 low-emission vehicles, extending from 2026 to 2032 and securing long-term factory utilization.

The Net Promoter Score (NPS) for IVECO Trucks was only 6.5% higher in 2025 compared to its 2021 baseline, representing a significant lag against the strategic target of achieving a 25% NPS improvement by 2026.

Once customers activate the telematics service, real-world data feeds directly into the Control Room to prevent unplanned downtime, with the IVECO Customer Uptime Centre actively monitoring more than 3,000 electric vehicles at year-end 2025.

Supplier and Distribution Channel Dependencies

Iveco Group manages purchases worth over €8.6 billion from a network of 1,790 direct material suppliers, with the top 150 suppliers representing strategic partners generating 75.00% of the total purchase value.

Iveco relies on single-source suppliers for certain critical components that cannot be quickly re-sourced, with its smaller scale relative to peers resulting in less relative bargaining strength during raw material negotiations.

Iveco's average invoice payment time compressed from 81 days to 77 days in FY25, while standard contractual payment terms tightened to 67 days in FY25, down from 70 days in FY24.

Disruption Threats and Long-Term Structural Evolution

Electrification functions as an industry-wide reset where fleet purchasing decisions shift from brand loyalty to objective battery specifications, though heavy battery-electric trucks remain extremely expensive to acquire, costing approximately twice as much as equivalent diesel models.

Daimler and Volvo Group co-established the Coretura joint venture to develop an open, standardized software-defined commercial vehicle platform, enabling advanced software applications to deploy rapidly and separating physical hardware from digital vehicle control layers.

Autonomous driving threatens to consolidate the OEM customer base, shifting industry purchasing power to consolidated megafleets, which possess superior volume purchasing leverage that structurally compresses high-margin small-fleet sales.
