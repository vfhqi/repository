  • Board size: 9–11 directors as of most recent disclosed composition (2024 Annual Report) — within normal Swiss corporate governance range (VegüV Swiss Ordinance on Excessive Compensation recommends effective boards; no statutory size requirement)

  • Independent directors: Formally, Galderma discloses a majority of non-executive directors as "independent" per Swiss standards — however, the EQT-nominated directors (who hold multiple board seats and sit on the Nomination Committee) represent a structural principal-agent tension; "independence" under Swiss law is not the same as independence from the controlling shareholder

  • Insider directors: CEO Flemming Ørnskov sits on the board (executive director); EQT-affiliated directors include GP partners with fiduciary obligations to EQT funds that retain a significant Galderma stake post-IPO — these directors are technically non-executive but not substantively independent

§6 — Governance Structure + Red Flags

J-front verdict: Structurally mixed — no acute governance red flags, but EQT concentration + Swiss corporate governance (weaker minority protection vs Anglo-Saxon standards) warrants ongoing monitoring.

  • Voting rights structure:
  • Galderma operates a standard one-share-one-vote structure — no dual-class shares, no golden share, no differential voting rights
  • However, EQT-led consortium retains a majority or near-majority economic stake post-IPO (exact % varies with secondary transactions; estimated 50–60% consortium ownership at IPO, with gradual sell-down expected over the lockup/post-lockup window) — effective voting concentration without a formal dual-class structure

  • Anti-takeover provisions: under Swiss company law, Galderma's articles may include statutory restrictions on large share acquisitions; standard Swiss "opting-out" vs "opting-in" on mandatory bid rules — these are Swiss structural features, not specific red flags

  • Related-party transactions:

  • EQT management fees and advisory fees charged to Galderma during the PE ownership period — standard PE practice; these should have terminated at IPO
  • Post-IPO related-party transactions not materially disclosed in available press — positive signal
  • EQT-affiliated board directors receive standard NED fees, not disclosed as RPTs — appropriate

  • No press or regulatory disclosures of unusual RPTs with controlling shareholders post-IPO

  • Audit history:

  • PricewaterhouseCoopers AG (Zurich) is the statutory auditor — has been auditor through the EQT ownership period and through IPO
  • No auditor change at IPO (some PE-backed IPOs switch from a non-Big4 auditor at listing; Galderma maintained PwC throughout) — positive continuity signal
  • No qualified audit opinions, no material weaknesses disclosed in IPO prospectus or FY2023/FY2024 annual reports in the available public record

  • Audit fees not separately disclosed at granular level in available public summaries — standard Swiss disclosure limitation

  • Restatement history: No disclosed restatements in the available public record; the IPO prospectus restated historical financials under IFRS (including carve-out-period adjustments from Nestlé) — these were carve-out normalisation adjustments, not restatements due to error; distinction is important

  • Legal/regulatory issues:

  • No disclosed SEC/FINMA investigations
  • No disclosed material class actions (Galderma is listed on SIX, not NYSE/NASDAQ, so US-style securities class actions are structurally less common; FINMA enforcement would be the Swiss equivalent)
  • Galderma has faced historic product liability litigation related to dermal fillers (aesthetic injectables) — this is an endemic industry risk (also faced by Allergan, Merz, Sinclair) and is not a company-specific governance failure; no material undisclosed liabilities flagged in IPO prospectus

  • Regulatory: Galderma's products are subject to FDA/EMA/Swissmedic regulatory oversight; no disclosed consent decrees, warning letters, or manufacturing site shutdowns in the public record L3Y

  • Whistleblower / governance complaints: No public record of whistleblower actions, significant employee complaints, or governance-breach disclosures; absence of evidence is not evidence of absence, but no active signal

  • Swiss-specific governance note: Swiss VegüV (Ordinance Against Excessive Compensation) requires AGM votes on aggregate compensation and board remuneration — Galderma has complied; Swiss Code of Best Practice for Corporate Governance is a soft-law framework to which Galderma adheres in disclosed materials; Swiss governance standards provide weaker minority shareholder protections than UK Corporate Governance Code or Delaware law — this is a structural risk factor for any Swiss-listed investment with a dominant shareholder

  • Sceptical bullet — bearish read on governance: The single most plausible governance failure scenario is a related-party capital event post-lockup: as EQT monetises its remaining stake, the Compensation Committee (which includes EQT-affiliated directors) may approve management retention packages or deal-related compensation structures that extract value from public shareholders to facilitate an orderly PE exit; this would be legal under Swiss law and would be difficult for minority shareholders to block given EQT's voting weight; the absence of a formal dual-class structure does not protect minority shareholders from this risk
