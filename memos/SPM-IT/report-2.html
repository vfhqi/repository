§1 — Brief recap + key components summary

J-front verdict: Something materially interesting is happening at Saipem — a genuine three-year financial rehabilitation is now backed by record backlog, third consecutive year of growth, balance sheet net cash positive (pre-IFRS 16), and a transformational merger announced. The volume of simultaneous change vectors is unusual. [#J]

Scope of change identified (L12-18M and plausible N3Y):

  • (1) CEO/senior leadership: Alessandro Puliti appointed CEO August 2022; re-confirmed with new three-year board mandate May 2024; CFO Antonio Paccioretti in post since June 2021. No senior turnover signals. [#I]
  • (2) M&A/corporate activity: Cross-border merger with Subsea 7 announced February 2025; merger agreement signed July 2025; share swap 6.688 Saipem per 1 Subsea 7 share; 50/50 ownership post-close; new entity "Saipem7"; expected close H2 2026. [#I]
  • (3) Margin/financial profile: EBITDA margin: 4.5% (2022) → 7.8% (2023) → 9.1% (2024) → 11.1% (2025); net debt post-IFRS 16 turned net cash pre-IFRS 16 by end-2024; FCF improving meaningfully. [#I]
  • (4) Shareholder returns: Dividend reinstated May 2025 (€0.17/share; €333m total); dividend policy 30-40% of FCF; first dividend since before 2022 crisis. [#I]
  • (5) Demand/revenue changes: Revenue €11.9bn (2023) → €14.5bn (2024) → €15.5bn (2025); record order intake €18.8bn in 2024; backlog all-time high €34bn at end-2024. [#I]
  • (6) Goals/targets: Plan 2025-2028 published February 2025; EBITDA target €2.0bn by 2028; cumulative FCF ≥€2.2bn; €50bn order intake target across 4 years, €15bn from low-carbon. [#I]
  • (7) Competitor/political/regulatory: Subsea 7 merger changes competitive landscape materially; Russia/Arctic LNG 2 exit completed December 2023; EU energy security policy supporting offshore investment. [#I]
  • (8) Macro/industry: Offshore capex set to exceed $300bn in 2025, growing 11% y/y; construction vessels fully booked 2026; EPC award opportunities $54bn in 2025 per Westwood forecasts. [#I]

Sceptical bullet: The simultaneity of positive signals — record backlog, dividend restart, merger, margin expansion — risks masking underlying complexity; the EBITDA-to-EPS gap (EBITDA +29% in 2025, net profit flat at ~€310m) signals high absorptive costs that suppress EPS even as operating metrics shine. [#J]

§2 — Change in leadership

J-front verdict: Leadership change is real but evolutionary, not revolutionary — Puliti is a long-tenured Eni/Saipem insider who brought operational discipline, not a radical external hire; mandate confirmed through 2026, signal is continuity. [#J]

  • CEO profile: Alessandro Puliti; career started as reservoir geologist at Agip/Eni, 30+ years in technical, operational, management roles in Italy and abroad; joined Saipem February 2022 as General Manager; formally appointed CEO August 2022 post-rights issue; board re-confirmed mandate May 2024 through to AGM approving 2026 accounts. [#I]
  • CEO background assessment: Puliti is not an industry outsider or turnaround specialist — he is a technical-commercial insider who knows where the bodies are buried; his credibility with clients (NOCs, majors) is a structural asset for a contractor. [#A]
  • CFO: Antonio Paccioretti; in post since June 2021; no CFO change in the restructuring period; CFO continuity provides institutional memory across the balance sheet repair. [#I]
  • Board renewal (2024): New three-year board mandate from May 14, 2024 Shareholders' Meeting; Eni and the Italian state (via CDP) remain major shareholders; board composition reflects dual Italian industrial-state governance overlay, which limits pure financial-investor agency. [#I]
  • Culture signals: No direct eNPS data available in public filings; anecdotal evidence from earnings transcripts suggests management tone shifted from defensive/crisis-management (2022-2023) to confident/constructive (2024-2025); CEO language moved from "stabilising" to "accelerating." [#A]
  • Assessment: Puliti-led team is meaningfully different from pre-2022 management in that they engineered the rights issue, restructured the balance sheet, and now have three consecutive years of delivery; but the gene pool is Eni/Saipem insiders — not a "new broom" in the private-equity sense. [#A]

Sceptical bullet: ❌ Insider management with Eni and state-ownership overhang limits the pace of corporate transformation; strategic decisions on the Subsea 7 merger involve Italian political and industrial considerations, not purely shareholder-value optimisation. [#J]

§3 — Change in strategy/structure

J-front verdict: Strategy has shifted materially from crisis-survival mode to growth-with-discipline; the four-pillar 2025-2028 plan is internally coherent; the Subsea 7 merger, if completed, is the single largest structural change in Saipem's history and creates a step-change in competitive scale. [#J]

  • Post-2022 strategic reset: Strategy pivoted from volume-at-any-cost to "value over volume" per Puliti's explicit framing; selective bidding on lump-sum onshore EPIC; shift toward asset-based offshore and reimbursable-type structures. [#A]
  • Four strategic pillars (2025-2028 Plan): (1) Execution excellence; (2) Capitalise on oil and gas upstream upcycle; (3) New paradigm for onshore E&C and PMC services offering; (4) Broaden energy transition exposure. These pillars are credibly supported by backlog composition and order intake trajectory. [#I]
  • Financial commitments (2025-2028 Plan): EBITDA ≈€2.0bn by 2028; cumulative operating cash flow (post-lease repayment) ≥€3.7bn; cumulative capex ≈€1.5bn; cumulative FCF ≥€2.2bn; investment-grade credit rating as explicit target. [#I]
  • 2025 guidance (as updated): Revenue ≈€15bn; EBITDA ≈€1.6bn; operating cash flow ≈€900m; subsequently exceeded — FY 2025 delivered €15.5bn revenue and €1.7bn EBITDA. [#I]
  • Subsea 7 merger — key terms: Share swap 6.688 Saipem per 1 Subsea 7 share; 50/50 post-completion ownership; Subsea 7 shareholders receive pre-close extraordinary dividend ≈$529m; merger agreement signed July 24, 2025; shareholder votes September 25, 2025; close expected H2 2026; new entity Saipem7 S.p.A.; listed Milan and Oslo. [#I]
  • Subsea 7 strategic rationale: Complementary geographies, vessel fleets, and competencies; combined backlog €43bn; combined revenue ≈€20bn; annual cost and capex synergies ≈€300m from year 3 post-close; one-time synergy cost ≈€270m; four business units post-close: Offshore E&C, Onshore E&C, Sustainable Infrastructures, Offshore Drilling. [#A]
  • Onshore E&C repositioning: Away from pure lump-sum EPC (which caused 2022 crisis) toward project management consultancy (PMC) and reimbursable models; this is a structural risk-reduction move with direct parallel to the root cause of 2022 losses. [#A]
  • Asset base: No major asset disposals since 2022 crisis; strategy has been to invest in fleet expansion (new charters) rather than divest; gross debt pre-IFRS 16 is €2.2bn with €2.9bn liquidity — comfortable but not yet investment grade. [#I]

Sceptical bullet: The Subsea 7 merger introduces significant integration risk, management bandwidth dilution, and regulatory complexity (antitrust in multiple jurisdictions); market concentration scrutiny could delay or impose conditions; a 50/50 ownership structure historically creates governance friction and strategic deadlock risk. [#J]

[#J]

  • Ambition shift — evidence: 2022-2023 focus was survival, balance sheet repair, and "not burning ourselves on lump-sum again"; by 2024, language had shifted to "capturing the upcycle," "fleet fully booked," "booking fees from clients while awaiting FID" — structurally different confidence signals.   • Tone analysis: Transcript language 2024-2025 includes frequent references to multi-year visibility, fleet utilisation through 2027-2028, and "acceleration" — qualitatively higher confidence register than 2022-2023 "stabilisation" framing. [#A]

Sceptical bullet: Management tone confidence always peaks at cycle highs; Saipem's current ambition level (€50bn 4-year intake, €20bn combined entity) mirrors pre-crisis 2012-2014 when management was also confident and then delivered catastrophic lump-sum losses; cycle-peak overconfidence is a known sector risk. §5 — Financial profile change

Sustained, but the EPS-EBITDA divergence is a meaningful caveat. [#J]

Revenue trajectory:
- 2022: ≈€9.5bn (crisis year, severe contract writedowns)
- 2023: €11.9bn (+25% y/y)
- 2024: €14.5bn (+22% y/y)
- 2025: €15.5bn (+7% y/y, deceleration but absolute level a record)
- 2026 guidance: ≈€15.5bn (implies plateau consistent with merger transition)
- Consensus: €15,764m sales — broadly in line with actuals trajectory [#I]

EBITDA margin trajectory:
- 2021: negative (crisis losses)
- 2022: ≈4.5% (post-rights issue base)
- 2023: 7.8%
- 2024: 9.1% (+130bps y/y)
- 2025: 11.1% (+200bps y/y)
- 2026 target: ≈€1.9bn EBITDA on ≈€15.5bn revenue = ≈12.2% guided
- 2028 target: ≈€2.0bn EBITDA; margin implied ≈13%+ at that revenue scale [#I]

Peer margin comparison (2024):
- Saipem EBITDA margin: 9.1%

- Technip Energies EBITDA margin: 8.3% (slightly below Saipem but different mix — onshore-heavy)
- Saipem converging toward Technip Energies parity but Subsea 7 shows how far the margin gap persists for more asset-heavy offshore specialists [#A]

EBITDA-EPS divergence (SSEM signal — EBITDA revisions +9% L12M, EPS revisions -14%):
- Root cause: D&A surged 44% in 2025 (€723m → €1.04bn) due to fleet expansion on charter basis
- Financial expenses surged 122% (€85m → €189m) due to hedging costs and EUR/USD interest rate differential
- Net profit held flat: €306m (2024) vs €310m (2025) despite EBITDA growing 29%
- EBIT grew only 12% (to €679m from €607m) after D&A load — much less impressive than EBITDA headline
- EPS consensus €0.293 suppressed by high D&A and financing costs absorbing operating gains [#A]
- This divergence is structural not a one-off: fleet expansion via operating leases continues to build D&A; as long as Saipem grows its chartered fleet, D&A scales proportionally [#A]

Leverage trajectory:
- Post-2022 rights issue: heavily net debt burdened (peak net debt post-crisis ~€2bn+ pre-IFRS 16)
- End-2023: pre-IFRS 16 net cash €216m
- End-2024: pre-IFRS 16 net cash €683m; post-IFRS 16 net cash €23m
- End-2025: pre-IFRS 16 net cash €999m; post-IFRS 16 net debt €498m (lease liabilities €1.497bn)
- Balance sheet repair substantially complete on pre-IFRS 16 basis; IFRS 16 lease load growing with fleet expansion [#I]

FCF generation:
- 2025 operating cash flow ≈€900m; capex ≈€382m; FCF pre-IFRS 16 lease repayments meaningful and positive
- Committed to ≥€2.2bn cumulative FCF 2025-2028 (≈€550m/year average) — achievable on current trajectory [#I]

Shareholder returns:
- First dividend €0.17/share paid May 2025 (€333m total; ~30% of FCF on 2024 results)
- 2026 dividend guided ≥€300m based on 2025 results
- No buyback announced; no buyback commitment in current plan [#I]

Sceptical bullet: ❌ The Subsea 7 merger, if completed, will materially increase D&A and integration costs, likely suppressing EPS further in 2026-2027 even if EBITDA improves on a combined basis; EPS trajectory may remain depressed long after EBITDA reaches target, creating ongoing divergence risk. [#J]

J-front verdict: Demand environment is the strongest it has been since the 2012-2014 super-cycle; backlog at all-time high; fleet fully booked through 2026; customer NOC capex growing double-digit; the immediate demand picture is as favourable as it gets for Saipem. [#J]

Customer capex:
- Global offshore capex exceeded $300bn in 2025, growing ≈11% y/y; 2024 saw ≈12% growth
- Middle East NOCs (Saudi Aramco, ADNOC, QatarEnergy) driving nearshore and LNG project pipeline
- UAE targeting 5.0m b/d capacity by end-2026 (from 4.85m b/d); significant brownfield/expansion work pipeline
- China offshore activity accelerating, led by CNOOC; Asia Pacific contributing materially [#I]

Saipem backlog:
- End-2024: €34,065m (all-time high)
- Asset Based Services (offshore): €22,470m
- Energy Carriers (onshore E&C, LNG): €10,270m
- Offshore Drilling: €1,325m
- Of which €12,092m to be executed in 2025 (≈35% near-term coverage = strong) [#I]
- End-2025: €31,469m (lower as execution ran ahead of replenishment)
- Asset Based Services: €21,163m
- Energy Carriers: €9,345m
- Offshore Drilling: €961m [#I]

Order intake:
- 2024: €18.8bn (record; book-to-bill ≈1.3x for full year; Q4 alone €5.3bn at 1.2x)
- 2025 full year: €13bn (deceleration from 2024 record; still materially above burn rate)
- H1 2025 key wins: ExxonMobil Hammerhead deepwater project (Guyana); QatarEnergy subsea pipeline repair
- ⚡ RARE — Order intake quality: recent wins show geographic diversification across Middle East, Guyana, North Africa — not concentrated on a single NOC customer [#A]

Fleet utilisation and pipeline:
- Construction vessel utilisation: fully booked 2025 and 2026; 50% booked 2027-2028 — multi-year lock-in
- Booking fees being collected from clients awaiting FID converts — unusual level of customer commitment pre-FID signals genuine excess demand for Saipem's vessel capacity [#I]

LNG and Energy Carriers:
- Energy Carriers segment backlog €10.3bn (2024); LNG is a key driver as global LNG supply expansion continues post-Russia gas disruption
- LNG projects provide scale EPIC work; Saipem has specific LNG track record (Qatar, Mozambique, Algeria history) [#I]

Sceptical bullet: 2025 order intake deceleration (€13bn vs €18.8bn in 2024) is a yellow flag — partly explained by high base effect and timing of project FIDs; but if oil price falls sharply or NOC capex budgets are cut, backlog replenishment could slow further and book-to-bill could deteriorate below 1.0x. §7 — Value chain — Competition

J-front verdict: Competitive landscape is constructive for Saipem; supply remains tight; the Subsea 7 merger will reduce the number of Tier 1 offshore contractors from effectively 3 to 2, raising market concentration; pricing discipline appears broadly holding. [#J]

Market structure:
- Tier 1 offshore EPIC contractors historically: Saipem, Subsea 7, McDermott, Technip; post-McDermott bankruptcy (2018), market contracted
- Post-Saipem/Subsea 7 merger (if approved): two dominant players in subsea/offshore (Saipem7 + TechnipFMC/Technip Energies); significant concentration [#A]
- Combined Saipem7 backlog €43bn, revenue €20bn — would be largest offshore services company globally by backlog [#I]

New capacity additions:
- No major new entrants in asset-heavy offshore EPIC (high capital barriers); Asian yards building new construction vessels but primarily for Asian clients
- Fleet scarcity industry-wide: Saipem's fleet fully booked 2025-2026 and 50% for 2027-2028 — industry-wide vessel scarcity, not just Saipem-specific [#A]

Contract model discipline:
- "Value over volume" mantra explicitly targets higher-margin selective bidding; management has emphasised avoiding lump-sum exposure on onshore mega-projects (historically the loss driver)
- Offshore subsea and EPCI work is more reimbursable or hybrid — structurally better risk profile than onshore lump-sum EPC
- Market-level: $54bn EPC offshore award activity expected 2025 (Westwood), up from prior years; demand backlog exceeds available contractor capacity [#A]

Pricing dynamics:
- Fleet scarcity and high utilisation rates enabling contractors to push pricing; Saipem receiving booking fees pre-FID (unusual) signals clients paying for capacity reservation
- No evidence of pricing capitulation; management has explicitly declined lower-margin work [#A]

Antitrust risk on merger:
- Combined entity Saipem7 would create significant offshore EPIC market concentration
- EU and other jurisdictions may impose conditions or behavioural remedies; plausible merger obstacle that could delay timeline or force asset disposals [#J]

Sceptical bullet: If macro deteriorates (oil price drop) and NOC capex is cut, contractors historically compete aggressively on price to fill vessels, eroding current pricing discipline; Saipem's lump-sum discipline is untested in a down-cycle since the current management took over. [#J]

§8 — Value chain — Disruption

J-front verdict: Energy transition is a modest revenue opportunity for Saipem (offshore wind installation, CCUS), not yet a material driver; digitisation is incremental; the dominant disruption risk is the traditional cycle turn, not technology substitution. [#J]

Renewables/new energy exposure:
- Saipem has active offshore wind installation capability (vessel-based); participates in European offshore wind projects
- Strategic plan 2025-2028 targets €15bn from "low-carbon projects" out of €50bn total intake (30% of 4-year intake target)
- Current revenue split not precisely disaggregated by energy type in public filings; renewables/energy transition not yet a standalone reportable segment at material revenue scale
- CCUS, geothermal, bio-fuels/SAF, nuclear cited as growth verticals [#I]

Energy transition revenue quality:
- Offshore wind installation competes with specialist wind installers (Heerema, DEME, Van Oord); Saipem is not the dominant player in offshore wind installation
- CCUS is early-stage commercially; few projects at EPIC scale worldwide to date
- Low-carbon €15bn target is aspirational and depends on FID activity not yet confirmed [#A]

Technology/digitisation disruption:
- AI and digital engineering tools (BIM, digital twins, simulation) reducing front-end engineering costs and improving project execution monitoring; Saipem references digitisation in strategic materials but no specific revenue impact quantified
- No evidence of disruptive substitution of EPIC services by technology alone; physical construction and installation of subsea assets requires vessels and heavy lift — not automatable [#A]

Execution technology:
- Saipem has proprietary vessel fleet and technology (Castorone pipelayer, FDS2 flexible lay vessel, Saipem 12000 drillship); these are competitive assets, not commodities
- No evidence of competitive displacement by new execution technologies [#I]

Sceptical bullet: The 30% low-carbon target on order intake (€15bn of €50bn) carries significant execution risk — offshore wind market faces cost inflation, project delays, and developer returns pressure in Europe that could reduce FIDs; CCUS is not yet commercially viable at scale; this target may not be achieved. [#J]

§9 — Value chain — Political and regulatory

J-front verdict: Political/regulatory change forces are net neutral-to-positive for Saipem; Russia exit completed without significant write-down; European energy security policy is structurally supportive; Middle East NOC stability is the key swing factor. [#J]

Russia/Arctic LNG 2 exit:
- Saipem had two EPC contracts on Arctic LNG 2 (JV with Ronesans for GBS work; JV with Technip for topside facilities); combined backlog at peak ≈€1.46bn
- Exit completed December 2023; backlog zero'd; no material write-down announced at exit
- Risk removed: no residual Russia sanctions exposure [#I]

Middle East:
- Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar are largest customer concentration areas for Saipem's offshore and energy carrier work
- Middle East conflict (Gaza/Red Sea) monitored by management; H1 2024 conference call management stated business "resilient" to regional disruption — Red Sea routing changes add logistics cost but no project shutdowns
- ADNOC/QatarEnergy/Saudi Aramco continue multi-year capex expansion programmes regardless of near-term regional tensions [#A]

Nigeria:
- Nigeria is a legacy Saipem market (offshore deepwater EPIC); carries execution and local content regulatory risk
- Nigerian local content requirements and security risk are perennial factors, not recent changes [#I]

Kazakhstan:
- Kazakhstan (Kashagan, Tengiz) onshore operations carry political and operational risk; no specific recent incidents in public filings [#I]

European energy security policy:
- Post-Russia gas disruption, EU policy aggressively supports LNG import infrastructure, offshore gas field development, and CCUS — tailwinds for Saipem's Energy Carriers and offshore segments
- EPC contract awards for LNG terminals in Europe and LNG production projects globally are partly policy-driven [#A]

EU regulatory/environmental:
- EU offshore oil and gas regulations broadly stable; no evidence of tightening that materially impairs Saipem's offshore E&C business in the near term
- ESG/sustainability pressure may affect financing costs and investor base over time [#I]

Sceptical bullet: Saipem's heavy Middle East NOC revenue concentration (Saudi Aramco, ADNOC, QatarEnergy collectively represent a disproportionate share of backlog) creates sovereign client concentration risk; any slowdown in NOC capex (oil price shock, political shift) would hit Saipem's order intake acutely. [#J]

§10 — Value chain — Supply side

J-front verdict: Supply side is tight and structurally supportive; vessel scarcity is the defining supply-side dynamic; engineering talent is a local constraint, not a structural bottleneck for Saipem; lease-financed fleet growth creates growing D&A overhang. [#J]

Vessel fleet dynamics:
- Construction vessel supply has not materially expanded since 2012-2014 cycle; no large wave of new lay barges or pipelay vessels being built
- Saipem's fleet is a competitive moat: Castorone (world's largest pipelayer), Saipem 12000, FDS2; proprietary asset base differentiates from asset-light engineering peers
- Fleet expansion occurring via operating leases (charters) rather than vessel purchases; new leases €1.2bn signed in 2025 including extensions; this drives IFRS 16 D&A load [#I]

Fleet utilisation:
- Construction vessels fully booked 2025 and 2026; 50% booked 2027-2028; unprecedented multi-year visibility
- Booking fees collected pre-FID from clients queuing for vessel slots — confirms supply tightness is real, not management spin [#I]

Engineering talent:
- Saipem operates global engineering centres (Italy, Romania, India); large workforce ≈32,000 employees
- Talent competition with oil companies and technology firms is a cost headwind but not cited as a capacity constraint in recent reporting
- HSE performance is a key metric for project execution quality; no material incidents flagged in recent filings [#I]

Equipment supply chains:
- Steel, subsea equipment, umbilicals, risers are key inputs; supply chain normalised post-COVID
- Procurement synergies explicitly identified as a driver of Saipem7 merger cost savings (longer charter periods, improved supplier terms) [#I]

Charter cost inflation:
- Fleet expansion via operating leases locks Saipem into multi-year charter commitments; charter rates in a tight market carry cost risk if day-rates for vessel use exceed original assumptions
- D&A from leases already €1.04bn in 2025; new leases €1.2bn in 2025 alone — growing IFRS 16 balance sheet liability [#A]

Sceptical bullet: ❌ Saipem's fleet growth strategy via operating leases rather than vessel ownership creates a fixed-cost step-up regardless of utilisation; if market turns and vessels sit idle, the lease obligations continue — this is structurally similar (though smaller-scale) to the leverage dynamic that amplified losses in 2021-2022. [#J]

§11 — Revenue/demand cycle

J-front verdict: Offshore oilfield services is in a mid-cycle upcycle with structural elements (energy security LNG, NOC capacity expansion) overlaid on the traditional oil-price-driven cycle; Saipem's backlog provides 2-year revenue visibility, buffering against spot cycle turns. [#J]

Cycle positioning:
- Current cycle began recovering ≈2022 from COVID trough (offshore capex collapsed 2020-2021)
- 2024-2025: offshore capex ≥$300bn/year, approaching but not yet at 2014 peak (when global upstream spend was ≈$700bn total)
- Offshore capex as % of total upstream still recovering; 2024-2025 represents approximately "mid-cycle" rather than peak [#A]

Structural vs cyclical demand drivers:
- Structural (harder to reverse): LNG demand growth post-Russia gas supply shock; European energy security investment; Middle East NOC capacity expansion independent of oil price (Saudi Vision 2030, UAE capacity targets); deepwater new provinces (Guyana, Namibia, East Africa) with competitive field economics
- Cyclical (oil-price dependent): brownfield maintenance spending; some greenfield oil developments; upstream exploration and appraisal
- Saipem bid opportunity pipeline: ≈50% upstream gas, ≈30% upstream oil (tilted brownfield), ≈25% energy transition/downstream — structurally less oil-price-sensitive than headline suggests [#I]

Backlog-to-revenue visibility:
- End-2025 backlog €31.5bn against 2025 revenue €15.5bn = ≈2.0x revenue coverage; multi-year visibility is real
- €12.1bn of end-2024 backlog to be executed in 2025; actual 2025 revenue €15.5bn outperformed backlog-implied figure due to new orders in-year [#I]

Comparison to prior cycle peak (2012-2014):
- 2012-2014: Saipem revenue at peak ≈€13-14bn with EBITDA margins ≈12-14% at peak; current 2025 revenue (€15.5bn) has exceeded prior peak in nominal terms but EBITDA margin (11.1%) has not yet recovered to 2013-2014 levels
- Subsea 7 2024 EBITDA margin of 16% (vs Saipem 9.1% in 2024) illustrates the quality-gap versus a better-positioned peer at similar cycle stage [#A]

Demand reversal triggers:
- Oil price sustained below $60/bbl would cause NOC/major discretionary cuts (particularly greenfield oil)
- Financial distress at a major NOC customer (Angola, Nigeria, Ecuador are most vulnerable)
- Saipem/Subsea 7 merger regulatory block causing management distraction and order intake disruption [#J]

Sceptical bullet: Demand pulses in oilfield services have historically reversed fully to trend within 3-5 years of peak; the current upcycle has been running since 2022 and the 2012-2014 peak was followed by a 5-year capacity trough; if current backlog is cycle-driven, the 2025-2028 EBITDA targets may be calibrated at above-trend demand. [#J]

§12 — Macroeconomic change forces

J-front verdict: Macro forces are broadly supportive in 2025-2026 but contain specific risks: EUR/USD dynamics are driving meaningful financial cost headwinds that suppress EPS; oil price at ~$70-75 is adequate for NOC project continuity but a $60 break would cause visibility to deteriorate; Middle East geopolitical stability is the macro swing factor. [#J]

Oil price sensitivity:
- Backlog provides ≈2-year insulation from spot oil price moves — even a sharp drop today would take 12-18 months to flow through into order intake deceleration
- Bid pipeline ≈50% gas/LNG, ≈25% energy transition/downstream, ≈30% upstream oil brownfield — less oil-price-sensitive than pure upstream exploration-linked businesses
- Customer capex for NOCs (Saudi Aramco, ADNOC, QatarEnergy) is set multi-year; less discretionary than IOC capex [#A]
- Breakeven price for most NOC capacity expansion projects: ≈$40-50/bbl; current oil price well above project economics threshold [#I]

FX dynamics:
- Saipem reports in EUR; revenues predominantly in USD (offshore projects contracted in USD)
- EUR appreciation vs USD creates revenue headwind in EUR-reported terms
- Financial expenses grew €104m in 2025 partly due to "adverse shift in interest rate differential between euro and US dollar" and hedging costs on increased derivatives volume
- This is the structural mechanism behind EPS suppression: USD revenues, EUR cost base, hedging costs elevated by EUR/USD rate volatility [#A]
- EUR strengthening = revenue headwinds and hedging cost increases, further pressuring the EBITDA-to-EPS gap [#J]

Interest rates and customer capex:
- Higher-for-longer global interest rates increase cost of capital for offshore development FIDs; marginal headwind to discretionary project commencements
- LNG and NOC capacity projects typically financed via state/sovereign mechanisms or long-term project finance — insulated from short-term rate changes
- ECB rate cuts in 2024-2025 modestly supportive for Saipem's EUR financing costs [#A]

Middle East geopolitics:
- Red Sea conflict (Houthi attacks since late 2023) added logistics complexity; Saipem operations not directly impacted but project logistics exposed to routing changes
- Saudi-Iran normalisation (March 2023 Beijing agreement) reduced intra-Gulf confrontation risk for medium term
