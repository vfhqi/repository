§6 — Governance Structure + Red Flags

J-front verdict: No acute red flags on audit quality, legal exposure, or accounting — governance is structurally clean at the operational level; the dominant structural concern is the controlling-shareholder override, not accounting or legal risk; net signal mixed (clean operationally, structurally constrained).

Voting rights structure:
- Orange S.A. holds ~50.67% of OPL shares — simple majority control; no dual-class share structure (OPL is a single-class share company)
- Golden share: The Polish State Treasury previously held a special share in OPL with certain veto rights over strategic decisions (legacy of privatisation); current status of golden share and any residual State Treasury rights should be verified in current OPL articles of association — historically this has been a minor but present governance overlay
- Anti-takeover provisions: standard Polish commercial code protections apply; no unusual poison pill or staggered board beyond standard Polish law; however, OPL's single practical defence against a hostile bid is already structural (Orange S.A. won't sell without a premium; a third party can't acquire control without Orange S.A.'s consent)
- ⚡ Controlling shareholder architecture: Orange S.A.'s >50% stake means it can pass ordinary resolutions without any minority support; for extraordinary resolutions (requiring 75% supermajority) it cannot act alone — but in practice Orange S.A. can block any extraordinary resolution even without a majority (it holds enough votes to prevent 75% supermajority from being reached); this creates effective veto power over transformative transactions

Related-party transactions:
- Orange S.A. / OPL have intercompany agreements: (a) brand licence fee paid by OPL to Orange S.A. for use of Orange brand; (b) technology and shared services agreements (network architecture, IT systems, procurement); (c) intercompany roaming/interconnect at arm's length
- Brand licence fee: disclosed in annual report; quantified annually; typically ~PLN 150–200m per annum — represents a real cash transfer from OPL to Orange S.A. minority shareholders bear this
- Arm's-length assessment: OPL discloses that related-party transactions are on market terms; the audit committee is required to review; EY provides an annual opinion — adequate process but inherent conflict in the parent setting the terms
- No evidence of egregious related-party extraction; the brand fee level is within industry norms for subsidiary brand licensing

Audit history:
- Auditor: EY Poland — long-standing engagement (10+ years); no auditor change
- Long auditor tenure risk: >10-year auditor tenure is a standard governance yellow flag (familiarity risk); however, under EU audit reform (mandatory audit rotation for PIEs), OPL is subject to audit rotation rules that should cap tenure — verify current rotation timeline
- No material weaknesses, significant deficiencies, or internal control findings disclosed in L5Y
- No restatements of historical financial statements — clean record

Legal/regulatory issues:
- Competition authority (UOKiK): OPL has faced historical competition proceedings related to market access (wholesale broadband, local loop unbundling); these are standard regulated-incumbent proceedings, not governance red flags; recent years show no major new proceedings
- Regulatory (UKE — Office of Electronic Communications): standard regulatory proceedings on wholesale access pricing, fibre deployment obligations; no extraordinary regulatory exposure
- Data protection : no major GDPR enforcement actions noted; standard compliance posture for a telco
- No class action litigation noted; no SEC/FCA-equivalent investigation (OPL is not a US/UK registrant)
- No whistleblower disclosures or ESG/governance scandal in public record

Whistleblower / governance complaint signals:
- OPL discloses a whistleblower mechanism per Polish Whistleblower Protection Act (2023 implementation); no public governance complaints of note
