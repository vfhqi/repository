2. KEY FINDINGS - VERDICT — Top-3 most-diagnostic questions identified: injectable aesthetics volume/price split in H2 2024→2025, Cetaphil dermo private-label risk, and EBITDA-to-FCF conversion friction are the three questions whose answers most discriminate bull from bear

  • Question 1 (Q2.1) is the load-bearing diagnostic: management's real-time volume/price decomposition in Aesthetic Injectables reveals whether the growth story is unit-volume-driven (durable) or ASP-recovery-driven (fragile to competitive pricing)
  • Question 3.2 is the second-most-diagnostic: the gap between reported adj. EBITDA (~22-23% margin) and reported FCF conversion is large enough that a clean walk of accrual-to-cash will surface either working-capital normalisation or structural leakage
  • Question 5.1 is the third-most-diagnostic: management incentive alignment — specifically whether LTI vesting is tied to organic revenue growth or absolute EPS — predicts whether the M&A pipeline is disciplined or driven by size hunger
  • Key uncertainty not resolvable from public filings: the cannibalization rate between Sculptra and HA fillers within the same customer; publicly reported numbers blend both into "Aesthetic Injectables"; management has not disaggregated
  • Post-IPO overhang still unresolved publicly: EQT/ADIA residual stake sell-down cadence has not been communicated; any M&A funded by equity issuance would be dilutive; management should be pressed on the timeline
  • Dermatology segment disclosure is thin: Cetaphil/Alastin revenue breakdown, DTC vs. pharmacy channel mix, and private-label competitive pressure are not disclosed; this segment is ≈40% of group revenue but receives disproportionately little management airtime
  • Bear-case anchor from public context: sector-level data shows injectable aesthetics procedures declined or softened in US/Europe H2 2023 and into early 2024; Galderma's volume recovery thesis requires end-market normalisation that has not yet been confirmed; questions should test whether this is in-progress or still aspirational
  • Sceptical overlay: management is post-IPO (March 2024 Nasdaq Stockholm listing); incentive to present positively is at its highest; the "rehearsed" fluency risk is acute — see §2 sceptical bullet

3. THEME §1 — Revenue Drivers + Visibility

VERDICT — Revenue-side: organic growth story plausible but requires confirmation that volume (not price) is the primary driver; customer concentration and channel-mix questions are under-answered in current public disclosure

Q1.1 — Volume vs. price decomposition in Aesthetic Injectables

  • Question: "Can you walk us through the Aesthetic Injectables revenue growth in your most recent reported period — specifically the volume vs. price/mix split, and whether HA fillers or toxins are growing faster on a volume basis?"
  • Expected answer: Management likely cites double-digit growth overall, but may resist granular volume/price split — expect high-level "both contributed" framing
  • Good answer signature: Explicit volume-growth number cited (e.g. "X% of Y% total growth was unit-volume"); toxin and filler trajectories given separately
  • Bad answer signature: "Growth was broad-based across the portfolio" with no decomposition; or price/mix defended but volume not specified
  • Follow-up probe: "Specifically, is toxin volume in the US growing above or below the injectable aesthetics market overall, and how does Galderma's channel data support that?"
  • Diagnostic note: Volume-led growth = bull case (durable, market-share-gain story); price-led growth = false-friend (fragile to Evolus/Revance price competition and to end-market softening); ambiguous answer = bear signal on disclosure quality

Q1.2 — Sculptra/PLLA vs. HA filler cannibalization

  • Question: "Is Sculptra growing at the expense of HA filler revenue within the same accounts, or are you seeing genuinely incremental procedure volume per customer?"
  • Expected answer: Management likely claims Sculptra expands the pie (different patient profile, different longevity profile); expect limited hard data
  • Good answer signature: Concrete customer-level data cited (e.g. "accounts that added Sculptra grew HA revenue by X% in the following 12 months") — evidence of incremental rather than substitutive demand
  • Bad answer signature: Qualitative "they serve different patients" with no supporting data; or acknowledgment that some trade-down from HA to Sculptra is occurring in price-sensitive markets
  • Follow-up probe: "Which geography or customer segment shows the clearest incremental volume signal, and which shows the most substitution risk?"
  • Diagnostic note: Incremental = bull (TAM expansion per Galderma's positioning); substitution = bear on HA revenue sustainability; cross-ref ACH matrix — bull hypothesis requires multi-product per-account wallet expansion

Q1.3 — Dermatology segment channel-mix and Cetaphil private-label risk

  • Question: "Within Dermatological Skincare, what portion of Cetaphil revenue is through pharmacy/drugstore vs. e-commerce vs. specialty dermatology channels — and where are you seeing the most private-label competition?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely give high-level geographic diversification framing; may not disaggregate by channel; private-label question may be deflected
  • Good answer signature: Channel split disclosed (even approximately); acknowledgment that pharmacy channel is under pressure in specific markets (e.g. Germany, France) with a clear defensive playbook
  • Bad answer signature: "We compete on efficacy and trust, not price" with no channel-mix data; or silence on where private-label share is gaining
  • Follow-up probe: "In markets where private-label OTC skincare has gained share in the last 24 months, has Cetaphil's retail shelf position (facing, SKU count) been maintained or reduced?"
  • Diagnostic note: Channel-mix data would materially improve revenue-visibility modelling; absence of this data = information risk; private-label answer discriminates between durable-brand-moat (bull) and commoditizing-OTC (bear)

Q1.4 — Geographic revenue visibility and China/EM trajectory

  • Question: "China and EM markets were cited as growth vectors at IPO. Are China aesthetics revenues currently growing, flat, or declining — and what is your visibility on H2 2025 trajectory?"
  • Expected answer: China aesthetics market softened in 2023-2024 on consumer confidence; management likely acknowledges headwind but frames recovery
  • Good answer signature: Honest acknowledgment of softness + specific recovery indicators cited (procedure volumes, practitioner sentiment surveys, channel inventory levels)
  • Bad answer signature: Deflects to "long-term opportunity" without current-state data; or implies recovery without timeline specifics
  • Follow-up probe: "Is the China aesthetic recovery led by tier-1 cities or broadening to tier-2/3, and how does your distribution model differ across those tiers?"
  • Diagnostic note: China recovery timing is a meaningful swing factor on 2025-2026 revenue estimates; management answer here directly tests the "international optionality" bull pillar

Sceptical bullet: A fluent, well-rehearsed answer on revenue visibility — especially post-IPO — is not comfort; it may be the Investor Relations polish that was installed specifically for this meeting; the diagnostic value is in the follow-up probe, not the primary answer; press for numbers, not narratives

4. THEME §2 — Margin Trajectory + Levers

VERDICT — Margin-side: EBITDA margin expansion story (guided toward ~25%+ medium-term) requires validation on gross margin durability, R&D reinvestment adequacy, and FCF conversion quality; multiple moving parts make this the highest-complexity analytical theme

Q2.1 — EBITDA-to-FCF conversion bridge

  • Question: "Your reported adj. EBITDA conversion to FCF appears to involve significant below-the-line items. Can you walk the bridge from EBITDA to FCF — specifically the cash taxes, capex, and working-capital drag?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely cite normalization of working capital, cash tax timing, and investments in commercial infrastructure; expect a high-level bridge without full detail
  • Good answer signature: Full numerical bridge provided (or offered as follow-up); cash tax rate explained with statutory vs. effective reconciliation; working capital identified as normalizing vs. structural
  • Bad answer signature: "Our FCF will improve as EBITDA grows" without a bridge; or deflection to adj. EBITDA as the primary metric with FCF de-emphasised
  • Follow-up probe: "Is the gap between adj. EBITDA and reported FCF primarily timing/normalisation, or are there recurring items (e.g. earn-outs, restructuring cash costs) that should be considered permanent?"
  • Diagnostic note: FCF conversion quality is the single most important financial metric for a 12-24M hold; a management team that can't cleanly walk this bridge is either not yet focused on FCF culture (bad) or has structural leakage they are reluctant to surface (worse)

Q2.2 — Gross margin trajectory by segment

  • Question: "Can you give us a sense of gross margin by segment — Aesthetic Injectables vs. Dermatological Skincare vs. Therapeutic Dermatology — and which segment is driving margin expansion vs. dilution?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely state that Aesthetic Injectables is the highest-margin segment (biologics/injectables typically 60-70%+ gross margin) and that mix toward this segment is the primary expansion driver
  • Good answer signature: Segment-level gross margin ranges disclosed; clear articulation that injectable mix-up is the structural driver with quantified contribution
  • Bad answer signature: "We don't disclose segment-level margins" without any qualitative guidance; or conflation of adj. EBITDA margin with gross margin
  • Follow-up probe: "As Aesthetic Injectables scales, are there COGS items (fill-finish, cold-chain, HA raw material) that create operating leverage, or does unit COGS remain roughly fixed?"
  • Diagnostic note: Mix-driven gross margin expansion is the bull-case engine; if Dermatological Skincare gross margins are under pressure (private label, channel shift), this could offset injectable mix benefit

Q2.3 — R&D adequacy and pipeline reinvestment

  • Question: "R&D spend as a percentage of revenue — where is it now, and is the current level sufficient to sustain the pipeline through the next approval cycle? What is the risk of underinvestment?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely frame R&D as appropriately funded relative to stage-gated pipeline; may cite specific programmes (Riluzole, next-gen toxin, Nemluvio)
  • Good answer signature: Specific R&D percentage cited (and compared to prior year); named programmes with expected inflection points; acknowledgment that post-approval commercial spend will shift absolute amounts but R&D ratio maintained
  • Bad answer signature: R&D "adequately funded" without percentage or programme detail; or heavy emphasis on commercial programmes at the expense of pipeline renewal
  • Follow-up probe: "On Nemluvio (nemolizumab, the anti-IL-31 for prurigo nodularis/atopic dermatitis) — what is the commercial launch spend budget, and how does it compare to your internal IRR threshold for therapeutic launches?"
  • Diagnostic note: R&D adequacy is a latent risk in specialty pharma; underinvestment shows up in the pipeline 3-5 years out; this question tests whether Galderma is managing for current-cycle margins or for sustained franchise value

Q2.4 — Restructuring / cost programme cadence

  • Question: "Are there discrete cost programmes embedded in the current margin bridge — headcount actions, manufacturing consolidations — and what is the timeline for those benefits to fully annualise?"
  • Expected answer: Post-EQT-ownership restructuring benefits largely locked in by IPO; management may cite further operational leverage but no major new programme
  • Good answer signature: Clear articulation of "no major new programme needed — expansion is organic leverage on fixed cost base"; alternatively, if a programme is underway, full P&L impact timeline provided
  • Bad answer signature: Vague reference to "ongoing efficiency initiatives" without specifics; or an implication that current margin trajectory depends on cost actions not yet initiated
  • Follow-up probe: "Is any of the guided EBITDA margin expansion dependent on cost actions vs. pure revenue/mix leverage? What proportion?"
  • Diagnostic note: Distinguishes between structural operating leverage (durable, high-quality) and one-off cost extraction (fragile, pulls forward future years' costs); bear case depends heavily on this split

Sceptical bullet: Margin expansion guided at IPO is the easiest promise to make; the question is whether the bridge is primarily revenue-leverage (management can show clearly) or cost-out (management may have already exhausted; the next leg requires genuine revenue growth); press for the mix between the two

5. THEME §3 — Capital Allocation + Balance Sheet

VERDICT — Capital allocation: post-IPO cash deployment intentions are under-disclosed; M&A appetite vs. leverage reduction vs. return of capital framework needs explicit probing; RONIC discipline is the key discriminator between good and bad management

Q3.1 — M&A philosophy and RONIC discipline

  • Question: "Given you have a stated M&A appetite, what is your internal RONIC hurdle for acquisitions, and how do you think about the build-vs-buy decision in Dermatological Skincare vs. Aesthetic Injectables?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely cite a quantitative hurdle (e.g. WACC + X%) and reference recent deal track record (Alastin acquisition by predecessor); may be vague on specific thresholds
  • Good answer signature: Explicit RONIC/IRR hurdle stated (e.g. ">15% unlevered IRR"); distinction drawn between asset-light bolt-ons (preferred) and platform acquisitions (selective); evidence of discipline in walking away from deals
  • Bad answer signature: "We look at value-creative opportunities as they arise" with no hurdle; or emphasis on strategic fit without financial discipline framing
  • Follow-up probe: "In the last 24 months, has Galderma passed on any acquisition opportunities because they didn't clear your financial hurdle? What category were those deals in?"
  • Diagnostic note: Passing on deals is the single best evidence of RONIC discipline; management that can cite specific walk-aways is credible; management that can't likely overpays

Q3.2 — Leverage target and de-leveraging trajectory

  • Question: "What is your target net debt / EBITDA range, and what is the timeline to reach that target? Does M&A compete with de-leveraging for free cash flow allocation?"
  • Expected answer: Galderma was heavily leveraged pre-IPO (EQT LBO vintage); IPO reduced leverage; management likely targets ~2x or below; tension between M&A and de-leveraging is real
  • Good answer signature: Explicit leverage target stated with timeline; clear priority hierarchy (e.g. "de-lever to X first, then M&A/returns") — evidence of financial policy, not just ambition
  • Bad answer signature: "We are comfortable with our balance sheet" without a target or timeline; or framing that implies M&A and de-leveraging can both be prioritised simultaneously without tension
  • Follow-up probe: "If an attractive acquisition arose in the next 12 months at 3x revenue, would you fund it with cash, debt, or equity — and what would trigger an equity issuance?"
  • Diagnostic note: Equity issuance risk is the single most underappreciated risk for recent IPO stocks; a management answer that explicitly rules out equity issuance at current prices is highly informative; an equivocal answer is a yellow flag

Q3.3 — Working capital management and cash conversion cycle

  • Question: "Where is the working capital cycle currently — DSO, DPO, inventory days — and is there a normalisation programme underway, or is the current level structural?"
  • Expected answer: Post-private-equity ownership working capital is often stretched on payables; management may guide to normalization; channel inventory in aesthetics (distributor-held) is a specific risk
  • Good answer signature: Specific DSO/DPO/inventory day figures cited; management frames the current level as at-target or provides a roadmap to target with timeline and cash-release quantum
  • Bad answer signature: "Working capital is well-managed" without metrics; or an implication that the current FCF conversion gap vs. EBITDA is "timing" without a specific normalisation timeline
  • Follow-up probe: "Is there any risk that distributor channel inventory in Aesthetic Injectables (particularly in the US) is elevated vs. underlying sellout — and how do you monitor that in real time?"
  • Diagnostic note: Channel inventory risk is the most common source of revenue-recognition timing risk in aesthetics/specialty pharma; the bear case involves a de-stocking quarter that hits reported revenue hard

Sceptical bullet: Capital allocation discipline is most visible in what management has NOT done (acquisitions passed on, buybacks not done at cyclical highs, leverage not re-levered); asking about past decisions is more diagnostic than asking about future plans

6. THEME §4 — Strategy + Competitive Position

VERDICT — Competitive position: Aesthetic Injectables moat is real but contested (Allergan/AbbVie on toxins, Evolus on pricing, Merz on fillers); Therapeutic Dermatology differentiation through Nemluvio is the highest-optionality strategic call but also the highest-uncertainty execution item

Q4.1 — Competitive response to Evolus on pricing

  • Question: "Evolus is competing aggressively on price in neuromodulators. Has this changed your pricing strategy in Dysport in any market, and has it affected your gross margin on the toxin franchise?"
  • Expected answer: Management likely frames Dysport as differentiated on efficacy/onset and frames Evolus as targeting the value segment; may acknowledge some pricing pressure in specific markets
  • Good answer signature: Honest acknowledgment of pricing pressure in specific geographies; evidence of proactive response (e.g. loyalty programme, practitioner training investment) that defends volume at maintained price rather than matching on price
  • Bad answer signature: "Dysport competes on science, not price" — a rehearsed non-answer that doesn't engage with market reality; or silence on where pricing pressure is most acute
  • Follow-up probe: "In the last reported quarter, was Dysport US volume growth above or below the neuromodulator market growth rate? What does that tell us about share trends?"
  • Diagnostic note: Dysport share trends vs. market are the most precise indicator of competitive position quality; a company losing share even in a growing market is a false friend

Q4.2 — Nemluvio commercial launch strategy and IRR expectations

  • Question: "Nemluvio is a significant investment. What is the commercial launch spending budget for 2025-2026, what patient population do you expect to reach in year 1, and what is the internal revenue ramp assumption?"
  • Expected answer: Management will frame Nemluvio as a transformational opportunity in prurigo nodularis and atopic dermatitis; launch spend will likely be framed as a significant P&L headwind to EBITDA in the near term
  • Good answer signature: Specific launch spend quantified; year-1 patient/revenue ramp with sensitivity assumptions disclosed; comparison to competitor launches (Dupixent trajectory as a precedent) acknowledged
  • Bad answer signature: "We are excited about the launch potential" without financial specifics; or an implication that launch costs are already "in the guidance" without quantifying the headwind
  • Follow-up probe: "Is Nemluvio's IRR case dependent on the atopic dermatitis indication, or does prurigo nodularis alone justify the investment? What share of peak sales comes from which indication?"
  • Diagnostic note: Nemluvio is the single largest swing factor on 5Y revenue trajectory; management's willingness to put a financial framework around it is a test of execution culture vs. hope culture

Q4.3 — Moat durability in Aesthetic Injectables vs. biosimilar/competitor risk

  • Question: "Botulinum toxin biosimilars are a recurring debate. What is your honest assessment of the 5-10 year biosimilar risk to Dysport's pricing power, and what is your IP/regulatory moat?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely cite the complexity of botulinum toxin manufacturing, the patient-safety regulatory pathway, and the practitioner brand loyalty as durable moats
  • Good answer signature: Specific patents/regulatory exclusivities cited with expiry dates; manufacturing complexity articulated with reference to competitor failures; acknowledgment of some risk at the margin with quantified pricing sensitivity
  • Bad answer signature: Unqualified "biosimilars are not a meaningful risk" — unverifiable and often wrong in specialty pharma; or deflection to "we have a strong brand" without IP-specific anchoring
  • Follow-up probe: "What is the earliest date at which a head-to-head Dysport biosimilar could receive regulatory approval in the EU and US — and what does your modelling assume for the pricing impact?"
  • Diagnostic note: Long-term pricing power is the core bull-case assumption; any crack in this answer weakens the long-term margin expansion story materially

Sceptical bullet: Strategic questions invite the most rehearsed, polished answers; the most diagnostic moment is when management is asked about a specific competitor move or a specific market-level data point they hadn't anticipated — a clean, specific answer to an unscripted question is the best quality signal

7. THEME §5 — Management Quality + Operator Track Record

VERDICT — Management quality: Flemming Ørnskov as CEO is a specialist pharma operator with a measurable track record (Shire); the key question is whether Galderma under his tenure is being managed for sustainable franchise value or for near-term IPO-window earnings quality; incentive structure is the diagnostic

Q5.1 — LTI/compensation structure and vesting metrics

  • Question: "What metrics drive your long-term incentive plan — is it EPS, organic revenue growth, EBITDA margin, or total shareholder return? What is the vesting cliff and look-back period?"
  • Expected answer: Post-IPO LTI likely includes a combination of relative TSR and absolute financial metrics; management may deflect to "competitive" without specifics
  • Good answer signature: Explicit metrics stated; revenue growth and/or ROIC included (not just absolute EBITDA); vesting period of ≥3 years confirmed; management acknowledges what they'd sacrifice in the short term to protect long-term metrics
  • Bad answer signature: LTI primarily tied to EBITDA margin or EPS without a growth or ROIC component — incentivises cost extraction over investment; or refusal to discuss compensation structure in investor meeting
  • Follow-up probe: "If Nemluvio launch spending created a 200bps EBITDA headwind in 2025-2026, would your LTI structure reward or penalise management for making that investment — and what does that tell you about the right behaviour?"
  • Diagnostic note (Power of incentives — Concept A): This is the single most predictive question in the memo; LTI metrics predict behaviour more reliably than any strategy presentation; an LTI tied to absolute EBITDA penalises pipeline investment and predicts under-reinvestment

Q5.2 — Sandbagging discipline and guidance track record

  • Question: "Over the last 3-4 reported periods, has Galderma's guidance been conservative-to-actuals (sandbagged) or optimistic-to-actuals, and how do you think about the guidance philosophy?"
  • Expected answer: Post-IPO companies often guide conservatively initially; management may frame their approach as "committing to what we can see" — expect credibility framing
  • Good answer signature: Evidence of consistent beat-and-raise cadence; management articulates a philosophy of under-promising and over-delivering with specific reference to a prior period where actuals beat guidance
  • Bad answer signature: Mixed guidance history with misses explained by macro; or implication that guidance will be raised when "visibility improves" — this is not guidance discipline, it's retrospective framing
  • Follow-up probe: "In the most recent period where actuals diverged from guidance — which line diverged most, and was that variance within your control or driven by external factors?"
  • Diagnostic note: Guidance discipline is the most reliable proxy for management's epistemic humility and operational control system quality; a management that can't explain a miss cleanly doesn't have tight operational visibility

Q5.3 — Crisis playbook and decision under uncertainty

  • Question: "What is the most significant operational or commercial problem Galderma faced in the last 18 months that was not visible externally — how did you find out about it, and how was it resolved?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely have a prepared answer on supply chain or regulatory timing; the value is in the texture of the response, not the surface content
  • Good answer signature: Specific incident described with clear causality, a defined decision process, and a verifiable outcome; demonstrates that management has operational visibility and escalation processes that work
  • Bad answer signature: A generic "we navigate challenges well" framing; or a shift to macro headwinds (external attribution) rather than internal resolution (internal accountability)
  • Follow-up probe: "What process change did you implement as a result — and how would you know if a similar issue was developing now?"
  • Diagnostic note (Animal-CEO probe): The quality of the answer to this question reveals whether management has a crisis playbook (operator archetype) or manages by narrative (communicator archetype); investor needs an operator at this stage

Sceptical bullet: Management quality questions are the most susceptible to social desirability bias — a confident, self-aware answer to "what went wrong" is exactly what good IR coaching produces; the real test is the follow-up probe, where the prepared answer runs out

8. THEME §6 — Risk + Downside (Pre-Mortem Cross-Reference)

VERDICT — Downside: bear case centres on three failure modes — injectable aesthetics volume stalls on end-market softness, Nemluvio underperforms at launch, and FCF conversion disappoints vs. guided EBITDA trajectory; all three are testable in management meeting

Q6.1 — End-market softness and procedure volume monitoring

  • Question: "If injectable aesthetics procedure volumes in the US and Europe were declining on a like-for-like basis right now — how would you know, and what leading indicators do you monitor in real time?"
  • Expected answer: Management will cite practitioner surveys, Allergan market data, distributor sell-out reports; may reference their own account-level data
  • Good answer signature: Specific leading indicators cited with monitoring frequency; acknowledgment that there is a lag between procedure volumes and shipped product revenue; discussion of current trends on those indicators
  • Bad answer signature: "We monitor the market carefully" without specifying what data; or deflection to the long-term structural growth trend without addressing current-quarter visibility
  • Follow-up probe: "Are any of those leading indicators currently flashing amber — and if so, in which geography or product category?"
  • Diagnostic note (Failure mode test): This question directly tests the primary bear-case scenario; a management team that has specific, current data on this question is well-controlled; one that deflects likely doesn't have real-time commercial visibility — itself a risk

Q6.2 — Nemluvio launch risk and competitor response

  • Question: "What is your base-case assumption for Dupixent's competitive response to Nemluvio in atopic dermatitis — and what would a bad launch outcome look like in Year 1?"
  • Expected answer: Management will frame Nemluvio as differentiated on pruritis/itch mechanism (IL-31) vs. Dupixent (IL-4/13); may cite Phase III data superiority on specific endpoints
  • Good answer signature: Specific differentiation articulated at the mechanism level; acknowledgment that Dupixent has a massive installed base and payer contracts; transparent discussion of what share Nemluvio needs to reach breakeven vs. NPV-positive
  • Bad answer signature: "Our clinical data speaks for itself" with no competitive response modelling; or dismissal of Dupixent competition on grounds that markets don't overlap
  • Follow-up probe: "If Nemluvio achieves 20% of your base-case Year 3 revenue assumption, what is the impact on group EBITDA margin guidance, and is that contemplated in your financial planning?"
  • Diagnostic note (Bear-case probe): Nemluvio disappointment is the single highest-impact bear-case event; management's willingness to quantify the downside scenario demonstrates financial discipline; unwillingness is either overconfidence or incomplete modelling

Q6.3 — Regulatory and pricing risk in Therapeutic Dermatology

  • Question: "What is the net pricing risk for Therapeutic Dermatology in the EU — specifically on reference pricing, IRA spillover effects, and government reimbursement decisions in Germany and France?"
  • Expected answer: Management will likely cite formulary wins and note that therapeutic derm products are specialty/biologic-category and less exposed to simple reference pricing than generics
  • Good answer signature: Specific pricing decisions cited (e.g. AMNOG outcome in Germany for specific product); acknowledgment of IRA indirect effect on negotiation dynamics in Europe; stated confidence with specific evidence
  • Bad answer signature: "We operate in the specialty space and pricing is well-protected" — often said and often wrong in European pharma; or deflection to US pricing as more relevant
  • Follow-up probe: "In your last AMNOG or HAS submission, what was the outcome vs. your internal assumption — and what did you learn from that process?"
  • Diagnostic note (Regulatory exposure): European specialty pharma pricing is the most underappreciated risk in the Galderma thesis given the market does not assign this risk specifically; a clean, specific answer is reassuring; a vague answer is a flag

Q6.4 — EQT/ADIA stake overhang and equity issuance

⚡ [Outlier detection — pre-empts a risk the consensus investor meeting doesn't typically probe directly]

  • Question: "EQT and ADIA retain significant stakes post-IPO. What is the lock-up expiry timeline, and is there any coordination mechanism between Galderma management and existing shareholders on the sell-down pace?"
  • Expected answer: Lock-up periods post-March 2024 IPO will have expired or be expiring; management will likely defer to shareholders on their own decisions; may note that secondary sell-downs are managed through bookbuilds
  • Good answer signature: Transparent acknowledgment of residual stake sizes; confirmation that management does not coordinate or have information advantage on sell-down timing; statement that the company would not issue primary equity to fund acquisitions below a price that management considers undervalued
  • Bad answer signature: "That's a question for our shareholders" without any management perspective on overhang management; or an implication that management is indifferent to the impact of large secondary blocks on the share price and cost of capital
  • Follow-up probe: "Has any secondary sell-down to date required the company to allocate management time to investor education / roadshow support — and what is the opportunity cost of that on operational focus?"
  • Diagnostic note (False-friend probe): Overhang is the most underappreciated tactical risk in post-PE IPO stocks; management's answer reveals whether they view their job as running the business or managing the stock price — ideally both, but the priority ordering matters

Sceptical bullet — ACH matrix gaps not covered: The Q19 pre-mortem ACH matrix items not addressed in this memo's questions include (a) manufacturing capacity constraint risk if Aesthetic Injectables demand re-accelerates faster than fill-finish capacity can scale, (b) FX translation risk given CHF reporting base with USD/EUR revenue mix, and (c) key-person risk on the scientific/medical affairs leadership specifically within the Therapeutic Dermatology pipeline team — these should be added if the meeting includes the CFO and time permits

9. CROSS-CUTTING META + NON-OBVIOUS QUESTIONS

VERDICT — Non-obvious angles: the most-diagnostic questions are those that invite candid acknowledgment of a specific near-term negative rather than a rehearsed strategic positive; management that can do this cleanly is high-quality; management that cannot is a yellow flag

Most-diagnostic questions (ranked)

  • RANKED #1 — Q5.1 (LTI metrics): The answer predicts management behaviour more reliably than any strategy question; an LTI structure that punishes near-term EBITDA compression for the sake of pipeline investment is the best predictor of under-reinvestment
  • RANKED #2 — Q2.1 (EBITDA-FCF bridge): A management team that cannot cleanly walk this bridge in an investor meeting either lacks financial controls or is obscuring something; either outcome is diagnostic
  • RANKED #3 — Q6.2 (Nemluvio downside scenario): Management's ability to quantify a bear case for their own flagship launch reveals whether they have scenario planning culture or hope culture

Non-obvious angles (WH Smith probe pattern)

⚡ [Outlier — unconventional question designed to surface what consensus wouldn't ask]

  • Non-obvious angle — the "worst practitioner feedback" probe: "What is the most negative piece of practitioner feedback on Dysport or Sculptra that you received in the last 6 months — not from KOLs, but from a typical injecting nurse or aesthetic doctor in a tier-2 city?"
  • Why this matters: KOL feedback is curated; typical practitioner feedback is the ground truth on product-market fit; a management team that can answer this specifically has real commercial intelligence; one that deflects to KOL surveys has a top-down intelligence problem
  • Good answer: A specific, unglamorous piece of feedback (e.g. "nurse injectors in Birmingham find the reconstitution process for Sculptra more time-consuming than HA fillers, which limits adoption in high-volume clinics") — this is signal
