§8 — Quality of Earnings & Accounting

Net signal: Earnings quality is structurally high for GET-FR — cash conversion is excellent, NWC requirements are minimal, and the core toll revenue model has essentially no working capital dynamics; the primary quality question is maintenance capex adequacy and the gap between statutory depreciation and economic capital consumption [#J]

  • NWC days: Not a material metric for GET-FR; toll infrastructure collects cash at point of service with minimal receivables or inventory; NWC is structurally near-zero relative to peers [#A]

  • Peer norms: contrast with manufacturing or B2B service peers where NWC days of 30-60+ are typical; GET-FR's near-zero NWC is a quality positive

  • Cash conversion: OCF conversion from EBITDA is high — estimated at 80-90% in normal years; the primary leakage is maintenance capex and interest payments [#A]

  • Free cash flow yield (FCF / market cap) is a more useful earnings-quality metric than EPS for GET-FR given the D&A structure

  • OCF vs EPS: Reported EPS is depressed below economic earnings by the D&A amortisation charge (approximately €200-220m/year) on a concession asset valued at original construction cost; "cash EPS" or "FCF per share" more accurately reflects distributable earnings [#A]

  • Investors who rely solely on reported P/E will misread the quality of GET-FR's earnings; FCF-based valuation is more appropriate

  • One-offs: GET-FR has historically included COVID-related items, refinancing costs, and regulatory settlement charges as exceptional; the frequency of "exceptional" items warrants scrutiny [#A]

  • Adjustments: the gap between reported and adjusted EPS is material in some years; investors should test whether the adjustments are truly non-recurring or represent ongoing operational costs

  • Non-GAAP gap: Management's preferred metrics (EBITDA, "free cash flow") exclude D&A, interest, and some exceptional items; the resulting non-GAAP presentation makes the business look more profitable on a cash basis than GAAP EPS suggests [#A]

  • This is structurally appropriate for a concession business but creates a risk that investors anchor on a non-GAAP metric that could be selectively presented

  • Inventory days / Receivables days: Not applicable in the traditional sense; toll revenues are collected immediately; no material receivables aging or inventory risk [#I]

  • Maintenance capex: The key quality risk is whether maintenance capex is sufficient to keep the tunnel's infrastructure in optimal condition; the Channel Tunnel's ventilation, electrical, and track systems require ongoing significant investment [#A]

  • If management has deferred maintenance (capitalising costs that should be expensed, or delaying investment cycles), free cash flow metrics would be inflated; this risk is not publicly flagged but is structurally present in any long-life infrastructure asset

  • Concession clock: The 2086 concession expiry means the asset will eventually be returned to the bi-national authorities; this is a 60-year horizon and does not affect near-term earnings quality, but it does mean terminal value in a DCF is effectively zero [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on earnings quality is that "cash EPS" and "free cash flow" presentations systematically understate the true economic cost of maintaining a 50km sub-sea tunnel — a uniquely challenging and expensive infrastructure asset; periodic major maintenance cycles (the last major refurbishment was post-2008 fire damage) could create step-change capex requirements that would materially reduce the FCF profile; management's smoothing of these costs in normalised capex guidance may be masking lumpy future capital requirements

  • In this scenario, total returns are dominated by the FCF yield (~4-5%) plus modest growth; the 25%+ IRR threshold is unachievable without a strategic event

  • ❌ Eurostar risk: Eurostar's passenger recovery remains approximately 10-15% below 2019 peak; GET-FR has no operational lever to accelerate this, and Eurostar has a history of operational disruptions (train failures, Brexit transition friction, infrastructure incidents)

  • Early warning signals forming: The most recent data shows revenue growth decelerating toward low-single digits AND consensus being modestly revised down — both early warning indicators of a growth story transitioning to an ex-growth story [#A]

  • The combination of slowing growth + stable margins + modest downward revisions is the fingerprint of a "steady-state compounding" story, not a re-rating catalyst story
