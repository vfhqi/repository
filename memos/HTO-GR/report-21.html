§3 — Landscape Dimension: When Q21 Is and Is Not the Right Tool

J-front verdict: Q21 is the correct tool for exploratory, evidence-gathering landscape mapping; it is the wrong tool when the analyst already has a strong prior and needs a verdict

  • Q21 vs Q22 differentiation: Q21 answers "what is the landscape?" — Q22 answers "given the landscape, what is the judgement?" — the distinction matters because Q21 should not render a verdict; it maps
  • Appropriate Q21 triggers: High uncertainty on a single load-bearing RA or CQ after ESA completion; contradictory signals from multiple sources that require landscape-wide synthesis; a new external development (regulatory, competitive, technological) that may invalidate a prior pillar grade
  • Inappropriate Q21 triggers: Questions where the analyst already has high-confidence evidence from ≥3 independent sources; questions that are not on the critical path for a position-size or hold/sell decision; questions better addressed by a Q22 (ACH-disciplined) run
  • Stage fit: Q21 can technically run at any stage (IG / Triaging / ESA / DD) but is optimally deployed at DD stage where the remaining uncertainty holes are specific and well-defined
  • Downstream use: APM uses Q21 evidence to inform pillar-level grading — Q21 output lands on the Research Stages dashboard in one of three ad-hoc KQ slots; it is not a standalone investment memo
  • Breadth-first design: The "5L SS breadth gate ≥40%" Hard requirement signals that Q21 is explicitly designed to force the analyst outside their anchor sources — it is a breadth tool, not a depth tool
  • Expert call integration: The [AS] version's sell-side breadth mandate (≥Y_MIN distinct brokers from the Z-broker pool) means Q21 is also an expert-call synthesis tool — it surfaces on-the-ground evidence that public filings cannot provide
  • Non-consensus obligation: The Concept A instruction embedded in every §2-§N section means Q21 has a built-in obligation to go beyond the consensus view — it is not a consensus summary tool
  • Sceptical read: The risk of over-using Q21 is real — commissioning a full dual-source Q21 on a question that's not genuinely load-bearing wastes analytical capacity; the APM should gate-keep KQ selection before commissioning the run

§4 — Landscape Dimension: Confidence Classification Methodology

J-front verdict: The section-level confidence classification table is the most undervalued component of the Q21 framework; it makes epistemic quality explicit rather than hiding it in hedging language

  • Three-tier system: High / Medium / Low confidence per section — not grades, not percentages, not shorthand; the full phrasing is "high plausibility / medium plausibility / low plausibility" at the aggregate level
  • Anchor requirement: Every confidence level must be anchored to a ≤25w evidence summary — the analyst cannot simply assert high confidence; they must identify what evidence supports it
  • Table format: The confidence classification table (Section | Dimension | Confidence | Anchor) forces the analyst to review every section simultaneously, which surfaces inconsistencies that section-by-section reading misses
  • Aggregate verdict logic: The aggregate confidence verdict synthesises all section-level verdicts; it should not simply average them — a single Low-confidence section on a load-bearing dimension can dominate the aggregate even if all other sections are High
  • Epistemic function: The confidence classification serves downstream APM grading — if a dimension has Low confidence, the APM cannot assign a high-conviction pillar grade without additional research; the table is therefore a to-do list as much as a summary
  • Concept B application: Where two sections surface contradictory confidence levels on related dimensions (e.g. high confidence on market structure but low confidence on competitive dynamics), the contradiction is itself signal — the analyst should name the elephant
  • Source quality weighting: Expert calls (AS proprietary) typically support higher confidence than public filings alone — the [C] version will structurally tend toward Medium confidence on dimensions where expert-call evidence is absent
  • Historical precedent dimension: Q21 instructions list "historical precedent" as a canonical dimension — evidence from historical analogues typically warrants only Medium confidence because analogues are never exact
  • Sceptical read: The confidence classification system can become a rubber-stamp if the analyst assigns High confidence to underpinned claims out of narrative convenience; the sceptical reader should specifically interrogate the High-confidence anchors

§5 — Landscape Dimension: Data Gaps and Second/Third-Order Implications

J-front verdict: The data gaps and second/third-order implications sections are the highest alpha-generative components of Q21; they are also the most frequently under-populated in practice

  • Data gap taxonomy: Critical / Material / Minor / Resolvable via / Unresolvable from desk / Expert call would resolve / Field visit would resolve / Wait-and-monitor — the eight-tier taxonomy forces the analyst to distinguish between gaps that can be closed now versus gaps that require different methods or longer time horizons
  • Critical gap definition: A Critical data gap is one that, if it resolved differently than assumed, would change the pillar grade and potentially the position-size decision — the analyst should have no more than 2-3 Critical gaps after a DD-stage Q21 run; more than that implies the DD is not yet complete
  • Wait-and-monitor category: The Wait-and-monitor gap type is particularly important for the 12-24 month holding period audience — some uncertainty is irreducible at time of investment and must be tracked via monitoring rather than research
  • Sceptical bullet on data gaps (standalone): The single most important sceptical question on data gaps is whether the analyst is failing to look in the right places (Concept B CRITICAL ACTION 1 — go to the most-different source) rather than genuinely facing thin evidence; the distinction matters because "no evidence" and "evidence I haven't found yet" are very different epistemic states
  • Second-order implications: The Concept A edge-detection mandate (§N+3) requires ≥8-12 parent bullets and ≥2-3 M/M/O (Means/Motive/Opportunity) applications — this is a hard minimum, not a guideline
  • M/M/O test application: The M/M/O test asks: does any actor (competitor, regulator, supplier, customer, management team) have the means, motive, and opportunity to drive the KQ toward the non-consensus resolution? This is the primary tool for surfacing second-order risks that are not visible in the consensus narrative
  • Cross-pillar implications: Second-order implications should explicitly cross pillar boundaries — a KQ on competitive dynamics may have third-order implications for balance sheet risk or management credibility; the analyst should map these connections
  • Third-order implications: Third-order effects are typically the most underweighted in standard equity research — they are the implications of the implications; the Marcus Aurelius "obstacle is the way" framing means the analyst should explicitly surface the implication they are most tempted to flinch from
  • Inversion signpost: The Inversion signpost (canonical vocabulary for §N+3) is a specific instruction to apply inversion thinking — "what would have to be true for the non-consensus resolution to be correct?" — this is the most efficient antidote to confirmation bias
  • ⚡ markers in second/third-order section: The ≤3 ⚡ markers per memo rule means the analyst should reserve ⚡ flags for genuinely outlier implications, not merely interesting ones; the scarcity of ⚡ is what gives it signal value
  • Confidence: Medium confidence on this section — anchor: the section instructions are explicit but the quality of execution depends entirely on analyst willingness to apply genuine inversion thinking rather than superficial edge-case generation

Sceptical bullet: The second/third-order implications section is the most intellectually demanding part of Q21 and therefore the most vulnerable to superficial completion — if the M/M/O bullets all point to risks the analyst is already aware of and comfortable with, the edge-detection has not been done properly
