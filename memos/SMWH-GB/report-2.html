Business Strategy and Operational Performance
Overarching Strategy: A Pure-Play Global Travel Retailer

The Group officially completed its transformation into a pure-play global travel retailer following the strategic sale of its High Street and funkypigeon.com businesses. The company's overarching business strategy is now driven by four fundamental pillars: securing new space growth, increasing average transaction value , developing adjacent product categories, and maintaining disciplined cost and cash management. A central element of this strategy is the rapid rollout of the one-stop-shop format, which replaces the traditional news, books, and convenience model with a unified destination for all travel essentials.

UK Division: One-Stop-Shop Rollout and Category Development

The UK division remains the operational cornerstone of the Group, generating £392 million in total revenue during the first half of fiscal 2026. Within the Air segment, the company executed its largest-ever store development program, which temporarily disrupted trading but culminated in the successful launch of flagship one-stop-shops across Heathrow Terminals 3, 4, and 5. These high-density flagship locations showcase the complete Travel Essentials proposition, setting a new global benchmark by incorporating extensive food-to-go ranges and full instore pharmacies.

Beyond the Air channel, the multi-format Hospital segment delivered a strong 8% revenue increase in H1 2026. This hospital growth was driven by tailored partnerships with M&S Food and Costa Coffee, alongside the deployment of the internal Smith's Family Kitchen brand. The Rail segment also demonstrated format evolution with the introduction of a new one-stop-shop at London Bridge station, integrating a dedicated coffee and breakfast proposition to maximize customer convenience.

Category development continues to be a major operational catalyst across the UK estate. The Health & Beauty category has driven substantial ATV growth, highlighted by the recent launch of the company's first own-brand product range under the 'Roame' label. Furthermore, the food-to-go category was significantly enhanced through the Smith's Family Kitchen brand, which expanded its meal deal offerings to 44 main options and introduced entirely new seasonal and bakery ranges.

North America: Travel Essentials Expansion and Remediation

In North America, the strategic focus has aggressively shifted toward the core Travel Essentials business, which now accounts for over 55% of the division's revenue. This highly profitable segment generated 22% total revenue growth on a constant currency basis in H1 2026, driven by new store openings and increased spend per passenger. The operational rollout of the one-stop-shop concept is advancing in the US through new marketplace formats, such as the Duke City Station at Albuquerque airport and the East Bank Market at Portland airport.

Conversely, the Group is actively rationalizing its underperforming North American portfolios. The InMotion brand is currently undergoing a strategic review, with future store openings now strictly limited to strategically important tender packages. Additionally, the Resorts business is simplifying its footprint by permanently exiting unprofitable fashion and specialty stores across Las Vegas.

Operationally, the North American division is undergoing a comprehensive remediation plan following an independent review by Deloitte, which identified overstated supplier income and a target-driven culture lacking adequate financial oversight. In response, the Group appointed a new regional CEO, implemented global accounting policies, and embedded rigorous financial reporting controls into month-end processes. The company is also accelerating the implementation of a new supplier income management system and is fully cooperating with an ongoing investigation by the Financial Conduct Authority .

Rest of the World: Shifting to a Franchise-Led Model

The Rest of the World division is executing a strategic pivot toward a less capital-intensive, franchise-led operating model. To optimize profitability, the Group is systematically reducing its exposure to sub-scale markets, highlighted by the closure of four uneconomic stores at Dusseldorf airport and a complete exit from its Norwegian business.

Future investments are being strictly targeted at core markets where the brand already possesses operational scale, primarily Ireland, Spain, and Australia. Operational momentum in these core territories includes the introduction of the successful one-stop-shop format to Dublin, Cork, and Shannon airports. Meanwhile, the division expanded its franchise footprint with new store openings in Malaysia, the Philippines, and Saudi Arabia. Despite facing inflationary pressures on staff and logistics costs that resulted in an H1 2026 trading loss of £4 million, the strategic adjustments helped the ROW division achieve a constant currency revenue growth of 8%.

Financial Performance and Capital Allocation
Recent Financial Results and Revenue Growth

WH Smith's recent financial performance reflects a transitional period marked by solid top-line growth but pressured profitability. Building on the fiscal 2025 results where the Group achieved total revenue of £1,553 million, the first half of fiscal 2026 continued to demonstrate the resilience of the travel segment. During H1 2026, total Group revenue increased by 5% year-over-year to £748 million. This growth was supported by a 2% increase in like-for-like revenue across the global estate. By division, total revenue in the UK grew by 2% to £392 million, North America expanded by 10% on a constant currency basis to £204 million, and the Rest of the World division increased by 8% to £152 million.

Despite the positive revenue trajectory, profitability in the first half was heavily impacted by planned investments and external headwinds. Headline Group trading profit for H1 2026 declined to £32 million, down from £47 million in the prior-year period. Correspondingly, Headline Group profit before tax and non-underlying items fell to £3 million, compared to £21 million in H1 2025. The Group anticipates full-year FY26 Headline profit before tax to land between £90 million and £105 million. This lowered guidance incorporates a more cautious outlook due to geopolitical uncertainties in the Middle East, which have driven flight disruptions and a softer consumer confidence environment.

Margins, Cost Inflation, and Mitigation Efforts

Margin compression has been a prominent feature of the current financial year, necessitating robust cost management strategies. The business is currently facing sustained inflationary pressures of approximately 4% to 5% across most major cost lines, including staff wages, logistics, and supply chain expenses. In the UK, the H1 2026 Headline trading profit decreased by £6 million to £34 million, reflecting these inflation headwinds combined with the temporary trading disruption from extensive store refurbishments. North America delivered a trading profit of £2 million, while the ROW division posted a loss of £4 million due to location mix and logistics inflation. For the full year, management anticipates Headline trading profit margins of approximately 13% to 14% in the UK, 7% in North America, and 4% in the ROW division.

To defend profitability against these structural cost increases, the Group has accelerated several operational efficiency and transformation programs. Capital Allocation and Dividend Suspension

In response to near-term financial pressures and the need to fund significant growth initiatives, the Board has aggressively rebalanced the Group's capital allocation framework. The immediate strategic priority has shifted entirely toward strengthening the balance sheet, maintaining strict cash control, and driving debt reduction. As a necessary first step in this defensive posture, management made the prudent decision to suspend the dividend. The Board intends to review the reinstatement of shareholder returns only when the business achieves an appropriate level of surplus cash and leverage targets are met.

Capex for the Store Development Program

Despite suspending the dividend, WH Smith continues to heavily prioritize its "invest to grow" mandate through disciplined capital expenditure. The company is currently executing its largest-ever store development program, which requires significant upfront capital to secure premium, high-returning space. Total capital expenditure for FY26 is guided at approximately £90 million. Around 30% of this total annual CapEx is heavily concentrated on flagship developments at major global hubs, including Heathrow, JFK, and Orlando airports. During H1 2026, the Group invested £50 million in capital expenditure, up from £38 million in the prior year, directly supporting the opening of 36 new global stores and extensive IT upgrades.

Cash Generation, Debt Reduction, and Leverage Targets

The Group's underlying operations remain inherently cash-generative, evidenced by a Headline EBITDA of £48 million in H1 2026. However, the intensive capital investment cycle and seasonal working capital requirements resulted in a free cash outflow of £61 million for the period. The £54 million working capital outflow was largely anticipated, driven by new store openings, business seasonality, and a one-off payables timing headwind linked to a major franchise partner.

Consequently, Headline net debt expanded to £496 million at the end of February 2026, up from £390 million at the end of August 2025. This pushed the Group's leverage ratio up to 2.9x Headline EBITDA. A core pillar of the revised capital allocation strategy is to rapidly reduce this leverage position to below 2.0x. Subject to investment timing, management targets a reduction in Headline net debt to approximately £420 million by the end of FY26. To ensure robust liquidity during this deleveraging phase, the Group successfully smoothed its debt maturity profile. The financing structure now relies on a diversified stack, including a £400 million revolving credit facility maturing in 2030, a £327 million convertible bond due in May 2026, £200 million in US Private Placement notes, and a £120 million extended term loan maturing in March 2029.

ESG, Sustainability, and Corporate Governance

WH Smith considers environmental and social sustainability a fundamental component of its long-term success and strategic risk management. The Group's efforts are externally recognized, holding an AAA rating from MSCI and ranking as the top-performing specialty retailer in Morningstar's Sustainalytics benchmark.

Climate Action and Net Zero Progress

At the core of the Company's environmental strategy is a commitment to achieve Net Zero emissions across its full value chain by 2050. To track progress, WH Smith established near-term targets validated by the Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi). The Group committed to reducing absolute Scope 1 and 2 greenhouse gas emissions by 80% by 2030 against a 2020 baseline. The company has already exceeded this trajectory, achieving an 89% reduction in Scope 1 and 2 emissions for its continuing business.

For Scope 3 emissions, the target requires 75% of suppliers by emissions to have their own science-based targets in place by 2027. Currently, 53% of these supply chain emissions are covered by such targets. Looking ahead, management plans to seek SBTi revalidation and introduce a new Forestry, Land and Agriculture target to address emissions tied to its expanding food-to-go category. The Board also oversees climate-related financial disclosures and scenario analysis to ensure business resilience. While the company faces long-term risks such as rising policy-driven energy costs and extreme weather disrupting supply chains, current scenario analyses indicate no significant impact requiring a shift in the core business model.

Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion

On the social front, WH Smith has embedded Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion into its organizational strategy to improve gender and ethnic representation. As of the end of fiscal 2025, women held 50% of the positions on the Group Board. Within the continuing business, the proportion of female senior managers reached 33%, while 6% of senior managers were from ethnic minority backgrounds. The company actively addresses its Gender Pay Gap through its Gender Equity Network , which facilitates talent conversations, succession planning, and targeted mentoring for female employees. External partnerships with organizations like Diversity in Retail and the Stonewall Diversity Champions programme further benchmark and support these inclusion efforts.

Supply Chain Ethics and Modern Slavery

The Group maintains a zero-tolerance approach to modern slavery and human rights abuses across its operations. Supplier expectations are governed by Responsible Sourcing Standards aligned with the Ethical Trading Initiative Base Code. To enhance due diligence, WH Smith updated its onboarding processes for the Travel divisions to assess trade suppliers' human rights policies prior to placing orders. A major milestone in supply chain ethics is the worker representation initiative, which has successfully established freely elected employee committees in 20% of own-brand factories. This program has improved dialogue between management and over 5,000 workers, successfully resolving issues related to working hours, overtime pay, and dormitory conditions.

Corporate Governance and Executive Compensation

Governance of these initiatives is driven by the Board-level ESG Committee, which sets strategy, monitors targets, and evaluates compliance. To align leadership behavior with sustainability goals, ESG metrics are integrated into the executive compensation framework. Historically, Scope 1, 2, and 3 emission reductions carried a 10% weighting in the Long-Term Incentive Plan . While the Committee recently shifted the forthcoming LTIP metrics entirely toward financial returns (ROCE and absolute TSR) to reflect near-term strategic priorities, management remains explicitly incentivized to deliver the ESG strategy through personal objectives in the annual bonus plan and in-flight LTIP awards. Furthermore, ESG performance directly impacts the balance sheet, as the Group's revolving credit facility includes interest rate adjustments tied to the successful delivery of carbon reduction targets.
