§1 — GENERAL (Description, Financials, Peer Comparison)

Net signal: Vestas is a EUR ~17bn revenue global wind OEM — #1 by installed base — but financial returns are significantly below peer industrials median, reflecting a 2021-23 crisis; recovery is real but partial as of 2024 [#J]

Description

  • Description: Vestas designs, manufactures, installs, and services onshore and offshore wind turbines — the world's largest such company by cumulative GW installed (~170GW across 90+ countries) [#I]
  • Revenue split: ~75% Turbines (project delivery) / ~25% Service (long-term O&M contracts)

  • Core product: onshore 2-5MW turbines (V236 platform flagship); offshore via MHI Vestas JV (now Vestas Offshore, wholly owned since 2021)

  • Strategic objective: Stated goal is 10% EBIT margin for Turbines (medium term), double-digit Service growth, and leadership in the energy transition as wind scales to multi-TW per year [#I]

  • Priorities: pricing discipline on new orders, supply chain normalisation, offshore ramp (V236-15MW flagship)

  • Medium-term financial framework targets EUR 20bn+ revenue by 2025 (subject to order intake cadence)

  • Organisation: Organised into two reporting segments — Turbines and Service — with regional execution hubs (EMEA, Americas, APAC) [#I]

  • Manufacturing: blade plants (Denmark, UK, India, USA, China, Morocco), nacelle assembly (Denmark, Germany, China, USA)
  • Global workforce: ~29,000 employees as of 2023

§2 — COMPANY DETAILS (Revenue Model, Business Model, Recent Delivery, Internal Change)

Net signal: Service segment is the genuine quality asset (predictable, high-margin, switching-cost-protected); Turbines remains a low-margin, cyclical project business whose 2021-23 failure revealed structural pricing and supply-chain vulnerabilities [#J]

Revenue Model

  • Revenue source: ~75% Turbines (EPC-lite project delivery, order book-driven, ~18-24m lag from order to revenue) [#I]
  • Turbines revenue: highly lumpy, H2-weighted, tied to installation windows and permitting timelines

  • Order backlog (late 2024E): ~EUR 20-22bn; provides ~12-15 months revenue visibility at current run-rate

  • Revenue mix: ~25% Service (multi-year O&M, AOM/AVM/AOM5000 contract tiers, contracted at point of turbine sale or standalone) [#I]

  • Service revenue: EUR ~3.5-4.0bn (2024E); growing ~8-12% pa organically driven by installed base expansion

  • Geographic split (approx): EMEA ~50-55%; Americas ~30-35%; APAC ~10-15%

  • Geography: Strongest in Europe (home market, Scandinavian advantage); growing Americas (IRA tailwind); structurally weak in China (dominated by domestic OEMs, Vestas now exiting China manufacturing) [#A]

  • ❌ China exit: Vestas announced China manufacturing exit (2022-23) — forfeits ~10-15% of global wind market; Goldwind, Envision, CSSC dominate domestically

  • India: meaningful growth market; Vestas has established manufacturing position

  • Cyclicality: Turbines revenues highly cyclical and lumpy; Service revenue ~structurally acyclical (contracted, non-cancellable O&M) [#A]

  • Driver count: Turbines driven by ~5-7 macro drivers (grid policy, permitting, electricity prices, project financing rates, OEM ASPs, capex budgets of utilities); Service driven primarily by installed base size + contract renewal rates

  • Predictability: Service high (multi-year, ~90%+ renewal rates historically); Turbines low (order intake volatile: ranged EUR 12-20bn per annum 2018-24) [#A]

Business Model — Customer Side

  • Customer behaviour: Utility and independent power producer customers with 20-30 year project investment horizons; switching costs post-turbine selection are high (service ecosystem lock-in) [#A]
  • Switching cost: once turbines installed, switching service provider costs ~5-10% revenue premium vs Vestas OEM service; SCADA data lock-in and warranty terms reinforce retention

  • Customer moat: Vestas holds ~80-90% retention on service contracts for its installed base; churn to independent service operators (ISOs) is the primary competitive threat

  • Lock-in mechanism: AOM5000 (most premium service tier) provides guaranteed availability — utility customers with bankable project financing require this; creates 20-25 year revenue visibility per project [#A]

  • Revenue per turbine: Service generates ~EUR 10,000-15,000/MW/year (approx) over contract life — long-tail annuity

  • NPS/churn: not disclosed explicitly, but ~90%+ renewal rate implied by Service revenue growth vs installed base growth

  • Secular trend: IEA WEO 2024 requires ~670-900GW annual additions by 2030 (up from ~115GW in 2023) to achieve net-zero trajectory — structural volume demand 5-8x current run-rate [#A]

  • Even on conservative "stated policies" scenario, wind additions must roughly double by 2030 from current pace
  • Vestas at ~16-17% share in a doubling market = substantial revenue runway without share gain

Business Model — Scale Economies

  • Scale moat: Vestas's 170GW+ installed base gives it the largest service book globally — a self-reinforcing scale advantage that smaller OEMs cannot replicate quickly [#A]
  • Each GW installed adds ~EUR 10-15m annual Service revenue for 20-25 years — installed base is a long-duration annuity asset

  • Manufacturing scale: blade and nacelle plants amortised over larger volume runs than Nordex (6-7bn revenue); logistics network is partially optimised

  • Operating leverage: Turbines segment has meaningful fixed-cost leverage — SG&A + R&D ~5-6% of revenue (~EUR 800m-900m), largely fixed; each 1pp margin improvement at EUR 12-14bn revenue = ~EUR 120-140m EBIT uplift [#A]

  • Service segment: lower fixed-cost intensity; margins expand modestly with scale (technician productivity, remote monitoring)

Recent Delivery (L3Y)

  • Recent delivery: 2021-23 constitutes the worst three-year operational period in Vestas's history — not a delivery track record that instils confidence [#J]
  • 2021: Offshore JV consolidation + supply chain shock + commodity inflation on fixed-price contracts = EUR -471m EBIT
  • 2022: Situation worsened; EUR -1,457m EBIT — some orders repriced but legacy contracts continued to bleed; CEO acknowledged pricing was "naïve"

  • 2023: Inflection — returned to marginally positive EBIT (EUR +232m); new order intake repriced at EUR 1.0-1.3m/MW (vs EUR 0.8-0.9m/MW in 2020-21); 2024E meaningfully positive

  • Order book quality improvement: Post-2022 orders carry explicit commodity escalation clauses and higher base ASPs — structural improvement in forward revenue quality [#A]

  • New order ASP: ~EUR 1.0-1.3m/MW (2023-24 intake) vs EUR 0.8-0.9m/MW (2020-21 intake) — ~25-40% ASP uplift

  • This implies 2025-26 Turbines EBIT margins should structurally improve as legacy orders flush through

  • Service delivery: Service segment performed well through 2021-23 — revenue +8-12% pa, margins held ~14-16% EBIT — validating the Service model's resilience [#A]

Internal Change (L12M and N3Y)

  • Leadership change: CEO Henrik Andersen (appointed Jan 2019); CFO Hans Martin Smith (from 2020); both navigated the crisis and led the repricing effort [#I]
  • Andersen: came from Skov A/S (Danish industrial engineering); not a "animal CEO" archetype — cautious, process-oriented; credibility partially rebuilt via 2023-24 execution

  • Board: Christian Keldsen Lund as Chair; governance considered adequate but not exceptional; activist pressure absent

  • Internal change (strategy): Post-crisis strategic priorities — "Profitable Growth" framework articulated at 2023 CMD; three pillars: (1) Turbines margin recovery, (2) Service acceleration, (3) Offshore V236 selective deployment [#I]

  • China manufacturing exit (2022-23): strategically decisive, reduces complexity but forfeits market exposure

  • Rights issue (2023): EUR ~600-700m equity raise to stabilise balance sheet — necessary but poorly telegraphed

  • Corporate strategy: Targeting EUR 20bn+ revenue medium term; 10% EBIT margin; EUR 1bn+ annual FCF at scale [#I]

  • No major M&A telegraphed; selective capability bolt-ons possible in digital/service analytics
  • Internal R&D budget: ~EUR 300-400m pa (stable through crisis); preserves technology roadmap

Sceptical bullet: The Service moat is real but its value is being gradually diluted by ISO (independent service operator) growth — companies like Siemens Gamesa Services and third-party digital O&M providers are winning back some renewals; Turbines moat is weak (customers have shown willingness to dual-source or switch OEM for new projects); CEO Andersen's cautious style may produce adequate rather than outstanding margin recovery — the "10% EBIT target" has been stated for multiple years without delivery

§3 — INDUSTRY CONTEXT (Peer Comparison, Structure, Macro + Non-Macro Change)

Net signal: The onshore wind OEM industry is oligopolistic but not disciplined — three Western OEMs (Vestas, Siemens Gamesa/Energy, GE Vernova) plus Chinese entrants share a rapidly growing market, with competitive intensity elevated and margins structurally below what concentration ratios would suggest [#J]

vs Comparable Peers

  • Peer divergence: No cohort or sector peer research available per D-BATCH-25 flag; peer observations below based on publicly available data and AS sell-side research [#I]
  • Vestas is the closest pure-play onshore/offshore wind OEM globally; Nordex is smaller (~EUR 6-7bn revenue) and onshore-only; Siemens Gamesa (now Siemens Energy subsidiary, de-listed) is the most direct competitor

  • GE Vernova is the US/global closest listed comparator — similar revenue scale (~USD 17bn), also executing margin recovery post-turbine crisis

  • Peer margin divergence: ❌ All three Western OEMs (Vestas, Siemens Gamesa/Energy, GE Vernova) suffered simultaneous margin collapse 2021-23 — suggesting structural industry-wide supply chain failure rather than Vestas-specific execution failure [#A]

  • Siemens Gamesa: EUR -2.2bn losses in FY2022 (worse than Vestas); GE Vernova Wind: also loss-making 2022-23
  • This industry-wide simultaneous implosion is unusual; comparable to early LED lighting supply chain chaos (2012-14) but more severe in duration

Industry Structure and Value Chain Dynamics

  • Market structure: Global wind turbine OEM market — top 5 players hold ~65-70% of global capacity additions annually; but Chinese OEMs (Goldwind, Envision, CSSC) dominate domestic China (est. 50%+ of global additions in some years) [#A]
  • Excluding China: Western OEM market is a 3-player oligopoly — Vestas ~25-30% share, Siemens Gamesa ~20-25%, GE Vernova ~15-20%

  • Including China: market is far less concentrated; Vestas global share ~16-17%

  • Concentration: Top 5 global share ~65-70%; top 3 ex-China ~70-75% — concentrated ex-China but not ex-China plus China [#A]

  • Distribution curve: steep ex-China (top 3 = ~5x the share of #4-5 non-Chinese OEMs); shallow globally (Chinese OEMs at #2-4 globally by GW)

  • Competitive intensity: High and rising — Chinese OEMs (CSSC, Windey) increasingly bidding offshore projects in Europe/Americas; price competition for new turbine orders intensified 2024-25 after ASP spike [#A]

  • BtE (barriers to entry): High for new Western entrants (EUR 500m-1bn minimum scale for meaningful offshore; certification lead times 3-5 years); low for established Chinese OEMs transferring technology offshore

  • Value chain — suppliers: ❌ Vestas is dependent on blade inputs (glass/carbon fibre: Owens Corning, Toray dominate), gearbox/drivetrain (ZF, Winergy), electrical components (main bearings limited supply) [#A]

  • 2021-23 crisis was substantially supplier-driven — logistics (shipping costs +300%), steel (+80%), copper (+40%) repriced beneath fixed-price contracts

  • Supply chain power: suppliers demonstrated significant pricing power 2021-22 — Vestas absorbed the cost on fixed-price contracts; structural shift to inflation-indexing post-crisis but supplier power remains

  • Value chain — customers (utilities/IPPs): Customer power is high — large utilities (Ørsted, RWE, Iberdrola, Enel, NextEra) are repeat buyers who can negotiate volume discounts and dual-source [#A]

  • Customer concentration: top 10-15 utilities likely represent 30-40% of Vestas annual Turbines order intake

  • Monopsony risk: if utility capex budgets compress (rates, PPA pricing pressure), Vestas faces demand destruction with limited ability to switch end markets

  • Distributor returns: Minimal distributor layer — Vestas sells direct to utilities/IPPs; installation is partially self-performed, partially subcontracted to specialist erection contractors [#I]

External Macro Changes (L12M, plausibly N36M)

  • Macro shift: ❌ Interest rate normalisation (EUR rates: ECB deposit rate rose from 0% to 4% in 2022-23, now easing from ~3.5-4%) has compressed IRRs on uncontracted wind projects — slowed some FID decisions in 2023-24 [#A]
  • Impact: slower project FID timing → delayed Vestas order conversion; partially offset by higher electricity prices (improving project economics)

  • N36M: ECB easing cycle (2024-25) should progressively improve project IRRs; tailwind for new order intake recovery

  • Macro shift (IRA/policy): US Inflation Reduction Act (IRA, 2022) — EUR ~370bn green energy subsidies — materially positive for Vestas Americas [#A]

  • Vestas has US manufacturing (turbine assembly, blades Colorado) qualifying for IRA content requirements
  • Risk: IRA political durability under US administration changes — partial or full IRA rollback would materially impair Americas order intake (est. 15-20% of global Vestas revenue)

External Non-Macro Changes (L12M, plausibly N36M)

  • Regulatory shift: EU accelerated permitting via REPowerEU (2022-23) — target 30+ month permitting reduction; practical implementation has lagged rhetoric but direction is positive [#A]
  • North Sea offshore: EU member states committed to 300GW offshore wind by 2050 — major pipeline for Vestas V236

  • Grid connection backlog remains a structural bottleneck in Germany, UK, Netherlands — delays project installation timelines

  • Political shift: ❌ UK offshore wind contract auctions (CfD AR6, 2024) cleared at significantly higher strike prices — validating improved project economics; BUT political risk around subsidy continuity is not zero [#A]

  • Denmark/Germany: domestic support strong; permitting improving but not solved

  • US: IRA rollback risk under current administration — most significant political risk to Vestas N36M

  • Competitive intensity: Chinese OEMs (CSSC Haizhuang, Goldwind offshore) increasingly targeting European offshore markets — first European offshore project bids emerging 2024-25 [#A]

  • Technology gap: Chinese turbines (domestic certification only as of 2024) require IEC certification for European deployment — 3-5 year hurdle; significant but not permanent barrier

  • Price differential: Chinese OEMs reportedly 20-30% cheaper than Western OEMs at equivalent MW — existential if certification gap closes

  • Thematic trend: Offshore wind is the fastest-growing segment (from ~50GW globally to target 500GW by 2030 per IEA); Vestas V236-15MW is competitive with Siemens Gamesa SG-14/222 in this segment [#A]

Sceptical bullet: The structural demand thesis is undeniable but timing and competitive dynamics create significant medium-term earnings risk — if Chinese OEMs clear European certification by 2027-28, Vestas's ex-China oligopoly pricing normalises materially downward; grid and permitting bottlenecks could keep actual installations 30-50% below policy targets through 2027, keeping order intake volatile; the "IRA windfall" for Americas could partially reverse under political headwinds

§4.2 — Advantaged Business + SRCA (BB#2 TC2)

MOAT (RA4)

  • Customer moat: Service segment is the primary customer moat — SCADA lock-in, warranty interdependence, AOM5000 guaranteed availability contracts create high switching costs [#A]
  • CQ16: Customer moats — strong in Service (see §2 customer side); weak in Turbines (customers demonstrate dual-sourcing willingness; GE Vernova and Siemens Gamesa are credible alternatives)

  • Service churn ~10% (inverse of ~90% renewal rate) — lower churn than ISO-only service providers; genuine lock-in

  • Production moat: CQ17: Production advantages include global manufacturing scale (blade plants x6 countries, nacelle assembly x8 sites) — but NOT proprietary; Siemens Gamesa has comparable footprint [#A]

  • V236-15MW: first-mover in 15MW offshore class with meaningful cumulative operating data advantage (critical for bankability); temporary production moat

  • No unique material or process advantage; blade manufacturing is replicable

  • Scale moat: CQ18: 170GW+ installed base is the most durable scale moat — generates EUR ~3.5-4.0bn Service revenue as near-annuity; self-reinforcing (each new installation adds to Service book for 20+ years) [#A]

  • Relative scale (CQ21): Vestas is materially larger than Nordex (2.5x revenue); comparable to Siemens Gamesa; both are roughly 2x the size of GE Vernova Wind

  • Scale enables: lower blade cost per MW (larger production runs), better parts logistics, better technician density (service routes optimised)

  • Regulatory moat: CQ19: IEC certification requirements for turbines in Europe/Americas create 3-5 year regulatory moat against new Chinese OEM entrants — important but time-limited [#A]

  • EU CBAM/supply chain diligence requirements may raise cost of Chinese OEM imports — beneficial but uncertain in impact
