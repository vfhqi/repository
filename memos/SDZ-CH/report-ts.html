Section 7 – Key risks and the other side 9 findings

Section summary
  1. Sandoz faces a highly competitive and legal risk environment, particularly regarding US payer contracting barriers and patent litigation, though its geographic diversification mitigates extreme downside.

Commercial and Payer Risks

1.
US payer barriers: The US market is characterized by complex rebate structures with pharmacy benefit managers, making it difficult for new biosimilars to displace originators
2.
Stelara litigation: Sandoz's interchangeable Stelara biosimilar launch has faced intellectual property and antitrust litigation, reflecting high legal hurdles from reference brand originators
3.
Rebate trap: High rebate demands from pharmacy benefit managers represent a false friend signal where high gross sales growth masks negative net-price realization [#r]

Manufacturing and Geopolitical Risks

4.
Penicillin dumping: Chinese API manufacturers diverted penicillin volumes into Europe in H2 2025, triggering a sharp decline in Sandoz's generic sales that will last through H1 2026
5.
Slovenia capex drag: Sandoz is investing over USD 1bn in its new Slovenia biomanufacturing hub, creating a major cash flow drain before peak production is reached by 2028E
6.
Separation compliance: Building independent global compliance and IT infrastructure post-spin represents a major corporate risk, which has led to high separation-related operating expenses [#a]

Early Warning Signals

7.
Early warning signal: Investors should closely monitor the sequential market share uptake of denosumab in the US, as slower volume gains would signal poor US payer contracting and rebate structures execution
8.
Other side of the story: Scale-driven cost advantages and a broad portfolio of 27 biosimilars will allow Sandoz to comfortably absorb price erosion that would bankrupt smaller, non-integrated competitors [#g]
