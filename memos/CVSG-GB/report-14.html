§2 — Key Driver: ATV (Average Transaction Value / Fee-Per-Visit) Recovery

  • J-front verdict: ATV is the earnings engine — evidence shows real-terms pricing discipline but consumer-facing resistance is emerging; medium plausibility for sustained 24-month outperformance [#J]

Driver Definition

  • Driver definition: Companion-animal ATV recovering from COVID-distorted highs (FY22-FY23 price surge) to a new normalised growth trajectory of +4-6% annually in real terms [#A]
  • Magnitude required: ATV must expand from current ~flat/modest growth back toward 4-6% p.a. to drive the 200-350bps EBIT margin recovery underpinning the thesis [#I]
  • Baseline: CVS reported ATV broadly flat to +low-single-digit in FY24-FY25 vs the +10-14% CAGR seen in FY21-FY23 as pandemic-era demand and labour costs distorted pricing [#A]
  • Target state: ATV of +4-6% p.a. over 18-24 months, consistent with pre-2019 sector pricing power trend [#I]
  • Required trajectory: Quarterly ATV progression needs to accelerate from current +1-3% to +4-6% without triggering visit volume abandonment [#I]

Bull Case

  • Bull — historical: CVS delivered 10%+ ATV growth in FY21-FY23, demonstrating the pricing infrastructure exists; the baseline capability is not in question [#A]
  • Bull — pricing power: UK companion-animal vet services have structurally low elasticity — pet owners exhibit strong attachment behaviour and limited service substitution options [#A]
  • Bull — services mix: Specialist referral and advanced diagnostics have structurally higher ATVs (2-4× FO base ATV); growing the specialist/referral mix naturally lifts blended ATV [#A]
  • Bull — mgmt: CVS management have explicitly committed to pricing discipline; FY25 commentary referenced focus on "sustainable fee structures" rather than volume-at-all-costs [#A]
  • Bull — industry: UK vet sector pricing has outpaced CPI since 2015 (ABI/Which? data); structural premium to general healthcare spend is established [#A]

Bear Case

  • Bear — recent deterioration: ATV growth decelerated sharply in FY24 as CVS absorbed post-COVID demand normalisation; real-terms ATV may be negative once inflation adjustments are applied [#A]
  • Bear — regulatory risk: ❌ CMA investigation into UK veterinary services (launched 2023, final recommendations expected 2025-26) creates regulatory overhang on pricing — sector could face mandatory transparency or pricing caps [#J]
  • Bear — consumer resistance: Cost-of-living pressures are documented as causing pet-owner treatment deferrals; the Which? / Blue Cross data shows ~30% of UK pet owners reported cost as a barrier to vet visits in 2023-24 [#A]
  • Bear — competitive: Vet consolidators (Medivet, IVC Evidensia, Vets4Pets/Pets at Home) are competing on price and accessibility in certain segments, limiting CVS's pricing room [#A]
  • Bear — structural: ❌ If visit volumes are soft, ATV growth above inflation requires higher-acuity case mix (sicker pets, more advanced procedures) — not a sustainable growth driver [#J]

Forward Trajectory — 18-24M Plausibility

  • Acceleration needed: High relative to recent trend; no clear short-term catalyst beyond CMA resolution (which may be negative not positive) [#J]
  • Key dependency: CMA final report must not impose pricing constraints; specialist mix shift must continue at current pace [#A]

§3 — Key Driver: Colleague Cost Normalisation (Vet Wage Inflation + Turnover)

  • J-front verdict: The single largest cost headwind of FY22-FY25 is partially easing but structural vet supply constraints mean full normalisation within 24 months is optimistic; medium plausibility [#J]

Driver Definition

  • Driver definition: Vet wage cost as a percentage of revenue declining from peak FY23-FY24 levels (~45-50% of revenue) toward historical norm (~40-43%) as supply-demand dynamics for qualified vets rebalance [#A]
  • Magnitude required: ~300-500bps improvement in colleague cost ratio to drive EBIT margin recovery to thesis-implied levels [#I]
  • Baseline: CVS's colleague cost ratio peaked sharply in FY22-FY24 driven by: (a) RCVS-qualified vet shortage, (b) post-COVID attrition wave, (c) locum premium inflation [#A]
  • Target state: Colleague cost ratio approaching pre-COVID norms of ~40-43% of revenue within 18-24 months [#I]
  • Required trajectory: ~150-200bps per annum reduction; requires combination of wage growth moderation + turnover normalisation + locum dependency reduction [#I]

Bull Case

  • Bull — supply-side turning: RCVS new graduate data shows increased intake from UK vet schools + international recruitment (post-Brexit visa route); supply is recovering, albeit slowly [#A]
  • Bull — attrition peaking: Industry surveys (British Veterinary Association 2024) indicate vet "intention to leave" metrics are declining from peak — the FY22-FY23 attrition wave may have passed [#A]
  • Bull — mgmt initiatives: CVS has invested in colleague retention programmes (wellbeing, CPD, flexible working) — management commentary references improved retention KPIs in FY25 [#A]
  • Bull — locum normalisation: Locum premium vs permanent vet cost was ~25-35% in FY23; industry reports suggest compression to ~15-20% by FY25 as permanent supply returns [#A]
  • Bull — sector wages: UK vet starting salaries are moderating from the 2022-23 spike; RCVS salary benchmarking suggests wage inflation below CPI in 2024-25 [#A]

Bear Case

  • Bear — structural supply gap: ❌ RCVS estimates a structural shortage of 4,000-5,000 qualified vets in the UK by 2030; this is not a short-cycle problem that resolves within 24 months [#J]
  • Bear — recruitment competition: IVC Evidensia, Medivet, and Pets at Home are all competing aggressively for the same vet talent pool — CVS does not have a structural recruitment advantage [#A]
  • Bear — wage floor sticky: Once wages are ratcheted up, nominal reductions are extremely rare in professional services; normalisation will be a "grow into" story over 3-5 years, not 24 months [#J]
  • Bear — locum dependency structural: For specialist vet roles (oncology, cardiology, neurology), the locum market is global and premium-priced; CVS cannot eliminate this cost line [#A]
  • Bear — international vet supply: Post-Brexit, international vet recruitment faces RCVS language + qualification requirements; lead time is 12-18 months per hire [#A]

Forward Trajectory — 18-24M Plausibility

  • Quarterly trajectory: Requires 75-100bps per quarter colleague cost ratio improvement — ambitious but partially evidenced by FY25 H1 management commentary [#I]
  • Acceleration needed: Moderate — trend is already moving right but the structural supply issue caps the pace of improvement [#J]
  • Key dependency: New graduate intake sustaining; locum premiums continuing to compress; no further attrition wave [#A]
  • Downside scenario: If colleague costs stay at peak ratio and ATV is also soft, EBIT margin could be 400-500bps below thesis — a devastating double-miss [#I]
  • Probability — verbal: Medium plausibility for the 24-month timeline; High plausibility for the 36-48 month resolution [#J]

Evidence Gaps

  • Evidence gap: CVS does not publicly disclose locum cost as a percentage of total colleague cost — this is the most volatile sub-line and APM should request it at any analyst meeting [#A]
  • Evidence gap: No public peer comparison of colleague cost ratios (IVC Evidensia, Medivet are private) — benchmarking is constrained [#A]

Plausibility verdict: Medium plausibility — directional improvement is real and supported by emerging supply-side data, but the structural UK vet supply gap is a 3-5 year resolution story, not 24 months. Fulcrum-level vs APM Pass 1: VALIDATES (colleague cost is almost certainly identified as fulcrum; the dispute is the timeline, not the mechanism).

  • Bear — substitution: Online pharmacy, home diagnostics, and nurse consultation substitution are structurally reducing vet visit frequency for lower-acuity cases [#A]
  • Bear — competitor capacity: IVC, Medivet, and Pets at Home Vets are all expanding capacity — even if total visits recover, CVS may not recapture share [#A]

  • Downside scenario: If volumes stay flat or decline 1-2% p.a., revenue growth depends entirely on ATV — creating a concentrated single-variable thesis with less margin of safety [#I]
