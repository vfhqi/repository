§6 — Value Chain: Demand

J-front verdict: Long-term structural demand for offshore wind is among the strongest in the energy sector — but near-term demand expression is being throttled by auction price caps, permitting delays, and project economics stress [#J]

  • Customer behaviour: Offtake demand (utilities, corporates via PPAs, government via CfDs) remains robust; UK CfD Allocation Round 6 (2024) cleared at higher strike prices than AR5, confirming demand-side price discovery is improving [#A]

  • Volume: UK offshore allocation in AR6 was lower than government target — supply side (developers pulling back) rather than demand side (government willingness to pay) is the binding constraint

  • Pricing power: New contracts pricing at materially higher levels than legacy — UK CfD AR6 clearing prices ~£73–80/MWh (2012 prices, approx. £100–115/MWh nominal) vs AR3/AR4 legacy contracts at £50–60/MWh nominal [#A]

  • Transmission mechanism: as legacy fixed-price contracts roll off 2025–2030, Ørsted's realised revenue per MWh should step up significantly — this is the primary margin recovery mechanism

  • Demand pulse caution: Government offshore wind targets (UK 50GW by 2030, EU offshore targets) are aspirational; actual auction volume regularly undershoots targets by 20–40% [#J]

  • Demand pulse test: the 50GW UK target is almost certainly not deliverable by 2030 per National Grid ESO's own modelling — this is a structural demand promise that will be partially delivered, not 100% delivered

  • Delays: UK Hornsea 4 paused/deferred; US Northeast pipeline cancelled; these are supply-side decisions by Ørsted, not demand-side cancellations — distinction matters for the recovery thesis [#A]

  • Route to market: CfD mechanism (UK, DK) + Merchant + Corporate PPA — all three channels active; CfD is the most economically reliable; corporate PPA market growing but smaller-ticket [#I]

  • Cycle stage: Demand cycle is in a "pause and reset" phase — governments pushing volume, developers pushing back on economics — likely to resolve as supply chain costs normalise and CfD strike prices adjust upward [#J]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish demand read: government offshore wind targets are politically driven and subject to reversal (UK offshore targets have already been adjusted multiple times); corporate PPA demand is price-sensitive and may plateau if power prices fall as renewable build accelerates; the primary demand driver (government mandate) creates a monopsony-like dynamic where governments can squeeze developer returns if political circumstances change (as seen in the US with IRA implementation uncertainty) [#J]

§7 — Value Chain: Competition

J-front verdict: Competitive intensity in offshore wind is rising structurally; Ørsted's first-mover advantages are eroding as oil majors, European utilities, and sovereign funds increase offshore exposure [#J]

  • Industry structure: Offshore wind development was historically concentrated (Ørsted, Vattenfall, Equinor, RWE, Iberdrola as primary developers); now includes BP, Shell, TotalEnergies, ADNOW, Korean conglomerates — effectively doubling the competitive field [#A]
  • New competitor: BP offshore wind — explicitly targeting positions Ørsted has vacated in US Northeast and partnering with Equinor in NY/NJ

  • New competitor: Masdar (Abu Dhabi) — bidding aggressively in UK, EU auction rounds

  • Market structure: Offshore wind CfD auctions are government-administered, so competitive intensity manifests at the auction bid stage — lower bids win capacity rights; developer with lower cost of capital can bid more aggressively [#A]

  • BtE: barriers to entry are high (development rights, permitting, supply chain relationships, grid connection slots) but not prohibitive — TotalEnergies entered UK offshore meaningfully within 5 years

  • Competitor health: RWE is well-capitalised with no leverage stress; Iberdrola maintains investment-grade ratings and growing dividend; Equinor/BP have oil cash flows to cross-subsidise offshore wind development losses — all three are structurally better-positioned than Ørsted on balance-sheet flexibility [#J]

  • Competitor returns: Peer offshore wind developers generally avoided the fixed-price contract losses that damaged Ørsted — Vattenfall returned Hornsea 3 development rights rather than accept fixed-price losses, a decision Ørsted did not make at equivalent decision points [#J]

  • Disruption — substitute: Floating offshore wind is a long-range competitive threat to Ørsted's fixed-bottom expertise; Ørsted is investing in FLOW but is not yet a leader (Equinor's Hywind is the technology leader) [#A]

  • Intensity: Tender competition for UK offshore acreage (ScotWind, round 4 licensing) was oversubscribed 5–10x — confirming entry interest is high and Ørsted's exclusive developer positioning has ended [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish competitive read: Ørsted's greatest moat was its operational track record and supply chain relationships, both of which are being eroded — if supply chain relationships normalise across the industry (which is happening as OEM capacity expands), and operational track records of competitors improve, Ørsted's premium positioning in auctions may not survive the decade; the oil major cross-subsidy model (BP, Shell using hydrocarbon cash flows to bid aggressively on renewables) is specifically advantageous in a high-cost-of-capital environment that Ørsted cannot match [#J]

§8 — Value Chain: Disruption

J-front verdict: Disruption forces are real but medium-term (5–10 years); the more immediate disruption is within the capital cycle rather than technology [#J]

  • Technology disruption — turbines: Wind turbine OEMs (Vestas, Siemens Gamesa) have experienced their own severe cost and quality crises (Siemens Energy profit warning 2023; Vestas margin collapse) — disrupting Ørsted's project delivery timelines and cost assumptions [#A]
  • Disruption cost: SGRE (Siemens Gamesa) quality issues on offshore blades contributed directly to cost overruns on multiple Ørsted projects 2022–2024

  • Incumbent response: Ørsted is attempting to diversify OEM supply (bringing in GE Vernova, MHI Vestas variants) — multi-OEM strategy reduces concentration

  • New entrant — innovative: Floating offshore wind is the technology most likely to structurally disrupt fixed-bottom offshore; Equinor's Hywind Scotland is the proof-of-concept; commercial scale FLOW could open deeper water markets [#A]

  • Adoption curve: commercial-scale FLOW is 5–10 years from material deployment; Ørsted is a minority investor in FLOW projects (Kaskasi+ etc.) but not a technology leader

  • Disruption pace: FLOW disruption is slow enough that Ørsted has time to position — but requires capital investment while the balance sheet is stressed

  • Technology disruption — grid: Long interconnection queues and grid bottlenecks are disrupting project timelines across Europe and US; National Grid UK's transmission investment programme is 3–5 years behind the generation build curve [#A]

  • Disruption cost: grid connection delays add 12–24 months to project timelines, increasing carrying costs and reducing project IRR by 100–200bps

  • New entrant — traditional: Oil majors (BP, Shell, TotalEnergies) are not technology disruptors but are capital-scale disruptors — they can absorb early-stage losses and bid below economic hurdle rates in auctions to establish position [#J]

  • Green hydrogen: Ørsted had positioned green hydrogen (Power-to-X) as a major growth vector; this has been quietly deprioritised as economics remain challenging — a disruption victim rather than disruption beneficiary [#I]

  • Disruption — battery storage: Co-located offshore wind + battery storage is emerging as a grid services product; Ørsted has capability here but it is not yet a meaningful revenue line [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish disruption read: if turbine OEM quality issues persist for another 2–3 years, Ørsted's remaining construction programme faces further cost overruns that are not yet priced into consensus; the longer-run disruption risk from FLOW is systematically underweighted by fixed-bottom incumbents including Ørsted — if FLOW costs fall faster than expected, the entire fixed-bottom asset base could face residual value pressure before end-of-life [#J]

§9 — Value Chain: Political and Regulatory

J-front verdict: Regulatory environment is simultaneously the strongest structural tailwind and the most acute near-term operational headwind for Ørsted — policy intent is clear; implementation is chaotic [#J]

  • Regulatory shift — EU: REPowerEU targets 300GW offshore wind by 2050 (vs ~30GW today); EU Offshore Renewable Energy Strategy mandates 60GW by 2030 — creates clear political demand floor for European offshore [#I]
  • Political actor: European Commission as mandating body; transmission through national auction frameworks (UK CfD, Danish tender, German SDE++)

  • Geography: UK, Denmark, Germany, Netherlands are Ørsted's core markets — all aligned with offshore expansion

  • Regulatory shift — UK: UK Labour government (elected Jul 2024) has accelerated offshore wind ambition to 50GW by 2030 — more aggressive than prior Conservative target; GB Energy (new state body) specifically tasked with offshore investment [#I]

  • CfD reform: AR6 (2024) strike prices increased materially from AR5's failed clearing — AR5 had zero offshore wind capacity awarded as prices were too low; AR6 correction is structurally important for Ørsted's pipeline

  • Regulatory shift — US: IRA (Inflation Reduction Act) extended offshore wind PTCs and ITCs; but implementation uncertainty under the Trump administration (2025+) creates project-level risk for any remaining US activity [#A]

  • Tariff: US steel/component tariffs under Trump 2.0 (2025) increase offshore construction costs by estimated 5–15% for US-located projects — directly affecting Ørsted's any residual US assets

  • Political actor: US federal offshore leasing under BOEM has been paused/reviewed under Trump 2.0 — significant headwind for US pipeline

  • Subsidy: Danish state (Ørsted's 50.1% controlling shareholder) provides implicit support but not explicit subsidies post-initial project development; Danish government has been patient through the crisis but has publicly pushed for balance sheet stabilisation [#I]

  • Compliance cost: New EU taxonomy disclosure requirements + supply chain due diligence add compliance burden but are broadly symmetric across European utilities — not Ørsted-specific [#I]

  • Environmental regulation: Scottish Nest licensing restrictions and UK seabed survey requirements continue to add permitting complexity; UK Planning Inspectorate DCO process remains a 3–5 year lead time item [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish political/regulatory read: the single most important regulatory risk is that governments facing energy price pressures (voter sensitivity) freeze or reduce CfD strike prices below economic thresholds — this has already happened once (UK AR5); if inflation does not moderate and grid costs rise, there is a credible scenario where European governments decide offshore wind economics are unacceptable and pivot to gas peakers, SMRs, or demand reduction instead of further offshore build [#J]

§10 — Value Chain: Supply Side

J-front verdict: Supply chain stress has been the primary operational driver of Ørsted's crisis — it is partially normalising but structural risks remain elevated vs 2019 levels [#J]

  • Supplier economics: Wind turbine OEM (Vestas, SGRE, GE Vernova) margins were severely compressed 2021–2024; SGRE required a €4bn capital injection from Siemens Energy (2023); Vestas returned to profitability in 2024 [#A]
  • Concentration: Ørsted's offshore portfolio is heavily concentrated in Siemens Gamesa turbines — creating significant counterparty/quality risk that materialised in blade quality failures 2023–2024

  • Peer anchor: RWE has more diversified OEM relationships; Iberdrola uses Vestas as primary supplier — Ørsted's SGRE concentration is above-median for the sector

  • Supplier health: SGRE's stabilisation (post Siemens Energy rescue) is a positive development for Ørsted's construction pipeline but blade quality issues are not fully resolved [#A]

  • Supply risk: if SGRE encounters further quality or solvency issues, Ørsted has fewer switching options given long-lead-time turbine procurement commitments

  • Supplier returns: Turbine OEM returns on capital remain negative-to-marginal across the sector (Vestas ~3% ROIC in 2024 vs 10%+ pre-2019); healthy OEM margins are prerequisite for long-run supply chain stability [#A]

  • Vertical integration: Ørsted has no meaningful vertical integration into turbine manufacturing; installation vessels (jackups, heavy lift) are contracted rather than owned; cable supply is contracted [#I]

  • Backward integration: Ørsted has explored but not pursued backward integration into cable manufacturing or turbine components — a potential long-run margin lever that remains untaken

  • Cable supply: Submarine cable supply is a bottleneck — Prysmian and Nexans are capacity-constrained through 2027; cable lead times of 3–4 years are a real project timeline risk [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish supply-side read: if SGRE encounters a second quality event or financial distress episode, Ørsted's in-construction projects (Hornsea 3, Thor) face significant cost and delay risk with limited ability to switch suppliers on projects already under construction; the cable bottleneck is consistently underweighted by company communications and could materially delay commissioning timelines for projects already in the financing plan [#J]

§11 — Revenue/Demand Cycle

J-front verdict: Ørsted is navigating a sector-specific capital cycle trough — offshore wind development activity has declined sharply since 2023 peak ambition, with signs of early recovery in European auction volumes but not yet in US [#J]

  • Cyclicality: Offshore wind revenue is largely contracted (CfD/PPA) once assets are operational — this portion (~75% of operating asset revenue) is genuinely non-cyclical and predictable [#A]

  • Cyclical portion: development pipeline value, farm-down proceeds, and construction-phase revenue are all highly cyclical — this is where the crisis originated

  • Cycle stage: Offshore wind development cycle appears to be in a trough-to-recovery phase in Europe (2025–2026); US is in trough (2024–2026) with recovery timing highly uncertain given political environment [#J]

  • Cycle length: offshore wind development cycles are long (5–10 year capex cycles for individual projects); this limits the speed of any earnings recovery

  • Through-cycle profile: Operating asset EBITDA (existing fleet of ~9GW globally) is highly stable and growing as new capacity is commissioned; the crisis was concentrated in the development/construction segment [#A]

§12 — Value Chain: Macroeconomic

J-front verdict: Macro headwinds were the proximate trigger for Ørsted's 2023 crisis and remain partially elevated; rate stabilisation and potential easing is the single most important macro variable for the recovery thesis [#J]

  • Rates: Risk-free rate rise of 350–400bps (2022–2024) was the dominant macro change force — it simultaneously increased project discount rates (making marginal US projects NPV-negative), raised Ørsted's financing costs, and compressed the NPV of long-duration contracted cash flows [#A]
  • Transmission mechanism: 1% rise in risk-free rate reduces NPV of a 25-year CfD/PPA asset by approximately 8–12% — for a company with DKK 200bn+ of such assets, the NPV impact of the full rate cycle was DKK 15–25bn

  • Current status: ECB has begun cutting rates (2024–2025); US Fed cutting; if long-end rates stabilise or decline, this reverses approximately 30–40% of the NPV headwind

  • FX: USD/DKK (EUR-pegged) is relevant for residual US revenue and USD-denominated costs; GBP/DKK is material for UK operations (~40% of EBITDA) [#A]

  • GBP exposure: GBP weakness vs EUR/DKK (2022–2023) was a modest headwind; GBP has stabilised at ~1.17–1.19 vs EUR — manageable

  • Inflation: Construction cost inflation of 30–50% (2021–2024) was the operational killer — higher steel prices, vessel day rates, specialised labour, cable costs all compounded [#A]

  • Current status: construction cost inflation moderating (2024–2025); Vestas and SGRE have both guided lower turbine cost inflation going forward; not fully reversed but directionally improving

  • Energy prices: Lower European power prices (wholesale) in 2024–2025 vs 2022 peak reduce merchant revenue but Ørsted's contracted-revenue structure insulates most of the portfolio [#I]

  • Macro shift: prolonged low power prices would reduce farm-down premiums as asset NPVs fall — an indirect macro transmission mechanism

  • Geopolitical: European energy security concerns (post-Ukraine invasion) structurally elevated political urgency around domestic renewable build — a genuine macro tailwind for Ørsted's European pipeline [#A]

  • Counter-risk: geopolitical tension specifically between China and Taiwan is a real operational risk for Ørsted's Greater Changhua operational assets in Taiwan (completed but ongoing O&M)

  • End-market macro: European industrial demand for renewable energy (corporate PPAs) is growing but price-sensitive; recession risk in Germany/Netherlands could reduce corporate PPA appetite [#A]
