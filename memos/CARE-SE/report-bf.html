EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (BLUF)

Careium operates as a leading European technology-enabled care provider, delivering stable recurring service revenues through integrated hardware, proprietary software platforms, and managed alarm receiving centers across mature Nordic and UK public markets while navigating strategic leadership transitions toward operating integration . The business demonstrates robust cash conversion dynamics with free cash flow improving to SEK 41.2 million in 2025, supported by working capital normalization and gross margins stabilizing above 43 percent despite temporary headwinds from Swedish lease accounting changes . However, operating margins remain significantly below peer benchmarks such as MedCap's Assistive Tech segment EBITA margin of 26.5 percent, reflecting lower operating leverage and ongoing integration costs . Financial output generation shows sub-peer capital returns with 2025 ROIC at 6.40 percent and reported EBIT margins compressed to 5.8 percent, requiring heroic integration efforts to close the profitability gap while structural moats remain anchored in high public- sector switching costs with 60 percent of Swedish users migrated to the proprietary i-Care platform .

Core business model and revenue architecture

Careium supports over 550,000 seniors across Europe, controlling the entire alarm chain from hardware devices to 24/7 staffed alarm receiving centers . The company operates as a single unified segment generating SEK 854.1 million in 2025 net sales, with 73 percent derived from stable recurring service revenues and 27 percent from hardware product sales . Secular demand drivers include rapidly aging European demographics and chronic healthcare personnel shortages underpinning structural long-term demand . 4 5

Value chain integration and margin distribution

Careium differs from single-stage peers by controlling the entire value chain from device design to direct managed service delivery . Software platforms represent the most profitable value-chain stage generating gross margins of approximately 70 percent with near-zero marginal distribution costs expanding operating margins as they scale . Alarm receiving center managed services carry significant operational labor burdens with semi- fixed staffing costs requiring high subscriber density as centers under 5,000 connections run below cash break- even . Transitioning to the proprietary i-Care Center platform allows Careium to eliminate third-party technology licensing fees operating as an attractive long-term software margin expansion play . Vertical integration shifts the corporate mix toward high-visibility recurring services reaching 73 percent of sales with Nordic operations achieving steady gross margin improvements toward 40 percent .

Customer concentration and switching dynamics

Multi-year public procurement framework agreements generate highly predictable non-cyclical recurring revenues with typical contract durations of 4 to 5 years, with contracted services accounting for 73 percent of total sales in 2025 . Customer lock-in is secured through deep integration with Careium's proprietary i-Care software ecosystem with 60 percent of Swedish user base migrated to i-Care Center by late 2025 . Direct-to-consumer private pay segments offer highest pricing power and margin profile with Careium's Dutch business achieving impressive gross margins of 61 percent driven by efficient B2B2C structures . Low customer concentration protects recurring revenues with the largest single client representing under 10 percent of sales.

Competitive landscape and market structure

Tunstall and Legrand hold a 70 percent share of European telecare equipment in the concentrated traditional base . Careium commands a 40 percent market share in Sweden and Norway but holds only a 14 percent share in the UK . The European telecare market is projected to expand at an estimated CAGR of 11-13 percent with next-generation telecare users expanding at 21 percent CAGR while traditional telecare grows at slower 3 percent CAGR . Alarm receiving centres require at least 5,000 connections to break even establishing high operational thresholds . Nordic Capital acquired Sensio in 2024 to target digital-first care monitoring accelerating consolidation transactions in the sector . 18 19

Supply chain exposure and logistics risks

Careium's hardware manufacturing is concentrated in Asia accounting for 85 percent of its product climate footprint with very limited opportunities to alter component choices or shift production partners quickly . Component production faces ongoing direct material cost inflation with input cost and freight inflation historically pressuring gross margins forcing component design swaps . Geopolitical maritime delays in the Suez Canal forced Careium to extend forecasting cycles by two months . Upstream chip and component suppliers hold material pricing leverage due to specialized semiconductor manufacturing constraints while mature hardware products face steady price declines . Careium designs innovative hardware in close cooperation with long- term Asian contract manufacturers while mitigating disintermediation risks by owning the custom molding tools used in external production .

Operator quality and strategic execution

Careium completed pivotal leadership transition with Tove Christiansson joining as permanent CEO on June 1, 2026, bringing extensive assistive technology expertise with primary mandate to build unified modern European health-tech company powered by scalable platform . Interim CEO Peter Heuman successfully stabilized operations over ten-month tenure focusing on integrating historic acquisitions and harmonizing operational processes though integration work created upfront costs temporarily weighing on operating margins during H1 2026 . The proprietary i-Care Center platform represents Careium's most critical structural and technological asset combining all service offerings into single shared interface while eliminating expensive third-party software licensing fees . Careium prioritizes disciplined capital deployment retaining all cash to fund development with no dividend proposed for FY24 to preserve cash for vital technology upgrades while management selectively evaluates complementary M&A with strict focus on measurable synergies .

Foundational moats and competitive positioning

Industry economics require massive scale as receiving centers with under 5,000 connections run below cash break-even with Careium's scale supporting 40 percent market share in Sweden and Norway . Structural demand is driven by mandatory analogue-to-digital switchover in UK telecare market with upgrading older telecare equipment representing non-discretionary volume driver for hardware sales . Realizing margin expansion requires intense operational coordination to integrate acquired regional businesses with standardizing all acquired services onto i-Care Center as the single most important driver for operating leverage . Disruption vectors and long-term growth drivers

Next-generation systems are shifting from reactive to proactive monitoring with solution providers integrating machine learning to identify daily routine irregularities while proactive welfare calls assess early health changes . Closing legacy telecommunication infrastructure represents powerful medium-term volume driver with around 1.6 million analogue devices in UK requiring urgent replacement . Europe's aging population provides multi-decade structural growth tailwind with senior cohorts projected to reach 154 million by 2050 and over 90 percent of seniors preferring remaining in independent domiciliary housing . Blending telecare and telehealth broadens long-term addressable market with connected systems tracking chronic diseases to keep seniors out of hospitals while proactive monitoring increases average revenue per user .
