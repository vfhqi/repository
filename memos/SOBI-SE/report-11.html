KEY FINDINGS - Structural shape: Sobi occupies a high-margin, specialist commercialisation stage within a hub-and-spoke orphan-drug value chain — clinical differentiation and regulatory barriers, not manufacturing scale, are the primary moat determinants [#J]

  • Positional concentration: Sobi is a multi-stage operator covering drug development through commercial delivery, but its economic identity is concentrated in the late-development and commercialisation stages, not upstream biologics manufacturing [#J]
  • Margin capture: The orphan-drug commercialisation stage is the most economically favoured stage in the chain — gross margins structurally above 70% for differentiated rare-disease products — and Sobi's blended gross margin (~68-72%) is consistent with this position [#J]
  • Pricing power: Sobi exercises above-average pricing power in most markets via small-patient-population economics and high unmet need, but is structurally constrained by concentrated public/national payer customers who deploy HTA and reference-pricing mechanisms [#J]
  • Supplier leverage: ❌ Sobi's reliance on third-party contract development and manufacturing organisations (CDMOs) and plasma fractionators for biological inputs creates meaningful supplier-side exposure; switching costs for complex biologics are high [#I]
  • Competitive tier: At its core stage, Sobi competes against a small tier of specialist rare-disease operators — AstraZeneca/Alexion, Takeda Rare, Sanofi Genzyme, Ultragenyx, Recordati Rare — plus large-pharma units with dedicated orphan franchises; stage concentration is moderate, not oligopolistic [#J]
  • Vertical integration risk: Upstream CDMOs (Samsung Biologics, Lonza, Catalent/Novo) are not yet integrating into orphan commercialisation, but large pharma's in-house CDMO capacity blurs the line for self-originated assets; customer-side (payers/hospitals) have no integration incentive [#J]
  • Structural evolution: The orphan-drug chain is bifurcating — gene therapy entrants are compressing time-to-market and threatening long-duration replacement-therapy revenue models; Sobi's haemophilia franchise faces the most acute structural pressure [#A]
  • Single largest positional risk: Gene therapy displacement of SOBI's haemophilia products (particularly haemophilia A/B extended-interval therapies) represents the highest-probability structural shift — not a tail risk but a base-case timing question [#J]
  • ⚡ RARE: Sobi's combined access infrastructure + established rare-disease HTA navigation capability across 30+ markets is genuinely scarce at scale — few mid-cap specialists have built it; this is the chain-positioning asset that large-pharma in-licensors value most [#J]

§1 — Value Chain Map + Stage Table

The orphan/rare-disease pharmaceutical value chain differs structurally from mass-market pharma: patient populations are small (EU definition ≤5/10,000; US ≤200,000), the commercial logic is reimbursement-access rather than volume-driven, and the chain is best conceptualised as hub-and-spoke rather than linear — the specialist commercialisation hub links heterogeneous upstream (biotech discovery, CDMO manufacturing, raw material) and downstream (hospital pharmacy, HTA payer, patient/advocacy) spokes.

  • Structural shape: The orphan-drug value chain is hub-and-spoke with the commercialisation/licensing hub at centre, not a simple linear production chain — value aggregation occurs through IP assembly and market access expertise, not manufacturing throughput [#J]
  • Stage count: Seven analytically distinct stages run from raw biological inputs to patient receipt — each with materially different capital intensity, margin profile, and competitive structure [#I]
  • Hub identity: The mid-chain commercialisation stage — where Sobi sits — captures the highest value-added because it assembles regulatory approval, reimbursement navigation, medical education, and distribution logistics [#J]
  • Separation dynamic: In rare disease, discovery and manufacturing are frequently decoupled from commercialisation — small-cap biotechs originate assets, CDMOs manufacture, and specialists like Sobi commercialise — this is the opposite of mass-pharma's vertical integration model [#I]

§2 — Sobi's Position Within the Chain

  • Multi-stage presence: Sobi is a multi-stage operator spanning late-stage R&D (Stage 4) through commercialisation (Stage 6), with selective manufacturing (Stage 2) for specific legacy products and partial distribution ownership (Stage 7) via patient-support infrastructure [#J]
  • Economic identity: Despite multi-stage presence, Sobi's economic identity is firmly Stage 6 — commercial revenue and margins dominate the P&L; R&D is a cost centre feeding Stage 6; manufacturing is largely outsourced [#J]
  • Upstream integration (partial): Sobi owns or co-owns manufacturing for some haematology/immunology products (Kineret production partially in-house via its Stockholm biologics facility) but relies on CDMOs for the majority of biological drug substance for newer pipeline assets [#I]
  • Sub: The Stockholm facility provides some supply security for legacy products but does not represent competitive scale vs dedicated CDMOs [#I]
  • Upstream integration (in-licensing): Sobi's primary "upstream" move is in-licensing from Stage 3-4 biotechs — it acquires late-stage or recently approved assets and commercialises them; this is IP-stage integration rather than physical manufacturing integration [#J]
  • Sub: Key partnerships — AstraZeneca (ticagrelor, legacy), Bioverativ/Sanofi (haemophilia A/B — Elocta/Alprolix), Apellis (iptacopan, complement) demonstrate the licensing hub model [#A]
  • Downstream integration (partial): Sobi runs patient-support programmes, patient registries, and nurse-support services in many markets — partial Stage 7 ownership that builds switching costs at the patient/HCP level rather than logistics ownership [#A]
  • Sub: This is differentiated from mass-pharma distribution: Sobi's patient programmes create relational assets; they do not own distribution infrastructure [#I]
  • Integration vs peer pattern: Relative to large-pharma rare-disease divisions (AstraZeneca/Alexion, Sanofi Genzyme), Sobi is less integrated upstream (less in-house discovery/manufacturing) but more commercially focused; relative to pure-play small-cap biotechs, Sobi is more integrated downstream (multi-market commercial infrastructure vs single-market launches) [#J]
  • In-licensing model vulnerability: ❌ Sobi's Stage 4-6 model depends on a continuous flow of licensable assets from Stage 3-4 biotechs — if biotech funding dries up or larger pharma bids more aggressively for the same assets, Sobi's pipeline feed is disrupted [#A]
  • Geographic footprint: Sobi's Stage 6 infrastructure covers 30+ markets including all major EU5 + Nordics + select CEE/LatAm/MEA — this breadth is a structural advantage in attracting in-licensing partners who need global rare-disease commercialisation without building their own network [#J]
  • Capital intensity position: By operating primarily in Stage 5-6 (regulatory + commercial), Sobi is lower capital-intensity than Stage 1-2 operators — SG&A-heavy, not capex-heavy — which supports free cash flow conversion relative to revenue [#I]
  • Sub: Stage 6 capex intensity is typically 3-8% of revenue vs Stage 2 CDMO capex at 15-25% of revenue; Sobi's capex/revenue ratio is consistent with Stage 6 norms [#I]
  • Positional concentration risk: Sobi's concentrated exposure to haemophilia A/B products within Stage 6 means that gene-therapy displacement of that franchise would not just be a product-level loss but a Stage 6 revenue de-concentration event affecting the P&L disproportionately [#A]

Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on Sobi's positional choice is that single-stage specialisation in commercialisation is replicable by any large pharma that chooses to allocate commercial resources to rare disease — the "rare-disease expertise" moat depends on institutional knowledge and relationships that are observable, learnable, and recruitable; if Roche/Novartis/AstraZeneca decided to fully replicate Sobi's Stage 6 capabilities organically, the 5-10 year build time is the only structural barrier [#J]

§4 — Margin Distribution Across the Chain

  • Margin pool location: The majority of economic profit in the orphan/rare-disease value chain accrues at Stage 5-6 — commercialisation and IP management — not at upstream manufacturing or downstream distribution stages [#J]
  • Manufacturing compression: Stage 1-2 (raw materials + CDMO) is margin-compressed by capital intensity and commoditisation pressure; CDMO blended gross margins 25-45% (Lonza FY24 ~40%; Samsung Bio ~45%) lag Stage 6 significantly [#I]
  • Discovery destruction: Stage 3 (early R&D) is a value sink at the asset level — most assets fail; portfolio-level EBIT margins are deeply negative; value is realised only by surviving assets moving downstream, not at Stage 3 itself [#I]
  • Clinical development cost: Stage 4 clinical trials are capital-destructive during execution — Phase II/III rare-disease trial costs typically $50-500m per asset — but successful assets generate 10x+ NPV vs trial cost, creating extreme skewness; Stage 4 is a toll gate, not a value accrual point [#A]
  • Stage 6 apex: Orphan drug commercialisation at Stage 6 generates gross margins 65-80% for differentiated biologics — Sobi's reported blended gross margin ~68-72% is consistent with this range, confirming correct stage occupation [#J]
  • Sub: Mass-market generic pharma at Stage 6 achieves 30-50% gross — the orphan premium is 20-30pp structural margin lift, driven by pricing power and small-population commercial economics [#I]
  • Stage 7 thinness: Distribution/homecare generates 5-15% gross margins — McKesson/AmerisourceBergen specialty distribution operates at ~3-8% gross; specialty pharmacy at 10-20%; Sobi correctly avoids margin-thin logistics ownership [#I]
  • EBIT vs gross divergence: Stage 6 gross margins are high but EBIT margins are moderated by R&D reinvestment and SG&A — Sobi's EBIT margin ~15-22% represents the gap between 70%+ gross and high operating expense; this is structurally normal for a mid-cap specialist that has not yet achieved full scale leverage [#J]

  • Sobi's margin capture is structurally appropriate: Stage 6 is the right stage to own in orphan pharma — the company's gross margin is in line with the stage's theoretical range [#J]
  • EBIT gap to sector leaders: Sobi's EBIT margin (~15-22%) is below Stage 6 theoretical ceiling of 35% — the delta reflects mid-cap scale penalties on SG&A + ongoing R&D reinvestment; scale leverage would theoretically push EBIT toward 25-30% if the revenue base doubled [#A]
  • Margin pressure vector: The primary Stage 6 margin pressure is HTA/reference pricing tightening across EU markets — NICE, IQWiG, HAS, and AIFA are all applying more systematic cost-effectiveness scrutiny to orphan products; gross-to-EBIT conversion is being compressed at the pricing line [#A]
  • Biosimilar erosion: For Sobi's older biologics (Kineret/anakinra, legacy haemophilia concentrates), biosimilar or competitor entry compresses Stage 6 margins over time — net price erosion of 5-15% per year post-patent is the orphan-biologics historical rate, slower than mass-market but real [#I]
  • Gene therapy margin model: If gene therapies (one-and-done curative treatments) capture haemophilia share, the Stage 6 margin model changes structurally — one-time pricing at €1-3m per patient is high absolute margin but revenue is episodic, not annuity; this is a business model shift, not just a pricing change [#A]

Sceptical bullet: The stability of Stage 6's margin advantage over Stage 2 may be narrowing structurally as CDMOs consolidate (Lonza/Samsung/Fujifilm capacity buildout), reducing CDMO margins relative to Sobi — but also as payer sophistication increases, compressing the commercialisation margin; the gap that makes Stage 6 attractive may narrow from both sides simultaneously [#J]

§5 — Power Dynamics + Pricing Leverage

  • Power verdict: Sobi holds above-average pricing power within its product stage — orphan product economics structurally favour sellers in countries with unmet-need acknowledgment — but faces structural offset from monopsonistic public payers in its core EU markets [#J]
  • Pricing power — product-level driver: Sobi's drugs in categories with no approved alternatives (Kineret in SJIA flares, Doptelet/avatrombopag in ITP, Elocta in severe haemophilia A) command list prices of €100k-€800k/patient/year — a level impossible in non-orphan disease [#J]
  • Sub: Orphan drug designation in EU/US provides 10-year market exclusivity / 7-year + regulatory advantages that structurally insulate pricing during exclusivity period [#I]
  • Pricing power — switching cost: Haemophilia and rare inflammatory patients have very high switching costs — established tolerability profile, dosing regimen familiarity, patient-physician comfort — making mid-therapy switches uncommon unless clinically forced [#A]
  • Pricing power — differentiation: Sobi's best-positioned assets have meaningful clinical differentiation (extended half-life haemophilia products, IL-1 inhibition in autoinflammatory) that reduces direct substitutability [#J]
  • Customer leverage — payer concentration: ❌ Sobi's primary "customers" are national health systems or hospital formulary committees — monopsonistic or near-monopsonistic buyers with increasing HTA power; in Germany, AMNOG review; in UK, NICE; in France, HAS/CEPS — all have demonstrated willingness to restrict or delay orphan reimbursement [#J]
  • Sub: Reference pricing linkages across EU markets mean a discount conceded in one country can mechanically reduce prices in others — cross-border price arbitrage risk is structural to EU market access [#I]
  • Customer leverage — hospital pharmacy: Hospital pharmacies that dispense Sobi's biologics are aggregated through group purchasing organisations (GPOs) and national tender systems in some markets — reducing Sobi's individual account leverage [#I]
  • Customer leverage — patient advocacy: In rare disease, organised patient advocacy groups have meaningful political leverage to push for reimbursement — this is a structural counterweight to payer monopsony; Sobi has strong advocacy relationships in haematology and autoinflammatory [#A]
  • Supplier leverage — CDMOs: ❌ Sobi's dependence on a small number of CDMOs for biological drug substance manufacturing means supplier leverage is moderate-to-high — switching a CDMO for a biologic requires re-validation, regulatory re-filing, and typically 18-36 months of transition time; CDMOs can negotiate hard on pricing at contract renewal [#I]
  • Sub: Lonza, Samsung Bio, and similar large CDMOs have pricing power during periods of high-utilisation — post-COVID capacity tightening was an example where CDMO negotiating leverage increased [#I]
  • Supplier leverage — IP originators: When Sobi in-licenses assets from biotech partners (e.g., Apellis for iptacopan in complement), the originator retains economics through royalties and milestone payments — effectively a permanent royalty-based supplier with leverage [#A]
  • Sub: Royalty rates for orphan in-licenses typically 10-20% of net sales — a meaningful structural cost that caps Sobi's effective gross margin on in-licensed products vs wholly-owned [#I]
  • Supplier leverage — plasma: For plasma-derived products, Sobi is a buyer in a global plasma supply market where CSL, Grifols, and Octapharma collectively dominate collection infrastructure — supply tightness (as seen in 2020-22) can raise input costs [#I]
  • Pricing power — segment comparison: Sobi's power is strongest in products with (a) no approved alternatives, (b) life-threatening indication, (c) small, organised patient communities; weakest where alternatives exist or where HTA bodies have established cost-per-QALY ceilings for the indication [#J]
  • Net power balance: Sobi's net position is moderately favourable but not structurally dominant — orphan pricing power is real but structurally capped by EU payer sophistication; CDMO and originator supplier leverage is structurally real and growing [#J]

Sceptical bullet: The apparent pricing power may overstate durable economics — EU payer reference pricing + IRA-type mechanisms propagating globally means the pricing environment for high-cost orphan drugs in 2026-2030 is structurally tighter than 2015-2022; the historical pricing achievement is not a reliable guide to future reimbursement dynamics [#J]

§6 — Competitive Map at Sobi's Stage

  • Competitive tier verdict: Stage 6 (orphan commercialisation) is moderately concentrated at the global level but effectively tiered — large-pharma orphan divisions outscale Sobi materially; Sobi is the largest pure-play European mid-cap specialist; the small-cap specialist tier is fragmented [#J]
  • Tier 1 — large pharma orphan divisions: AstraZeneca/Alexion (~$9bn orphan revenue), Sanofi Genzyme ($8bn+), Takeda Rare Disease ($6bn+), Roche/Genentech, Novartis — dramatically outscale Sobi on revenue, R&D spend, and geographic reach; compete in overlapping therapeutic areas (haematology, immunology) [#J]
  • Sub: Tier 1 has 5-10x Sobi's revenue at orphan-division level and can absorb higher trial costs, offering more competitive in-licensing bids [#I]
  • Tier 2 — mid-cap specialist: Sobi (~€1.8-2.0bn revenue FY24E), Recordati Rare Diseases, Ultragenyx (~$600m), Biomarin ($2bn+) — Sobi is a genuine leader within Tier 2; geographically differentiated (Sobi strong in EU/Nordics, Ultragenyx/Biomarin strong in US) [#J]
  • Sub: Sobi's European market-access infrastructure is arguably the deepest in Tier 2 for EU-centric rare disease — a structural differentiator vs US-centric peers [#J]
  • Tier 3 — small-cap specialists + single-asset companies: Hundreds of rare-disease focused biotechs in late-stage development — most lack multi-market commercial infrastructure; they are potential in-licensing targets or competitors only once approved [#I]
  • Scale-cost dimension: Sobi's R&D spend/revenue (~15-20%) is lower than Tier 1 peers (~20-25%) — a structural constraint on pipeline generation but appropriate for a commercialisation-focused model; the gap raises questions about long-term pipeline self-sufficiency [#A]
  • Differentiation dimension: Sobi's differentiation vs Tier 2 peers is primarily European market-access depth + haematology/autoinflammatory franchise concentration — narrow but deep; less therapeutically diverse than Sanofi Genzyme or Takeda Rare [#J]
  • Geographic dimension: Sobi is uniquely positioned as the leading EU-domiciled rare-disease commercialiser — Swedish regulatory access, European HTA relationship network, and CEE/Nordic commercial infrastructure are not matched by US-centric competitors [#J]
  • Customer-segment dimension: Sobi targets hospital/haematology/immunology specialist physicians as prescribers — a concentrated target audience that reduces SG&A burden vs mass-pharma; 500-1,500 prescribers per indication in major EU markets [#I]
  • Stage concentration: The Stage 6 orphan commercialisation market is moderately concentrated at the top (5 Tier 1 players control ~60-70% of global orphan drug revenue) but functionally fragmented at the mid-cap tier — no single mid-cap player has dominant market share across all therapeutic areas [#J]
  • Pricing-dimension competition: Competition at Stage 6 in orphan disease manifests primarily as clinical differentiation and access speed (who gets reimbursed first), not price competition — head-to-head price wars are rare in approved orphan products; HTA bodies drive outcomes [#I]
  • Key differentiation asset: Sobi's established haematology relationships (haemophilia treatment centres, HTC networks) create genuine sales force efficiency — lower cost-per-call vs a new entrant building haematology coverage from scratch [#A]

Sceptical bullet: Sobi's position as "largest pure-play EU rare-disease mid-cap" may be a scale-trap narrative — the EU market-access advantage is observable and replicable by any Tier 1 player willing to invest in local market infrastructure; if Tier 1 consolidation continues (further AZ/Alexion-style acquisitions), the mid-cap specialist tier may be structurally squeezed between scale competitors above and asset-light biotech-licensors below [#J]

§7 — Competitive Maps at Adjacent Stages + Vertical Integration Threats

  • Adjacent-stage verdict: The upstream CDMO and biotech originator stages are consolidating but not integrating into commercialisation; downstream hospital/payer stages are consolidating with no commercial integration incentive; the primary vertical integration threat is top-down from Tier 1 large-pharma orphan divisions integrating Sobi's own stage through acquisition [#J]

Upstream Stage — CDMO (Stage 2) Competitive Map

  • Supplier — leader: Lonza Group is the largest biologics CDMO globally (~$5bn biologics revenue) — sets industry pricing benchmarks; Samsung Biologics (#2, ~$1.5-2bn) is a fast-growing challenger with scale advantages [#I]
  • Sub: Catalent (Danaher-acquired 2024), Fujifilm Diosynthetics, and Rentschler complete the top tier; >30 mid-scale CDMOs compete at the margins [#I]
  • Supplier consolidation: The CDMO market is consolidating upward — Catalent acquisition by Danaher/Novo Holdings, Thermo Fisher acquisitions, Lonza expansion all concentrate capacity in fewer hands [#I]
  • Sub: Consolidation increases CDMO pricing leverage over clients like Sobi at contract renewals — a structural headwind to Sobi's manufacturing cost line [#A]
  • Supplier vertical integration: CDMOs have not historically integrated into drug commercialisation — their business model is manufacturing services, not brand/IP ownership — and this is unlikely to change; however, large CDMOs increasingly offer formulation, fill-finish, and regulatory filing support, creeping toward Stage 4 services [#I]
  • Sub: This represents process-stage integration (deeper into development services) not product-stage integration (owning commercial products) — Sobi is not threatened by CDMOs becoming commercialisers [#I]

Upstream Stage — Biotech Originator (Stage 3-4) Competitive Map

  • Originator structure: The Stage 3-4 biotech originator market is highly fragmented — thousands of biotechs globally, hundreds in rare disease; Sobi competes with all large-pharma orphan divisions for the same pool of late-stage assets [#J]
  • Originator consolidation: The biotech funding cycle drives originator pipeline availability — 2022-24 biotech bear market created a buyer's market for in-licensing; 2025 recovery is tightening asset availability and raising deal prices [#A]
  • Originator vertical integration threat: Some biotechs with approved orphan assets are choosing to commercialise independently in the US (building their own Stage 6 presence) rather than licensing — this reduces the pool of assets available to Sobi and to other commercialisers [#A]
  • Sub: The US commercialisation barrier is lower than EU (single FDA approval vs 27-country EU HTA landscape) — making self-commercialisation more attractive for US-focused biotech; Sobi's EU advantage may protect its in-licensing flow for EU rights even if US rights stay with originators [#J]

Downstream Stage — Hospital/Payer (Stage 7) Competitive Map

  • Customer — leader: In rare disease distribution, national wholesalers (Alliance Healthcare, PHOENIX Group, McKesson for US equivalents) handle logistics; hospital pharmacy is the functional gatekeeper [#I]
  • Customer consolidation: Hospital systems are consolidating into larger networks across EU (NHS trust mergers, German Krankenhausreform, French GHT groupings) — concentrating formulary decision-making power in fewer hands [#A]
  • Sub: Concentrated hospital formulary buyers increase per-account negotiating leverage vs suppliers like Sobi — a structural downward pressure on net realised prices [#I]
  • Customer vertical integration — payer/biosimilar: Some national health systems (NHS England, Italian SSN) are actively mandating biosimilar switching for biologics where biosimilars exist — this is a form of downstream customer exercising integrative control over the product selection process, effectively overriding clinical preference [#A]
  • Sub: This is structurally damaging to Sobi's older products (Kineret biosimilar entry) but irrelevant for assets under orphan exclusivity [#I]
  • Customer vertical integration — hospital in-house: Hospital in-house compounding or hospital formulary preference for cost-alternatives (where clinically acceptable) is a low-probability threat in rare disease — the specialist nature of haemophilia treatment centres makes wholesale substitution unlikely without clinical evidence [#I]

Top-Down Vertical Integration Threat (Tier 1 large pharma → Sobi's stage)

  • ⚡ RARE: Acquisition threat: The most credible vertical integration "threat" at Sobi's stage is not a competitor integrating from below or above — it is a Tier 1 large-pharma acquirer valuing Sobi's EU Stage 6 infrastructure as an acquisition target; Sobi has been the subject of acquisition speculation (AZ/Alexion discussions cited in trade press), which structurally means its positional value is recognised externally [#J]
  • Sub: At ~€5-7bn market cap (approximate 2025-26 range), Sobi is acquirable by any Tier 1 orphan division — this creates an option floor on valuation but also creates strategic uncertainty for independent execution [#A]

Sceptical bullet: The most credible vertical integration vector is top-down acquisition by Tier 1, not organic integration by CDMOs or hospital systems — if Sobi remains independent, it must generate returns from Stage 6 economics without the scale advantages of Tier 1; the independence premium only holds if Sobi's organic ROIC exceeds what an acquirer would pay in synergies [#J]

§8 — Structural Changes + Evolution

  • Evolutionary verdict: The orphan-drug value chain is undergoing parallel restructuring across three dimensions — therapeutic (gene therapy disruption of replacement-therapy models), regulatory (HTA tightening + joint EU HTAR assessment), and commercial (biosimilar biologics entry + payer sophistication) — all of which create headwinds for Sobi's current positioning [#J]
  • Stage consolidation — upstream: CDMO Stage 2 is consolidating — top-5 players capturing larger share of global biologics manufacturing; this concentrates supplier power and reduces Sobi's CDMO optionality at contract renewals [#A]
  • Stage consolidation — commercialisation: Tier 1 large-pharma orphan divisions are growing faster via M&A than organic pipeline — AstraZeneca (Alexion), Sanofi (Translate Bio + pipeline), Takeda (Shire acquisition legacy) — concentrating Stage 6 share in fewer hands; this squeezes the mid-cap specialist tier [#J]
  • Vertical integration trend — Tier 1 downward: Tier 1 players are integrating their own manufacturing through M&A (Novo Holdings/Catalent, Roche/CMC manufacturing) — partially insulating themselves from CDMO leverage; Sobi does not have scale to replicate this [#I]
  • Disintermediation — DTC/patient direct: In rare disease, DTC is not a meaningful disintermediation threat — products require physician prescription and hospital dispensing; however, digital patient registries and remote dosing programmes represent a form of direct patient engagement that Sobi is building [#A]
