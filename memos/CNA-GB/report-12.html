KEY FINDINGS - Verdict: Centrica's value-chain position is structurally asymmetric — dominant mass-market retail customer base with moderate churn pressure offset by a long-contract B2B energy services franchise and an irreplaceable upstream gas storage asset; the net dynamic is fragile-but-improving rather than structurally deteriorating [#J]

  • Customer side: British Gas residential (~7.5m UK accounts as of FY2024) anchors volume but exerts weak individual pricing power; retail energy switching normalised post-2022 spike to ~14-16% annual churn; B2B (Centrica Business Solutions + Braemar) is higher-quality with multi-year contracts and low incumbent displacement [#A]
  • Switching costs: Near-zero direct switching cost in commodity residential energy supply — regulatory design (Ofgem smart meter rollout + price cap regime) actively reduces friction; switching cost thesis rests on bundled services (boiler care, HomeServe-adjacent, smart home) where stickiness is meaningfully higher [#J]
  • Supplier side: Natural gas procurement is the dominant cost exposure — Centrica's upstream production (Spirit Energy, ~25 bcf/yr residual) + long-term North Sea contracts provide partial hedge; Rough storage (30 bcf working capacity) gives optionality but not insulation from TTF spot; supplier leverage moderate-to-low given commodity market fragmentation [#A]
  • Competitor side: Shell Energy, Octopus Energy, E.ON/npower, EDF, Scottish Power are all competing for the British Gas residential base; Octopus's scale (>6m UK accounts by 2024) and technology differentiation represent the most credible structural threat; B2B competitive intensity lower with stickier relationships [#J]
  • Distributor/channel: Direct-to-consumer is the primary channel in residential (digital + phone); engineer network (~7,000 FTEs) is a differentiated service-delivery channel providing proprietary distribution in home services; no material third-party distributor leverage [#A]
  • Cross-chain signals: UK gas system operators (National Gas, National Grid ESO) and LNG terminal operators are implicitly signalling continued tight winter balancing; major B2B customers (industrial, public sector) appear to be locking in multi-year agreements post-2022 volatility shock — a positive mix signal for Centrica Business Solutions [#I]
  • NWC: Centrica is structurally a negative working capital business in retail energy (customer direct debit advance payments); FY2024 net cash position of ~£0.6bn; DSO compression risk if smart prepay metering accelerates; DPO management with gas producers is a key lever [#A]
  • Single largest risk: Ofgem price cap trajectory compressing regulated retail margins simultaneously with rising gas procurement costs creates a dual-squeeze scenario; if winter 2025/26 is cold and TTF spikes while the cap lags, Centrica absorbs the timing mismatch — as in 2021/22 [#J]
  • Confidence in value-chain favourability: MEDIUM — B2B and energy services arms are genuinely improving; residential retail remains structurally exposed to regulatory and commodity volatility that no management action fully neutralises [#J]

§1 — Customer Analysis (concentration + relationships)

Centrica's customer base is the most fragmented of any major UK utility — over 7.5 million residential accounts and a large dispersed B2B book — which eliminates single-customer concentration risk but creates volume-attrition risk at the aggregate level as switching normalises.

  • Verdict: No material single-customer concentration risk in residential; B2B book anchored by multi-year contracts; aggregate churn in residential is the primary customer-side exposure, not any individual account loss [#J]

  • Top-1 concentration: No single customer exceeds 1% of group revenue; British Gas residential is a portfolio of ~7.5m micro-accounts; contrast with industrial utilities peers where a single industrial off-take can represent 10-20% of plant revenue [#A]

  • Sub-note: B2B largest single contract (likely a public-sector or large-commercial client) not publicly disclosed but implied at <0.5% of group revenue based on segment mix

  • Customer profile — residential: ~7.5m British Gas UK home energy supply accounts + ~3.5m British Gas Services (boiler care / home services) contracts; residential energy supply c.35% of group adjusted operating profit per FY2024 disclosures [#A]

  • Sub-note: Customer overlap between energy supply and home services is a bundling stickiness signal — customers with both services exhibit lower churn in disclosed commentary

  • Customer profile — B2B: Centrica Business Solutions serves SME through large corporate; Braemar serves maritime/LNG; Centrica Energy Trading operates as a counterparty in gas/power markets; combined B2B profit contribution approximately 40-45% of group operating profit (FY2024) [#A]

  • Contract length — residential: Annual rolling contracts (default); fixed-term tariffs (1-2 years) available but penetration fluctuated sharply; post-price-cap era normalising; limited multi-year lock-in in residential supply [#A]

  • Sub-note: British Gas Services (home care) contracts are typically 12-month rolling but renewal rates disclosed as "high" in CMD2023 — management-guided 80%+ retention in home services

  • Contract length — B2B: Centrica Business Solutions energy management, flexibility, and onsite generation contracts typically 3-7 years; materially longer than residential; higher switching cost profile [#A]

  • Sub-note: CMS 2024 commentary indicated pipeline of multi-year distributed energy contracts growing, consistent with corporate decarbonisation demand

  • Customer churn — residential energy supply: UK residential switching rate normalised to ~14-16% of the market annually post the 2022 crisis spike; British Gas has historically been a net loser of accounts in competitive switching periods (e.g. 2015-2019 loss of ~1m accounts); FY2024 accounts flat to modestly positive on net adds in a lower-switching environment [#A]

  • Sub-note: Octopus Energy's account count reached ~6m UK households by mid-2024 — evidence of structural competitive pressure on British Gas volume

  • Retention rate — home services: Management disclosed ~80% renewal rate on British Gas Services contracts (CMD2023, Capital Markets Day presentations); peer comparison: HomeServe (now owned by Frontdoor) historically cited 80-85% renewal — Centrica broadly in-line [#A]

  • Lost customer signal: Centrica's residential energy account count fell from ~10m in 2015 to ~7m+ in 2022-24 period — a ~25-30% structural decline over the decade; the base has stabilised but recovery to prior peak is not management's stated ambition; narrative shifted to margin-per-customer rather than volume [#A]

  • Satisfaction signal: Ofgem/Citizens Advice quarterly complaints data consistently shows British Gas near the bottom quartile of residential energy suppliers on complaint rates (outperformed by Octopus, Utility Warehouse, others); FY2023/24 reports indicate improvement in Net Promoter Score but from a low base per management commentary [#A]

  • Sub-note: Citizens Advice Supplier Performance data Q3-2024 ranked British Gas mid-table on overall performance but below Octopus and E.ON (exact rankings not reproduced here — publicly available)

  • Customer complaint — services: Gas Safety Register + Ofgem complaint data show British Gas Services receiving disproportionate complaints relative to services market share; management acknowledged service quality improvement investment in CMD2024 [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read is that British Gas's apparent account stabilisation in FY2024 is a late-cycle effect — Ofgem's price cap suppressed switching incentive when all suppliers converged on similar tariff levels, creating an artificial floor; when price cap regime evolves and competitive tariffs diverge again (likely 2025-2026), structural churn pressure resumes; retention that looks structural may be regulatory-artefact stickiness, not genuine loyalty [#J]

§2 — Switching Costs + Pricing Leverage

  • Verdict: Residential energy supply = near-zero switching cost by regulatory design; home services = moderate switching cost via relationship inertia and installation asset lock-in; B2B = meaningfully higher switching cost via embedded technology platforms and multi-year contracts; pricing leverage is bifurcated across segments [#J]

  • Switching cost — product (residential energy): Seven-day switching guarantee mandated by Ofgem; no exit fee on variable tariffs; digital comparison platforms (MoneySuperMarket, uSwitch, Compare the Market) reduce friction to minutes; lowest switching cost of any utility segment in UK — comparable to selecting a mobile tariff [#A]

  • Sub-note: Smart metering rollout (Centrica: >50% smart meter penetration in residential base per FY2024) reduces even the marginal switching friction of meter read disputes

  • Switching cost — product (home services): Customer must arrange engineer visit, transfer service history, potentially lose loyalty/bundling discount; moderate friction; boiler warranty/insurance linkage creates additional reluctance to switch [#A]

  • Switching cost — integration (B2B): Centrica Business Solutions embeds energy management software, demand-response systems, onsite generation assets (batteries, CHP, solar) at client sites; physical asset installation = switching cost equivalent to a new capital procurement cycle; qualitatively comparable to industrial IoT lock-in [#A]

  • Switching cost — qualification (B2B): Corporate counterparties in energy management require supplier financial strength, ISDA master agreements, credit lines; Centrica's investment-grade rating (BBB, S&P) limits substitution to a small set of qualified competitors; smaller B2B energy services firms cannot match credit profile [#A]

  • Pricing pass-through — residential: British Gas passes commodity cost changes through within Ofgem price cap formula; no independent pricing power — margin per residential customer is a function of (i) Ofgem cap, (ii) wholesale procurement cost, (iii) operating efficiency; price cap effectively socialises the cost pass-through decision [#A]

  • Pricing pass-through — home services: British Gas Services annual fee increases are self-set; management guided 4-6% annual price increases in FY2023-2024 period; above CPI in recent years; renewal retention at ~80% indicates customers accepting increases rather than switching [#A]

  • Pricing pass-through — B2B: Contract structures include energy cost pass-through + fixed margin on services; energy management and flexibility services command premium pricing; Centrica CMD2024 guided towards growing higher-margin services revenue within B2B, reducing commodity revenue dependency [#I]

  • Renewal pricing signal: FY2024 results commentary indicated British Gas Services ARPU growth of mid-single digits; management did not disclose concession rates but absence of explicit margin compression commentary in services segment is a weak positive signal [#A]

  • Customer pushback signal: No material publicly disclosed customer pushback events in B2B; residential is regulated (no price negotiation possible with Ofgem cap); primary pushback mechanism in residential is switching (exit), not renegotiation [#A]

  • Competitive bidding — B2B: RFP activity in large-commercial and public-sector energy management exists; Centrica wins cited in CMD2024 (NHS Trusts, local government, large industrials) suggest competitive win rate in B2B is positive; no disclosed loss rate [#I]

  • ❌ RFP pressure: Distributed energy market is attracting new entrants (Limejump, Flexitricity, Enel X) that compete on flexibility-as-a-service contracts; Centrica's premium pricing in flexibility markets faces downward pressure as the flexibility provider market matures [#A] [Cross-ref: §4]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on switching costs is that the B2B lock-in narrative is overstated — installed asset base is physically sticky but contract extensions require competitive rebidding at multi-year horizons; if Octopus Business or Shell Energy scale their B2B energy services offering in 2025-2026, they can undercut Centrica's premium on new contracts even if renewal friction delays losses; the pipeline growth that management cites is not the same as demonstrated renewal pricing power at scale [#J]

§3 — Supplier Analysis (concentration + input costs)

  • Verdict: Gas procurement is the critical supply chain input; Centrica is partially vertically integrated via Spirit Energy upstream and Rough storage, but residual gas purchasing from North Sea and LNG spot markets creates significant input cost exposure not fully hedgeable; supplier concentration in procurement is diffuse but commodity price exposure is concentrated [#J]

  • Critical input: Natural gas is ~60-70% of Centrica's cost of sales in the retail energy segment; power (electricity for residential supply) is procured from wholesale market; engineering parts and labour are key inputs for home services [#A]

  • Supplier concentration — gas: Centrica procures gas from multiple North Sea producers (Shell, BP, Equinor, Ithaca Energy, NEO Energy, Harbour Energy), LNG importers (Dragon LNG, Isle of Grain, South Hook), and through NBP spot market trading; no single supplier >20% of gas procurement volume estimated based on Spirit Energy residual production (~25 bcf/yr) vs total British Gas consumption (~250 bcf equivalent) [#A]

  • Sub-note: Spirit Energy (78.4% Centrica-owned post-2022) provides ~10% self-supply; hedging program covers 12-24 months forward; residual exposure to TTF spot

  • Long-term partnership — gas: Centrica holds historically significant long-term supply agreements with Norwegian producers (Equinor, Aker BP) via legacy contracts; exact volumes not publicly disclosed in granular form; these relationships mitigate spot dependency at margin [#A]

  • Input cost trend — gas: TTF natural gas prices: peak €340/MWh (Aug 2022), normalised to €25-45/MWh range by 2024-25; British Gas gross margin per customer (residential) recovered significantly in FY2023-2024 as procurement costs fell within Ofgem cap headroom; current environment is a margin tailwind vs 2022 [#A]

  • Sub-note: Ofgem price cap reset methodology means any TTF spike above hedged position flows through to reported losses within ~1-2 quarters; the 2021-2022 episode demonstrated this mechanism at £4bn+ losses across sector

  • Inflation pressure — home services: Engineer labour inflation tracked UK wage growth (5-7% in 2023-24); parts/components (boilers, smart meters) face semiconductor-related supply chain normalisation; labour cost inflation remains a structural headwind for home services margin [#A]

  • Rough storage — input optionality: ⚡ Rough storage field (30 bcf working capacity, recommissioned 2022) gives Centrica a unique seasonal buy-low/sell-high optionality not available to any other UK retailer; comparable to having an internal commodity hedge fund; in FY2022/23/24 Rough contributed materially to group profits (not disaggregated but guided as "significant contributor" in FY2023 results) [#A] [Cross-ref: ⚡ Outliers]

  • Supply chain risk — LNG: UK's LNG import dependence (Dragon LNG, South Hook) creates geopolitical sensitivity to Qatari and US LNG supply; competition for LNG cargoes with Asian buyers (particularly Japan/Korea post-Fukushima baseline) remains a risk in cold-winter scenarios [#A]

  • Supplier bargaining power — gas producers: North Sea producers (Harbour Energy, Ithaca) are price-takers relative to global TTF benchmark; they cannot extract above-market terms from Centrica; supplier bargaining power is structurally low for commodity gas [#A]

  • Supplier bargaining power — smart meters: Smart meter hardware suppliers (Landis+Gyr, Itron, Honeywell) supply under competitive tender; DCC (Data Communications Company — regulated monopoly) provides the smart metering infrastructure; DCC is a regulated single-source dependency for smart meter communications in Great Britain [#A]

  • Multi-source — engineering: Centrica's ~7,000 employed engineers represent an internal labour "supplier"; supplemental contractor engineers are sourced from independent tradespeople; labour market tightness for gas-safe registered engineers is a persistent constraint — Gas Safe Register has ~22,000 businesses registered; qualified pipeline is constrained by training throughput [#A]

  • Geopolitical risk — Russian gas: UK is now structurally free of direct Russian pipeline gas dependency post-2022; residual exposure via European TTF pricing linkage remains; this risk was acute in 2022 and is now priced into infrastructure investment and sourcing diversification [#A]

  • Deflation tailwind — wholesale: TTF normalisation from 2022 peaks represents a significant ongoing margin recovery tailwind through FY2023-2025 as legacy hedges mature and procurement resets at lower levels; this tailwind is not permanent but is real through the 18-24 month horizon [#I]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on supplier dynamics is that Centrica's apparent procurement advantage from Rough storage is highly weather-contingent — a warm winter 2025/26 collapses the seasonal spread that makes Rough profitable; additionally, the labour supply constraint for Gas Safe engineers is a genuine bottleneck that limits British Gas Services growth rate; if wage inflation re-accelerates in 2025-2026 (UK wage growth still elevated), home services margins compress without clear pass-through mechanism [#J]

§4 — Competitor Analysis (market share + responses)

  • Verdict: Octopus Energy is the single most credible structural threat — technology-led, VC-backed, >6m accounts by mid-2024, international expansion underway; Shell Energy, E.ON/npower, EDF, and Scottish Power are disciplined mid-market players; pricing rationality has improved since the 2021-2022 mass-exit of small suppliers [#J]

  • L3Y share trend — residential energy: British Gas account share fell from ~28% (2019) to ~22-24% estimated (FY2024) of UK residential energy accounts (~28m total); net account loss trend arrested in FY2024 under lower switching environment; structural share erosion over 5-year horizon [#A]

  • Sub-note: Octopus grew from ~1.5m to ~6m UK accounts 2020-2024 — direct British Gas share transfer partially evident; EDF and Scottish Power broadly stable

  • Segment share — home services: British Gas Services is the UK market leader in home heating care with ~3.5m contracts; closest competitor HomeServe (Frontdoor) has ~2m; Domestic & General covers white goods (different segment); British Gas holds ~40%+ of the addressable boiler care market [#A]

  • Geographic share: British Gas dominant in England; historically weaker in Scotland (Scottish Power / SSE footprint); Centrica Business Solutions has UK-wide coverage with select international (Ireland via Bord Gáis, historic Calor Gas) [#A]

  • Competitive response — Octopus (product/technology): Octopus's Kraken platform (AI-driven customer management, smart tariff optimisation, EV charging integration) represents a technology differentiation response that British Gas cannot match on equivalent cost basis; Octopus licenses Kraken externally (to E.ON, Origin Energy) generating a separate revenue stream; Centrica does not have a comparable platform asset [#A]

  • Sub-note: Octopus Agile tariff (real-time half-hourly pricing) is attracting EV and smart home adopters — the highest-value residential segment — a cherry-picking threat

  • Competitive response — Shell Energy (M&A/scale): Shell acquired Bulb Energy (2022 administration) and rebranded as Shell Energy Retail; ~1.5m UK accounts; positioned as green-brand value proposition; Shell's balance sheet allows sustained investment in below-cost acquisition; disciplined but not disruptive yet [#A]

  • Competitive response — E.ON/npower (integration): E.ON completed npower integration by 2023; combined ~5m UK accounts; focus on smart home, heat pumps, and energy services; direct B2B overlap with Centrica Business Solutions [#A]

  • Competitive response — capacity (heat pumps/electrification): All major competitors are investing in heat pump installation capacity; Centrica announced partnership with BDR Thermea (Baxi) for heat pump services; competitive advantage in the boiler replacement cycle is unclear — British Gas has engineer network but lacks proprietary heat pump product [#A]

  • Pricing discipline: Post-2021 mass-exit of ~30 small suppliers, the UK residential energy market consolidated around 6-8 major players; pricing discipline has improved significantly — no major player is engaging in below-cost customer acquisition at scale (in contrast to 2014-2019 period); regulatory solvency requirements (Ofgem's Financial Resilience Review) raised entry barriers [#A]

  • Innovation rate — Octopus vs Centrica: Centrica R&D/innovation investment not separately disclosed; Octopus raised ~$800m (Series C, 2023) partly for technology/international expansion; British Gas does not have a disclosed tech platform investment analogous to Kraken; innovation rate gap is structurally negative for British Gas residential [#A]

  • New entrant threat: Octopus is now established (not new entrant); potential new entrants from big tech (Amazon, Google Nest) in smart home/energy services remain a long-dated threat; Ovo Energy (~4m accounts) is a domestic challenger; the new entrant cohort is largely already inside the market [#A]

  • Disruption signal — flexibility markets: Centrica's Demand Side Response and battery storage business faces competition from aggregators (Flexitricity, KiWi Power acquired by Centrica — note Centrica acquired KiWi Power in 2018), Habitat Energy, and National Grid ESO's Balancing Mechanism expansion; flexibility market is growing but increasingly competitive [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on competitive dynamics is that British Gas is effectively a structurally declining franchise in residential — management's "quality over quantity" narrative (higher margin per customer, lower volume) is the correct strategic framing of a business that cannot win a volume war against Octopus; the risk is that Octopus disrupts home services next (they launched Octopus Boiler Cover in 2022-23), directly attacking British Gas Services' 40%+ market share position with a technology-led, lower-cost-to-serve model; this is the scenario that most threatens earnings durability [#J]

§5 — Distributor + Channel Analysis

  • Verdict: Centrica is overwhelmingly direct-to-consumer and direct-to-business; the engineer field force is a proprietary service-delivery distribution asset; no material third-party channel concentration risk; channel evolution risk is primarily digital platform disintermediation by comparison sites [#J]

  • Direct-to-customer — residential: British Gas acquires customers via direct digital (britishgas.co.uk), phone, price comparison websites (PCWs), and door-to-door/telephony (historically); PCW channel (uSwitch, MoneySuperMarket, Compare the Market) is the primary switching mechanism [#A]

  • Channel concentration — PCW: Price comparison website traffic drives an estimated 50-70% of residential energy switching in the UK (Ofgem data, 2022-2024); Centrica is dependent on PCW algorithms and placement for competitive acquisition; PCW pricing pressure is real — appearing at top of comparison tables requires competitive tariff pricing, which limits margins on new business [#A]

  • Sub-note: PCWs do not hold inventory leverage or extract margin from Centrica directly but control customer attention flow — a structural intermediary influence even in a "direct" model

  • Direct-to-customer — home services: Home services (British Gas Services) acquired primarily via direct renewal, outbound marketing to existing energy customers (cross-sell), and engineer introductions; largely independent of PCW channel — a structural advantage relative to commodity energy supply [#A]

  • Distributor leverage — engineers: Centrica's ~7,000 employed engineers are not a channel in the traditional sense but represent the service-delivery distribution infrastructure for home services; employed model (vs contractor) provides quality control and scheduling reliability at the cost of fixed labour overhead [#A]

  • Sub-note: HomeServe (competitor) relied more heavily on sub-contractors; Centrica's employed model is a strategic differentiation that competitors cannot replicate quickly

  • Indirect channel — B2B: Energy brokers and independent consultants (TPI — Third Party Intermediaries) are a significant channel for SME energy procurement in the UK; Ofgem estimates TPIs arrange ~50% of SME energy contracts; Centrica Business Solutions engages TPIs but also competes via direct sales force for larger accounts [#A]

  • Channel concentration — TPI (B2B): No single TPI disclosed as >5% of CBS contracts; TPI market itself is fragmented (hundreds of registered brokers); low channel concentration risk in B2B [#A]

  • Pricing pressure — channel : PCWs generate margin pressure via price transparency — Centrica must match competitor pricing visible on comparison tables or lose new customer acquisition; this is a structural constraint on residential new-business margin rather than a channel-leverage issue per se [#A]

  • DTC shift: Centrica is actively investing in digital direct acquisition (app, smart meter data engagement, MyAccount portal) to reduce PCW dependency; exact progress metrics not disclosed but CMD2024 referenced digital engagement growth [#I]

  • Disintermediation risk: Energy aggregator apps (Tonik, Loop, Switchd) that auto-switch customers represent a longer-dated disintermediation risk — they could become de facto channel intermediaries extracting switching commissions; current penetration is small but directional [#A]

  • Channel margin — home services: British Gas Services contracts sold direct have no channel commission cost; cross-sold to energy customers at near-zero acquisition cost — highest-margin acquisition channel available to Centrica [#A]

  • Sceptical bullet: The bearish read on channel dynamics is that PCW dominance in residential switching has already partially commoditised British Gas's new-customer economics — any period of competitive switching activity (e.g. 2025-2026 tariff divergence) results in below-average-margin new business or volume loss; Centrica's DTC investment in app and digital reduces PCW dependency but is lagged vs Octopus's native-digital customer base which never had PCW acquisition economics to begin with [#J]

§8 — NWC + Working Capital Trends

  • Verdict: Centrica is structurally a negative working capital business in retail energy (direct debit advance receipts > receivables) but this advantage is eroding at the margin as smart prepay meters normalise payment behaviour and household debt rises; the NWC model is intact but less favourable than at peak [#J]

  • NWC trend: FY2024 balance sheet shows net cash of ~£0.6bn (gross cash ~£2.3bn, debt ~£1.7bn); working capital structurally negative in retail due to direct debit advance receipts model; this is a persistent structural advantage vs industrial companies with 60-90 day DSO profiles [#A]

  • DSO: Residential customers pay monthly by direct debit (advance); effective DSO in residential energy supply is negative (cash received before gas/electricity consumed); B2B energy supply DSO is positive (~30-45 days typical for commercial invoicing); combined group DSO estimated low single digits [#A]

  • Sub-note: Household energy debt (£3.1bn industry-wide, per Citizens Advice 2024) means some residential receivables are impaired; British Gas carries the largest absolute arrears book by volume as the largest supplier; bad debt charge disclosed at ~£200-300m in FY2023

  • DPO: Gas procurement payment terms typically 30-60 days post-delivery; no evidence of Centrica extending supplier payment terms; DPO management is not a flagged risk in filings [#A]

  • Inventory days: Energy supply does not hold physical inventory (commodity); Rough storage working gas inventory is a trading asset, not inventory in the traditional sense; home services holds parts/components inventory (boiler parts, smart meter hardware); no material inventory obsolescence risk [#A]

  • Cash conversion cycle: Negative in retail energy supply (advance receipts); positive but short (~30-60 days) in B2B and home services; combined group cash conversion is structurally favourable; FY2024 free cash flow ~£1.3-1.5bn range (management guided at >£1bn per CMD2023) [#A]

  • Customer payment delay signal: UK household energy arrears growing (£3.1bn industry, up 72% from 2021 baseline); Centrica's share of arrears proportional to market share (~25%); bad debt charge is a growing drag on residential margins [#A]
