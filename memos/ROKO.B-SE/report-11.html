§1 — Value Chain Map

Roko operates in the industrial capital goods sector — broadly equipment design, manufacturing, and delivery of configured machinery or systems to industrial end-users. The chain from raw material to operational asset runs through several structurally distinct stages with sharply different economic characteristics.

  • Chain shape: Predominantly linear from raw material to end customer, with a service/aftermarket loop that feeds back value from the installed base — approximates a "linear chain with recurring-revenue tail" rather than a pure platform or hub [#J]
  • Sub: The service loop is strategically critical: it is where the most durable economic profit accrues in capital goods, and its size is proportional to installed-base depth

  • Sub: Platform characteristics are emerging at the data/connectivity layer (IoT-enabled monitoring, predictive maintenance software) but remain nascent and are not yet structurally dominant

  • Stage count: Six primary stages from raw material to end customer, plus one cross-cutting aftermarket/service stage that operates simultaneously across stages 4-6 [#J]

  • Raw input stage: Highly commoditised — steel, aluminium, copper, specialty alloys, electronic components — produced by large global materials companies (ArcelorMittal, Novelis, Codelco equivalents); margins thin (steel EBIT 3-8%, aluminium similar); no differentiation [#A]

  • Sub: Pricing is market-driven (LME/commodity indices); no single industrial assembler controls enough volume to dictate terms at this stage

  • Precision fabrication/casting stage: Moderately concentrated — specialist foundries, precision machinists, and metal fabricators; regional in footprint; capital-intensive (forge presses, CNC machining centres); gross margins typically 25-35%, EBIT 8-15% for higher-value castings [#A]

  • Sub: Some consolidation has occurred (e.g., Precision Castparts, Howmet Aerospace in aerospace-adjacent markets) but industrial capital goods foundries remain more fragmented

  • Component/sub-system manufacturing stage: Globally distributed, moderately concentrated by component type — motors (Nidec, WEG, ABB), drives (Danfoss, Siemens, Rockwell), seals/bearings (SKF, Schaeffler, NSK), hydraulics (Bosch Rexroth, Eaton, Parker) [#A]

  • Sub: This stage is the most globally integrated — component suppliers are themselves large multinationals with significant pricing leverage toward assemblers

  • Sub: Gross margins at leading component suppliers: 35-50%; EBIT 12-22% — materially better than raw materials but below the integration stage for best-in-class operators

  • System integration/assembly stage (Roko's primary stage): The engineering complexity and configurability layer — combines procured components with proprietary mechanical design, software/controls integration, and application engineering to produce a configured system or machine [#J]

  • Sub: Typical gross margins 35-55%; EBIT 12-25% at leading operators — the range is wide because differentiation varies enormously by niche

  • Sub: Capital intensity is moderate (assembly facilities, test rigs, engineering labour) versus raw fabrication but well below materials or component-level production

  • Distribution/channel stage: Fragmented and geographically segmented — industrial distributors (Würth, Rexel, Hagemeyer), specialist dealers, and direct sales forces depending on product complexity and order size [#A]

  • Sub: High-value configured capital goods increasingly sold direct — the distributor stage is being compressed as OEMs invest in direct sales capacity

  • Sub: Distribution margins thin: gross margin typically 20-30%; EBIT 3-8% — structurally the weakest stage in the chain

  • Installation/commissioning stage: Specialist labour-intensive — either performed by Roko's own field engineers or subcontracted EPC/installation contractors; margins here are service-rate driven (gross 30-45%) but volume is lumpy and project-dependent [#A]

  • Aftermarket/service stage: Structurally the most attractive recurring-revenue layer — spare parts (very high gross margin: 50-70%), service contracts, retrofits, and upgrades; access gated by installed-base ownership and proprietary service knowledge [#J]

  • Sub: Industry base rate for aftermarket as % of total OEM revenue: 20-45% depending on product complexity; best-in-class capital goods operators (e.g., Atlas Copco, Alfa Laval reference point) reach 35-45% aftermarket share with EBIT margins of 25-35%

§2 — Roko's Position Within the Chain

  • Positional verdict: Roko is a single-primary-stage specialist at the integration layer with partial downstream (aftermarket/service) extension — not a fully integrated multi-stage operator, but not a pure single-stage assembler either [#J]

  • Stage occupied: System integration / application engineering (Stage 4) is the primary revenue and margin driver; aftermarket/service (Stage 7) is the secondary and growing contributor [#A]

  • Sub: This is the canonical "smart OEM" position in industrial capital goods — full control of the engineering intellectual property, configuration complexity, and application knowledge

  • Sub: The position is structurally similar to Atlas Copco (Compressor Technique segment), Alfa Laval (Heat Transfer), or SPX Flow before breakup — all primarily occupying Stage 4 with a service tail

  • Upstream integration: Limited — Roko does not appear to own major component or sub-system suppliers; it is a net buyer of motors, drives, controls, and structural fabrications from independent suppliers [#A]

  • Sub: Some in-house fabrication capability (precision machining, sheet-metal work) is likely for critical or proprietary components, but this is partial, not strategic

  • Sub: Absence of upstream integration is the norm for mid-size capital goods OEMs — owning component supply chains requires scale and creates inventory/cycle risk

  • Downstream integration — distribution: Mixed — direct sales is the dominant channel for high-value configured products (consistent with the sector norm for complex capital goods), with some third-party distribution for standard product lines or geographically thin markets [#A]

  • Sub: Direct sales penetration is a structural positive — it captures distribution margin and deepens customer relationship, creating installation and aftermarket pull-through

  • Downstream integration — aftermarket: Partial but strategically important — Roko operates its own field service and spare-parts business; the degree to which this is proprietary (locked to Roko's installed base vs open to ISOs) is the critical variable [#J]

  • Sub: If spare parts are proprietary and service access gated, the aftermarket position creates a compounding lock-in that strengthens with every new OE sale — classic capital goods flywheel

  • Multi-stage span vs peers: Roko's integration depth appears broadly comparable to mid-size industrial capital goods OEMs — more integrated than pure contract manufacturers, less vertically integrated than Atlas Copco-level global leaders with captive distribution networks [#A]

  • Sub: No cohort/sector peer data available; this assessment is anchored on the structural norm for similarly-sized capital goods OEMs based on public filings and industry structure

  • Peer integration vs peer: Larger global capital goods OEMs (Spirax-Sarco, Graco, Atlas Copco at segment level) typically show higher aftermarket revenue penetration — 35-45% of revenue — versus smaller or younger OEMs at 15-25%; the gap reflects installed-base maturity [#A]

  • Single-stage focus risk: ❌ Concentration at Stage 4 without deep upstream control creates input cost pass-through risk in a commodity-price spike — Roko's margin is a spread between component costs and system selling price [#J]

  • Sub: In 2021-22 supply chain inflation cycle, mid-size industrial OEMs without upstream integration typically saw gross margin compression of 200-400bps before price recovery

  • Sceptical lens: What's the bearish read on Roko's positional choice? Through what mechanism might single-stage focus prove fragile? If leading component suppliers (ABB, Danfoss, Siemens) continue expanding their own system-integration capabilities, they close the gap between Stage 3 and Stage 4 — potentially commoditising the pure-integration layer Roko occupies without owning the component IP underneath [#I]

§4 — Margin Distribution Across the Chain

  • Margin pool verdict: The margin pool is heavily skewed toward Stages 4 (integration) and 7 (aftermarket/service) — together these two stages capture the majority of economic profit in the capital goods chain; Roko's position in both is structurally favourable [#J]

  • Stage 1 — raw materials: Structurally thin — gross margins 15-25%, EBIT 3-8%; commodity pricing, high capital intensity, cyclical; economic profit is episodically captured during commodity super-cycles but mean-reverts [#A]

  • Stage 2 — precision fabrication: Moderately thin — gross 25-35%, EBIT 8-15%; better than raw materials but still subject to overcapacity cycles; differentiation only for the most technically demanding geometries (aerospace, energy) [#A]

  • Stage 3 — component/sub-system manufacturing: Moderate-to-attractive — gross 35-50%, EBIT 12-22%; leading component suppliers (SKF, Danfoss, Parker) earn structurally above-average returns; differentiation through IP, brand, installed base [#A]

  • Sub: This stage benefits from network effects in standards — if your drive or seal becomes the reference specification, switching cost accrues to the component supplier, not the assembler

  • Stage 4 — system integration (Roko's primary): Attractive for differentiated operators — gross 35-55%, EBIT 12-25%; the wide range reflects heterogeneity by niche; operators with strong application engineering IP and sticky installed bases occupy the upper end [#J]

  • Sub: Atlas Copco Compressor Technique reported EBIT margin ~23% in 2023 — a useful upper-bound reference for a highly optimised integration business

  • Sub: Mid-size industrial OEMs with less niche depth typically land at 12-16% EBIT; Roko's actual position within this range is a key DD question

  • Stage 5 — distribution: ❌ Structurally the weakest stage — gross 20-30%, EBIT 3-8%; thin spreads, working capital intensity, competition from OEM direct [#A]

  • Sub: This stage is being systematically disrupted by OEM direct sales and digital procurement; margin pressure is structural, not cyclical

  • Stage 6 — installation/commissioning: Moderate returns, volatile — gross 30-45%, EBIT 8-15%; labour-rate driven; cyclical with capital expenditure programs; margin expansion limited by labour market tightness [#A]

  • Stage 7 — aftermarket/service: Structurally the highest-margin stage — spare parts gross margin 50-70%, service contracts 35-50%, EBIT 20-35% for best-in-class operators [#J]

  • Sub: Spirax-Sarco Steam Division aftermarket/service contribution underpins group EBIT margins of ~25% — among the highest in specialised industrial capital goods
  • Sub: The aftermarket stage is also the most recession-resistant: maintenance spending is less deferrable than new capital expenditure, creating counter-cyclical earnings stability

§5 — Power Dynamics + Pricing Leverage

  • Power verdict: Roko holds moderate-to-strong pricing power with end customers at the integration stage, constrained by sophisticated industrial procurement; supplier leverage over Roko is moderate and manageable for standard components but concentrated for proprietary sub-systems [#J]

  • Pricing power — differentiation: Application-specific engineering creates genuine differentiation — a configured system for a specific industrial process cannot be trivially substituted at point of replacement; this is the foundation of pricing power at Stage 4 [#J]

  • Sub: Specification-in ("spec-in") dynamic: if Roko engineers a system into a customer's process design, competitors must demonstrate equivalence to displace — a significant barrier at replacement and expansion

  • Pricing power — switching costs: High for installed-base customers — changing system supplier requires re-engineering, retraining, spare-parts restocking, and operational disruption; switching cost estimates in industrial capital goods typically translate to 2-5x annual maintenance spend as an implicit lock-in value [#A]

  • Sub: The switching cost compounds over the system lifetime: the longer the installed base ages, the more institutional knowledge of Roko's product accumulates in the customer's maintenance team

  • Pricing power — alternatives: Partial constraint — for greenfield projects, customers can evaluate alternatives across the competitive set; Roko's pricing power is strongest at the replacement/expansion cycle, weaker at greenfield new-project bids [#J]

  • Sub: Industry bid win rates at greenfield vs repeat: typically 30-50% at greenfield vs 60-80% at installed-base replacement — implying significant pricing premium opportunity at the replacement cycle

  • Pricing power — regulatory requirements: Sector-specific regulatory compliance (safety standards, environmental certifications, EU Machinery Directive, ATEX, PED, etc.) acts as a structural pricing floor — customers cannot substitute non-compliant systems, and compliance certification is a barrier to new entrants [#A]

  • Sub: Regulatory compliance requirements also create switching costs: customers who have certified Roko's systems into their safety cases face additional re-certification costs if they switch

  • Pricing power — customer concentration: Unknown without Q12 empirical data — structural analysis suggests industrial capital goods OEMs at Roko's scale typically serve a mix of concentrated large industrial customers and a fragmented long tail; concentration skews power toward large customers [#I]

  • Sub: If top-5 customers exceed 40-50% of revenue (a common threshold for concentrated industrials), large customers likely have procurement leverage to negotiate discount structures, extended payment terms, and price concessions

  • Segment-specific power — large EPC/infrastructure customers: ❌ Large EPCs and major industrial operators have significant procurement leverage — they run competitive tenders, maintain approved vendor lists with multiple qualified suppliers, and negotiate framework agreements with volume-based price reductions [#A]

  • Sub: EPC-sold equipment typically commands 5-15% lower net realised price than direct-sold equipment due to EPC procurement margin extraction

  • Segment-specific power — SME customers: Fragmented SME customers have minimal pricing leverage — they depend on Roko's application knowledge and aftermarket support; pricing is closer to list minus modest discount [#J]

  • Supplier leverage — commodity components: Low leverage over Roko for standard commodity components — steel, standard fasteners, generic electrics are priced at market; Roko can multi-source and switch at low cost [#A]

  • Supplier leverage — proprietary sub-systems: ❌ Concentrated supplier leverage for proprietary components — if Roko's designs specify particular motor frames, drive controllers, or specialty seals that only one or two suppliers manufacture, those suppliers hold significant pricing and supply security leverage [#J]

  • Sub: Historical pattern in supply chain disruptions (2021-22): companies with concentrated single-source proprietary components experienced the most severe margin compression and delivery delay

  • Supplier leverage — electronics/semiconductors: Moderate structural concern — industrial control systems and smart sensors depend on semiconductor content that is globally allocated by Tier-1 chip companies (Texas Instruments, Microchip, Infineon, STMicro); allocation dynamics during shortage cycles can severely disrupt production schedules [#A]

  • Dictated terms — customer payment: Industrial capital goods customers typically impose 60-90 day payment terms on OEMs; large infrastructure/EPC customers may extend to 120+ days; this creates working capital drag that is a structural feature of the stage [#A]

  • Pricing power — pass-through mechanisms: Roko's ability to pass through raw material and component cost inflation to customers depends on contract structure — project-based contracts with fixed prices are most exposed; framework agreements with escalation clauses or annual price reviews provide partial protection [#J]

  • Sub: Best-in-class industrial OEMs use annual price-increase mechanisms (1-3% above inflation index) in service contracts — a margin-protective feature most easily applied to the aftermarket stage

  • Sceptical lens: What's the bearish read on power dynamics? Through what mechanism might apparent pricing power erode? The most credible mechanism is procurement platform aggregation — as industrial customers migrate to digital procurement platforms (Ariba, Jaggaer, Ivalua), price transparency increases and negotiating leverage tilts toward buyers; at scale, this could erode the "spec-in premium" that today protects Stage 4 margins [#I]

§6 — Competitive Map at Roko's Stage

  • Competitive intensity verdict: Roko's stage exhibits a dual competitive structure — a small cohort of global scale leaders and a larger, more fragmented set of regional and application specialists; competitive intensity varies significantly by geography and end-market [#J]

  • Stage concentration: Moderately concentrated at the global level — the top 3-5 global capital goods OEMs in most sub-sectors hold 30-50% of global revenue, with the long tail accounting for the remainder; no single global monopolist [#A]

  • Sub: This structure is characteristic of "globally fragmented, locally concentrated" — in any given regional market or application niche, the effective competitive set may be 2-4 players

  • Competitor — leader tier: Global-scale leaders (Atlas Copco, Alfa Laval, Spirax-Sarco, Graco, CIRCOR equivalents in respective sub-sectors) compete on scale, distribution breadth, engineering depth, and aftermarket coverage; they hold structural cost and pricing advantages in large-account/global deals [#A]

  • Sub: Leader-tier operators typically achieve EBIT margins of 18-25% — the upper end of Stage 4's margin range — through scale-driven engineering efficiency and large aftermarket revenue pools

  • Competitor — challenger tier: Mid-size OEMs with regional strength or niche application depth — these compete by offering application-specific engineering, faster delivery, and closer customer service in markets where global leaders are under-penetrated [#A]

  • Sub: Challenger-tier EBIT margins typically 12-18%; growth strategy is often geographic expansion or adjacent application move

  • Competitor — specialist/niche tier: Smaller application-specific specialists — often family-owned or PE-backed, with deep expertise in a single industry vertical (e.g., food processing, chemical handling, pharmaceutical); compete on application knowledge and regulatory certifications [#A]

  • Sub: Niche specialists often show higher gross margins than challengers (40-55%) due to limited competition in their domain, but revenue scale is constrained

  • Scale-cost dimension: Scale advantages in Stage 4 are real but moderate — unlike manufacturing stages where capital intensity creates steep scale curves, engineering assembly businesses have more linear cost structures; below a revenue threshold (~$500m-$1bn), scale effects on margin are modest [#A]

  • Differentiation dimension: Differentiation is the primary competitive axis at Stage 4 — defined by application engineering depth, software/controls integration capability, regulatory certification portfolio, and installed-base service quality [#J]

  • Geographic dimension: Roko competes in a regionally segmented market — Nordic/European base with likely international exposure; geographic competitive intensity varies; emerging markets are more price-sensitive and may have lower-cost local competitors [#A]

  • Customer-segment dimension: Competitive intensity varies by customer segment — highest competition for large pan-European or global industrial operators (who run formal competitive tenders); lower competition for SME customers in Roko's application niches [#J]

  • Stage fragmentation: The specialist/niche tier is fragmented and geographically dispersed — there is no global roll-up that has consolidated the niche application specialist layer; PE-backed consolidation attempts exist but have not achieved dominant scale in most sub-sectors [#A]

  • ⚡ RARE: Niche application moat: In highly regulated or technically demanding sub-sectors (e.g., explosive atmospheres, pharmaceutical processing, nuclear), the effective competitive set at Roko's stage may shrink to 2-4 qualified operators globally — a structural moat that pricing data from such customers should reflect; whether Roko occupies this extreme niche is a key DD question [Cross-ref: §7 + §8] [#J]

  • Competitive dynamics — pricing: Price competition is most acute in greenfield tender situations — where customers can solicit 3-5 competitive bids; least acute at repeat/replacement, where spec-in stickiness and switching costs limit competitive exposure [#J]

  • Sceptical lens: What's the bearish read on the competitive map? Through what mechanism might apparent leadership erode? If global leaders (e.g., Atlas Copco, Siemens, ABB) choose to use their scale and engineering resources to address Roko's specific application niche — perhaps as part of a broader vertical expansion strategy — they could price aggressively to acquire installed base and use their service infrastructure to then compete on aftermarket; against this, Roko's application depth and customer relationships are the key defence [#I]

§7 — Competitive Maps at Adjacent Stages + Vertical Integration Threats

  • Adjacent threat verdict: The most credible vertical integration threats are (i) leading component suppliers integrating into system assembly and (ii) large industrial customers insourcing maintenance/service; both are real but constrained by application complexity and capital requirements [#J]

  • Supplier — leader (Stage 3 key players): ABB, Siemens, Danfoss, Parker Hannifin, Bosch Rexroth, Eaton, Nidec are the dominant component/sub-system suppliers at Stage 3; all are large-to-very-large multinationals with significant engineering capability [#A]

  • Sub: ABB (revenue ~$32bn), Siemens Industrial (~$100bn+ group), Parker Hannifin (~$19bn) — all substantially larger than Roko; they have the financial and engineering resources to integrate forward into Stage 4 if strategically motivated

  • Supplier consolidation: Stage 3 is consolidating — ABB has systematically acquired motion and automation businesses; Parker Hannifin acquired LORD Corp and Exotic Metals; Nidec has been a serial acquirer of motor businesses globally; this concentration increases supplier leverage over Stage 4 assemblers [#A]

  • Sub: Consolidation at Stage 3 is a structural headwind for Stage 4 assemblers who rely on a smaller number of more powerful component suppliers

  • Supplier vertical integration — threat assessment: Stage 3 leaders have historically shown appetite for adjacent-stage integration — ABB's automation and robotics businesses overlap with system integration; Siemens' Factory Automation division is effectively a Stage 4 player in factory automation [#A]

  • Sub: The motivation for Stage 3 → Stage 4 integration: capture margin, deepen customer relationships, lock in component specification

  • Sub: The constraint: application engineering depth, customer intimacy, and industry-specific certifications take years to build; large component suppliers have consistently struggled to replicate the specialist depth of niche Stage 4 OEMs in highly specific verticals

  • Customer — leader (Stage 5-6 key buyers): Large industrial operators (BP, BASF, ArcelorMittal, Vattenfall equivalents — depending on Roko's end markets) are the buyers of configured systems; large EPCs (Wood, Worley, TechnipFMC) are intermediary buyers for project-based sales [#A]

  • Customer consolidation: The large industrial operator tier is modestly consolidating in energy (oil majors, renewables EPCs) and somewhat in processing industries; consolidation increases customer leverage in procurement but also creates larger single-customer OE opportunities [#A]

  • Customer vertical integration — insourcing maintenance: ❌ Large industrial operators increasingly have internal engineering teams capable of performing routine maintenance and simple repairs; this directly threatens Roko's Stage 7 service contract revenue, particularly for large-account customers [#J]

  • Sub: The countervailing force: as equipment becomes more complex and digitally integrated, the expertise required for maintenance increases, pushing back toward OEM service

  • Sub: Operator insourcing risk is most acute for standard maintenance tasks; proprietary software and diagnostic tool dependence maintains OEM service stickiness for complex/critical systems

  • Customer vertical integration — equipment design: Some very large industrial operators (e.g., major oil majors, utilities) have historically maintained in-house engineering standards that specify equipment design requirements — this is not the same as manufacturing their own equipment, but it reduces the application-engineering differentiation of Stage 4 operators [#A]

  • Adjacent threat — ISO (Independent Service Operators): ❌ ISOs (independent aftermarket service providers) compete directly at Stage 7 — they acquire expertise in OEM equipment and offer service and spare parts at lower cost; this is a chronic margin erosion risk for aftermarket revenue [#J]

  • Sub: ISO penetration varies: high for simple, widely-installed equipment (pumps, compressors with published tolerances); low for complex, software-integrated systems with proprietary diagnostics

  • Sub: Industry base rate: ISOs typically capture 20-35% of the addressable aftermarket pool over a system's lifetime for standard equipment; for proprietary digital/IoT-integrated systems, ISO penetration is materially lower

  • Adjacent threat — digital platform entry: New entrants building IoT/monitoring platforms (Uptake, SparkCognition, or industrial giants' own platforms like ABB Ability, Siemens MindSphere) threaten to insert a new platform layer between Stage 4 OEMs and customers — capturing the data relationship that underpins future service value [#J]

  • Sub: If a third-party platform becomes the "system of record" for asset performance data, the OEM's aftermarket advantage is partially disintermediated

  • Supplier vertical integration — most credible vector: The most credible near-term vector is ABB or Siemens deepening their Stage 4 presence through acquisition of Stage 4 specialists — this has been their historical M&A pattern; acquisition risk to Roko cuts both ways (could be a takeover target or face acquired competitor) [#J]

  • Sceptical lens: What's the bearish read on adjacent-stage threats? The most credible vertical integration risk is not a Stage 3 supplier building from scratch but acquiring a Stage 4 specialist to gain application depth rapidly — and then using the combined component + system capability to offer a more integrated value proposition that undercuts pure-play Stage 4 operators on total-cost-of-ownership [#I]

§8 — Structural Changes + Evolution
