Key Takeaways

[C]: Something materially interesting is happening at Saipem — a genuine three-year financial rehabilitation is now backed by record backlog, third consecutive year of growth, balance sheet net cash positive (pre-IFRS 16), and a transformational merger announced. The volume of simultaneous change vectors is unusual.

[AS·Jefferies]: Saipem is "Structurally Essential, Fundamentally Improved" — the company has reached Q1 2026 EBITDA margin of 12.3%, the highest group-level profitability since Q4 2019, marking a structural escape from zero-margin legacy contracts.

[AS·MorganStanley]: Management has successfully delivered a complete corporate turnaround, moving the narrative away from crisis management and firmly toward long-term M&A and sustainable dividend distributions. CEO Puliti has established a credible two-year track record of consistently meeting or beating guidance.

[AS·Bernstein]: The proposed Saipem/Subsea 7 merger will structurally consolidate the subsea SURF (Subsea Umbilicals, Risers, and Flowlines) sector toward a near duopoly alongside TechnipFMC. Brazil's CADE declared the review "complex" in April 2026, triggering a 330-day deadline to November 2026 — this is the primary completion hurdle.

[AS·DNBCarnegie]: The combined Saipem7 entity is estimated to trade at a 7%-10% FCF yield through 2027, vastly outperforming the 4%-5% yields typical of large-cap US oilfield service peers. Capacity constraints are capping vessel supply and shifting pricing power firmly in favour of contractors through at least 2028.

[C]: Sustained transformation is real at operating level, but the EPS-EBITDA gap is the key underappreciated risk: EBITDA +29% in 2025, net profit flat at ~€310m — structural D&A (+44%) and hedging costs (+122%) are absorbing operating gains. Six consecutive quarters of positive FCF validate the cash quality, but EPS consensus of ~€0.293 on €15.8bn sales implies an EPS margin below 2%.

[AS·RothschildCoRedburn]: The corporate narrative has shifted decisively from survival to cash flow strength — Saipem now presides over a business with high earnings visibility and one of the strongest balance sheets in the sector.

§1 — Brief recap + key components summary

[C]: The scope of simultaneous change at Saipem is unusual for a single 18-month window. Eight distinct vectors are all moving in the same direction at once.

Scope of change identified (L12-18M and plausible N3Y):

  • (1) CEO/senior leadership: Alessandro Puliti appointed CEO August 2022; re-confirmed with new three-year board mandate May 2024; CFO Paolo Calcagnini in post since May 2022 (replacing Antonio Paccioretti). Board refresh: Elisabetta Serafin appointed Chair May 2024; Monica Girardi co-opted March 2026.
  • (2) M&A/corporate activity: MOU with Subsea 7 signed February 2025; merger agreement signed July 2025; share swap 6.688 Saipem per 1 Subsea 7 share; 50/50 ownership post-close; new entity "Saipem7 S.p.A."; listed Milan and Oslo; expected close H2 2026. UK CMA cleared November 2025; Brazil CADE review complex with hard deadline November 2026. Parallel: KCA Deutag 10% stake sold for $89.5m (early 2025); Deep Value Driller drillship acquisition cancelled (early 2026).
  • (3) Margin/financial profile: EBITDA margin: negative (2021) → 6% (2022) → 7.8% (2023) → 9.1% (2024) → 11.1% (2025) → 12.3% Q1 2026. Pre-IFRS 16 net cash €999m at end-2025. Six consecutive quarters of positive FCF.
  • (4) Shareholder returns: Dividend reinstated 2025 (€0.17/share; €333m total) plus €40m tactical buybacks. 2026 dividend guided ≥€330m. Progressive return policy formalised at ≥40% of FCF post-lease liabilities from 2027.
  • (5) Demand/revenue changes: Revenue €9.98bn (2022) → €11.9bn (2023) → €14.5bn (2024) → €15.5bn (2025). Backlog peak €34.1bn (end-2024); €29.6bn at Q1 2026 — still covering 89% of 2026 revenue guidance.
  • (6) Goals/targets: Plan 2025-2028: EBITDA ≈€2.0bn by 2028; cumulative FCF ≥€2.2bn; €50bn order intake target, €15bn from low-carbon. 2026 targets: €15.5bn revenue, €1.9bn EBITDA, >€600m FCF post-leases.
  • (7) Competitor/political/regulatory: Subsea 7 merger moves market toward SURF near-duopoly. Russia/Arctic LNG 2 exit and strategic plan prohibition on future Russian contracts. Kazakhstan onshore drilling exit (June 2024). EU ETS/CBAM regulatory tightening underway.
  • (8) Macro/industry: Offshore capex approaching $300bn+; fleet fully booked 2025-2026; 70%-80% of new peak global oil supply now derived from offshore (Maybank); structural super-cycle thesis gaining traction across sell-side.

[C] Sceptical bullet: The simultaneity of positive signals — record backlog, dividend restart, merger, margin expansion — risks masking underlying complexity; the EBITDA-to-EPS gap (EBITDA +29% in 2025, net profit flat at ~€310m) signals high absorptive costs that suppress EPS even as operating metrics shine.

Prior to the CFO role, Calcagnini served as Saipem's Chief Project Control and Financial Advisory Officer from February 2022. Assessment:

§3 — Strategy/structure change

[C]: Strategy has shifted materially from crisis-survival mode to growth-with-discipline; the 2025-2028 plan is internally coherent; the Subsea 7 merger, if completed, is the single largest structural change in Saipem's history and creates a step-change in competitive scale.

Post-2022 strategic reset:
- [C]: Strategy pivoted from volume-at-any-cost to "value over volume" — selective bidding on lump-sum onshore EPIC; shift toward asset-based offshore and reimbursable-type structures.
- [AS·TranscriptEarnings]: The 2025-2028 Strategic Plan is anchored by five core pillars: (1) execution excellence; (2) "One Saipem" integrated approach; (3) capital-light vessel strategy for operational and financial flexibility; (4) innovation for the energy transition; (5) return to shareholder remuneration.
- [C]: Financial commitments 2025-2028: EBITDA ≈€2.0bn by 2028; cumulative operating cash flow (post-lease repayment) ≥€3.7bn; cumulative capex ≈€1.5bn; cumulative FCF ≥€2.2bn; investment-grade credit rating as explicit medium-term target.

Asset rationalisation:
- [AS·Jefferies]: KCA Deutag 10% stake sold to H&P for $89.5m (early 2025), completing the divestiture of Saipem's onshore drilling business.
- [AS·PressRelease]: Deep Value Driller ($272.5m) acquisition cancelled in early 2026 — demonstrating capital discipline and commitment to the capital-light leasing model.
- [AS·PressRelease]: Strategic alliances formed: Mitsubishi Heavy Industries for post-combustion CO2 capture technologies; expanded Eni collaboration for domestic biorefining infrastructure.

The Subsea 7 merger — key terms and strategic rationale:
- MOU signed February 2025; binding merger agreement signed July 24, 2025; shareholder votes September 25, 2025; Saipem shareholders and Subsea 7 shareholders both approved.
- Share exchange: 6.688 new Saipem shares per Subsea 7 share; 50/50 pro-forma ownership post-completion.
- [AS·Bernstein]: Subsea 7 shareholders receive extraordinary pre-merger dividends totalling €555m — comprising €450m baseline payout plus €105m from a permitted business divestment.
- [C]: Combined backlog €43bn; combined revenue ≈€20bn; 60+ construction vessels; 45,000+ employees; operations in 60+ countries.
- [AS·InvestorPresentation]: Annual run-rate cost and capex synergies ≈€300m by year 3 post-close; one-off implementation costs ≈€200m-€270m; synergy drivers: fleet optimisation, procurement leverage, process efficiencies.
- [AS·Bernstein]: The combination creates a near duopoly in the SURF market alongside TechnipFMC, with four business units post-close: Offshore E&C, Onshore E&C, Sustainable Infrastructures, Offshore Drilling.

Antitrust timeline:
- UK CMA clearance secured November 2025.
- [AS·Jefferies]: Brazil CADE declared the merger "complex" in April 2026, triggering a strict 330-day statutory deadline with a final decision by November 2026. CADE scrutiny is focused on deepwater rigid pipelay market — the combined entity would control roughly seven or eight of the ten to twelve globally available specialised vessels capable of executing deepwater rigid infrastructure, heavily dominating Petrobras's deepwater requirements.
- [AS·DNBCarnegie]: Potential remedies could involve forced sale or divestment of specific vessels to maintain adequate Brazilian market competition.
- [AS·Bernstein]: Management considers potential remedies manageable, assuming divestitures remain below the €500m aggregate threshold that allows either party to withdraw from the agreement.

Management tone and framing:
- [AS·TranscriptEarnings]: Management framing avoids aggressive hyperbole, presenting the Subsea 7 deal as a logical, complementary evolution designed to provide comprehensive technological solutions for clients. Internal language is "obsession" with cash flow generation and systematic de-risking of legacy contracts.
- [AS·RothschildCoRedburn]: Management has successfully reoriented the business toward its traditional strengths in complex offshore E&C, driving a steady and tangible recovery in overall investor credibility.

[C] Sceptical bullet: The Subsea 7 merger introduces significant integration risk, management bandwidth dilution, and regulatory complexity (antitrust in multiple jurisdictions); market concentration scrutiny could delay or impose conditions; a 50/50 ownership structure historically creates governance friction and strategic deadlock risk.

§7 — Value chain — Competition

[C]: Competitive landscape is constructive for Saipem; supply remains tight; the Subsea 7 merger will reduce the number of Tier 1 offshore contractors from effectively 3 to 2, raising market concentration; pricing discipline appears broadly holding.

The SURF near-duopoly thesis:
- [AS·Bernstein]: The Saipem/Subsea 7 combination structurally consolidates the SURF sector toward a near duopoly alongside TechnipFMC.
- [AS·Jefferies]: The combined entity would control roughly 7-8 of the 10-12 specialised deepwater rigid pipelay vessels globally available. Subsea expert Ian McIntosh (Jefferies) observes that combining Saipem's vessels with Subsea 7's spool bases will unlock massive Reel-lay capacity, allowing Saipem7 to aggressively challenge TechnipFMC in Norway and the US Gulf of Mexico.
- [C]: Combined Saipem7 backlog €43bn, revenue €20bn — would be largest offshore services company globally by backlog.

Middle East market share and competitive positioning:
- [AS·Bernstein]: Saipem holds 10% Middle Eastern offshore E&C market share, on par with aggressive Indian entrants like Larsen & Toubro, ahead of Bechtel, Technip Energies, and Maire. European E&C firms have successfully fought back against Asian and Indian competitors by leveraging superior execution track records and NOC relationships.
- [AS·Jefferies]: The threat of new competitors in ultra-deepwater space is practically non-existent. The subsea installation market is experiencing "total supply control among a limited number of consolidated players" — zero new-build vessel capacity entering the key enabler segment.

Pricing power and industry discipline:
- [AS·DNBCarnegie]: Limited global capacity is capping supply within SURF, meaning the top three contractors are fully booked into 2027 and 2028. Pricing power has shifted firmly in favour of contractors, supporting sustainable, healthy margins and proving bidding discipline is maintained rather than price competition.
- [AS·Jefferies]: Oil majors have recognised the tight capacity and altered procurement behaviours — instead of open tenders seeking cheapest price, clients are engaging contractors early to co-engineer projects and negotiate margins collaboratively.

Evolution of EPIC contracting models:
- [AS·Jefferies]: Fundamental shift toward integrated EPIC (iEPCI) contracts — bundling subsea production system and installation into a single contract, delivering projects approximately one year faster than traditional fragmented methods. This model, pioneered by TechnipFMC, is a primary growth engine for Subsea 7 through its Subsea Integration Alliance, and is partly the driver forcing Saipem to merge to remain competitive in integrated offerings.
- [AS·Bernstein]: Across the broader E&C sector, contractors are uniformly demanding price pass-through mechanisms, indexation clauses, and collaborative delivery models to shield backlog profitability from raw material volatility and labour inflation.

[C] Sceptical bullet: If macro deteriorates (oil price drop) and NOC capex is cut, contractors historically compete aggressively on price to fill vessels, eroding current pricing discipline; Saipem's lump-sum discipline is untested in a down-cycle since the current management took over.

§8 — Value chain — Disruption (energy transition)

[C]: Energy transition is a modest but growing revenue opportunity for Saipem (offshore wind installation, CCUS, floating wind); digitisation is accelerating faster than expected; the dominant disruption risk remains the traditional cycle turn, not technology substitution.

Renewables and new energy exposure:
- [AS·ESG]: Approximately 14% of Saipem's current commercial backlog consists of low or zero-carbon projects. 12% of Saipem's total turnover is EU Taxonomy-eligible. €754m in explicitly taxonomy-aligned turnover generated during the year.
- [C]: Strategic plan 2025-2028 targets €15bn from "low-carbon projects" out of €50bn total intake (30% of 4-year target). CCUS, geothermal, bio-fuels/SAF, nuclear cited as growth verticals.
- [AS·ARS]: Saipem's proprietary floating wind technology (Star1) and CCUS capabilities (Bluenzyme) position it as a participant in hard-to-abate decarbonisation solutions.

Subsea 7 merger — amplifying transition capabilities:
- [AS·ARS]: Subsea 7's dedicated Renewables business unit generated 17% of total group revenue in 2025, operating as a market leader in fixed offshore wind installation. 15% of Subsea 7's total revenue was taxonomy-aligned in 2025.
- [AS·Bernstein]: The combined Saipem7 entity will pair Saipem's Middle Eastern presence with Subsea 7's offshore wind dominance in the North Sea. The combination positions Saipem7 as a dominant force in hard-to-abate decarbonisation.
- [AS·MorganStanley]: The proposed merger is timed to capture renewed European energy security mandate, allowing the combined entity to leverage Subsea 7's renewables scale alongside Saipem's traditional infrastructure capabilities.
