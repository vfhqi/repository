[W] HTWS — Value-Chain Map: Helios Towers plc & Sub-Saharan African Passive Telecoms Infrastructure [C] @ 29-Apr-26

KEY FINDINGS

  • Helios occupies the structurally strongest link in the chain. The towerco stage sits between fragmented, commodity-priced upstream construction inputs and capital-constrained MNO customers who cannot afford to own towers. This pinch-point position, secured by 10-15 year take-or-pay contracts, generates EBITDA margins of ~55% (FY 2025) — higher than both the MNOs it serves (~45-50%) and the construction contractors upstream.

  • The tenancy ratio is the single most important operational lever. With 14,325 sites and 31,944 tenancies (FY 2025 tenancy ratio: 2.17x, up from 1.91x in FY 2023), each incremental colocation adds revenue at near-zero marginal cost. The path to 2.5x by 2030 (Impact 2030 strategy) is the clearest earnings growth driver in the value chain. At 2.5x, EBITDA margins could approach 58-60%.

  • MTN's acquisition of IHS Towers ($2.2bn deal, 2026) is the most significant competitive development in the sector. IHS was Helios's primary independent competitor in sub-Saharan Africa (~37,700 towers). Re-absorbing IHS under MTN vertically re-integrates infrastructure and service provision at scale, creating a competitor who is simultaneously a potential customer, a structural peculiarity that raises both colocation risk and negotiating complexity for Helios in markets where MTN is a tenant.

  • FX risk is partially but not fully mitigated. 71% of FY 2025 EBITDA is hard-currency-exposed (52% from dollarised/pegged markets, 19% contractually linked), but 29% remains exposed to local currencies including the Tanzanian Shilling, Congolese Franc, and Malawian Kwacha — currencies with histories of devaluation. Helios has USD/GBP-denominated debt: a mismatch that requires leverage discipline to navigate.

  • The solar programme is a genuine margin enhancer, not just ESG optics. Energy accounts for ~30% of site OPEX on diesel-heavy towers. Solar-battery retrofits deliver estimated OPEX savings of ~$17,000/site/year against a ~$42,000 capex outlay, implying a sub-3-year payback and >35% IRR. At 430 solar systems installed in DRC alone, this programme structurally improves unit economics over time.

  • Helios's geographic diversification is a competitive differentiator vs IHS (pre-acquisition). IHS's Nigeria concentration (~59% of total revenue from a single market) left it acutely exposed to Naira devaluation and MTN-Nigeria dependency. Helios operates across nine markets (Tanzania, DRC, Congo Brazzaville, Ghana, Senegal, South Africa, Madagascar, Malawi, Oman), spreading single-country risk that appears more attractive from an infrastructure investor's perspective.

  • Starlink/LEO satellite risk is real but medium-term, not a near-term threat to tower demand. Starlink entered African markets in 2023 and held ~0.5% market share in Kenya by mid-2024. Rural deployments are constrained by ~$300 hardware costs and ~$50/month subscriptions, unaffordable for most sub-Saharan consumers. Critically, Airtel Africa and Vodacom have begun partnering with Starlink (not competing), using it as backhaul complement to terrestrial towers — a trend that actually keeps MNO anchor tenancies intact.

  • The contracted revenue book provides exceptional downside protection. At end-FY 2025, Helios held $5.3bn of contracted future revenue, average remaining contract term of 6.6 years — all without assuming new business. This structural certainty at Stage 3 is unique among the value chain stages; MNOs' revenue is market-volume dependent, construction contractors have no recurring income, and end consumers churn freely.

  • Net leverage falling to 3.4x (FY 2025 vs 4.0x previously) signals the balance sheet transition. The company is now cash flow positive ($208m recurring FCF in FY 2025, +40% YoY) and has initiated dividends ($25m inaugural) plus share buybacks ($50m). The value-chain position always justified premium multiples; the balance sheet is now catching up with the quality of the underlying business.

  • MNO capex discipline is the primary demand risk. If Airtel Africa, MTN or Orange reduce their African network expansion programmes — driven by FX headwinds, regulatory pressure, or financial stress — new colocation demand slows. Helios is protected on existing tenancies (take-or-pay), but incremental growth depends on MNO willingness to add new sites, deploy 4G/5G, and densify existing coverage. This is the swing variable for the 2.17x→2.5x tenancy ratio target.

Executive Summary (~500 words)

The sub-Saharan African telecoms passive infrastructure value chain runs from raw material inputs (steel, cement, diesel, solar panels) through tower construction, into passive infrastructure ownership (the towerco stage), then power systems management, and finally through active network equipment, core network, mobile services, and ultimately the end consumer subscriber. Each stage has distinct economics, capital intensity, and competitive dynamics, but value concentration is overwhelmingly at the towerco stage — not upstream with construction contractors, and not downstream with the MNOs.

Helios Towers plc occupies Stage 3 (passive infrastructure ownership) and Stage 3.5 (power systems management) of this chain. These are the two stages with the highest recurring margin, longest asset lives, and strongest contractual protection. The business model is built around acquiring or building towers, then leasing space on them to multiple MNOs under 10-15 year take-or-pay contracts, with CPI and power escalators embedded. Revenue is therefore double-protected: contractually guaranteed at a minimum level regardless of actual tenant utilisation, and inflation-linked via annual escalators. FY 2025 revenue was $854m (+8% YoY); Adjusted EBITDA was $471m (55% margin, +2pp YoY); recurring FCF was $208m (+40% YoY).

The structural economics at Stage 3 are compelling. A single anchor tenant covers the site's cost base. Every additional co-tenant (colocation) adds revenue at near-zero marginal operating cost, since the tower, ground lease, and power infrastructure are already deployed. This incremental margin dynamic — sometimes described as the "factory model" of passive infrastructure — drives the tenancy ratio as the sector's primary valuation and performance metric. Helios's tenancy ratio of 2.17x (FY 2025) compares to IHS Towers at 1.51x, reflecting the structural advantage of Helios's more diversified market mix, where lower MNO-per-market concentration creates co-location opportunity.

Power dynamics within the chain are nuanced. Helios is not a price-setter against the MNOs: large customers like MTN and Airtel Africa exert commercial pressure at contract renewal. However, take-or-pay structures provide a pricing floor, and CPI escalators deliver pass-through. At the supplier level, construction contractors are price-takers relative to Helios — the company maintains in-house build capability, reducing dependence on external contractors and enabling cost discipline. Power and fuel costs (historically ~30% of site OPEX) remain a commodity-price exposure, partially mitigated by the solar programme and the fact that power escalators in almost all contracts pass through cost increases to MNO customers.

The most significant structural development in the competitive landscape is MTN's $2.2bn acquisition of IHS Towers (announced 2026). This effectively re-verticalises passive infrastructure under one of the sector's largest MNO customers, creating a new type of competitor for Helios — one with deep pockets, existing MNO relationships, and a captive tower estate from which to offer colocation to third parties. Whether MTN operates IHS commercially (as an open-access tower company) or proprietary will determine whether this reshapes Helios's competitive position materially.

Helios's value-chain position is structurally advantaged. The investment thesis rests on three pillars: (1) tenancy ratio expansion as colocations grow, (2) solar-programme-driven OPEX reduction improving unit economics, and (3) balance sheet deleveraging as FCF generation compounds. The risks are FX exposure on the 29% of non-hard-currency EBITDA, MNO customer concentration, and the long-term but real possibility that LEO satellites erode rural tower demand. None of these risks negate the fundamental quality of the value-chain position, but they place a ceiling on the multiple at which the position should be valued.

Stage 1: Raw materials and components
- Key inputs: structural steel (towers), cement and aggregate (foundations), diesel fuel (power backup), solar panels and battery storage, generator sets, telecom mounting hardware.
- Operators: global commodity and equipment suppliers — no single dominant provider in African markets; local sourcing where possible, imports for specialist equipment (solar panels often sourced from China).
- Capital intensity: low per-unit but significant in aggregate; steel represents ~40% of new tower construction cost.
- Typical margins: commodity level; steel producers ~10-15% EBITDA, solar panel manufacturers ~8-12%.
- Geographic footprint: global supply chains; local content requirements in some markets (Nigeria, South Africa) push some localisation.
- Helios's relationship: price-taker at this stage; no ownership or direct leverage over commodity suppliers; partially mitigated by scale purchasing.

Stage 2: Tower construction and civil engineering
- Key activities: site acquisition/ground lease negotiation, civil foundation work, tower erection, power system installation, equipment shelter construction.
- Operators: mix of specialist telecoms contractors (Huawei, Ericsson subcontractors, Nokia), local civil engineering firms, and in-house build teams where towercos have that capability.
- Capital intensity: moderate; tower build cost in Africa typically $30,000-$80,000 per site depending on market, soil conditions, height, and power configuration.
- Typical margins: thin — civil contractors operate at ~8-15% EBITDA; competitive procurement keeps margins compressed.
- Helios's position: maintains in-house build capability, reducing exposure to external contractor pricing and scheduling; this is a partial competitive advantage vs peers who outsource entirely.
- Geographic footprint: largely local contractors; Helios deploys own teams in core markets (DRC, Tanzania, Ghana).

Stage 3: Passive infrastructure ownership — THE TOWERCO STAGE (Helios primary position)
- Key activities: owning and operating tower structures, ground lease management, site access management, multi-tenant colocation, SLA management for MNO customers.
- Operators: Helios Towers (~14,325 sites, 9 markets), American Tower Africa (~10,100 sites), MTN-IHS (post-acquisition: ~37,700 towers across Africa), smaller regional players (Pan African Towers, local independents).
- Capital intensity: high upfront (site acquisition or tower build), then low maintenance recurring; asset lives of 20-30 years; sustaining capex estimated at ~$5,000-$10,000/site/year.
- Typical margins: gross margin ~65-70% at site level (excluding corporate overheads); EBITDA margins 50-55% for Helios (FY 2024: 53.2%; FY 2025: 55%).
- Revenue model: anchor tenancy (MNO 1) covers fixed costs; each additional tenancy is incremental margin at ~80-90% gross contribution.
- Pricing: take-or-pay, inflation-linked, 10-15 year terms.

Stage 3.5: Power systems and energy management (Helios secondary position)
- Key activities: diesel procurement and management, hybrid solar-battery system installation and operation, power monitoring, energy cost pass-through to MNO customers.
- Why it matters: power is the largest variable OPEX at tower sites in sub-Saharan Africa — grid reliability is poor, necessitating diesel generators as primary or backup power in many markets; energy represents ~30% of total site OPEX.
- Helios's role: manages power infrastructure as part of its tower service; has installed 430+ solar systems in DRC alone; incorporates power price escalators in virtually all customer contracts (half quarterly, half annual).
- Financial impact: solar-battery systems cost ~$42,000 per site; estimated annual OPEX saving ~$17,000/site; sub-3-year payback; >35% IRR; structurally improves unit economics as roll-out scales.
- Margin capture: to the extent Helios can reduce its diesel bill while maintaining power escalator revenue from MNOs, it captures margin that would otherwise flow to fuel suppliers.

Stage 4: Active equipment — MNO-owned, not towerco
- Key activities: installation and operation of antennas, base transceiver stations , remote radio heads , microwave backhaul links.
- Operators: MNOs (Airtel Africa, MTN, Orange, Vodacom) own and deploy this equipment; vendors (Ericsson, Nokia, Huawei) supply it.
- Capital intensity: high; 4G/5G active equipment refresh cycles every 7-10 years.
- Typical margins: active equipment vendors operate at ~20-35% gross margins; MNOs capitalise this as network assets.
- Relevance to Helios: MNO active equipment upgrades (e.g., 4G rollout, 5G densification) drive demand for additional tower tenancies — a positive feedback loop for Helios's colocation pipeline.

Stage 5: Core network — MNO-owned
- Key activities: IP/MPLS transmission networks, radio network controllers, EPC (Evolved Packet Core) for 4G/5G, data centres, international gateways.
- Operators: exclusively MNOs and their vendor partners.
- Capital intensity: extremely high; not relevant to towerco investment thesis directly.
- Typical margins: not separately reported; absorbed within MNO capex/EBITDA.

Stage 6: Mobile service provision (MNOs)
- Key activities: subscriber acquisition, mobile voice, data, mobile money services.
- Operators: Airtel Africa, MTN Group, Orange Africa, Vodacom, local/regional operators.
- Financial scale: Airtel Africa EBITDA margin ~49% (H1 FY2026, reported currency); MTN Group Africa operations broadly similar.
- Relevance to Helios: MNO financial health determines ability and willingness to pay tower rents and invest in network expansion. Airtel Africa's balance sheet has improved materially with FX recovery, reducing previously flagged counterparty risk.

Stage 7: End consumer (mobile subscribers)
- ~800m+ mobile subscribers across sub-Saharan Africa; ARPU typically $3-15/month depending on market; data consumption growing at high single digit to double digit rates annually.
- Low penetration of 4G/5G (well below 50% of subscribers) drives long-term tower demand via network densification.
- Data demand trajectory: African tower infrastructure faces a fourfold data surge by 2030 — the primary structural demand driver for incremental tenancy growth.

2. Helios's Position Within the Chain

Primary occupation: Stage 3 (passive infrastructure ownership)
- Helios owns 14,325 tower sites across nine markets: Tanzania, DRC, Congo Brazzaville, Ghana, Senegal, South Africa, Madagascar, Malawi, Oman.
- Tenancy base: 31,944 tenancies at end-FY 2025, tenancy ratio 2.17x.
- Revenue: $854m (FY 2025); contracted future revenue of $5.3bn with average 6.6-year remaining term.

Secondary occupation: Stage 3.5 (power systems management)
- Helios manages power infrastructure as part of its service offering, not merely as a passive landlord.
- Power escalators embedded in virtually all contracts (half quarterly, half annual adjustments) protect against diesel price volatility.
- 430+ solar systems deployed in DRC as of 2024; expanding programme across portfolio.
- This downstream extension within Stage 3/3.5 creates a revenue-and-cost managed package that MNOs value precisely because it removes operational complexity from their own workflows.

Upstream integration: partial construction capability
- Helios maintains in-house build teams in core markets rather than outsourcing entirely to external contractors.
- This reduces construction cost inflation risk, improves build quality control, and enables faster site deployment when MNOs commit to expansion.
- Not full backward integration to raw materials — Helios does not manufacture steel structures or solar panels.

Comparison to competitors: more integrated than American Tower, comparable to IHS
- American Tower Africa (~10,100 sites): largely an asset manager — buys or builds towers, leases space, outsources construction and most O&M; less operationally intensive.
- IHS Towers (~37,700 sites, pre-MTN acquisition): comparable integration — also manages power systems actively; Nigeria-heavy concentration creates single-market dependency.
- Helios: geographic diversification across nine markets is the key differentiating feature vs peers, reducing single-country concentration risk while maintaining operational depth.

3. Adjacent Value Chain Stages

Direct customers: mobile network operators
- Airtel Africa: largest customer in DRC, Tanzania, Malawi, and other markets; post-FX-recovery, financial position improved materially (EBITDA margin ~49%); key counterparty risk that has receded.
- MTN Group: significant tenant across multiple Helios markets (Ghana, South Africa, Congo Brazzaville); also the buyer of IHS (competitive complexity emerging).
- Orange: present in Senegal, Congo Brazzaville, Madagascar; investment-grade parent (France Telecom SA).
- Vodacom: South Africa and other markets.
- IHS Towers had 57% of revenue from MTN+Airtel combined; Helios's customer mix is more geographically distributed, implying somewhat lower per-customer concentration, though precise splits are not publicly disclosed.

No intermediaries between Helios and MNOs
- The towerco-to-MNO relationship is direct; no distributors, agents, or intermediaries sit between them.
- This is a structural advantage: revenue recognition is clean, contract enforcement is bilateral, and relationship management is concentrated.

MNO consolidation trend
- Consolidation among MNOs in several African markets: Vodafone/Vodacom exits, Orange acquiring Maroc Telecom assets, MTN rationalising portfolio.
- In the short term, consolidation reduces the number of distinct MNO counterparties but does not reduce tower demand per se; post-merger entities still need all towers from both legacy networks during integration, and often rationalise only after years.
- Net effect on Helios: modest risk if merged entities renegotiate anchor tenancy rates; modest upside if network overlap drives decommissioning at rival towercos while keeping Helios sites live.

5. Power Dynamics and Pricing Leverage

Helios vs MNOs: structural pricing power confirmed, with nuances
- Take-or-pay contracts mean Helios receives minimum contracted payments regardless of actual MNO network usage — the most powerful contractual protection available in B2B service agreements.
- Inflation escalators (CPI-linked annually) ensure real revenue is maintained even in high-inflation markets; power cost escalators (quarterly or annual) ensure diesel/electricity cost increases are passed through.
- Contract lengths of 10-15 years mean pricing is re-set infrequently; at renewal, MNOs have significant leverage if they represent a large share of a given tower's tenancy — particularly for anchor tenants.
- The stickiness of tower infrastructure for MNOs is high: migrating from one towerco to another requires physical de-rigging and re-installation of active equipment, costing MNOs time and capital they rarely want to spend on non-competitive infrastructure.

MNO leverage — concentration risk
- IHS Towers: 57% of revenue from MTN + Airtel in 2024; MTN alone ~63% of IHS revenue — this extreme concentration was the key vulnerability that MTN has now resolved by acquiring IHS outright.
- Helios: customer mix more diversified across nine markets, but precise customer concentration data not publicly disclosed. Given the limited number of MNOs per market (typically 2-4), individual MNO dependency at country level is high.
- Airtel Africa financial health: historically the primary credit concern (high debt, FX stress); by FY 2025/2026 the picture has improved materially — EBITDA margins back above 49%, revenue up 25.8% in H1 FY2026.
- MTN's acquisition of IHS creates a new asymmetry: if MTN decides to route new tenancies through its own IHS-owned towers rather than independent towercos like Helios, it could dampen co-location growth in markets where both compete.

Supplier side: Helios is price-setter relative to construction contractors
- Local civil contractors are fragmented and in competition for Helios's build programme.
- Helios's scale (14,325 sites across nine markets, plus ongoing build programme of ~400+ sites/year) gives it significant purchasing leverage.
- In-house build capability provides a credible walk-away option from external contractors, further strengthening Helios's negotiating position.

Power/diesel suppliers: Helios is price-taker but partially hedged
- Diesel is procured locally in each market — no global purchasing advantage; prices fluctuate with oil markets and local refining/logistics costs.
- Power escalators in contracts mean Helios passes cost increases through to MNOs — the residual risk is the timing lag between cost increase and contract escalation (half of contracts adjust quarterly, half annually).
- Solar programme is the structural hedge: reducing diesel dependency at each retrofitted site removes that site from diesel market exposure entirely.

7. Competitive Maps at Adjacent Stages

Supplier stage (construction contractors) — fragmented, Helios-advantaged
- No single dominant construction contractor for African tower civil works; market is fragmented with hundreds of local firms across nine operating markets.
- Helios's in-house build capability is a structural competitive advantage: it can build towers faster, at lower cost, and to its own specification without dependence on external contractors.
- American Tower Africa outsources construction more heavily, exposing it to contractor pricing and timeline risk.
- IHS Towers also maintains some in-house capability but has been more reliant on external partners in Nigeria.

Customer stage (MNOs) — consolidating, which has mixed implications
- Active MNO consolidation across sub-Saharan Africa: Orange-Maroc Telecom acquisitions, Vodafone exits from several markets, MTN asset rationalisation.
- In the short term, consolidation reduces the addressable customer count per market (fewer distinct MNOs to add as co-tenants).
- In the longer term, post-merger network rationalisation often sees the acquiring operator keep towers in the best-coverage locations (likely Helios sites if they represent anchor coverage) while decommissioning overlap sites.
- Net effect: ambiguous but manageable; Helios's diverse market footprint reduces exposure to any single market's MNO consolidation dynamic.

Risk: MNO vertical integration — bringing towers in-house
- MTN's acquisition of IHS is the clearest recent example of MNO vertical re-integration of tower assets.
- However, MTN's motivation appears to be financial (IHS was distressed and deeply dependent on MTN revenue anyway) rather than a strategic pivot away from the towerco model per se.
- Other MNOs (Airtel Africa, Orange) continue to divest towers to towercos: the general direction of travel remains pro-towerco despite the MTN exception.
- Helios has carved out a position in markets where MNO balance sheets are under sufficient stress that outright tower re-acquisition would require capital MNOs do not have.

Risk: technology disruption from LEO satellites
- Starlink operational in 16+ African countries as of 2025; but market share remains sub-1% in most markets due to affordability constraints ($300 hardware, $50/month subscription).
- Airtel Africa and Vodacom have signed Starlink partnership deals, using satellite as backhaul complement in rural areas — not as a tower replacement.
- Near-term (0-3 years): zero material impact on tower demand. Medium-term (3-7 years): risk of LEO reducing incremental tower builds in ultra-rural areas. Long-term (7-10 years): a real but manageable risk, particularly if handset costs fall and satellite coverage becomes competitive with mobile broadband at consumer price points.
- Assessment: Starlink/LEO is a long-run risk factor to monitor, not a near-term investment concern. The partnership dynamic (MNOs using Starlink as backhaul, not competition) is the most likely outcome for the next 5+ years.

8. Structural Changes and Evolution

Tower carve-outs: MNO divestments continue, creating Helios growth opportunities
- The primary source of Helios's historic tower estate is MNO sell-leaseback transactions: MNOs divest towers to towercos, lease them back, and use the cash proceeds to fund spectrum, network, or balance sheet needs.
- This structural trend remains intact: African MNOs remain capital-constrained relative to their network expansion ambitions; tower divestment is a logical capital recycling tool.
- Helios has participated in large divestment transactions in DRC (Airtel towers acquired), Tanzania, Congo Brazzaville, and others.
- Remaining pipeline: several African markets still have significant MNO-held tower estates that could be divested; Helios's operational footprint and MNO relationships position it as a preferred buyer.

Sector consolidation: IHS/ATC, now MTN/IHS — reducing number of independent players
- ATC acquired Eaton Towers in 2019 (expanding to ~7,000 African towers at the time).
- MTN's acquisition of IHS in 2026 removes the largest independent towerco from the market.
- The result is a more concentrated competitive landscape: Helios + ATC as the two remaining large independent towercos in sub-Saharan Africa, alongside the newly verticalised MTN-IHS.
- This consolidation paradoxically strengthens Helios's position with MNOs who want to preserve access to multiple independent towerco options rather than becoming captive to a competitor-owned infrastructure provider.

New entrants: low probability given capital intensity
- Building a meaningful tower estate requires hundreds of millions of dollars and years of site-by-site development.
- Ground lease negotiation, regulatory approvals, and MNO relationships are all barriers that favour incumbents.
- Private equity-backed new entrants (as seen in East Africa with Pan African Towers) remain niche; scaling to compete with Helios's 14,325-site portfolio would require a decade.
- Assessment: new entrant risk is low; the more relevant competitive risk is the MTN-IHS vertical integration dynamic.

Data demand trajectory: structural tailwind
- African tower infrastructure faces a fourfold data surge by 2030 (per Broadband Breakfast/GSMA data).
- 4G penetration in sub-Saharan Africa remains well below 50% of subscribers, with 5G deployment in early stages.
- This implies significant organic demand for additional tower tenancies (4G/5G equipment deployments on existing towers) without requiring new site builds — directly supporting Helios's 2.17x→2.5x tenancy ratio target.

9. Helios's Competitive Advantages

Geographic diversification: the clearest differentiation from peers
- Nine markets across sub-Saharan Africa and Oman provide portfolio diversification that no competitor matches at comparable scale.
- IHS's Nigeria concentration (59% of revenue) meant that Naira devaluation in 2023-2024 disproportionately damaged its reported financials — a risk Helios structurally avoided.
- Smaller markets (DRC, Congo Brazzaville, Madagascar) have lower competition (fewer independent towercos bidding for MNO relationships) but higher political risk — a trade-off that has served Helios well in terms of market positioning and pricing.

Long-term take-or-pay contracts: switching costs are near-prohibitive
- 10-15 year contract terms, with $5.3bn of contracted future revenue at 6.6 years average remaining term (FY 2025), mean that Helios's revenue base for the next 6+ years is visible and largely immune to near-term MNO commercial decisions.
- Physical switching costs for MNOs (de-rigging, re-installation, service downtime) are estimated at $50,000-$100,000+ per site, creating a structural lock-in that goes beyond the contractual term.

Solar programme: structural margin advantage compounding over time
- $42,000 upfront cost, $17,000/year OPEX saving, >35% IRR, sub-3-year payback — a genuinely attractive internal return project that improves unit economics site by site.
- 430+ solar systems deployed in DRC alone (2024 data); programme expanding across portfolio.
- Power escalators mean Helios captures cost savings without sacrificing revenue (it charges MNOs power-indexed rates while reducing its actual power cost through solar).
- Competitors without equivalent solar programmes carry structurally higher OPEX, creating a cost advantage for Helios that widens as diesel prices remain volatile and solar costs continue to fall.

First-mover position in challenging markets
- DRC and Congo Brazzaville in particular: entry barriers are high (political risk, logistics, local relationship requirements), meaning once a towerco establishes presence, competitors are reluctant to invest at scale.
- Helios has operated in DRC since early 2012 (Airtel tower acquisition); deep operational knowledge, established ground leases, and MNO relationships create a durable moat in these markets.

Can competitors replicate?
- Yes, in theory: capital and operational expertise are available to large well-capitalised entrants.
- In practice: the combination of existing ground leases (25-year terms in some cases), established MNO contracts, solar-enabled OPEX structure, and local operational presence would take 7-10 years and $1-2bn+ to replicate at Helios's scale.
- ATC is the most capable potential replicator but has shown less appetite for Helios's frontier market footprint.

10. Vulnerabilities in Helios's Position

MNO customer concentration: the primary operational risk
- With four to five major MNO tenants across nine markets, any single large customer represents a meaningful revenue concentration at country level.
- DRC, for example, is heavily dependent on Airtel (acquired towers from Airtel as the original MNO), meaning Airtel's financial health in DRC directly affects Helios's revenue security in that market.
- While take-or-pay provides contractual protection, enforcing payment from a financially stressed MNO in a frontier market is complex; renegotiation of terms is more likely than litigation.

FX risk: 29% of EBITDA in non-hard currencies
- 71% hard-currency EBITDA provides substantial protection, but the residual 29% is exposed to Tanzanian Shilling, Congolese Franc, Malawian Kwacha, and other local currencies.
- USD/GBP-denominated debt ($2.8bn gross, per Fitch rating analysis) creates a structural mismatch when local currency EBITDA is translated into dollars for debt service.
- Net debt/EBITDA at 3.4x (FY 2025) is manageable within the 2.5-3.5x target range, but any significant local currency devaluation in a large market (DRC or Tanzania) could push leverage above comfort zone.

Country risk: high-risk operating environments
- DRC (armed conflict, weak rule of law, infrastructure challenges) and Congo Brazzaville (political concentration, governance risk) represent elevated country risk.
- Ground lease agreements in these markets may be subject to expropriation risk, regulatory re-rating, or local-content requirements that increase cost.
- Helios has operated in these environments for 10-15 years without major state-level adverse action, suggesting the risk is manageable but not eliminable.

Leverage: high net debt amplifies any operational shortfall
- Net debt/EBITDA at 3.4x is within target range but not low; a 1x deterioration in the leverage ratio from a combination of FX devaluation and EBITDA miss would put the balance sheet in stress territory.
- Helios's debt is at fixed rates (high-yield bonds, BB- rated by Fitch), meaning refinancing risk is limited near-term but coupon costs are elevated vs investment-grade peers.
- The path to investment grade — which would structurally reduce funding costs — requires sustained FCF generation and leverage reduction to ~2.5x or below; achievable under Impact 2030 but not imminent.

MTN's acquisition of IHS: competitive landscape complexity
- MTN becomes a dual-role participant: both a major tenant of Helios's towers (in markets where both operate) and a competitor-owned infrastructure provider (through IHS-branded towers in other markets).
- If MTN operates IHS as an open-access independent towerco, competitive intensity increases for co-location mandates.
- If MTN uses IHS primarily as a captive internal infrastructure provider, the competitive risk to Helios is lower but MNOs in those markets have fewer independent alternatives, potentially strengthening Helios's pricing in markets where it is the primary remaining independent.

MNO capex discipline: demand risk for incremental tenancies
- Existing take-or-pay tenancies are protected; the growth story (2.17x → 2.5x by 2030) depends on MNOs adding new network equipment, deploying 4G/5G, and commissioning new sites.
- If MNO capex budgets tighten (FX pressure, regulatory burden, competitive margin compression), the pace of new colocation additions slows.
- 2,538 new tenancies in FY 2025 (9% growth) is the run-rate the business needs to maintain to reach 2.5x; this requires MNO network investment to continue at current pace.

11. Strategic Implications for Investment

Value-chain position is structurally advantaged — the core thesis holds
- The towerco stage of the sub-Saharan African telecoms value chain is the most contractually protected, highest-margin, and most capital-efficient stage in the entire chain.
- Helios's 55% EBITDA margin, $5.3bn contracted revenue backlog, and take-or-pay structure represent a quality of earnings that most listed businesses in emerging markets cannot match.
- The capital recycling logic — pay high yield on debt, invest in towers, collect contracted cash flows at 55% EBITDA margin, use FCF to deleverage — is straightforward once the balance sheet inflection point is passed; Helios crossed that point in FY 2024 (first positive FCF of $19m) and accelerated in FY 2025 ($208m recurring FCF).

The tenancy ratio expansion is the core investment driver
- 2.17x (FY 2025) → 2.5x (Impact 2030 target) represents ~15% growth in revenue contribution per tower, almost entirely at incremental margin rates of 80-90%.
- If achieved, this drives Adjusted EBITDA from $471m (FY 2025) toward $600m+ by 2030 on modestly growing revenues, with leverage falling structurally as FCF compounds.
- The quality question is whether 2,000-2,500 new tenancies/year is achievable — FY 2025 delivered 2,538, so the run-rate is confirmed. The risk is sustainability if MNO capex moderates.

Solar programme as value-chain differentiation — worth pricing in
- Each solar-equipped tower improves site-level margin by ~$17,000/year permanently (post-payback).
- At 14,325 sites, if Helios solar-equips 50% of its portfolio over 5 years (~1,400 sites/year), that is $10m+ of incremental EBITDA annual accretion from year 3 onward — directionally positive and structurally differentiated.

M&A optionality: further MNO tower carve-outs remain the primary growth avenue
- Several African MNOs continue to hold tower estates that could be divested.
- Helios's nine-market operational presence, balance sheet (now within leverage target), and MNO relationship depth position it as a preferred buyer for any carve-out in its existing footprint.
- Management has characterised M&A as lower priority vs organic growth and deleveraging under Impact 2030 — appropriate given the current focus on demonstrating FCF discipline to the market.

What must happen for Helios to defend and extend its position:
- Maintain MNO relationships and contract renewal success rates at historical levels.
- Continue solar programme roll-out at pace to capture OPEX savings before competitors do.
- Deleverage toward 2.5x to access investment-grade funding and reduce interest burden.
- Navigate MTN-IHS competitive development without losing co-location share in overlapping markets.
- Execute tenancy additions at 2,000-2,500/year, driven by MNO 4G/5G deployment timelines.

12. Sceptical View on Value Chain Position

Bear case: contracted structure does not eliminate fragility
- Take-or-pay sounds ironclad but depends on MNO counterparty solvency. In 2022-2023, Airtel Africa's balance sheet deterioration (FX losses, high leverage) raised genuine concern about its ability to service tower leases. IHS was actively managing 9mobile in Nigeria — a mid-market MNO — through financial stress rather than enforcing contracts commercially. The "contractual protection" is diluted in practice when the counterparty is sovereign-adjacent (state-owned or strategically important).
- Helios's leverage profile means any significant revenue shortfall — even a 10% miss vs expectations — has amplified impact on equity valuation given ~3.4x net debt multiple.

Could IHS/ATC outcompete Helios on scale economics?
- IHS pre-MTN: ~37,700 towers vs Helios's 14,325 is a 2.6x scale advantage. On paper, scale should drive lower per-site OPEX. In practice, IHS's Nigeria concentration undermined this advantage — FX devaluation on majority of its EBITDA base negated the scale benefit.
- Post-MTN acquisition: if MTN-IHS operates at scale with MTN's balance sheet behind it, it could theoretically outbid Helios for new tower carve-out transactions.
- ATC: the global scale of American Tower Corp ($5bn+ EBITDA globally) provides potential for cross-subsidisation of African operations; in practice ATC has shown less appetite for frontier African markets.
- Assessment: scale economies are real but partially offset by Helios's geographic diversification advantage and higher tenancy ratio.

Is sub-Saharan African tower ownership genuinely high-barrier-to-entry?
- A well-capitalised new entrant (e.g., a PE-backed infrastructure fund with $3-5bn) could theoretically build a competing estate through MNO carve-outs.
- Ground leases, regulatory approvals, and MNO relationships are the key barriers — all surmountable with time and capital.
- The more practical barrier is finding willing sellers: most of the easy MNO tower carve-outs have already been completed in Helios's markets.
- Assessment: barriers are real but not permanent; a sustained period of above-market towerco returns would attract capital and erode competitive advantages over a 10-15 year horizon.

Could Airtel Africa financial stress lead to renegotiated contract terms?
- FY 2025/2026 data suggests Airtel Africa has stabilised (EBITDA margins ~49%, revenue up 25.8% in H1 FY2026) — the acute stress scenario has receded.
- However, Airtel Africa remains a high-leverage emerging market telecoms business exposed to further FX devaluation across multiple African currencies.
- Were Airtel Africa to face a second FX-driven deterioration, the financial pressure to renegotiate tower lease terms would re-emerge.
- Helios's contractual protections are strong on paper; the practical question is whether it would enforce them or negotiate commercially with its largest customer relationship at stake.

Value Chain Position Sustainability Rating: YELLOW-GREEN

Rating: YELLOW-GREEN

The towerco stage of the sub-Saharan African telecoms value chain is the most structurally sound position in the chain — contracted revenues, inflation-linked escalators, take-or-pay protection, near-zero incremental cost of colocations, and long asset lives create a highly defensible business.

The rating is not full GREEN for three reasons:

  1. FX exposure on 29% of EBITDA in local currencies with devaluation histories; USD/GBP debt creates a structural mismatch that requires sustained leverage discipline to manage.

  2. MTN-IHS vertical integration introduces competitive and structural uncertainty into a landscape that was previously purely independent-towerco-vs-independent-towerco; the medium-term implications for co-location competition and MNO negotiating dynamics are not yet clear.

  3. MNO customer concentration and counterparty risk — particularly Airtel Africa — means the quality of the contracted revenue book is only as good as the MNOs' ability to pay. The take-or-pay structure protects cash flow in normal conditions but does not eliminate credit exposure in stress scenarios.

None of these factors undermine the fundamental quality of Helios's value-chain position. They place it at YELLOW-GREEN — structurally strong, defensible, and improving, but not without meaningful risk factors that require ongoing monitoring.
