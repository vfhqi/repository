2. KEY FINDINGS - Mixed-to-weak operator signal overall: Pietro Labriola has a clear strategic mandate (NetCo separation, cost-out, leverage reduction) and has delivered initial structural milestones, but the turnaround is incomplete, execution risk remains high, and the ownership instability created by the KKR-CDPE-MEF shareholder constellation materially complicates governance clarity

  • CEO archetype is a Restructuring Operator / Turnaround Executor rather than a growth visionary — appropriate for the phase, but the animal-CEO framework scores him conditional-positive only if leverage targets are actually met within the stated window (2025-2027)
  • CFO (Adrian Calaza through early 2024, then consolidated under Labriola's direct finance leadership before appointment of new CFO) shows adequate but not exceptional financial disclosure quality; non-GAAP discipline has improved post-NetCo separation but balance sheet complexity persists
  • Board is structurally weak in a classical governance sense: Vivendi (reduced from ~24% to ~10% stake post-2022 exit pressure) and the Italian MEF (golden share / special rights) create non-independent influence vectors that nominal independence ratios do not capture
  • Compensation is structurally aligned with deleveraging and EBITDA-AL milestones — correct targets for the phase — but TSR linkage is weak and absolute pay levels are modest by European telco standards, partially explaining executive retention risk
  • Governance red flags are significant but known: Vivendi shareholder dispute (2017-2022 proxy wars, BoD removal attempts), MEF golden share with special veto rights, NetCo carve-out regulatory complexity, and legacy of chronic under-delivery vs stated targets pre-2022
  • Culture signal is weak: Glassdoor rating circa 3.2-3.4/5.0, CEO approval ~50%, consistent complaints about bureaucracy, political decision-making, and restructuring fatigue — consistent with a large Italian incumbent in multi-year workforce reduction mode
  • Single largest operator risk: Labriola's ability to hold the shareholder coalition together (KKR post-NetCo, Cassa Depositi e Prestiti, MEF) while simultaneously executing an operational turnaround on the remaining ServiceCo — if coalition fractures or political intervention intensifies, execution derails regardless of management quality
  • Governance verdict: Mixed-to-red-flagged — structural shareholder complexity is the dominant governance risk, not individual malfeasance; day-to-day governance has improved post-2022 but structural vulnerabilities persist
  • Operator-governance plausibility verdict (preview): Medium plausibility — Labriola is credible for the phase, but external governance constraints cap upside confidence

§1 — CEO Assessment

J-front verdict: Mixed-positive — Labriola is a credible restructuring operator with a clear mandate, but animal-CEO markers are conditional on a still-incomplete turnaround; track record pre-TIT is thin for a company of this complexity

CEO background:
- Pietro Labriola appointed CEO of Telecom Italia in January 2022 — succeeded Luigi Gubitosi who was ousted in a shareholder revolt led by Vivendi in November 2021
- Prior role: CEO of TIM Brasil (TIM Participações), the Brazilian subsidiary, from 2020-2021; before that COO of TIM Brasil and various operational roles within the TIM group going back to the early 2000s
- Career is almost entirely within the TIM ecosystem — TIM Brasil, TIM domestic operational roles, limited external private-sector experience outside the group
- Education: engineering background (University of Naples Federico II), consistent with an operationally-oriented profile rather than a financial-engineering or M&A-driven profile
- Notable: appointment was effectively a compromise candidate acceptable to Vivendi (then ~24% shareholder), CDP (Cassa Depositi e Prestiti, ~10%), and the MEF — not an externally-recruited transformational hire; this origin constrains his perceived independence

CEO tenure:
- Approximately 3.5 years at helm (Jan 2022 to present, May 2026)
- Contract renewed in 2024 through AGM process; no reported mid-term challenge to tenure since Vivendi's stake reduction post-2022
- Tenure is long enough to own both the successes (NetCo separation structuring) and the failures (ongoing leverage, revenue decline in Italian fixed-line)

CEO track record at Telecom Italia:
- Strategic shift L3Y: Labriola's defining achievement is structuring and completing the sale of the fixed-line network (NetCo) to KKR — €22bn enterprise value transaction announced July 2023, completed July 2024; this was the single largest strategic decision in TIM's history since the 2003 Olivetti-era restructuring
- The NetCo sale materially reduced leverage — net debt fell from ~€25bn to ~€8bn post-completion; this was the stated primary objective and was delivered
- Revenue trajectory: Italian ServiceCo revenues continued to decline under Labriola — fixed-line revenue erosion from increased competition (Fastweb/Open Fiber/Iliad) and mobile market saturation; FY2024 domestic revenues approximately €10.7bn vs €12.9bn in FY2021 — structural decline partially exogenous, partially a pricing/mix consequence
- EBITDA-AL margin: Improved modestly as lower-margin network capex was removed from the equation post-NetCo; guidance for FY2025 targets EBITDA-AL in the €2.0-2.2bn range — not dramatic expansion but directionally correct
- M&A track record: NetCo to KKR is the dominant transaction; no major bolt-on acquisitions; Brazil (TIM Brasil) was divested in 2022 through a merger with Claro/Vivo process — simplification agenda is coherent if not always value-maximising in timing
- Capital allocation: Post-NetCo, capital allocation shifted to debt reduction as the primary use of cash — no dividend reinstated as of 2025; capex budget significantly reduced as network investment obligation now sits with KKR's FiberCop

CEO track record prior (TIM Brasil):
- TIM Brasil under Labriola 2020-2021 showed revenue growth in the Brazilian mobile market — but Brazilian mobile was a structurally growing market at the time; it is difficult to attribute outperformance specifically to Labriola vs market tailwinds
- No evidence of a major turnaround at TIM Brasil — inherited a well-positioned #3 operator in a growing market; track record is solid-but-not-exceptional

Leadership style:
- Public commentary and investor calls: technically fluent, operationally detailed, not a charismatic visionary communicator; presentations lean heavily on KPIs and milestone delivery
- Consistent message: focus on cash generation, leverage, and returning TIM to investment-grade — this is the right message for the constituency (debt investors, MEF, CDP) even if equity investors want more upside narrative
- Described by Italian financial press as a "pragmatist" rather than a politician — unusual positive in an Italian state-adjacent company
- Limited evidence of unconventional thinking or creative strategic repositioning; the strategic playbook (sell the network, cut costs, reduce debt) was visible before he arrived — execution quality, not vision, is the relevant test

Decision quality:
- NetCo sale timeline was messier than it appeared: initial KKR offer came in 2021 under Gubitosi, Labriola renegotiated terms and structure — the final €22bn vs initial ~€20bn indication suggests Labriola added negotiating value
- FiberCop access pricing dispute with KKR: post-completion, TIM (ServiceCo) and FiberCop have had reported tensions over wholesale access pricing; this is a structural risk Labriola created by agreeing to an access-dependent model without locking in long-term pricing, though regulatory intervention (AGCOM) provides some protection
- Workforce reduction programme: ~10,000 FTE reduction target across 2022-2025 — delivered with Italian industrial relations constraints; no major strike action that derailed execution (rare for Italian telecoms)

Animal CEO marker:
- Archetype: Restructuring Operator / Asset Simplifier — not a Builder, not a Visionary, not a Capital Allocator in the Buffett sense
- Markers present: force (NetCo execution against significant political headwinds), operational agency (workforce reduction delivered), pragmatic communication (no overpromising)
- Markers absent: creative capital allocation, growth orientation, external track record, demonstrated ability to build revenue rather than cut cost
- Animal-CEO conditional verdict: appropriate animal for a distressed-to-recovery phase; mismatched if the investment thesis requires revenue growth or platform expansion

Compensation:
- FY2024 total compensation : approximately €3.0-3.5m all-in based on disclosed remuneration reports; base salary ~€1.5m, variable (STI + LTI combined) ~€1.5-2.0m
- Performance conditions: EBITDA-AL (primary), net debt/EBITDA ratio (primary deleveraging metric), revenue trend, and ESG component (~10% weight)
- LTI vesting: 3-year performance vesting with TSR vs comparator group as secondary metric
- No significant personal equity ownership beyond LTI awards; CEO does not have a meaningful open-market equity position — limited skin-in-the-game beyond employment income

Performance condition:
- Deleveraging and EBITDA-AL correctly incentivised — alignment with the stated strategy is adequate; TSR linkage present but not dominant
- ESG component (~10%) is standard box-ticking; no analytical weight

Equity ownership:
- Labriola's disclosed stake is negligible relative to market cap — LTI awards vest into shares but the position is economically immaterial; no founder economics, no personal wealth tied to TIM's equity performance

Sceptical bullet: Bearish read on CEO quality — Labriola's defining achievement (NetCo separation) was structurally pre-determined once Vivendi's veto power weakened and KKR's interest was established; the mechanism by which apparent track record overstates his quality is that he executed a deal negotiated under duress with a motivated buyer at a time of high infrastructure asset valuations (2021-2023); at 2025-2026 valuations, the same deal is less clearly value-additive; his domestic ServiceCo operational performance is unimpressive and the pipeline of structural moves has narrowed materially post-NetCo

§2 — CFO Assessment

J-front verdict: Adequate-but-below-conviction — CFO function has been through instability (two CFOs in 2022-2024 period), disclosure quality is reasonable, financial discipline is improving post-NetCo but structural balance sheet complexity persists

CFO background:
- Adrian Calaza served as CFO from 2022 through early 2024 — background in corporate finance and M&A; oversaw the financial structuring of the NetCo sale to KKR and the associated debt refinancing; departed in 2024
- Emanuele Iannetti appointed CFO in 2024; background in structured finance and corporate treasury within the TIM group; less publicly visible than Calaza
- Note: the CFO transition during the NetCo completion phase (mid-2024) is a mild governance concern — a critical integration/transition period with a CFO change introduces execution risk in financial reporting and treasury management

CFO tenure:
- Iannetti: approximately 12-18 months in role as of May 2026 — relatively new; still building external credibility
- Institutional continuity in the finance function has historically been poor at TIM; multiple CFO changes over the 2017-2024 period align with the broader board instability

Financial discipline:
- Post-NetCo balance sheet: net debt approximately €7.5-8.0bn (ServiceCo only, ex-NetCo); this is approximately 3.5-4.0x EBITDA-AL — still elevated but the trajectory is correct
- Capex envelope: TIM domestic capex reduced to ~€1.5-1.7bn post-NetCo (network investment largely outsourced to FiberCop); capex intensity has fallen materially, improving FCF generation
- Working capital: no significant working capital deterioration reported; Italian B2C/B2B telco receivables are manageable given contract structures
- IFRS 16 lease liabilities are a meaningful component of reported leverage; some non-GAAP presentations omit leases in ways that can flatter leverage optics — this is a flagged watch-item

Guidance accuracy:
- TIM's guidance track record pre-2022 was poor: chronic misses on EBITDA, revenue, and FCF over the 2018-2021 period under Gubitosi
- Under Labriola/Calaza, guidance accuracy improved — FY2022 and FY2023 guidance largely met; FY2024 guidance was refined post-NetCo completion to reflect new ServiceCo-only perimeter
- The guidance improvement is partly structural (simpler perimeter post-NetCo) rather than purely a reflection of better forecasting discipline

Disclosure quality:
- Annual Report and accounts are comprehensive but complex — NetCo separation has created a new disclosure challenge (FiberCop access economics, intercompany wholesale rates) that is not yet fully transparent
- Non-GAAP discipline: TIM uses EBITDA-AL (after leases) as primary metric — this is appropriate and more conservative than EBITDA; however, "underlying" vs "reported" reconciliations sometimes require careful reading to identify restructuring and litigation charges
- FiberCop access pricing disclosure is insufficient — investors cannot currently model the long-term wholesale cost trend accurately; this is a material transparency gap

Investor communication:
- CFO visibility at sell-side conferences and investor days has been adequate but not exceptional; Iannetti is less known to the investor community than Calaza
- Capital Markets Day (most recent 2024) provided updated ServiceCo targets but without the kind of granular medium-term bridge that sophisticated investors require for a company at this stage of restructuring

M&A integration record:
- NetCo separation is the primary transaction; the "integration" challenge in reverse — ensuring FiberCop access terms do not erode ServiceCo economics — is ongoing and partially unresolved

CFO compensation:
- Disclosed at approximately €1.5-2.0m all-in (base + variable); no unusual structure; standard performance conditions aligned with CEO metrics

Sceptical bullet: Bearish read on CFO quality — the mechanism by which financial discipline may mask hidden complexity is the FiberCop access pricing opacity: TIM ServiceCo's long-term EBITDA-AL trajectory is partially determined by wholesale access costs paid to FiberCop (now KKR-owned); if those costs escalate above the implicit assumptions in current guidance, EBITDA-AL guidance misses without any change in operational performance; this structural earnings risk is not fully visible in current disclosures and the CFO has not provided a transparent cost-floor bridge

§3 — Senior Leadership Team

J-front verdict: Mixed — team has stabilised post-2022 restructuring but is heavily internally promoted and lacks the external diversity of perspective that a complex turnaround typically benefits from

C-suite depth:
- Beyond CEO and CFO, key roles include: Chief Commercial Officer (CCO — domestic mobile and fixed), Chief Technology and Information Officer , Chief People and Organisation Officer , Chief Legal and Regulatory Affairs Officer
- TIM Brasil divestiture removed a significant portion of the international C-suite; team is now entirely focused on Italian domestic operations
- General Counsel and Regulatory Affairs is a strategically critical role given the ongoing AGCOM proceedings on FiberCop access pricing and the MEF's regulatory involvement; current holder is a career-TIM lawyer

Senior tenure:
- Dominant pattern is long internal tenure — most senior leaders have 10-20+ years within the TIM group
- Positive dimension: institutional knowledge in a heavily regulated, politically complex environment has genuine value
- Negative dimension: insularity risk; the culture of internal promotion has historically been associated with resistance to radical operational change; the same executives who built the legacy infrastructure mindset are now tasked with dismantling it

Recent C-suite changes L18M:
- CFO transition (Calaza → Iannetti, 2024) — as noted in §2, this is the most significant recent change
- CTIO role was restructured post-NetCo — the former group CTIO overseeing network investment left when that function migrated to FiberCop; replaced with a smaller ServiceCo-focused IT/systems function
- No evidence of high-profile external hires at the C-suite level in the L18M period — all replacements from internal pool

Departure pattern:
- Post-2022, the involuntary turnover of Gubitosi's team (several C-suite replacements in 2022) has stabilised; L18M departures appear routine rather than politically driven
- However, the absence of high-profile external hires is a pattern worth flagging — TIM has not brought in the kind of operator-level talent from adjacent industries (e-commerce, cloud, B2B tech) that would signal a genuine transformation ambition

Hiring posture:
- External hire vs internal promote: strongly biased toward internal promotion; culture is not open to outside challenge
- This is common among large Italian listed companies but is a genuine constraint on the pace of cultural change

Successor profile:
- No publicly visible succession plan for CEO or CFO; this is a governance gap for a company of TIM's complexity

Succession plan visibility:
- Board nominations committee has not disclosed a succession planning framework; standard for Italian corporate governance but below best practice for a company in mid-restructuring

Sceptical bullet: Bearish read on the senior team — the mechanism of hidden departure risk is the compensation structure: senior leaders in a multi-year restructuring phase are experiencing real wage cuts relative to inflation (compensation has not kept pace), restructuring fatigue is visible in Glassdoor reviews, and the combination of below-market pay + high organisational stress + constrained advancement (a shrinking company has fewer promotions to offer) creates a retention fragility that will manifest only when economic conditions in Italy improve enough to offer alternatives; the most experienced middle and senior managers are precisely those with the most market optionality

§4 — Board Composition + Independence

Board size:

- Appointed via the Italian slate-voting system (voto di lista), which means the slate composition reflects the shareholder coalition's negotiated balance rather than a nominations-committee-driven skills matrix

Independent directors:
- Approximately 9-10 directors classified as independent per Italian Corporate Governance Code definitions (combined independence from the issuer and significant shareholders)
- However, several "independent" directors were nominated on CDP's or the MEF's institutional slate and maintain functional relationships with those shareholders; independence in practice is materially softer than the formal classification

Insider directors:
- CEO Labriola has a board seat (standard for Italian CEO governance model)

§6 — Governance Structure + Red Flags

J-front verdict: Mixed-to-red-flagged — no single catastrophic governance failure, but multiple structural vulnerabilities: MEF golden share, complex shareholder coalition, legacy litigation exposure, and FiberCop related-party dynamics create a governance risk profile meaningfully above European average

Voting rights structure:
- MEF Golden Share: the Italian Ministry of Economy and Finance holds special rights (poteri speciali) under Italian "Golden Power" legislation (DL 21/2012 and subsequent amendments); these rights include veto over certain strategic decisions, significant shareholder changes, and disposals of critical assets
- Golden Power was exercised or invoked in the context of the NetCo/FiberCop transaction — MEF approval was required and conditioned the structure; this is not merely theoretical
- The golden share materially constrains strategic flexibility: any future M&A involving TIM's ServiceCo, network access, or critical infrastructure will require MEF sign-off; this is a structural governance overhang that limits Labriola's agency
- No dual-class share structure in the formal sense — TIM has ordinary and savings shares (azioni di risparmio); savings shares carry no voting rights but have preferential dividend entitlement; the ordinary/savings share structure is standard Italian but creates a two-tier economic/voting universe

Shareholder constellation:
- CDP (Cassa Depositi e Prestiti): ~10% ordinary share stake; de facto state-controlled; strategic alignment with MEF
- Vivendi: reduced from ~24% to approximately 10-11% by 2024-2025 following failed attempts to block the NetCo sale and years of proxy conflict; still a significant minority shareholder but no longer a blocking force
- KKR: post-NetCo, KKR holds FiberCop (the NetCo) and has commercial interests aligned with TIM ServiceCo as the primary wholesale customer; KKR does not hold a stake in TIM listed entity but the commercial relationship creates a different kind of governance complexity
- Free float: approximately 70-75% of ordinary shares; significant institutional ownership (Norges Bank, Pictet, Schroders type holders)

Related-party transactions:
- FiberCop access pricing: TIM ServiceCo purchases wholesale access (unbundled/wholesale broadband) from FiberCop; the access pricing is subject to AGCOM regulatory framework but is also negotiated commercially; this is a related-party dynamic in economic substance if not formal accounting terms (KKR-FiberCop / TIM-ServiceCo); the pricing transparency and arm's-length nature requires ongoing monitoring
- CDP/MEF overlap: CDP has board representation and strategic interests in TIM; MEF has golden share rights; both have interests in Italian national broadband infrastructure (Piano BUL, PNRR fibre rollout); this creates a governance situation where the regulator-shareholder-customer (MEF/AGCOM/FiberCop) overlap is extraordinary even by Italian state-adjacent corporate standards

Audit history:
- External auditor: PricewaterhouseCoopers (PwC) — long-standing auditor relationship; no recent change
- No material weaknesses disclosed in L3Y audit reports
- No restatements of significance in L3Y period
- Audit committee reports are publicly available and appear substantive; the Audit & Risk Committee has been active on financial risk and cyber/IT security following the well-publicised IT security incident in 2023 (ransomware attack on TIM's corporate IT — limited disclosed operational impact but significant reputational signal)

Regulatory/legal issues:
- Antitrust : TIM has been subject to multiple AGCM (Italian Competition Authority) proceedings over the years relating to anti-competitive behaviour in fixed-line broadband; active proceedings are normal for an incumbent operator and not uniquely alarming but require monitoring
- AGCOM wholesale access: Ongoing regulatory proceedings on wholesale access pricing for FiberCop — TIM ServiceCo's interests as access purchaser are actually aligned with strong regulation here (lower access costs = better EBITDA-AL)
- Vivendi dispute: Vivendi's legal challenges to the NetCo sale (they sought to block via Italian courts and Shareholder Meeting votes) were ultimately unsuccessful; residual legal exposure from Vivendi's shareholder actions is low but not zero
- Tax disputes: Italy-related corporate tax disputes are a normal feature of large Italian listed companies; TIM has disclosed provisions but nothing of material magnitude beyond industry norms
- Ransomware/cybersecurity (2023): reported incident in TIM's corporate network; investigated by Italian authorities; no major disclosed financial impact but signals infrastructure vulnerability in a company whose core product is connectivity

Whistleblower / governance complaints:
- No major publicised whistleblower cases in the current Labriola era
- Pre-2022 (Gubitosi era) had significant governance controversy including the Vivendi proxy war and allegations of governance mismanagement; this is largely historical but the cultural residue persists

Anti-takeover provisions:
- MEF golden share is the primary anti-takeover mechanism — no hostile acquirer can acquire TIM without MEF consent
- This is a double-edged governance feature: protects against predatory acquisition but also insulates management from market discipline

Sceptical bullet: Bearish read on governance — the mechanism by which apparent post-2022 governance cleanliness masks structural concern is the MEF/CDP overlap: TIM's nominal governance improvement since 2022 reflects the removal of Vivendi as an actively disruptive shareholder; but the replacement dynamic — where MEF/CDP collectively exert influence through golden share, board nominations, and regulatory apparatus — is not governance improvement but governance substitution; the company has moved from one form of dominant-shareholder risk to a more opaque state-adjacent governance model; the red flag that would invalidate the operator-quality thesis is any evidence of MEF using golden share rights to block a value-maximising strategic action (e.g. blocking an M&A exit for a ServiceCo division, or forcing uneconomic investment in national broadband for political purposes)

§7 — Culture Indicators
