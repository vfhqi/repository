§6 — Governance Structure + Red Flags

J-front verdict: Governance is functionally clean — no red flags identified — but the dual-class share structure and Swedish nomination committee model are structural concentrations worth understanding.

Dual-class structure:
- A/B shares: Sdiptech has A shares (1 vote) and B shares (1 vote) — importantly, the publicly traded share (SDIP.B) is the B class; the voting structure as disclosed does not appear to be a severe dual-class arrangement (e.g. 10:1 super-voting) of the type common in founder-controlled US tech companies
- Effective control: Major shareholders (anchor investors + founding interests) hold a combined stake that provides effective governance influence through the nomination committee rather than through super-voting shares; this is the Swedish model and is standard
- Risk assessment: The dual-class structure, to the extent it exists in its current form, does not represent an extreme shareholder rights concern — but the concentration of nomination committee power in major shareholders is the structural equivalent and should be monitored if major shareholders change

Founder control:
- Structure: Sdiptech is not a founder-CEO company in the strict sense (Holm joined as CEO in 2017/18 post-IPO); the company was founded by others and has an anchor shareholder base rather than a single founder controlling via equity
- Practical control: The CEO-major shareholder alignment is positive (both oriented toward long-term compounding) but means there is no structural tension between management and ownership that would check CEO behaviour

Related-party transactions:
- Assessment: No material related-party transactions of a concerning nature are identified in available public filings; Sdiptech's acquisition activity is with unrelated third parties; no executive self-dealing or family-company transactions disclosed
- Subsidiary management: The decentralised model creates a theoretical risk of subsidiary-level related-party transactions below Group disclosure thresholds, but no specific concerns are visible in public sources

Audit history:
- Auditor: Sdiptech is audited by a Big Four firm (to be confirmed — likely Deloitte or KPMG based on Nordic mid-cap norms); no auditor change in the recent historical period is identified
- Material weaknesses: No material weaknesses disclosed in available public filings
- Restatements: No restatement history identified; financial reporting continuity appears clean

Legal/regulatory issues:
- Class actions: No class-action lawsuits identified in available public sources
- Regulatory probes: No SEC/FI (Finansinspektionen) or equivalent regulatory probe identified; Sdiptech is subject to Swedish regulatory oversight appropriate for a Nasdaq Stockholm listed company
- Competition/ESG compliance: Infrastructure technology subsidiaries operate in regulated sectors (water, energy, telecom) — each subsidiary is subject to sector-specific regulation; no Group-level compliance failures publicly disclosed

Anti-takeover provisions:
- Assessment: The Swedish corporate model provides limited formal anti-takeover protection beyond the voting structure; no poison pill or shareholder rights plan equivalent identified; the major shareholder base provides de facto anti-takeover protection through ownership concentration

Whistleblower signals:
- Assessment: No whistleblower reports, governance complaints, or employee dispute escalations identified in available public sources; absence of signal is not conclusive but is a baseline positive

⚠️ Sceptical bullet — governance: The bearish read is that the Swedish nomination committee model, the aligned major shareholder base, and the structurally CEO-friendly board composition mean that if Jakob Holm were to make a capital allocation mistake of significant scale (e.g. a large strategic acquisition at an elevated multiple), the governance architecture has no structural mechanism to stop him — the board is populated by individuals who share his strategic worldview, and the major shareholders are aligned with growth-oriented compounding; the governance architecture is designed to facilitate value creation, not to check it — and this architecture works brilliantly when the CEO is right and catastrophically when he is wrong.
